Chapter 1: The Station

The train stopped at a platform with no name board. Rose stepped down with her kitbag and the wind hit her, salt-thick and cold. She was the only passenger. The stationmaster had already gone back inside his box, a white mug steaming in his hand.

She walked the length of the platform and down the wooden steps to a gravel road. Beyond the road, the sea was a flat sheet of gunmetal under a low sky. No trees. No houses. Just the concrete blockhouse a quarter mile ahead, its roof bristling with aerials and the heavy drum of a receiver array turning in the wind.

Her orders said Shingle Cove. The paper was creased from sitting in her coat pocket for three days. She had read it so many times the words had worn thin: Wren Rose Clayton, HMS Shingle Cove, Signals Intelligence.

She had never seen the sea before September. Now she lived inside its noise.

A lorry rattled past, then stopped. The driver leaned across and pushed the passenger door open. "You the new Wren?"

"Yes."

"Get in. It's further than it looks."

Rose climbed in. The seat was torn, stuffing bleeding from a split seam. The lorry lurched forward. Through the windscreen she watched the blockhouse grow larger. No flag flew. No sign marked what happened inside. That was the point.

She had been a schoolteacher in May. Now she carried secrets she was not supposed to understand. The lorry bumped over the gravel and the sea kept its own counsel.

Chapter 2: The Wrens

Mabel Finch wore a serge uniform so heavily pressed the creases could have drawn blood. She stood behind a steel desk with a clipboard in her hand and read Rose's name without looking at her face.

"Rose Clayton. Somerset. You trained at Bletchley."

"Yes, ma'am."

"We don't say ma'am. We say Finch." She finally looked up. Her eyes were the color of wet slate. "You know the Morse tables?"

"Yes."

"The Type-X?"

"Some."

"You'll learn the rest." Finch turned and walked down a narrow corridor. Rose followed. The walls were painted the green of a doctor's surgery. Pipes ran along the ceiling, sweating condensation.

The decoding room held six Type-X machines in two rows. Three Wrens sat at them, headsets on, fingers moving across keyboards. The room smelled of ink, machine oil, and damp wool from drying greatcoats hung on a rack by the door. A single stove in the corner glowed red.

"Your station is number four," Finch said. "Weaver will show you."

Alice Weaver lifted her headset and grinned. She was round-faced and dark-haired, no older than Rose. "New blood. Thank God."

Finch left. Alice pulled out the chair at station four. "She grows on you. Like a rash. Sit."

Rose sat. The keys of the Type-X were worn smooth from thousands of hands. The paper tape fed out in a curl, empty for now. Waiting.

"We work four on, eight off," Alice said. "But the war doesn't know that. Sometimes it's twelve on, four off. Commander Bryce likes to say the Germans don't take tea breaks."

"Does he?"

"No. I just said that. He's terrifying. You'll see."

Rose touched the keys. They were warm from the previous shift. She was no longer a teacher. She was something else now.

Chapter 3: First Signal

The signal came through at 14:17 hours. Alice tapped Rose's shoulder and pointed at the paper tape feeding from the receiver.

"Yours."

Rose's mouth went dry. She had decoded hundreds of signals in training, but those were exercises, fabricated messages about fictional convoys carrying mock supplies to pretend ports. This was real.

She started with the preamble. The call sign resolved to a merchant vessel, SS Fort Cumberland, eastbound in convoy SC-177 from Halifax. Position: 54.12 North, 22.08 West. Speed: 8 knots. No contact. No incidents.

The decoding was straightforward. Slip the encrypted groups from the tape, align them to the daily key sheet, transpose the letters. Rose's fingers moved slowly at first, then faster as the pattern locked in. The message came through clear.

All well. Weather moderate. ETA Londonderry 48 hours.

She wrote the decoded text on a signal form and handed it to Finch, who read it and nodded once. That was all. No congratulations. No ceremony.

Rose sat back down. Her hands were shaking. Across the room, Alice winked.

She had done it. She had reached across the Atlantic and pulled a whisper from the water. The war was still out there, but for a moment she had held a piece of it in her hands.

Chapter 4: Commander Bryce

Commander Phillip Bryce was a tall man with a sunken chest and the posture of someone who had spent twenty years bending over charts. His office was a converted storeroom with a single porthole painted over in gray. The only light came from a green-shaded desk lamp.

He did not ask Rose to sit.

"Wren Clayton. Your file says you were a teacher."

"Yes, sir."

"I don't care what you were. I care what you are. You are a machine that turns noise into sense. You will not talk about what you decode. You will not write it down anywhere except on the forms provided. You will not keep copies. You will not tell your mother, your priest, or your God." He paused. "Do you have a sweetheart?"

Rose blinked. "No, sir."

"Good. Keep it that way. Your work requires your full attention. Distraction sinks ships."

He handed her a folded paper. "Your shift rotation. Learn it. Be on time."

Rose took the paper. Her fingers brushed the edge of his desk. She saw a photograph face-down in the corner. A man in naval uniform, visible only as the back of a head.

She did not ask.

"Thank you, sir."

"Close the door on your way out."

She closed it. The corridor was dark. She stood for a moment with her hand on the latch, breathing. The photograph haunted her more than the warnings.

Chapter 5: The Routine

Two weeks passed in a blur of watches. Rose learned to sleep in three-hour segments. She learned that the stove in the decoding room gave off heat only on one side, so the left half of her body was always warm and the right half cold. She learned to read Finch's moods by the set of her shoulders.

The work followed a rhythm. Signals arrived in bursts, then silence. A convoy crossing the Atlantic generated a position report every four hours. A wolfpack attack produced a flood of frantic traffic, overlapping and garbled, the decoders working at full stretch while the tape piled up on the floor.

Rose was good. She could decode faster than Alice, faster than any of the other Wrens. Finch began giving her the priority signals, the ones marked Most Immediate. Rose did not ask what happened after she handed them over. She knew they went to Bryce, and from Bryce to the operations room, and from there to the ships at sea.

Sometimes, when a signal reported a ship lost, the decoding room would go quiet for a moment. Then someone would feed fresh tape into the machine and the clatter would resume.

There was no time for grief. There was only the next signal, the next tape, the next set of coordinates.

Chapter 6: HMS Archer

The first message about HMS Archer came through at 03:44 on a Thursday morning. Rose was alone on the night watch, nursing a cup of tea that had gone cold two hours ago.

The signal was garbled. Bad atmospherics, or damage to the transmitter. Rose ran it twice before the shape emerged.

CALL SIGN: ARCHER. CLASS: DESTROYER. STATUS: DAMAGED.

The coordinates put her west of Ireland, making for Londonderry at reduced speed. U-boat contact at 21:12 the previous evening. One torpedo struck aft. Casualties unknown. Engineering party working to restore propulsion.

Rose transcribed the decoded text onto a form and set it in the out-tray. Then she sat back and stared at the paper.

HMS Archer. She had never heard of the ship. But now she knew it was out there, holed below the waterline, crawling home through the dark.

She finished her watch in silence. When Alice came to relieve her at 08:00, Rose did not mention the Archer. But she thought about it all through breakfast, the image of a steel hull filling with water, men working in the dark. She did not know why the ship had taken hold of her imagination, but the feeling refused to loosen. It anchored itself in her chest like a hook.

Chapter 7: Davies

The follow-up signal arrived twelve hours later. Rose caught it at the start of her afternoon watch.

ARCHER. CASUALTY REPORT. 3 KILLED. 7 WOUNDED. NAMES FOLLOW.

She decoded the names slowly, letter by letter.

KILLED: Able Seaman Thomas McGrath. Leading Seaman Frederick Poole. Ordinary Seaman James Walsh.

WOUNDED: Lieutenant D. Davies. Able Seaman George Henshaw. Stoker Arthur Pease.

The list went on. Seven names. Lieutenant D. Davies was the first officer listed.

Rose wrote the name on the form and paused. D. Davies. That was all. An initial and a surname. No age, no hometown, no face. Just a name on a list of the living.

She handed the form to Finch and went back to her machine. But the name stayed with her. D. Davies. She said it silently as she worked. Davies. Like it owed her something.

She told herself it was just the natural pull of a story. A damaged ship. A wounded officer. The next chapter would come, or it would not. She had no reason to care about this man. But her mind kept returning to his name as if to a splinter she could not dislodge.

She was wrong. The next chapter came. She did not know yet that it would change everything.

Chapter 8: The Casualty List

Rose decoded three more signals about the Archer before the week was out. The first confirmed the ship had reached Londonderry. The second listed the damage to the aft section as severe but not fatal. The third was a medical evacuation manifest.

Lieutenant D. Davies was among those transferred to the Royal Naval Hospital in Londonderry for treatment of shrapnel wounds to the torso and left leg.

Rose read the medical detail and felt her stomach tighten. She had no right to feel anything. She had never met this man. She did not know his first name. She did not know if he was kind or cruel, young or old, married or alone. She knew only that he had been standing somewhere on a destroyer when a torpedo hit, and that metal had torn through the space where he stood.

She folded the signal form and placed it in the out-tray.

"You're thinking too hard," Alice said from the next machine. "I can hear it."

"I'm decoding."

"You're brooding. There's a difference."

Rose did not answer. Outside, the wind rattled the windows. The sea was rough. She imagined it was rough everywhere, and that every ship felt it, every man on every deck.

Chapter 9: Fascination

Rose began checking every incoming signal for mention of the Archer. She told herself it was professional curiosity, nothing more. A damaged ship on the Atlantic run was a story with no ending yet. She wanted to know how the story finished.

But it was not professional curiosity. It was something else, something she could not name.

Alice noticed. Alice noticed everything.

"You're doing it again," Alice said one evening, pulling off her headset. "You're staring at the paper tape like it's a love letter."

"I'm not staring."

"You've been staring for three minutes. The machine isn't even running."

Rose looked down. The tape was still. She had been watching nothing.

"There's an officer on the Archer," she said. "Lieutenant Davies. He was wounded. I keep wondering if he made it."

"Made it where?"

"Made it." Rose shrugged. "Through."

Alice sat back. The lamp light caught the side of her face. "You can't do that. Finch told you. Bryce told you. The names are just names."

"I know."

"You know, but you're doing it anyway."

Rose said nothing. Alice sighed and turned back to her machine.

"Just don't let Finch catch you keeping a scrapbook," Alice said.

Rose flushed. In her coat pocket, folded small, was the signal flimsy with Davies's name on it. She had taken it from the waste bin that morning.

She told herself she would throw it away later.

She did not.

Chapter 10: Bryce's Warning

Commander Bryce called the Wrens into his office on the first of December. He stood behind his desk with his hands flat on the blotter. The photograph was still face-down.

"Some of you have been here since August. Some of you arrived yesterday. I don't care how long you have been here. I am telling you one time, and one time only."

He looked at each of them in turn. When his eyes reached Rose, she felt them settle and hold.

"These signals are not stories. They are not puzzles. They are operations reports. The men in those reports are not characters in a novel. They are real. You cannot save them by thinking about them. You cannot help them by imagining their faces. The only thing you can do is decode accurately and pass the information along."

He paused. The silence was absolute.

"If you find yourself becoming attached to a name, report to Mister Finch and request a transfer to clerical duties. No questions asked. But if I catch any of you keeping material from the decoding room, you will be discharged. No second chance."

Rose felt the signal flimsy in her coat pocket, pressed against her ribs. It might as well have been on fire.

"Dismissed."

The Wrens filed out. Alice touched Rose's elbow as they walked. Rose did not meet her eyes. The flimsy weighed against her ribs like an accusation.

Chapter 11: Silence

Four days passed with no news of the Archer. Rose checked every signal. Nothing.

She imagined the ship in drydock, cradled in iron braces, men crawling over its hull like ants. She imagined Lieutenant Davies in a hospital bed, his torso wrapped in bandages, staring at a ceiling.

She had no evidence for any of it. The imagination felt like a betrayal of the work. But she could not stop.

The December days were short and dark. Rose walked from the blockhouse to the mess hall with her head down against the wind. She ate without tasting. She slept without dreaming.

On the fourth day, Alice cornered her in the corridor.

"What is it?"

"Nothing."

"You're not eating. You're not talking. You look like you haven't slept."

"I'm fine."

"You're lying."

Rose pressed her back against the wall. The pipes above her head ticked as hot water moved through them.

"There's a ship," Rose said. "The Archer. I've been following it. There's been no signal in four days."

Alice stared at her. "That's not your problem."

"I know."

"Then stop."

"I can't."

Alice opened her mouth, closed it, and walked away. Rose stayed against the wall until the ticking pipes quieted. She could still hear the Archer in her head.

Chapter 12: News at Last

The signal came on the fifth day, during Rose's afternoon watch.

ARCHER. DAMAGE ASSESSMENT COMPLETE. REPAIRS UNDER WAY. ESTIMATED RETURN TO SERVICE: THREE WEEKS.

Rose read the decoded text three times. Then she closed her eyes and let out a breath she had not realized she was holding.

Alice watched her from the next machine. "Good news?"

"The Archer made it."

"You mean the ship you're not supposed to care about?"

"The same."

Alice shook her head, but she was smiling. "You're hopeless, Clayton."

"Probably."

Rose transcribed the signal and handed it to Finch. Her hands were steady now. The knot in her chest had loosened. She walked back to her station and sat down. The Type-X sat silent, waiting for the next transmission. Outside, the sky was a pale winter blue, the first clear sky in weeks. No clouds. No rain. Just light falling on the water like a promise.

The Archer was not dead. Not yet. Rose picked up her headset and waited for the next signal to come through. The relief loosened something in her chest that she had not known was so tight. She let herself feel it for a moment before turning back to the machine.

Chapter 13: The Medals Signal

The commendations list arrived on December 12. A general signal from the Admiralty, circulated to all stations for information.

Rose was not supposed to decode it. It was a priority C, routine traffic, usually handled by the junior Wren on administrative duty. But the junior Wren was sick, and Finch handed the stack to Rose.

She worked through the traffic mechanically. Convoy routes. Supply manifests. A request for shore leave from a depot in Greenock. And then:

THE FOLLOWING OFFICERS AND RATINGS ARE COMMENDED FOR GALLANTRY IN ACTION.

The list was long. Rose scanned the names, not looking for anything in particular. Then she found it.

LIEUTENANT D. DAVIES, HMS ARCHER. FOR COURAGE UNDER FIRE DURING TORPEDO DAMAGE CONTROL.

She read the line again. Then she read it a third time.

Davies had done something. During the attack, after the torpedo hit, while the ship was taking on water and men were dying, he had done something brave enough to earn a mention in an Admiralty signal.

Rose reached for a blank form and transcribed the line. Her hand was steady. She folded the paper and slid it into her pocket.

Later, in her bunk, she read it by torchlight under the blanket. The words had not changed. He was brave. He was alive.

She did not know him. But she knew that.

Chapter 14: Mabel Notices

Mabel Finch found Rose in the mess hall the next morning, staring at a cup of tea that had long gone cold. Finch sat down across from her. The bench creaked under her weight.

"You took a signal flimsy from the waste bin three days ago."

Rose's blood went cold. "I--"

"And you took another one yesterday."

Finch's voice was flat, her face unreadable. Rose could not tell if she was angry or merely stating a fact.

"I'm sorry," Rose said.

"Don't be sorry. Be smart." Finch leaned forward. "You think I don't know what it's like? I've been doing this since 1940. I have decoded the names of every man I grew up with. I decoded my own brother's death notice."

Rose stared at her.

"You think you're the first Wren to keep a name? You're not. You won't be the last. But you need to understand something." Finch tapped the table. "The man on that paper is not the man you imagine. You have invented him. He cannot live up to it."

"I haven't invented anything."

"You've invented everything. You don't know his voice, his smell, the way he takes his tea. You know a name and a medal citation. That is not a person."

Rose said nothing. Finch stood up.

"Burn the papers. Before they burn you."

She walked away. Rose sat alone at the table, the cold tea in front of her, the flimsies still in her pocket.

Chapter 15: The Archer Sails

On Christmas Eve, the Archer was declared fit for service.

The signal arrived at dusk. Rose was rotating the evening watch with Alice when the tape fed through. She decoded it in silence.

ARCHER. REPAIRS COMPLETE. CREW REEMBARKED. DEPARTING LONDONDERRY 26 DECEMBER. ASSIGNED TO CONVOY DUTY.

She transcribed the form and set it in the out-tray. Then she sat back and looked at the words.

The Archer was going back to sea. Lieutenant Davies was going with it.

Rose told herself she felt nothing. The ship was a ship. The officer was an officer. She had never met him. She would never meet him. They existed in separate worlds, connected only by the thin thread of encrypted radio waves.

But the thread was about to go silent. The Archer would leave port and vanish into the Atlantic, and Rose would not know, would never know, what happened next. That was the work. That was the war. She had no right to expect more than the position reports and the casualty lists that filled the out-tray. But she expected it anyway.

She picked up her headset and waited for the next signal. The night stretched ahead, full of static and silence.

Chapter 16: Davies's Voice

The personal signal arrived on Christmas morning, routed through the station by mistake.

It was addressed to Lieutenant D. Davies, HMS Archer, care of Londonderry. A family telegram, the kind that traveled through official channels when the recipient was at sea. The operator at Londonderry had forwarded it to Shingle Cove in error.

Rose decoded it before realizing what it was.

DEAREST DAVID. STOP. THINKING OF YOU THIS CHRISTMAS. STOP. YOUR FATHER HAS MADE A ROAST. STOP. WE SAVE A PLACE FOR YOU AT TABLE. STOP. ALL OUR LOVE. STOP. MOTHER.

David. His name was David.

Rose sat with the decoded text in front of her. The words were ordinary. A mother writing to her son at Christmas. A roast that would be eaten without him. A place saved at a table three hundred miles away.

But the words hit her like a physical blow. David. He had a mother who worried about him. He had a father who made roasts. He had a place saved at a table.

She forwarded the signal to the correct address and logged the error. Then she went to the latrine and stood in a stall with her hands pressed against the cold wall until her breathing steadied.

His name was David.

Chapter 17: The Attack

January 3, 1945. The night watch.

Rose was decoding a routine position report from a tanker when the emergency frequency lit up. The tone was different, higher, cutting through the static like a blade.

She switched channels. The signal was faint, transmitted under duress.

ATTACK. ATTACK. MULTIPLE CONTACTS. 55.31 NORTH 15.20 WEST. U-BOAT WOLFPACK.

The call sign resolved to a convoy under escort. Rose's hands moved across the keyboard, pulling the encrypted groups from the tape, matching them to the key sheet.

The escort vessels were engaging. Depth charges. Gunfire. One merchant ship hit, sinking, requesting assistance.

And then a call sign she knew better than her own name:

ARCHER ENGAGING. ARCHER ENGAGING.

Rose stopped breathing. Her fingers paused above the keyboard.

The signals came faster now, a flood of overlapping transmissions. Each one described a worsening situation. Rose worked through them, her fingers cold, her vision narrowing to the strip of paper tape feeding through the machine. The Archer was in the middle of it, firing and taking fire, and there was nothing Rose could do but transcribe the facts as they arrived. She did not think about David. She did not think about the name. She thought only about the letters on the tape, the groups of numbers, the mechanical work of translation.

Chapter 18: Distress

At 02:11, the Archer's call sign appeared on the distress frequency.

ARCHER. STOP. ENGINES FAILED. STOP. TAKING ON WATER. STOP. POSITION 55.28 NORTH 15.18 WEST. STOP. NUMBER OF CASUALTIES UNKNOWN. STOP.

Rose transcribed the signal with a mechanical precision that felt disconnected from her body. Her hands knew what to do. Her mind was somewhere else, underwater, in a steel room filling from below.

She handed the form to Finch. Finch read it and reached for the telephone to Bryce's quarters.

The room was silent except for the crackle of the receiver.

Rose went back to her machine. More signals were coming. She decoded them one by one.

ARCHER. FLOODING IN ENGINE ROOM. STOP. PUMPS INOPERABLE. STOP.

ARCHER. FIRES ON FORWARD DECK. STOP. MAGAZINE FLOODED. STOP.

ARCHER. PREPARING TO ABANDON SHIP. STOP.

Rose kept decoding. Her face was dry. Her hands were steady. Inside her chest, something was collapsing, a structural failure she could not stop. She was a machine that turned noise into sense, but the machine was breaking. She did not pause. She could not. The next signal was already feeding through the receiver, demanding her attention with every pulse of light on the dial and every crackle in the headphones.

Chapter 19: All Hands

The final signal from the Archer arrived at 03:04.

ARCHER TO ALL STATIONS. STOP. ABANDONING SHIP. STOP. GOD SAVE THE KING. STOP.

Rose read the words on the paper tape. She transcribed them onto the form. She placed the form in the out-tray.

Then she sat still.

The machine beside her was silent. The other Wrens were watching. Finch stood by the telephone, the receiver pressed to her ear, her face unreadable.

"Any response?" Rose asked.

"Not yet," Finch said.

They waited. The minutes passed. The receiver crackled with empty static. No further signals came from the Archer.

At 04:30, the Admiralty sent a general signal:

ARCHER. SANK AT 03:17. STOP. SURVIVORS IN WATER. STOP. RESCUE VESSELS EN ROUTE. STOP.

Rose read the signal. Then she stood up, walked past Finch, past Alice, out of the decoding room, down the corridor, and out the rear door into the cold January dark. The air hit her face like a slap. Her legs carried her without permission.

Behind the coal shed, she bent over and vomited. Her body emptied itself onto the frozen ground while the wind pulled at her hair. She stayed bent over, hands on her knees, waiting for her stomach to settle.

Chapter 20: Grief

Alice found her behind the coal shed, leaning against the wall with her eyes closed.

"Rose."

"I'm fine."

"You're not. Come inside."

"In a minute."

Alice stood beside her. The wind came off the sea, sharp and cold. The stars were out. A million of them, cold and indifferent.

"Did you know him?" Alice asked.

"No."

"But you feel like you did."

Rose opened her eyes. "I decoded his name. I decoded his mother's telegram. I knew his first name. I knew he was brave. I knew he was wounded. I knew he was going back to sea."

"That's not knowing him."

"I know."

Alice was quiet for a long moment. Then she said, "It's still grief. You don't have to have known someone to grieve them."

Rose turned to look at her. Alice's face was pale in the starlight.

"I think I was in love with him," Rose said. "Does that sound insane?"

"It sounds like you spent weeks thinking about him. That's enough to make a feeling."

"I invented him."

"We all invent." Alice took her arm. "Come inside. You'll freeze."

Rose let herself be led back inside. The decoding room was warm. The machines were running again. She sat down at her station and picked up her headset.

But she did not hear the signals anymore. The noise was just noise now, hollow and empty of meaning. She sat at her station and moved her hands across the keys like an automaton.

Chapter 21: The Telegram

The official confirmation arrived at 09:00.

ADMIRALTY TO ALL STATIONS. STOP. HMS ARCHER. STOP. CONFIRMED SUNK WITH ALL HANDS. STOP. NO SURVIVORS LOCATED. STOP. NEXT OF KIN NOTIFIED. STOP.

Rose sat at her machine. The words did not surprise her. She had known since 03:17. She had felt it in her body before the signal confirmed it, a cold that had nothing to do with the January air.

All hands. David Davies. Mother, father, saved place at the table. The words went into Rose like a blade.

She logged the signal in the book. She filed the flimsy. She did not keep a copy.

The war continued. Other signals arrived, other ships, other names. Rose decoded them all. She did not pause at any of them.

Alice watched her. Finch watched her. They said nothing.

Rose stopped reading the names. She decoded the letters. She transcribed the words. She did not think about the men behind them. It was the only way to keep working. It was not living. But it was working.

The machine did not care who Rose grieved. The machine only wanted the next message to flow through its circuits, clean and complete. The paper tape curled on the floor like a dead vine.

Chapter 22: Winter

January bled into February. The war news was good. The Allies were pushing into Germany. The Atlantic was quieter now, the wolfpacks broken, the convoys crossing with fewer losses.

But at Shingle Cove, the routine did not change. Signals still arrived. Rose still decoded them. She slept, ate, worked, slept again. The days blurred together.

Alice tried to draw her out. She talked about London, about the dances she would attend when the war was over, about the men she would kiss. Rose listened and nodded and said nothing about her own plans.

She had no plans. The war had swallowed her future. She did not know what she wanted anymore. The days were interchangeable, gray and cold.

Finch assigned her to the night watch permanently. Rose did not object. The night watch was quiet. Fewer signals, fewer names.

Finch assigned her to the night watch permanently. Rose did not object. The night watch was quiet. Fewer signals, fewer names.

She sat alone in the decoding room with the hiss of the receiver and the red glow of the stove. She drank tea. She decoded signals. She did not think about David Davies.

Or she tried not to.

Sometimes, when the receiver went silent, she heard his name in the static. She always shook it off.

But it always came back.

Chapter 23: Transfer Orders

Commander Bryce announced the news at morning briefing on February 12.

"We're getting a new signals officer. Lieutenant Davies. Transferring from Londonderry."

Rose felt the blood drain from her face. She gripped the edge of the table.

Alice looked at her sharply. Finch looked at nothing.

"He'll arrive next week," Bryce continued. "You will accord him the proper courtesies. He's had a difficult tour. Some of you may be aware that his previous ship was lost."

Rose was aware.

"The Archer," Bryce said. "He was the only surviving officer. Transferred off before the final voyage due to wounds. He's been in hospital and on light duty. Now he's fit for service. He'll be your commanding signals officer."

The only surviving officer. Transferred off.

Rose excused herself. She made it to the latrine before her knees gave out. She stood with her hands on the sink, staring at her reflection in the spotted mirror. David Davies was alive. He had never been on the Archer when it sank.

The telegrams had not told her that. The signals had carried facts, but not the whole truth.

She had grieved a man who was still breathing. She did not know whether to laugh or weep.

Chapter 24: Arrival

Lieutenant David Davies arrived on February 19.

Rose watched from the decoding room window as the staff car pulled through the gate. A man in naval uniform got out. He was taller than she had imagined. He walked with a slight limp, favoring his left leg.

He carried a single bag. He paused at the gate and looked at the blockhouse, at the aerials, at the sea beyond. Then he walked inside.

Rose did not go to meet him. She stayed at her station, her hands on the keys of the silent Type-X.

Alice came back from the mess hall five minutes later.

"He's real," Alice said.

"I know."

"You looked like you'd seen a ghost."

"I thought he was one."

Alice sat down beside her. "He's not. He's here. He's eating a sandwich in the mess. He has brown eyes and a crooked nose and he looks tired. He's real, Rose."

Rose nodded. She did not trust herself to speak.

Later, she crossed the corridor and stood outside the mess hall door. She could hear voices inside. One of them was new, lower than the others, with a Welsh lilt.

She turned around and walked back to the decoding room.

She was not ready to meet him.

Chapter 25: Shock

Rose avoided the mess hall for three days. She ate at her station, bread and cheese from her coat pocket, washing it down with cold tea.

Alice brought her updates. Lieutenant Davies had introduced himself to the Wrens. He had inspected the decoding equipment. He had asked about the shift rotation.

"He's a proper officer," Alice said. "Courteous. No airs."

Rose nodded and said nothing.

"I'm going to start charging for these reports," Alice said.

"You don't have to bring them."

"You're not curious?"

"I was curious. Now I'm just--" Rose stopped. She did not have a word for what she was.

"Terrified?"

"Something like that."

"He's a person, Rose. Not a ghost. Not a name on a paper."

"I know." Rose did not know if she believed it.

Alice sat down. "He's not going to bite you, Rose. He's a man. A man who survived something terrible. He doesn't know you've been tracking him through the war. You can start fresh."

"I know his mother sent him a Christmas telegram. He doesn't know I know that."

"You could tell him."

"I could not."

Alice laughed. "Then eat in the mess like a normal person and pretend you don't know anything."

Rose considered it. The idea was terrifying. It was also the only way forward.

"All right," she said. "Tomorrow."

"Attagirl."

Chapter 26: Confrontation

Rose walked into the mess hall at 07:00. Lieutenant Davies was sitting alone at the far table, a mug of tea in his hands, a newspaper folded beside him.

She got her tea from the urn. She stood at the counter for a long moment. Then she walked to his table.

"May I sit?"

He looked up. His eyes were brown, as Alice had said. They carried a weight that made Rose's chest tighten.

"Of course."

She sat. The bench felt too hard. The mug in her hands was too hot.

"I'm Rose Clayton. Wren decoder."

"David Davies." He extended his hand. She shook it. His grip was firm, his fingers warm.

"I know," she said. Then, quickly: "I mean, Commander Bryce told us you were coming. From Londonderry."

"Ah. Yes." He picked up his tea. "I was at Londonderry for a few months. Before that I was on a destroyer."

"Which one?"

He paused. "The Archer. She was sunk in January."

"I'm sorry."

"Thank you." He looked down at his tea. "I was lucky. I got off before the end. A lot of men didn't."

Rose said nothing. She did not trust her voice.

He smiled, a small tired smile. "You have a good station here. Quiet."

"It has its moments."

She drank her tea. He read his newspaper. The silence was not uncomfortable.

She had met him. He was real. The knowledge settled into her like a stone dropped in still water.

Chapter 27: The Story

Over the following days, Rose learned the story in fragments.

Davies had been transferred off the Archer on December 26, the day the ship sailed from Londonderry. The wound from the November attack had not healed properly. The medical board had ordered him to stay ashore for another month.

He had watched the Archer leave without him.

"It felt like a failure," he told Rose one evening in the decoding room. They were alone on the night watch, the machines silent. "I should have been on that ship."

"You would have died."

"Maybe. But I would have been with my crew."

Rose considered this. "Is that what you wanted? To die with them?"

Davies was quiet for a long moment. "I wanted to not be the one who lived."

"I understand."

He looked at her. "Do you?"

She thought about the signal flimsies in her pocket, the ones she had never burned. She thought about the night she had vomited behind the coal shed.

"I understand more than you know," she said. The confession hung between them like a live wire.

He did not ask what she meant. But he looked at her differently after that, with something like recognition.

Chapter 28: Fragments

The next night, Rose brought the clippings to the decoding room. She spread them on the desk between them. The medal citation. The casualty list. The Christmas telegram transcript she had reconstructed from memory.

Davies stared at them.

"Where did you get these?"

"The waste bin. The signal log. My memory."

"You kept them."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Rose had practiced this answer. She had rehearsed it in her bunk, in the latrine, on the walk to the blockhouse. But sitting across from him, the practice deserted her.

"Because I thought you were dead," she said. "And I had never met you. But I couldn't stop thinking about you."

Davies did not speak. He picked up the medal citation and read it. His jaw tightened.

"This isn't me," he said.

"It's your name."

"It's a piece of paper. It's not me."

"I know that now."

He looked at her. His eyes were wet. "I don't know what to say."

"You don't have to say anything." Rose gathered the papers and folded them. "I just wanted you to know that someone thought about you. While you were out there."

Davies reached across the desk. His hand covered hers.

"Thank you," he said. The words were simple. They carried everything two people could carry between them.

Chapter 29: Tension

Commander Bryce noticed.

He called Rose into his office on March 1. The photograph was still face-down on his desk.

"You've been spending time with Lieutenant Davies."

"On the night watch, sir. We're the only ones awake."

"Don't be clever with me, Wren Clayton. I know what I see."

Rose said nothing. Bryce stood and walked to the porthole. The gray paint blocked any view, but he stared at it as if he could see through.

"I lost my son at sea," he said. "He was a signalman. Died on a destroyer in '42. I received the telegram here. I decoded it myself."

Rose felt the air leave the room.

"You think you understand what it means to care about a man in uniform. You don't." Bryce turned to face her. "You're young. You think love is a choice. It's not. It's a trap. And war is full of traps."

"I understand, sir."

"You understand nothing. But you will." He sat down. The chair groaned under his weight. "I'm reassigning you to the day watch. You will not work night shifts with Lieutenant Davies. That's an order."

"Yes, sir."

Rose left the office. Her legs were shaking.

She understood nothing. But she was learning.

Chapter 30: Night Shift

The reassignment did not work.

Rose and Davies found ways to be near each other. A shared breakfast in the mess. A walk along the seawall during her break. A book passed from hand to hand, returned with notes in the margins.

Alice watched it all with amusement. "You're not as subtle as you think."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"The way you look at him. The way he looks at you. It's not subtle."

Rose flushed. "There's nothing--"

"There's everything. And Commander Bryce is going to have an apoplexy when he finds out you're meeting him on the seawall."

"He won't find out."

"He's a commander in the Royal Navy. He finds out everything."

Rose knew Alice was right. But she could not stop.

One evening, Davies met her at the seawall as the sun set over the water. The sky was orange and gray. The wind was cold. He stood close enough that she could feel the warmth of his arm through his coat.

"I've been thinking," he said.

"About what?"

"About what happens after."

Rose looked at him. "After the war?"

"After everything."

She did not ask him to explain. She understood.

They stood together in the fading light, not touching, not speaking. The sea moved below them. The war moved around them.

For a moment, they were still.

Chapter 31: Cardiff

Davies talked about Cardiff on the nights when the signals were slow.

He had grown up in a terraced house on Sloper Road, two streets from the rugby ground. His father worked on the docks, loading and unloading cargo ships. His mother was a nurse at the Royal Infirmary.

"I used to think I'd work the docks too," he said. "But my mother wanted something else for me."

"She was right."

"Was she? The war made me an officer. That's not the same as choosing."

Rose listened. She liked the shape of his stories. The details were concrete, unglamorous. A leaky roof. A cat that brought dead mice to the doorstep. A Christmas when the only gift was an orange.

"You're lucky," she said.

"Lucky?"

"You know where you come from. You can go back."

Davies considered this. "Where do you come from?"

Rose looked out at the dark water. "A village in Somerset. My father is a vicar. My mother is his wife. I was a schoolteacher. I don't want to go back."

"Why not?"

"Because I left. And I don't think you can go back to a place you've outgrown."

Davies nodded. He did not argue.

He understood.

Chapter 32: The Scar

Rose saw the scar for the first time on a rainy afternoon in March. Davies had taken off his tunic in the signals office, the room too warm from the stove. His shirt was open at the collar.

The scar ran from his ribs to his hip, a raised line of puckered tissue, still pink at the edges.

"That's from the Archer," Rose said.

He looked down at his torso as if surprised to find the scar visible. "Yes."

"Does it still hurt?"

"Sometimes. When it's cold. When it rains."

"It's raining."

"It always rains." He pulled his shirt closed. "It's not the worst of it."

"What is?"

He was quiet for a moment. "The worst is knowing I should have been there at the end. My men died. I lived. The scar reminds me every day."

Rose stepped closer. She did not touch him.

"You're not wrong to be alive," she said.

"Tell that to the families."

"They got the telegrams. I know. I decoded them."

Davies looked at her. The recognition passed between them, silent and complete. She was not a stranger to him, and he was not a stranger to her. The signals had already done that work.

They stood in the warm room with the rain on the windows and did not speak.

Chapter 33: Proximity

Rose began volunteering for every extra shift she could take. Not to decode more signals. To be near the signals office, where Davies worked.

Alice confronted her in the bunkroom.

"You're being obvious."

"I don't know what you mean."

"You spend every spare moment hovering near his office. You bring him tea twice a day. You found out he likes ginger biscuits and now you keep a tin in your coat."

Rose opened her mouth to deny it, then closed it.

"Fine," she said. "I'm obvious."

"Everyone knows. Even Finch knows. Finch knows everything."

"Has she said anything?"

"She doesn't have to. She looks at you the way she looks at a signal that's about to go bad."

Rose sat on her bunk. The mattress sagged under her weight.

"What am I supposed to do?"

"Officially? Stop." Alice sat beside her. "Unofficially? Don't stop. But be careful. Bryce will send him away if he thinks it's affecting the work."

"It's not affecting the work."

"It's affecting you. And Bryce doesn't care about the difference."

Rose lay back on the bunk and stared at the ceiling. The pipes ticked overhead.

"I can't stop," she said.

"Then don't. But be ready for what comes."

Rose was not ready. But she did not know that yet.

Chapter 34: The Raid

The Luftwaffe reconnaissance plane appeared on March 14, at 14:00 hours.

The air raid siren went off before anyone saw it. Rose was in the decoding room, hands on the Type-X, when the wall shook and the lights flickered.

"Everyone to the shelter. Now."

Finch's voice cut through the noise. The Wrens moved. Rose hesitated.

"Clayton. Now."

"Davies is in the signals office."

"Davies can take care of himself. Move."

Rose ran.

The shelter was a concrete bunker behind the blockhouse. She huddled with the other Wrens, counting the seconds between the drone of the engine and the sound of explosions.

The bombs fell wide. They were not the target. The plane was gathering photographs, not destruction.

But in the shelter, Rose could not stop thinking about Davies. She imagined him in the signals office, exposed, the windows offering no protection.

She did not know she was crying until Alice took her hand.

"He's fine," Alice said. "He's fine."

The all-clear sounded ten minutes later. Rose ran to the blockhouse. Davies was standing in the corridor, a cut on his cheek from a piece of flying glass, alive.

He saw her face and smiled.

"Told you. Hard to kill."

Rose did not smile back. She turned and walked away before she did something she could not take back.

Chapter 35: Aftermath

That night, Rose admitted it to herself.

She loved him.

It was not the invented love she had felt for the name on the signal flimsy. That had been a story she told herself in the dark. This was different. This was real. It had weight and texture and the smell of his coat when he stood close.

She loved him, and the war was not over.

She lay in her bunk and stared at the ceiling. The pipes ticked. Somewhere in the blockhouse, Davies was probably still awake, reading or writing letters or staring at his own ceiling.

She could not tell him. Not now. Not while the war could still take him.

But she also could not pretend.

The next morning, she brought him tea and a ginger biscuit. He thanked her. Their fingers touched when he took the cup. She felt it all the way through her body, a current that would not be denied.

She was in love with a man whose death she had already grieved. There was no way to explain that to anyone. She did not try. Some truths could only be carried, not spoken aloud. They lived in the body and nowhere else.

Chapter 36: The Archive

Rose went to the archive room on March 20. She told herself she was looking for a signal form from the previous week, something she had misfiled.

She was lying.

The archive was a narrow room in the basement, lined with steel shelves holding bound volumes of signal logs. Rose ran her finger along the spines until she found the Archer's logs from November.

She pulled the volume and opened it on the table.

The pages were covered in handwritten transcriptions. Rose scanned them until she found what she was looking for.

LIEUTENANT D. DAVIES. WOUNDED. TRANSFERRED TO HOSPITAL.

Then the December logs.

LIEUTENANT D. DAVIES. MEDICAL BOARD. DECLARED UNFIT FOR SEA DUTY.

She read the entry three times. Unfit for sea duty. That was why he had survived. A medical board had looked at his wound and decided he was not healthy enough to die.

The Archer had sailed without him.

The Admiralty's telegram had said all hands lost. But the logbooks told a different story. Davies had been written out of the Archer's fate before the Archer met its fate.

Rose closed the volume and returned it to the shelf.

The telegrams carried facts. The truth was messier.

Chapter 37: Davies Finds Her

Davies found her in the archive room, her hands on the steel shelf, the logbooks arranged around her.

"What are you doing?"

Rose turned. His face was unreadable.

"I was looking for something."

"It looks like you were looking for me."

She did not deny it.

Davies stepped into the room. The space was too small for both of them. He stood close.

"You've been keeping track of me since November," he said. "Since before we met."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Rose took a breath. "Because I decoded the signal about the Archer being hit. I decoded the casualty list. I decoded your mother's Christmas telegram. I decoded the distress signal. I decoded the confirmation that all hands were lost."

She paused.

"I thought you were dead, David. I didn't know you. I had never seen your face. But I grieved you. And then you arrived, alive, and I had to grieve the grief itself."

Davies was silent. His hand came up and touched her face.

"You were the first person to mourn me," he said. "I don't know how to feel about that."

"Neither do I."

He kissed her. It was gentle, a question more than an answer.

In the archive room, surrounded by logs of the dead, Rose kissed him back.

Chapter 38: Understanding

Afterward, they sat on the cold floor of the archive room, their backs against the shelves.

"The telegrams only carry facts," Davies said. "They don't carry truth."

"What's the difference?"

"A fact is that the Archer sank. The truth is that I am here. They contradict each other. Both are real."

Rose leaned her head against his shoulder. "I spent weeks thinking about you. I built a whole person out of fragments. You weren't that person."

"Was I worse?"

"Different. Better. Real." She looked at him. "The man I invented was a hero. The man I met is a man."

"Is that disappointing?"

"It's the opposite."

Davies took her hand. His fingers were calloused. Warm. Alive.

"You're the first person to see me as a man instead of a survivor," he said. "Thank you."

"You're welcome."

They sat in the quiet archive while the war continued above them, the muffled thud of boots and the distant drone of aircraft passing overhead. It was the most honest conversation Rose had ever had. No code. No encryption. Just two people speaking plainly about what they had lost and what they had found in the wreckage. The words did not need to be transcribed or logged.

Chapter 39: Christmas Eve

The station's Christmas Eve party was held in the mess hall. A gramophone played records borrowed from a nearby garrison. Someone had found a bottle of sherry. The lights were dimmed to a single bulb.

Rose wore her best uniform, which was also her only uniform. Alice had found a red ribbon and tied it in her hair.

"You look festive," Alice said.

"I look ridiculous."

"You look like a person who might get kissed tonight."

Rose's face went hot. She did not answer.

Davies arrived late, his hair damp from the rain. He had changed into a clean shirt. He crossed the mess and stood beside her.

"You have a ribbon in your hair."

"Alice put it there."

"It suits you."

Rose could not think of a reply. She held her sherry glass and did not drink from it.

At midnight, someone turned the gramophone down. The room grew quiet.

Davies looked at her.

"Walk with me?"

She nodded.

They stepped outside. The rain had stopped. The sky was clearing, stars appearing between the clouds.

"Happy Christmas," Davies said.

"Happy Christmas."

He reached into his pocket and handed her a small package. Wrapped in brown paper, tied with string.

Rose opened it. It was a book of poetry -- Wilfred Owen. The pages were soft from use.

"I thought you should have it," he said. "Because you understand what the words mean."

Rose held the book. She could not speak.

She reached into her coat and pulled out the scarf she had knitted, rough at the edges, imperfect.

"Your mother would have done a better job."

Davies laughed. "I doubt it. Thank you."

He put the scarf on. It was too long. He wore it anyway.

Chapter 40: The Kiss

After midnight, behind the blackout curtains of the mess hall doorway, Davies kissed her.

It was not the careful kiss of the archive room. It was fuller, hungrier, the kiss of a man who had been at war for four years and was tired of waiting.

Rose's back pressed against the wall. Her hands found his shoulders. The blackout curtains smelled of dust and wool.

"We shouldn't," she said, against his mouth.

"I know."

He kissed her again.

Outside, the sea was dark. The war was still happening. But behind the curtains, there was no war. There was only David's hands on her waist, his breath in her hair, the solid weight of him alive against her. She had never felt anything more real.

When they broke apart, his forehead rested against hers.

"I love you," he said.

The words hung in the air between them, delicate and heavy at once, like a signal that could not be unsent.

Rose closed her eyes. "I know."

It was not the answer he deserved. But it was the only one she had. She loved him too. She was terrified of what that meant for both of them, for the fragile future they had begun to imagine in stolen moments.

Chapter 41: Bryce Warns Again

Commander Bryce summoned Rose to his office on December 27. The photograph was still face-down. It was always face-down.

"I have reports that you and Lieutenant Davies were seen together after midnight on Christmas Eve."

Rose said nothing.

"Is it true?"

"Yes, sir."

Bryce sat back in his chair. The leather creaked. He looked older than he had in November, the shadows under his eyes deeper.

"I told you once that distraction sinks ships. I meant it."

"I am not distracted, sir. My work has not suffered."

"Your work is not the only thing at risk."

Rose understood. Davies could be reassigned. He could be sent back to sea. The navy did not tolerate fraternization between officers and ratings, even Wren ratings.

"Sir--"

"I don't want to hear it. I want you to end it. Quietly. Completely."

Rose stood in the silence of the office. The ticking clock. The painted porthole. The face-down photograph.

"Yes, sir."

She left the office. The door clicked shut behind her. The corridor was empty and dark. She did not cry until she reached her bunk, the pillow muffling the sound.

But she knew, even as the tears came, that she would not obey. She could not give him up.

Chapter 42: Secrecy

They began meeting in secret. Not the mess hall, not the decoding room, not the seawall. They met in the archive room, in the coal shed, in the narrow passage behind the boiler.

Rose kept a schedule in her head. Tuesday at 14:00. Thursday after sunset. Sunday at dawn, when the watch changed and no one noticed two people walking in opposite directions.

The secrecy made everything sharper. Every glance across the decoding room carried meaning. Every brush of fingers passing a mug of tea was a message.

Alice knew. Finch suspected. Bryce watched from his office window.

Rose did not care. The risk was worth the reward.

In the dark behind the boiler, with Davies's arms around her, the war was small. The station was small. There was only David, his heartbeat under her ear, his breath moving through her hair. She memorized the rhythm of each sound and held it like a talisman against what might come.

"The war will end," he said one night. "And then what?"

"Then we decide."

"Decide what?"

"Everything."

He kissed her forehead. "I want to decide it with you."

Rose closed her eyes and did not think about what might happen between now and the end.

Chapter 43: The Last Convoy

On April 2, 1945, the news arrived. The war in Europe was ending. The last Atlantic convoy was being assembled, a final crossing to bring supplies to the advancing armies.

The station buzzed with the news. Wrens talked in excited whispers. Even Finch looked lighter.

Rose should have felt relief. Instead, she felt dread.

The last convoy meant the last mission. The last deployment. The last chance for something to go wrong.

She found Davies in the signals office, reading the convoy orders.

"You're not going," she said.

He did not answer.

"David. You're not going."

He looked up. His eyes were tired.

"I've requested a transfer as liaison officer."

Rose felt the floor drop beneath her.

"Why?"

"Because I can't spend the rest of the war in a signals office. I need to finish what I started."

"You started it on the Archer. You finished it when you survived."

"That's not how it feels."

Rose crossed the room. She took his face in her hands.

"You are not required to die to prove you deserved to live."

Davies closed his eyes. "I know."

"Then don't go."

"I have to."

He said it gently. It made it worse, the kindness in his voice when he was about to leave her.

Chapter 44: Davies's Request

Bryce approved the transfer the next day.

Rose heard the news from Alice, who heard it from the administrative clerk. Davies was to join the convoy as signals liaison officer. Departure: April 10. Duration: approximately three weeks.

Rose did not go to the signals office. She did not go to the mess. She went to the coal shed and sat on a pile of sacks and stared at the wall until her vision blurred.

Alice found her an hour later.

"He's an idiot," Alice said.

"I know."

"But you love him anyway."

"I know that too."

Alice sat beside her. The coal dust got on their uniforms. Neither of them brushed it off. The dirt did not matter now.

"What do I do?" Rose asked.

"You let him go. And then you wait."

"And if he doesn't come back?"

Alice took her hand. "Then you grieve him again. And you survive. Again." Alice's voice was flat, not unkind. She had done this before too.

Rose closed her eyes. She had already grieved David Davies once. She was not sure she could do it twice.

But the war did not ask what she could survive. It only asked what she could endure.

Chapter 45: Anger

Rose confronted Davies in the signals office at 22:00, the station quiet, the night watch at their posts.

"You didn't tell me."

"I was going to."

"When? After you'd already gone?"

Davies set down his pen. "I knew you'd try to stop me."

"Damned right I would."

"Rose--"

"Don't. Don't say my name like that." Her voice was shaking. "I decoded the signal that said you were dead. I vomited behind the coal shed because I thought you had drowned. And now you're volunteering to go back."

"I have to."

"You don't have to do anything. You want to. There's a difference."

Davies stood. He looked at her across the desk.

"I can't be the man who stayed safe while others sailed. That's not who I am."

"Then be someone else. Be alive. Be here." Her voice broke on the last word.

"I can't."

Rose stared at him. The tears came. She did not try to stop them.

"I can't watch you leave," she said.

"You won't have to."

He was wrong. She watched him leave the next morning from the decoding room window. She watched everything. Every detail burned itself into her memory: the way the morning light caught the windscreen, the tilt of his head as he spoke to the driver, the fade of the car's tail lights into the coastal mist.

Chapter 46: Departure

Davies departed on April 10, 1945.

Rose stood at the decoding room window and watched the staff car pull away. Davies sat in the back. He did not look back.

Alice stood beside her.

"You could have said goodbye properly." Alice's voice was gentle, not scolding.

"I don't know how to say goodbye properly to someone I might not see again."

"You could have tried."

Rose said nothing. The staff car reached the gate. It paused at the turn, then disappeared behind the hill. The dust settled. The gate was empty.

She turned away from the window. The glass was cold. The sky was empty.

"Back to work."

She sat at her Type-X and fed in the tape. The signal was a routine position report. She decoded it without thinking. Her hands knew the work. Her heart was on a convoy in the North Atlantic. There was nothing she could do about either.

She worked the rest of her shift in silence, not speaking, not stopping. The machine did its job, and so did she. There was nothing else to do but wait for the signals that would tell her if he was still alive somewhere out on the gray water.

Chapter 47: Waiting

The first five days were the worst.

Rose worked her shifts. She decoded signals. She ate when Alice put food in front of her. She slept when exhaustion took her.

The convoy reported regularly. Routine signals. No contact. Weather moderate.

Rose read every transmission with a hunger that she could not hide. Her eyes scanned each line for the convoy's call sign before she let herself breathe. Finch did not comment. Alice did not tease.

On the sixth day, a storm hit the North Atlantic. The convoy signals became erratic. Rose sat at her machine for eight hours, waiting for the next position report. Every minute of silence felt like a confirmation of disaster.

It came at 03:00, a weak signal fighting through static. All vessels accounted for. Heavy seas but no damage.

Rose set down her headset. Her hands were shaking.

Finch appeared beside her with a mug of tea.

"This is the worst part," Finch said. "The waiting."

"Yes."

"But it's better than the alternative."

Rose knew what alternative she meant. The alternative was the telegram she had decoded for Finch's brother.

She drank the tea. It was hot and bitter.

She stayed at her post. There was nowhere else to go. The war did not permit running from it, and neither did the work she had chosen.

Chapter 48: The Signal

The telegram arrived on April 18, at 10:32.

Rose was not on duty. She was in the mess, trying to eat a piece of toast, when Alice burst through the door.

"Rose. Come. Now."

Rose ran.

The decoding room was quiet. Finch stood at the machine, a strip of paper tape in her hand.

"It's from the convoy," Finch said. "You need to decode it."

"Why me?"

"Because it's about Davies." Finch's voice was level, but something in her eyes softened. She did not add anything else. She simply held out the tape.

Rose took the tape. Her hands were steady. She fed it through the Type-X and worked the keys.

The signal came through clean.

CONVOY. STOP. MINOR DAMAGE FROM STORM. STOP. NO CASUALTIES. STOP. ALL PERSONNEL SAFE. STOP. DAVIES CONFIRMED ALIVE. STOP.

Rose read the decoded text. She read it again.

Then she sat down and cried.

Alice put a hand on her shoulder. Finch turned away to give them privacy.

The war was almost over. David was alive. The telegrams had brought her good news for once. She did not know how to receive it. Good news was a foreign language she had never learned to speak.

Chapter 49: Return

Davies returned on April 28.

Rose was on the seawall when the staff car pulled through the gate. She watched it stop in front of the blockhouse. The door opened.

Davies got out. He was thinner. His uniform was wrinkled. He had a bandage on his right hand.

He looked up and saw her on the seawall. He walked toward her.

It started to rain.

They stood facing each other. The rain soaked through their uniforms.

"You're alive," Rose said.

"I told you. Hard to kill."

"You look terrible."

"I feel terrible." He smiled. "But I feel alive."

Rose stepped forward. She put her arms around him. He held her. The rain fell. The sea moved below them.

"I was so angry at you," she said into his shoulder.

"I know."

"I was so afraid."

"I know that too."

She pulled back and looked at his face. The scar on his cheek had healed. His eyes were tired but clear.

"Don't ever do that again," she said.

"I won't. The war's almost over. I'm done." He said it like a man convincing himself as much as her.

"Promise?"

"I promise."

They stood in the rain, holding each other, and Rose believed him. The war had not killed him. She had to learn to trust that.

Chapter 50: The Last Telegram

On May 7, 1945, the station received its final operational signal.

Rose was on watch. The machine clattered. The tape fed through.

She decoded the message slowly, deliberately, the way she had decoded hundreds before it.

TO ALL SHIPS AND STATIONS. STOP. HOSTILITIES WILL CEASE AT 00:01 HOURS ON MAY 8. STOP. GERMAN HIGH COMMAND HAS SURRENDERED. STOP. GOD SAVE THE KING. STOP.

Rose read the words. The war was over.

She transcribed the signal onto the form. She placed it in the out-tray. Then she sat back and looked at the Type-X, the machine that had carried the voices of the dead and the living for four years.

She thought about David, asleep in his bunk, the war finally finished for him too.

She thought about the Archer, at the bottom of the Atlantic, the men she had never met whose names she had decoded.

She thought about the last telegram, the one she would never have to decode again. The one that said the war was over.

She took off her headset. She walked out of the decoding room.

Davies was waiting in the corridor, still in his shirt sleeves, his hair disheveled from sleep.

"Is it true?"

"It's true."

He took her hand. "What do we do now?"

Rose looked past him, through the window at the end of the corridor. The sky was lightening. The sea was calm. The war was finished.

"We find out," she said.

They walked out of the blockhouse together. The spring air was cool and clean. The aerials were silent. The war machines had nothing more to say.

Rose did not look back.

Behind her, in the decoding room, the last telegram sat in the out-tray. The words were final. They were also a beginning.

She had spent the war reading messages from the dead. Now she would learn to speak to the living.

She held David's hand and walked toward the gate.

The war was over.

The rest of their lives waited on the other side.
