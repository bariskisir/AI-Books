Chapter 1: The Fixer

The coffee was black and bitter and tasted like the inside of a used ashtray. Remy Deschamps drank it anyway. The Café de l'Oasis was quiet for a Tuesday morning. A fan turned slowly overhead, stirring the smoke. Outside, the heat of Casablanca pressed against the windows like a hand.

The man who sat down across from him wore a suit that cost more than Remy made in a month. French, probably. A merchant. He placed a thick envelope on the table between them. He did not open it.

"My name is Moreau," the man said. "Lucien Moreau. I need you to find someone."

Remy did not touch the envelope. "Who told you about me?"

"A man at the port. He said you can find anything."

"Not anything. Just things that want to be found."

Moreau pushed the envelope closer. "Her name is Leila Bensoussan. She is my wife's sister. She has been missing for six days."

Remy looked at the envelope. The seal was broken. He could see the edge of a banknote. Enough to live on for a month. He thought about his rent, about the bottle of whiskey he had finished the night before, about the photograph of a woman he used to love that he kept in his coat pocket.

"Six days is a long time," Remy said.

"The police will not help. They say she is a runaway."

"Is she?"

Moreau's jaw tightened. "No. She is not a runaway. She is in trouble."

"What kind of trouble?"

"I do not know. That is why I came to you."

Remy picked up the envelope. He weighed it in his palm. Then he opened it and counted the money. Enough for two months. He took a cigarette from his pocket and lit it.

"I will find her," he said. "But I do not make promises. I only find what is there."

Moreau nodded. He stood up and left without another word.

Remy sat alone and watched the smoke curl toward the ceiling. He had a bad feeling about this one. He always had a bad feeling. But the rent was due.

Chapter 2: The Client

Lucien Moreau's office was above a wine shop in the European quarter. The stairs were dark. The air smelled of cork and dust. Remy climbed slowly, his hand on the rail.

Moreau sat behind a heavy wooden desk. A photograph stood in a silver frame: a woman with dark hair and a careful smile. His wife. Not Leila.

"Tell me about her," Remy said. He sat down without being invited.

Moreau folded his hands. "Leila is twenty-six. She works as a teacher. She lives alone in a room near the medina. She has no husband, no debts, no enemies."

"No one has no enemies."

"She is a quiet woman. She goes to work. She comes home. She reads."

"Then why is she missing?"

Moreau hesitated. The hesitation told Remy more than words could.

"There was a man," Moreau said. "A Frenchman. She was seeing him. I did not approve."

"His name."

"I do not know it. She would not tell my wife."

"But you know something."

Moreau opened a drawer. He took out a photograph and slid it across the desk. A man in his forties. Military bearing. A scar above his left eyebrow.

"I had him followed," Moreau said. "He works at the prefecture."

Remy picked up the photograph. The man's face was familiar. He had seen him in the medina, in a car that did not belong there. A grey Citroen with government plates.

"Did you give this to the police?"

"I told Inspector Roux. He said he would look into it."

"And did he?"

Moreau's face went pale. "He told me to drop the matter. He said Leila was a woman of loose morals and I should not waste his time."

Remy studied the photograph. The man had cold eyes. The kind of eyes that belonged to someone who was used to getting answers.

"You hired the wrong man," Remy said. "I do not find missing women. I find lost luggage. I find debts. I am a fixer, not a detective."

"You found the photograph. That is more than the police did."

Remy stood up. He put the photograph in his pocket.

"I will look into it," he said. "But I am not promising anything."

Chapter 3: The Mother

Simone Bensoussan lived in a narrow building near the old port. The stairs smelled of garlic and cooking oil. A radio played somewhere, tinny and distant.

She opened the door before he knocked. A small woman in a black dress. Her hair was grey. Her eyes were the same color as Leila's. The same shape. The same sadness.

"You are the man," she said. "Lucien sent you."

"Yes."

She stepped aside. The apartment was cramped. A sofa, a table, a crucifix on the wall. A photograph of a man in a military uniform. Leila's father.

"She is not dead," Simone said. "I would know if she was dead."

"I believe you."

"She is a good girl. She writes every week. Every week, a letter. Then nothing."

Remy sat down. "When did the letters stop?"

"Two weeks ago. The last one said she was afraid. She said she had done something dangerous."

"What did she do?"

Simone looked at her hands. "She did not tell me. But she asked for her father's papers. The ones he kept from before."

"Before what?"

"Before he died."

Remy waited. Simone stared at the wall.

"Your husband," he said. "How did he die?"

"He was arrested. The police said he was a subversive. They kept him for three months. Then they told me he had a heart attack."

"Did you believe them?"

"No."

She stood up and walked to a small cabinet. She opened it and took out a bundle wrapped in cloth. She handed it to Remy.

"These are the papers she wanted. I do not know what they mean. But she risked her life for them."

Remy unwrapped the cloth. Inside were old letters, a photograph, and a page torn from a ledger. Names and dates. Something about a shipment.

"May I keep these?"

Simone nodded. "Find her. Please. She is all I have."

Remy wrapped the papers and put them in his coat. He did not tell her what he was thinking. That the last person who touched these papers was dead.

Chapter 4: The Last Sighting

The medina was a maze of narrow alleys and hidden courtyards. Remy walked through the morning crowd. Merchants called out their prices. Children ran between the legs of donkeys. The smell of spices hung in the air like a curtain.

The shopkeeper was an old man with one eye and a wooden leg. He sold bread and olives and cigarettes from a stall near the mosque. He remembered Leila.

"Pretty girl. Dark hair. She came every Tuesday."

"Was she alone?"

"Usually. But that day, there was a man."

"Describe him."

"French. Grey suit. He stood across the street and watched her."

Remy took out the photograph from Moreau's office. The man with the scar. "This man?"

The shopkeeper squinted. "Maybe. Hard to say. They all look the same to me."

"Did she see him watching?"

"She was nervous. She kept looking over her shoulder. She bought her bread and left quickly."

"Which way did she go?"

The shopkeeper pointed toward the southern gate. "Toward the European quarter. She usually went home. That day, she went the other way."

Remy thanked him and walked in the direction the shopkeeper had pointed. The streets widened. The buildings became taller. French architecture. Balconies and shuttered windows.

He stopped at the southern gate. A road led toward the prefecture. Another led toward the cathedral. He thought about Leila walking this road alone, carrying her bread, her father's secret, and the knowledge that a man in a grey suit was following her.

He turned toward the prefecture. He had a name now, and a face. The man in the photograph. And he had Inspector Roux, who had told Moreau to drop the case.

Remy lit a cigarette. The game had begun.

Chapter 5: The Inspector

Inspector Marcel Roux was a large man with sad eyes and a patient voice. He sat behind his desk in the prefecture and looked at Remy the way a priest looks at a sinner who has come to confess.

"I know why you are here," Roux said.

"Then you know more than I do."

"I know you are working for Lucien Moreau. I know you are looking for the Bensoussan woman. And I know you should stop."

"Why?"

Roux leaned forward. The light from the window fell across his face. He looked tired.

"Because this case is not what it seems. The woman is not a victim. She is a fugitive."

"Fugitive from what?"

"I cannot tell you. But I am telling you as a professional courtesy: walk away."

Remy studied the inspector. The man's uniform was pressed. His office was clean. Everything about him said order and discipline. But his eyes said something else.

"You knew her father."

Roux went very still. "Yes."

"You knew he died in custody."

"I knew he died. I did not know the circumstances."

"But you suspected."

Roux did not answer. He stood up and walked to the window. The street below was busy. People going about their lives.

"Deschamps," Roux said quietly. "There are forces at work in this city that you cannot see. German intelligence. Vichy collaborators. And people like me, trying to keep the peace. The woman is a threat to that peace."

"Who is she a threat to?"

"Everyone. Including herself."

Remy stood up. He picked up his hat.

"Thank you for the warning, Inspector. But I was hired to find her. And I do not quit."

Roux turned from the window. His face was unreadable.

"Then God help you both."

Chapter 6: The Ledger

The shop was at the bottom of a stairwell that led nowhere. Youssef Benali sold things that did not exist. Fake passports, stolen jewelry, rumors purchased and resold at a markup. He was a small man with quick hands and a smile that never reached his eyes.

"The police were here," Youssef said. He did not look up from the ledger he was writing in.

"When?"

"Yesterday. They asked about the girl."

"What did you tell them?"

"That I did not know her. Which was true at the time."

"And now?"

Youssef closed the ledger. He looked at Remy with something like pity.

"She came to me three weeks ago. She needed a travel pass. A good one. She paid in gold."

"Did you make it?"

"I made it. It cost her everything she had."

"Where was she going?"

"Lisbon. Then London."

Remy sat down. The chair was broken. He balanced on the edge.

"Why Lisbon?"

"She did not say. But she had a contact there. A British name."

Youssef opened a drawer. He took out a slip of paper and handed it to Remy. An address in Lisbon. A name: Thompson.

"She was supposed to pick up a package. That is all I know."

"Did she make it to Lisbon?"

"No. The pass was never used. I checked with the port authority. She never boarded."

Remy folded the paper and put it in his pocket.

"Someone tipped them off," he said.

"Someone always does."

Remy stood up. He put a hand on Youssef's shoulder.

"Be careful. This is getting dangerous."

Youssef smiled. The smile still did not reach his eyes.

"When is it not?"

Chapter 7: The Room

Leila's room was above a bakery. The smell of bread drifted through the floorboards. Remy picked the lock in thirty seconds. The door swung open.

The room was small. A bed. A desk. A wardrobe. A photograph of a woman who might have been her mother. A crucifix. A book of poems by Rilke.

He searched methodically. The desk drawers held nothing but paper and pens. The wardrobe held clothes. A grey dress. A blue coat. Shoes worn at the heels.

He checked the mattress. Nothing. He checked the walls for loose boards. Nothing. He was about to leave when he noticed the floorboard near the window. It was slightly raised.

He pried it up with his knife. Underneath was a leather folder. Inside was a railroad timetable. Not for civilian trains. A military schedule. Transport routes through Morocco.

He unfolded it and studied the markings. Someone had circled a date and a location. A port south of Casablanca. The name of a ship.

And beneath the timetable, a charred fragment of a letter. The handwriting was German.

He held it up to the light. The words were burned at the edges. But he could read enough.

"...the shipment must proceed as planned. The woman knows too much. She must be contained."

Remy read it twice. He folded it carefully and put it in his pocket. He replaced the floorboard.

He stood in the center of the room and looked around. The sun was setting. The light through the window was orange and dusty. He thought about Leila living here, reading her Rilke, knowing that someone wanted her contained.

He locked the door behind him.

Chapter 8: The German

The Café de la République faced the prefecture across a wide boulevard. Remy sat at a table by the window. He drank coffee and watched.

At four o'clock, a black Citroen pulled up. The driver got out and opened the rear door. A man in a grey uniform stepped out. German. An officer.

Captain Dietrich.

He was tall. Blonde. His boots were polished. He walked with the easy confidence of a man who had never been told no.

He entered the prefecture. Remy checked his watch. Twenty minutes later, Dietrich emerged. He was carrying a folder. The folder had Leila's name on it.

Remy watched him get into the car. The Citroen pulled away. He memorized the license plate.

He finished his coffee. He thought about what he had seen. A German officer with a folder bearing Leila's name. A Vichy inspector who wanted the case closed. A dead father who had been a subversive.

The pieces were starting to form a picture. But the picture was not complete. He needed to know what was in that folder.

He paid for his coffee and stood up. The sun was lower now. The shadows were longer.

He walked toward the cathedral. He had another name to check.

Chapter 9: The Confession

Father Antoine was an old priest with white hair and a gentle voice. He met Remy in the sacristy of the Cathedral of the Sacred Heart. The room smelled of incense and old wood.

"I do not hear many confessions from fixers," the priest said.

"I am not here to confess."

"I know why you are here. You want to know about Leila Bensoussan."

Remy sat down. "She came to you."

"Yes. Three days before she disappeared."

"What did she say?"

The priest folded his hands. "She was frightened. She said she was carrying something that belonged to a dead man. She would not tell me what it was."

"Did she say who the dead man was?"

"She did not need to. I knew her father. I buried him."

Remy leaned forward. "What can you tell me about Antoine Bensoussan?"

"A good man. A historian. He was documenting the activities of the Vichy administration in Morocco. The arrests. The deportations. The shipments."

"Shipments of what?"

The priest's eyes were grave. "People, Monsieur Deschamps. He was documenting the transport of Jewish prisoners to labor camps."

Remy felt the weight of it settle on him. The timetable. The port. The German letter about the shipment.

"Leila has his documentation?"

"Yes. He gave it to her the night he was arrested. She has been carrying it ever since."

"Where is she now?"

The priest shook his head. "I do not know. But she told me she would try to reach the British. She said they were the only ones who could help."

Remy stood up. "Thank you, Father."

"Find her, Monsieur Deschamps. Before the others do."

Chapter 10: The Café

Marguerite Duvall owned the Café de l'Oasis. She was a large woman with a loud laugh and a memory like a steel trap. She saw everything. She forgot nothing.

"The man in the grey suit," Remy said. He was leaning against the bar. The café was empty. It was late.

"Vernet," Marguerite said. She wiped a glass with a rag. "His name is Georges Vernet. He works for the prefecture."

"What department?"

"Special Branch. He does the dirty work that Inspector Roux is too clean for."

"He was following Leila."

"Of course he was. She was a priority target."

"Since when?"

"Since her father died. Since she started asking questions he should have kept buried."

Remy lit a cigarette. "What do you know about the ledger?"

Marguerite stopped wiping the glass. She put it down.

"Enough to know you should not touch it."

"I am already touching it."

"Then you are a fool."

"Probably. But I am a fool who was paid to find a woman."

Marguerite studied him. Her eyes were hard.

"The ledger is Antoine Bensoussan's work. He was documenting the collaboration. Names. Dates. Shipments. The Germans want it. The Vichy want it. And the Resistance wants it."

"Which side are you on?"

She smiled. It was not a warm smile.

"I am on the side that pays. But if I had to choose, I would rather the ledger reach London than Berlin."

"Do you know where Leila is?"

"No. But if I did, I would not tell you. Not yet. I need to know whose side you are on first."

Remy put out his cigarette.

"I am on the side of the woman who paid me. That is all."

He walked out into the night. The streets were empty. The stars were hidden by clouds. Somewhere in the city, Leila was waiting. And the clock was ticking.

Chapter 11: The Tail

Vernet left the prefecture at six in the evening. He walked south through the European quarter. He did not look behind him.

Remy followed at a distance. He kept to the shadows. He watched Vernet cross the boulevard and turn into a narrow street near the post office.

Vernet stopped outside a tobacco shop. He lit a cigarette. He waited.

A man in a fez approached. They exchanged words Remy could not hear. Then Vernet handed him a leather satchel. The man took it and walked away.

Remy made a decision. He followed the man in the fez instead of Vernet.

The man walked quickly. He knew the streets. He turned corners. He checked behind him. Remy stayed low, stayed quiet.

The man entered a building near the old mosque. A sign above the door: Import-Export, Abdellah Benoit.

Remy memorized the address. He walked past without slowing. He would come back later, when the street was empty.

He was learning. Vernet was the visible threat. But the man in the fez was the connection. The pipeline from the prefecture to somewhere else.

He found a café across the street and ordered tea. He watched the door of the import-export office. The man did not come out.

An hour passed. The street grew dark. Remy finished his tea and left.

He had a name now: Abdellah Benoit. And a satchel full of documents that had come from the prefecture. He was getting closer.

Chapter 12: The Break-In

The prefecture was quiet at two in the morning. Remy circled the building twice. He found a window on the east side. Unlocked.

He climbed through. He was in a storage room. Boxes of files. Dust. The smell of old paper.

Vernet's office was on the second floor. Remy moved through the dark corridors. His footsteps were silent.

The door was locked. He picked it in fifteen seconds.

Inside, he searched the desk first. Drawers. Papers. A pistol. A bottle of cognac. No Leila.

He searched the filing cabinet. Alphabetical. He found Bensoussan.

The dossier was thick. Photographs of Leila. A list of names. Jewish intellectuals. Resistance sympathizers. Her father's name was at the top.

He read through it quickly. The dossier tracked Leila's movements for three months. Every meeting. Every letter. Every purchase.

And at the back, a report in German. Attached to it was a note from Captain Dietrich.

"The subject must be apprehended before she reaches the British. The materials she carries are of strategic importance. Use whatever means necessary."

Remy photographed the pages with a small camera he carried. He put the dossier back exactly as he found it.

He was about to leave when he heard footsteps in the corridor. He froze. The footsteps stopped outside the door.

A pause. Then they continued down the hall.

Remy waited until the silence returned. Then he slipped out the window and into the night.

He had what he needed. But he also had something he did not want: proof that the Germans were running the operation.

Chapter 13: The Confrontation

The alley was dark and narrow. Remy saw the movement too late.

Vernet stepped out of the shadows. A knife in his hand.

"You should have stayed out of it, Deschamps."

Remy backed up. His hand went to his coat. His pistol was there.

"Put the knife down, Vernet. We can talk."

"There is nothing to talk about. You broke into my office."

"I found your dossier."

Vernet's face tightened. He lunged.

Remy sidestepped. The knife caught his coat. He felt the blade cut through fabric and skin. Warm blood ran down his side.

He grabbed Vernet's wrist. They struggled. Vernet was strong. But Remy had been fighting longer.

He twisted. Vernet cried out. The knife clattered to the ground.

Remy punched him. Once. Twice. Vernet went down.

He stood over him, breathing hard. His side was bleeding.

"Tell me where she is."

Vernet laughed through broken teeth. "You will never find her. Dietrich will see to that."

"I already found Dietrich."

"Then you are a dead man."

Remy knelt down. He searched Vernet's pockets. Found a set of keys. A wallet. A folded piece of paper with an address.

"What is this?"

Vernet did not answer.

Remy stood up. He pressed his hand to his side. The blood was seeping through his fingers.

"If I die, I am taking you with me."

He left Vernet in the alley and staggered toward the street. He needed a doctor. He needed answers. He needed to get off the street before the blood drew attention.

Chapter 14: The Doctor

The basement was lit by a single bulb. The air smelled of iodine and old bandages. The medic was a woman with grey hair and steady hands. She did not ask his name.

"The knife went deep," she said. "But it missed the organs. You are lucky."

"I do not feel lucky."

She stitched the wound. Remy bit down on a leather strap. The pain was bright and sharp.

"Who sent you?"

"The priest."

She nodded. She finished the stitches and bandaged the wound.

"You will live. But you need rest."

"I do not have time for rest."

"Then you will die. It is that simple."

Remy sat up slowly. The room spun. He waited for it to settle.

"The man who died," he said. "Antoine Bensoussan. Did you know him?"

The medic stopped. She put down her scissors.

"Yes."

"How did he die?"

She looked at him. Her eyes were hard.

"They beat him for three months. He told them nothing. They beat him until his heart stopped."

"Who is they?"

"The same people who are after his daughter. The Germans. The Vichy. It does not matter which. They are the same."

Remy stood up. The wound pulled. He ignored it.

"Where is she?"

"I do not know. But if I did, I would tell you to stay away from her. She is marked. Anyone near her is marked too."

"I was marked the day I took the job."

He put on his coat. The blood had soaked through the bandage. But it was slowing.

"Thank you."

"Thank me by staying alive."

He climbed the stairs into the night. The address from Vernet's pocket was still in his hand. He had a place to go.

Chapter 15: The Archive

Simone Bensoussan opened the door. Her face was pale. She looked past Remy into the dark hallway.

"Are you alone?"

"Yes."

She let him in. The apartment was the same. The crucifix. The photograph. The smell of grief.

"I need to ask you about your husband."

Simone sat down. Her hands were folded in her lap.

"What about him?"

"His work. He was a historian."

"Yes."

"He was documenting the collaboration. The arrests. The deportations."

Simone nodded slowly.

"Did you know what he was doing?"

"I knew he was writing something. He did not tell me what. He said it was safer that way."

"But you kept his papers."

"I kept everything. I thought maybe someday someone would need them."

Remy sat down across from her. "Leila needed them. She came to you before she disappeared."

"Yes."

"Did she take anything?"

Simone stood up. She walked to the cabinet and opened it. Inside was a metal box.

"She took copies. She left the originals with me."

She opened the box. Inside were notebooks. Dozens of them. Filled with Antoine Bensoussan's handwriting.

Remy picked one up. He opened it. The pages were dense with dates and names. Shipment manifests. Letters. Photographs of documents.

"This is what they are after."

"Yes."

"Does anyone else know you have these?"

"No."

Remy closed the notebook. "Keep them safe. Do not tell anyone. Not the priest. Not Marguerite. No one."

Simone nodded. She locked the box and put it back in the cabinet.

"Find my daughter, Monsieur Deschamps."

"I will."

But as he walked back into the night, he was not sure he believed it.

Chapter 16: The German Request

The note arrived at the Café de l'Oasis at noon. A single sheet of paper. No signature. But the handwriting was precise, military.

"Come to the Hotel Continental at four o'clock. Bring nothing."

Remy read it twice. He showed it to Marguerite.

"Dietrich," she said.

"I know."

"You cannot go."

"I have to go. He knows I am involved. If I do not show, he will come for me."

"Then you are walking into a trap."

"Probably."

He arrived at the Hotel Continental at four. The lobby was all marble and polished brass. A German soldier met him at the door and escorted him to a private room.

Captain Dietrich was seated at a table. A bottle of wine was open. Two glasses.

"Please, sit."

Remy sat. He did not touch the wine.

"You know who I am," Dietrich said.

"I know."

"Then you know I am not a man who wastes time. I want the woman. I want the documents she carries. And I want your cooperation."

"My cooperation?"

Dietrich smiled. It was a thin smile.

"You are a fixer. You find things. I want you to find Leila Bensoussan and deliver her to me."

"And if I refuse?"

Dietrich's smile did not waver. "Then you become a problem. And I solve problems."

Remy looked at the wine. He thought about the dossier. The timetable. The knife wound.

"How much?"

"Five thousand francs. And your life."

Remy picked up the glass. He drank.

"I will find her. But I do not deliver people. I find them. That is all."

Dietrich nodded. "Acceptable. You have one week."

Remy stood up. He walked to the door. Then he turned.

"The shipment. The one scheduled for next month. What is on it?"

Dietrich's face did not change. But his eyes went cold.

"Find the woman, Deschamps. Do not ask questions you do not want the answers to."

Chapter 17: The Compromise

Remy found Youssef's body in the alley behind the shop. The shop itself was a blackened shell. Burned. The fire had taken everything.

He stood in the darkness and looked at what was left of his friend. Youssef's face was peaceful. They had killed him quickly. That was something.

He knelt down. He closed Youssef's eyes.

This was his fault. He had come to Youssef for help, and Youssef had died for it. The ledger. The travel pass. The questions.

He searched Youssef's pockets. Empty. They had taken everything.

He stood up. His hand went to the wound in his side. It was healing. But it hurt.

He walked back to the Café de l'Oasis. Marguerite saw his face and knew.

"Youssef?"

"Yes."

She poured him a drink. He drank it in one swallow.

"Dietrich's men?"

"Or Vernet. It does not matter."

"It matters."

"No. It does not. They are all the same."

He put the glass down. He looked at his hands. They were still shaking.

"I agreed to work for Dietrich."

Marguerite's eyes widened. "You are working for the Germans?"

"I am pretending to work for the Germans. While I find Leila and get her out."

"And if Dietrich finds out?"

"Then I will be dead. Like Youssef."

Marguerite was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded.

"Then we had better make sure he does not find out."

She reached under the bar and pulled out a pistol. She placed it on the counter.

"You will need this."

Remy picked it up. It was heavy. It was loaded.

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Just stay alive."

Chapter 18: The List

The railroad timetable was spread across a table in Marguerite's back room. Remy studied it with a magnifying glass. Youssef had marked the margins with notes.

The route started in Casablanca. It went south through Safi, then Essaouira, then to a small port called Sidi Ifni. A ship was scheduled to meet it there.

Remy traced the route with his finger. Prisoners. Political prisoners. Jewish families. Taken from their homes, loaded onto trains, shipped south.

To where? The timetable did not say. But the German letter had mentioned labor camps.

He thought about Leila's father. Antoine Bensoussan had been documenting this. Names. Dates. The evidence of a crime.

And now Leila had the evidence. And Dietrich wanted it destroyed.

Marguerite came in with coffee. She looked at the timetable.

"What is it?"

"A death list."

She set the coffee down. She did not ask more.

Remy folded the timetable and put it in his pocket. He had to find Leila before Dietrich did. But he also had to stay alive long enough to help her.

"There is a courier," he said. "Someone who can get a message to Tangier. To the British."

"Father Antoine."

"Can you contact him?"

"I can try. But it is dangerous."

"Everything is dangerous now."

She nodded. She left the room.

Remy sat alone with the timetable and the coffee. Outside, the city was waking up. People going to work. Children going to school. Normal life.

He did not remember what normal felt like.

Chapter 19: The Telegram

The telegram arrived at the Café de l'Oasis the next morning. It was addressed to Remy. One line.

"I am alive. The mountains. Come alone. — L."

He read it three times. He memorized it. Then he burned it in an ashtray.

"She is in the Atlas foothills," he told Marguerite.

"How do you know?"

"The telegram was routed through a relay in Marrakech. There is only one direction from there."

"Can you reach her?"

"Maybe. If I leave tonight."

"It is a three-day journey."

"Then I had better start."

He packed a bag. Water. Food. Bandages. The pistol. The timetable. A copy of Leila's photograph.

He stopped at the door.

"If I do not come back, tell Simone that her daughter was brave."

"Tell her yourself."

He walked out into the night. A farmer's truck was waiting at the edge of the city. The driver was a contact of Youssef's. He did not ask questions.

They drove south. The city fell away. The road became dirt. The mountains rose in the distance, dark against the stars.

Remy watched them grow closer. Somewhere in those hills, a woman was waiting. And he was going to find her.

Chapter 20: The Departure

They passed through the checkpoint at dawn. A Vichy soldier waved them through. Remy was hidden under a tarp in the back of the truck. He could smell the diesel and the dust.

The truck stopped an hour later. The driver tapped the side of the bed.

"Safe now."

Remy climbed out. The mountains were closer. The air was thin and cold.

"How far?"

"Two hours on foot. Follow the dry riverbed. There is a shepherd's hut at the end."

"Thank you."

The driver nodded. He turned the truck around and drove back toward Casablanca.

Remy walked. The sun rose. The heat came. He followed the dry riverbed. The stones were sharp. His boots were worn.

He thought about Leila. What she looked like. What she would say when she saw him. Whether she would trust him.

He thought about Dietrich. About the deadline. About the promise he had made to Simone.

He walked until the sun was high. The riverbed curved. And there it was. A stone shepherd's hut, half-collapsed, pressed against the side of the hill.

He approached slowly. His hand was on the pistol.

A figure appeared in the doorway. A woman. Dark hair. Thin. The photograph had not done her justice.

She looked at him with eyes that had seen too much.

"You are Remy."

"Yes."

"I am Leila. I have been waiting for you."

Chapter 21: The Mountain

The hut was small. One room. A fire pit. A bed of straw. A few cans of food. Leila had been living like an animal.

"It is not the Ritz," she said.

"It is better than the morgue."

She almost smiled. Almost.

"How did you find me?"

"The telegram. The shopkeeper in the medina. Your mother."

"Did they hurt her?"

"No. She is safe."

Leila sat down on the straw. She looked smaller than her photograph. Fragile. But her eyes were hard.

"Why did you come?"

"I was paid."

"By Moreau."

"Yes."

"Lucien is a fool. He thinks I am his sister-in-law who ran off with a man."

"Aren't you?"

Leila laughed. It was a bitter sound.

"I am a Jew with a dead father and a document that could bring down the Vichy government in Morocco. No. I did not run off with a man."

Remy sat down across from her. He took out the photograph of the man with the scar.

"Who is this?"

Leila looked at it. Her face went pale.

"Vernet. He works for Dietrich."

"I know."

"Then you know more than most."

"I broke into the prefecture. I read your dossier."

She looked at him with new eyes. "You broke into the prefecture?"

"Yes."

"Then you are either very brave or very stupid."

"Probably both."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she reached into her coat and pulled out a leather folder.

"This is what they want."

Remy looked at it. "Your father's testimony."

"Yes."

"I need to see it."

She hesitated. Then she handed it to him.

He opened it. The pages were covered in handwriting. Names. Dates. Shipments. Testimonies.

He read for ten minutes. When he looked up, his hands were shaking.

"This is..."

"Proof. Of what they are doing. The arrests. The deportations. The camps."

"There are names here. French officials. German officers."

"Yes."

"This is a bomb."

"It is a bomb that needs to reach London."

Remy closed the folder. He looked at Leila.

"I will get you to London."

"How?"

"I do not know yet. But I will."

Chapter 22: The Document

The testimony was comprehensive. Antoine Bensoussan had been methodical. Every page was dated. Every name was verified.

Remy read through the night. Leila slept in the corner. The fire burned low.

The document detailed three years of collaboration. French officials who had helped the Germans identify Jewish families. Police officers who had conducted raids. Shipment schedules to labor camps in Algeria.

And at the end, a list of names. Men who had profited from the suffering. Men who were still in power.

Remy recognized some of them. Officials at the prefecture. Businessmen in the city. Lucien Moreau's name was not on the list. But his business partner's was.

He closed the document. He sat in the dark and listened to the wind.

Leila stirred. "You are still reading."

"I finished."

"What do you think?"

"I think your father was a brave man."

"He was murdered for it."

"Yes."

She sat up. The firelight caught her face.

"Do you have a plan?"

"I am working on one."

"We cannot stay here. Dietrich's men will find us."

"I know."

He stood up. He walked to the door and looked out at the mountains. The stars were bright.

"There is a route. Through the mountains to Marrakech. From there, we can reach the coast."

"Then what?"

"Then we find a boat."

"Do you know someone with a boat?"

"I know someone who knows someone."

She nodded. She did not ask who.

"When do we leave?"

"Dawn."

She lay back down. Remy stayed at the door, watching the stars.

Tomorrow they would run. And they would keep running until they reached the sea.

Chapter 23: The Story

They walked at dawn. The mountain air was cold. Leila moved quickly, her breath coming in white clouds.

"Tell me about your father," Remy said.

She was quiet for a long time. Then she spoke.

"He was a professor. History. He taught at the university in Rabat. He was respected."

"Until?"

"Until he started asking questions. He noticed that Jewish students were disappearing from his classes. He started investigating."

"And that is what got him arrested."

"Yes. But before they took him, he gave me his notebook. He said it was the most important thing he would ever write."

"Do you know who arrested him?"

"Vernet. And two other men. They came to the house at night."

"What did they want?"

"His sources. He would not give them up."

"So they beat him."

"Yes. For three months. Then they killed him."

Remy walked beside her. He did not know what to say.

"I am sorry."

"I do not want your pity. I want justice."

"Justice is not something I can give you."

"Then help me get this document to people who can."

"I will."

They walked in silence. The sun rose higher. The shadows shortened.

"The night he was arrested," Leila said, "he told me something. He said that history is written by the people who survive. And that if he did not survive, I had to be the one to tell the story."

"And now you are telling it."

"Yes."

They reached a ridge. Below them, the land stretched out toward the coast. Green and gold and blue.

"Marrakech is two days east," Remy said.

"I know."

"We should keep moving."

She nodded. They walked down into the valley.

Chapter 24: The Hunter

They made camp in a dry streambed. No fire. The cold bit through their coats.

Remy slept in shifts. Leila slept curled against the bank. He watched the stars and listened for sounds that did not belong.

He heard them at midnight. Footsteps. Multiple sets. Coming from the south.

He shook Leila awake. "They are here."

They gathered their things. They moved north along the streambed. The footsteps behind them grew louder.

A voice in French. "They went this way."

Remy drew his pistol. He pushed Leila behind a boulder.

A shot rang out. Rock splintered near his head.

He fired back. Three shots. A cry. The footsteps stopped.

"Move," he said.

They ran. The streambed curved. They climbed the bank and ran through the scrub. The moonlight was bright enough to see.

Another shot. Closer.

Remy pushed Leila down. He turned and fired again. Two shots. Silence.

Then a voice, closer now. "Give us the woman, Deschamps. You do not have to die."

He did not answer. He grabbed Leila's hand and pulled her forward.

They ran until the shooting stopped. They ran until the voices faded. They ran until the sun began to rise.

When they stopped, they were in a forest. Oak trees. Thick undergrowth. The sounds of birds.

Leila was bleeding from a cut on her arm. A bullet graze.

Remy tore a strip from his shirt and bandaged it.

"Thank you," she said.

"Do not thank me yet. We are not out."

Chapter 25: The Village

The Berber village appeared at sunset. A cluster of stone houses against a hillside. Smoke rising from chimneys. The sound of goats.

Remy approached slowly. An old man met him at the edge of the village. He wore a blue djellaba. His face was lined with age.

"You are running from something," the old man said.

"Yes."

"From the French?"

"From the Germans."

The old man nodded. He gestured for them to follow.

The village took them in. A family gave them food. Couscous and lamb. Goat's milk. It was the best meal Remy had eaten in months.

An old woman took Leila's hand. She looked at the palm. She studied it for a long time.

"There is a man," she said. "He has two faces. One smiles. One kills."

Leila's face went pale. "What else?"

"The sea. I see you crossing the sea. But not alone."

"Who is with me?"

The old woman looked at Remy. Then back at Leila.

"A man who owes a debt he does not know he has."

The old woman released Leila's hand. She walked away without another word.

Remy looked at Leila.

"Do you believe that?"

"I do not know what to believe anymore."

They ate in silence. The stars came out. The village was quiet.

Remy thought about the old woman's words. A man with two faces. He thought of Roux.

Chapter 26: The Trap

They left the village at dawn. The old man gave them bread and water. He pointed toward a pass through the mountains.

"Two days to Marrakech. Stay off the main road."

They followed his directions. The trail was steep. The air was thin.

By midday, they reached a dry riverbed. The same type of terrain as before. Remy scanned the ridges.

"They will expect us to take the pass."

"Then we should not take it."

"What choice do we have?"

Remy studied the map. There was another route. Longer. Through a canyon. But it would add a day.

"We go through the canyon."

They turned east. The canyon walls rose up on either side. The sun was blocked. The air was cool.

They were halfway through when the shots came.

From above. Two shooters on the canyon rim.

Remy pushed Leila against the wall. He returned fire. The angles were impossible.

"We have to go back."

"Too late."

The shooters were closing. Bullets hit the ground around them.

Then a new sound. A truck engine. Coming from the canyon exit.

Remy looked. A green truck. French military markings.

It stopped. A man jumped out. He was wearing a uniform.

Inspector Roux.

"Get in," Roux shouted.

Remy grabbed Leila. They ran for the truck.

The shooters fired. Roux returned fire, covering them.

They dove into the truck bed. Roux got behind the wheel. The truck lurched forward.

Bullets hit the tailgate. Then they were clear, speeding out of the canyon.

Remy looked at Leila. She was shaking.

"That was Inspector Roux."

"The Vichy inspector?"

"Yes."

"Why did he save us?"

"I do not know."

But he had a suspicion. The old woman's words. A man with two faces.

Chapter 27: The Pact

Roux drove for an hour before he stopped. They were in a pine forest. The road was unpaved.

He got out. He walked to the back of the truck. His face was unreadable.

"You are both alive. That is something."

"Why did you save us?"

Roux looked at Remy. Then at Leila.

"Because I knew your father."

Leila stared at him. "You were the one who arrested him."

"No. I was the one who tried to save him."

"You were there."

"I was a lieutenant. I did what I was told. But I was not the one who beat him."

"Who was?"

"Vernet. Under Dietrich's orders."

Leila was silent. Her hands were shaking.

"Why should I believe you?"

"Because I am still alive. If I were working with them, I would have let the shooters kill you."

Remy stepped between them. "What do you want, Roux?"

"I want to help. I have been watching this case since the beginning. I knew that if I stayed close, I might be able to do something."

"And you waited until now?"

"I needed to know whose side you were on."

"Whose side did you think?"

Roux looked at him. "I think you are on the side of the woman. And that is enough for me."

He reached into his pocket. He pulled out a folded map.

"There is a safe house in Marrakech. Resistance. They will help you get to the coast."

"Why are you doing this?"

Roux's face was tired. "Because someone has to. And because I am tired of being the man with two faces."

Chapter 28: The Return

They drove through the night. Leila slept in the truck bed. Remy sat beside Roux in the cab.

"You could have been killed," Remy said.

"I know."

"Why risk it?"

Roux was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was low.

"The document your friend carries. I have seen parts of it. My name is in it."

Remy turned. "Are you on the list?"

"Yes. But not as a collaborator. As a witness. Your father documented a conversation I had with him. I told him about the shipments. I told him what I knew."

"So you have been working against them the whole time."

"No. I have been doing my job while trying not to become a monster. There is a difference."

Remy thought about this. He did not know if he believed it.

"How did you know where to find us?"

"Vernet. He reported to Dietrich this morning. I listened at the door."

"And Dietrich sent the shooters?"

"Yes. He wants the document. He wants it badly enough to kill a Vichy inspector if he gets in the way."

They drove into Marrakech at dawn. The city was waking. The call to prayer echoed from the minarets.

Roux stopped at a house near the medina. White walls. A blue door.

"Go inside. They will take care of you."

"What about you?"

"I will go back. I will pretend I know nothing."

"And if Dietrich finds out?"

Roux smiled. It was a sad smile.

"Then I will see you in the next life."

Chapter 29: The Betrayal

The safe house was run by a French woman named Claire. She was in her fifties. She had been in the Resistance since 1940.

She gave them clean clothes and hot food. She examined Leila's wound.

"You will live," she said. "But you need rest."

"How long?"

"A day. Two. Then we move you to the coast."

That night, Remy did not sleep. He sat by the window and watched the street.

Leila came to sit beside him.

"What are you thinking about?"

"Youssef. Roux. The people who are going to die if we fail."

"We are not going to fail."

"You do not know that."

"No. But I know that if I stop believing I will survive, I am already dead."

Remy looked at her. In the moonlight, she looked younger. But her eyes were old.

"Your father would be proud of you."

She did not answer. She put her hand on his.

"We are going to make it," she said.

He wanted to believe her.

The next morning, Claire came with bad news. "The British contact in Tangier has been arrested. The network is compromised."

"How compromised?"

"Completely. I do not know who talked. But someone did."

Remy looked at Leila. She was pale.

"Then we need a new plan."

"There is only one option," Claire said. "The Portuguese freighter. It leaves from Casablanca in three days."

"Casablanca is crawling with Vichy police."

"Yes. But the captain is sympathetic. And he owes me a debt."

Remy looked at Leila. "Do you trust her?"

Leila looked at Claire. Then back at Remy.

"I trust you."

Chapter 30: The Grief

They returned to Casablanca at night. Claire drove them in a delivery truck. They hid under crates of vegetables.

The city was different. Darker. The streets were empty. The prefecture was lit up like a fortress.

Claire dropped them at a warehouse near the port. "Stay here until I come back."

She left. They were alone.

Leila sat on a crate. She stared at the wall.

"Are you all right?"

"I am thinking about Youssef."

"I know."

"He was kind to me. He made me a passport. He did not ask questions."

"He was a good man."

"He was your friend."

"Yes."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet.

"I am sorry."

"Me too."

They sat in the dark. The warehouse smelled of fish and oil. The port sounds drifted in.

He told her about Youssef. About the shop in the medina. About the ledgers and the secrets and the smile that never reached his eyes.

She listened. She did not interrupt.

"He died because he helped me," Remy said.

"He died because he chose to do the right thing."

"That does not make it easier."

"No. It does not."

She moved closer. She put her head on his shoulder.

They sat like that until the sun rose.

Somewhere in the city, Dietrich was waiting. Somewhere, Roux was playing his double game. And somewhere, a Portuguese freighter was being loaded for departure.

They had two days.

Chapter 31: The Proposal

Marguerite came to the warehouse at noon. She carried bread and cheese and a bottle of wine.

"I can get you on the freighter," she said.

"I already have a contact."

"Claire. Yes. But Claire does not know the captain like I do."

"What is the price?"

Marguerite looked at Leila.

"The document. I want a copy."

"No," Leila said.

"Then you do not get the boat."

Remy stepped between them. "You are working for them?"

"No. I am working for myself. The document is valuable. I want insurance."

"Insurance for what?"

"For the day when the Germans lose and the French come back. I want to be on the right side."

Leila was silent. She looked at Remy.

"I will make a copy," Leila said. "But the original stays with me."

"Agreed."

Marguerite left. Remy turned to Leila.

"Are you sure?"

"I do not trust her. But I trust her greed. She will not betray us as long as the document is valuable."

"And after?"

"After, we will be gone."

She sat down. She began copying pages into a fresh notebook.

Remy watched her work. Her hands were steady. Her face was calm.

He thought about what came after. If they survived. If they made it to Lisbon.

He did not know what he would do. He had never thought beyond the next job.

But for the first time, he was thinking about it.

Chapter 32: The Priest's Secret

Father Antoine came to the warehouse that evening. He wore civilian clothes. He looked nervous.

"I have a message from Roux."

"What does it say?"

"The operation is set for tomorrow night. The freighter departs at midnight. Roux will be on duty at the port gate."

"And Dietrich?"

"Dietrich is meeting with the German consul. He will be occupied until eleven."

"Can we trust this?"

Father Antoine hesitated.

"Roux has been passing information to the Resistance for six months. He is one of us."

"Then why did he arrest Leila's father?"

"He did not. He was ordered to. He delayed the arrest by three hours. It gave Antoine time to give the document to Leila."

Leila looked up. "He did that?"

"Yes. And he has been protecting you ever since."

Father Antoine reached into his coat. He pulled out a small envelope.

"A letter from your mother."

Leila took it. Her hands were shaking.

She opened it. She read it. When she finished, she was crying.

"She says she loves me. She says she knows I am doing the right thing."

"She is proud of you."

Leila wiped her eyes. She folded the letter carefully and put it in her pocket.

"Tell her I love her. Tell her I will come back."

"I will."

Father Antoine left. Remy and Leila were alone again.

"He was the one," Leila said. "The two-faced man. Roux. He was the one who helped my father."

"Yes."

"He has been playing both sides the whole time."

"To survive. And to help."

She nodded. She looked at the document. The copied pages.

"I want to live long enough to thank him."

"You will."

Chapter 33: The Double Game

Remy met Dietrich at the Café de la République. He wore a clean shirt. He had shaved. He looked like a man who was cooperating.

"You have news," Dietrich said.

"I have a location. The woman is hiding in the medina. I can deliver her tomorrow."

"Tomorrow is too late. I need her tonight."

"I cannot. She is moving between safe houses. I need time to track her."

Dietrich studied him. His eyes were cold.

"You are lying to me."

"Why would I lie?"

"Because you have chosen a side."

Remy held his gaze. "I chose the side that pays. You offered five thousand francs. I want the money."

Dietrich smiled. It was not a friendly smile.

"You will get the money when I have the woman."

"Then I will deliver her tomorrow night."

"Make sure you do."

Remy stood up. He walked out of the café. His heart was pounding.

He had bought twenty-four hours.

He found a phone booth. He dialed the number Claire had given him.

"The meeting is set for tomorrow. Midnight at the port."

"Confirmed."

"Roux will be on the gate."

"Understood."

He hung up. He leaned against the wall of the phone booth.

One more day. Then it was over.

He walked back to the warehouse. Leila was waiting.

"How did it go?"

"He bought it."

"Good."

"Tomorrow night. We get you on that boat."

She looked at him. "We."

"I am coming with you. At least as far as Lisbon."

She did not argue.

Chapter 34: Roux's Choice

Roux arrested Remy at noon. In the middle of the street. Handcuffs. Spectacle.

"Deschamps, you are under arrest for theft of government property."

Remy did not resist. Roux pushed him into the car.

They drove. Not toward the prefecture. Toward the residential quarter.

"What are you doing?"

"Making it look real."

Roux's apartment was small. Clean. A few books. A photograph of a woman.

"You are married?"

"Was. She died in 1939."

Remy sat down. Roux unlocked the handcuffs.

"I need to tell you something."

"Tell me."

"The network is compromised. The British contact. The priest. They are all being watched."

"How do you know?"

"Because I am the one watching them."

Remy stood up. "You are still working for them."

"I am working for everyone. It is the only way to stay alive."

"And the operation tonight?"

"It is a trap. Dietrich knows about the freighter. He is going to let it leave. Then he is going to sink it."

Remy's blood went cold. "Leila."

"She will be on the boat. And she will die."

"Why are you telling me this?"

Roux looked at him. His face was tired.

"Because I decided I do not want to be the man who let her die."

He reached into his desk. He pulled out a folder.

"The real plan. There is a fishing boat. It leaves from a different dock at eleven. It will take her to a British ship waiting offshore."

"And Dietrich?"

"Will be watching the wrong boat."

Remy looked at the folder. Then at Roux.

"Why now?"

"Because I have run out of time to choose. And I choose this."

Chapter 35: The Network

Roux spread the documents across his table. Maps. Schedules. Photographs.

"The network was built by your father," he said to Leila. She had been brought to the apartment an hour later. "He recruited me. He trusted me."

"And you betrayed him."

"I delayed his arrest. I gave him time to pass the document. I could not save his life."

"But you tried."

"Yes."

Leila looked at the maps. "What is the plan?"

"The fishing boat. It leaves at eleven. The captain is a Corsican named Bellini. He is Resistance."

"Where does he take me?"

"A British freighter at sea. From there, to Gibraltar. Then London."

"And Remy?"

"Remy comes with you to the fishing boat. Then he is on his own."

Leila nodded. "What about you?"

Roux was silent.

"I stay. I continue the work. Or I die. It depends on what happens tonight."

"Then come with us."

"Someone has to stay and fight."

Remy looked at him. For the first time, he saw respect.

"You are not the man I thought you were."

"I am worse. But I am trying."

They went over the plan three times. Every detail. Every contingency.

At ten, they left. Roux drove. Leila and Remy in the back.

The streets were dark. The port was quiet.

Roux stopped at a gate. He flashed his badge. The guard waved them through.

"Good luck," Roux said.

"Thank you."

"Thank me by living."

They got out. They walked toward the dock. The fishing boat was there. Its engine was running.

Chapter 36: The Plan

Bellini was a small man with a scarred face and quick hands. He shook Remy's hand.

"Roux sent you."

"Yes."

"Get on board. We leave in ten minutes."

Leila climbed aboard. Remy followed.

The boat was small. A cabin. A hold full of fishing nets. It smelled of salt and diesel.

Bellini cast off. The engine throbbed.

They moved away from the dock. The port lights receded.

Remy stood at the rail. He watched Casablanca shrink.

"We made it," Leila said.

"We are not there yet."

They passed the breakwater. The open sea was ahead. Dark and cold.

Bellini pointed. "See that light? That is the British freighter."

Thirty minutes. Then she would be safe.

Leila stood beside him. The wind was cold.

"What will you do?"

"After I drop you off? I do not know."

"Come with me."

"To London?"

"To wherever."

He looked at her. The moonlight was on her face.

"I am a fixer. I do not belong in London."

"Neither do I. But we can find out together."

He did not answer.

The British freighter grew larger. Lights. A flag.

Bellini slowed the engine.

"Get ready to transfer."

Leila touched Remy's arm.

"Thank you."

"I was paid."

"You know it is more than that."

He knew. He did not say it.

The transfer was quick. A ladder. Strong hands. Leila climbed aboard the freighter.

She stood at the rail. She looked down at him.

"Come with me."

"Go. I will find you."

The freighter pulled away. The fishing boat drifted.

Remy watched until he could not see her anymore.

Then he sat down in the boat and did not move.

Chapter 37: The Preparation

Roux's plan had worked. But Remy knew it was not over.

The fishing boat returned to Casablanca at dawn. Bellini dropped him at a dock near the old medina.

"If anyone asks, you were never on my boat."

"I understand."

Remy walked through the empty streets. The city was waking. Bread shops opening. The call to prayer.

He went to the Café de l'Oasis. Marguerite was behind the bar.

"You look terrible."

"I feel worse."

He sat down. She poured him a coffee.

"Did she make it?"

"Yes."

"Good."

She sat across from him.

"Dietrich knows something went wrong. He has put the city on alert."

"How much does he know?"

"He knows the freighter was a decoy. He does not know about Bellini."

"Then we have time."

"Not much."

He drank the coffee. It was hot and black.

"Roux?"

"Alive. He reported that you escaped custody. There is a warrant for your arrest."

"That was the plan."

"Yes. But now it is real."

He nodded. He had expected this.

"Where are you going?"

"I do not know yet. But I cannot stay here."

She reached under the bar. She pulled out a packet.

"Papers. Money. A new identity."

He looked at the packet. Then at her.

"Why?"

"Because you did the right thing. And someone should help you for once."

He picked up the packet. He put it in his pocket.

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Just stay alive."

He walked to the door. He turned.

"Marguerite. The ledger she copied. Keep it safe."

"I will."

He walked out into the morning light.

Chapter 38: The Vigil

Remy spent the day in hiding. A room above a carpet shop. The window faced the street.

He watched the police go by. Twice. Three times. They were looking for him.

He sat on the floor with his back to the wall. He held the pistol.

He thought about Leila. About the freighter. About the cold sea.

He thought about Roux. The two-faced man who had finally chosen a side.

He thought about Youssef. His body in the alley. The shop burned.

He thought about his mother. Dead ten years. He had not visited her grave in five.

He was alone. He had always been alone.

But for the first time, he felt the weight of it.

He slept. He dreamed of the sea. Of a woman standing at a rail.

He woke to darkness. The street was quiet.

He checked his watch. Eleven at night.

He stood up. He stretched. His body ached.

He walked to the window. The city was dark.

Somewhere, Leila was crossing the sea. Somewhere, the document was reaching London.

And he was here. In a rented room. Waiting for the next move.

He did not know what came next. He only knew that he was not done.

Chapter 39: The Handoff

The prefecture was quiet. Remy moved through the shadows. He had a package to deliver.

Not the real document. A copy. A very good copy.

He found Roux's office. The light was on.

He knocked.

Roux opened the door. His face was pale.

"You should not be here."

"I know. But I need you to deliver this."

He handed over the package. Roux looked at it.

"What is it?"

"A gift for Dietrich."

"This is the document?"

"A copy. I want you to give it to him. Tell him you found it on a dead courier."

Roux understood. "He will think the document is destroyed."

"Yes. And he will stop looking for Leila."

"It is dangerous."

"Everything is dangerous."

Roux took the package. He weighed it in his hands.

"I will do it. But you need to disappear."

"After tonight."

"No. Now."

Remy shook his head. "There is one more thing I need to do."

"What?"

"I need to see Vernet."

Roux stared at him. "That is suicide."

"Probably. But he killed Youssef. And I owe him."

Roux was silent. Then he nodded.

"The warehouse at the south dock. He is there tonight."

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Just stay alive."

Remy left. He walked toward the south dock.

The night was cold. The stars were hidden.

He was going to kill a man. And he was not sure he would walk away.

Chapter 40: The Collapse

The warehouse was dark. Remy entered through a side door.

Vernet was there. Alone. He was sitting at a desk, reading a newspaper.

"Hello, Georges."

Vernet looked up. His face went white.

"You."

"Me."

Remy closed the door behind him.

"You killed Youssef."

"Youssef was a traitor."

"He was a friend."

"He was a smuggler. A criminal. The world is better without him."

Remy walked closer. His hand was on the pistol.

"You killed him because he helped us."

"I killed him because he was in the way."

Vernet stood up. His hand went to his desk drawer.

"Do not."

"You cannot shoot me. The whole prefecture will hear."

"I know."

Remy took out the pistol. He pointed it at Vernet.

"Tell me why."

"Why what?"

"Why you do this. Why you hunt people."

Vernet laughed. It was a hollow sound.

"Because I am paid to. Because it is easier than thinking."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only answer there is."

Remy looked at him. The gun was steady.

He thought about Youssef. About the shop. About the smile.

He lowered the pistol.

"I am not going to kill you."

Vernet blinked. "Why?"

"Because you are not worth it."

He turned and walked out.

He heard Vernet laugh behind him. "Coward."

He kept walking.

He was not a coward. He was tired. Tired of death. Tired of the game.

He walked into the night. The city swallowed him.

Chapter 41: The Hunt

The next morning, the city was alive with police. Dietrich had discovered the deception.

Remy saw the wanted posters. His face. Leila's face. A reward.

He pulled his hat low. He walked through the medina. The merchants looked at him. They looked away.

He found a café. He ordered tea.

The proprietor was a young man with nervous eyes.

"They are looking for you."

"I know."

"The German offered a reward."

"Are you going to collect?"

The young man looked at him. He shook his head.

"Youssef was my cousin."

Remy was quiet. "I am sorry."

"He helped you. I will help you."

The young man brought him food. Bread. Olives. Cheese.

"Stay in the back. I will tell you when it is safe."

Remy ate. His body needed fuel.

He thought about Leila. She was at sea now. Safe.

He thought about himself. Trapped in a city that wanted him dead.

He wondered how it had come to this. A simple job. Find a missing woman.

Nothing was simple. Not in Casablanca. Not in this war.

He finished eating. He waited.

The young man came back.

"They are closing the medina. You have to leave now."

"Where?"

"There is a tunnel. Behind the mosque. It leads to the port."

Remy stood up. "Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Youssef would have done the same."

Chapter 42: The Sacrifice

The tunnel was dark. Narrow. It smelled of water and rats.

Remy moved through it. His hand on the wall. His pistol ready.

He emerged at the port. The docks were busy. Freighters. Fishing boats.

He saw the Portuguese freighter. It was still in port. Marguerite's contact.

He walked toward it. A voice stopped him.

"Deschamps."

He turned. Roux was there. His uniform was torn. His face was bloody.

"Roux."

"There is no time. Dietrich is coming. He knows everything."

"How?"

"Vernet. He talked."

Roux stumbled. Remy caught him.

"You are hurt."

"It does not matter. You need to get on that boat."

"Come with me."

"No. I am done running."

Roux pushed something into Remy's hand. A letter.

"For Leila. Tell her I am sorry."

"You can tell her yourself."

"Go. Now."

Remy hesitated. Then he ran.

He heard the shots behind him. He did not turn.

He reached the freighter. He climbed the gangplank.

He looked back. Roux was on the dock. His pistol was raised.

Dietrich's men were advancing. Roux fired.

He fell.

The freighter pulled away.

Remy watched the dock shrink. Roux's body was still there.

The two-faced man had finally made his choice.

Chapter 43: The Boat

The Portuguese freighter was called the Santa Maria. It was old. It smelled of cargo and diesel.

The captain was a man named Ferreira. He was thin. His eyes were kind.

"I was told to expect you."

"By whom?"

"Marguerite. She said you needed passage."

"To where?"

"Lisbon. Then you are on your own."

Remy sat down in the cabin. His hands were shaking.

"Are you all right?"

"No."

"Roux?"

"He is dead."

Ferreira nodded. He poured two glasses of wine.

"He was a brave man."

"He was a complicated man."

"Is that not the same thing?"

Remy drank the wine. It was cheap. It was warm. It was the best thing he had ever tasted.

"What happens in Lisbon?"

"You find the British. You tell them what you know."

"And then?"

"Then you decide what kind of man you want to be."

Ferreira left. Remy sat alone.

The ship moved through the dark water.

He thought about Leila. About the document. About the war.

He thought about Youssef. About Roux. About all the dead.

He did not know if it was worth it.

But he was still alive. And that would have to be enough.

Chapter 44: The Chase

The German patrol boat appeared at dawn. A grey shape on the horizon.

Ferreira shouted from the bridge. "They are hailing us."

Remy went to the deck. The patrol boat was closing.

"What do we do?"

"We run. This freighter is faster than it looks."

The Santa Maria picked up speed. The patrol boat followed.

A shot across the bow. A warning.

"They want you."

"I know."

Ferreira looked at him. "I can slow down. Let them board."

"No. Keep going."

The patrol boat fired again. Closer.

Remy went to the stern. He watched the boat grow larger.

He thought about jumping. Swimming for it.

But that would give them what they wanted.

He went back to the bridge.

"Can we make it?"

"Maybe. If the wind holds."

The Santa Maria pushed through the waves. The patrol boat fell behind.

Then it turned. It was heading back.

"They are giving up."

"No. They are calling for reinforcements."

"How long?"

"Two hours."

"Then we need to be gone in one."

Ferreira nodded. He pushed the engine harder.

The sun rose. The sea was empty.

Remy watched the horizon. Waiting for the next ship.

It did not come.

They made it to international waters.

They were safe.

For now.

Chapter 45: The Truth

Lisbon was a shock. Lights. Cafes. People who were not at war.

Remy walked through the streets. He felt foreign. Out of place.

He found the British consulate. He gave them the letter from Roux.

They took him to a room. They asked questions. He answered.

The document. The shipments. The names.

They listened. They took notes.

Then they left him alone.

He sat in the room. The window faced the street. Trams. Bicycles. Normal life.

A woman entered. British. Officer's uniform.

"Mr. Deschamps. I am Captain Shaw."

"Where is Leila?"

"She is safe. She arrived two days ago."

"I need to see her."

"That can be arranged."

Shaw sat down across from him.

"The document you helped deliver. It has already been sent to London."

"Good."

"It will be published. It will change the war."

"Good."

"You saved lives."

Remy looked at her. "I killed people too."

"You did what you had to do."

"I do not know if that makes it right."

Shaw was quiet.

"Leila is at a safe house. I can take you there."

"Now?"

"Now."

He stood up. He followed her out.

Chapter 46: The Shot

The safe house was in a quiet neighborhood. White buildings. Orange trees.

Shaw stopped at the gate.

"She is inside. I will wait here."

Remy walked up the path. The door opened.

Leila stood there. She looked different. Clean. Rested.

"You are alive."

"I am."

She stepped forward. She hugged him.

"I thought you were dead."

"I thought so too."

They stood in the doorway. Holding each other.

"I heard about Roux."

"Yes."

"He saved us."

"Yes."

"I am sorry."

"Me too."

She pulled back. She looked at him.

"What now?"

"I do not know."

"Stay."

"Here?"

"In Lisbon. With me."

"I am a fixer. I have nothing to offer."

"You have yourself. That is enough."

He looked at her. The sun was behind her. She was beautiful.

"I do not know how to be anything else."

"Then learn."

He thought about it. The war was not over. There was still work to do.

Maybe he could do it from here.

Maybe he could do it with her.

"All right."

She smiled. It was the first real smile he had seen from her.

"All right."

Chapter 47: The Float

The days passed. Remy stayed in Lisbon. He found work. Legitimate work. An import-export office.

He did not carry a gun. He did not break into buildings.

He went to the cafe. He read the newspapers. The document had been published. The Vichy administration in Morocco had collapsed.

Dietrich had been recalled to Berlin. His fate was unknown.

Vernet had fled. No one knew where.

Simone Bensoussan had been evacuated. She was safe. She was coming to Lisbon.

Remy sat in the cafe. The sun was warm. The coffee was good.

Leila came to sit with him.

"You look different."

"Better, I hope."

"Peaceful."

"I think I am."

She took his hand.

"My mother arrives tomorrow."

"I know."

"Will you come to meet her?"

"If you want me to."

"I want you to."

He squeezed her hand.

The war continued. But for a moment, in the Lisbon sun, there was peace.

He thought about Youssef. About Roux. About all the dead.

He hoped they could see this.

Chapter 48: The Aftermath

Simone arrived on a Wednesday. She was thinner. Older. But her eyes were the same.

She hugged Leila for a long time. Then she turned to Remy.

"You brought her back."

"She brought herself back. I just opened the door."

"You are modest."

"No. I am honest."

Simone took his hand. "Thank you. For everything."

"I did what anyone would do."

"No. You did what no one else would do."

They went to dinner. A small restaurant near the water.

They talked. About the past. About the future. About the war.

Simone wanted to know about Youssef. About Roux. About the people who had helped.

Remy told her. He told her about the priest. About Marguerite. About the young man in the medina.

She listened. She nodded.

"These are good people."

"The best."

"And what about you? What will you do now?"

"I do not know. I am learning to be still."

"That is harder than fighting."

"Yes."

She looked at him. Her eyes were kind.

"You are welcome in our family. Always."

He did not know what to say. He nodded.

Leila smiled.

They ate. They drank wine. The war seemed very far away.

But it was not. And he knew it.

But for this one night, he let himself forget.

Chapter 49: The Return

A month later, Remy returned to Casablanca. The war was still on. But the city had changed.

The Vichy officials were gone. The German flags were down. The prefecture was under new command.

He walked through the medina. The merchants called out. The children ran.

He stopped at the alley where Youssef had died. The shop was still a shell.

He stood there for a long time.

He visited Marguerite. The Café de l'Oasis was the same.

"You look good," she said.

"I feel good."

"Leila?"

"Safe. In Lisbon."

"Good."

She poured him a drink.

"What brings you back?"

"I needed to see it. To close the circle."

"And?"

"It is closed."

They drank together. They talked about the old days.

He told her about Roux. About the sacrifice.

She listened. She did not cry.

"He was a good man."

"In the end."

"The end is what matters."

He stayed until the evening. Then he walked to the cathedral.

Father Antoine was there. He was older. Tired.

"Deschamps. I did not expect to see you."

"I came to say goodbye."

"To me?"

"To the city."

The priest nodded.

"Will you come back?"

"I do not know."

"Then I will pray for you."

"Thank you, Father."

He left. He walked to the port.

The sun was setting. The sea was golden.

He boarded a ship. He did not look back.

Chapter 50: The Hireling

He sat at the Café de l'Oasis. It was a Tuesday morning. The coffee was black and bitter.

A man approached. He wore a suit. He carried an envelope.

"Are you Remy Deschamps?"

"I am."

"I need you to find someone."

Remy looked at the envelope. The money was thick.

He thought about Leila. About Lisbon. About the peace he had found.

He thought about Youssef. About Roux. About the cost.

"I am retired."

"The man I spoke to at the port said you were the best."

"He was wrong."

The man put the envelope on the table.

"Just look at the photograph. That is all I ask."

Remy looked at the envelope. Then at the man.

He picked up the photograph. A woman. Dark hair. Afraid.

He put it down.

"I am sorry. I cannot help you."

The man took the envelope. He left.

Remy sat alone. The fan turned overhead.

He finished his coffee. He thought about Lisbon.

The fixer was done fixing.

He stood up. He walked to the door.

He stepped out into the Casablanca sun.

And he did not look back.
