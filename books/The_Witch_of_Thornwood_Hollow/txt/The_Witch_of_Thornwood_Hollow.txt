Chapter 1: The Boundary

Morwen woke before dawn. The hollow was still, the air thick with dew and the scent of rotting leaves. She pulled on her boots without lighting a candle — she knew every knot in the floorboards by touch.

The boundary needed walking. Every morning for ten years.

She stepped off the porch and into the wood. The path had narrowed in the night. New shoots of black thorn pushed through the leaf litter, their tips glistening with something wet. She knelt and touched one. It was warm. Living things should not be warm at this hour.

Morwen pressed her palm to the soil and felt the pulse beneath. The Thorn King was pushing again. Harder than last month. Harder than last week.

She stood and walked the full circuit anyway, speaking the old words under her breath. The words meant less each year. The boundary held, but she could feel it thinning like a worn sheet.

By the time she returned to the cottage, the sun had begun to light the treetops. Lark sat on the windowsill, feathers ruffled, watching her with black-bead eyes.

"He's waking up," Morwen said.

Lark tilted his head. He already knew.

Chapter 2: The Stranger

Dr. Elias Thorne stepped off the coach into mud. The village of Thornwood consisted of twelve buildings, a stone well, and a sign that read WELCOME in letters so faded they were almost prayer.

He had been sent by the Royal Institute to investigate reports of botanical blight in the Thornwood Forest. Three farmers had written letters describing crops that blackened overnight and soil that steamed in the cold. The Institute classified the affair as "flora pathology — suspected fungal."

Elias did not believe in suspicion. He believed in data.

He found the village inn — a crooked building with a thatched roof that sagged like an old horse. The innkeeper was a woman named Margit who looked at him the way a cat looks at a dog that has wandered into its yard.

"You'll be the botanist," she said.

"I am."

"Then you'll want to stay out of the hollow."

"The hollow is exactly where I need to go."

Margit wiped a glass and studied him. "Suit yourself. But don't say I didn't warn you."

She showed him to a room with a narrow bed and a window that faced the forest. Elias set down his bag and unpacked his instruments: microscope, vials, tweezers, notebook. He placed them in a neat row on the windowsill.

The forest was dark against the evening sky. He thought he saw a light moving between the trees. When he blinked, it was gone.

Chapter 3: The Warding

Morwen felt the stranger before she saw him. The thorns rippled, disturbed by his presence at the edge of the hollow. She opened the front door and stood on the porch, barefoot now, the cold wood familiar under her arches.

Lark flew into the trees and returned within minutes. He landed on her shoulder and chirped a description into her ear: tall man, brown coat, glass lenses, carrying a leather case.

A scholar. The worst kind.

Morwen went inside and began the warding. She took salt from the clay jar and drew a line across each windowsill. She hung sprigs of rowan above the door. She pressed her thumb to the iron nail in the doorframe and whispered the prohibition.

No scholar had ever brought anything good to a witch. They measured what should not be measured. They wrote down what should not be remembered.

The warding complete, she sat in the dark and listened. The forest was quiet. Too quiet. The stranger had brought a silence with him, and the Thorn King was using it to listen.

She could feel the old malice gathering under the oak.

"Not yet," she said to the empty room. "Not while I draw breath."

Chapter 4: The Warning

Elias ate dinner in the common room. The other patrons — three farmers and a man who smelled of sheep — watched him from the corners of their eyes. No one spoke to him.

Old Margit brought him a bowl of stew and sat across from him. The stew was good. Hearty. He told her so.

"You're the first scholar to come here in twenty years," she said.

"The Institute sent me. There are reports of crop failure."

"The crops are fine. It's the forest you should worry about."

Elias set down his spoon. "Tell me about the forest."

Margit leaned forward. Her voice dropped to a rasp. "There's a witch in the hollow. She keeps the thorns at bay. Without her, the village would have been swallowed years ago. If you go in there with your glass lenses and your questions, you'll weaken the boundary."

"I don't believe in witches."

"You will."

She stood and took his bowl. The conversation was over.

Elias went to his room and opened his notebook. He wrote: Subject — Thornwood Forest. Preliminary observation — local superstition runs deep. He underlined superstition and added a question mark.

Then he looked out the window again. The light was there. A pale flicker deep in the trees. He watched it until his eyes burned.

It did not go out.

Chapter 5: The Fox

Morwen found the fox at first light. It lay in a tangle of briars at the edge of the northern boundary, its flank torn, its breath shallow. The thorns had wrapped around its hind leg, digging in with each movement it made.

It did not snarl when she approached. It was too far gone for that.

She knelt and spoke the old words. Not the words of warding. The words of soothing, the ones her mother had taught her when she was small enough to sit in her lap. They were not magic. They were just sounds that told a creature it was safe.

The fox's eyes tracked her. It trembled.

Morwen took her knife and began cutting the thorns. They resisted, curling away from the blade as if they sensed her intention. She worked patiently, strand by strand, until the fox's leg was free.

The wounds were deep. She carried the fox back to the cottage and laid it on a blanket by the hearth. Lark watched from the rafters.

"Don't look at me like that," she said. "It's still alive."

She mixed a paste of comfrey and yarrow and applied it to the wounds. The fox's heartbeat was a small, rapid drum against her palm.

The thorns had been hungry. Hungrier than usual. They had reached beyond the boundary, and she had not noticed.

That was the trouble with being alone. There was only so much one person could watch.

Chapter 6: The Samples

Elias entered the forest at midday with his leather case and a resolve to apply scientific method to what he was certain was a fungal outbreak. The trees thickened quickly. Within a hundred yards, the village sounds vanished. The birds fell silent.

He found the first black thorn patch twenty yards off the path. He knelt and examined it. The thorns were not like any plant he had seen. They had no leaves, no flowers, no root structure he could trace. They emerged from the soil like veins surfacing on a body.

He took samples. Scraped bark from nearby trees. Collected soil in labeled vials. He noted a purple-black discoloration in the leaf litter, as if something had bled into the earth.

The air was wrong. Too still. Too warm for the season.

He walked deeper, following the trail of corruption. The thorns grew thicker, forming walls on either side of the path. He had to squeeze through gaps. The thorns snagged his coat, his trousers, his hands.

He stopped when he reached a clearing. In the center stood an oak so massive it seemed to hold up the sky. Its bark was black. Not the black of age or rot, but the black of something burned from within.

He took a sample of the bark. It crumbled in his fingers.

Something moved in the shadows beneath the oak. A shape. A suggestion of a shape.

Elias held his breath. The shape did not move again.

He turned and walked back to the village, his samples clutched to his chest.

Chapter 7: The Thorn King

Under the oak, in the dark where no light reached, the Thorn King dreamed.

He had been dreaming for three hundred years. Trapped by the first witch's blood, sealed beneath a root that wrapped his prison like a fist, he had learned to be patient. He had learned to wait.

The new man interested him. A scholar. A skeptic. A mind that hungered for answers and would not accept magic as one.

The Thorn King knew about skeptics. They were the easiest to break. You did not need to convince them. You only needed to make them doubt what they already believed.

He sent a tendril of thought through the soil. It found the oak, climbed the trunk, and entered the air. The man would breathe it. The man would dream.

The Thorn King smiled. He did not have lips anymore — the seal had dissolved that flesh long ago — but he remembered how.

The witch had grown careless. She had let her grief soften her. And now she had company.

He would use the scholar to find the cracks in her armor. Then he would push through.

Chapter 8: The Meeting

Morwen saw him coming. The thorns warned her — a ripple along the boundary, a tightening in her chest. She stepped out of the cottage and waited.

He emerged from the trees at the edge of the hollow. Brown coat, glass lenses, leather case. Lark had described him well. Up close, he looked tired. His eyes were sharp, but the skin around them was lined with sleeplessness.

"You're the witch," he said.

"I'm the woman who lives here. You're the scholar who should not have come."

"I'm Dr. Elias Thorne. Royal Institute of Botanical Sciences."

"I know what you are. I felt you enter the forest."

He frowned. "That's not possible."

"And yet here we are."

They stood in silence. The fox watched from the cottage doorway, still too weak to move. Elias noticed it and his expression changed — softened, almost imperceptibly.

"You tend to animals," he said.

"I tend to what needs tending."

"I'd like to study the thorns."

"You'd like to study everything. That's your weakness."

He did not deny it. "The forest is dying. My job is to find out why."

"Your job is to write a report and go home."

She was right, and they both knew it. He looked at the cottage, the garden of herbs, the small bones hanging from the eaves. His face was unreadable.

"I'm not leaving," he said.

"That's your choice." She turned and went inside. "Don't step on the salt."

Chapter 9: The Gift

Elias returned to the village frustrated. The witch was guarded, hostile, and dismissive of his work. But she was not ignorant. She knew the forest in a way he did not. If he wanted to understand the blight, he needed her cooperation.

He spent the evening at the inn, reviewing his samples under the microscope. The thorn tissue showed no cellular structure he recognized. The cells were irregular, dark-walled, and pulsed with a faint internal movement he could not explain.

He looked up to find Old Margit watching him.

"She won't trust you," Margit said. "Not unless you give her something first."

"I'm a scientist, not a tradesman."

"The forest doesn't care about your titles. Give her something real. Not a bribe. A gift."

Elias thought about it. He had nothing of value. His instruments were practical. His clothes were worn.

Then he remembered the pressed flower in his notebook. He had carried it for three years. A larkspur from his wife's garden, dried and flattened between pages she would never read again.

He took it out. The petals were fragile, the color faded to pale blue.

He put it in his pocket and did not sleep well that night.

Chapter 10: The Acceleration

Morwen read the thorns. It was not a metaphorical reading. She pressed her hands to the soil, closed her eyes, and let the pulses travel up her arms. Each pulse was a word. Each word told her something.

The curse was accelerating. The Thorn King had been feeding on something new. Not just her isolation. Something sharper. Fresher.

The scholar's arrival had disturbed the balance. His skepticism was a salt that irritated the old wound. The Thorn King liked it. It gave him strength.

She walked the boundary and found six new breaches. Small, but growing. She sealed them with salt and iron filings, pressing the mixture into the soil with her bare hands.

The thorns resisted. They curled away from her fingers, then curled back, testing.

"You have to hold," she told them. "Just a little longer."

But she could not say what they were waiting for. She could not see the end of this. Only the spreading dark.

Lark brought her a torn piece of paper. It had writing on it — small, precise handwriting in a language she recognized as science. Diagrams of cell walls. Notes about fungal activity.

The scholar had been studying the thorns from the inside. He was already closer to the truth than he knew.

She folded the paper and put it in her pocket.

Chapter 11: The Offering

Elias returned to the hollow the next morning. The sky was gray, the air heavy with coming rain. He found Morwen in her garden, pulling bindweed from between the herb rows.

She did not look up.

"I brought you something," he said.

She looked at him then. Her eyes were the color of wet slate. "I don't want your money."

"It's not money."

He took the pressed flower from his pocket. The larkspur had survived the journey, though one petal had loosened. He held it out.

Morwen stared at it. Then she took it, turning it over in her fingers.

"This was your wife's."

It was not a question. He had not told her about his wife. But she knew.

"It was," he said.

"Why give it to me?"

"Because Margit said I should give you something real. And that-" he pointed at the flower. "That is the most real thing I have."

Morwen looked at the flower for a long time. Then she tucked it into her apron pocket.

"Come inside," she said. "I'll make tea."

Chapter 12: The Thorn

Morwen's cottage was small and dark. Herbs hung from the rafters in bundles. A fire crackled in the stone hearth. The fox lay by the fire, its bandaged leg stretched out.

Elias sat at the wooden table. Morwen poured hot water over dried leaves in two clay cups. The tea was bitter and earthy.

"You want to see the thorns up close," she said.

"Yes."

She sat across from him. Without speaking, she rolled up her left sleeve. Her forearm was crisscrossed with old scars. But what made Elias lean forward was the newest one — a thin black line that ran from her wrist to her elbow, rising slightly above the skin like a vein that had decided to surface.

"This is what the thorns do," she said. "When you spend too long near them."

"Can I examine it?"

She nodded.

He took a magnifying glass from his case and bent close. The black line was not scar tissue. It moved. Very slowly, like a worm beneath glass, it shifted under her skin.

"I've been cutting it back," she said. "But it grows faster now."

"Does it hurt?"

"Only when I forget to be afraid."

He looked at her. In the firelight, she looked younger than he had first thought. And more tired.

"There's something I need to show you," she said. "Tomorrow. At the old oak."

Chapter 13: The Dream

Elias returned to the inn and fell into bed exhausted. His dreams came fast and wrong.

He was walking through the forest, but the trees were made of bone. The thorns grew from the ground like fingers reaching up. At the center of the clearing stood the oak, and beneath it, a shape made of shadows and roots.

The shape had a voice like stones grinding together.

"She lies to you," it said. "She serves the curse, not the cure."

Elias tried to wake himself. He could not.

"The witch draws power from the thorns. Without them, she is nothing. She will let the forest rot before she gives up her magic."

The shape moved closer. It had no face, but Elias felt it watching him from every direction at once.

"Trust your instruments. Trust your science. She will try to make you believe in things that do not exist."

Elias woke with a gasp. The window was open. The forest was dark. His hands were shaking.

He lit a candle and wrote down the dream in his notebook. Then he crossed it out. Dreams were not data.

But the words stayed in his head. She lies to you.

He did not sleep again that night.

Chapter 14: The Binding

Morwen felt the disturbance before Lark brought the warning. Something had touched the scholar's mind. Something old and patient and cruel.

She went to the boundary under the full moon. The ritual was the same every month — she drew the circle, spoke the words, pressed her blood into the soil. The binding held the Thorn King beneath the oak. It had held for three hundred years.

Tonight, it did not hold.

She felt the resistance the moment she began. The soil pushed back. The thorns around the circle writhed, twisting upward like snakes. One caught her arm, drawing blood.

She finished the ritual anyway. The words came through gritted teeth. She poured everything she had into the seal.

The thorns stopped moving. The soil settled.

But something was wrong. The seal was thinner than before. The Thorn King had found leverage somewhere — a crack in her focus, a doubt she did not know she carried.

She went back to the cottage and cleaned the wound on her arm. The black line had grown. It reached her shoulder now.

The fox watched her from the hearth. Its leg was healing.

"One step at a time," she whispered.

Neither of them believed it.

Chapter 15: The Light

Elias could not stop thinking about the dream. He tried to dismiss it as a product of stress and unfamiliar surroundings. He ate breakfast mechanically, checked his samples again, and decided to return to the forest with better equipment.

He walked deeper than before. Past the oak. Into the part of the forest where the thorns formed tunnels overhead.

The light changed. It was no longer daylight. It was something else — a pale phosphorescence that rose from the soil in waves. He knelt and touched it. His fingers came away glowing.

He held them up. The light faded after a few seconds, but the memory of it stayed in his vision like a flash from a camera.

He took samples. The soil was normal in appearance. But under the microscope, the grains shone with a faint internal radiance.

He did not have a name for this. He did not have a category.

He sat down on a fallen log and pressed his palms to his eyes. He had spent his life believing that the world was knowable. That every phenomenon had a cause. That the universe followed rules.

The glowing soil did not follow rules.

He looked at the forest around him. The thorns pulsed with a rhythm he could almost hear.

For the first time since arriving, Dr. Elias Thorne admitted to himself that he did not know what he was dealing with.

Chapter 16: The Notebook

Lark brought Morwen the torn page while she was grinding herbs. The sparrow landed on her shoulder and dropped a scrap of paper into her hand.

She unfolded it. More of the scholar's notes. But this one was different. This one was not about cell walls or fungal theories.

It was about her.

"The witch — Morwen — carries a thorn-mark on her left arm. She claims it grows from proximity to the forest. If this is true, she is not the cause of the blight but a symptom of it. Or perhaps a vessel. Further observation required."

She read it twice. Then she folded it and tucked it into her apron.

Lark chirped a question.

"He thinks I'm part of the disease," she said. "He's not wrong."

She had told herself the same thing many times. The blood-oath bound her to the forest. The thorns grew from her blood as much as from the soil. She was the seal, but she was also the wound.

She went to the window. The scholar was walking through the trees, his head down, his notebook in his hand.

He was looking for answers. She was looking for a way out.

Neither of them had found either.

Chapter 17: The Resolve

Elias woke from another dream of the Thorn King. But this time the voice did not fill him with fear. It filled him with irritation.

He sat up and looked at the dark forest through his window.

"You're trying to manipulate me," he said aloud.

The forest said nothing.

"You want me to distrust her. That means she threatens you."

The logic was simple. If the Thorn King wanted him to turn against the witch, then the witch was the Thorn King's enemy. And Elias's enemy's enemy was worth understanding.

He dressed and packed his bag. Dawn was still an hour away. He did not care.

He walked to the hollow through the cold morning air. The thorns seemed to reach for him, but he stepped around them. He knocked on Morwen's door.

She opened it, wrapped in a shawl. Her hair was loose. She looked like she had not slept.

"I need to see the oak again," he said. "And I need you to tell me everything."

She studied his face for a long moment.

"You believe me?"

"I believe something is here. I don't know what to call it. But I'm done pretending it isn't real."

She stepped aside and let him in.

Chapter 18: The Heart

Morwen led Elias through the forest. The path she took was not the one he had followed before. It was a hidden trail, barely visible, marked by stones she had placed years ago.

The thorns were thicker here. They arched overhead, forming a tunnel that grew darker as they walked.

"The oak is the heart of the corruption," she said. "But it was not always evil. It was the first tree planted in this hollow. The Thorn King was bound to it because he loved it."

"He loved a tree?"

"He loved a woman who became a tree. But that story is older than me, and I don't know all of it."

They reached the clearing. The oak loomed before them, black and massive. Its branches twisted like arthritic fingers against the gray sky.

"This is where the seal is," she said. "Under the roots."

She led him to the base of the trunk. There, carved into the wood, was a symbol. A circle with a line through it. The edges were crusted with dried blood.

"Every full moon, I renew the seal. But it's not enough anymore."

Elias knelt and examined the symbol. He touched the blood-crust with his fingertip.

"Whose blood?"

"Mine. And the first witch's. And every witch before me."

He looked up at her. "How long has this been going on?"

"Three hundred years. My family has been the seal for three hundred years."

Elias stood and looked at the oak. For once, he had nothing to say.

Chapter 19: The Threat

The Thorn King felt them standing above him. The witch and the scholar. Together.

He had not anticipated this. The scholar was supposed to divide them, not unite them. His dreams of betrayal should have driven a wedge between them. Instead, the man had stayed.

He pressed upward against the seal. The roots creaked. The soil shifted. The symbol on the trunk glowed faintly, then dimmed. The old binding held, but it had begun to fray.

The witch was stronger than she had been. The scholar's presence had not weakened her. It had steadied her. He could feel it in the way she walked now — less weight on each step. Less resignation.

He withdrew his pressure and thought. He had been patient for three hundred years. He could be patient a little longer. But he needed a different approach. The direct attack had failed. The indirect one might still succeed.

He reached into the dark parts of the forest, through the roots that connected all things, and found the sleeping part of Elias's memory. There was a grief there, old and deep. A woman's face. A disease that had no name.

The Thorn King leaned close and whispered:

"The witch knew your wife was dying. She dreamed of it. She did nothing."

Elias stiffened in his sleep. The dream came. The seed was planted.

The Thorn King settled back into the dark and waited for it to grow.

Chapter 20: The Impossible

Elias returned to the inn with bark samples from the oak. He spent the evening at his microscope, adjusting the lens, refocusing, then adjusting again.

The oak's cellular structure was not botanical. It was not animal either. The cells were crystalline. Geometrical. They refracted light in patterns that formed tiny rainbows on the slide.

He had never seen anything like it in twenty years of study.

He wrote in his notebook: Sample O-7 — oak bark. Cellular structure suggests mineralization. Possible petrification process. But petrification took centuries, and the oak was still alive.

He looked at the sample again. The crystals pulsed faintly. He pressed his finger to the slide. It was warm.

He closed his notebook and set it aside.

The problem with impossible data was that it required impossible explanations. And the only impossible explanation available was the one he had been avoiding since he arrived.

Magic.

He said the word aloud. It sounded ridiculous in the small room.

But he could not find a better one.

He went to bed with the word still in his mouth. It tasted like copper and soil.

Chapter 21: The Oath

Morwen knew she could not keep Elias out of the truth forever. He was too observant, too methodical. He would find the cracks in her story.

Better to show him the cracks herself.

She found him at the edge of the hollow, examining a thorn patch with his magnifying glass. She sat down on a stump and waited for him to finish.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

He looked up. "I'm listening."

She rolled up her sleeve. The black line had grown again. It now branched at her elbow, sending thin tendrils toward her wrist and shoulder.

"This is the blood-oath," she said. "My grandmother made it with the first witch's spirit. The seal is tied to my bloodline. If I die, the seal breaks."

"Then you can't die."

"I can't leave. I can't stop. I can't choose anything else."

She took a knife from her belt. Before Elias could react, she drew the blade across her arm. Blood welled up, but something else came with it — a thin, black thread that emerged from the wound and curled in the air like smoke.

"The thorns grow from me," she said. "I am the prison and the prisoner."

Elias stared at the black thread as it dissolved in the air.

"I can help," he said.

"How?"

"I don't know yet. But I'm going to find out."

Chapter 22: The Wound

Elias took Morwen's arm and examined the cut. The bleeding had already stopped. The wound was closing as he watched — not with flesh, but with dark fibers that knit together like roots.

He cleaned it anyway. The motion was automatic, medical. He had done the same thing a hundred times in a hundred clinic rooms.

"Does the oath let you heal faster?" he asked.

"No. The thorns do. They don't want their vessel to die."

He applied a bandage. The dark fibers pushed against it immediately, trying to reach the air.

"You need to get this curse out of your blood."

"The curse is my blood."

He looked at her. She was not being dramatic. She was stating a fact.

"Then we need to change the blood," he said.

"That's not how magic works."

"Then maybe magic needs to learn how science works."

She almost smiled. Almost.

He held her arm a moment longer than necessary. She did not pull away.

Above them, Lark chirped a warning. The thorns at the edge of the hollow had begun to move.

Chapter 23: The Storm

The storm came without warning. One moment the sky was gray. The next, the clouds split open and rain fell in sheets that blurred the world to water.

Morwen and Elias made it to the cottage just before the downpour. The door slammed behind them. The windows rattled. The fire hissed as drops found their way down the chimney.

Lark had taken shelter in the rafters. The fox watched from its blanket, ears flat.

Morwen lit more candles. The cottage filled with warm, flickering light.

"Thank you," Elias said, "for showing me."

"I showed you because you asked."

"I could have asked the wrong way."

"You did, at first. But you changed."

He sat at the table. Rain dripped from his coat onto the floor. She hung it by the fire and poured two cups of tea.

They sat in silence for a while. The storm raged outside. The cottage held.

"I should tell you about my wife," he said.

"You don't have to."

"I know. I want to."

He told her about the wasting disease. The specialists who could not diagnose it. The months of decline, the final week, the empty bed.

Morwen listened without interrupting.

"When she died," he finished, "I stopped believing in anything I couldn't prove. It was safer."

"And now?"

He looked at the fire. "Now I'm not sure what's safe anymore."

Chapter 24: The Warning

Lark's song cut through the storm — sharp, urgent, wrong.

Morwen was on her feet before the sound finished. She threw open the door. Rain lashed her face. The forest was alive with movement.

The thorns had breached the inner boundary.

She grabbed her iron knife and ran. Elias followed without question.

They found the breach at the back of the garden. The thorns had pushed through the salt line in a dozen places, sending black shoots toward the cottage walls. They moved slowly but deliberately, like fingers reaching for something.

Morwen began cutting. The thorns resisted the iron, hissing where the blade touched them.

Elias grabbed a fallen branch and used it to beat the thorns back. It was not elegant. It was not scientific. But it worked.

They worked together through the rain, cutting and pulling and throwing the severed thorns into a pile. Morwen's hands bled. Elias's coat was torn.

By the time they finished, dawn was breaking. The storm had passed. The garden was scarred but intact.

They stood together, breathing hard, covered in mud and blood and thorn-sap.

"We held," she said.

"For now," he said.

They went inside. Morwen built up the fire. Elias did not let go of the branch.

Chapter 25: The Night Work

They worked through the night. The fire burned high. Morwen stitched the garden back together with salt and iron filings while Elias reinforced the cottage's defenses — boarding the windows, sealing the cracks.

The thorns had tested the boundary. They had found weak points. Now Morwen had to find them first, before the Thorn King did.

She worked in silence, her hands moving automatically. Elias watched her from across the room.

"Tell me what to do," he said.

"Watch the windows. If the thorns come through, burn them."

He sat by the window with a candle and a stack of kindling. The forest was dark. The thorns were patient.

"You've been doing this alone for ten years," he said.

"Longer. The first year was the hardest."

"And now?"

"Now I'm used to it."

He looked at the black line on her arm. It had grown again.

"You shouldn't have to be used to it."

She did not answer. She kept working.

But the words stayed with her. They stayed for the rest of the night, and into the dawn, and beyond.

Chapter 26: The Laughter

Under the oak, in the dark where no moonlight reached, the Thorn King laughed.

The sound did not travel through air. It traveled through root and soil and stone, a vibration that reached every living thing in the forest. The trees shivered. The animals hid in their burrows. The birds stopped singing.

The witch and the scholar had fought his thorns. They had pushed them back from the cottage, sealed the breaches, repaired the garden. They had won.

He did not care.

Winning a battle did not win the war. He had been fighting this war for three centuries. He had learned patience. He had learned to take the long view.

He had felt them working together in the garden. Felt their breath synchronize as they cut and pulled and fought. Their hands had moved in the same rhythm. He had felt something else too — a warmth between them that was not survival instinct. Something softer. Something that could be exploited.

He knew what that warmth could become. He had seen it before, in the first witch and her lover. It had made them vulnerable. It had made them careless. It had given him the opening he needed then, and it would give him one now.

The Thorn King settled deeper into his prison and smiled.

"Grow close," he whispered into the dark. "Grow very close. It will make the ending hurt so much more."

Chapter 27: The Discovery

Elias examined the soil samples again, using the moonlight that streamed through his window. Something had been nagging at him since the storm. The salt had worked in the garden, but it had worked unevenly — stronger in some places than others.

He isolated a salt compound from the soil near the boundary line. When he mixed it with a scraping of thorn tissue, the thorn disintegrated. Rapidly. Completely. He watched through his microscope as the cells collapsed.

He repeated the experiment with different concentrations. The result was consistent. He measured the time to disintegration at each concentration and graphed it.

Then he tried moonlight. Not directly — but he left a sample on the windowsill overnight and tested it in the morning. The moon-exposed salt had a different crystalline structure. More reactive. He photographed it through the lens.

He ran through the forest to find Morwen.

"The salt works better when it's been in moonlight," he said.

She looked at him blankly. She had been grinding herbs, her hands dusty with powder.

"The photons from the moon change the salt's structure. It becomes more effective at breaking down the thorns."

"You want to weaponize moonlight."

"I want to weaponize science. The moon is a tool. The salt is a tool. We use what we have."

She considered this, brushing the herb dust from her hands. Then she went to her shelves and pulled down a jar of salt.

"Show me."

Chapter 28: The Names

Elias stayed in the hollow. It was not a decision he made consciously. He simply did not leave.

He helped Morwen with the garden. He learned which herbs did what. He catalogued the patterns of the thorns — when they moved, how they moved, what made them retreat.

But what changed him was the forest.

Morwen walked it with him, naming things. Not the scientific names he knew. The old names. The ones the trees answered to.

"This is Aelwen," she said, touching a birch. "She remembers the first snow."

"This is Cael," she said of a rowan. "He guards the west path."

Elias wrote the names in his notebook. He drew maps of the tree-spirits, their territories, their relationships.

"I am a scientist," he told himself. "I am collecting data."

But the data did not explain why the birch seemed to lean toward Morwen when she spoke. Or why the rowan's branches dropped a flower at his feet.

He started leaving small offerings. Berries. Bits of bread. He did not tell Morwen.

She noticed anyway. She said nothing.

Chapter 29: The Journal

Elias wrote in his journal every night. It was the only habit he had kept from his old life — the discipline of recording, of making sense through language.

"Day 14. The thorns have reactive cellular structures unlike any known organism. They respond to salt, iron, and — inexplicably — to lunar phase. I have observed Morwen's blood-oath mark growing at a rate of approximately 2mm per day. If the growth continues linearly, she has roughly six months before the thorns reach her heart."

He paused. Six months. He underlined it. Then he underlined it again.

"Day 15. The old oak's crystalline bark shows no analog in botanical literature. I sent a sample to the Institute. They responded that the sample was 'contaminated' and requested a replacement. I have not responded."

He stared at that line. The Institute had been his home for fifteen years. He had published in their journals. He had won their awards. They had trained him to doubt everything except the scientific method. And now they were telling him his evidence was contaminated because it did not fit their framework.

"Day 17. I am beginning to understand the forest's language. Not literally. But I can predict where the thorns will move based on the wind and the phase of the moon. Morwen calls this 'listening.' I call it pattern recognition. The difference is semantic."

He dipped his pen and wrote the last entry.

"Day 19. I do not want to leave."

He closed the journal and set it aside. The last line embarrassed him. It was not scientific.

But it was true.

Chapter 30: The Visit

Old Margit came to the hollow. She arrived at midday, carrying a basket of bread and dried meat. She looked at the cottage, the garden, the fresh salt lines.

"You've had company," she said.

"The storm pushed the thorns through."

"I meant him."

Morwen took the basket. "He's been helpful."

"I can see that."

They sat on the porch. Lark perched on the roof, watching both of them.

"The village boundary is failing," Margit said. "The thorns reached the churchyard last night. We held them back with torches and shovels, but we can't do that forever."

Morwen closed her eyes. "I know."

"You can only hold this alone for so long, girl."

"I'm not alone anymore."

Margit looked at her sharply. Then she smiled — a rare thing, like frost melting.

"No," she said. "You're not."

She left the basket and walked back to the village. Morwen watched her go.

Elias came out of the cottage. "Was that good news or bad?"

"Both."

"Good."

She handed him a piece of the bread. They ate in silence, watching the forest.

Chapter 31: The Limit

Morwen sat alone that night and faced the truth she had been avoiding for months.

The blood-oath was failing. She could feel it in her bones, in the thorns that grew from her arm, in the soil that no longer answered her the way it used to. The forest was slipping away from her.

She had poured herself into the seal for ten years. She had given blood and sleep and hope. She had given the sister she could have saved and the life she could have lived. And it was not enough.

The Thorn King was older than her. Older than her grandmother. Older than the first witch. He had time. He had patience. He had the weight of centuries on his side.

She looked around the cottage. The herbs on the rafters. The fire in the hearth. The bed she had not shared with anyone. She had a cottage, a sparrow, and a botanist who believed in moonlight salt.

She pressed her hands to the floor and felt the pulse of the forest beneath. The Thorn King was gathering his strength. She could feel him coiling like a spring.

She went to the window. Elias was outside, examining a thorn under his portable microscope, his brow furrowed, his lips moving as he narrated his observations to himself.

"What if I can't hold?" she said quietly.

The forest did not answer. The wind did not answer. Only the fox, still healing by the fire, lifted its head and looked at her with dark, knowing eyes.

Chapter 32: The Proposal

Elias found Morwen at the oak the next morning. She was sitting at the base of the trunk, her hand pressed to the carved symbol.

"The seal is thinning," she said.

He sat beside her. "I've been thinking about alternatives."

"Alternatives to what?"

"Alternatives to you dying."

She looked at him.

He pulled a notebook from his pocket and opened it. Diagrams filled the pages — fungal life cycles, crystalline structures, chemical compounds.

"I isolated a microbe from the phosphorescent soil. It feeds on thorn tissue. When I cultured it with the oak bark, it attacked the crystalline cells."

"The oak is the seal."

"I know. But what if we introduced the microbe at the root of the oak? Let it consume the Thorn King's prison from the outside while we reinforce the seal from the inside?"

Morwen stared at the diagrams. "You want to use a fungus against a curse."

"I want to use biology against a curse. The curse is a biological system. It lives in cells. The microbe attacks those cells."

She was quiet for a long time.

"How do we reinforce the seal from the inside?"

He met her eyes. "I don't know yet. But I thought we could figure it out together."

Chapter 33: The Briars

The Thorn King struck that night.

Elias was asleep in the cottage's second room when the thorns came through the wall. Not the outer wall — the inner wall, as if they had grown through the wood from the forest into his chest.

He woke with a briar wrapped around his throat. It was tightening. He could not breathe.

He clawed at it. The thorns dug into his hands. Blood ran down his neck.

Then Morwen was there. Her iron knife flashed. The briar severed. She grabbed his arm and pulled him out of the bed.

The thorns were everywhere — crawling across the ceiling, down the walls, reaching for them both.

"Out!" she shouted.

They ran. The cottage door was blocked. She kicked through the back window and pulled him through. Glass cut her arms. She did not feel it.

They fell into the garden, gasping. The cottage behind them was alive with black movement.

"He knows," Morwen said. "He heard you. He's afraid of what you'll do."

Elias touched his throat. His fingers came away bloody.

"Good," he said.

Chapter 34: The Argument

They argued at dawn. The garden was wrecked. The cottage was compromised. Elias's samples were lost inside.

"I should have told you before I started the cultures," he said.

"You should have told me before you started anything."

"I was trying to help."

"You were trying to fix it. That's different."

They stood facing each other across the ruined garden. Lark watched from the fence. The fox had limped to the edge of the forest and stopped, unwilling to go further.

"If I had told you, you would have said no."

"Of course I would have said no! You were experimenting with the seal. The seal is the only thing keeping the Thorn King contained."

"And it's failing."

She had no answer to that.

"I'm not trying to replace your magic, Morwen. I'm trying to give it something it can work with."

She looked at the cottage. The thorns had receded, but they had left their marks — black veins in the wood, cracks in the walls.

"If we do this," she said slowly, "we do it together. Every step."

"Together," he agreed.

Chapter 35: The Isolation

Elias built a new lab in the woodshed. Morwen helped him clear the space, reinforce the walls, seal the cracks. They worked side by side, handing each other tools without needing to speak.

He set up his microscope and his cultures on a plank he had sanded smooth. The light came through a gap in the roof boards, focused by a lens he had improvised from a magnifying glass.

The microbe grew well. It was a fungus, technically — a slender white mold that consumed thorn tissue with remarkable efficiency. Under the microscope, it moved like a hunting animal, extending tendrils toward the black cells and dissolving them.

Morwen watched him work. She learned the names of his tools. She learned to hold the slides without breaking them. She learned to recognize when the cultures were healthy and when they were contaminated.

"You're a good teacher," she said.

"You're a good student."

They worked in comfortable silence. The thorns stayed away from the woodshed. The wind carried their voices to the forest, and the forest listened.

Elias looked up from his microscope and rubbed his eyes. "The microbe is ready. We can introduce it tomorrow."

Morwen nodded. She did not say what she was thinking.

Tomorrow would change everything.

Chapter 36: The Divination

Morwen performed the divination alone. She did not tell Elias. Some things a witch did not share.

She sat in the ruined cottage, in the center of the circle she had drawn with ash and salt from the pantry. The candles burned low. The air was still and cold. She closed her eyes and let her mind sink into the dark.

She asked the question: What must be sacrificed?

The answer came not in words but in images. A sapling growing from ash. A hand reaching for another hand in the dark. A door opening onto a forest she did not recognize — green and alive, full of light.

And then a voice, ancient and tired, speaking words that were not her mother's or her grandmother's but the first witch's own. She recognized it the way you recognize your own heartbeat.

"Something precious. Something you have just begun to keep."

Morwen opened her eyes. Her hands were trembling.

She knew what the divination meant. The forest would not be saved by more blood or more sacrifice. It would be saved by something she had only just found. Something she was not ready to give up.

She sat in the dark for a long time, watching the candles burn down to nothing, trying to find the courage for what came next.

Chapter 37: The Confession

Elias found her on the porch. The moon was full. The forest was silver and shadow.

"I need to tell you something," he said.

She did not look at him. "So tell me."

"My wife's name was Anna. She died of a disease that had no name and no cure. I spent three years trying to find it. I failed."

"I know."

"I couldn't save her. And when I came here, I thought I could save the forest. I thought it would make up for her."

"Has it?"

"No. But I don't think I came here to save the forest anymore."

She looked at him then.

"Why did you come?"

He sat beside her. The porch creaked under his weight.

"I came here because I was lost. And I think I found something."

She did not ask what. She did not need to.

They sat together in the moonlight. The thorns at the edge of the hollow rustled. The wind carried the sound of the oak creaking in the dark.

Something precious, the first witch had said.

Morwen looked at the man beside her. She understood now what the sacrifice would be.

Chapter 38: The Sister

Morwen told Elias about her sister in the quiet hours before dawn.

"She was twelve. I was fourteen. The thorns took her."

Elias did not interrupt.

"We were playing in the forest. We knew the rules — never cross the salt lines, never touch the black thorns, never go near the oak. But she was curious. She was always curious."

Morwen's voice was flat. She had told this story once before, to her grandmother's ghost. She had not told it since.

"She touched the oak. Just put her palm against the bark. The thorns came out of the wood and wrapped around her wrist. I tried to pull her free. They cut me. They cut her."

"What happened?"

"My grandmother found us. She cut the thorns away, but the poison was already in Lily's blood. It took three days."

"And the blood-oath?"

"I took it the day Lily died. I thought if I could hold the seal, no one else would lose anyone to the forest."

Elias reached for her hand. She let him take it.

"You were fourteen," he said.

"I was old enough."

"You were a child."

She did not argue. She did not pull away.

The fox came to the porch and lay at her feet. Lark settled on her shoulder.

She was not as alone as she had been.

Chapter 39: The Bones

Lark led them to the hidden grove. The path was invisible from the forest floor — a narrow corridor between thorns that Morwen had never noticed before.

The grove was a circle of standing stones, moss-covered and ancient. At the center lay a skeleton. Human. Female. The bones glowed with a faint silver light.

"The first witch," Morwen said.

She knelt beside the remains. The bones were warm. They hummed with a power that had not faded in three centuries.

"This is where she died," Elias said.

"This is where she bound the Thorn King. She gave her life to seal him. Her bones hold the second seal."

Morwen touched the skull. The silver light flared.

"You have her blood," Elias said.

"I have her curse. And her purpose."

"No. You have her magic. That's different."

Morwen looked at him. He was not speaking as a scientist. He was speaking as a man who had seen too much to doubt.

"The microbe and her bones," he said. "Science and magic. Together."

She understood.

She pressed her palm to the skull and felt the first witch's spirit stir.

"We're coming," she said.

Chapter 40: The Fusion

They prepared the ritual on a moonless night.

Morwen built the circle in the clearing before the oak. She used ash and salt and water from the hidden spring. She placed the first witch's bones at the four cardinal points.

Elias brought his culture of the microbe. It had grown into a silver-gold mold that pulsed with a light of its own.

"We do this together," she said.

"Together."

She cut her palm. The blood welled up, black-tinged with the thorn poison. She held her hand over the microbe culture.

Elias took her wrist. He pressed his own hand to hers, palm to palm, mixing their blood.

"The words," she said.

She spoke them. The old words, the binding words, the words the first witch had spoken under this same sky.

Elias spoke them after her, stumbling over the syllables. His voice was uncertain. But it was there.

The microbe met her blood. The reaction was immediate — silver light exploded from the culture dish, racing up their arms, across the circle, into the soil.

The oak screamed.

The seal was changing.

Chapter 41: The Breach

The Thorn King felt the light before he understood it. It burned. Not like iron or salt — those were surface pains. This was a fire in the root of his being, something that reached into the oldest part of him and pulled.

He threw everything against the seal. Every root. Every thorn. Every ounce of hatred he had hoarded for three centuries. He did not hold back. He could not afford to.

The seal cracked.

The sound was like rock splitting. Black vines erupted from the base of the oak, tearing through the soil, climbing the surrounding trees. They moved faster than Morwen had ever seen — faster than a man could run — racing toward the village.

"Elias!" she shouted.

He saw. He was already running. "Get to the village. Warn them."

"What about you?"

"I'll hold the circle. Go."

He hesitated. The thorns rose around them like a tide. They were up to his knees now, his waist.

"Go!"

He ran. The thorns chased him. He stumbled, caught himself, and kept going.

Morwen turned back to the oak. She pressed her bleeding palm to the symbol on the trunk. The silver light flared, then dimmed. The black was stronger.

The Thorn King was escaping.

And she was running out of blood.

Chapter 42: The Run

Elias ran faster than he had ever run in his life. The thorns chased him. They erupted from the soil behind him, beside him, ahead of him. He dodged left, then right, then leaped over a vine that rose to catch his ankle.

The village lights appeared through the trees. He burst into the clearing, gasping.

The thorns were already there.

They had climbed the walls of the inn. They wrapped around the well like black snakes. They crawled across the church roof, feeling for gaps in the stone. The villagers stood in the square with torches and pitchforks, fighting a retreating battle.

Margit saw him first. Her face was streaked with soot. "Where is she?"

"Holding the circle. The seal is breaking."

"The whole forest—"

"I know."

He grabbed a torch from a farmer's hand. The man did not resist. Elias stood beside the villagers. The thorns advanced, then paused, then advanced again.

They fought through the night. Elias used everything he had — salt packs from his bag, his iron forceps, the last of his microbe culture. He threw it at a wall of thorns and watched it eat through them, silver light flaring where it touched.

But there were too many. For every vine they burned, ten more grew.

The village was losing.

Chapter 43: The Arrow

Morwen felt the arrow before she saw it. A thorn — long and sharp as a spear — shot from the split in the oak and struck her in the side.

She fell. The pain was immediate and deep. It pushed through her ribs, through the space between her lungs, and settled somewhere she could not reach.

The black poison spread through her blood. She felt it climbing her veins, reaching for her heart. Her fingers went numb. Her vision blurred at the edges.

She kept her hand on the symbol. The silver light was fading. The wood felt cold now, dead.

Lark flew down and landed on her chest. His chirps were frantic, insistent. He pecked at her collar, trying to pull her upright.

"Go," she whispered. Her voice was a thread. "Find him. Go."

Lark hesitated. His black eyes met hers. Then he shot into the sky like an arrow of his own.

She lay alone at the base of the oak. The Thorn King's laughter vibrated through the soil, through her bones.

The seal was dissolving. She could feel it going, thread by thread, like a rope fraying in water.

She pressed harder. The symbol burned her palm. The pain kept her conscious.

"Not yet," she said. "Not yet."

Chapter 44: The Return

Lark found Elias at the village line. The sparrow dove at his face, screeching a sound that cut through the chaos of battle. He dodged the bird's claws, and Lark flew a tight circle, then shot back toward the forest.

He understood.

He left the village without a word. Margit called after him. He did not stop.

The thorns were everywhere now, but Lark led him through gaps he could not see — weaving between black walls, over fallen branches, under vines that would have caught a larger target. Elias followed blindly, trusting the bird.

They reached the clearing. The split oak loomed against the sky. And at its base, Morwen lay crumpled, her hand still pressed to the fading symbol on the trunk.

Her face was white. Too white.

The thorn protruded from her side. The wood around the wound was black.

"No," he said.

He knelt beside her. His hands were shaking. He had nothing. No tools. No medicine. No bandages. Nothing but the last culture of the microbe, still warm in his pocket, still glowing with that impossible silver light.

He pulled out the dish. The silver-gold mold pulsed.

He had no idea if this would kill her.

He poured it directly into her wound anyway.

Chapter 45: The Reaction

The microbe met the thorn poison. The reaction was not gentle.

Morwen's body arched off the ground. Silver light erupted from the wound — not from the surface, but from deep inside. It poured from her eyes, her mouth, the pores of her skin. The black line on her arm writhed like a living thing, then began to recede.

Elias held her down. He kept his hands on her shoulders. He did not let go.

The silver light spread through the circle. It climbed the oak's trunk, finding the cracks in the bark, following the channels where the Thorn King's power had flowed for centuries. It reached the carved symbol and burned brighter than the sun.

The oak groaned. The soil trembled. Roots shifted deep underground.

Morwen's eyes opened. They were silver, pupil and iris and white all the same luminous color.

"I can see him," she said. Her voice was not her own.

"Who?"

"The Thorn King. I can see where he's bound. I can see the knot."

She reached up and pressed her hand to the oak. The silver light poured into the wood, into the symbol, into the heart of the tree.

"Help me," she said.

Elias put his hand beside hers on the trunk. The light burned his palm.

He did not move.

The oak split from crown to root in a single, clean line.

Chapter 46: The Words

They spoke the words together.

Morwen's voice was clear now, strengthened by the silver light that flowed through her. She did not stumble over the old syllables. She knew them the way she knew her own breath.

Elias's voice followed hers. He did not know the language. He mimicked the sounds, finding the rhythm of the old tongue through repetition. His voice was rough, uncertain. But it was there.

The first witch's bones rose from the circle. They lifted into the air, shedding silver like water, orbiting the split oak in a slow spiral.

The oak cracked open further. Inside was a hollow space — a chamber that had been sealed for three centuries. In that space was a shape. It was made of root and thorn and the fragments of human bone. It had been a man once, three hundred years ago.

The Thorn King.

He had no face. But he had eyes. They burned red in the dark, fixed on Morwen with a hatred so old it had calcified into something else entirely.

"You cannot destroy me," he said. His voice was the sound of branches breaking.

"We're not destroying you," Morwen said. "We're releasing you."

She pressed the silver light into his core, her hand passing through the thorn-wall of his chest.

The Thorn King screamed.

The scream lasted a long time. It shook the forest. It shook the village a mile away. It shook the earth itself, rattling stones in the soil.

And then it stopped.

The bones fell to the ground. The silver light faded.

The oak was empty.

Chapter 47: The Splitting

The oak split fully, from crown to root, in a clean line. The two halves fell apart, each landing with a sound like thunder rolling through the earth. The hollow was open to the sky for the first time in living memory.

The silver light spread from the split like water from a broken dam. It flowed across the forest floor, touching every thorn, every root, every patch of black soil. It rose up the surrounding trees and dripped from their branches.

The thorns withered. They did not burn or break. They simply dried and crumbled, turning to gray ash that the wind carried away like snow. Where the ash fell, green shoots pushed through the soil.

The black vines on the village walls collapsed. The soil stopped steaming. The purple-black discoloration faded from the bark of the trees.

In the clearing, Morwen watched the transformation with silver-blind eyes. She felt the blood-oath dissolving inside her chest. It was like a knot untying — a tension she had carried so long she had forgotten it was there, releasing all at once. She gasped.

The oak's two halves settled. The forest was quiet.

Lark landed on the fallen trunk and sang. The song was unlike any he had made before. It rose and fell in patterns that felt ancient and new at the same time.

The forest had not heard that song in three hundred years.

Chapter 48: The Green

Dawn came. The forest was changed.

The thorns were gone. The soil that had been black was now brown, then green. Shoots pushed through — grass, ferns, wildflowers. Trees that had been dead began to bud.

Elias walked through the hollow in disbelief. The samples he had taken, the data he had recorded — none of it described this.

Morwen sat on the porch. Her arm was pale and clean. The black line was gone.

"It worked," she said.

"It worked."

The fox emerged from the cottage. It tested its leg, then walked into the forest without looking back.

"Is the Thorn King gone?" Elias asked.

"Not gone. Released. He was never a king. He was a wound that had not been allowed to heal."

"A wound?"

"Betrayal. Grief. The things the first witch could not let go. She bound them into the oak, and they became a spirit."

"And now?"

"Now the forest can heal. Properly. For the first time."

Elias sat beside her. The sun was warm. The air smelled of new leaves.

"What happens now?" he asked.

She looked at him.

"I have no idea."

Chapter 49: The Freedom

Morwen felt the absence of the oath as a physical thing. The constant pressure in her chest — the weight she had carried so long she had forgotten it was there — was gone.

She walked through the forest without feeling the thorns pull. She passed the oak's split trunk without the symbol calling her blood.

She was free.

It should have felt triumphant. It felt terrifying.

She went to the cottage and looked at her reflection in the window. She looked older than she remembered. Softer around the edges. The black line on her arm was gone, leaving clean skin that had not seen sunlight in a decade.

She could leave. She could walk to the village and buy bread without anyone asking why. She could take a coach to the city. She could go anywhere.

She looked at the split oak. She looked at the garden. She looked at the woodshed, where Elias was packing his microscope.

She did not want to go anywhere.

She went to the woodshed and stood in the doorway.

"Where will you go?" she asked.

He turned. His face was unreadable.

"The Institute will want a report."

"Will you give them one?"

"Not the real one."

She waited.

"I was thinking," he said slowly, "that the forest will need someone to watch it. Now that the curse is gone. Someone who knows the old names."

"I thought you were a scientist."

"I am. And scientists observe. It's my job to observe this forest as it heals."

"That could take years."

"Yes."

He looked at her.

"I hope it does."

Chapter 50: The Sapling

They planted the sapling together. It was a young oak, no taller than Morwen's knee, dug from a healthy grove on the other side of the hollow.

Elias dug the hole. Morwen placed the roots. Together, they filled the earth back in.

They stood back. The sapling looked small against the empty space where the old oak had stood. But it was alive. It leaned toward the sun.

"The first witch planted the old oak," Morwen said. "She chose this place because the soil was rich and the water ran clean."

"She chose well."

"It took three hundred years for that choice to become a prison."

Elias did not answer. There was nothing to say.

Lark landed on the sapling's highest branch. The branch bent under his weight, then sprang back.

"An omen," Morwen said.

"Good or bad?"

"Just an omen. We'll find out which in time."

Elias smiled. It was a small smile, a careful one. But it was real.

Morwen took his hand. They stood together at the edge of the hollow, watching the forest wake.

The hollow had held its secrets long enough. The sun was rising. The thorns were gone.

They did not let go.
