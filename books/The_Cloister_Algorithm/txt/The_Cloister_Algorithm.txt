Chapter 1: Blue Scanner Light

The abbey library was dark except for a blue scanner line moving under a locked door. Dr. Elian Morse stopped in the cloister walk with rain on his coat and watched the light pass, return, pass again.

Sister Mara Vale appeared beside him with a key in her hand. "No scans were scheduled tonight."

"Then the machine is praying on its own."

She did not smile. Good librarians saved humor for rooms that deserved it.

Inside, the overhead lights flicked on in strips, one after another, and the long reading room lifted out of shadow. Oak tables waited under green-shaded lamps. The old catalogue drawers gave off the dry odor of paper, brass, and wax. At the far end, behind the new security glass, the digitization suite looked like a surgical room that had lost its patient.

The scanner bed was warm. Elian held his hand an inch above the glass and felt the faint stored heat of recent work. A vellum cradle stood open beside it. No manuscript lay in the cradle, but a strand of pale fiber clung to the scanner glass, caught in the corner where the black gasket met the lid.

"Do not touch that," he said.

Mara stopped with her hand already reaching toward it. "I was not going to."

"You were thinking about it."

"I think about many foolish things. It does not make them policy."

He photographed the fiber from three angles, then photographed the scanner control panel, the lid, the empty cradle, the floor, and the door. The machine's status light pulsed a patient blue. The room was cold enough that his breath might have shown if the lamps were not so bright.

"Who has access after hours?"

"Myself, Prior Olan, the digitization contractor, two staff librarians, and security." Mara's voice had become formal. "The visiting consultants are supposed to request supervised access."

"Supposed to?"

"This abbey survives on exceptions. That is not the same as approving them."

Elian opened his notebook. He had come to Saint Oran's Abbey to audit a set of digital transcriptions being used in a land dispute. He had expected bad labels, overconfident machine output, perhaps a lazy vendor. He had not expected a scanner operating behind a locked door at midnight in a building whose stones were older than most countries' property law.

The rain struck the high windows in little bursts. Beyond them the cloister garth was black, except where water shone on the paving stones.

Mara went to the terminal and woke the screen. It asked for a password. She typed without looking down. The recent jobs panel appeared, then vanished, replaced by a blank error window.

"That is new," she said.

"Good or bad?"

"New is bad until explained."

He liked her for that. He had built a career around the same principle.

The job log showed one scan session beginning at 11:43 p.m. and ending at 12:58 a.m. The operator field was empty. The file path ended in a string of saint names and dates: oran_blue/brigid_final/columba_gold. Pretty names, chosen by someone who wanted archival work to sound devotional even while it behaved like software.

"Were any leaves scheduled for rescanning?"

"No." Mara looked past him to the open cradle. "The disputed charters are under preservation hold. I ordered no movement until you arrived."

He copied the log to a clean drive and generated a hash. The ritual steadied the room. Name the evidence. Fix the time. Make a small fence around what can still be known.

Mara watched his hands. "You were hired to audit files."

"Files begin somewhere," he answered. "Usually with hands."

"And if the hands belong to someone inside?"

He sealed the fiber in an evidence envelope and wrote the time in block letters. "Then we learn that before anyone else teaches us a cleaner story."

She looked at the locked cabinets along the north wall. The abbey kept its charters there, each in a folder, each folder in a box, each box in a catalogue whose trust depended on ordinary people following ordinary rules. Her face was calm, but the skin around her mouth had tightened.

"I will pull the handling registers," she said.

"Now?"

"If someone used my room in the dark, I do not owe them a comfortable morning."

They moved together through the reading room, their footsteps sounding too loud on the old boards. Behind them, the scanner light blinked blue, idle and innocent in the manner of machines.

Chapter 2: Common Meadow

Iris Senn spread the county map across the guesthouse table. The disputed meadow lay below the abbey wall, green even in poor weather because a millstream split under it.

"Families have grazed there for centuries," she said. "The developer says the charters show abbey fee simple ownership with no common use."

"And the machine agrees?"

"With ninety-eight percent confidence."

Elian disliked confidence before breakfast.

The guesthouse smelled of damp wool, coffee, and old radiators. Someone had left a bowl of apples on the sideboard, each one polished to a red that looked improbable in the gray morning. Iris had taken the largest table and covered it with documents before Elian arrived: vellum photographs, modern title summaries, planning notices, hydrology studies, and a glossy brochure showing Calder Pike's proposed data campus in clean aerial renderings.

On the brochure, the meadow had been flattened into a pale green rectangle. The millstream was a decorative blue line. Cooling ponds sat where cattle had crossed in winter. A service road cut through the old lower gate.

"They call it underused land," Iris said.

"Most land is underused if you do not count the people using it."

She gave him a quick look. "Do you testify like that?"

"Only when someone else has already lost."

Iris was the county solicitor, compact and direct, with a raincoat folded over the chair behind her. She had the strained posture of a lawyer who had slept in a bad hotel and dreamed in exhibit numbers. A yellow pencil held her hair in a loose knot. Her left hand kept returning to the map, smoothing one corner that would not lie flat.

"The abbey needs money," she said. "The roof is failing. The kitchen wing is half closed. Calder is offering an endowment large enough to make everyone forgive the footprint."

"Everyone except the commoners."

"We do not use that word in pleadings unless we want opposing counsel to smirk."

"What do you use?"

"Households with appurtenant pasture and water rights."

"Less smirking, more syllables."

"Law is built on that exchange."

Elian drank coffee that had been left too long on a hot plate. It tasted punitive but useful. He studied the old map Iris had weighted with a salt shaker. The meadow lay between abbey wall and low fields, with a path that crossed from village lane to millstream. Names clustered there: Lower Meadow, Hay Gate, Summer Cut, Common Brook. Practical names. People named what they needed to find again.

"Where are the charters?"

"In the abbey vault and in the model output."

"Then we will begin with the one that can be held."

Iris slid a folder toward him. "Before we do, you should understand the politics. Calder's company has already bought options on surrounding parcels. The planning committee likes jobs. The abbey's donors like tech money because it arrives without the odor of ordinary commerce. The village is divided because some people see a meadow and some see their children leaving for work elsewhere."

"And you?"

"I see a clause being read too conveniently."

That was enough for him. He did not require purity in clients. Suspicion, properly supported, could do good work.

He opened the folder. The contested transcription rendered a damaged Latin sentence as exclusive abbey ownership over pasture and water. The model report gave a confidence score to one decimal place. It cited "normalized abbreviation expansion" and "contextual estate inference."

"Contextual estate inference," Elian said.

"Is that bad?"

"It is the kind of phrase that asks you to stop asking what happened."

Outside, rain pressed against the windows like static. Through the glass he could see the slope falling toward the meadow, though the meadow itself was hidden by mist. A line of fence posts stood dark against the wet grass. Beyond them, the millstream moved under alder branches, carrying yesterday's rain toward the lower fields.

Iris tapped the model report. "If the charters say what Calder says they say, we have a hard fight. If the algorithm made a mistake, we have an expensive mistake. If someone shaped the mistake, we have something else."

"We do not start with something else."

"No?"

"We start with the paper, the images, the logs, and the people who touched them. Something else will introduce itself if it wants attention."

She smiled for the first time, not warmly but with professional relief. "Mara said you were unbearable."

"She has known me less than a day."

"She is quick."

He gathered the charter photographs, leaving the brochure untouched. In its perfect rendering, the data center glowed at sunset, every roof clean, every pond blue, the abbey preserved as a tasteful neighbor to power consumption. No mud. No stream narrowed by summer. No old households explaining that access was not sentiment but survival.

The old meadow was not romantic. It was water, hay, passage, habit, and memory made practical. That made it harder to sell and easier to steal.

Chapter 3: Training Set

Talia Reed waited in the server closet with a laptop under one arm and the expression of an engineer who had warned everyone too early.

"My model did not produce those readings," she said.

"Whose did?"

"A retrained build. Same name, different weights. Management called it cleanup."

The server closet had once been a linen room. A strip of faded wallpaper survived behind the rack, patterned with blue flowers now browned by heat. Fans pushed warm air against Elian's face. Cables hung in labeled bundles from the ceiling tray. Somebody had taped a saint card to the battery backup, perhaps as joke, perhaps as nervous insurance.

Talia set her laptop on an upturned crate. She was in her thirties, sharp-shouldered, with cropped black hair and a tired engineer's habit of answering the question she wished management had asked. Her badge said SCRIPTORIUM AI PROJECT. Someone had drawn a small frown under the logo.

"Start at the beginning," Elian said.

"The beginning is that no one wanted a model. They wanted a miracle. I built a transcription assistant."

"Difference?"

"An assistant says, I am uncertain here, ask Jonas. A miracle says, Congratulations, your land problem is solved."

She woke the laptop and opened a comparison dashboard. Two columns appeared. In the left, a March build marked abbreviations in amber and attached warnings: possible common pasture clause; paleographic review required. In the right, a May build rendered the same line as exclusive abbey possession and assigned a confidence score above ninety-eight percent.

"Who retrained it?"

"That is the trick. The repository says the build pipeline did. The build pipeline says an external review package was merged. The merge ticket says cleanup. The cleanup notes say deprecated noisy hands removed."

"Noisy hands."

"Scribes with inconsistent abbreviation patterns. Except one of those hands wrote all the meadow charters."

Elian leaned closer. The log lines moved in their neat indifferent order. He had seen fraud written in panicked emails, in spreadsheets with hidden columns, in procurement systems where the approving officer had the same home address as the vendor. Code was different only in costume. It preserved tiny choices with excellent manners.

The first model flagged uncertainty around abbreviated Latin and scribal hands. The second flattened uncertainty into ownership language favorable to Calder Pike's development company. The change was not one error. It was a procession.

"Who authorized retraining?"

"The abbey contract manager and Calder's technical consultant."

"Name?"

"Not in the ticket. Just CP-EXT."

Elian wrote it down. Hidden names were rarely accidental in systems designed by people who knew how logs worked.

Talia rubbed her eyes with the heel of one hand. "I complained."

"To whom?"

"Project channel. Then direct to Olan. Then to Calder's project manager, which was stupid."

"Because?"

"Because they stopped inviting me to meetings and left my name on the public demo."

The fans filled the room while that settled. On the rack, green and blue LEDs blinked with the democracy of all machines doing exactly what they had been instructed to do.

"Do you still have the March model?"

She hesitated. Not long enough to be false. Long enough to measure the risk of truth. "Yes."

"Where?"

"Not here."

"Good."

"You are not supposed to say good when I admit to keeping unauthorized backups."

"I audit consequences. I do not offer moral symmetry to overwrites."

Her mouth twitched. "It is in my home directory under recipes."

"Of course it is."

"No one searches recipes until after disaster."

He copied the available logs to his evidence drive. Talia watched the progress bar and spoke more softly.

"The system was designed to be uncertain. That was the whole point. Medieval documents are damaged, abbreviated, contradictory. A useful model slows people down at the right places. What they deployed speeds up exactly where it should stop."

"Can you prove that?"

"If I can show the original outputs and the retraining changes, yes. But they will say version drift. They will say model improvement. They will say the new build learned from more data."

"Did it?"

"It learned from less data. Cleaner data. Convenient data."

Behind the server rack, a chapel bell rang once, absurdly gentle. The sound passed through the wall and through the warm mechanical air as if reminding the closet what building it occupied.

Elian closed the log export and checked the hash. "Do not delete anything. Do not confront anyone. Do not make jokes in writing."

"Engineers communicate by jokes in writing."

"Become temporarily less interesting."

"That may be the hardest requirement."

When they stepped back into the corridor, Mara was waiting beside a cart of acid-free boxes. She glanced at Talia, then at Elian.

"Well?"

"The machine did not act alone," he said.

Mara looked at the locked server closet. "I had assumed machines were more obedient than monks."

Talia said, "They are. That is the problem."

Chapter 4: Abbreviated Latin

Jonas Quill had left the abbey ten years earlier but still read its charters like family letters. He met Elian in the reading room and pointed to a cramped mark over a word.

"This is not decorative."

"Abbreviation?"

"Common use. Pasture shared after Michaelmas. Remove the mark and it becomes enclosed holding."

Jonas had arrived on a bicycle, trouser cuffs clipped, gray hair flattened by a wool cap. He carried no briefcase, only a cloth satchel full of pencils, a magnifier, and a battered Latin grammar with tape on the spine. Mara introduced him as a former novice, former archivist, current schoolteacher, and permanent nuisance.

"The abbey keeps me humble by forgetting which of those it regrets most," Jonas said.

He took his place under a lamp and asked for no tea. His hands were narrow and stained faintly with ink. When Mara brought out the charter photograph, he did not touch it at first. He looked. The room seemed to change pace around him.

"This hand," he said, "always saves space. See here, and here. He uses suspension marks aggressively. Miserable man to read. Excellent man to trust once you learn his habits."

"You know the scribe?"

"Not his name. His temper."

He pointed to a cramped mark over a word near the damaged edge of the line. It looked to Elian like a tiny hook, made in brown ink that had faded toward the color of weak tea.

"This is not decorative."

"Abbreviation?"

"Common use. Pasture shared after Michaelmas. Remove the mark and it becomes enclosed holding."

Mara stood behind them with her arms folded. She had given Jonas the look of someone prepared to disapprove of theatrics. Jonas offered none. He enlarged the image on the screen and brought up three undisputed charters from neighboring estates. The same mark appeared over related terms, always where shared pasture or access was being reserved.

"The model saw the mark," Elian said.

"How do you know?"

"The image includes it. The transcription excludes its effect."

"Then the model is rude."

"The model is dependent."

The algorithm had removed the mark in transcription. Not visibly. Semantically. It translated the clause as exclusive possession. On the side-by-side view, the old ink remained, but the output had walked past it with clean shoes.

"Could it be error?" Elian asked.

"Once, yes. Across six charters, no."

Jonas pulled a pencil from behind his ear and wrote the expanded Latin on a pad. Then he wrote the model's rendering under it and struck through three words.

"This is what troubles me," he said. "The machine does not merely fail to expand. It chooses the opposite legal sense."

"Could a postprocessor do that?"

"You tell me."

"Yes."

"Then ask it who taught it bad manners."

Mara went to the catalogue desk and returned with the physical folder list, though not the charters themselves. The preservation hold required two witnesses, and she had become devout about paperwork since midnight.

"The developer's brief rests on those six," she said.

"Of course it does," Jonas answered. "A house built on six small erasures."

Elian magnified the mark again. A tiny stroke of brown ink held back acres of concrete. He had seen lawsuits turn on commas. This was older and less forgiving. The scribe had pressed harder at the beginning of the mark than at the end. The image captured the pressure, the drag, the lift of the pen. A human hand, economical and impatient, had made a legal distinction no glossy report wanted to keep.

"What would you need to testify?" Iris asked from the doorway.

Jonas looked up. "Time with the originals. Comparative examples. Permission to be boring."

"Courts can endure boring if it is useful."

"Then I am well suited."

Mara signed the access slip. Elian signed as witness. Jonas washed his hands at the small sink and dried them on a lint-free towel. No white gloves. Clean hands, steady light, supported parchment. The rituals mattered because they slowed desire.

When the first charter came out of its folder, the reading room went quiet. Even the rain seemed to lower its voice. Jonas bent over the leaf and did not speak for almost a minute.

"Yes," he said at last. "It is clearer in the flesh."

"The mark?"

"The mark, the spacing, the following clause. The scribe is reserving use. Whoever made that transcription either did not understand this hand or did not wish to."

Elian wrote down both possibilities. He did not yet know which was true. The difference mattered in court, but not to the meadow, which would be lost either way if nobody forced the distinction into daylight.

Chapter 5: Metadata Saints

The image files carried devotional names: oran_blue, columba_gold, brigid_final. Pretty labels for ugly version control. Elian traced their hashes and found one saint had risen from the dead three times in a week.

"Brigid_final was changed after filing," he said.

Mara leaned over the monitor. "Changed how?"

"Same visible image. Different embedded crop, different transcription region, different output."

"Can that happen by accident?"

"Only if accident has repository access."

They were working in Mara's office because the reading room had filled with village representatives and lawyers pretending not to listen to one another. Her office was barely large enough for two chairs, one desk, a kettle, and a filing cabinet that refused to close. A framed photograph of the abbey in snow hung crooked above the radiator. On the windowsill, three dead flies lay in a row as if catalogued.

Elian had three file manifests open. The abbey's archival export, Calder's exhibit set, and the vendor's repository dump did not match. They failed to match politely. Image names were the same. Thumbnail previews were the same. The differences hid in embedded coordinates, crop masks, and transcription zones.

"Here," he said, enlarging a metadata panel. "Brigid_final was exported Monday at 09:12. It appears in Calder's brief as the image supporting charter twelve. But the crop region inside the file was modified Wednesday at 22:41, after filing. Same surface image. Different active region."

"Active region."

"The part the model reads."

Mara understood at once. Her eyes moved from the coordinates to the charter photograph beside them. "The margin is outside the box."

"In the filed version, yes."

"In the archive version?"

"Inside."

The margin in question contained a later note, a tight hand from perhaps two centuries after the original charter: meadow, millstream, winter beasts. Not dispositive by itself, Jonas had said, but important. It showed how the abbey had understood the right over time. The altered crop treated the note as empty edge.

Mara sat back slowly. "They left enough decoration to make the image look whole."

"Margins are where inconvenient things live."

"Do not sound pleased."

"I am never pleased by evidence. I am relieved when it is legible."

He traced the hashes again. Oran_blue had two histories. Columba_gold had a deleted branch. Brigid_final had been final three times, each time losing a little more of the same kind of evidence. The saint names let the team discuss versions without saying legal edit. A calendar of devotion had become a filing cabinet for concealment.

Mara made tea without asking whether he wanted any. The kettle clicked and sighed. Outside her office, Bram the night guard spoke to someone in the corridor, his voice low and flat. The abbey had learned to murmur since the audit began.

"Who named the files?" Mara asked.

"The original project convention came from your staff. The revisions follow it."

"So our own piety gave them camouflage."

"Your naming convention gave them ambiguity. Camouflage came later."

She put a mug in front of him. It had a crack under the handle. "That distinction may comfort you."

"It helps me assign blame accurately."

"I am not sure accuracy comforts me either."

He opened the commit metadata. A service account had pushed the changed crop regions. The service account had been used by an external credential linked to the Calder review environment. The user field did not name a person, but people had a way of appearing through habits: time zones, folder choices, typos, impatience.

"This user always writes final without capitalization," Elian said.

"Is that useful?"

"Sometimes. Small habits are less guarded than signatures."

The altered files masked deleted marginal zones where common-right notes lived. They did not delete the notes from the visible image because that would be crude. They simply instructed the machine not to see them. The court, presented with output rather than process, might never know the difference.

Mara's face hardened. "They used the calendar of saints as a filing cabinet for fraud."

"For concealment," Elian said. "Fraud comes after intent."

"You are patient."

"No. I am admissible."

She looked at him over the mug. "Does that sentence make lawyers like you?"

"It makes them tolerate me."

"Archivists may do the same."

He exported the manifests, the crop overlays, and screenshots of the differences. For each, he wrote a short note in plain language. Same visible image; different machine-readable region. Marginal evidence excluded. Modification after filing. The words felt dry. They needed to be dry. Outrage evaporated in court. Procedure condensed.

When he finished, Mara took one printed overlay and held it under the desk lamp. The red crop line ran just inside the marginal note, precise as a cut.

"It knew where to stop," she said.

"Someone did."

She placed the printout in a folder and wrote the time on the tab. Her handwriting was angular, compact, and angry without losing legibility.

Chapter 6: Developer Visit

Calder Pike arrived in a coat that had never met mud. Prior Olan Marr walked beside him, smiling like a man carrying a bowl too full.

"The data center would preserve the abbey," Calder said. "Endowment, jobs, infrastructure."

Mara answered, "And the meadow?"

"Underused land."

Iris said, "Common land."

Calder turned to Elian. "Dr. Morse, I trust you will bring technical clarity."

"I bring audit trails."

"Excellent. Clarity by another name."

No, Elian thought. Audit trails often made clarity impossible for those who had purchased it in advance.

They met in the chapter room because it was the only space large enough to hold Calder's entourage without making them look like an occupation. The room had a long oak table, limewashed walls, and a row of narrow windows facing the garth. Rainwater fell from the roof in steady ropes. A plastic bucket near the fireplace caught drips from a cracked flashing, each drop landing with a small metallic note.

Calder noticed the bucket. He was too disciplined to smile.

"This is exactly the kind of deferred maintenance our partnership can address," he said.

Prior Olan's smile flickered. He was a tall man made narrower by worry, with silver hair and a habit of touching the cuff of his sleeve when money was mentioned. Need had worn paths into his face.

"We are not here to discuss partnership terms," Iris said. "We are here because disputed evidence has been used in a public filing."

Calder looked wounded in the mild way of men accustomed to turning accusation into discourtesy. "Our filings rely on the best available technical process."

"That process is under audit."

"Which we welcome."

Mara said, "You welcomed it by sending us a revised exhibit set without telling us the crop regions changed."

One of Calder's lawyers shifted in his chair. Calder did not look at him. That was interesting.

"I am not a technician," Calder said.

"No," Elian said. "But you are the beneficiary of the technical choices."

The room grew still. Prior Olan lifted one hand as if to soften the moment, then let it fall.

Calder folded his gloves on the table. They were dark leather, expensive and new. "Dr. Morse, my understanding is that you were retained to determine whether the model output is reliable. I hope we can avoid implying misconduct before the facts are in."

"I prefer facts too."

"Good."

"So far they are not flattering."

Iris looked down at her notes. It might have been to hide a smile. Mara did not bother.

Calder's technical consultant was not present, though his name appeared nowhere on the sign-in sheet. Elian asked about CP-EXT. Calder's lawyer answered that external technical resources were routed through vendor channels. The sentence contained no person.

"We will need names," Elian said.

"Subject to confidentiality," the lawyer replied.

"Subject to the court."

The bucket received another drop. Olan's eyes went to the ceiling, then the cracked plaster, then the floor. Need has a way of looking everywhere except at the cost. Elian had seen it in museums selling deaccessioned work to cover payroll, in universities licensing research data to companies with softer ethics than contracts, in hospitals accepting donated systems they could not afford to question. Institutions did not wake one morning corrupt. They negotiated with shortage until a line became a path.

Calder turned his attention back to Mara. "Sister Vale, no one wants to harm the abbey's heritage. Quite the opposite. We want to preserve it. Digitization, climate control, public access. These are not trivial benefits."

"Nor are old rights trivial because they are inconvenient."

"The question is whether those rights exist."

"And whether your team helped erase the answer."

Prior Olan said her name softly. "Mara."

She looked at him but did not apologize.

Elian watched Calder watch that exchange. The developer was measuring fault lines: librarian against prior, county against abbey, preservation against survival, old meadow against new money. He did not need everyone to lie. He needed enough people to want different truths.

After the meeting, Calder paused beside Elian in the corridor. Through an open door, the cloister stones shone with rain.

"You have a reputation," Calder said.

"Most reputations are maintained by people who need shorthand."

"For making projects difficult."

"Only where difficulty was already present."

Calder smiled then, briefly, almost with pleasure. "Saint Oran's is not a museum piece. It must live in the present."

"Agreed."

"Then you understand tradeoffs."

"I understand records."

"Records are never neutral."

"Neither are edits."

Calder put on his gloves. "Be careful, Dr. Morse. Technical disputes can become expensive for everyone."

"So can land."

The developer walked away with his lawyers and his clean coat. At the end of the corridor, Bram opened the outer door. Wind came in with the smell of wet grass and diesel from the waiting cars. Calder stepped over a shallow puddle without looking down.

Mara had been watching from the chapter room doorway. "He threatened you politely."

"He threatened the invoice."

"Will it work?"

"I invoice in advance for politeness."

She gave him the smallest possible laugh, gone before it became warmth. The bucket in the chapter room kept filling, drop by drop, while the abbey tried to decide what kind of preservation it could afford.

Chapter 7: Wormhole Match

Nadia Hesk used wormholes the way detectives used fingerprints. She placed two charter images side by side and aligned the tiny insect tunnels.

"This disputed image is not from folio twelve," she said.

"The metadata says twelve."

"Worms disagree."

Nadia had set up in the conservation room, where the air was cooler and smelled faintly of starch paste. She wore a black apron over a green sweater and had the calm severity of someone who trusted material evidence more than human explanation. On her bench lay magnifiers, raking lights, a color target, and a box of sharpened pencils arranged by length.

"I was told you could help with image provenance," Elian said.

"I was told you were allergic to mysticism."

"Only when it lacks logs."

"Good. Parchment has logs. They are just made by damage."

She brought up two high-resolution images on a calibrated monitor. One was labeled folio twelve, the disputed charter image used in Calder's exhibit. The other was folio nineteen, a devotional grant to a chapel fraternity, unrelated to the meadow. At first, Elian saw only brown text, ruled lines, stains, and the pale skin of prepared animal hide. Then Nadia adjusted the contrast and marked a cluster of tiny tunnels near the lower margin.

"Bookworm path," she said. "Not random in aggregate. Holes pass through stacks of leaves. If you map them, adjacent leaves share a pattern with small shifts. It is like watching a needle pass through cloth."

She overlaid the images. The holes met: one crescent near the ruling line, two pinpricks by a faded initial, a longer channel that bent slightly toward the gutter. The fit was too exact to ignore.

"This disputed image is not from folio twelve," she said.

"The metadata says twelve."

"Worms disagree."

Mara, who had come despite claiming she had no time, leaned closer. "Could the label be an honest cataloguing mistake?"

"In the nineteenth century, yes. In a modern image export linked to a lawsuit, less charming."

Nadia clicked through the stack. Folio twelve should have shown a different pattern, with a tear near the lower edge and no channel by the initial. The exhibit image had been paired with folio twelve's metadata but physically belonged to folio nineteen, a devotional grant with no land clause. Someone had made a leaf wear another leaf's legal name.

Elian felt the case shift under his hands. Bad transcription was one thing. Mispaired source images were another. Courts liked to think documents sat still. Digital archives allowed them to become costumes.

"Who prepared the exhibit set?" he asked.

"Vendor export," Mara said. "Reviewed by Calder's technical team. Approved by the abbey contract office."

"Olan?"

"Olan or the assistant bursar. The signatures are electronic."

Nadia printed the overlay on heavy paper. The wormholes looked absurdly small to bear such weight, but that was often evidence's habit. A scratch, a timestamp, a file size, a guard's note. Tiny things carried large refusals.

"Can you make a report?" Elian asked.

"With pictures ugly enough for court."

"Perfect."

"Do not say perfect until you see my captions. I write captions like a disappointed surgeon."

"Judges appreciate disappointment when properly labeled."

Mara stood very still. "If they changed leaves digitally, what did they do physically?"

The question entered the room and changed its temperature. Nadia stopped aligning images. Elian looked toward the vault door visible through the conservation room window. Behind that door, the charters sat in their boxes. Or were supposed to.

"We count," he said.

No archivist liked that sentence. It always meant loss was possible.

The counting took three people and two hours. Mara pulled boxes. Nadia checked folders against the shelf list. Elian recorded each presence, each absence, each sign of recent handling. The preservation rules had slowed them. They had not stopped the small fear that opened with every folder.

Most leaves were present. Some had old repairs. Some had new slips noting digitization. One folder held only a placeholder card where a palimpsest leaf should have rested. Mara did not speak when she saw it. She set the folder down as if noise might make the absence worse.

"Later," Elian said quietly. "We finish the count first."

She nodded once.

By late afternoon, the conservation room table held a line of completed check sheets. Outside, the rain had thinned into mist. Nadia taped the wormhole overlay beside the manifest and wrote in precise block letters: image labeled folio twelve corresponds physically to folio nineteen.

That helped.

Iris's shoulders eased a fraction. Elian wrote the sentence down. Judges revealed themselves in small preferences. Arda preferred the concrete.

Calder's slides showed the disputed clauses in three columns: image excerpt, machine transcription, plain-English summary. The summary used words like exclusive, unencumbered, consolidated. "I do."

"Maybe."

Chapter 10: Excluded Hands

Talia opened the training report and pointed to a scribal cluster marked low utility.

"That is the meadow hand," Jonas said.

Six charters had been written by the same clerk, a cramped worker with heavy abbreviation marks. The original model treated that hand as difficult. The new build excluded it from training examples, then translated its marks as if difficulty meant absence.

"Could exclusion improve accuracy elsewhere?" Elian asked.

"Yes," Talia said. "And destroy accuracy exactly where the lawsuit lives."

They had taken over the guesthouse dining room after lunch. The table now held laptops, printouts, coffee cups, a dish of untouched biscuits, and one actual charter facsimile Jonas kept pulling toward himself whenever software began to dominate the conversation. Rain had stopped, leaving the windows filmed with gray light. Somewhere below the abbey wall, a tractor moved with a low patient sound.

Talia projected the training report onto a portable screen. It showed clusters of scribal hands represented as colored points. Most clustered neatly. One group sat apart, ragged and dense, labeled H-17 LOW UTILITY. The label looked administrative. Jonas treated it as an insult.

"That is the meadow hand," he said.

"The report says removal improved overall character accuracy by two percent," Iris said.

"Across the whole corpus," Talia answered. "Not across the disputed documents."

Elian asked her to slow down. She did, visibly fighting the engineer's urge to repair misunderstanding with speed.

"The original system used all known hands and flagged H-17 as high uncertainty. It was annoying but honest. The retrained build removes H-17 from the representative examples because it causes errors. After removal, the model performs better on clean devotional texts and worse on the meadow charters. The summary reports the average improvement. The lawsuit uses the edge case."

"An average can hide the place where harm lives," Elian said.

"Exactly."

Jonas copied one of the abbreviated lines by hand on a pad. His pencil moved quickly, then stopped at the disputed mark. "The old scribe wrote like a man saving parchment."

"The new model reads like a man saving Calder money," Mara said.

No one corrected her.

Talia opened evaluation logs. In the March build, H-17 triggered warnings: abbreviation ambiguity; possible shared pasture; human review required. In the May build, the warnings vanished. The output gained confidence not because it understood the hand better, but because the hand had been excluded from the evidence used to teach caution.

"Could someone defend this as a technical choice?" Iris asked.

"Yes," Talia said. "If the goal were a general demo. You remove difficult outliers to make the system look smooth."

"And if the goal were a land dispute?"

"Then you disclose the limitation in red letters and do not use the output as title evidence."

Elian looked at the report author line. Service account. External package. No human name. He had begun to dislike the absence personally.

"Intent still has to be proved," he said.

Iris nodded. "Pattern is not intent."

"But pattern is beginning to raise its hand."

Mara leaned over the table. "How many choices point the same way now?"

Elian counted on his fingers because juries and judges liked visible arithmetic. "After-hours scan. Changed crop regions. Mispaired leaf image. Excluded scribal hand. Confidence increase on disputed clauses. Missing palimpsest. External consultant with access. That is before we review postprocessing."

"Say that in court," Iris said.

"Eventually. Not all at once."

"Why?"

"Because long lists make judges feel invited to choose one weak item and ignore the structure. We build the structure first."

Talia closed the training report and opened the raw configuration file. The exclusion list contained H-17 and three other hands. Beside H-17, someone had typed: noise; creates common-right false positives. She stared at that note.

"False positives," she said quietly.

"What should it say?"

"Unresolved positives. Possible positives. Anything but false."

Jonas read the phrase over her shoulder. "They called the scribe false before reading him."

The room went quiet. That was the intellectual sin beneath the legal one. Difficulty had been renamed error. Uncertainty had been renamed noise. The machine had been tuned to prefer a clean answer over a faithful one.

Elian saved the configuration and wrote a plain note: Target scribal hand excluded after producing common-right warnings. He could feel the dryness of the language resisting the anger in the room. Good. Dry words traveled farther in law.

Outside, the tractor stopped. The sudden absence of sound revealed the small noises inside: laptop fans, pencil scratch, Mara turning a page, Talia breathing through her nose like someone refusing to break.

"We need the postprocessor," Elian said.

Talia nodded. "If they changed the terms after transcription, it will be there."

"And if it is gone?"

"Then we look for where it used to be."

For the first time since the audit began, she sounded less frightened than busy.

"Funding. Equipment. The usual sins with clean hands."

The folder held a placeholder slip and nothing else.

For a moment, the room narrowed.

"Because of everything."

That was better. Parchment left traces. People left laziness. Digital systems left versions even when ordered to forget. Archives were not magical; they were stubborn. Elian built his work on that stubbornness.

He did not answer. She was not wrong in any useful way.

Chapter 12: Confidence Score

The model's confidence score was most certain where the manuscript was most damaged. Elian found that offensive.

Real systems knew when to hesitate. Fraudulent ones were trained to sound managerial.

Talia ran the original image through her archived build. Output: uncertain abbreviation; possible common pasture clause. The new build: exclusive abbey ownership, 98.2 percent.

"Can postprocessing do that?"

"If someone writes rules after the model."

They were in the server chapel after midnight because Talia said the network was quiet then and because nobody trusted the ordinary workday anymore. Saint Cyra's side chapel had been stripped of pews years earlier and filled with racks, battery backups, cable trays, and a portable air conditioner vented through a lancet window. The stained glass still showed a woman holding a book and a knife. Blue LEDs blinked beneath her in patient rows.

Talia worked from a folding table. Elian sat beside her with a notebook. Mara stood near the door as witness, refusing the chair offered to her.

"You should sit," Talia said.

"I sit when machines apologize."

"Then you may be waiting."

Talia mounted her archived March build from an external drive. She did it slowly, narrating each step for Elian's record. File path. Hash. Environment. Source image. No live network. No overwrite permissions. The rituals made her less nervous. Or gave nerves a job.

The original image loaded. The March model produced its output: uncertain abbreviation; possible common pasture clause; recommend paleographic review. The language was modest, almost embarrassed.

Then Talia ran the same image through the May build preserved under court order. Exclusive abbey ownership. Confidence: 98.2 percent.

Elian stared at the number. The manuscript edge in the source image was stained, scraped, and partly shadowed by the old binding curve. The most damaged region had become the model's most certain sentence. He found that offensive in a way that was not professional but helped.

"Can postprocessing do that?"

"If someone writes rules after the model."

They found one in a configuration folder labeled normalizers. It was not hidden well. People who believed in their own cleverness often mislabeled the visible world and called it security. The rule table mapped ambiguous forms of communis and related abbreviations to proprius when document class equaled land title and developer review flag equaled true.

Talia stared at the screen. "That is not machine learning."

"No," Elian said. "That is a clerk with a mask."

Mara stepped closer. "Translate."

"Communis means common or shared. Proprius means one's own, private in this context. The rule says when the model sees certain ambiguous common-right terms in land-title documents marked for developer review, convert them toward private ownership."

"Marked by whom?"

"That is next."

Talia searched the commit history. The rule had been introduced in a batch called estate cleanup. The author was a service account. The approver field referenced CP-EXT. A comment described the change as reducing false common claims.

"False," Talia said. "Again."

She opened the test cases. None came from the meadow hand. None included marginal notes. None included palimpsest undertext. The rule had been tested on clean documents where exclusive ownership was already known, then applied to damaged documents where common rights were precisely the issue.

"It is like calibrating a scale with feathers and using it to weigh stone," Mara said.

"Close," Elian said. "Except someone then sells the stone."

He exported the code, hashed it, and made three copies. One went to Iris. One to the court evidence drive. One stayed sealed with Mara. He wrote the rule in plain English on a yellow pad because judges should not have to peer through syntax to see intent.

Talia leaned back and covered her eyes. The server fans filled the chapel with white noise.

"My name is on the demo," she said.

"Not on this rule."

"That will matter?"

"It will matter to people who care about evidence."

"That is a smaller group than I hoped."

Mara surprised them by placing a hand on Talia's shoulder. Briefly. Awkwardly. "Then we enlarge it."

The stained-glass saint watched above the racks, book and knife in hand. Elian had no religious vocabulary for the room, but he understood an image of learning paired with consequence.

"We keep going," he said.

Talia lowered her hands. "There are more normalizers."

"Then we read them."

They worked until the chapel windows paled. Each rule told the same story in a slightly different accent: uncertainty narrowed, common terms replaced, margins ignored, damaged regions made confident where developer review was true. By dawn, the server chapel felt less like a machine room than a confession made in configuration files.

Chapter 13: Water Clause

Iris brought well maps from families who had used the meadow stream longer than any corporate charter. The disputed water clause mattered more than grazing. Cooling ponds would draw from the same shallow system.

"If the clause stands, Calder needs public consent," she said.

"And if it falls?"

"The abbey can lease water access privately."

Elian looked at the old Latin. A narrow phrase, half scraped, held the millstream in common use during dry months. The model ignored it as devotional residue.

No poetry, just survival.

The families came in the morning with maps folded in biscuit tins, rolled in mailing tubes, pressed inside account books. Iris had asked for records. The village produced memory with receipts attached. There were well-depth notes in pencil, drainage complaints, photographs of the lower gate under flood, a parish notice about August water rotation from 1947, and a farmer's diary that recorded dry weeks by the number of buckets drawn before noon.

They spread it all across the guesthouse floor because the table was too small. Mud dried on boots by the door. The room smelled of paper, wool, and the sharp mineral tang of wet fields.

"My grandfather said the brook was never the abbey's alone," said Martin Bell, a sheep farmer with red hands and a tie that looked borrowed. "He said the monks could pray over it if they liked, but they could not keep it from the lower beans."

Elian liked him at once.

"Public risk converted to private signature."

"Yes."

Elian looked at the old Latin under Jonas's lamp. A narrow phrase, half scraped, held the millstream in common use during dry months. The model ignored it as devotional residue, because the upper text on one palimpsest leaf had been devotional and because someone had found that label convenient. The clause was not grand. No poetry, just survival.

"The living could learn," Iris said.

Nadia arrived with a portable light and asked everyone to move their mugs away from the table in a tone that made compliance immediate. "Boards?" Iris asked.

"At Summer Cut. There is a gate under the alder. Rusted now, but it moves."

"Not in those shoes."

By afternoon they were in the meadow. Rain had softened the ground, and Elian's city shoes took revenge on him immediately. Ahead, the millstream moved narrow and quick under alder roots.

Iris photographed the gate. Elian recorded its location. Mara stood with her coat pulled tight, looking from the stream to the abbey and back again.

"It is still here," she said.

"Most systems are," Elian answered. "Until someone notices they are inconvenient."

"If we connect it to the clause, the use records, and the hydrology."

Elian wrote the sentence down, though he would never use it in a report.
