Chapter 1:

The queue began forming at four in the morning, as it always did on the first of the month. Mara joined it at twenty past, taking her place behind a man whose coat had been mended so many times the original fabric was barely visible. The November cold bit through her own threadbare sleeves, but she had learned long ago not to complain. Complaints were noted.

Three hundred and fourteen people stood in the Allocation Square by the time the Distribution Bureau opened its doors at six. Mara counted them automatically, the habit engrained by years of standing in lines that never moved quickly enough. The concrete beneath her feet was slick with frost, and the only light came from the single floodlamp mounted above the Bureau entrance, casting everything in a jaundiced yellow glow.

When her turn came, she stepped up to the window and presented her card. The clerk took it without meeting her eyes, slotting it into the reader. Mara watched the machine hum, a sound she associated with hope and dread in equal measure.

"Mara Estis," the clerk recited. "Unit 41, Block 7. Two adults, one minor."

"Yes."

The clerk's fingers moved across the terminal. The printer chattered and spat out a strip of perforated paper. Ration tickets. Mara took them with both hands, the way one might receive a fragile thing, because in a way that was exactly what they were. Fragile. Worthless outside these walls. Everything inside them.

She did not count the tickets in front of the clerk. That was against protocol. Instead she folded them into the inner pocket of her coat, the one she had reinforced with an extra layer of stitching, and stepped away from the window.

Outside, the square was emptying. The first pale light of dawn had begun to touch the rooftops, revealing the state of the buildings: peeling paint, boarded windows, the perpetual smell of damp coal ash. Mara walked quickly, her hand pressed against her pocket as if the tickets might somehow dissolve before she reached home.

The apartment was cold when she let herself in. Elias had banked the fire before leaving for his shift at the water treatment plant, but the coals had burned low. Mara knelt by the grate and added two of the precious briquettes from the bucket beside the hearth. Not enough to warm the room, but enough to take the edge off.

She unfolded the tickets on the kitchen table. The ration allowed for one kilogram of grain per adult per week, half that for a minor. Two hundred grams of protein. One hundred of sugar. The vegetable allotment was seasonal, and in November that meant potatoes and cabbage. A liter of milk for the minor, though Tessa was sixteen now and technically no longer eligible. The Bureau had not yet updated its records, and Mara did not intend to remind them.

She laid the tickets out in neat rows, calculating the days ahead. Four weeks until the next distribution. She had made the math work for eight years now, ever since the Shortage had become official policy. She would make it work again.

But this month, there was something else. A separate slip of paper, smaller than the ration tickets, with a single line of text typed across its surface.

*Report to the Bureau for reassignment. 8:00 AM, November 3rd.*

Mara read it three times before folding it carefully and placing it in her pocket.

Chapter 2:

Elias came home at noon for his meal break, as he did every day. The water treatment plant ran on staggered shifts meant to keep production constant, but the midday break was sacred. He washed his hands at the sink, the water running thin and gray before clearing, then sat at the table where Mara had laid out their portions.

Bread, two slices each. A thin spread of the vegetable paste that came in bulk tins, labeled only as Protein Supplement No. 3. A cup of hot water with a tea leaf saved from yesterday's pot. Tessa was at school, so her portion sat wrapped in a cloth on the counter.

"You got the tickets," Elias said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"How bad?"

"The same." Mara sat across from him. "The vegetable allotment is all cabbage again this month."

Elias nodded, chewing methodically. He was a large man who had grown thinner over the years, his work clothes hanging looser on his frame. But his hands were still strong, still capable, and he never complained about the food she set before him. That was one of the reasons she had married him.

"There was something else," she said.

He looked up.

She slid the slip of paper across the table. Elias read it, his expression unchanging, then set it down beside his plate. "Reassignment," he said. "That could mean anything."

"It could mean the mines. It could mean the agricultural zones."

"It could mean a desk job."

"Nobody gets a desk job by being reassigned from distribution."

Elias picked up the slip again, turning it over as if the back might contain more information. It did not. "When do you report?"

"Tomorrow morning."

He was quiet for a long moment. Then he reached across the table and took her hand, his fingers rough against hers. "We'll find out what it is first. No point in worrying until we know."

Mara wanted to argue, but he was right. Worrying was its own ration, and she had long ago learned not to spend it before it was needed.

That evening, Tessa came home with a bruise on her cheekbone. Mara saw it as soon as the girl walked through the door, the purple bloom standing out against her pale skin. Tessa tried to hide it by turning her face, but her mother's eyes were trained for shortfalls and discrepancies.

"What happened?"

"Nothing. I walked into a door."

"Tessa."

The girl's jaw tightened. She was sixteen, with her father's build and her mother's stubbornness, and she had been a child when the Shortage began. She did not remember a world where food was plentiful, where the shops were full, where a person could walk into a market and buy whatever they pleased. That made her both harder and more fragile than her parents.

"There was a protest at the school gates," Tessa said finally. "The Security Corps broke it up. I was in the way."

"A protest about what?"

"The same thing as always. The food. The quotas. The fact that we're all starving while the Bureau sits in its heated offices and counts our tickets."

"Keep your voice down."

"Why? Who's going to hear me? The neighbors are all too busy being hungry to listen at walls."

Mara pressed a cloth soaked in cold water to her daughter's face. "There's nothing to be gained by drawing attention."

"There's nothing to be gained by silence either."

Mara did not answer. She could not. Because the truth was that silence had kept them alive for eight years, and she was not ready to give it up yet.

Chapter 3:

The Bureau building looked different when approached as a supplicant rather than an employee. Mara stood before its gray facade at five minutes to eight, watching the other workers file through the side entrance that led to the distribution floor. She had been one of them yesterday. Today, she was something else.

The security guard at the main entrance checked her slip of paper against a list on his clipboard, then gestured for her to proceed. The corridors inside were narrow and poorly lit, the walls painted the same shade of institutional green that covered every government building in the city. Mara followed the directions she had been given, past closed doors and empty desks, until she reached an office on the third floor.

The man behind the desk was named Copley. He was perhaps fifty, with the soft hands and soft belly of someone who had never missed a meal. His office was warm, warmer than Mara's apartment had been in months, and she felt an irrational surge of anger at the sight of the radiator hissing steam in the corner.

"Mara Estis," he said, consulting a file. "Ten years in Distribution. Exemplary record. No infractions."

"No."

"And now you've been selected for the Garden Program."

Mara felt the floor shift beneath her feet. The Garden Program was not a secret, exactly, but it was spoken of in whispers. A project on the outskirts of the city, behind fences and security checkpoints. They grew food there, it was said. Real food. Not the gray paste and wilted cabbage that made up the standard ration, but tomatoes and peppers and leafy greens that most children in the city had never tasted.

"What would I be doing?"

"Growing," Copley said, as if the answer were obvious. "The Program needs reliable workers. People who understand the importance of their work and will not be tempted by ... proximity."

The pause told her everything. The gardens grew food, yes. But that food did not go into the general ration. It went to the Bureau, to the Corps, to the people whose loyalty needed to be purchased. The people who worked there were expected to handle abundance without taking any for themselves.

"I don't have any agricultural experience," Mara said.

"You'll be trained. The work is not difficult. The conditions are considerably better than what you're used to." Copley smiled, a thin expression that did not reach his eyes. "And the pay is better too. An additional protein ration per week. Three hundred grams."

Three hundred grams. Mara calculated automatically. Her family's total protein allotment was four hundred grams per week. This would nearly double it, provided she did not eat the extra herself during her shift.

"When do I start?" she asked.

"Tomorrow. A transport will pick you up at the Block 7 gates at six in the morning."

She walked home through streets that looked the same as they always had, but felt different now. She had been given something. An opportunity, perhaps, or a trap. It was too early to tell which.

On her way, she passed the distribution center and saw the queue still stretching around the block, the same faces she had seen every month for years. They looked tired. They looked hungry. They looked at her as she walked past, and she looked away.

Back in the apartment, she stood at the window and watched the city go about its slow decay. Three hundred grams of protein. It would help. It would not be enough.

It never was.

Chapter 4:

Transport 7A was a repurposed military vehicle with bench seats along both sides and a canvas roof that did little to keep out the cold. Mara sat with seven other women, none of whom she recognized from the distribution center. They did not speak. The engine was too loud for conversation in any case, and there was something about the armed guard at the front that discouraged questions.

The journey took forty minutes, winding through streets Mara had never seen, past checkpoints where the transport was waved through without stopping. The buildings grew smaller, then sparse, then gave way to fields. Real fields, enclosed by high chain-link fences topped with razor wire.

The Garden was larger than she had imagined. Rows of glasshouses stretched into the distance, their panels gleaming in the weak morning light. Between them, plots of open soil showed the dark green of winter vegetables. Mara saw a section planted with what looked like young fruit trees, their branches bare and skeletal.

She was assigned to Greenhouse 14, where a woman named Yulia showed her how to check the soil moisture, how to regulate the ventilation flaps, how to identify the first signs of blight on the tomato plants. Yulia was perhaps sixty, with hands that had been in the soil for decades, and she spoke about the plants the way other people spoke about their children.

"You have to listen to them," she said, pressing her palm flat against the earth in a raised bed. "The soil tells you what it needs. Dry on top but damp beneath means the roots are fine. Dry all the way through means you've waited too long."

Mara listened. She learned. She spent the morning thinning seedlings, her fingers cold in the unheated greenhouse, her breath misting in front of her face. The work was physical in a way that distribution had not been, and by lunchtime her back ached and her nails were rimmed with dirt.

But at noon, when the guard brought around the meal cart, she received her first taste of what the Garden produced. A bowl of soup, thick with vegetables she had not seen in years. Carrots. Celery. Actual pieces of potato, not the powdered substitute that filled the standard ration packs. And bread, real bread, with a crust that cracked when she broke it.

She ate slowly, deliberately, making each mouthful last. The women around her did the same, their faces solemn with the gravity of the meal. Nobody spoke. There was nothing to say that the food did not say better.

At the end of the shift, they were searched before boarding the transport. Mara stood with her arms raised while a female guard ran practiced hands over her clothing, patting down pockets, checking seams. She had nothing hidden. She had known better than to try.

But as she sat in the transport on the way back, she watched the glasshouses recede in the distance and thought about the three hundred grams of protein she had been promised. She thought about the soup she had eaten. She thought about Tessa's face, the hunger that never quite left it.

She thought about how easy it would be to hide a tomato in her coat.

Chapter 5:

The first week passed without incident. Mara rose before dawn, rode the transport to the Garden, worked in Greenhouse 14 until the midday meal, worked again until the afternoon search, and returned home in time to prepare the evening ration. She did not try to steal anything.

But she watched.

She noted the patterns of the guards, their rotations, their blind spots. She observed which women were searched more thoroughly than others, and which were waved through with barely a glance. She learned that the midday meal was when security was laxest, the guards gathered in their own mess, distracted by their own abundance.

On the eighth day, she took a tomato.

It was small, imperfect, on the verge of being culled. She had been instructed to remove such fruits from the vines and deposit them in the compost bin. Instead, she palmed it into her pocket and held her breath.

The afternoon search was cursory. The guard patted her shoulders, her waist, her thighs, but the tomato was flat against her hip, hidden in a fold of fabric she had sewn into the lining. The guard did not find it.

Mara sat on the transport with the tomato pressed against her leg, her heart hammering. The other women dozed or stared at nothing. Nobody looked at her.

She walked through the Block 7 gates with it still in her pocket, past the security booth where the guard was reading a newspaper, up the stairs to her apartment, and into the kitchen. She set the tomato on the counter and stared at it.

It was imperfect. Slightly misshapen, with a pale patch where it had not received enough light. But it was real. It was fresh. It was not a ration.

When Tessa came home, Mara sliced the tomato into three pieces and served it alongside the evening bread. Elias looked at his portion, then at her, but did not ask. He bit into it, and his eyes closed briefly, and when he opened them again they were bright.

"Where did you get this?" Tessa whispered.

"The Garden."

"Does anyone know?"

"No."

Tessa ate her piece in two bites, then pressed her finger to the plate to collect the juice. "Can you get more?"

Mara did not answer. She was thinking about the compost bin, full of imperfect vegetables that would never be distributed. She was thinking about how many people could be fed with what the Garden threw away.

The next day, she took two tomatoes and a handful of carrots.

The day after that, an entire cabbage that had split from too much rain.

She stored them in a box beneath the floorboards, under the loose plank behind the coal scuttle. Every evening, she added to the stash. Every morning, she checked that it was still there.

She did not tell Elias what she was doing. She did not tell Tessa where the food was hidden. Some knowledge, she had learned, was a burden best carried alone.

The reassignment to the Garden was supposed to be a reward. But standing in her cold kitchen, looking at the vegetables hidden beneath her floor, Mara wondered if it was something else entirely. A test. A trap. A rope that, once pulled, could not be let go.

She realized it did not matter. Her daughter had tasted a fresh tomato. There was no going back from that.

Chapter 6:

The notice appeared on the morning of the second week. Pinned to the bulletin board in Greenhouse 14, typed on the same official paper as everything else.

*Effective immediately, the Garden Program will implement a quota system. Each greenhouse is expected to increase output by fifteen percent before the end of the quarter. Failure to meet targets will result in ration reduction for all greenhouse staff.*

Mara read it twice, then looked at Yulia. The older woman's face was unreadable.

"Fifteen percent," Mara said. "How?"

"We push the plants harder," Yulia said. "More fertilizer. Longer light hours. Less culling."

"Less culling means more imperfect fruit."

"Imperfect fruit is still fruit." Yulia picked up a watering can and began moving down the row, her motions precise and unhurried. "The Bureau doesn't care about appearance. They care about weight."

The work became harder after that. The days grew longer, the quotas more pressing. Mara found herself working through her meal break, eating standing up, her hands never leaving the soil. She took less from the compost bin, afraid that the reduced volume would be noticed. But she did not stop entirely.

At home, the stash beneath the floorboards grew. Tessa had stopped asking where it came from. She simply ate what was given and asked for more. Her cheeks had begun to fill out, the hollows beneath her eyes less pronounced. Elias noticed but said nothing.

It was Tessa who broke the silence, on a night when the wind rattled the windows and the coal fire burned low.

"The Resistance is organizing," she said.

Mara's hand stopped halfway to her mouth. "What resistance?"

"In the city. People who want to change things. Real change, not just petitions and protests."

"There is no resistance. The Security Corps would have—"

"The Security Corps is part of the problem." Tessa's voice was low, urgent. "I've met people, Mother. I've talked to them. They have a plan."

Mara set down her bread. "What kind of plan?"

"They want to take the Garden."

The words hung in the air between them, heavy and dangerous. Mara thought of the glasshouses, the fences, the armed guards. She thought of the food, enough to feed the city for months, maybe years, locked away for the benefit of the few.

"That's suicide."

"Maybe. But so is starving slowly."

"Tessa, listen to me. You cannot be part of this."

"I already am."

Mara felt the floor drop away. She grabbed her daughter's arm, her grip harder than she intended. "You will end this. Whatever you have agreed to, whatever promises you have made, you will end it."

"I won't."

"Tessa—"

"Mother, look at us." Tessa pulled her arm free, her eyes bright with frustration. "Look at what we've become. We live in fear of a piece of paper. We calculate our survival in grams. You sneak home vegetables like a criminal, and you're ashamed of it, but you do it anyway because you know it's wrong that they have so much and we have so little."

"The system is what it is."

"Then we change the system."

Mara wanted to argue, but the words would not come. Because Tessa was right. She knew Tessa was right. And that was the most terrible thing of all.

Chapter 7:

The contact came through Yulia.

It happened on a Thursday, three weeks into Mara's assignment to the Garden. The quota pressure had intensified; two women had been dismissed for failing to meet their targets, their rations cut by half. The remaining workers moved through the greenhouses in silence, their faces drawn with exhaustion and fear.

Yulia found Mara in the compost shed, sorting culled vegetables into bins. The old woman closed the door behind her and stood in the dim light, her shadow falling across the piles of spoiled produce.

"You've been taking food," Yulia said.

Mara's blood went cold. "I don't know what you mean."

"Don't lie to me. I've been watching you since your first week. You're careful, but not careful enough."

There was no point in denial. Mara set down the cabbage she had been holding and turned to face the other woman. "Are you going to report me?"

"Report you?" Yulia's weathered face split into something that might have been a smile. "I've been doing the same thing for six months. The difference is, I have a system."

"A system?"

Yulia reached into her coat and produced a small cloth bag. She loosened the drawstring and tipped the contents onto the sorting table: a handful of dried beans, a carrot, two small turnips. "The guards search us going out. They don't search the compost bins going in."

Mara looked at the vegetables, then at Yulia. "What do you want from me?"

"I want you to meet someone."

The meeting took place in a basement on the south side of the city, accessible through a bakery that had not produced bread in three years. Yulia led Mara down a flight of crumbling stairs and through a door that had been reinforced with steel plate.

The man inside was younger than she had expected. Perhaps thirty, with the lean, hungry look of someone who had spent years on short rations. But his eyes were sharp, and he moved with the confidence of someone who knew exactly where he stood.

"Garrick Voss," he said, extending his hand. "I run distribution for the eastern networks."

"Distribution of what?"

"Everything the Bureau doesn't want you to have." He gestured to a table covered in papers, maps, lists. "We move food from the Garden. Medical supplies from the warehouses. Information from inside the Bureau itself."

"I work in the Garden," Mara said carefully. "I'm not a revolutionary."

"No. You're a mother who wants to feed her family. That's more useful to me than a hundred revolutionaries." Garrick picked up a piece of paper and handed it to her. "Here's what I'm offering."

The paper listed quantities. Vegetables, mostly, but also grains and protein. Enough to feed her family for months. Enough to share.

"In exchange for what?"

"Information. Access. A few minutes unattended in Greenhouse 14 on the night of the new moon."

Mara read the list again. Then she folded it and put it in her pocket, alongside the ration tickets and the guilt she had been carrying since her first stolen tomato.

"I'll think about it."

"Don't think too long," Garrick said. "The clock is running for all of us."

She walked home through streets that seemed darker than before, the shadows deeper, the patrols more frequent. The city was changing. She was changing. And somewhere in the darkness, her daughter was already part of a plan that Mara had only just begun to understand.

Chapter 8:

The new moon was nine days away when Mara made her decision.

She did not arrive at it through careful deliberation. She arrived at it through Tessa, who came home on a Tuesday evening with blood on her knuckles and news of another protest broken up by the Corps. This time, people had been taken. Mara knew the building they were taken to. Everyone knew that building.

"You have to stop," Mara said, her voice breaking. "Tessa, you have to stop before they take you too."

"They will take me anyway," Tessa said. "Sooner or later. Everyone who speaks out gets taken. The only question is whether we make it count before they do."

Mara looked at her daughter's hands, scraped and bruised, and thought about all the things she had done to keep this girl alive. The extra portion of bread she had given up. The hours of sleep she had sacrificed to stand in queues. The quiet compliance, the folded tickets, the silent obedience to a system that was slowly killing them all.

She had stolen a tomato to feed her daughter. What would she steal to save her?

The next morning, she found Yulia in Greenhouse 14 and said, "I'll do it."

The plan was simple. On the night of the new moon, Mara would remain behind when the transport departed, hidden in the compost shed. She would disable the lock on the rear gate of Greenhouse 14. Garrick's people would enter, load the waiting crates, and leave before dawn. Mara would slip back into the compost shed, emerge when the morning transport arrived, and claim she had fallen asleep.

"There's a flaw," Mara said. "The search."

"You'll hide nothing. The transfer happens the same night. By morning, the food will be in fifty different kitchens across the city. Nobody will find it."

"And if I'm caught?"

Yulia met her eyes. "Then you shout. You say you were forced. You name me as the ringleader. I'm old. They will not execute an old woman for stealing vegetables."

"And the greenhouse lock?"

"I'll leave it with a fault. You will only need to trigger it."

The days passed in a blur of tension and routine. Mara went through her shifts mechanically, her hands performing the tasks she had learned while her mind ran through the plan again and again. Every patrol seemed to look at her longer. Every search seemed more thorough. Every conversation with the other workers seemed to carry a hidden meaning.

She stopped taking food from the compost bins. She could not risk suspicion now.

At home, she avoided Tessa's eyes. She had not told her daughter about the plan. She had not told Elias. She carried the secret alone, a weight that grew heavier with each passing day.

The evening before the new moon, Mara sat at the kitchen table and looked at her family. Elias was reading by the dim light of the oil lamp. Tessa was mending a tear in her coat. They looked ordinary. They looked safe.

"How was work?" Elias asked.

"Fine," Mara said. "The same as always."

She was lying to the people she loved most in the world. She was doing it to protect them. And she was doing it because she had finally understood something she should have known years ago: that the system would not change until people like her decided to change it.

Tonight, she would decide.

Tomorrow, she would act.

Chapter 9:

The transport left without her.

Mara had hidden in the compost shed twenty minutes before the end of the shift, pressing herself behind a stack of empty crates as the other women filed past. She heard the guard call roll. She heard her name, and the pause that followed, and the guard's voice again, impatient.

"Mara Estis?"

A beat of silence. Then Yulia's voice, calm and unconcerned, "She was in the latrine when I passed. Must have missed the call."

The guard grunted. More silence. Then the sound of boots on concrete, fading, and the rumble of the transport engine starting, and then nothing.

Mara waited in the dark, counting her breaths. The shed smelled of rot and earth, the sweet decay of vegetable matter. She had spent weeks in this place, sorting what the Garden deemed worthless. Tonight, she would prove that nothing in this world was truly worthless.

When she judged that enough time had passed, she pushed open the shed door and stepped into the night.

The Garden was different in the dark. The glasshouses glowed faintly, their panels catching the starlight, and the rows of crops stretched away into shadow. The perimeter lights cast pools of harsh white every twenty meters, leaving the spaces between them deep and dark.

Mara moved along the edge of the path, keeping to the shadows. Greenhouse 14 was at the far end of the row, and she had covered half the distance when she saw the patrol.

Two guards, walking the perimeter road. Their flashlights swept across the ground in lazy arcs. Mara dropped flat, pressing herself into the soil at the base of a cabbage plot. The light passed over her, once, twice. Then the guards moved on.

She counted to sixty before rising.

The lock on Greenhouse 14's rear gate was exactly where it should be, and Yulia had done her work well. A hairline crack ran across the casing, invisible to anyone not looking for it. Mara inserted the thin metal rod she had hidden in her boot and applied pressure. The lock gave with a click that sounded like a gunshot in the silence.

She slipped inside.

The greenhouse was warm, humid, alive with the smell of growing things. The tomato plants stood in rows, their fruits visible even in the dim light. Mara moved between them, her hands brushing the leaves, and reached the rear door.

She opened it.

Outside, in the darkness, shapes moved. Garrick's people, twelve of them, carrying empty crates. They entered without a word, spreading through the greenhouse like shadows. Within minutes, the first crates were full.

A woman touched Mara's arm. "Time to go."

"No," Mara said. "I stay. If I'm not on the transport in the morning, they'll know."

The woman hesitated, then nodded. She disappeared into the night with the others, and the door closed behind them.

Mara stood alone in the greenhouse, surrounded by the plants she had tended for weeks. She had fed her city tonight. Not much, not nearly enough, but something. She had taken from those who had too much and given to those who had nothing.

The lock clicked back into place behind her. She was alone again.

But as she made her way back to the compost shed, she heard something that made her blood run cold. A voice. A radio crackling. The patrol, but closer now, and running.

And behind her, distant but growing, the sound of an alarm.

Chapter 10:

The alarm did not stop.

Mara crouched in the compost shed, her back against the wall, her heart slamming against her ribs. The sound was everywhere, a pulsing wail that seemed to come from the ground itself. She heard running footsteps, shouted orders, the slam of vehicle doors.

They had been discovered. Something had gone wrong. Someone had been caught.

She had to get out.

The transport would not come for her now. The search would begin at the Garden and spread outward, into the city, into the streets where Tessa was probably still asleep, where Elias would be waking to the sound of sirens. They would find the empty crates in Greenhouse 14. They would find the broken lock. They would find her.

Unless she was not here to be found.

Mara pushed open the shed door and ran.

She did not follow the paths. She cut through the crops, her feet sinking into the soft soil, her breath coming in ragged gasps. The perimeter fence was ahead, topped with razor wire, but she had worked here long enough to know where the drainage ditch passed beneath it.

She found the grate exactly where she remembered it: rusted iron, half hidden by weeds. She pulled at it, the metal cold against her fingers, and it shifted. Not enough. She pulled again, using her whole body, and felt it give.

The gap was narrow, barely wide enough for her shoulders. She squeezed through, her coat tearing on the edge of the grate, and dropped into the ditch below. The water was cold, reaching her knees, and the smell was indescribable. She waded forward, bent double, her hands touching the slimy walls on either side.

The tunnel went for what felt like an eternity. She lost track of time, of distance, of anything except the need to keep moving. Her legs were numb. Her fingers were bleeding.

And then the tunnel opened, and she was out.

She emerged into a culvert beneath a road she did not recognize. The sirens were distant now, muted by the concrete above her. She climbed out of the ditch and stood on the roadside, shivering, her clothes soaked and filthy.

The city was dark. The patrols would be mobilizing. But for this moment, this single moment, she was invisible.

She began to walk.

The apartment was quiet when she reached it, four hours before dawn. She let herself in, stripped off her wet clothes, and stood in the dark kitchen, her skin goosebumped and raw. The box beneath the floorboards was still there. The food she had stolen was still there. Her family was still safe.

Elias found her in the morning, standing at the window, watching the smoke rise from the direction of the Garden. He did not ask where she had been. He did not ask about the state of her clothes, or the scratches on her hands, or the distant wail of sirens that had not stopped all night.

He simply stood beside her and watched the smoke, and after a long time he said, "Tessa is not here."

Mara turned. "What?"

"Tessa is not in her room. I checked when the alarm started. She's gone."

The world tilted. Mara grabbed the window frame to steady herself. Outside, the sirens continued, and the smoke rose higher, and somewhere in the city her daughter was doing exactly what she had taught her not to do.

Fighting.

"Elias, I have to find her."

"I know."

"I have to—"

"I know." He took her hand. "We'll find her together."

They walked out into a city that was changing, a city that had heard the alarm and was waking to something new. The streets were filling with people, not running from the sirens but moving toward them.

And at the front of the crowd, somewhere in the smoke and the noise, was her daughter.
