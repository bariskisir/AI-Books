Chapter 1: The Whiteout

The plane flew into a wall of white. One moment there was sky, a pale gray lid over the Greenland ice sheet. The next moment there was nothing — no horizon, no cloud, no difference between air and earth. The window showed only milk.

Erik Larsson gripped the yoke. His instruments spun. The altimeter unwound like a thread pulled from a spool. He had flown this route a hundred times. He knew the weather patterns, the katabatic winds that fell off the ice, the sudden fists of turbulence. He knew better than to fly into a whiteout.

He had been late. That was all. An hour late leaving Nuuk, and the passenger from the mining company had made it clear his time was expensive. Larsson had checked the forecast. He had seen the front moving in. He had gone anyway.

The plane shuddered. Ice formed on the wings. The stall warning chirped once, twice, then became a continuous shriek.

Behind him, the passengers felt the shift. Dr. Singh looked up from his notes. Richard Mercer tightened his seatbelt. Freja Lindholm pressed her palm flat against the cold window and closed her eyes. Sergeant Maya Chen had been asleep. She woke when the nose dropped.

Levi felt it in the cargo hold, curled between two duffel bags. The floor fell away beneath him. His stomach rose into his throat.

Larsson pulled back on the yoke. The engines screamed. The plane did not respond.

The ice rose to meet them.

Chapter 2: Impact

The first impact was not the ground. It was the wingtip catching a pressure ridge at forty degrees. The wing tore away like paper. The fuselage yawed hard, spun, and the world became a barrel of noise.

Dr. Singh's head struck the window. Blood ran into his eye. He tasted metal. Beside him, Mercer had both hands braced against the seat ahead. His face was white. His lips moved but made no sound.

In the cargo hold, Levi wrapped his arms around a duffel bag and held on. The metal around him howled. Something sharp cut his cheek. He did not feel it.

Freja Lindholm had her crash position memorized from a hundred safety briefings. She bent forward, hands over her head. She counted the seconds of impact, knowing the number did not matter, counting anyway.

Sergeant Chen did not brace. She had been in crashes before. Not in the air, but in vehicles that rolled and tumbled and came to rest upside down. She knew the trick was to stay loose, let the body go with the force, fight nothing. She went limp.

In the cockpit, Larsson did not close his eyes. He watched the ice come through the windshield. He watched it because he owed them that much — to see what he had done.

The fuselage dug a trench across the ice for two hundred meters. It shed pieces of itself like a snake shedding skin. Seats tore free. Bags exploded. Then it stopped.

Silence settled. Then the wind returned.

Chapter 3: Survivors

The first sound was a child crying. No, not a child — the tortured metal of the fuselage cooling, contracting, weeping in the subzero air.

Dr. Singh opened his eyes. His vision swam, then cleared. He was alive. He tested his limbs one by one. Left arm, right arm, left leg, right leg. All functional. His head ached. The blood on his face had begun to freeze.

He unbuckled and stood. The cabin was canted at fifteen degrees. Seats had torn from their rails. Baggage hung from overhead bins like sleeping bats.

Mercer was already up, patting his pockets, checking for his phone. His hands shook.

Freja had a gash across her forearm. She wrapped it with a scarf, methodical, unhurried.

Sergeant Chen pulled herself from a tangle of seats. Her right hand was numb. She flexed the fingers. They moved slowly.

"Anyone else?" Singh called out. His voice was steady. Surgeon's voice. Command voice.

The cockpit door was crumpled. Larsson sat motionless. His harness held him upright. His eyes were open.

From the rear, a sound. A cough. A scrape of movement.

And then, from the cargo hold, a knock. Three quick raps against the bulkhead.

Levi had found his way to the access panel. He pushed it open and looked out at the strangers. His face was dirty, cut, and scared. He did not cry.

Chapter 4: Triage

Dr. Singh worked by the light of a phone that would soon die. He had no gloves. The cold bit into his fingers, slowing them. He did not let it slow him.

Henrik was the worst. A construction worker from Copenhagen, he had been sitting near the wing root. The impact had folded the seat row around his right leg. The femur was compound-fractured, the bone visible through a tear in his coveralls. He was conscious, barely.

"Need to set this," Singh said. "Need to clean it."

No supplies. No antibiotics. No anesthetic.

"I can hold him," Chen said.

Singh looked at her. She met his gaze. She understood what he needed.

He counted to three and pulled. Henrik screamed. The bone ground against bone. Freja turned away. Levi watched.

Mercer found a first-aid kit behind the cockpit bulkhead. It contained gauze, tape, a tourniquet, and a small bottle of antiseptic. He brought it to Singh.

"Not enough," Singh said. "This man needs a hospital."

The words hung in the air. They all knew what he meant. There was no hospital. There was no help. There was only the ice.

Freja took stock of the others. Minor cuts, contusions, one possible concussion — Mercer, who refused to admit it. Hypothermia setting in already, visible in the shivers and the cyanotic lips.

"We need shelter," she said. "The fuselage will hold heat for a few hours. After that, we build."

They looked at her. She was a stranger. But she spoke with certainty.

Chapter 5: The Pilot

Erik Larsson had not moved from the cockpit. His hands were still on the yoke, frozen there by habit or shame. Blood trickled from a cut above his eyebrow. He did not wipe it away.

Chen found him when she went forward to assess the damage. The instrument panel was dead. The windshield was a spiderweb of cracks. Wind pushed through the gaps in a thin, persistent whine.

"Are you hurt?" she asked.

He shook his head.

"You need to come out. The others need to see you."

He turned to look at her. His eyes were hollow. "I ignored the weather report. I pushed through. I thought I could beat it."

She had seen that look before. On soldiers who made the wrong call. On men who came home when their squad did not.

"Get out of the seat," she said. Not kind, not cruel. Just a fact: the seat was useless now, and so was the guilt.

He unbuckled. He followed her into the cabin.

The others looked at him when he appeared. Mercer's face twisted with recognition. "You. You were the pilot."

"Yes."

"We trusted you."

"I know."

Mercer stepped forward. Chen blocked him.

"Not now," she said. "We need everyone alive. Sort it out later."

Larsson found a corner of the fuselage and sat down. He did not speak again for hours.

Chapter 6: The Stowaway

Levi sat apart from the adults, his knees drawn up to his chest. He had not spoken since emerging from the cargo hold. He watched them move, cataloging their faces, their voices, their smells.

Chen sat down next to him. Not too close. Close enough.

"What's your name?"

He looked at her.

"Levi."

"How old are you, Levi?"

"Twelve."

She nodded. "Where are your parents?"

He did not answer. She let the silence stretch, then expand, then break.

"I don't have any," he said. "I was in a home. In Nuuk. I didn't want to be there anymore."

"You stowed away."

He nodded.

"Where were you going?"

"Anywhere."

Chen looked at the others. They were all too busy with their own survival to notice a child. She looked back at him.

"Stay close to me. Help me if you can. We're going to get through this."

He did not believe her. But he nodded anyway, because she was the first adult in a long time who had spoken to him like he mattered.

Chapter 7: Inventory

Dawn came gray and bitter. The wind had not stopped. It whistled through the rents in the fuselage, carrying fine snow that glittered in the weak light.

Singh organized the inventory. Everyone brought what they had. The results were sobering.

One first-aid kit, partially depleted. Three liters of bottled water. A box of granola bars — twelve total. Two chocolate bars — Mercer's, which he produced reluctantly. A flask of whiskey — Larsson's, already half-empty. One pocketknife. One satellite phone, cracked screen, non-functional. One flare gun — no, wait. A flare gun was not on the list.

"Anyone have a flare gun?" Singh asked.

No one answered.

Levi kept his eyes on the floor.

Freja had a lighter, a compass, a wool blanket she used as a shawl. Chen had a survival knife, a coil of paracord, and a waterproof match case. Singh had a penlight and a stethoscope. Mercer had a wallet thick with euros and dollars.

"Money is useless here," Singh said.

"It's not useless everywhere," Mercer replied.

They took stock of the scattered wreckage. The plane had shed pieces across a wide debris field. Luggage, seats, structural panels. Chen and Freja would search the field later.

But one thing was clear: they did not have enough food or water to last a week.

Chapter 8: The Ration Kit

Levi found it while the adults argued. He had wandered to the edge of the debris field, looking for anything useful. The wind cut through his thin jacket. His fingers were already numb.

The crevasse was narrow — two meters across, maybe thirty deep. The ice walls glowed blue in the morning light. At the bottom, wedged between two rocks, was a yellow plastic case.

He lay flat on his stomach and reached down. His fingers brushed the handle. He stretched further. The ice was smooth and cold against his chest. He grabbed the handle and pulled.

The case was heavy. It came up with a scrape of plastic on ice.

He carried it back to the fuselage and set it down in front of the group. The clatter silenced the argument.

"What is that?" Mercer asked.

Levi opened the latches. The lid swung up.

Inside: meal packs. Twenty of them. Compact, high-calorie, designed for polar conditions. Water purification tablets. A small medical kit with antibiotics. A signaling mirror. A folding stove and fuel tablets.

Seven faces stared at the contents. No one spoke.

Then Mercer said what they were all thinking.

"One kit. Seven people. How do we decide who gets it?"

Chapter 9: Triage Logic

Singh answered Mercer's question before anyone else could. His voice was calm, clinical. He had been preparing for this conversation since the moment he saw the inventory.

"The kit has enough supplies for one person to survive seven days. Possibly longer if the food is rationed carefully. A rescue window of four days was predicted before the crash. With favorable conditions, search aircraft will be over this area within the week."

"Favorable conditions," Chen repeated. "We are in a whiteout."

"The whiteout will break. They always do. The question is how many of us can survive until then."

Singh laid out his logic. The group's resources were insufficient for seven people. The rate of consumption would outpace any realistic rescue timeline. The only way to maximize survival probability was to concentrate resources on the strongest candidate.

"The one with the highest chance of surviving to rescue should get the kit," he said. "Then walk out when the weather clears. Bring help back for the others."

"The others," Freja said. "You mean the ones left behind."

"Yes."

"Who performs this calculation?" she asked.

Singh did not flinch. "I do."

Chapter 10: The Argument

"It's murder," Freja said. "You are proposing we let five people die so one can live."

"I'm proposing we maximize survival probability. Those are different things."

No one spoke. Levi looked from face to face, watching the argument move through expressions before it reached words.

Mercer broke the silence. "I can pay. For the kit. I have thirty thousand in my account. I can wire it."

"You can't wire anything," Singh said. "There's no connection."

"Then when we get back."

"If we get back."

Chen listened. Her frostbitten fingers throbbed. She knew Singh's logic. She also knew that logic had limits.

"In the army," she said, "we had a rule. No one gets left behind. It wasn't practical. It cost lives sometimes. But it kept us human."

"Humanity is a luxury," Singh said. "Survival is mechanical."

"Then you'd let a child starve?"

The question dropped like a stone into still water.

Singh looked at Levi. The boy looked back. His face showed nothing.

"I would make the calculation based on physiology, not sentiment," Singh said.

"But you'd let him starve."

"I would let the math decide."

Chapter 11: Henrik's Offer

Henrik heard the argument from where he lay, wrapped in coats the others had given him. His leg was a ruin. Infection was setting in. He could smell it himself — the sweet wrongness of rot beneath the bandage.

"Give him the kit," he said.

The argument stopped. Everyone turned.

"He's the doctor. He knows what he's talking about. Give him the kit. Let him pick."

"That's not what I—" Singh started.

"Pick me," Henrik said. "And then don't give it to me. I'm dead already. We all know it. Use my portion for someone else."

His voice was flat. Practical. The voice of a man who had already done the calculation himself.

Freja knelt beside him. "Don't say that."

"It's true. I can feel it. The leg is going black. Another day, maybe two. Then the fever takes me."

"Then we treat it," Freja said.

"With what?"

She had no answer.

Singh examined the wound. He had been avoiding it. He knew what he would find. The skin was mottled purple above the bandage. The smell was unmistakable. Gas gangrene, likely. Untreatable in these conditions.

"I can amputate," he said quietly. "But I have no anesthetic. No sterile instruments. No way to close the wound properly."

"Then you'll kill me on the table," Henrik said. "Better to let me go naturally. Give the food to someone who can use it."

Mercer looked away. Levi stared at the floor.

Chapter 12: Survival

Freja took Chen and Levi out onto the ice. They needed supplies. They needed to understand their environment. And she needed to get away from the fuselage, from the argument, from the smell of Henrik's leg.

She had grown up in Denmark, but she had spent years in Greenland, living with Inuit families, learning their ways. The ice was not her enemy. It was a teacher.

"Look," she said, pointing at the snow surface. "See how it drifts around the pressure ridges. The wind packs it hard on the windward side. You can cut blocks from that. Build a shelter."

Chen followed her gaze. The ice stretched to every horizon, a white plain under a white sky. There was no feature, no landmark. Only the wreckage behind them and the cold ahead.

"Can you find water?" Chen asked.

Freja smiled. "We're sitting on it. Old ice is fresh. You melt it, you drink it. We'll never die of thirst here."

"Just of everything else."

They walked in silence for a while. Levi stayed close to Chen, his feet shuffling in his too-large shoes.

"We need to find the rest of the wreckage," Freja said. "There may be more supplies. More food. Maybe a working radio."

"And if there isn't?"

Freja did not answer.

Chapter 13: The Debris Field

The plane had broken apart in stages. The wingtip strike, the tumble, the slide. Each phase had shed pieces across the ice. Freja, Chen, and Levi spent the morning walking the debris trail.

They found scattered luggage, most of it burst open, clothes frozen stiff. Chen picked through a woman's suitcase, looking for anything useful — warm layers, waterproof fabric, medications. She found a bottle of ibuprofen and a fleece jacket that fit Levi.

Levi found a backpack with a half-eaten sandwich inside. The bread was frozen, the meat still edible. He brought it to Chen.

"Good find," she said.

They found the tail section another two hundred meters out. It had separated cleanly and come to rest upright. Inside, the toilets had frozen and burst. But the emergency transmitter was still there.

Chen climbed into the wreckage and pulled it free. The casing was cracked. She pressed the test button. Nothing.

"Dead," she said.

Freja pointed at the ridge line. "There's more debris beyond that. Maybe the cargo compartment."

They walked on. The wind picked up, driving snow into their faces. Levi pulled the fleece hood tight. He did not complain.

They found cargo containers scattered across a kilometer of ice. Most were empty, their contents strewn across the frozen plain. But one was intact.

Chen pried it open. Inside: blankets. A box of high-energy biscuits. A case of bottled water. And a medical kit — larger than the one from the fuselage.

"Christmas," Chen said.

They loaded what they could carry and began the walk back. The fuselage was a dark speck on the white horizon. It took them an hour to reach it.

Chapter 14: The Calculation

Singh organized the new supplies with military precision. The biscuits were divided into seven equal piles. The water was portioned. The medical kit was opened, inventoried, rationed.

"We have three days of food at half-rations," he announced. "Five if we stretch to quarter-rations."

"That's not enough," Mercer said.

"No. It's not."

Singh looked at the ration kit. The yellow case sat in the corner of the fuselage, untouched since Levi brought it in. Everyone looked at it.

"If one person takes the kit," Singh said, "that person can survive two weeks. Maybe longer with the water purifier. The rest of us would need to survive on what we have until rescue comes."

"Or until we don't," Chen said.

"Correct."

Freja stood up. "I will not participate in this calculation. I will share what I have with whoever needs it. That is the only answer I can give."

"Admirable," Singh said. "And impractical."

"Practicality is not the only virtue."

"On the ice, it is."

Levi watched them argue. He had seen adults fight before. In the group home, they fought over funding, over policy, over whose fault it was that the children went hungry. The arguments were always the same. The stakes were just higher now.

He looked at the ration kit. It was just a box. But it had become something else. It had become a question that no one wanted to answer.

He wondered if they would kill each other before the ice did the job.

Chapter 15: The Pilot's Confession

Night came early at this latitude. The sky darkened to deep violet, then black. The stars emerged, hard and bright and utterly indifferent.

Larsson had not eaten. He had not taken his portion. He sat apart from the others, his back against the twisted metal of the cockpit frame.

Freja brought him a biscuit and a cup of melted snow.

"Eat," she said.

"I'm not hungry."

"Hunger is irrelevant. You need the calories."

He took the biscuit. He held it without eating.

"I was drinking," he said.

She sat down beside him.

"Before the flight. I had a flask in my jacket. I told myself it was just for the cold. But it wasn't. I was scared. I knew the weather was turning. I drank to push through it."

"How much?"

"Enough to dull my judgment. Not enough to fail a test. But enough."

She did not say anything. She let the silence hold the confession.

"If I hadn't been drinking," he said, "I would have turned back. I would have waited out the front. They would all be alive and warm and nowhere near this ice."

"You don't know that."

"Yes. I do."

He set the biscuit down. He had not taken a bite.

Freja picked it up and pressed it into his hand. "Eat. You will have time to regret later. Right now, you need to survive. We all do."

He looked at her. His eyes were wet. The cold had already frozen the tears on his lashes.

Chapter 16: The Soldier's Hands

Chen sat alone outside the fuselage, watching the stars. Her hands ached. She flexed them slowly, testing the range of motion. The fingers were stiff. The tips were white.

Frostbite. Early stage, but advancing.

She had seen it before. In Norway, during the winter exercise that went wrong. Two of her squad had lost toes to frostbite. One lost fingers. The medics said it was a function of time and temperature and circulation. Her circulation was good. But the time was running.

She had not told Singh. He would add it to his calculation. He would factor her diminishing capabilities into his survival algorithm. She did not want to be a variable.

Levi came out and sat beside her. He did not say anything. He just sat, his small shoulder near her arm.

"It's cold," she said.

"I know."

"Why aren't you inside?"

"Same reason you are," he said. "Too many people."

She almost smiled.

"Show me your hands," he said.

She hesitated. Then she held them out.

He looked at the white fingertips. He did not flinch.

"My mother had hands like that," he said. "Before she died. She worked in a fish processing plant. The cold water did it."

Chen looked at him. He was twelve. He had already seen more than a child should.

"She used to put them in warm water," he said. "Not hot. Warm. She said hot made it worse."

"Your mother was right."

They sat in silence, watching the stars wheel slowly overhead.

Chapter 17: The Businessman's Offer

Dawn came gray and bitter. Mercer had not slept. He had spent the night calculating his own odds, and he did not like the numbers.

He approached Singh while the others were packing snow for water.

"I'll buy the kit," he said. "Name your price."

"There is no price."

"Everything has a price."

Singh looked at him. "Money is paper. On the ice, paper is fuel."

"I have a company. Resources. I can wire funds when we're rescued. Ten thousand. Twenty. Enough to change your life."

"My life is already changed."

Mercer's voice hardened. "I'm not asking permission. I'm making an offer. The kit is not yours. It belongs to the group."

"The group has not decided what to do with it."

"Then let the group decide. Democracy."

Singh shook his head. "Democracy is for people who have time. We do not have time."

He walked away, leaving Mercer standing in the snow.

Mercer watched him go. His hands were shaking — from cold, from rage, from fear. He had never been in a situation his money could not fix. The realization that this was such a situation was the most terrifying thing he had ever faced.

Chapter 18: Freja's Wisdom

Freja gathered the group in the lee of the fuselage. The wind had dropped, and the sun was a pale disc behind the clouds. It was the warmest they had been since the crash.

She had built a small fire from fuel-soaked debris and was melting snow in a metal cup. The water would be distributed equally. She made sure of it.

"The Greenlandic people have survived here for thousands of years," she said. "Not because they were stronger than the ice. Because they shared. When a hunter caught a seal, the whole village ate. When someone fell through the ice, everyone helped pull them out."

"This is not a village," Mercer said. "This is seven strangers."

"Seven strangers is a village. All a village is, is people who decide to share."

She passed the cup around. Each person took a sip. When it reached Levi, he drank deeply before passing it to Chen.

"The old way," Freja continued, "is to treat the group as one body. If the foot freezes, the hands warm it. If the stomach is empty, the mouth does not eat alone."

"That's poetic," Singh said. "But poetry does not create calories."

"No. But it creates the will to survive. And without that, the calories do not matter."

Singh had no answer. He took the cup and drank.

Chapter 19: The Wound

Henrik's leg was worse. The black had spread to his knee. The smell filled the fuselage, sweet and sick. Levi had to go outside to breathe.

Singh examined him by the light of the penlight. The infection had entered the bloodstream. Henrik's face was gray. His pulse was thready.

"I can amputate," Singh said again. "It is the only chance."

"Do it," Henrik whispered.

"I have nothing to stop the bleeding. No proper tools."

"Then I will bleed out. But at least I will die trying."

Singh prepared what he could. The survival knife, sterilized in the fire. Bandages torn from clothing. A tourniquet from the first-aid kit.

Chen held Henrik down. Freja held the light. Levi stayed outside, his back to the fuselage, his hands over his ears.

Singh cut.

Henrik screamed. The sound carried across the ice, thin and sharp and utterly alone.

Then the tourniquet held. The bleeding slowed. Singh packed the wound with gauze and bound it tight.

Henrik passed out. His breathing was shallow but steady.

Singh sat back. His hands were covered in blood. They were shaking.

He had not been steady in years — not since the patient in Delhi, the one he had walked away from. He had thought the ice would be different. He had been wrong.

Chapter 20: Aftermath

The amputation took something from the group. They moved more slowly, spoke more quietly. The fuselage felt smaller, the air thicker.

Henrik drifted in and out of consciousness. Chen watched him between shifts. She checked his pulse, his breathing, the color of his remaining flesh.

"You're not a medic," Singh said.

"I know."

"Then why are you watching him?"

"Because someone should."

Singh sat down opposite her. His hands were clean now, but he still saw the blood on them.

"I was a surgeon in Delhi," he said. "Good one. Trauma specialist. I saved lives every day."

"Why did you leave?"

The question hung between them.

"I lost a patient," he said. "A girl. Seventeen. Stabbed in a street fight. I operated for six hours. I did everything right. She died anyway."

"That happens."

"Her father came to the hospital the next day. He had a knife. He said I had killed his daughter. He said I was a butcher, not a doctor."

"What did you do?"

"I walked away. From the hospital. From Delhi. From everything."

"But you didn't stop being a doctor."

"I stopped being a good one."

Chen looked at him. In the dim light, his face was gaunt, haunted.

"You just saved a man's life," she said. "With a pocketknife and a bottle of vodka. That makes you a good doctor."

He did not answer.

Chapter 21: The Map

Freja had been drawing. Using charcoal from the fire, she sketched on a piece of flattened cardboard. Lines, ridges, features. A map of the terrain she had observed.

"We are here," she said, pointing to a mark near the center. "About seventy kilometers east of the coast. There are research stations scattered along the coast. Some are staffed year-round."

"How far to the nearest one?" Chen asked.

"Fifty kilometers. Maybe less."

A murmur went through the group. Fifty kilometers. Walkable, if the weather held. If they had supplies. If they had strength.

"I can make the walk," Chen said.

"With frostbite," Singh said.

"I can still walk."

"The question is not whether you can walk. The question is whether you can walk fifty kilometers and then lead a rescue team back."

Chen looked at her hands. The fingers were worse now. The white had spread to the first knuckles.

"Someone has to go," she said.

"Why you?"

"Because I'm the only one here with cold-weather training."

"I'm a bush pilot," Larsson said quietly. "I've walked out of crashes before."

"You were drinking," Mercer said. "You said so yourself."

"That was one time."

"One time we know about."

The argument flared, then died. No one had energy for it.

Freja looked at the map. "We do not have to decide today. But we will have to decide soon."

Chapter 22: The Signal

Levi had not forgotten the flare gun. It was hidden in his jacket, the weight a secret he carried against his chest.

He took it out when the adults were arguing. He examined it in the dim light. The gun was orange plastic, the flare cartridge seated in the breach. One shot.

He had seen flares before. In Nuuk, the fishing boats used them when they ran into trouble. Bright red, visible for miles.

He could fire it now. But what would be the point? If no one was looking, the flare was just a light that died.

He needed to wait. Wait until he heard the sound of an engine. Wait until he saw a shape in the sky.

He put the gun back in his jacket.

Later, Chen found him sitting alone, watching the horizon.

"You okay?" she asked.

He nodded.

"What are you looking at?"

"The sky," he said. "Waiting for something."

She sat down beside him. "That's all any of us are doing."

They watched the horizon together. The sky was the color of old bone. No planes came.

Chapter 23: Day Four

The fourth day began with frost on every surface. Inside the fuselage, breath hung in clouds. The cold had penetrated the metal, the insulation, the bones.

Singh distributed breakfast: half a biscuit and a sip of water each. No one complained. They were past complaining.

Henrik was lucid for the first time since the amputation. His eyes were clear. His voice was weak but steady.

"Water," he said.

Freja brought him a cup. He drank slowly, then lay back.

"How bad is it?" he asked.

"Bad," Singh said. "But you're alive."

"I shouldn't be."

"No. You shouldn't."

Henrik laughed, a dry rasp. "You're honest. I appreciate that."

"I don't know how to be anything else."

"Then tell me the truth. Will I make it?"

Singh paused. He looked at the wound. The black had stopped spreading, but the flesh around it was angry and swollen. Infection was still present, merely contained.

"I don't know," he said.

"That's honest enough."

Henrik closed his eyes. His breathing slowed. For a moment, they thought he had slipped away. But then his chest rose again.

"Keep fighting," Freja whispered. "We need you."

She did not know if he heard. But she said it anyway.

Chapter 24: The Split

Singh called a meeting. The group gathered in a circle, huddled in their coats, breath steaming.

"We need to decide about the kit," he said.

"We already decided," Freja said. "We share."

"Sharing means we all die slowly instead of one person surviving."

"Or it means we all survive until rescue."

"The odds do not support that."

Chen spoke. "What are the odds, exactly?"

Singh pulled a piece of paper from his pocket. He had been calculating all night. "Based on our current consumption rate, the remaining food will last two more days at half-rations. After that, we have nothing."

"Two days," Mercer said. "Rescue is four days out."

"That's if the weather clears."

"The weather never clears here."

"Exactly."

The circle was silent. The wind picked up, rattling the fuselage.

"I'm not going to let a child starve," Chen said quietly.

"Then what do you propose?" Singh asked.

"We split the kit. Take half the supplies for the group. Reserve half for whoever makes the walk."

"That halves the survival time for the walker."

"Better than nothing."

Singh considered it. The logic was flawed — half a ration kit was not enough for a full trek. But it was better than the current trajectory.

"Agreed," he said.

Freja looked at him with surprise.

"Half for the group," Singh said. "Half for the walker. We draw lots for who goes."

No one argued.

Chapter 25: The Lot

Chen found a piece of broken plastic from the fuselage. She broke it into seven pieces, each roughly the same size. On one, she marked a small X.

"Whoever draws the X makes the walk," she said.

She held them in her closed fist. The group gathered around.

Mercer drew first. A blank.

Freja drew. Blank.

Larsson drew. Blank.

Henrik, lying on his side, reached up and drew. Blank.

Singh drew. Blank.

Seven pieces. Two remaining. Chen and Levi.

Levi reached into her hand. He did not hesitate. He pulled out a piece and looked at it.

Blank.

Chen opened her hand. The last piece had the X.

"You don't have to go," Freja said.

"Yes, I do."

"Your hands—"

"Will be fine."

She looked at the piece. The X was small, black, final.

"I walked in Norway," she said. "I can walk here."

"Fifty kilometers," Singh said. "On ice. With frostbite."

"I've done worse."

She did not say goodbye. She packed the supplies — half the ration kit, the compass, the map, the matches. She took the survival knife.

Levi watched her prepare. He wanted to give her the flare gun. But he held back. He might need it still.

Chen looked at the group. "I'll bring help. You just hold on."

She walked into the white.

Chapter 26: The Walk

Chen walked south by southeast. The compass was steady in her hand. The ice stretched ahead, flat and featureless. Each step was the same as the last.

Her fingers throbbed. The cold had penetrated the gloves. She flexed them, felt the stiffness, the loss of sensation.

She had been trained for this. The army had taught her to walk in extreme cold. She knew how to regulate her temperature, how to manage sweat, how to preserve energy.

Knowing and doing were different things.

The wind picked up. Fine snow began to drift across the ice. The visibility dropped. The world became a narrow corridor of white.

She kept walking.

After an hour, she stopped to rest. She ate a quarter of an energy bar and drank a sip of water. The wind bit through her layers.

She thought about the others, huddled in the fuselage. She thought about Levi, his small face, his hidden flare gun. He thought she had not noticed. She had.

She pushed on.

The horizon wavered. The line between ice and sky blurred. She checked the compass. Still south by southeast.

One step. Another. Another.

She did not look back.

Chapter 27: The Long Wait

Back at the fuselage, the hours crawled. Without Chen, the group felt smaller, more fragile. She had been a presence — quiet, capable, steady. Her absence was a hole.

Levi sat in the corner, his hand on the flare gun in his pocket. He watched the sky through a gap in the fuselage. Nothing moved out there.

Singh checked Henrik's wound. The black had not spread further. The infection was holding, but only barely.

"Can you walk?" Singh asked.

"No."

"Then we carry you."

"Carry me where?"

"To the rescue."

Henrik smiled, weak and rueful. "You're a stubborn man, Doctor."

"I know."

Freja went outside to check the snow conditions. The surface was hard-packed, good for walking. But the wind was rising again. A new front was moving in.

"We may have another whiteout," she said.

"How bad?"

"Bad enough."

Mercer sat apart from the others. He had not spoken since the draw. His face was gray, his eyes hollow. He had lost something in that moment — not the chance to walk, but the illusion that he was in control.

He had always been in control. Money bought control. But the ice did not take money.

He looked at his hands. They were shaking.

Chapter 28: The Storm

The whiteout hit in the afternoon. It came fast, a wall of wind and snow that swallowed the fuselage whole. The temperature dropped. The wind howled through the gaps, carrying ice that stung like needles.

Freja had seen storms like this before. She knew they could last hours or days. There was nothing to do but wait.

The group huddled together for warmth. Body heat was their only defense now.

Levi was pressed between Freja and Singh. He was shivering violently. Singh put an arm around him. The boy did not pull away.

"Tell me a story," Levi said.

"Now?" Singh asked.

"Storms are for stories. That's what my mother said."

Singh was silent for a moment. Then he began.

"There was a surgeon in Delhi. He was very good at his job. He thought he could save everyone."

"Did he?"

"No. He could not save the one who mattered most."

"Why not?"

"Because some things are beyond medicine. Some things are beyond control."

Levi looked up at him. "Is that why you're here? Because you couldn't save her?"

"Something like that."

The fuselage creaked. The wind screamed. But the story continued, and the group listened, and the cold did not seem quite so absolute.

Chapter 29: Chen's Night

Chen found shelter in a crevasse. The ice walls rose on either side, glowing blue. The wind passed overhead. It was quiet, almost peaceful.

She had walked eight hours. Maybe fifteen kilometers. Maybe less. Distance was hard to judge in the white.

She checked her fingers. The frostbite had spread. The tips were black now. She could not feel them.

She knew what that meant. She would lose the fingers. Maybe the hand.

But that was a problem for later. For now, she needed to rest.

She ate another quarter-bar. Drank the last of her water. She would need to melt snow in the morning.

She thought about the group. About Levi. About the yellow ration kit and the argument it had caused.

She had volunteered for the walk because it was the right thing to do. But also because she could not bear the debate anymore — the careful calculation of who deserved to live.

Better to walk. Better to act. Better to freeze with purpose than to argue while the ice claimed them one by one.

She closed her eyes. The cold was a blanket. It did not let her sleep deeply, but it let her rest.

Tomorrow, she would walk again.

Chapter 30: Morning

Dawn broke clear and cold. The storm had passed. The sky was a hard, deep blue. The sun was a white coin low on the horizon.

Freja was the first to emerge. She checked the snow surface, the visibility, the wind. The conditions were good. Better than good.

She woke the others.

"We have a window," she said. "Maybe a day. Maybe less."

Singh distributed the last of the biscuits. Half a biscuit each. The portions were pitiful, but no one complained.

Levi noticed that Singh had not taken his portion. He had divided it among the others.

"You didn't eat," Levi said.

"I'm not hungry."

"Liar."

Singh looked at him. The boy's eyes were sharp, accusing.

"Adults lie to children to protect them," Singh said.

"That's a stupid reason."

"Maybe."

But Levi broke his own biscuit in half and held out the piece to Singh. "Eat."

Singh stared at the offered food. His throat tightened.

"You need it more than I do," Singh said.

"I need you alive more than I need a full stomach."

Singh took the half-biscuit. He ate it slowly. It was the most humbling meal of his life.

Chapter 31: The Sound

They heard it in the late morning.

A distant drone, thin and mechanical. An engine.

Everyone froze. The sound grew louder, then began to fade.

"A plane," Freja said.

"We need a signal," Singh said.

Levi's hand went to his jacket. The flare gun. He pulled it out.

"How long have you had that?" Mercer demanded.

"Since the first day."

"Why didn't you tell us?"

"Because I didn't trust you."

Levi ran outside, cocked the gun, and fired.

The flare shot into the sky, a bright red star against the blue. It climbed, arced, and began to fall. It was beautiful and terrible.

The engine sound faded. The plane was leaving.

No. It was turning.

The drone grew louder. The plane was coming back.

Levi watched it appear — a small speck against the blue, growing larger. He did not move. He stood in the snow, the empty flare gun in his hand, and watched rescue approach.

Chapter 32: The Rescue

The plane circled once, then twice. It was a Twin Otter, skis on the landing gear. It came in low, assessing the debris field, the survivors, the feasibility of landing.

The pilot found a stretch of relatively flat ice and set down. The skis skidded, caught, and held.

A door opened. A woman in a parka leaned out.

"How many?" she called.

"Seven," Singh shouted back. "But one is out on the ice. She left yesterday. Heading south by southeast."

The woman nodded. She pulled out a radio and spoke rapidly in Danish.

"We have a ground team in the area," she said. "They can search for your walker. Let's get you out of here."

The survivors gathered their meager belongings. Henrik was carried on a makeshift stretcher. He was barely conscious.

Levi was the last to board. He looked back at the wreckage, at the yellow ration kit still sitting in the fuselage. They had not needed it in the end.

He climbed into the plane. The door closed.

The Twin Otter lifted off and banked south.

Chapter 33: The Search

The ground team found Chen six hours later. She had collapsed half a kilometer from the research station. Her fingers were black. Her lips were blue. Her pulse was faint.

They carried her inside, stripped her wet clothing, wrapped her in warm blankets. The station medic worked on her for an hour, slowly bringing her temperature up.

When she opened her eyes, the first thing she saw was a ceiling. A real ceiling. Warmth. Light.

"Did they make it?" she asked.

"They're all alive," the medic said. "Thanks to you."

Chen closed her eyes. She did not cry. Soldiers did not cry. But something loosened in her chest, something she had been holding since the crash.

She slept.

In the fuselage, Levi's flare had been spotted by the search plane fifteen minutes before she would have passed the point of no return. The timing was that thin. The margin that narrow.

But it held.

Chapter 34: Nuuk

The hospital in Nuuk was small, modern, and overwhelmed. The survivors filled every available bed.

Henrik was taken into surgery immediately. The amputation site was infected, but treatable. He would lose the leg, but he would live.

Chen arrived by helicopter the next day. Her hands were wrapped in bandages. The frostbite had claimed three fingertips. She would recover.

Singh sat in the hospital corridor, staring at nothing. He had not slept. He could not sleep.

A nurse brought him tea. He drank it without tasting it.

"You saved them," Freja said, sitting beside him.

"The pilot found us."

"The pilot found us because Levi fired a flare. And Levi fired a flare because you kept the group alive long enough for him to do it."

Singh shook his head. "I argued about the ration kit. I talked about triage. I was ready to let people die."

"You were preparing for the worst. That is not a crime."

"It feels like one."

Freja put her hand on his arm. "We are all alive. That is what matters. The rest is just noise."

Singh looked at her. He wanted to believe her. He was not sure he could.

Chapter 35: The Debrief

The transportation authority interviewed each survivor separately. They wanted to know what happened. They wanted to understand the chain of decisions that led to the crash and its aftermath.

Larsson sat alone in a small room. His hands were flat on the table in front of him. He did not fidget.

"Were you intoxicated during the flight?" the investigator asked.

"Yes."

"Can you estimate your blood alcohol level at the time of the crash?"

"No."

"Why did you fly into the whiteout?"

"I was late. I thought I could beat it."

The investigator made notes. The silence stretched.

"Your license will be revoked," the investigator said.

"I know."

"There will be legal consequences."

"I know."

Larsson looked at his hands. They were clean, steady. They had not been steady in years.

"I'm sorry," he said. "For all of it."

The investigator looked at him. "Sorry does not undo anything."

"No. But it's where I have to start."

Chapter 36: The Question

Levi sat in a social worker's office. The woman across the desk was kind. She had a soft voice and a gentle smile. He did not trust her.

"Your file from Nuuk says you ran away," she said.

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because the home was not a home. It was a place where children waited. I did not want to wait anymore."

"Where were you going?"

"Anywhere."

The social worker nodded. She made notes.

"The airline is arranging transportation for the survivors," she said. "They've offered to cover your return to Nuuk, or to any other destination in Greenland."

"I want to stay."

"Stay where?"

"Here. With the others."

The social worker hesitated. "That's not how it works, Levi. You're a minor. You need a guardian."

"Then find me one."

She looked at him. His face was serious, determined. He had survived a plane crash and a whiteout. He had fired the flare that saved them all.

"I'll see what I can do," she said.

Levi nodded. It was not a promise. But it was not a no.

Chapter 37: The Visit

Chen woke to find Levi sitting beside her bed. His hands were folded in his lap. He looked small in the hospital chair.

"You came back," he said.

"I promised I would."

"Your hands."

"Will heal. Mostly."

He looked at the bandages. The white wrapping, the careful knots.

"Can you still hold things?"

"Not yet. But I will learn again."

He nodded. He had known people who lost parts of themselves. In Nuuk, the fishermen lost fingers to the lines, the cold, the machinery. They adapted.

"Where will you go?" he asked.

"Back to the army, probably. Medical discharge. They'll find something for me."

"That's not what I meant."

She looked at him. In the dim light of the hospital room, his eyes were old. Older than twelve years should be.

"I don't know," she said. "I haven't thought that far ahead."

"Think now."

She leaned back on the pillow. The ceiling was white, blank, full of possibilities.

"I could stay in Greenland," she said. "For a while. There are things I could do here."

"And me?"

"If you want me to stay, I'll stay."

Levi looked at her. Something in his face shifted. A looseness, a softening.

"Okay," he said.

Chapter 38: The Doctor's Reckoning

Singh had not left the hospital. He sat in the waiting room, watching the clock, watching the door. He did not know what he was waiting for.

A man approached him. Middle-aged, Greenlandic, dressed in a heavy coat.

"Dr. Singh?"

"Yes."

"I am Tupaarnaq. Henrik's sister."

Singh stood. He did not know what to say.

"Your name was on the surgery authorization," she said. "My brother told me what you did. On the ice."

"I did what I could."

"You saved his life. With a pocketknife and a bottle of vodka."

"Yes."

She looked at him. Her eyes were dark, searching.

"My brother is alive because you were there. I came to thank you."

Singh opened his mouth. Closed it. He did not know how to receive gratitude.

"I was ready to let him die," he said.

"But you didn't."

"I wanted to. It would have been easier."

She shook her head. "What you wanted and what you did are two different things. The second one is the one that counts."

She held out her hand. He took it.

"Thank you," she said again. Then she walked away.

Singh sat back down. His hands were shaking. But he was smiling.

Chapter 39: Mercer's Reckoning

Mercer sat in his hotel room, staring at his phone. It was connected again. He had three hundred emails, forty missed calls, and a world that had moved on without him.

His company was running. His deals were being made. He was replaceable.

He had always known this intellectually. The ice had taught him it was true.

He picked up his phone and called his wife.

"Richard," she said. "You're alive."

"I'm alive."

"Thank God. The news said there were survivors. I've been calling."

"I know. I'm sorry. I didn't know what to say."

A pause. "Say you're coming home."

"I am. I will."

"You sound different."

"I am different."

He meant it. The ice had stripped away something — the certainty, the armor. He did not know what would grow in its place.

But he was curious to find out.

He booked a flight home. He did not check his email again.

Chapter 40: The Anthropologist

Freja stood on the hospital roof, looking at the ice. The fjord was frozen solid. The mountains in the distance were white and blue and utterly beautiful.

Singh found her there.

"You should be resting," he said.

"I needed air."

He stood beside her, looking at the same view.

"Will you stay in Greenland?" he asked.

"Of course. This is my home."

"I thought you were Danish."

"I am. But Greenland is where I belong."

He nodded. He understood the difference.

"What about you?" she asked.

"I don't know. I left Delhi. I cannot go back."

"Then go forward."

"To where?"

She turned to look at him. "The ice teaches you where you need to be. If you listen."

"I did not learn anything from the ice. I learned how little I know."

"That is the lesson."

He looked at her. She smiled. For the first time since the crash, he felt something like peace.

Chapter 41: The Pilot's Sentence

The transportation board revoked Larsson's license. He was grounded, pending a full investigation. There would be charges — criminal negligence, endangerment. He would likely serve time.

He accepted the news without argument.

"You have nothing to say?" the investigator asked.

"I have nothing to justify."

"You could lose your freedom."

"Freedom is a strange word. I have not been free since I flew into that whiteout."

The investigator did not know what to make of him. Most pilots fought. They blamed the weather, the equipment, the air traffic controller. Larsson blamed no one but himself.

He was not being noble. He was being honest.

He thought about the flask in his jacket, the warmth it promised, the judgment it cost.

He thought about the seven people on the plane. The way they had looked at him when he emerged from the cockpit.

He had been drinking. He had killed them. Not literally — they were alive. But he had brought them to the edge of death, and that was the same thing.

He would serve his time. He would do it without complaint.

It was the least he owed them.

Chapter 42: The Ward

Henrik was moved to the general ward. The amputation site was healing. The infection was under control. He would live.

He sat propped against pillows, looking at the stump where his leg had been. It was strange — the absence, the phantom sensation. He had imagined losing a leg. He had not imagined how much it would weigh on the rest of him.

Freja visited him. She brought flowers — not real ones, but a potted plant from the hospital lobby.

"Thank you," he said.

"For the plant?"

"For being there. On the ice. For not letting me give up."

"You did not give up. Your body fought."

"My body. My mind wanted to quit."

She sat beside him. "The mind lies when it is afraid. The body knows the truth."

"You sound like you've been through this before."

"Not this. But I have seen people die on the ice. The ones who survive are the ones who choose to."

"Then I chose well."

"Yes. You did."

He looked at the window. Outside, the mountains were white and distant.

"I will learn to walk again," he said.

"With a prosthetic?"

"With whatever I can find."

She smiled. "Good."

Chapter 43: The Group

The survivors gathered one last time before going their separate ways. The hospital cafeteria. Gray light through tall windows. Plastic chairs.

Mercer had arranged it. He had paid for the coffee. No one mentioned it.

They sat in an awkward circle, seven people who had shared everything and nothing.

"I wanted to say," Mercer began, "that I am sorry. For the way I acted. The money. The offers. I was afraid."

"We were all afraid," Freja said.

"That does not excuse it."

"No," Singh said. "But it explains it."

Silence. The coffee grew cold.

"I have something," Levi said.

He reached into his jacket and pulled out a piece of plastic — a fragment of the yellow ration kit. He had kept it as a memento.

"I want you all to have this," he said. "To remember."

He broke the plastic into seven pieces and passed them around.

Each person took one.

"It was never about the kit," Levi said. "It was about what we did with it."

Singh looked at his piece. It was small, irregular, worthless.

He put it in his pocket. He would keep it forever.

Chapter 44: Departure

They left Nuuk on different flights. Different destinations. Different futures.

Larsson went south to face the courts. Mercer went west to his company and his family. Henrik flew to Copenhagen for rehabilitation. Chen stayed in Greenland, at least for now.

Freja drove Singh to the airport.

"Where will you go?" she asked.

"East, I think. There is a clinic in Tasiilaq that needs a doctor."

"You're going back to work."

"I never stopped being a surgeon. I just forgot why I started."

"And why did you start?"

"To help people. I lost that somewhere. On the ice, I found it again."

She smiled. "Then the ice gave you something."

"It gave me nothing. But it took away what I did not need."

They stood at the terminal entrance. The wind was cold, but not bitter. Not anymore.

"Will you write?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Good."

She hugged him. He stiffened, then relaxed. It had been a long time since anyone had hugged him.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For being who you are. Even the parts you do not like."

She let him go. He walked into the terminal.

He did not look back. But he thought about her for a long time.

Chapter 45: The Clinic

Tasiilaq was a small town on the east coast, accessible only by boat or helicopter. The clinic was a single-story building with three rooms and a constant shortage of supplies.

Singh arrived in February. The cold was familiar now. It did not frighten him.

The clinic staff was skeptical of the new doctor — an outsider, a surgeon from Delhi, a man who had been in a plane crash. But they needed help, so they gave him a chance.

His first patient was a fisherman with a cut that had gone septic. Singh cleaned it, stitched it, prescribed antibiotics. The man survived.

His second patient was a child with pneumonia. The clinic had no ventilator. Singh stayed up for two nights, monitoring the child's breathing, adjusting the oxygen. The child recovered.

His third patient was an elderly woman who had been waiting for a hip replacement for three years. Singh could not do the surgery here — not without an operating room. But he could manage her pain. He could listen.

He sat with her for an hour, holding her hand.

"Why did you come here?" she asked.

"Because I needed to help people."

"That is a good reason."

"Yes. I think so too."

Chapter 46: The Soldier's New Post

Chen stayed in Nuuk. The army granted her a medical discharge. She took a job at the search and rescue coordination center.

Her hands had healed. She had lost two fingertips on her left hand and one on her right. She could still type, still write, still hold a cup. She could still do her job.

She coordinated rescue missions for people lost on the ice. Fishermen, hunters, hikers, pilots. People who had made mistakes or been unlucky. People who needed someone to find them.

Every time a call came in, she thought about the fuselage. She thought about the yellow ration kit. She thought about Levi.

Levi had been placed with a foster family in Nuuk. Chen visited him every week. They went for walks along the waterfront. They talked about nothing and everything.

"I want to learn to fly," he said one day.

"Why?"

"Because then I can rescue people too."

She looked at him. He was taller now. The hollows in his cheeks had filled out.

"That's a good reason," she said.

"I learned it from you."

She did not cry. Soldiers did not cry.

But something in her chest loosened, warmed.

Chapter 47: The Flight

Larsson served six months in a Danish prison. He was released in summer, when the sun did not set and the world was green for a few brief weeks.

He did not return to flying. His license was gone. His career was over.

He took a job at a boatyard in Sisimiut. Repairing engines, hauling lines, working with his hands. The work was honest. The pay was poor. He did not care.

He thought about the crash every day. The white wall of snow. The impact. The faces of the survivors.

He would never stop thinking about it.

But the guilt had changed. It had softened from a blade to a weight. It was still there. It always would be. But he could carry it now.

He met Chen once, by accident, in a grocery store. They stood in the aisle, looking at each other.

"You look better," she said.

"I feel better. Not good. But better."

"That's something."

"It's everything."

They did not hug. They did not shake hands. They just stood, two survivors, acknowledging the space between them.

"Take care of yourself," she said.

"I will. You too."

She nodded and walked away.

He watched her go, then turned back to the shelves.

Chapter 48: The Testimony

The official inquiry into the crash was held in Copenhagen. Singh was called as a witness.

He testified about the crash, the aftermath, the ration kit. He told the truth about everything — including his proposal to allocate the supplies based on triage.

"I suggested we let the weakest die," he said. "So that the strongest could survive."

The panel was silent.

"Why are you telling us this?" the chair asked.

"Because it is the truth. And because I was wrong."

"What changed your mind?"

"The group."

He told them about Freja's insistence on sharing. About Chen's decision to walk. About Levi's flare. About Henrik's offer to sacrifice himself.

"Each person chose the group over themselves," he said. "I was the only one who did not."

"And what did you learn from that?"

"That survival is not a calculation. It is a choice. The choice to be human."

The panel did not ask further questions.

Singh left the room. He felt lighter than he had in years.

Chapter 49: The Return

A year after the crash, they came back to the ice.

Not all of them. Not the same plane. But they gathered at the edge of the debris field, where the fuselage still lay, slowly being consumed by the elements.

Freja had organized it. She had called each of them. She had asked them to come.

Mercer arrived in a helicopter he had chartered. The same kind that had rescued them. He stood apart from the group, his hands in his pockets.

Henrik came on crutches. His prosthetic was still being fitted. He had made the trip despite the doctor's warnings.

Larsson came by boat, then snowmobile. He did not speak much.

Chen and Levi arrived together. Levi was taller. His face had lost its childhood roundness.

Singh came last, flying in from Tasiilaq. He looked older, but his eyes were clear.

They stood at the wreckage. The wind was cold. The ice stretched to every horizon.

"I don't know why I came," Mercer said.

"To remember," Freja said.

"Remember what?"

"That we lived."

Levi walked to the fuselage. He reached inside and pulled out a piece of the yellow ration kit. The remaining fragments were still there, frozen, waiting.

He held it up. The plastic glinted in the pale sun.

"It's still here," he said.

"Some things never leave," Singh said.

They stood together in the silence.

Chapter 50: The Bitter Wind

They did not stay long. The cold pressed in. The wind began to rise.

Freja was the last to leave. She stood at the edge of the debris field, looking at the fuselage one last time.

The bitter wind had shaped them. It had stripped them of comfort, certainty, kindness. It had left them raw and cold and afraid.

But it had also left them alive.

She thought about the old Greenlandic song she had sung in the fuselage, the one about the hunter who shared his seal with a stranger. She had learned it from an elder in a village north of here. The song was about survival, yes. But it was also about something else.

It was about how people survive together, or not at all.

She turned and walked to the helicopter.

The blades began to turn. The engine whined, caught, rose in pitch.

Inside, the seven survivors sat in silence. The door closed. The helicopter lifted.

Below them, the fuselage grew smaller, smaller, then vanished into the white.

The bitter wind would erase it. It would erase the tracks, the debris, the memory of their fear.

But it would not erase what they had become.

Levi pressed his face to the window. He watched the ice fall away.

"Will we ever come back?" he asked.

No one answered.

But Singh put his hand on the boy's shoulder.

"We will always be here," he said.

The helicopter banked south. The ice receded.

Ahead of them lay warmth, light, life.

Behind them lay the bitter wind.

They flew on.
