Chapter 1: Ultra-Quiet

The order to rig for ultra-quiet came at 0210, and after that the boat held its breath.

Reyes stood in the control room with his hands flat on the chart table and listened to the machinery stop. Ventilation fans wound down. The galley went dark. A man two compartments aft set a wrench on a rag instead of a shelf, and the small sound of it reached forward like a confession.

They were a hundred and eighty feet down, off a coastline that did not appear in any of the orders they carried. The USS Cuttlefish was a diesel boat, sixteen years old, and she had been sent here to listen.

"Bearing two-seven-zero, steady," the sonarman whispered. Eddie Mahon had his headphones pressed to his skull with both palms. "Twin screws. Slow turns. She doesn't know we're here."

Reyes wrote the time and the bearing in the log in pencil. They kept everything in pencil now. Paper could be burned; pencil could be erased.

"Hold her," he said.

He felt the deck tilt half a degree as the planesmen worked the boat by hand, no hydraulics that might whine. Two hundred and twelve men's worth of effort had become one slow, quiet thing.

Above them, somewhere in the cold dark water, a warship that belonged to another country crossed their stern and never knew the iron whale was waiting beneath her, recording the sound of her heart.


Chapter 2: The Terms

Captain Hale found Reyes by the chart at the change of watch and stood close so he could speak low.

"You understand the terms of this," Hale said. It was not a question, but he asked it like one.

"Hear everything. Be heard by nothing."

"That's the polite version." Hale's face was gray in the red light. He was fifty and looked older here. "If they detect us, we are not a submarine. We are an incident. There is no rescue. There is no admission we were ever here. We are a hole in the ocean, and if we make noise, we become a hole in the sea floor."

Reyes nodded. He had read the same paragraph in the same flat language.

"I'm telling you because you're my exec," Hale said. "If something happens to me, you'll be the one carrying it. So carry it now. Get used to the shape of it."

"Yes, sir."

Hale almost smiled. "You're a good officer, Dan. Too careful, maybe."

"There are worse faults down here."

"There are no other faults down here," Hale said, and went forward to his cabin to sleep four hours in a bunk the size of a coffin.


Chapter 3: The Signature

Mahon caught the new one at 0640.

He had been on the stack for three hours and his eyes were closed, the way the good ones got when the sound became more real than the room. His hand came up, fingers spread. Wait.

"New contact," he breathed. "Bearing zero-four-one. Real quiet." He frowned. "Too quiet."

Reyes leaned over the recorder rack. "Talk to me."

"Screws are wrong. I never heard this before." Mahon's voice had a kind of hunger in it. "Five blades. She's got a hum under her — turbines, but smooth. They built something new, Mr. Reyes."

This was the whole patrol in one bearing. This was the reason they had crept across half an ocean to sit in a dead man's silence.

"Get it on tape," Reyes said. "All of it. Every revolution."

The reels turned, drinking the sound of a ship that did not know it was being remembered. Mahon wrote the bearing on his palm in grease pencil and did not move for an hour.


Chapter 4: The Smell of the Boat

You stopped noticing the smell after the first week, and then on the fourth week you noticed it again, all at once.

Diesel and old grease. Hot insulation. The amine from the air scrubbers, sharp at the back of the throat. Two hundred and twelve men in a steel tube, their sweat and their breath and the food they ate from cans. Reyes walked aft through the boat at the end of his watch and the smell told him things the gauges didn't. Where a battery was working hard. Where a man had been sick and not reported it.

The passageways were eighteen inches wide. Men turned sideways and waited for each other in a choreography learned by the body. Bunks were stacked three high between the torpedoes, and a torpedoman slept with his face a foot from four thousand pounds of warhead and did not think about it.

In the crew's mess, four men played hearts in a silence so complete that the slap of a card sounded loud. They nodded at Reyes. He nodded back.

It was a small world and a closed one, and for now it held.


Chapter 5: The Corner

Lt. Avery Cole caught Reyes in the engine room and would not quite meet his eyes.

"The number two air compressor," Cole said. "The bearing's running hot. I logged it in the yard but they didn't have the part. They told me to monitor it."

"You're monitoring it."

"I'm watching a gauge climb a degree a day, sir." Cole was twenty-six and looked like he hadn't slept since the yard. "If it seizes, it could throw oil. Oil in a hot space, with the —" He stopped.

"With the reactor space right there," Reyes finished, low.

Cole flinched at the word. Cuttlefish was a diesel boat, but she had been refitted with an auxiliary unit forward of the after machinery, a thing the crew was told not to discuss and not to fear. Most of them managed the first.

"Keep monitoring it," Reyes said. "And keep the foam and the CO2 rigged tight back here. You did right to tell me."

Cole nodded and did not look comforted. Reyes filed the compressor in the back of his mind, in the place where he kept the things that could kill them.


Chapter 6: Brandt

Petty Officer Karl Brandt worked the electrical panels and spoke to almost no one.

He was older than the other electricians, mid-thirties, with a flat accent that came from nowhere in particular. He was good with his hands and exact in his logs. When the chief of the boat came through, Brandt straightened but did not warm.

Garrity noticed. The chief of the boat noticed everything; it was the whole of his job, written under all the other jobs. He didn't mention it. A man who kept to himself in a steel pipe full of strangers was not a crime. Half the boat would rather be alone.

But Garrity, drinking coffee that tasted of fuel, watched Brandt run a meter across a junction box with the care of a man who had something to lose, and he thought: I don't know that one. Forty days at sea and I don't know that one at all.

He let the thought sit. There would be time for it or there would not.


Chapter 7: The Trawler

The trawler came down on them at noon, and for eleven minutes no one breathed louder than they had to.

"Fishing boat," Mahon said softly. "Maybe. Diesel, single screw, knocking like it's about to die. Bearing drawing right." A pause. "Or it's a picket dressed up like a fishing boat."

"Down to two-fifty," Hale said. "Easy."

The boat sank. The hull took up the pressure with a series of small ticks, steel adjusting to the weight of the sea. Reyes counted the ticks the way another man might count a rosary.

The screws passed overhead, knock and knock and knock, and drew off to the south.

"Bearing opening," Mahon said. "He's leaving."

Nobody cheered. You didn't cheer on a boat at war that wasn't a war. But Reyes saw a torpedoman striker named Tran unclench his hands one finger at a time, and he understood the boy completely.


Chapter 8: How Long

"Three more days," Hale said. He had the orders spread on his bunk, though he knew them by heart. "We're to stay on station until the twenty-second, then clear the area submerged and transit home."

"The compressor won't love three more days," Reyes said.

"Nothing loves three more days." Hale rubbed his eyes. "But we came a long way for that five-bladed ghost of Mahon's. The recorders want more of it. Washington wants more of it."

"Washington isn't breathing this air."

Hale looked at him. "No. We are. Say your piece, Dan."

"My piece is that we have what we came for, mostly. Every extra day is a day closer to a failure we can't fix down here." Reyes kept his voice even. "But it's your boat and your call. I'll back it either way."

"Three more days," Hale said again, gently, like a man closing a door. "Then we run."


Chapter 9: Breathing

Reyes found Tran in the forward room, polishing a fitting that was already clean.

"You did all right today," Reyes said.

"I was scared, sir." Tran said it like an admission of a crime.

"Everybody was scared. The ones who say they weren't are lying, and you don't want a liar next to you when it matters." Reyes crouched on his heels. "You know what fear is? It's your body getting ready. Lungs want more air. Hands want to grab. The trick isn't killing it. You can't. The trick is using it slow."

He showed the boy: breathe in for four, hold for four, out for four. The oldest trick in any frightened service.

"When the boat goes quiet," Reyes said, "you go quiet with her. You become part of the steel. The fear's still there. You just put it where it can't make noise."

Tran tried it. His shoulders came down half an inch.

"There," Reyes said. "Now you're a submariner."


Chapter 10: A Small Fault

At 2300 a breaker tripped in the after battery compartment, and Brandt reset it, and logged it, and thought nothing more of it.

It was the kind of thing that happened forty times a patrol. A relay aged. A connection sweated in the heat and the salt. You found it, you fixed it, you wrote it down. Brandt wrote it down in his exact hand: 2304, AFT BATT, breaker 7, reset, no further fault.

He ran the meter across the bus and saw nothing wrong. The needle sat where it should sit. He stood a moment in the close hot dark of the compartment, the great lead-acid cells ranked beneath the deck plates like sleeping animals, and he listened.

Somewhere a drop of water fell. The hull ticked. The boat lived.

He went forward to his bunk. Behind him, in the after battery, where he could not see and the meter could not reach, a wire that should have been tight had begun, very slowly, to work itself loose against a sharp edge of steel.


Chapter 11: Deep

They went to three hundred feet at 0400 to slide under a patrol line, and the boat reminded them what she was.

Reyes had ridden her deep a hundred times and it never stopped speaking to something old in him. The hull groaned — not metaphorically, an actual long iron complaint as the frames took the load. Cork insulation popped off the overhead and rained down like snow. A man put out a hand to steady himself and found the bulkhead cold and faintly wet with condensation.

"Three hundred feet," the diving officer said. "Holding."

Test depth was four hundred. Crush depth was a number they did not say out loud. Between those two figures lived the whole margin of their lives, and the boat reminded them of it with every fathom.

"She's tight," Hale said. He had a hand on a pipe, feeling the boat the way a horseman feels a horse. "Good old girl."

Reyes said nothing. He was listening to the compressor, two compartments aft, and to the sound it made that was not quite right, climbing under the groan of the deep hull like a second, smaller voice.


Chapter 12: Fire

The fire announced itself as a smell before it was anything else.

Reyes was in control when it reached him — sharp, electric, the burnt-sugar stink of insulation cooking. Then the word came forward on the sound-powered phone in a voice gone tight: "Fire in the after battery! Fire in after battery, smoke in maneuvering!"

The lights flickered, browned, and died. For one second the boat was utterly black, three hundred feet down, and Reyes heard men's breath catch all around him in the dark.

Then the battle lanterns snapped on, white and small and terrible, throwing long shadows.

"Rig for fire," Reyes said, and was surprised at how level his own voice came. "All compartments report. Helm, all stop. Chief of the boat to control."

Smoke was already crawling along the overhead, gray and oily, smelling of acid and burnt rubber. Somewhere aft a man was coughing the deep, doubled cough of a lung that had taken in poison.

Captain Hale came through the watertight door at a half run, and the boat, blind and burning, began to fight for itself.


Chapter 13: Hand to Hand

They fought the fire in the dark, in masks, by feel.

Garrity led the party aft, dragging the hose, and Reyes stayed in control because that was the rule — the exec held the boat while the captain or the chief fought the casualty. He hated it. He stood at the chart and ran the boat by the voices coming back through the phone, each one stretched thin.

"Can't see a damn thing back here." Cole's voice. "Battery's venting. Hydrogen, maybe — get the ventilation —"

"No ventilation," Reyes said. "Not yet. We blow that overboard now and we put a flame in the night sky. Fight it sealed."

A silence that meant they understood and hated it.

The OBA masks gave them an hour of air apiece and made every man clumsy. They beat at the burning lagging with foam and CO2, choking, blind, their gloves smoking. A wire had arced and lit the cork, and the cork had lit, and now they were a fire crew in a sealed iron room with hydrogen building over their heads.

"It's coming down," Garrity gasped. "We're getting it. We're —"

Then a noise like a slap, and Garrity's voice stopped.


Chapter 14: The Captain Falls

A pocket of hydrogen lit off near the overhead, a soft, terrible whump, and the after battery flashed orange for half a second.

Hale had gone aft against the rule — the captain in the fire, because he was that kind of captain — and the flash took him across the arm and the side of the head. Garrity dragged him forward through the watertight door by the straps of his harness while two men kept fighting.

They laid Hale in the passageway. His left sleeve was gone, the arm beneath it shining and wrong. Blood ran from his scalp where he had hit a valve going down. He was breathing. He was not awake.

"Doc!" Garrity bellowed, mask askew. "Doc, forward!"

Reyes felt the boat tilt under him, the deck and the world both. He looked at the captain on the deck, and then at the phone in his hand, and then at the eyes of the men in control turning toward him in the battle-lantern light.

"I have the conn," Reyes said. The words came out steady. Inside him nothing was steady at all. "Continue fighting the fire. Doc to the captain. Helm, hold her."


Chapter 15: Out

They killed the fire at 0512, and the silence afterward was worse than the noise had been.

Smoke hung in every compartment, thinned but acrid, so that men's eyes streamed and the battle lanterns wore halos. Reyes walked the boat for the first damage report, stepping over hoses, his own lungs raw.

The after battery was a ruin of melted lagging and scorched steel. Half the cells in that bank were dead or dying. The radio mast — they had raised it in the first minutes, instinct, a hand reaching for the surface and command — had shorted and burned in its well and would not answer.

"We're deaf and we're dumb," Sam Okafor said quietly. The navigator had soot in the creases of his face. "No transmit, no receive. Even if we wanted to call home, we can't."

The boat hung heavy. With cells gone, the trim was wrong; she wanted to settle by the stern. The planesmen fought her with the bow planes and a thin margin.

"Pump from aft trim to forward," Reyes said. "Slow. We hold three hundred until I know what we are."

What they were was a boat that could no longer cry out, three hundred feet down, in waters that wanted them dead.


Chapter 16: The Air

By the second hour the air began to turn, and only the gauges knew it first.

Carbon dioxide had no smell at the levels that mattered. It crept up while men felt fine, and then it took the headache first, and then the temper, and then the thinking. Salas, the chief auxiliaryman, watched the CO2 reading climb past one percent and chewed the inside of his cheek.

"Scrubbers are running," Salas told Reyes. "Both units. But the burner's down — the fire took the power to it — so we're only absorbing, not regenerating. The amine's going to load up and quit."

"How long?"

Salas did the arithmetic he had done a dozen times in drills and never once for real. "Day and a half before it gets ugly. Two before it gets bad. After that —" He shrugged, a small honest motion.

Reyes did the second sum, the one that mattered more. To run the burner he needed power. Power was the battery. Half the battery was slag in the after compartment, and the rest had to move the boat and run the lights and the pumps and the men's lives.

Every amp he spent on air was an amp he could not spend on getting home. He stood in the control room and felt the math close around him like the sea.


Chapter 17: No Accident

Cole came to Reyes in the wardroom at noon, or what the clocks called noon, and laid a soot-blackened fitting on the table.

"This is from the after battery," Cole said. "The connection that arced. The one that started it." He turned it under the light. "Sir, this didn't work loose on its own. Look at the stud. That's a file mark. Somebody dressed this down so it wouldn't bite. So it would sit tight at the pier and shake loose at sea."

Reyes picked it up. He was an engineer enough to see it. A bright scrape across dull metal, deliberate, neat.

"It could be a yard error," Reyes said, because someone had to say it. "A sloppy hand in the refit."

"It could be." Cole did not believe it and did not pretend to. "But the breaker that tripped last night, the one Brandt reset — it was on this circuit. He was the last man in that compartment with a meter in his hand."

Reyes set the fitting down very carefully, the way you set down something that might go off.

"You've told no one else?"

"No, sir."

"Keep it that way," Reyes said. "We have a fire to survive before we have a traitor to find."


Chapter 18: Second Smoke

But suspicion did not wait for permission, and it moved through the boat like the second smoke of the fire.

It started as nothing — a man saying the fire came on awful sudden, another saying batteries don't just light off, do they. By the afternoon watch the word had crept forward and aft. Someone had heard the breaker had been worked the night before. Someone knew who'd worked it.

Reyes felt it the way Mahon felt a distant screw: not a fact yet, just a pressure on the ear. Men looked at each other a half-second too long. The hearts game in the mess broke up early. And Brandt, who already spoke to no one, found that no one spoke to him either, in a way that had a new edge to it.

Garrity put it plainly when he caught Reyes alone. "I got a boat that's hurt and scared, and now it's started looking for someone to blame. That's a fire too, Mr. Reyes. Different fuel."

"I know it."

"You want me to lean on Brandt?"

"No," Reyes said. "Not yet. A scared crew that's wrong about a man can do worse than the man ever did. Watch him. Don't touch him. And keep the others off him."


Chapter 19: Lucid

Hale surfaced from his concussion in pieces, like a man coming up through layers of water.

Doc Pruitt had him in the captain's bunk with the arm wrapped and the scalp stitched, dosing him for pain and fighting the swelling in his skull with the few tools a boat carried. For an hour at a time Hale would be almost himself, sharp and dry, and then his eyes would drift and he would ask what time it was, and then ask again four minutes later.

"Concussion's the least of it," Pruitt told Reyes in the passageway, voice low. "The burn's going to want to turn septic in this air. I'm rationing what I've got. And I can't tell you he'll be fit to command. Maybe in days. Maybe not on this patrol."

"Maybe not at all?"

Pruitt didn't answer that, which was an answer.

Reyes went in to see the captain. Hale's eyes found him and held, briefly clear.

"Dan." A dry whisper. "Did we get the fire?"

"We got it, sir."

"Good." The eyes wandered, came back. "Don't surface," Hale said. "Whatever happens. You hear me? Don't you surface in these waters." And then he was gone again, down into the gray.


Chapter 20: Overhead

By evening there were three contacts overhead where there had been one, and Mahon did not like any of them.

"Two of them are warships," he said. "Sharp screws, holding a line. And the third —" He pressed the cans to his ears. "The third's just sitting. Engines stopped. Listening."

"For us?"

"For anything." Mahon's young face was old in the red light. "If they got even a sniff of that fire — a slick of oil, a bubble of gas — they'll work this water like a grid."

The fire had vented gas they could not account for. A diesel boat that flashed a battery and burned cork put things into the sea, and the sea carried them up.

Reyes looked at the chart, at the pencil marks that were the only record of where they were and what hunted them. Stay deep and silent and the air killed them slow. Move and they made noise the listeners overhead were praying to hear.

"Hold her," he said. "Dead quiet. Every man not on watch in his bunk." He paused. "And no one runs a tool, touches a wrench, drops a cup. From now on we are a stone in the water."


Chapter 21: Inventory

That night Reyes did the only thing left to a man in his position: he counted.

He sat in the wardroom with Cole and Salas and Okafor and a single battle lantern, and they laid the boat's life out in numbers on a sheet of paper that would be burned before they came home, if they came home.

Battery: forty percent of capacity, dropping with every hour the pumps and lanterns ran. Air: a day and a half of breathable before the CO2 went dangerous, less if they exerted. Oxygen candles: enough to bleed in a thin supply for two more days. Fresh water, food, fuel for the diesels they dared not start — those did not matter. A man dies of bad air long before he dies of hunger.

"So the wall is the air," Okafor said. "Everything ends at the air."

"Everything ends at the air," Reyes agreed. He looked at the column of figures. "Unless we get the scrubber burner back. That buys us days. And to run the burner —"

"We spend battery we need to get clear," Salas finished. "We can breathe and stay, or save power and run. Not both."

The lantern hissed. Outside the steel, six hundred fathoms of black water pressed in, patient as arithmetic.


Chapter 22: A Hand, Not Chance

Salas found the second one at 0300, and this time he did not file it under bad luck.

A valve in the trim system, a small bronze thing the size of a fist, had been left half-shut behind its lagging — not failed, set, so that the pump fought it and overheated and tripped, just when they most needed to balance the wounded boat. Salas crouched over it with his light and felt the cold certainty come up through him.

"That valve was open three days ago," he told Reyes. "I opened it myself in the dive checks. Somebody closed it down. In the dark. During the fire, maybe, when nobody could see a hand reaching for a valve."

"Or it vibrated."

"It's a quarter-turn detent, Mr. Reyes. It doesn't vibrate shut. Somebody's hand did that."

Reyes felt the two facts grind against each other — the filed stud, the closed valve — and make a shape he didn't want. Somebody aboard wanted the Cuttlefish to fail. Not loudly. Not all at once. In small turns, in the dark, where no one could swear it wasn't simply the boat dying of her own old age.

"Say nothing," Reyes told him. "To anyone. I mean it, Joe."

Salas wiped his hands on a rag. "It's a small boat to keep a secret on."

"It's a small boat to start a panic on," Reyes said. "Choose."


Chapter 23: How Close

The truth they had been spared got loose in the night, and it did more damage than the fire.

It was Tran, of all people, frightened and young, who said it in the crew's mess — said the thing they all half-knew and none of them said. "The auxiliary unit. The thing forward of maneuvering they won't let us talk about. The fire was right next to it. We almost —"

"Shut your mouth," an older torpedoman said, fast and hard, but it was too late, because every man in the mess had gone still.

Reyes heard it the way you hear a glass break in another room. By the time he reached the mess, the fear had changed shape. It wasn't drowning anymore, or suffocating. It was the other thing, the unspeakable thing, the reason men had not wanted this refit on this old boat.

"Listen to me," Reyes said, and they listened because there was nothing else. "The unit is shut down. It was shut down before the fire and it stayed shut down through it. It has its own shielding and its own seawater. The fire did not reach it and was not going to. I have the readings. Doc has the readings. You are not poisoned and you are not going to be." He let that sit. "What's going to hurt you is bad air and bad nerves. Those, we can fix. Go back to your bunks and breathe slow."

They went. But the word was out of the bottle now, and Reyes knew it would whisper in the dark long after the lights came back.


Chapter 24: The Record

Garrity laid Brandt's service jacket on the wardroom table and put one thick finger on the page.

"I went through it," the chief said. "Every man's, so it wouldn't look like I was hunting one. But it's his that won't lie down." He turned the file. "Three years on a tender in Norfolk. Two years 'shore duty, instructional.' Then he transfers in to us right before this patrol, a senior man taking a billet a junior man should've had. And the references —" Garrity tapped them. "I called two of them in my head against names I know. One of those commands didn't have a man by that title. I'd swear to it."

"You can't swear to it. Not from down here."

"No. But a forged jacket's a forged jacket, and a man with a clean enough record doesn't need to forge one." Garrity sat back. "I don't say he lit the fire. I say I don't know what he is, and that's worse on a boat than knowing he's bad."

Reyes looked at the file, at the exact, careful entries, the same exact, careful hand that wrote the battery logs.

"All right," he said. "I'll talk to him. Alone. You'll be outside the door and you'll hear it, and you'll do nothing unless I call you."


Chapter 25: An Order He Can't Obey

Before Reyes could reach Brandt, Hale woke and gave an order, and it nearly broke the boat in half.

The captain came up clear-eyed for the space of ten minutes, propped in his bunk, and when Reyes laid out the truth — the dead battery, the dead radio, the foul air, the hunters overhead — Hale's jaw set in the old way.

"Then we stay," Hale said. "We sit on the bottom if we have to. We wait them out. We do not surface. Those are my orders and the Navy's orders and they don't change because the boat got hurt." His voice rose, thin and certain. "Better the whole crew than one word reaching the wrong ears. You know what we are out here, Dan. You know what we agreed to be."

Reyes stood very still. "Sir, if we sit, the air kills us in two days. There's no waiting them out. There's only choosing how we die."

"Then we die quiet." Hale's eyes were bright with fever and conviction both. "That's an order, Mister Reyes."

And then the clarity drained out of him between one breath and the next, and he was asking what time it was, and whether the fire was out, and Reyes was left holding an order he could not tell whether to obey — given by a man who might no longer be fit to give it.


Chapter 26: Cold

The boat began to grow cold, and the cold was the battery dying out loud.

To save power Reyes had ordered the heaters off and the lighting cut to one lamp in three. Now the chill of the sea, which the steel had always held at arm's length, came creeping in through the hull. Condensation ran down the bulkheads. Men pulled on every layer they owned and sat close in their bunks, breathing the thin air, watching their breath start to show.

It was strange, Reyes thought, to be cold in a steel pipe that smelled of fire. But the deep water was near freezing, and only power kept it out, and power was the thing they had least of.

He walked the boat in the dimness. In the forward room Tran lay shivering and wide-eyed, and Reyes touched his shoulder and the boy nodded, holding on. In maneuvering Cole worked by flashlight on a sketch of a jury-rig that might or might not save them. In the crew's mess, men who had played cards now sat silent, hoarding their warmth and their air, and watched their exec pass with eyes that asked the question no one would say:

What are you going to do?

Reyes did not yet know. That was the truth he carried, colder than the hull.


Chapter 27: Brandt's Answer

He found Brandt in the empty crew's mess and sat across from him, and Garrity stood in the passageway outside, just out of sight.

"Your record doesn't hold together," Reyes said. No use dressing it. "The references don't check. You transferred in late into a senior billet. You were the last man in the after battery before the fire. And the fire started at a connection that was filed to fail." He watched the man's face. "Talk to me, Brandt. Now, while it's me and not a frightened crew."

Brandt did not flinch. He folded his hands on the table, the careful hands.

"My record doesn't hold together," he said, "because it isn't mine. The name's real enough. The history's built." A pause. "I'm not what the jacket says. But I didn't light your fire, and I didn't touch that valve, and I'd give a good deal to know who did, because he's putting my work in the ground with the rest of you."

"Then what are you?"

Brandt's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "I'm aboard for the same reason you are. To make sure that five-bladed ghost gets home on tape, and the men who'd rather it didn't, don't win." He met Reyes's eyes. "There's a service that watches the watchers, Mr. Reyes. I'm the man they sent. And I think the man you're hunting is the man I came to find."


Chapter 28: The Wardroom Splits

Reyes brought the question to the wardroom because a thing that size could not be carried alone.

He told them what Brandt had said — not all of it, but enough. That the electrician claimed to be an intelligence officer placed aboard to guard the patrol. That if it was true, the saboteur was someone else. That if it was a lie, the saboteur was sitting across a mess table spinning a story to save his neck.

"It's too convenient," Okafor said. "He's caught, so he's suddenly a secret agent. I don't buy it."

"And if it's true and we lock him up," Cole said, "we've put away the one man trained to catch the one who's actually killing us."

"We can't prove either way," Salas said. "Not from down here. There's no signal to check him against."

Garrity spoke last, slow. "Here's what I know. Two acts of sabotage. Filed stud, closed valve. Both done by a hand that knew exactly where to reach in the dark. That's not a panicking man. That's a trained one." He looked around the table. "Could be Brandt lying. Could be Brandt telling true and it's somebody else trained just as good. Either way, there's a professional aboard who wants us dead, and we've got hours, not days, to sort the saint from the sinner."


Chapter 29: The Ping

At 0140 the ocean rang like a struck bell, and every man aboard felt it in his teeth.

Active sonar. A single ping from one of the warships overhead, sweeping the deep for an echo. It came through the hull as a high, pure note, and then its faint return — the sound of the pulse bouncing off the Cuttlefish and racing back up to the ears that had sent it.

"They've gone active," Mahon whispered, redundant, white. "That last one. He's pinging."

"Did he have us?" Reyes asked.

Mahon listened to the silence after, the long held breath of the sea. "Maybe. Maybe just searching. One ping's a question. If he pings again on the same bearing —"

It came. The bell rang a second time, and this time it held the bearing.

"He's got something," Mahon breathed. "He's not sure it's a boat. But he's got something."

Reyes felt the choice tighten another notch. To run was to be heard. To sit was to be found anyway, slowly, by a ship that now suspected the water it floated on. He put his hand flat on the chart table, the old habit, and made himself breathe four counts in.

"Rig for ultra-quiet," he said, though they already were. "And everybody pray that ghost loses interest."


Chapter 30: Rationing Air

By dawn Reyes was rationing breath itself, the way other men ration water in a lifeboat.

Pruitt had explained the arithmetic of it. A man at rest burned less air and made less CO2 than a man working or afraid. So the order went out, the strangest order Reyes had ever given: half the crew, every soul not on watch, would lie still in their bunks and do nothing — not talk, not move, not worry if they could help it. Slow the body. Slow the breath. Make the air last.

The boat became a thing of sleeping men in the cold, in the dark, while a skeleton watch held the conn and the pumps and the failing scrubbers.

Reyes walked among them with a flashlight hooded to a coin of light. Some slept. Some lay with open eyes fixed on the bunk above. Tran was awake and trying so hard to be still that he trembled with it. Reyes crouched beside him.

"Four in," he murmured. "Four hold. Four out. You taught the others yet?"

"Showed Pollard, sir," Tran whispered. "And Diaz."

"Good man. That's how the boat lives. One breath taught to the next." He squeezed the boy's shoulder and moved on, and behind him two hundred men breathed slow on purpose, spending their air a teaspoon at a time.


Chapter 31: The Jury-Rig

Cole and Salas spread their sketch on the deck plates of maneuvering and built a thing that should not have worked.

The scrubber burner was dead because the fire had taken its power supply. But the burner only needed a modest, steady current to regenerate the amine and pull the CO2 back out of the air. Cole's idea was to take that current from the one place they could spare it least and least dangerously — a small lighting bus, rewired by hand, fed through a resistor pack Salas was building out of the heating elements they no longer used.

"It's ugly," Cole said. "It runs hot. We'll have to babysit it every minute or it starts another fire."

"After the last one, I'll babysit it personally," Salas said.

They worked for three hours by flashlight in the cold, two pairs of hands and a soldering iron run off a battery they could barely afford, splicing a lifeline out of scrap. Reyes watched the CO2 gauge and the clock and the two men's faces.

When Salas closed the last connection, the burner ticked, hummed, and caught. A faint warmth came off it. And on the gauge, after twenty long minutes, the needle that had only ever climbed began, by a hair, to fall.

No one cheered. But Cole put his sooty face in his hands for a moment, and Reyes let him.


Chapter 32: The Man Who Broke

A torpedoman named Diaz broke at 1100, in the dark, the way Reyes had feared someone would.

It started as a sound — a high, fast breathing from the after end of the forward room — and then Diaz was on his feet in the narrow space, clawing at the watertight door, saying he had to get out, he had to get out, the boat was a coffin and the air was gone and he had to get out now.

Two men grabbed him. He fought them. In a space full of armed torpedoes and held breath, a man out of his mind was as dangerous as the sea.

Reyes came through fast and low. "Diaz." Sharp, then soft. "Diaz, look at me. Not the door. Me."

The man's eyes rolled and fixed.

"You can't get out," Reyes said, quiet and level. "There's no out. There's only down here, with us, breathing. So you're going to breathe with me. Four in." He breathed it, loud, exaggerated. "Come on. Four in." Diaz dragged a shaking breath. "Four hold. Four out. Again." Slowly the wildness drained. The man sagged between the two who held him and began, helplessly, to weep.

"That's all right," Reyes said. "That's the air doing it, not you. Lie him down. Tran, you sit with him. Talk to him low."


Chapter 33: What Doc Can't Do

Hale was worse, and Pruitt finally said the thing he had been carrying.

The burn had gone bad in the foul air despite everything — red, swollen, the heat of it coming off the arm. The fever rode the captain hard now, and the lucid windows came narrower and farther apart. Pruitt had used most of what he had.

"I'm a corpsman, not a surgeon," he told Reyes in the passageway, and there was grief in it under the flatness. "He needs a hospital. He needs things I don't have on this boat and never did. The infection's into him now. If we don't get him to real care —" He stopped. "Down here, in this air, he's not going to win it."

"How long?"

"Days if we surface and get him off. If we don't —" Pruitt shook his head. "He'd tell you himself, if he could think straight. He'd tell you the boat comes before the captain."

"He did tell me," Reyes said. "He told me not to surface."

Pruitt looked at him a long moment. "He told you that when he could still think it through? Or when the fever was already in him?"

Reyes had no answer. That was the whole weight of it. He no longer knew which orders had been the captain's, and which had been the wound.


Chapter 34: The Bilge

A pump failed forward and the bilge began to fill, and it was Tran who went down into it.

The space under the deck plates in the forward room was a foot of black, oily water and falling, with a jammed suction valve at the bottom that no grown man's shoulders could reach. Tran was the smallest aboard. He looked at the water and looked at Reyes and his face went the color of the bulkhead, and then he set his jaw.

"I can get to it, sir."

"You sure?"

"No, sir." He almost smiled. "But I'm the one that fits."

They lowered him head and shoulders into the cold filthy water with a wrench and a flashlight in a watertight bag, two men holding his ankles. He worked by feel, blind, his face underwater for ten seconds at a time while the boat creaked around them. Reyes counted his breaths from above, the old four-count, calling it down to him.

The boy came up gasping, went down again, came up. On the fourth time the valve gave. They heard the pump bite and the water begin to drop, and they hauled Tran out streaming and shaking and grinning like a fool.

"That's a submariner," Reyes said, and meant it all the way down, and the men in the forward room thumped the boy on his soaked back as hard as they dared in the quiet.


Chapter 35: Between Them

Garrity caught Reyes alone in the wardroom and settled the matter of command, the way the chief of the boat settles things — sideways and final.

"The crew knows the captain's down," Garrity said. "They know you've got the conn. What they don't know is whether you mean it." He poured cold coffee from the pot they no longer heated. "A boat smells doubt, Mr. Reyes. You've been carrying it around. They can see it on you. The captain said one thing, you're thinking another, and you don't know which way to jump."

"I don't," Reyes admitted. "He ordered me not to surface. I think the order's right and the man who gave it can't think anymore."

"Then here's the chief of the boat's opinion, which you didn't ask for and are going to get." Garrity leaned in. "Hale gave that order to a healthy boat. He'd give a different one to this boat, if he could see it. You're not disobeying him. You're doing what he'd do if the fever wasn't in him. But you have to decide to believe that and then stand on it. The men can ride out the air a while longer. They can't ride out a commander who won't command."

Reyes was quiet a long moment. Then he nodded once.

"All right, Tom," he said. "All right."


Chapter 36: Fear, Not Treason

The truth about the fire came out, and it was smaller and sadder than treason.

Brandt found it — the intelligence man, working his own quiet investigation alongside the boat's. He came to Reyes with a name. Not the filed stud. Not the closed valve. Those were still out there, still unexplained, still a professional's work. But the breaker that had tripped the night before the fire, the small fault Brandt had reset — that, Brandt had now traced, had been jammed deliberately by a frightened young striker named Pollard.

Reyes found the boy in the after room, and it came out in a rush of tears and shame. Pollard had wanted the patrol to end. He was sick with fear of these waters, of the auxiliary unit, of dying out here for a tape. He had thought a small electrical fault, nothing dangerous, might force them to break off and go home. He had not known how close the wire was to the cork. He had not meant a fire. He had not meant any of it.

"I just wanted to go home, sir," Pollard wept. "I just wanted us to go home."

Reyes looked at the shaking boy and felt his anger go out of him like air. Here was one mystery solved — and it was only fear, the same fear in all of them, that had reached out one stupid frightened hand.

But the filed stud was still no boy's panic. And the closed valve was still a professional's hand. The saboteur, the real one, was still aboard.


Chapter 37: Not a Vote

That afternoon Reyes did a thing that was not regulation and not a vote, but was the only honest thing left.

He had Garrity gather the off-watch in the crew's mess, as many as the space would hold, and the rest he spoke to through the open watertight doors, his voice carrying low through the cold boat. He told them the truth. All of it that he could.

"The air's holding, barely, thanks to Mr. Cole and Chief Salas. The captain's badly hurt and needs care we don't have. The radio's dead. There are warships over us, and one of them may suspect we're here." He let them have it plain. "If we stay deep and silent, we last maybe another day, day and a half, and then the air beats us, all of us, and the boat is never found and never explained. If we surface, we breathe — but we surface into hostile water under a foreign flag, and what happens then, I can't promise you."

He looked around at the gray, breathing faces.

"I'm not asking you to vote. This is my decision and I'll carry it. But I'll not make it without you hearing it first. So hear it. And then go back and breathe slow, and trust your boat, and trust me to choose right."

No one spoke. But something in the room eased — not hope, exactly, but the relief of men who have finally been told the truth.


Chapter 38: Hours, Not Days

The jury-rig held, and bought them hours, and the hours only made the arithmetic crueler.

Cole came to Reyes with the numbers, and they were better than before and still not enough. The patched scrubber pulled the CO2 down enough to keep men thinking, but it drank power, and the power was running out faster than the air was being saved. The battery was down to fifteen percent. The boat had to move eventually — to clear the area, to come shallow, to do anything but die in place — and moving cost amps they no longer had to spare.

"We can breathe and not move, or move and not breathe," Cole said. "Same wall as before, sir, just closer. The rig didn't change the math. It just gave us time to look at it."

Reyes studied the figures by the single lamp. Every path on the paper ended in the same place, except one. There was exactly one move left on the board that did not end in two hundred men quietly going to sleep forever in the cold and the dark.

"Then we go up," he said quietly. "We use what's left of the battery to come shallow, slow and silent, and we listen all the way, and at the last second we choose. Periscope depth, eyes on the surface, and then — then we decide whether we breathe or not." He looked at Cole. "Get the boat ready to rise."


Chapter 39: The Plan

Reyes laid the plan out in the wardroom, exact and small, because exact and small was all that could save them.

"We come up by stages," he said, drawing it on the chart. "Three hundred to two hundred, slow, on the planes and a little blown ballast — as little as we can. We hold at two hundred and we listen. Mahon tells us where every screw on the surface is. Then two hundred to a hundred. Listen again. Then up to periscope depth, but only if the water above us is clear enough to risk a look."

"And if it isn't clear?" Okafor asked.

"Then we hold at a hundred and pray it clears before the air doesn't. But we won't put a periscope or a sail into the middle of a destroyer's wake. We rise into a gap or we don't rise."

"Blowing tanks makes noise," Salas said. "Bubbles. They'll hear it if they're listening."

"Then we blow slow, in bursts, and we use the planes for everything we can." Reyes looked around. "We've got one battery's worth of rising in us and one chance to read the surface right. Mahon's ears are the whole plan. Everyone else holds the boat dead steady and dead quiet while he works." He folded the chart. "We go on my mark. Get the men to stations. Quiet stations."


Chapter 40: Trim by Hand

They trimmed the boat by hand for the ascent, the way submariners had since before there were machines to do it.

With power so low, Reyes would not trust the trim pump for the fine work. So men moved water by hand-pump and gravity, cup of effort by cup of effort, balancing the wounded boat so she would rise straight and slow instead of broaching or sinking back. Salas read the angle off a bubble in a curved glass tube, the simplest instrument aboard and now the most important.

"Bow's heavy," Salas murmured. "Move forty gallons aft. Slow."

Men cranked the hand-pump in the cold, their breath showing, the only sound the soft slosh of water finding its level and the tick of the patched scrubber.

Reyes stood at the chart with Garrity beside him and felt the boat come into balance under his feet, degree by degree, the way you feel a small boat settle when the weight is shared right.

"She's level," Salas said at last. "Trimmed for a slow rise. She'll go up like a feather if we let her."

"Then we let her," Reyes said. He looked at Mahon, hunched over the stack, eyes already shut, listening to the surface they were about to climb toward. "Sound, what've we got up there?"

"Quiet," Mahon breathed. "For now. Quiet."

"Then up we go," Reyes said. "Mark."


Chapter 41: The Ascent

The Cuttlefish began to rise, and the hull sang the whole song of the deep in reverse.

The groan of the frames eased as the pressure came off, frame by frame, the iron unclenching the way a fist unclenches. Cork that had not fallen during the fire fell now, ticking down through the cold dim spaces. Men lay or stood at their quiet stations and felt the deck tilt gently up and held still and breathed slow.

"Three hundred. Two-eighty." The diving officer read the depth in a whisper. "Two-fifty. Two-twenty."

"Hold at two hundred," Reyes said.

They blew a short, careful breath of air into the tanks — a soft hiss, a faint rush of bubbles they all heard and hated — and the boat steadied at two hundred feet, hanging in the black water like a held breath.

"Sound?"

Mahon's hand came up, fingers spread. Wait. The whole control room held still. Somewhere above, screws turned in the water, near and far, a pattern only Mahon could read.

"Two warships," he breathed at last. "Both drawing left, heading away. The third — the listener — I lost him. He's stopped, or he's gone." A pause. "There's a window, Mr. Reyes. Not a big one."

"Then we take it," Reyes said. "Up to a hundred. Slow."


Chapter 42: Counting Screws

At a hundred feet they held again, and Mahon counted the screws above them and named the danger one by one.

"The two warships are off to the north now, four, five thousand yards. Fading." He pressed the cans tight. "But there's a new one. Came in while we were rising. Big. Slow. Right —" He turned his head as if to look up through the hull. "Right above us. Close. Real close."

"How close?"

"On top of us, sir. A few hundred yards, no more. He's not pinging. He's just sitting there. Idling." Mahon swallowed. "A destroyer, I think. Just sitting in the water right over our heads."

The window had a wall in it. To go to periscope depth now was to push a periscope up into the shadow of a warship's hull — to surface, if it came to that, directly beneath the guns of the one ship in the ocean they most needed to avoid.

"Hold at a hundred," Reyes said. "Dead quiet. We wait for him to move."

"And if he doesn't move?" Okafor asked, very low.

Reyes looked at the air gauge, and at the clock, and at the battery meter, all three of them sliding the same direction.

"Then we'll have a decision to make," he said, "and not much time to make it in."


Chapter 43: Underneath

They hung at a hundred feet beneath the destroyer for forty minutes, and it was the longest forty minutes of any of their lives.

The big ship sat above them, idling, a vast slow heartbeat of screws turning just enough to hold station. Every man aboard could hear it now without headphones — a low presence in the steel, the weight of the enemy directly overhead. No one moved. No one spoke. They breathed the failing air in slow counts and stared at nothing and waited.

Reyes stood at the chart with his hand flat on it and made himself be still. Inside, the arithmetic ground on. The air was nearly gone. The battery was nearly gone. To wait was to die quiet under the keel of the very ship they were hiding from. To move was to be heard. To surface was to surrender the secret they had crossed an ocean to keep.

He thought of Hale's order. Don't surface. He thought of Garrity. He'd give a different order to this boat. He thought of two hundred men breathing slow on purpose, trusting him to choose.

"Sound," he murmured. "Is he moving?"

Mahon listened. The whole boat listened through him.

"No, sir," Mahon whispered. "He's not moving. He's just... sitting on us."

Reyes closed his eyes. The choice was no longer in days or hours. It was here.


Chapter 44: Live

Hale woke for the last clear minute he would have, and gave Reyes the only order that freed him.

The captain's eyes opened and found focus, and Pruitt, who had not left him, leaned close. "Skipper. The exec's carrying it. We're hurt bad."

"I know what we are." Hale's voice was a thread, but it was his own voice, clear under the fever. "Dan. Come here."

Reyes came and crouched by the bunk.

"I gave you an order," Hale whispered. "Not to surface. I remember." His ruined hand moved, found Reyes's sleeve. "That was the right order for a boat that could still fight. This boat can't fight. I can feel it. I can feel her dying around me." His eyes held Reyes's. "So here's the new order, and it's the last one I've got the wits to give. Get them home. Whatever it costs. Surface, surrender, sink the tapes if you have to, but get those men up into the air. The Navy can court-martial me for it. Don't you waste one breath on the secret. The secret isn't worth one of them." A long pause, breath rattling. "Live, Dan. Make them live. That's the order."

"Aye, sir," Reyes said, and his voice broke on it. "Aye, Captain. Make them live."

Hale's hand fell away, and the clarity went out of him, but it had stayed just long enough.


Chapter 45: The Choice

Reyes stood, and the boat felt different under him, and he understood that the weight he had been carrying was simply lifted.

"Chief of the boat," he said. "Captain's last order. We surface and we get this crew into the air. All hands hear it: we are going up."

He turned to the control room, and his voice was the captain's voice now, because someone's had to be.

"Sound, I want that destroyer's exact position the second we move. Diving officer, prepare to come to periscope depth. Mr. Okafor, the moment we have the surface, you'll signal — lamp, flag, anything they can read — that we are a submarine in distress, dead in the water, no threat. We are not an incident. We are a wreck full of sick men, and that is exactly what they'll see."

"And the tapes?" Brandt asked quietly, from the back.

"The tapes are Mr. Brandt's to handle as his service requires," Reyes said. "Mine is the air these men breathe." He put his hand flat on the chart one last time. "Periscope depth. Slow. Take her up."

The boat began to rise the last hundred feet toward a gray dawn she had not seen in forty days, toward a foreign sea and a foreign flag and whatever waited there — and every man aboard breathed in, deep, the first deep breath any of them had dared in days.


Chapter 46: Gray Dawn

The periscope broke the surface into a flat gray sea under a low gray sky, and Reyes turned it once around and saw the destroyer.

She lay a quarter-mile off, a long lean shape with a red star on her stack, and even as he watched, her signal lamp began to stutter, having seen his periscope feather the water. They had been found. There was no hiding now and nothing left to hide for.

"Surface the boat," Reyes said. "Blow everything. Get her up and get the hatches open. I want air in these compartments."

The last of the high-pressure air roared into the tanks. The Cuttlefish came up heavy and tired, water streaming off her black hull, and broke the surface for the first time in six weeks. The hatch in the sail was cracked open and the cold sea air came pouring down into the boat — clean, raw, freezing, the best thing any of them had ever breathed. Men gasped at it. Some wept. The CO2 went streaming up out of the open hatches into the gray morning.

Reyes climbed to the bridge into the wind with Garrity behind him, and stood in the open air on the wet steel, facing the warship across the cold water, and raised both empty hands so the men watching through their gunsights could see he held nothing at all.


Chapter 47: The Standoff

For an hour the two ships sat facing each other on the gray sea, and no one fired, and that hour decided everything.

The destroyer had her guns trained — Reyes could see the long barrels follow the Cuttlefish as she wallowed dead in the water. A signal lamp blinked at them, fast, demanding. Okafor stood at Reyes's shoulder reading it and answering, slow and plain, the simplest message in the world: We are damaged. We have injured men. We are no threat. We request assistance.

"They're asking who we are," Okafor murmured. "What flag."

"Tell them the truth they can already see. We're a submarine in distress." Reyes kept his hands visible, his posture open, a man with nothing to fight with. "Don't lie. Don't threaten. We're not a warship now. We're a lifeboat that happens to be made of steel."

It was the oldest law of the sea, older than any cold war, older than any flag — a ship in distress, men in danger of the water. Reyes was betting two hundred lives that it still held, even here, even now.

The minutes stretched. The guns held their aim. And then, slowly, the destroyer's lamp blinked a different rhythm, and Okafor let out a breath.

"They're acknowledging," he said. "They're... they're launching a boat. Sir, they're coming to help."


Chapter 48: A Line

A small boat came across the gray water from the destroyer, and what passed between the two crews was mostly silence, and the silence was a kind of mercy.

The Soviet sailors who came aboard the wallowing submarine were young and wary and did not speak English, and Reyes and his men did not speak Russian, and so it was all done with hands and eyes and the plain language of a casualty. They saw the burned compartment. They saw Hale, gray and barely breathing, lifted up through the hatch on a stretcher. They saw two hundred exhausted men gulping the cold air like water.

A line came across. Then a tow. The destroyer took the crippled boat under her stern, slow, and turned toward water that was neither side's, a neutral gray where a thing like this could happen and be quietly forgotten.

No one spoke of what the Cuttlefish had been doing in these waters. No one asked about the recorders, which had gone over the side in a weighted bag at Brandt's quiet hands the moment the periscope broke water. There was nothing to be gained by either crew in naming the truth out loud. Sailors understood the sea better than governments did. Men did not let other men drown over a tape.

Reyes stood on the bridge in the wind and watched the foreign flag tow them toward open water, and felt the boat live under him.


Chapter 49: The Passage Home

Hale died on the third day, in a hospital ashore in a country that was not the enemy and not home, and the report that followed could never be written true.

He had been lifted off by helicopter once they reached neutral water, and for a while it had seemed the burn might let him go and let him live. It did not. Pruitt was with him at the end and said the captain had been clear at the last, and easy, and had asked only whether the men were all right. They were. All of them. Every soul Reyes had been handed in the dark had come up into the air.

The Cuttlefish herself was patched enough to limp home under her own power, surfaced the whole way, the diesels running fat and loud after weeks of silence. The crew stood watches in the open air and could not get enough of the sky.

Reyes sat in the wardroom on the long passage and tried to write the report. It would say there had been a fire. It would say the boat had been damaged and forced to surface and had received humanitarian assistance from a foreign vessel. It would not say what they had been doing, or what they had recorded, or that a man named Brandt had quietly vanished from the crew list the moment they touched a friendly pier. Some truths the sea kept. Some truths the service kept. Reyes wrote the lie that let the men go home and signed his name to it.


Chapter 50: The Weight

Reyes came ashore on a cold bright morning, and the boat was behind him at last, and he found he could not stop breathing the open air.

They had been debriefed in rooms without windows by men who thanked them and warned them and made clear that the patrol of the USS Cuttlefish had not, in any way that could be spoken of, occurred. He signed the papers. He took the warnings. He understood the shape of the thing now, the way Hale had told him to, all those weeks ago in the red light: be a hole in the ocean, and if you make noise, become a hole in the sea floor.

They had made noise. They had surfaced. They had lived.

He walked down the pier in the cold sun. Behind him the men were going their ways — Garrity to another boat, Cole to a desk and a commendation no one could explain, Tran on his first liberty, grinning, taller somehow. Mahon, who could hear a five-bladed screw across an ocean, walking quiet into an ordinary morning. Salas. Okafor. Pruitt. Diaz, steadier now. Pollard, who would carry his small terrible mistake all his life and had been told, by Reyes, that he was forgiven.

Reyes stopped at the end of the pier and looked back at the black hull riding low in the water. He thought of the men who had breathed because he chose, and the captain who had given him the order to choose them. The weight did not lift. He understood now that it never would, and that this was right.

He turned and walked up into the living world, and took, as he went, one more deep and ordinary breath.
