Chapter 1: The Wharf at Tripoli

Captain James Morrison stepped off the French schooner and onto the stone wharf of Tripoli. The sun hammered the harbor. Salt-crusted walls threw back the heat. A purse of gold sovereigns weighed his coat down on the left side.

He watched the camels kneel in the dust, their handlers cursing in three languages. Blocks of salt gleamed like marble in the morning light. Bales of silk swayed between the beasts. Steel bars clanged as laborers loaded the last of them.

Morrison touched the dispatch case strapped beneath his shirt. Oilcloth and wax. Three layers. He had been told it would survive the crossing. He had been told many things.

A European in a linen suit brushed past him and dropped a folded paper at his feet without stopping. Morrison picked it up. A single line in French: "The rendezvous is moved forward. You leave at dusk."

He tore the paper into strips and let them fall into the harbor. The tide was going out.

Morrison found a bench in the shade of a customs house. He sat and watched the caravan assemble. Thirty camels. Five men. A mountain of supplies. He had never ridden a camel. The thought sat sour in his stomach.

A tall man in a blue robe walked among the beasts, checking every knot and strap. His movements were precise, economical. No wasted gesture. He wore a tagelmust — the Tuareg veil — and his eyes were the only visible part of his face. They were the color of old copper.

The man looked at Morrison once, briefly, then returned to his work. Morrison felt examined and dismissed in the same glance.

He lit a cigarette. The smoke tasted of salt and tar. The sun climbed higher. The heat pressed down.

---

Chapter 2: The Man Who Knew Too Much

Morrison found the French clerk in a back room of the customs office. The man was dying. His skin had the waxy sheen of a fever that had burned too long. A bottle of sour wine sat half-empty beside his cot.

"Captain Morrison?" The clerk's voice was a thread.

"Yes."

"Good. I do not have long." The clerk reached under his mattress and produced a leather folder. "The cipher. Names. Dates. Tribal payment schedules. Everything I copied from the governor's desk."

Morrison took the folder. It was warm from the clerk's body.

"You read it?" Morrison asked.

"Every word." The clerk coughed. Blood spotted the cloth at his lips. "France is buying every sheikh from the Nile to the Atlas. When they rise, Britain will be too busy putting down fires to notice the army marching on Cairo."

"And you stole this why?"

"Because I am French, but I am also a man." The clerk closed his eyes. "Beware the man who sells both sides. He travels with this caravan. I do not know his name, but he knows yours."

The clerk's breathing stopped. Morrison watched for a full minute to be certain. Then he tucked the folder beside the dispatch case and left through the back door.

The alley was empty. The dying sun painted the walls amber. Morrison walked toward the souk, measuring every shadow.

---

Chapter 3: The Souk Negotiation

The souk of Tripoli roared with trade. Merchants called over mountains of dates and pyramids of spice. A goat bleated somewhere in the chaos. Morrison pushed through the crowd, his hand resting on the purse in his coat.

He found Suleiman al-Rashid in a carpet shop at the edge of the square. The Trader was a barrel of a man, fifty years old, with a graying beard and gold rings on every finger. He sat cross-legged on a silk cushion, sipping mint tea.

"You are the Englishman," The Trader said. Not a question.

"I am."

"My contact said you would come. He did not say why you travel south." The Trader gestured for tea. A boy appeared with a steaming glass.

"I trade," Morrison said. "English rifles for Sudanese ivory. The profit is three hundred percent."

The Trader smiled. It did not reach his eyes. "Rifles. Of course. And what else?"

"Nothing else."

"The desert strips lies from men, Captain. Within a week, I will know everything about you. Tell me now, and save us both the performance."

Morrison sipped his tea. It was scalding and sweet. "I carry letters for the British consul in Kano. Commercial correspondence. You may inspect them if you wish."

The Trader laughed. "I will inspect nothing. I am paid to deliver cargo, not to read it. Three hundred gold sovereigns. Half now, half on arrival in Kano."

They shook hands. The Trader's palm was dry and hard as leather.

---

Chapter 4: The Guide's Silence

Amar ag Bah stood apart from the other men. He had finished checking the camels an hour ago. Now he stood motionless at the gate, watching the sun descend. His tagelmust was wound tight, leaving only a slit for his eyes.

Morrison approached. Amar did not turn.

"You are the guide?"

Silence.

"I was told you know the route to Kano."

Amar turned slowly. The copper eyes studied Morrison from turban to boots. "I know every route."

"I need a man who can read the sand. Find water. Keep us alive."

"I can do these things." Amar's voice was low, accented, the words measured. "My price is one rifle and two hundred cartridges. Delivered when we reach Kano. I keep the rifle."

Morrison calculated. The rifles were supposed to be trade goods, but he could spare one. "Accepted."

Amar nodded once and turned back to the sun.

Morrison stood beside him for a moment. "Is there anything else I should know?"

"The desert is not your enemy," Amar said. "It does not hate you. It simply does not care. That is worse."

He walked away without another word. Morrison watched him go. A strange calm settled over him. He had crossed the Khyber Pass and survived the snows of the Hindu Kush. The Sahara could not be worse.

He was wrong.

---

Chapter 5: Loading the Beasts

Dawn broke red over the harbor. The caravan stirred. Men coughed and spat. Camels groaned as they rose, their joints cracking like pistol shots.

Morrison helped load the last of the supplies. He had insisted on doing his share. The men watched him with amusement but said nothing. A European who worked with his hands was strange to them.

He noticed the man on the rooftop as he tied a water skin to a saddle. A European in a dark jacket, sitting cross-legged behind a low wall. Watching through a brass spyglass.

Morrison did not stare. He finished his work and walked to where Amar stood.

"Rooftop. East wall. Do not look."

Amar did not look. "I saw him yesterday. He arrived on a French naval cutter."

"He is intelligence."

"He is dead if he follows." Amar said it without malice. A statement of fact.

Morrison returned to his camel. He checked the cinch three times. The case against his chest was a hard, warm presence. The cipher folder was tucked into his saddlebag.

The Trader mounted his camel with surprising grace for a heavy man. He raised his hand. The drivers cracked their whips. The caravan lurched forward.

Morrison's camel rose behind the front legs first, then the back. The motion threw him forward. He grabbed the pommel and held on. The man on the roof lowered his spyglass and wrote something in a notebook.

The gate to the desert swung open.

---

Chapter 6: The Gate to the Desert

The caravan passed through Bab al-Bahr — the Sea Gate — and the city fell away behind them. The stone houses shrank. The harbor vanished. The world became sand and sky.

Morrison had imagined the desert as flat. It was not. The land rolled in long, slow waves like a frozen ocean. Pale gold and rose and ochre. The track wound between low hills, each one exactly like the last.

The heat came in a wall. Not the dry heat of an oven, but a wet, pressing heat that wrapped around the body like a blanket pulled from boiling water. Morrison's shirt was soaked within the first mile.

Amar rode at the head of the column. He did not look back. The camels followed him with the dumb faith of animals. His blue robe rippled in the hot wind.

The Trader swung his camel beside Morrison's. "How do you find our desert?"

"Hot."

The Trader laughed. "This is not hot, Captain. This is the doorway. The oven is ahead."

"How far to the first water?"

"Three days. Ghadames. A walled city of palm trees and cold springs. You will think you have reached paradise." The Trader's eyes narrowed. "You will be wrong. Paradise in the desert is always a lie. The truth is thirst, flies, and the endless negotiation of who owns the shade."

He spurred his camel forward and left Morrison alone with the heat.

---

Chapter 7: First Camp

They made camp in a dry riverbed. The walls of the wadi rose ten feet on either side, offering shade. The camels were hobbled and fed. A fire was lit, though no one needed its warmth. It was for tea.

Morrison's body ached. His thighs burned from gripping the camel. His spine felt compressed. He sat on a flat rock and watched the men work. They moved with a rhythm he did not have.

Amar approached. He carried a strip of leather in his hands. Without speaking, he knelt and examined Morrison's sandals. The right strap was frayed nearly through.

Amar cut a fresh length of leather and threaded it through the sole. His fingers moved quickly, surely. He did not look up.

"You will not survive a week in those," he said.

"I have others."

"Not in your bags. I checked."

Morrison had no answer. Amar finished the repair and rose. He met Morrison's eyes for the first time. "You are not a merchant. Your hands are too soft. But you are not a coward either. That is something."

He walked away before Morrison could respond.

Morrison touched the repaired sandal. The leather was tight and clean. He thought of Afghanistan, of the guides who had led him into ambush and abandoned him. Amar was different. He did not know yet whether that was good or dangerous.

The stars came out in a flood. More stars than Morrison had ever seen. The Milky Way was a river of light.

---

Chapter 8: The Oasis of Ghadames

Ghadames rose from the sand like a dream. White walls. Palm groves. The sound of running water. Morrison had not believed such a place existed. Now he stood at its gate, drinking from a clay cup that a child had pressed into his hands.

The water was cold. It tasted of minerals and life.

The caravan settled in a courtyard near the central spring. Men bathed. Camels drank. The Trader disappeared into a merchant's house, and Morrison watched him go with a hunter's instinct.

Night fell. The oasis glowed with lantern light. Morrison walked the narrow streets, past whitewashed walls and carved wooden doors. He found The Trader in a tent behind the mosque, speaking French to a stranger.

Morrison did not approach. He stood in the shadow of a date palm and listened.

The Trader's voice, low and smooth: "Tell the governor the Englishman carries papers. I have not seen them, but I know their weight."

The stranger replied: "How many men does he travel with?"

"Five. The guide is Kel Ahaggar. A hard man. Do not underestimate him."

Morrison slipped away. His blood hummed with the confirmation of his suspicion. The Trader was selling information. But to whom? And how much did they know?

He returned to his bedroll and lay awake, watching the stars wheel overhead.

---

Chapter 9: The Disappearing Scout

They woke to discover the scout was gone. His camel stood placidly at the picket line, still saddled. The man himself had vanished as if the sand had swallowed him.

Amar examined the ground around the camp. He traced patterns in the dust that meant nothing to Morrison. Then he stood and walked to the edge of the wadi.

"Two men," he said. "European boots. They came from the east. They took him."

"Took him where?" Morrison asked.

Amar pointed east. "To talk to him. He will tell them everything he knows, and then they will kill him."

"We have to find him."

"We do not." Amar's voice was flat. "He is already dead. The only question is how much he told them before he died. We must move. Now."

The Trader argued for delay. The men were tired. The camels needed rest. Amar won the argument by simply beginning to load his camel. The rest followed.

Within an hour, the caravan was moving again, leaving Ghadames behind. Morrison looked back at the white walls. They looked less like paradise now. More like a trap that had closed too slowly.

The desert stretched ahead, empty and indifferent.

---

Chapter 10: Councils in the Dark

That night, Morrison confronted The Trader.

They stood apart from the camp, in the space between firelight and darkness. The Trader's face was half in shadow, half in orange glow.

"I heard you in Ghadames," Morrison said. "Speaking French. Selling information about me."

The Trader did not flinch. He tilted his head, considering Morrison like a merchant considering a flawed rug. "You followed me. Good. You have instincts."

"I have a question."

"Ask."

"Whose side are you on?"

The Trader laughed. It was a full, genuine sound that echoed off the dunes. "Side? Captain, I am a merchant. My side is the one that pays." He pulled a silver flask from his robes and offered it. Morrison did not take it.

"In the desert," The Trader continued, "every man has secrets. Yours smell of gunpowder and queen. I do not need to know the specifics. I only need to know that you are worth more alive than dead. Currently, you are. That is enough."

"For now."

"For now." The Trader took a long drink and capped the flask. "But prices change, Captain. They always change."

He walked back to the fire, leaving Morrison alone in the dark.

---

Chapter 11: The Ruins of Germa

On the fifth day, the caravan passed through the ruins of Germa. Ancient walls of mud brick crumbled into the sand. Broken columns lay like fallen soldiers. The wind made a sound like voices in the empty doorways.

Amar called a halt. He dismounted and walked among the ruins. Morrison followed.

"What was this place?"

"The city of the Garamantes," Amar said. "They ruled this desert when Rome was still a village. They built tunnels underground to bring water from mountains a hundred miles away. They buried their queens in tombs of crystal."

"Where did they go?"

Amar picked up a shard of pottery and turned it in his hands. "They did not go anywhere. They stayed. The sand came. That is the story of every city in the Sahara."

He told Morrison a legend as they walked: a Garamantian queen who discovered her husband plotting to sell their kingdom to Roman traders. She invited the plotters to a feast, then sealed them in a underground chamber and let the sand bury them alive.

"Why are you telling me this?" Morrison asked.

"Because a woman buried her enemies alive by pretending to be their friend," Amar said. "A lesson you have not learned."

He dropped the pottery shard and walked back to his camel. Morrison stood alone among the ruins. The wind moaned. The desert stretched in all directions, patient and waiting.

---

Chapter 12: Sandstorm

The sky turned yellow at noon.

Morrison noticed it first as a strange light, a copper-colored haze that dulled the edges of things. The camels grew restless. Amar shouted something Morrison could not understand.

Then the wind hit.

It came not as a gust but as a wall. Sand struck Morrison's face like a thousand needles. He was thrown from his camel — the beast had knelt, dropping its front legs without warning. Morrison hit the ground hard.

The world disappeared. Sand filled his mouth, his nose, his eyes. He could not see his own hands. The wind roared like a living thing.

He crawled. He did not know toward what. The sand buried his arms to the elbows. He thought of the Garamantian queen, of being buried alive.

A hand closed around his wrist.

Amar's face appeared through the brown haze, the tagelmust pulled over his mouth, his copper eyes slitted. He dragged Morrison to his feet. He pressed a rope into Morrison's hand — the guide rope of the camel train.

"Do not let go," Amar shouted. "Walk when I walk. Stop when I stop. If you let go, you die."

Morrison gripped the rope. He walked. He had no idea where they were going. He trusted the rope.

The storm raged for three hours. When it passed, Morrison found himself on his knees, coughing sand, his skin raw. The caravan was intact. Amar stood over him, unharmed, as if he had simply waited for the weather to change.

---

Chapter 13: The Lost Well

The storm had buried the well.

Amar stood at the place where the water should have been — a depression between two low hills, marked by a cairn of black stones. The stones were still there. The well was gone. Sand had filled it completely.

The caravan had two days of water left. Maybe three, if they rationed.

The Trader called a council. "We turn back. Ghadames is four days behind us. We can make it."

"And then what?" Morrison said. "We lose a week. The French patrols will find us."

"Better captured than dead of thirst."

Amar spoke for the first time. "There is another well. Thirty miles east. I drank from it as a boy."

"How long ago?"

"Thirty years."

The Trader laughed. "A well you have not seen in thirty years? In a desert where the dunes move a mile a year? You will kill us all."

Amar looked at Morrison. His copper eyes were steady. "I remember the landmarks. Three peaks in the shape of a hand. A black rock that looks like a sitting camel. I can find it."

Morrison made the decision. "We go east."

The Trader spat in the sand. "Your lives. I am only hired to carry cargo."

They turned east. The men said nothing. The silence was heavier than the heat.

---

Chapter 14: The Trader's Ledger

Morrison waited until the camp was asleep. The fire had burned to embers. The men lay wrapped in their blankets, breathing slow and deep. The camels grunted softly in the dark.

He crawled to The Trader's saddlebag. The leather was warm from the day's heat. The buckle opened without a sound.

Inside: a change of clothes, a compass, a pouch of dried dates, and a leather-bound ledger. Morrison took the ledger and retreated to the shadow of a dune.

He opened it by starlight. The pages were filled with names. British officials in Cairo and Alexandria. French officers in Algiers. Tribal sheikhs from the Tuareg, the Tebu, the Hausa. Next to each name, a figure. Gold sovereigns. French francs. Dates.

The Trader paid everyone. He was not a merchant. He was a network.

Morrison found his own name on the last page: "Captain James Morrison — British intelligence — cargo value unknown — destination Kano — deliver or eliminate."

Eliminate.

He closed the ledger and put it back. His hands were steady, but his heart was not. He lay in his blanket and stared at the stars until dawn.

---

Chapter 15: Lines in the Sand

The next morning, Amar found Morrison at the edge of camp, staring east.

"You searched his bags last night."

Morrison did not deny it. "How did you know?"

"I watch the camp when you sleep. You do not." Amar pulled his pipe from his robes and packed it with tobacco. "What did you find?"

"A ledger. Names. Payments. My name, with instructions to kill me if the price is right."

Amar lit his pipe. The smoke was sweet and sharp. "You are surprised."

"I am not surprised. I am tired."

"The desert does that." Amar exhaled a cloud of smoke. "I do not care what you carry, Captain. I care that you survive to carry it out. Whatever is in that case, it is worth more than my rifle. That means I keep you alive."

"Why?"

"Because I gave my word. Among my people, that is still a thing of value."

Morrison looked at the guide. The copper eyes held nothing but truth. "Thank you."

"Do not thank me. Thank the code I was born into." Amar stood and knocked the ash from his pipe. "We reach the well today. If it is dry, we will all die anyway. The ledger will not matter."

He walked back to the camels. Morrison followed.

---

Chapter 16: The Tassili Plateau

The caravan climbed onto the Tassili n'Ajjer in the third week. The land changed. Sand gave way to rock — a high plateau of sandstone carved by ancient winds into strange shapes. Pillars and arches and towers that looked like the work of mad architects.

Amar stopped at a wall of rock covered in paintings. Morrison dismounted to study them. Horned figures danced across the stone. Men with animal heads. Women with arms raised to a sky that had not changed in ten thousand years.

"Who made these?"

"A people before my people," Amar said. "They lived here when the desert was grass and rivers. They hunted elephants and hippos. They painted what they saw."

"And now?"

"Now the sand has eaten everything. Only the paintings remain, to remind us that no empire lasts."

Morrison touched the stone. It was warm from the sun. The horned figures seemed to move in the flickering light.

"We camp here tonight," The Trader announced. "The men need rest before the crossing."

Morrison did not argue. He sat before the paintings as the sun set, watching the colors deepen and shift. The horned men watched him back. He felt their ancient gaze like a weight.

---

Chapter 17: The Riders on the Ridge

They appeared at midday — a line of figures on the eastern ridge, mounted on camels, their blue robes dark against the sky. They did not move. They simply watched.

Amar stopped the caravan. He stared at the riders for a long moment.

"Who are they?" Morrison asked.

"My people."

The riders raised lances. A single man detached from the line and rode down the slope toward them. He stopped fifty yards away and called out in Tamasheq. Amar answered.

The conversation was brief. The rider turned and rode back to the ridge. The line of riders dipped over the crest and vanished.

"What did he want?"

"He wanted to know why an exile travels with an Englishman." Amar's voice was flat. "I told him it was none of his business."

"And he accepted that?"

"Tuareg do not fight other Tuareg in the sight of outsiders. It is a law older than Islam." Amar urged his camel forward. "But they will follow us. They will wait until we are alone."

"How far to the next water?"

"Four days. And yes, they will wait that long."

Morrison checked his revolver. Six chambers. Five loaded. He kept the hammer on an empty chamber. Old habit.

---

Chapter 18: The Offer

The messenger came at midnight. A young Tuareg with quick eyes and a scarred chin. He appeared in the camp as if the darkness had formed him. The guards had not seen him arrive.

He spoke to Amar in Tamasheq, ignoring the others. Amar listened. His face revealed nothing.

The messenger finished and waited. Amar stared at the fire for a long moment. Then he gave his answer in a single word.

The messenger left as silently as he had come.

"What did he want?" Morrison asked.

"The Kel Ahaggar offer me reinstatement. They will forget my exile. I will be welcome in my father's tent again." Amar's voice was quiet. "There is a price."

"What price?"

"Your dispatch case. And your head."

Morrison felt the weight of the case against his chest. "You refused."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Amar turned to look at him. The firelight flickered in his copper eyes. "Because they killed my sister's son while I was in exile. They told me he died of fever. I know better. The same men who banished me killed him to erase my blood from the tribe."

He stood and walked into the darkness. "I do not sell my honor for a welcome that is worth nothing."

---

Chapter 19: Fever

The fever took Morrison on the fourth night after the Tassili.

It began as a chill, then a burning. His teeth chattered while his skin radiated heat. He could not stop shaking. The world dissolved into fragments — the fire, the stars, Amar's face swimming above him like a moon.

He was back in Afghanistan. A burning village. Children running. A young woman clutching a baby. The crack of rifles. He had given the order to search the compound, and the ambush had sprung. Half his men died. He had carried the blood of his sergeant on his hands for three days before he could wash.

"Captain."

Amar's voice pulled him back. He was on his back. A knife blade glowed in the firelight, heated until it was almost white.

"Fever poison in your blood. I must cut it out."

Amar pressed the hot blade to Morrison's side. The smell of burning flesh filled Morrison's nostrils. He screamed.

The world went black.

He woke hours later. Dawn. Amar sat beside him, wrapping a bandage around his ribs. The wound was a neat incision, already cleaned and dressed.

"You will live," Amar said. "The poison is out."

Morrison could not speak. He nodded. Amar gave him water, and he drank.

---

Chapter 20: The Wells of Tin Tarabin

They reached the wells on the eighth day. Tin Tarabin — a scatter of stone troughs fed by an underground spring. Palm trees grew in a cluster around the water, their fronds green and alive against the brown world.

The caravan arrived in the afternoon. The men cheered. The camels smelled the water and broke into a stumbling run. Morrison slid from his saddle and fell to his knees at the edge of the trough.

He drank until his stomach hurt.

The Trader produced a violin from his baggage — an instrument Morrison had not known he carried. He sat on a rock by the water and began to play. A Turkish melody, sweet and sad, the notes winding through the air like smoke.

The men stopped to listen. Even the camels seemed to quiet.

Morrison sat with his back to a palm tree and listened. The music spoke of things he had no words for. Loss. Distance. The ache of places you could never return to.

Amar stood apart, his back to the water, watching the horizon. Always watching.

The Trader played until the sun set, and then he played in the dark.

---

Chapter 21: The French Patrol

They had been at Tin Tarabin for one day when the patrol arrived.

A dozen French cavalrymen in blue coats and white helmets. A lieutenant led them, a young man with a waxed mustache and a sword that looked too large for his frame.

The officer dismounted and approached The Trader. He spoke in French, deliberately loud enough for the whole camp to hear.

"Your cargo. We must inspect it."

The Trader spread his hands. "Lieutenant, I am a simple merchant. I carry salt and cloth and a few rifles for the tribal chiefs downriver. Nothing that concerns the Republic."

The lieutenant was not persuaded. "We have reports of British intelligence moving through this region."

Morrison sat perfectly still, his hand resting on his saddlebag. His revolver was inside.

The Trader laughed. "British intelligence? In my caravan? Lieutenant, I am Algerian. I have no love for the British. Search everything. You will find nothing but honest trade goods."

The lieutenant hesitated. The Trader pressed a small pouch into his hand — silver, by the weight.

"Drink to the health of the Republic tonight, Lieutenant. The water here is bitter, but the dates are sweet."

The lieutenant weighed the pouch. He nodded. The patrol mounted and rode south. Morrison released the breath he had been holding.

---

Chapter 22: The Weight of Secrets

That night, Morrison told Amar everything.

They sat apart from the camp, on a dune overlooking the wells. The moon was a sliver. The stars were blinding.

"I am not a merchant," Morrison said.

"I know."

"I carry a dispatch. A cipher. Proof that France is bribing every sheikh from here to the Nile. They plan to provoke a rebellion against Cairo. When Egypt falls, France controls the route to India."

Amar was silent for a long time. "And you believe Britain is better?"

"I believe Britain is what I serve. I do not have to believe it is better."

Amar nodded slowly. "You are honest. That is rare among your people."

"If the dispatch does not reach Kano, the rebellion will happen. Thousands will die. The French will take Egypt."

"And if it does reach Kano?"

"Then the British consul will know which sheikhs are bought. He will counter-bribe or arrest them. The rebellion will collapse."

Amar looked at the stars. "You carry the fate of nations in a leather case. And you trust it to a Tuareg exile and a merchant who sells information to both sides."

"I trust you," Morrison said. "The Trader I pay to do a job."

"You are a fool, Captain. But you are a brave fool. That counts for something."

---

Chapter 23: Breaking Camp

They left Tin Tarabin at dawn. The caravan pointed south, toward the Ténéré — the desert of deserts. Amar had warned them: this stretch was two hundred miles of nothing. No water. No shade. No landmarks.

The camels had drunk their fill. The water skins were full. The men had eaten and rested. They were as ready as they would ever be.

The Trader rode at the front, his violin packed away. He was quiet, watchful. Morrison had not mentioned the ledger. Neither had The Trader. The knowledge sat between them like a loaded pistol.

The land flattened. The rocks gave way to pale sand, then to a hard-packed surface of dark gravel. The Ténéré. The horizon was a clean, sharp line. No hills. No trees. No birds.

Morrison felt exposed. The sky was too large. The land was too empty. His mind kept returning to the same image: a man floating in an ocean of stillness.

Amar rode beside him. "Do not think about the distance. Think only about the next hour. The next mile. The next breath."

"I will try."

"You will learn. Or you will die." Amar urged his camel forward. "The Ténéré teaches one lesson. It does not offer second chances."

---

Chapter 24: The Hanged Man

They found the body at the crossroads.

The crossroads was nothing but a place where two faint tracks met. No sign marked it. But a wooden post had been driven into the ground, and a man hung from it, his wrists bound above his head. He had been dead for days. His skin was blackened, his eyes gone.

The caravan stopped. The men crossed themselves and muttered prayers.

"A bandit," The Trader said. "The Tuareg left him as a warning to others."

Amar dismounted and examined the body. "He was a guide. He led a French patrol to a water source, and the patrol killed the people at the well. The Kel Ahaggar caught him."

"And this is their justice."

"This is the law of the sand. There is no appeal." Amar remounted. "We should not linger. The spirits of the unburied are restless."

The caravan moved around the post, giving it a wide berth. Morrison could not stop looking at the body. The hands. The fingers curled like bird claws. He had seen men hanged before. In Afghanistan, he had ordered men hanged. He had thought he was past the shock of it.

He was not.

The post dwindled behind them. The desert erased it.

---

Chapter 25: The Traitor's Confession

Morrison cornered The Trader during the midday halt. There was no shade. The men sat on the sand, their robes pulled over their heads, baking in the stillness.

"I found your ledger," Morrison said.

The Trader did not react. He continued to chew a strip of dried meat. "I wondered when you would mention it."

"You have my name, with instructions to eliminate me."

"You also saw the payments to half the officials in North Africa. You saw that I pay everyone. That includes the men who want you dead."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only answer I have." The Trader swallowed and took a sip of water. "I work for many masters, Captain. That is how I survive. But I have a contract with you. I deliver you to Kano. I have never broken a contract."

"Until the price changes."

"Until the price changes." The Trader smiled. "But the price has not changed. You are still worth more alive than dead. Enjoy it while it lasts."

Morrison returned to his camel. He did not feel reassured.

---

Chapter 26: The Cracked Cistern

The cistern was a disappointment carved in stone.

It sat at the base of a low escarpment, a tank of masonry half-buried in sand. The caravan had counted on its water to survive the crossing of the Ténéré. They had rationed for two days at the cistern, a full rest.

The cistern was cracked. A long, jagged fracture ran from the rim to the base. Water had leaked out and evaporated, leaving only a dark stain on the stone. A few inches of muddy liquid remained at the bottom, thick with sand and camel dung.

The caravan council was brief.

"We turn back," The Trader said. "We have four days of water. Three, if the men are careful. Ghadames is five days behind us."

"If we go back, we die," Amar said. "The sun has been growing hotter. The water will run out on the fourth day, and we will still be two days from Ghadames."

"What is your alternative?"

"We press forward. Three days to the Aïr Mountains. With rationing, the water can last."

"And if it does not?"

"Then we die closer to water than we are now."

Morrison made the decision. "We press forward." He looked at The Trader. "If we survive, I will double your payment."

The Trader nodded. "I will hold you to that."

They filled every container with the muddy water. They strained it through cloth. It tasted of earth and salt. Morrison drank his portion and tried not to think about what was in it.

---

Chapter 27: Amar's Scar

They rested in the heat of the day, sheltering under a blanket stretched between two camel saddles. The shade was barely enough for one man, but Amar insisted Morrison take it. Amar sat in the full sun, unmoving.

Morrison saw the scar for the first time. A thick, raised line across Amar's chest, visible at the collar of his robe. The scar of a blade, deliberately made.

"What happened to you?"

Amar touched the scar. "This is the mark of my exile. My uncle carved it with a knife from my father's tent. It means I am dead to my people."

"Why?"

"I refused an order. My uncle told me to lead a raid on a village south of the mountains. The goal was not war. It was slaves. Children to sell in the markets of Tripoli. I refused."

"How many men did you command?"

"Forty. They went without me. They brought back thirty children. My uncle sold them for rifles. He carved this scar himself, to remind me that disobedience has a price."

Morrison looked at the scar. "You could have lied. Said you would lead the raid, then freed the children."

"If I had been clever, yes. But I was young. I believed that the truth was enough." Amar's voice was bitter. "I was wrong."

"You saved my life when I had the fever."

"That was not truth. That was a choice."

"The same thing."

Amar looked at him with something that might have been respect. "Perhaps it is, Captain. Perhaps it is."

---

Chapter 28: The Mirage

The mirages began on the second day in the Ténéré.

At first, they were subtle — a shimmer on the horizon that could have been heat. Then they became elaborate. Lakes of blue water ringed by palm trees. Cities with white minarets. Ships sailing across waves of sand.

The men began to hallucinate. One man — a young porter named Hassan — screamed and ran toward a phantom lake. Two others had to tackle him and hold him down while he wept.

Amar gathered the men. His voice was sharp. "Do not look at the horizon. Look at the tail of the camel in front of you. See only the tail. The tail is real. Everything else is a lie."

The men obeyed. They marched with their eyes fixed on the switching tails of the beasts ahead.

Morrison forced himself to follow the instruction. It was harder than it sounded. The mirages pulled at his eyes like magnets. Every lake, every city promised relief. But he kept his gaze on the camel's tail, on the coarse hair, on the flies that clustered at the beast's flank.

He realized, hours later, that he had stopped seeing the mirages. His world had shrunk to the tail, to the rhythm of the camel's gait, to the breath in his chest.

The desert had won. He no longer expected to survive.

---

Chapter 29: Pursuit

Amar saw the dust first.

They had stopped for the evening rest. The sun was low, painting the sand in shades of copper and violet. Amar stood on a dune, his silhouette sharp against the sky.

"Riders," he said. "North. Many of them."

Morrison climbed the dune. He saw nothing at first, then a smudge on the horizon. Dust. Moving fast.

"How many?"

"Ten. Maybe fifteen."

"The Tuareg?"

"French. The dust is different. European horses, not camels."

Morrison's mind raced. "Can we outrun them?"

"No. But we can reach the Aïr before they catch us. The mountains are one day east. If we reach the canyons, we can lose them."

The caravan moved at dusk. Amar drove the men hard, pushing the camels beyond their normal pace. The beasts groaned and spat but moved faster. The light faded. The stars came out.

Morrison looked back. The dust cloud was larger now, closer. He could see individual riders toward the front, their shapes dark against the fading sky.

They would not make it to the mountains before dawn. He knew it. Amar knew it.

But they would make it closer than if they had stopped.

---

Chapter 30: The Night March

The caravan marched through the night.

The camels stumbled on rocks the men could not see. The rope line between them became a lifeline. Morrison walked with one hand on the rope, his camel following behind on instinct. He had not walked this far since his army days. His feet bled through the repairs Amar had made in the first week.

The men did not speak. Speech required breath, and breath was too precious. They moved like ghosts, following the rope, following the man ahead, trusting that the man at the front knew where he was going.

Morrison's boots disintegrated. The soles flapped loose, and he kicked them off. He walked barefoot. The sand was cool at night. The rocks were not.

When dawn came, the mountains were visible. Dark shapes on the horizon, still miles away.

Morrison looked back. The riders were closer. He could see the blue of their coats, the flash of a saber catching the first light.

Amar appeared beside him. "We are not going to make it."

"I know."

"Then we fight."

Morrison checked his revolver. Five rounds. A knife. He had led men into worse positions. He could not remember when.

---

Chapter 31: The Canyon Ambush

The canyon was a narrow wound in the rock, its walls a hundred feet high. The floor was sand and gravel, wide enough for three camels abreast. Amar led the caravan into it without hesitation.

The French riders reached the canyon mouth thirty minutes later. They paused, assessing the risk of ambush. The lieutenant — the same young officer from Tin Tarabin — gestured forward. The patrol entered the canyon.

Amar had positioned the caravan around a bend, out of sight. He had chosen his ground. He spoke to Morrison in a low voice: "They will come around the corner in single file. The canyon narrows ahead. They cannot flank us."

The first rider appeared. Then a second. A third.

Amar raised his rifle. He fired. The first rider fell.

Morrison fired at the second. The bullet struck the horse, not the man. The horse screamed and reared, throwing its rider into the path of the riders behind them.

Chaos. Horses panicked. Men shouted. Amar fired again, and again, each shot finding a target.

The French retreated around the bend, dragging their wounded. The canyon fell silent.

Amar counted the bodies. "Three dead. Two wounded. They will not try again soon."

Morrison leaned against the canyon wall. His hands were shaking. "They will send for reinforcements."

"Yes. We must be gone before they arrive."

---

Chapter 32: The Fight

The French regrouped at the canyon mouth. Morrison watched them from a notch in the rock. The wounded were being bandaged. A rider was already galloping north — for reinforcements.

"They will wait," The Trader said. "They have food and water. We do not."

"Then we do not wait." Amar reloaded his rifle. "We finish them."

It was not a plan Morrison would have chosen. But there was no other option.

They left the camels with one man — the porter, Hassan, who was too frightened to fight. The rest advanced through the canyon, using the boulders and walls for cover.

Morrison carried his revolver and a knife Amar had pressed into his hand. The steel was cold.

They reached the canyon mouth. The French had set a defensive line behind their dead horses. The lieutenant saw them and drew his saber.

"Surrender," he called in French. "You are outnumbered."

Morrison answered in French: "Count again."

Amar fired. A soldier fell.

The fight lasted six minutes. Morrison shot two men. Amar killed four. The Trader threw a knife into the chest of a soldier who had been aiming at Morrison's back.

The lieutenant was the last to fall. He died with his saber in his hand, his mustache still waxed, his eyes open to the sky.

Morrison was hit. A bullet had grazed his shoulder. The wound bled freely but would not kill him.

Amar wrapped it with a strip of cloth. "You owe me a life," he said. "I will collect when we reach the river."

Morrison nodded. He could not speak.

---

Chapter 33: Field Dressing

Morrison sat with his back to the canyon wall while Amar stitched his wound.

The needle was a curved steel thing, thin and sharp. The thread was camel tendon, dried and rehydrated in brandy. Amar worked without hesitation, pushing the needle through skin, pulling the thread tight, tying each stitch with a neat knot.

Morrison gritted his teeth. The pain was a bright, clean thing that cut through the fog of exhaustion.

"You have done this before," Morrison said.

"Many times. The desert does not discriminate. It wounds Tuareg and Englishman alike."

The Trader stood watch at the canyon mouth. The bodies of the French patrol lay where they had fallen. The vultures were already circling.

"You owe me a life," Amar said. "I will collect when we reach the river."

"So you said."

"I meant it. A debt is a debt."

Morrison watched the needle rise and fall. "What will you ask for?"

"I do not know yet. But the river is far. I have time to decide."

Amar tied the last stitch and cut the thread. He packed the wound with a paste of herbs and wrapped it in clean linen. "Do not use your right arm for a week. If you do, the wound will open, and I will not stitch it again."

"I understand."

"Good."

---

Chapter 34: The Mountains of Aïr

The caravan emerged from the canyon into the Aïr Mountains.

The change was immediate. The air cooled. The sand gave way to granite and volcanic rock. Streams ran between the peaks, narrow ribbons of clear water. The sound of moving water was so foreign that Morrison stopped and listened, unable to trust his ears.

Amar led them through the passes. The mountains were ancient, their peaks worn smooth by millennia of wind. They rose in spires and domes, colored in shades of gray and red and black.

Water was abundant. Small springs fed pools in the hollows of the rocks. The men drank until their stomachs swelled. The camels grazed on scrub grass that grew in the protected valleys.

Morrison felt the tension in his shoulders ease for the first time in weeks. They had survived the Ténéré. They had survived the French. The desert seemed, for a moment, almost welcoming.

Amar stopped at a high pass and pointed south. "Beyond these mountains, the land changes again. Savanna. Rivers. Villages. The desert ends."

"How long?"

"Two weeks. Three, if we move slowly."

"We move as fast as we can."

Amar nodded. "That is what I expected you to say."

---

Chapter 35: The Hidden Village

Amar led them to a village hidden in a fold of the mountains.

It was small — perhaps twenty stone houses clustered around a central well. Women in brightly colored cloth carried water jars on their heads. Children ran through the narrow streets, chasing goats. The smell of cooking fire and spices filled the air.

When the villagers saw Amar, the children ran to him. He lifted them, one after another, his copper eyes crinkling with a smile Morrison had never seen.

Children called him "uncle." An old woman embraced him and wept.

Morrison stood apart, an outsider. The Trader sat on a rock and lit his pipe.

"His sister's village," The Trader said. "He is a hero here."

"He told me he was exiled."

"He is exiled from the Kel Ahaggar. But these people are not Kel Ahaggar. They are a different clan. To them, Amar is the man who refused to sell children. That makes him a saint."

Morrison watched Amar accept a bowl of milk from the old woman. He drank it slowly, savoring it. In this place, among these people, he was not the silent guide. He was a man with a family, a history, a life beyond the caravan.

Morrison felt the distance between them more keenly than the desert had ever done.

---

Chapter 36: The Feast

The village prepared a feast.

A goat was slaughtered. Flatbread baked on hot stones. A pot of millet stew simmered over the fire. The men of the village gathered in a circle, passing a bowl of fermented milk that tasted of smoke and honey.

Morrison sat among them. They spoke a mix of Tamasheq and Hausa, and he understood little. But they smiled at him, offered him food, touched his arm with warm hands.

Amar's sister, Tama, sat beside him. She was a woman of perhaps thirty-five, with strong hands and the same copper eyes as her brother. She examined Morrison's wound without asking permission.

"You are healing," she said in French. "Amar's work is good."

"You speak French."

"I was married to a French trader. He died. I learned the language during the years I wished he would die." She said it without bitterness, as a simple fact.

She reapplied the dressing with fresh herbs, her fingers gentle but efficient. When she finished, she looked at him with her brother's directness. "You carry something heavy. It is not only the wound. Do not let it crush you before you reach your destination."

Morrison had no answer. She stood and returned to the cooking fire.

---

Chapter 37: The French Message

The runner arrived the next morning.

He was a boy of perhaps fifteen, barefoot, breathing hard, his robe caked with dust. He had come from a village two days north, carrying a folded paper sealed with red wax.

The paper was addressed to The Trader.

Morrison intercepted it before The Trader could read it. He broke the seal and read the French handwriting inside:

"Rashid — The Englishman's cargo has been confirmed by our sources in Tripoli. He carries a cipher naming every sheikh who has accepted payment. A price of 5,000 francs is placed on his head, dead or alive. The bounty is doubled if the cipher is recovered intact. Deliver him to our agent in Agadez. — Colonel Gerard, Bureau des Renseignements."

Morrison folded the paper and put it in his pocket.

The Trader watched him. "That was for me."

"It concerns me."

"Five thousand francs is a great deal of money, Captain."

"It is. But not as much as ten thousand. Which is what I will pay you when we reach Kano."

The Trader smiled. "I will hold you to that."

Morrison walked away, his heart hammering. He had bought more time. He was not sure how much.

---

Chapter 38: The Betrayal Weighed

Morrison found The Trader by the well that evening. The older man was washing his hands, the water running brown over his gold rings.

"I know about the bounty," Morrison said.

The Trader dried his hands on his robe. "I assumed you would read the message."

"Are you going to collect it?"

"That depends." The Trader sat on the edge of the well. "You offer me ten thousand francs to deliver you safely. The French offer five thousand to deliver you dead. The difference is not enough to justify betraying a contract."

"And if the bounty rises?"

"Then we renegotiate." The Trader's eyes were flat. "I am not a good man, Captain. But I am a predictable one. As long as you offer more than the other side, your cargo will reach its destination."

"And if I cannot pay?"

"Then I will be very sorry. And very rich."

Morrison turned away. He understood now. The Trader was not an ally. He was a force of nature, like the desert — moveable by gold, not by loyalty. Morrison would have to reach Kano before The Trader's price changed.

---

Chapter 39: The Price Set

Morrison spread his remaining gold on his blanket.

Two hundred and forty sovereigns. His emergency bank draft on the Bank of England, worth three hundred more upon presentation in Kano. A signed letter of credit from the British consulate in Tripoli. His father's silver watch.

It was not enough.

He calculated: The Trader's payment of three hundred sovereigns was already promised. The bonus of ten thousand francs — roughly four hundred sovereigns — was more than he could produce. He had been gambling on the British consul in Kano honoring his draft.

He found Amar and told him the truth about his finances.

"So you are broke," Amar said.

"Essentially."

"Then you cannot pay The Trader the bonus."

"No."

Amar considered this. "If you cannot pay him, he will sell you to the French. The only question is where."

"Agadez. The message said their agent is there."

"Agadez is six days south. We can reach it before he betrays us. But we cannot stay there."

"What is your advice?"

"Reach Agadez. Find the British agent there. Get funds. Pay The Trader before he decides to collect the French bounty." Amar's eyes were steady. "It is a race, Captain. You had better win it."

---

Chapter 40: Southward Again

They left the mountain village at dawn.

The children ran beside the camels for a mile, laughing. Tama pressed a pouch of dried herbs into Morrison's hand. "For the wound. Change the dressing every three days."

He thanked her. She looked at him with her brother's eyes. "Do not let him die," she said. "He has earned a peaceful old age."

"I will do what I can."

The caravan descended from the mountains. The air grew warmer, heavier. The granite gave way to red earth, then to scrub grass. The first baobab trees appeared — enormous, ancient, their trunks swollen with water.

The desert was behind them. Ahead lay the savanna. Morrison felt no relief. The dangers here were different, but no less real.

The Trader rode at the front, his back straight, his eyes on the horizon. Morrison watched him constantly, looking for the sign of betrayal he knew would come.

The days passed. The landscape grew greener. The nights were warm and full of insect sounds.

They were making good time. But Agadez was still far, and Morrison's gold was running out.

---

Chapter 41: The River

The Niger River appeared as a green thread on the horizon, then grew into a wide, brown ribbon of moving water. Morrison had never thought he would weep at the sight of a river. He wept.

The caravan reached the bank in the late afternoon. The water was muddy and warm. It tasted of fish and earth and life. Morrison waded in fully clothed, letting the current pull at his legs.

Amar stood on the bank, watching him. For the first time since they had met, he laughed. A low, rumbling sound that seemed to surprise even him.

"What is funny?"

"An Englishman, weeping in a river that has seen Roman legions and Songhai emperors." Amar shook his head. "You are not what I expected, Captain."

"What did you expect?"

"A stiff back. A monocle. A man who would complain about the tea."

Morrison laughed. It hurt his ribs. "I do not have a monocle."

"Then you have already exceeded my expectations."

They made camp on the riverbank. The sound of water was a constant presence. The men sang. The Trader played his violin. The stars reflected in the moving current.

Morrison lay awake, listening. He had never felt so far from home. He had never felt so alive.

---

Chapter 42: The Slavers' Camp

They found the slavers' camp the next day.

It sat on the east bank of the river, a sprawling collection of tents and makeshift pens. The pens were full of people — men, women, children — their wrists bound with rope, their eyes empty.

Morrison counted two hundred captives. Maybe more.

Amar stopped the caravan well before the camp was visible. He gathered the men in a grove of acacia trees.

"The slavers control the river route for the next ten miles," he said. "They will not let us pass without paying. And they may decide to add us to their cargo."

"Can we go around?" Morrison asked.

"Not through the swamp. The camels will founder."

"Then we go through at night."

The Trader shook his head. "The slavers post guards. They will see us."

"Then we go through the water."

Amar studied the river. "The current is strong. The camels can swim, but they will panic. We will lose supplies."

"How much?"

"Half, if we are lucky."

Morrison made the decision. "Night crossing. We leave everything that is not essential. The dispatch comes first. Everything else is expendable."

---

Chapter 43: The Night Crossing

They crossed at midnight.

The moon was a sliver. The river was black, its surface broken only by the dim reflection of stars. Amar stretched a rope across the current, tying it to trees on both banks. The men would guide the camels along the rope.

Morrison went first. The water was colder than he expected. It rose to his chest, then to his chin. He held the rope with one hand and the dispatch case above his head with the other.

The camels screamed as they entered the water. The sound was terrible — a high, desperate noise that seemed to carry for miles. Morrison was sure the slavers would hear.

One camel panicked. It thrashed against the rope, broke free, and was swept downstream. It carried half the remaining supplies with it — food, ammunition, the spare water skins.

The men cursed. The camel's screams faded into the darkness.

They reached the far bank at dawn, exhausted and soaked. The Trader counted what remained. "We lost the medicines. Half the food. Most of the ammunition."

Morrison looked at the dispatch case in his hand. Dry. Intact.

"Then we travel light," he said.

---

Chapter 44: The Man from Cairo

The village of Say sat on a bend of the river. It was a market town, a crossroads where traders from the north met traders from the south. The caravan arrived in the afternoon.

Morrison found the British agent in a tea house. The man was young, nervous, dressed in dusty European clothes. He introduced himself as Wilkins, a clerk from the Cairo office.

"You made it," Wilkins said. "We received word you were coming. We feared you had been lost."

"I nearly was." Morrison handed over the cipher folder. "This is everything. French payments, tribal schedules, the names of every bought sheikh. But I need you to carry a message as well — the details I memorized."

Wilkins took the folder with trembling hands. "This is — this is enormous. The ambassador will — "

"The ambassador needs to act before the French do. There is a copy of the dispatch in my head. Let me dictate."

Morrison spent an hour reciting names, dates, numbers. Wilkins wrote it all down. When they finished, the young man looked pale.

"I will ride north tonight," Wilkins said. "The French will never know we have this."

Morrison watched him go. Half the dispatch was safe. The other half was still in his head. He needed to reach Kano.

---

Chapter 45: The Last Betrayal

They were a day from Agadez when The Trader made his move.

Morrison had known it was coming. He had been watching the man's eyes, the way they calculated, the way he lingered over Morrison's saddlebag. The price had changed.

The French agent stepped from the shadows of a baobab tree as the caravan passed. A tall man with a waxed mustache and a revolver in his hand. He had been following them since Tripoli. He had finally caught up.

"Captain Morrison," the agent said in French. "I have been looking for you."

The Trader did not look surprised. He sat on his camel, watching, his face unreadable. He had sold Morrison to both sides — collected from the British for the journey, and from the French for the betrayal at the end.

"You have a choice," the agent said. "Hand over the dispatch, and I will let you live. The French government has no interest in a public execution. We simply want the information."

Morrison looked at Amar. The guide had his hand on his rifle, but there were three other French soldiers emerging from the trees. They were surrounded.

Morrison made his choice.

---

Chapter 46: The Standoff

Morrison reached inside his shirt and pulled out the dispatch case. The oilcloth was worn and stained. He had carried it for two months across a thousand miles of desert.

"I am going to burn this," he said.

The French agent laughed. "You are bluffing."

Morrison struck a match. He held it to the corner of the case. The oilcloth caught. The flame spread.

"No!" The agent lunged, but Amar stepped between them, his rifle raised.

Morrison held the burning case until the heat scorched his fingers. He dropped it in the sand and watched it turn to ash.

"The information in that case is already in my head," Morrison said. "I dictated it to a British agent at Say. The copy is halfway to Cairo. You have lost."

The French agent stared at the ashes. His face was white with fury. "I could kill you."

"You could. But France is not ready for a war with Britain. That is why you used bribes instead of bullets. Killing a British officer would change the equation."

The agent was silent. The logic was sound, and he knew it.

"You are lucky, Captain." The agent holstered his revolver. "Tell your queen she was lucky, too." He turned and walked back into the trees. His men followed.

---

Chapter 47: The Frenchman's Decision

Morrison watched the French agent disappear into the trees. His hands were shaking. The ashes of the dispatch case still smoked in the sand.

"Why did he let us go?" Amar asked.

"Because killing me would start a war. France is not ready. They wanted to control Egypt through proxies, not through open conflict. I made it clear their plan has failed."

"And the ledger?"

Morrison turned to The Trader. "The ledger. Give it to me."

The Trader hesitated. Then he reached into his saddlebag and pulled out the leather book. He tossed it to Morrison. "It is worthless now. The names are known. The payments have stopped."

"Worthless to you. Valuable to me." Morrison tucked the ledger into his shirt. "This is proof. Every British official who took French money. Every sheikh who was bought. This ledger will clean out the corruption in Cairo."

The Trader shrugged. "Take it. I have copies."

"Of course you do."

Amar remounted his camel. "We are still a day from Agadez. And I am still owed a debt."

Morrison smiled. It was a strange feeling, like a muscle he had forgotten how to use. "You will have it."

---

Chapter 48: The Ledger's End

The caravan reached Agadez in the evening.

The city rose from the savanna like a dream of mud and stone. A great minaret dominated the skyline, shaped like a pyramid with wooden beams projecting from its walls. The market was closing. The last light painted everything amber.

Morrison went straight to the telegraph office. He sent a coded message to Cairo: "Cipher destroyed. Contents memorized and transmitted via Say. Ledger of payments in hand. Arriving Kano within the week."

The telegraph operator took his gold and sent the message. Morrison watched the wire, imagining the words traveling under the earth, through the desert, across the sea. A thread connecting him to the world he had left behind.

The Trader found him at the telegraph office. The man looked tired. Older than he had at the start of the journey.

"The ledger," The Trader said. "It has my name in it. If you use it, I will be arrested."

"Yes."

"Then let me make you an offer. I will disappear. You will never see me again. In exchange, you do not mention my name in your report."

Morrison considered. "You sold me to the French."

"I also delivered you alive. And I gave you the ledger voluntarily."

"After the French agent failed."

The Trader spread his hands. "In the desert, Captain, every man survives as he can. I am no different."

Morrison looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded. "Disappear. If I ever see you again, I will shoot you."

The Trader bowed. "A fair arrangement." He turned and walked into the twilight.

---

Chapter 49: Kano

Kano was a city of walls and markets and noise. The population was forty thousand, maybe more. The market alone could swallow a man for days. Caravans from every direction converged here, bringing salt and gold and cloth and slaves.

Morrison found the British consulate on a quiet street near the palace. A flag flew above the door. The sight of it made his chest tight.

The consul was a thin man named Barrington, with wire-rimmed glasses and the air of a man who spent his life behind a desk. He received Morrison in a room with a fan and a cold drink.

"Captain Morrison. We received your message from Agadez. You have done remarkable work."

"The dispatch is in Cairo?"

"It is. The ambassador acted immediately. The sheikhs have been confronted. The French plan has collapsed."

Morrison sat down. The chair was soft. The fan stirred the air. He had forgotten what comfort felt like.

"The ledger," he said, pulling it from his shirt. "This is the proof. Every British official who took French money."

Barrington took the ledger with reverence. "This will change everything."

"I need a draft. Three hundred pounds. My expenses."

"Of course." Barrington wrote the draft. He handed it to Morrison. "And for yourself? A promotion. A return to England."

"I need a bed. And a bath."

Barrington smiled. "I can arrange both."

---

Chapter 50: The Last Caravan

Morrison found Amar at the north gate of Kano.

The guide sat on his camel, his rifle slung across his back, facing the direction they had come. The desert was out there, invisible beyond the city walls, but Morrison knew Amar could feel it. The same way a sailor felt the sea through a closed window.

"You are leaving," Morrison said.

"I am."

"I offered you a position. The British army needs men who know the desert."

"The British army needs men who obey orders." Amar's copper eyes were steady. "I have never been good at that."

Morrison nodded. He had expected this. "Then I will pay you the rifle and ammunition I promised. And one more thing."

He reached into his pocket and pulled out a heavy gold sovereign. His last one. He pressed it into Amar's palm. "For your sister. Tell her it is for the herbs."

Amar closed his hand around the coin. He looked at Morrison for a long moment. "The debt is paid, Captain."

"Goodbye, Amar."

"The desert will remember you. That is a rare thing."

Amar turned his camel and rode through the gate. His blue robe rippled in the hot wind. The dust swallowed him slowly, first his shape, then his color, then the last trace of his presence.

Morrison watched until there was nothing left to see. Then he turned and walked back into the city.

The last caravan had crossed.
