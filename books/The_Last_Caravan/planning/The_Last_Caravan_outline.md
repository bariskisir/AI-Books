# The Last Caravan — Outline

## Premise

In 1850, the last camel caravan to cross the Sahara carries a British spy, a Tuareg guide, and a secret that could topple an empire. Captain James Morrison travels undercover as a merchant, carrying a coded dispatch revealing French colonial plans to carve up North Africa. Amar, a Tuareg outcast, knows the forgotten water routes. The Trader, a shadowy middleman, serves his own masters. Across a thousand miles of sand and stone, the three men hurtle toward a confrontation that will decide the fate of empires.

## Core Cast

- **Captain James Morrison** — British intelligence officer, late thirties, haunted by a failed mission in Afghanistan. Carries a dispatch that proves France is bribing tribal leaders to rise against British Egypt. Speaks Arabic passably. Trusts no one.

- **Amar ag Bah** — Tuareg guide, forty, exiled from his Kel Ahaggar clan for refusing to lead a slaving raid. Knows every dry riverbed and hidden well from Timbuktu to Tripoli. His silence is a weapon.

- **The Trader (Suleiman al-Rashid)** — Half-Algerian, half-Turkish merchant, fifty. Runs caravans across the Sahara. Seems loyal to the highest bidder. His true allegiance is an enigma until the final dunes.

## Chapter Plan

### ACT ONE: The Departure (Chapters 1–15)

**Chapter 1: The Wharf at Tripoli** — Captain Morrison steps off a French schooner onto the docks of Tripoli. A purse of gold weighs in his coat. He watches a camel train load salt and steel.

**Chapter 2: The Man Who Knew Too Much** — Morrison meets his contact, a dying French clerk who passes him a cipher. The clerk's last words: "Beware the man who sells both sides."

**Chapter 3: The Souk Negotiation** — Morrison haggles for passage in a crowded souk. He is introduced to Suleiman al-Rashid, The Trader, who offers him a place in the last caravan south.

**Chapter 4: The Guide's Silence** — Amar is summoned to the caravan. He studies Morrison with cold eyes. He says nothing for an hour, then agrees to guide. His price: a rifle and ammunition.

**Chapter 5: Loading the Beasts** — The caravan assembles: thirty camels, five men, supplies for sixty days. Morrison notices a man watching from a rooftop — a French intelligence agent.

**Chapter 6: The Gate to the Desert** — The caravan departs Tripoli through Bab al-Bahr. Morrison feels the weight of the desert for the first time — a heat that presses down like a hand.

**Chapter 7: First Camp** — The first night under stars. Amar corrects Morrison's sandal-binding. A small kindness that builds the first thread of trust between them.

**Chapter 8: The Oasis of Ghadames** — The caravan reaches the walled oasis. Morrison overhears The Trader speaking French to a stranger in a tent. His suspicion deepens.

**Chapter 9: The Disappearing Scout** — One of the caravan scouts vanishes overnight. His camel returns riderless. Amar reads the tracks: two men, European boots, heading east.

**Chapter 10: Councils in the Dark** — Morrison confronts The Trader. The Trader laughs and offers brandy. "In the desert, Captain, every man has secrets. Yours smell of gunpowder and queen."

**Chapter 11: The Ruins of Germa** — The caravan passes the ancient Berber ruins of Germa. Amar tells the story of a queen who buried her enemy alive beneath the sand. Morrison feels the parable is meant for him.

**Chapter 12: Sandstorm** — A khamsin rises without warning. The caravan scatters. Morrison is thrown from his camel and nearly buried. Amar finds him by sound alone, pulling him from the storm.

**Chapter 13: The Lost Well** — The storm has buried the well they depended on. Amar leads them thirty miles east to a well he remembers from childhood. The water is bitter but drinkable.

**Chapter 14: The Trader's Ledger** — Morrison searches The Trader's saddlebag while the man sleeps. He finds a ledger with names — British officials, French officers, tribal chiefs. All paid.

**Chapter 15: Lines in the Sand** — Amar confronts Morrison about the search. Morrison admits he is not a merchant. Amar's response: "I do not care what you carry. Only that you survive to carry it out."

### ACT TWO: The Crossing (Chapters 16–35)

**Chapter 16: The Tassili Plateau** — The caravan climbs onto the rock plateau of Tassili n'Ajjer. Ancient cave paintings of horned gods and swimming men cover the cliffs. A strange place, Amar says. The spirits are restless.

**Chapter 17: The Riders on the Ridge** — A line of Tuareg riders appears on a distant ridge. They watch for an hour, then vanish. Amar recognizes the markings: men of his own tribe, hunting.

**Chapter 18: The Offer** — A messenger from the Kel Ahaggar arrives at midnight. He offers Amar reinstatement in the tribe. His price: the Englishman's dispatch case. Amar refuses.

**Chapter 19: Fever** — Morrison falls ill with a burning fever. His dreams are tangled with Afghanistan — a burning village, a child's hand reaching from rubble. Amar bleeds him with a hot knife.

**Chapter 20: The Wells of Tin Tarabin** — The caravan reaches a rare surface well. The water is sweet. For one afternoon, the men rest without fear. The Trader produces a violin and plays a Turkish melody.

**Chapter 21: The French Patrol** — A French military patrol intercepts the caravan at the well. The officer demands inspection of cargo. The Trader negotiates with silver. The patrol leaves.

**Chapter 22: The Weight of Secrets** — Morrison, still weak from fever, tells Amar the truth: he carries proof that France is bribing the sheikhs to rise against Cairo. If the dispatch is lost, Egypt falls.

**Chapter 23: Breaking Camp** — The caravan pushes south into the Ténéré — the desert of deserts. Nothing lives here. The camels' pace slows. Men ration water to swallows.

**Chapter 24: The Hanged Man** — They find a crucified corpse at a crossroads — a bandit left as a warning. The Trader studies the body and says, "This is the law of the sand. There is no appeal."

**Chapter 25: The Traitor's Confession** — Morrison presses The Trader on his ledger. The Trader admits he works for many masters. "But I deliver your dispatch to Cairo, Captain. That is my contract."

**Chapter 26: The Cracked Cistern** — A cistern halfway across the Ténéré has cracked. Half the water is lost. The men vote: press on or turn back. Morrison's vote carries the decision to push forward.

**Chapter 27: Amar's Scar** — During a rest, Amar reveals the scar across his chest: the mark of his exile, carved by his own uncle. He tells the story of the child he refused to sell. Morrison sees the man anew.

**Chapter 28: The Mirage** — A full day of mirages. The men see lakes, cities, ships sailing on sand. One man weeps. Amar forces the caravan to march with eyes on the camels' tails — do not look up.

**Chapter 29: Pursuit** — Amar spots dust on the northern horizon. Riders, coming fast. The caravan must reach the shelter of the Aïr Mountains before the riders catch them.

**Chapter 30: The Night March** — The caravan marches through the night by starlight. Camels stumble. Men walk blind, holding rope. Morrison's boots dissolve. He walks barefoot.

**Chapter 31: The Canyon Ambush** — The riders catch them at dawn in a narrow canyon. Amar negotiates in Tamasheq. The riders are slavers, not tribesmen. They demand Morrison as payment for passage.

**Chapter 32: The Fight** — Amar shoots the leader. Morrison fires his pistol. The Trader throws a knife. Three slavers fall, the rest flee. Morrison is hit in the shoulder — a graze, but bleeding.

**Chapter 33: Field Dressing** — Amar stitches Morrison's wound with camel tendon. "You owe me a life," Amar says. "I will collect when we reach the river." Morrison nods. The debt is acknowledged.

**Chapter 34: The Mountains of Aïr** — The caravan enters the granite peaks of the Aïr. Ancient volcanic spires rise like fingers. The air is cooler. Water runs in narrow streams. Hope returns.

**Chapter 35: The Hidden Village** — Amar leads them to a Tuareg village hidden in the mountains. Children run to greet him. He is a hero here, not an exile. Morrison sees a different man — relaxed, smiling, home.

### ACT THREE: The Reckoning (Chapters 36–50)

**Chapter 36: The Feast** — The village hosts a feast. Morrison meets Amar's sister, Tama, a healer with kind eyes and a sharp tongue. She examines his wound and pronounces him stubborn.

**Chapter 37: The French Message** — A runner arrives from the north with a French message for The Trader. Morrison intercepts and reads it: the French know about the dispatch. They have offered a bounty.

**Chapter 38: The Betrayal Weighed** — Morrison confronts The Trader with the message. The Trader does not deny it. "I deliver you or I collect the bounty. The price must exceed the temptation."

**Chapter 39: The Price Set** — Morrison offers The Trader twice the French bounty, payable on delivery of the dispatch to the British consul in Kano. The Trader accepts. The deal is sealed with a handshake.

**Chapter 40: Southward Again** — The caravan leaves the mountains, heading south toward the savannah. The terrain changes — scrub grass, acacia trees, the first baobabs. The desert is behind them.

**Chapter 41: The River** — They reach the Niger River. Green reeds, muddy banks, the sound of water moving. Morrison falls to his knees. Amar laughs — the first time Morrison has heard the sound.

**Chapter 42: The Slavers' Camp** — A large slaving camp blocks their route along the river. Hundreds of men, women, and children in chains. The caravan must evade or fight.

**Chapter 43: The Night Crossing** — Amar guides them across the river at night, using a rope line to bring the camels through the current. One camel panics and drowns. They lose half their remaining supplies.

**Chapter 44: The Man from Cairo** — A British agent meets them at the village of Say. Morrison passes half the dispatch. The agent rides north. The backup plan is in motion.

**Chapter 45: The Last Betrayal** — The Trader reveals that the French agent from Tripoli has been following them all along. He steps from the shadows. The Trader has sold Morrison to both sides.

**Chapter 46: The Standoff** — The French agent demands the dispatch. Morrison burns it in front of him. "I have already memorized it. The copy is halfway to Cairo. You have lost."

**Chapter 47: The Frenchman's Decision** — The agent could kill Morrison, but that would start a war France is not ready for. He lets Morrison live. "Tell your queen she was lucky," he says, and rides north.

**Chapter 48: The Ledger's End** — The Trader gives Morrison the ledger. "This will be more valuable than any dispatch. Names, dates, prices. The truth about who runs the desert." Morrison takes it.

**Chapter 49: Kano** — The caravan reaches Kano. The market hums with trade. Morrison delivers the memorized dispatch to the British consul. Amar stands beside him, no longer a guide but a friend.

**Chapter 50: The Last Caravan** — Morrison learns that the French scheme has collapsed — the tribes did not rise. He offers Amar a position with the British army. Amar refuses. "The desert is my only uniform." They part at the city gate. Amar turns south toward home. Morrison watches until the dust swallows him. The last caravan has crossed.
