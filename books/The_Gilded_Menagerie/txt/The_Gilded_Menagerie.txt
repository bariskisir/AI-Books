Chapter 1: A Cold Field at Dawn

The grass was silver with frost when Nell Sparrow climbed the center pole. Her hands knew the rungs without looking. Forty feet up, the canvas had not yet been hauled, so there was only the gray sky and the bones of the rigging against it.

She tested the trapeze cable with her weight. It gave a little more than it should. The hemp had stretched over the winter, and nobody had spliced it.

"You're early," Dewey Hale called from below. He stood in the wet grass with a coil of rope on his shoulder.

"Ropes are early too," Nell said. "They've gone slack."

"Everything's gone slack." Dewey spat. "Come down before you fall and we have to pay a doctor we can't pay."

She did not come down. She sat on the bar and looked out over the lot. The town beyond the trees was small, a church steeple and a grain elevator and a few hundred souls who might come if it didn't rain. The wagons sat in a ragged ring, their gold paint cracked, the lettering on their sides faded to a whisper. HOLLIS & CRANE COMBINED SHOWS. There had been no Hollis in twenty years, but August kept the name because the painted boards had cost money once.

Nell had come to this show eleven years ago with nothing but a stolen coat and a talent for not being afraid of height. August had let her stay. She had learned the wire from a Hungarian woman who was dead now, and the flying from Tom Bellamy, who was not. The trapeze had taken her in when nothing else would.

She gripped the cable again and felt the give. "Dewey," she called. "We need new hemp before opening. This will part by July."

"Tell August."

"I will."

She climbed down. The frost was already lifting where the sun touched it, turning the field to mud. By noon the lot would be a churn of horses and men and the smell of canvas dressing. By six there would be a crowd, or there would not.

Dewey watched her drop the last few feet. "You worry too much about the ropes," he said.

"Somebody has to," Nell said, and went to find August.


Chapter 2: The Spiel

August Crane stood in the center ring in his red coat, and even with the coat gone thin at the elbows he made the small crowd lean forward when he spoke.

"Ladies and gentlemen," he said, and his voice filled the tent without seeming to try, "you have come tonight to a place that does not exist anywhere else in the world. For two hours, the ordinary is barred at the door."

Nell watched from the performers' entrance, in her tights and her short spangled skirt, waiting for the flying act. The crowd was thin. She counted the benches by eye, the way she had learned to. Two hundred, maybe. The tent held twelve hundred.

August did not seem to see the empty benches. He saw the children in the front, and he spoke to them, and they believed him. That was his gift. He could make a half-full tent feel like a full one until the moment the money was counted.

The band struck up, four men where there had once been twelve. The horses came in. Then the clowns, then Madame Lorca's doves, then Silas leading old Juno with a chain that hung loose because the elephant did not need leading.

When Nell's turn came she went up the rope ladder fast, and at the top the noise fell away as it always did. Up here the show was not poor. Up here there was only the bar swinging toward her and Tom's hands waiting to catch.

She flew. For ten minutes the tent forgot itself and cheered.

Afterward, in the back lot, August counted the take by lantern. Nell saw his face when he thought no one watched. The gift went out of it. He looked old.

"Good house," she said, to give him something.

"Fair house," August said, and folded the bills into a tin box that did not need to be large.


Chapter 3: The Menagerie Tent

Silas Roe slept in the menagerie tent more nights than not, on a cot near Juno's stall, because the elephant slept badly in new towns and a familiar breath nearby steadied her.

He was up before the cookhouse fire that morning, mixing her feed. He measured the grain and stopped. The barrel was half what it should have been. He measured again, as if measuring twice would make grain.

"Short," he said to Juno. She reached her trunk over the rope and felt his shoulder, his collar, the back of his neck, reading him the way she read everything.

The hay man had not come. Silas had ordered the load three days ago and paid a deposit out of his own thin wages, and the man had not come, and when Silas had walked to the feed store in the last town the clerk had looked at him oddly and said the Hollis account was closed.

Closed. Silas had handled animals for fifteen years, four of them for a syndicate show he did not speak of, and he knew what a closed account meant on the road. It meant somebody upstream had stopped paying, or stopped being trusted to pay.

He gave Juno the half ration and the apples he'd saved and let the other animals make do. The two camels. The lions, who ate meat the butcher now sold them on a string of promises. The horses, who at least could graze.

"I'll find the rest," he told the elephant. She did not believe him, but she ate.

He went looking for Dewey, and found instead a man in a clean gray suit standing at the edge of the lot, watching the animals being watered. The man had a small book in his hand and was writing in it.

Silas stopped. "Help you?"

The man smiled without showing teeth. "Just admiring the stock," he said. "Fine old elephant. What is she, forty? Fifty?"

"She's not for sale," Silas said, though no one had asked.

"Everything's for sale," the man said pleasantly, and wrote something else, and walked away.


Chapter 4: The Man Who Took Notes

His name was Cornelius Pratt, and he came to the matinee and the evening show both, and sat in the same seat each time, near the end of a bench where he could see the whole ring.

Madame Lorca noticed him first. She noticed everyone; it was her trade. From inside her little curtained booth she watched the crowd pass and she had learned to read a man's coat and his hands and the way he held his money. Pratt held his money loosely, like a man who had a great deal of it and did not think about it.

"That one," she said to Nell, nodding through the curtain. "He is not here for the clowns."

Nell looked. The man wrote in his book during the flying act, which no one did. People watched the flying act or they did not come.

"Reporter?" Nell said.

"A reporter writes after," Lorca said. "He writes during. He is counting."

"Counting what?"

"Heads. Benches. Wagons. The age of the horses." Lorca shuffled her cards without looking at them, an old habit. "I have seen his kind. They came to a show I worked in Ohio, before I came to August. After they came, the show was gone by Christmas, and the people were scattered, and the wagons had new paint and a new name."

Nell felt something cold go down her back that had nothing to do with the night air. "You think he's buying."

"I think he is shopping," Lorca said. "There is a difference. A buyer has decided. A shopper is still deciding what he can take and how cheap."

She laid three cards on the small table between them, face up, and looked at them a long moment, and then turned them face down again without telling Nell what they were.

"Go to bed, sparrow," she said. "Fly tomorrow. Worry is a thing you do on the ground, and you are no good on the ground."


Chapter 5: The Catch

The next afternoon Tom Bellamy held the catch bar and watched Nell leave the platform, and caught her wrists clean, and swung her back, and she made the return and stood on the platform breathing hard and grinning.

"Clean," she said.

"Clean," Tom agreed. He was a big handsome man with a flier's vanity, and he had taught her everything in the air and very little on the ground. They had been something to each other once, briefly, years ago, and it had ended without bitterness because they were better as partners than as anything else.

They climbed down together. In the shade of the dressing tent Tom toweled his neck and said, not looking at her, "I had a letter."

"From who?"

"A man who scouts for a show out of Chicago. A big show. Indoor winters at a hippodrome. They want a flying act."

Nell pulled the resin bag off her hands. "And?"

"And the money's real, Nell. Twice what August can pay, when August pays at all." He glanced at her. "They asked about a partner. A girl who flies. They asked about you by name."

She said nothing for a moment. Out in the ring the band was playing the cradle song for the wire walker.

"By name," she said.

"You've got a name, whether you know it or not." Tom hung the towel. "I'm just telling you. There's a world out there bigger than this canvas, and it isn't sinking."

"This isn't sinking," Nell said.

Tom looked at her, and didn't argue, which was worse than arguing.


Chapter 6: The Cards

Madame Lorca read for Nell that night because Nell asked her to, which Nell almost never did.

The booth smelled of wax and cheap incense and the lavender Lorca wore. She had Nell cut the deck three times and lay it down, and she turned the cards slowly, the way she did for paying customers, except her face was different for Nell. Honest.

"A man comes to a house," Lorca said, turning a card. "Not to live in it. To weigh it." She turned another. "There is a tower. There is always a tower. It means a thing standing high that will not stand much longer."

"That could be the trapeze rig," Nell said.

"It could." Lorca turned a third card and stopped. She looked at it a long while. "And here is the door. The buyer is at the door, child. He has already knocked. The question on these cards is not whether he comes. It is whether the people in the house know he is there."

"August knows," Nell said. "Doesn't he?"

Lorca gathered the cards. "August knows more than he says and less than he should," she said. "That is August's whole life in seven words. Watch his wagon. When a man like Pratt visits a man like August, it is not for conversation."


Chapter 7: Papers in the Wagon

She watched the wagon. It was easy enough; the show did not give a flier much to do between performances but rest and mend tights, and Nell had never been good at resting.

On the third evening Pratt walked up the steps of August's wagon and went in, and the door closed, and the lamp inside went yellow against the canvas window. Nell sat on a feed crate in the dark forty feet away and waited.

They talked for an hour. She could not hear words, only the shapes of them — Pratt's low even murmur, never rising, and August's voice, which rose and fell and sometimes stopped for too long. Once August laughed, the loud false laugh he used on creditors and bishops.

When the door opened, Pratt came down the steps with his hat in his hand and his book in his pocket and the smooth look of a man who has gotten what he came for. He did not see Nell in the dark. He walked off toward the town and the hotel where he slept, because men like Pratt did not sleep on the lot.

Through the open door, before August shut it, Nell saw the table. There were papers on it. A sheaf of them, the heavy cream-colored kind that lawyers used, with a red ribbon and a place at the bottom for a name. August stood over them with the pen in his hand and did not sign, and did not put the pen down either.

Then he saw her in the doorway light and his face did a thing she would remember. It tried to be the ringmaster's face, generous and unbothered, and it could not quite manage it.

"Nell," he said. "You'll catch cold."

"What are the papers, August?"

"Business," he said. "Only business. Go to bed."

He closed the door. The lamp stayed yellow a long time.


Chapter 8: Teardown in the Rain

It rained when they tore down, which was the worst kind of teardown.

The canvas came heavy with water, and the men cursed it and folded it and it weighed twice what it should, and the wagons that had to be loaded last were loaded in the dark by lantern light with the rain coming sideways. Dewey Hale stood in the middle of it like a man in a battle he had fought a thousand times, and his voice cut through the rain and kept the work moving.

Nell worked the rigging down herself, because she would let no one else strip her ropes. She came down soaked to the skin with her hands raw and helped roll the platform canvas, and somewhere in the dark Silas was loading the lions, who hated the rain and said so.

By two in the morning the wagons were lined on the road, and the company was wet through, and the cookhouse fire had been struck and there was no coffee. They rolled out into the dark anyway, because the next town expected them at noon and the route did not wait for rain.

Nell rode on the seat of the rigging wagon next to old Pernod, the driver, with a tarp over both their knees.

"Bad night," Pernod said.

"Bad night," she agreed.

"I've had worse." He flicked the reins. "In ought-three we lost the whole cookhouse to a flash flood and ate cold beans for a week. August stood up on a barrel and made a speech about adversity and we all felt grand about the beans." He laughed, then stopped laughing. "He could do that. Make you glad of the beans."

"He still can," Nell said.

Pernod was quiet a while. The horses' feet sucked at the mud. "Can he, though," he said, and it was not a question, and Nell did not answer it.


Chapter 9: The Brand on the Crate

In the new town, in gray dawn, Silas unloaded the menagerie supplies and found the mark.

It was burned into the side of a crate of harness leather — his harness leather, Hollis & Crane stock that had been on the show since before he came. A small clean brand the size of a coin, pressed into the wood with a hot iron. A capital A inside a circle.

He had seen that brand for four years. He had worn it, in a way, on every crate and wagon and feed sack of the show that had owned him before this one. Apex Amusement Syndicate. The A in the circle. They burned it into everything they touched the day they took ownership, so that even a man who could not read knew whose property he was loading.

Silas stood with his hand on the brand for a long moment. Then he went and found the other crates. Three of them carried the mark. Harness, tent stakes, a barrel of rope dressing. Not the wagons yet. Not the animals. But the small things, the consumable things, the things a syndicate paid for first to get its hooks into a show's daily blood.

He knew exactly what it meant, because he had helped do it, once, to other men's shows, in the years he did not talk about.

He went to find Nell, because Nell was the only one on the lot who worried about the right things.


Chapter 10: Loan

"It's a loan," August said. "That's all it is, Nell. A bridge over a thin spring."

He sat in his wagon with the tin box on the table and the ledger open, and he would not quite look at her, and Silas stood by the door with his hat in his hands and the branded crate-board under his arm where he'd pried it loose to bring as evidence.

"A loan from who?" Nell said.

"From a finance house. Amusement men. They lend to shows in the off months and we pay it back over the season. Half the shows on the road do it."

"Apex," Silas said.

August's hand stopped on the ledger. "You know the name."

"I worked for them. Four years." Silas set the board on the table, brand up, so August had to look at it. "This was on our harness this morning. They don't brand a loan, August. They brand what they own. When the A's on your crates, the A's coming for your wagons."

August looked at the brand a long time. Then he closed the ledger over it, gently, as if covering something that ought not be seen.

"It's a loan," he said again, quieter. "I signed for a loan against the season. If we have a good summer we pay it back and they go away and the brand means nothing." He finally looked up, and his eyes were wet and stubborn. "I had to feed this company through the winter. I had to. Annie would have—" He stopped on the name. "There was no other money. There is never any other money. So I borrowed it, and we'll pay it, and that is the end of it."

"How much?" Nell said.

"That's my affair."

"It's all of our affair. We're all standing under the same canvas." She leaned on the table. "How much, August?"

August opened his mouth, and closed it, and did not say a number, and the not-saying was its own number, and it was very large.


Chapter 11: The Broken Axle

The rear axle of the lion wagon broke on a stretch of corduroy road eleven miles short of the next town.

It went with a sound like a shot, and the wagon dropped on one corner into the ruts, and the lions roared and slammed their bodies against the bars, and for a long minute it was chaos in the dark before dawn, men running and horses shying and Silas at the wagon talking low and steady through the bars until the cats settled.

There was no spare axle. There was money for a spare axle somewhere in a catalogue, but not in the tin box. So Dewey Hale did what showmen had done since the first wagon: he sent two men back to a farm they'd passed, and they came back with a hardwood beam and a sack of tools and a farmer who wanted two dollars and a free pass, and they jacked the wagon with fence rails and lashed and bolted the beam under it, and it held.

It took four hours. The show reached the lot at midday instead of dawn, and the men set up at a run, and the matinee went up forty minutes late to a crowd that had nearly given up and gone home.

Nell flew that afternoon on three hours' sleep, and caught the bar a half-second late, and felt the miss in her shoulders for a week.

In the back lot, Pratt was there again, watching, in his clean gray suit, not a speck of the road's mud on him. He was always clean. The show was always filthy. Nell understood, watching him, that this was the whole difference between them, and that it was not an accident.


Chapter 12: The Animals Come First

She caught Pratt at the edge of the lot, between shows, when he was alone.

"You came to a lot of small towns for a man from the city," Nell said.

Pratt turned, unsurprised, as if he'd expected her. "I go where the work is. You're the flier. Sparrow." He said her name like he'd practiced it. "You're very good. You could fly anywhere."

"I fly here."

"For now." He looked out at the menagerie tent. "You've a fine old elephant. The handler's protective of her. That speaks well of him. Animals know who to trust."

"What do you want with this show, Mr. Pratt?"

"Want?" He seemed to consider whether to bother lying. He decided, she thought, on a kind of truth. "I represent men who own a great many shows. We don't run them ourselves — God, no. We hold the paper. We make sure the routes don't overlap, the dates don't clash, the right shows play the right towns. We bring order to a disorderly business." He smiled. "Mr. Crane came to us. We were glad to help. We're always glad to help a show with such a fine reputation. And such fine stock."

"And if he can't pay you back?"

"Then we'd take it very hard," Pratt said gently. "We'd hate to see this company broken up. But I'll tell you this, Miss Sparrow, because I think you care more than most." He turned to her, and for the first time his eyes were entirely honest, and that was the most frightening thing about him. "Whatever happens, the animals come first. We always find a good home for the animals. You can tell the handler that. The animals are always looked after."

He tipped his hat and walked back toward town, and Nell stood in the trampled grass and felt that she had just been threatened with great kindness.


Chapter 13: The Half-Signed Page

August went to the bank in town the next morning, which gave Nell an hour.

She did not like what she did. She had never gone into August's wagon uninvited in eleven years. But she climbed the steps while Dewey kept the lot busy, and she found the sheaf of cream-colored papers in the drawer under the tin box, tied with the red ribbon, and she read them at the little table with her heart going hard.

She was not a lawyer. The pages were thick with clauses she could not parse. But she found the things that mattered, because the things that mattered were always written plainly enough that a frightened man would understand exactly what he was signing.

The Apex Amusement Syndicate had advanced Hollis & Crane Combined Shows the sum of eleven thousand dollars. Against the loan, the show had pledged its wagons, its canvas, its rolling stock, its route contracts — and, on a separate schedule with its own heading, its livestock and menagerie animals, itemized by name and value. She found Juno on the list. Asian elephant, female, est. 47 yrs, $1,800.

At the bottom of the contract was the line for August's name. It was signed. She had thought it was not. She had seen him standing over it with the pen and not signing, and she had let herself believe he had not signed. But there it was, August Crane, in his own loops, dated three weeks before the season opened.

And below that, a second clause she read four times to be sure. In the event of default, Apex held the right to take possession of all pledged property and to dissolve the show, selling its parts as the Syndicate saw fit, no court order required.

No court order required. The men who wrote it had been careful. They had been careful for years, on shows like this one, all over the country.

Nell tied the ribbon back exactly as it had been and put the sheaf in the drawer and went out and stood in the sun and did not feel the sun at all.


Chapter 14: What the Clause Means

She found Silas in the menagerie, mucking Juno's stall, and she told him what she'd read, the elephant's name and the price beside it and the clause about no court order.

Silas listened without stopping his work, the fork moving steady, and when she finished he leaned the fork against the post and was quiet a while.

"That's the standard paper," he said finally. "I've seen it a hundred times. The animal schedule is the worst of it. You know why?"

"Tell me."

"Because the animals are the only thing on a dying show that's still worth real money." He scratched Juno's flank where she liked it. "Canvas rots. Wagons break. A route's only worth something if a show's running it. But a healthy elephant, a pair of lions, a string of ring stock — that's cash. A buyer doesn't even need to keep the show alive. He just takes the animals and sells them off to zoos and fairs and other shows, and the canvas can rot where it falls."

"Pratt said they always find good homes for the animals."

Silas laughed, short and without humor. "That's what they say. Sometimes it's even true. A fair will pay top dollar for an old gentle elephant that gives rides. Juno would fetch her eighteen hundred easy." He stopped. His hand stayed on the elephant. "She wouldn't last two years among strangers. She's old. She knows my voice and the cookhouse smell and the way the band plays. Take that away and an animal that age just — stops. I've seen it. They take her to a good clean fairground with good clean keepers and she stands in a new stall and stops eating and that's the end."

Juno's trunk came over the rope and found his collar. He let it.

"Then we don't default," Nell said.

"No," Silas said. "We don't."


Chapter 15: The Offer to Tom

Pratt was thorough. He did not only buy paper; he bought people, when people were for sale, because a show stripped of its best acts was easier to break.

He took Tom Bellamy to supper at the town hotel — a real supper, beef and wine, the first either had eaten off the lot in weeks — and Nell heard about it because Tom told her himself, which she gave him credit for.

"He didn't even hide it," Tom said. They sat on the wagon tongue in the evening cool. "The Chicago show, the hippodrome — it's an Apex show. Pratt's people own it. The scout that wrote me, the letter, all of it. It was him the whole time, working me."

"And you went to supper."

"I went to supper. A man can eat a steak and still say no." But he didn't sound like a man who had said no. He sounded like a man practicing.

"What did he offer?"

"A two-year contract. Winter work indoors. Forty a week and a flying partner of my choosing." He looked at her sidelong. "He still wants you. Both of us, the act intact. He says a known act is worth more than two new ones."

"And what did you say?"

Tom was quiet. Out past the wagons the lions were restless; she could hear Silas's low voice working them down.

"I said I'd think on it," Tom said. "That's all. I said I'd think." He stood, and brushed off his trousers, and would not look at her now. "Forty a week, Nell. I haven't been paid in three. You do the arithmetic, because I keep doing it, and it keeps coming out the same."


Chapter 16: No Wages

The Friday payday came and there were no wages.

August stood on the cookhouse steps and told them himself, which Nell respected, because a lesser man would have sent Dewey. He told them the gate had been thin through the rain and there was money owed that had to be paid first to keep the show on the road, and that he was sorry, and that there would be wages next week or he would know the reason why.

The performers took it quietly. They had eaten the show's food and slept in the show's wagons and most of them had no place else to be. But the roustabouts, the canvas men and the prop hands who worked for cash and freedom both, took it hard.

That night Nell heard them by the stake-and-chain wagon, a dozen of them, voices low and ugly.

"—two weeks now—"

"—heard the show's been sold out from under us—"

"—won't pull a stake I'm not paid to pull. Let the canvas lie."

Dewey Hale walked into the middle of them in the dark. Nell watched from the shadow of a wagon. He didn't shout. He stood among them, a big slow man, and let the silence sit.

"You're right," Dewey said at last. "Every man of you. You're owed. I'm owed too — three weeks, same as you, and I've been on this show twenty years and never missed a payday till this spring." He let that sit. "So here's the truth of it. August is in a hole. Some city outfit's got a rope around his neck. If we walk, the show dies tonight and you're owed money by a corpse. If we stay and pull a season, there's a chance — not a promise, a chance — you get paid and you keep a place to sleep. That's the whole offer. It's a poor one. But it's the only one I've got."

A long silence. Then a man spat and picked up his maul. "Where do you want the stakes," he said, and that was the end of the mutiny, for now.


Chapter 17: Two Years

Dewey found Nell later by the rigging wagon and sat down heavily on the tongue and rolled a cigarette he could not afford the tobacco for.

"You went in his wagon," he said. It was not an accusation.

"I read the contract."

"Then you know more than you did." He lit the cigarette and the match showed his face, lined and tired. "You want to know how long it's been? Because I'll tell you, and August never will."

"Tell me."

"Two years he's been selling this show piece by piece. Not to Apex, not at first. Little debts. He sold the parade wagon — the big gold tableau wagon, you remember it — sold it to a museum man in St. Louis and told us it broke an axle and we left it for scrap. He didn't. He sold it." Dewey drew on the cigarette. "Sold four of the band's instruments. Half the ring stock. The good canvas, the new canvas, sold and replaced with the patched stuff we run now. Every winter a little more, to keep us eating, to keep the route, to keep the name on the wagons. And every spring a little less to sell." He looked at her. "This year there was nothing left to sell but the future. So he sold that. To Apex."

Nell watched the cigarette burn. "Why didn't he tell us?"

"Because we love him," Dewey said simply. "And a man can't bear to be loved by people he thinks he's failed. So he lies, and smiles, and gives his speech about adversity, and feels worse." He stood and ground the cigarette under his boot. "He's a good man, Nell. That's the hell of it. A good man drowning, and grabbing at us to stay afloat."


Chapter 18: The County Seat

Nell and Silas drove to the county seat on a borrowed buggy on a dark Monday between towns, because Monday was the only day the show did not move or play.

It was thirty miles. They did not talk much. Silas was not a talking man and Nell had learned not to mind it; there was a kind of company in his silence that asked nothing. He drove. She watched the road.

"Why do you stay?" she asked, somewhere past the halfway mark, surprising herself.

Silas was quiet so long she thought he wouldn't answer. "For the elephant," he said. Then, after another mile: "And because it's the only place I ever worked where the men were straight with each other. Apex shows — every man watching every other man. Spies. Reports filed on the handlers. They'd dock your pay for a sick animal like it was your fault the thing took cold." He flicked the reins. "Hollis & Crane's poor. But August never once asked me to lie about an animal, or sell one I said wasn't fit, or work a beast that was hurting. That's worth more than wages, some weeks."

"Some weeks it isn't," Nell said.

"No," Silas agreed. "Some weeks it isn't."

At the county seat they found the courthouse and the recorder's office, a dim room with a clerk who blinked at them and a wall of leather books. Nell asked, in her best off-the-lot voice, to see the filings against Hollis & Crane Combined Shows. The clerk found the volume. He laid it open on the counter and turned it toward them and pointed with an inky finger, and there it was, recorded for any man to read.


Chapter 19: The Lien

The lien was filed in full, with all its schedules, because Apex was careful and Apex always recorded.

Nell read it slowly. Silas read over her shoulder, and where the language went thick he knew it from his Apex years and translated it low.

"There," he said, tapping a clause. "That's not a loan clause. That's a dissolution clause. See — 'upon default the Syndicate may at its sole discretion wind up the business of the borrower and liquidate the pledged assets.' Wind up. That means kill the show and sell the bones. No judge, no nothing. They just send a man with the paper and a sheriff to keep the peace, and they take it."

"And the animals."

"Schedule C." He turned the page. There was Juno again, and the lions, and the camels, and the ring stock, each with a number beside it. "They've already got a value on every head. They knew the figures before August signed. This wasn't a loan to a man having a thin spring. This was a long bet on a man going under." He straightened. "Apex doesn't lend to shows it thinks will pay. It lends to shows it wants to own. The loan is just the legal way through the door."

Nell copied the numbers into the little book she carried for rigging notes. The total of the loan. The dates. The interest rate, which she copied without understanding, eighteen percent, compounding, which sounded high but she had no measure for it.

"What does a fair pay for an elephant?" she asked the clerk, on a guess, because clerks knew strange things.

The clerk considered. "There was a fair bought one over to Hartwell two summers back," he said. "Paid two thousand and felt cheated when it died in the fall. Why?"

"No reason," Nell said, and closed her book.


Chapter 20: Confession

They got back to the lot near midnight, and the lot was dark, and there was a lamp burning in August's wagon, and August was drunk.

He was not a drinking man, which made it worse. He sat at his little table with a bottle and the tin box and the ledger, and when Nell knocked and came in he looked up at her with his eyes swimming and did not pretend.

"You went to the courthouse," he said. "Don't tell me you didn't. I can see it on you. You've got the look of someone who's read the paper." He laughed, the loud false laugh, and it cracked in the middle. "Now you know. Now you all know. The great August Crane sold his own circus for eleven thousand dollars and a winter's hay."

"August—"

"You know what I thought?" He poured and spilled and drank. "I thought, one good summer. That's all. One good summer and a fair wind and we pay the man back and he goes away and I tear up the paper and nobody ever knows how close I came." He set the glass down hard. "But there's no good summer coming, is there. There's rain and broke axles and two-hundred houses and a man in a gray suit at every gate counting my benches. He's not waiting for me to fail, Nell. He's helping me along."

"How much have you paid back?"

"Nothing." The word came out of him like a tooth. "I can't even pay the interest. It just — grows. Eighteen of a hundred, on the whole of it, every year, on top of itself. It grows faster than I can earn it. I did the figures last night, sober, and then I bought this bottle." He put his face in his hands. "Annie kept the books. Annie would have seen it coming a mile off and slapped the pen out of my hand. But Annie's three years dead and I kept the books myself and I have ruined us. I have ruined all of you."


Chapter 21: The Brutal Arithmetic

In the morning, sober and ashamed, August showed Nell the figures, because Nell asked and because he had no fight left to refuse her.

They sat with the ledger and a stub of pencil. Nell was no scholar but she had counted gates by eye for eleven years and she could do sums. They worked it together.

The loan was eleven thousand. The interest, compounding, had already added near two thousand more in the time since he'd signed. The whole of it, principal and interest, came to just under thirteen thousand dollars, and grew by better than a hundred and fifty a month, and would grow faster as it fed on itself.

Against that, what could the show earn? They worked the gate. A good house was four hundred souls at a quarter and the children half — call it ninety dollars a show, two shows a day, six days a week. Five hundred a week, in a good week. Out of that came feed and fuel and railroad and the farmer with the axle beam and the butcher's meat and the canvas dressing and a hundred small bleedings, and wages last of all, when there were wages. In a good week the show cleared perhaps eighty dollars above its costs. In a bad week, in the rain, it cleared nothing or less.

"Eighty a week," Nell said, "to pay down thirteen thousand that grows a hundred and fifty a month."

"It can't be done," August said quietly. "That's the arithmetic. I've done it forty ways. The debt grows faster than we can ever pay it. He didn't lend me money, Nell. He sold me a slow rope and let me put my own neck in it."

Nell looked at the figures a long time. They did not change for being looked at.

"Then we don't pay it the slow way," she said. "We pay the whole of it, at once, before it grows. We buy back our own paper."

August laughed without hope. "With what? We haven't thirteen hundred, let alone thirteen thousand."

"Not yet," Nell said. "But the season's young."


Chapter 22: The Rival Show

Apex did not wait for the season to be young. Apex applied pressure.

The next town they played, a railroad town with money in it and a fair-sized crowd to be had, woke to find handbills nailed to every post and fence: a rival circus, bigger, with elephants in the plural and a hundred horses and a famous clown, playing the same dates, half a mile across town on the fairground.

"It's an Apex show," Silas said, when Nell brought him a handbill. He didn't even need to read the name. "That's their oldest trick. You owe them and you won't break easy, so they send another show they own to play right on top of you and bleed your gate dry. A town this size can't fill two circuses. They'll take the crowd, and we'll play to empty benches, and August can't pay even the interest, and the default comes that much sooner." He folded the handbill. "It costs them money to do it. But they've got money. That's the whole game. They've got money and time and we've got neither."

The two shows opened the same afternoon. From the top of her rigging Nell could see, across the rooftops, the bigger tent on the fairground, the longer line at its ticket wagon, the crowd flowing toward it like water finding the low place.

Her own tent, when she looked down, was a third full.

She climbed down and went to find August, and found him already on the cookhouse steps, watching the same thing she'd watched, the gift gone out of his face entirely now.

"They'll empty us out by Saturday," he said.

"Not if we give them a reason to come here instead," Nell said.

"And what reason is that?"

"Me," she said. "Without a net."


Chapter 23: Without a Net

August forbade it. Dewey forbade it. Silas said nothing, which from Silas was the loudest forbidding of all.

Nell did it anyway, because it was the one thing the bigger show could not match. They had two elephants and a famous clown. They did not have a girl who would fly forty feet up with no net under her, and the whole town would know it by noon, because Nell saw to it that the whole town knew it by noon.

She and Tom worked it out together, grim and careful, the way you work out a thing that can kill you. The net came down. The crowd that came — and they came, a real crowd now, the morbid and the daring and the merely curious, drawn by the word that had gone round the town like fire — the crowd that came saw bare sawdust under the trapeze, and they went quiet when she climbed, and the quiet was its own kind of money.

She flew clean. Of course she flew clean; she always flew clean; the net was a courtesy to the body, not the skill. But the crowd did not know that, and they held their breath when she left the bar, and they roared when Tom caught her wrists, and they came back the next day and brought their cousins.

The gate doubled. Then it tripled. The rival show across town, for all its elephants, had nothing the papers would print, and the papers printed Nell. THE GIRL WHO FLIES WITHOUT A NET. Hollis & Crane filled to the eaves three nights running.

In the back lot, counting the swollen take, August looked at her with something between gratitude and grief.

"You shouldn't have," he said.

"It worked."

"It worked," he agreed. "That's what frightens me. Because now you'll do it again. And the body only ever wins that bet so many times before the house collects."


Chapter 24: The House Collects

It worked too well, in the end, because it told Pratt exactly what the show was worth fighting for.

He came to the lot the morning after the third sold-out night, clean as ever, and asked to see August, and they talked in the wagon, and when he came out he found Nell waiting for him by the buggy he'd hired.

"That was quite a thing you did," Pratt said. "Flying without a net. The whole county's talking. I confess I came myself last night, with the crowd. You're worth more than this show, Miss Sparrow. I've said so before."

"We had three good nights," Nell said. "We'll have more."

"I'm sure you will." He drew on his gloves, finger by finger. "I'll be honest with you, because I've come to admire you, and I admire very few. Those three nights changed my arithmetic, not yours. I had thought this show would simply fall, like the others. Now I see it has fight in it. A flier who'll risk her neck. A handler who won't sell his elephant. A ringmaster the towns still love." He smiled. "Fight raises a property's value. So I've adjusted the note. The rate goes up. Twenty-two from eighteen, as the original paper allows on default of the interest payments — and Mr. Crane has, of course, defaulted on every interest payment since he signed." He climbed into the buggy. "You flew beautifully. You also just told me precisely how hard to squeeze. Good day, Miss Sparrow."

He drove off, clean and unhurried, and Nell stood in the mud and understood that she had won three nights and lost ground, both at once, and that this was how Apex won — by making your strength cost you.


Chapter 25: The Flood

The rain came back in the last week of June and did not stop.

It rained for three days while the show sat on a low river-bottom lot that had been dry when they pulled in. On the second night the river came up over its banks, slow and brown, and crept across the field, and the company woke in the dark to water under the wagons.

It was not a flash flood, not a killing wall of water; it was worse in its way, a steady patient rising that gave them time to be afraid. The wagons stood to their hubs in muddy water by dawn. The cookhouse fire was out and would not light. The big top, half struck, lay sodden and heavy as lead, impossible to move.

Dewey ran it like the old soldier he was. The animals first — the horses led to high ground at the field's edge, the lions' wagon hauled by the team to the road, the camels complaining every step. Then the people, then the gear, then whatever could be saved of the rest.

But the rigging wagon, the heaviest on the show, had sunk axle-deep in the soft bottom and the horse team could not budge it, and it held Nell's trapeze and the platform and the king poles, the heart of her act, and if it stayed it would be lost to the rot and the rust and the river.

They put four horses to it and the horses screamed and slipped and could not move it.

"Leave it," Dewey shouted over the rain. "It's gone, Nell. We can't—"

"Get Juno," Silas said.


Chapter 26: The Elephant Hauls

Silas brought the old elephant down off the high ground himself, on no chain at all, just his voice, leading her into the rising water that she did not want to enter and entered anyway because he asked her to.

He set her to the rigging wagon. He rigged the harness himself, fast, in the rain, the old harness that still bore the Apex brand on its crate, and he ran the lines and he put his hand on Juno's vast wet shoulder and he spoke to her low, words Nell could not hear over the river, an old patter, steady and certain.

Juno leaned into the harness.

For a long terrible moment nothing moved but the water. The elephant strained, her feet sinking, her breath going out of her in great gusts, and the wagon sat in the sucking mud and held. Silas did not shout. He kept talking, the same low steady stream, and he leaned his own weight against her shoulder as if his small weight could matter, and maybe it did, maybe it only mattered that she was not alone in the pulling.

The wagon came free with a sound like the earth letting go, and once it moved it kept moving, and Juno walked it up out of the bottom to the road through water to her knees, and the company stood in the rain and did not cheer because they hadn't the breath, but they watched as if they were watching something holy.

When it was up and safe Silas unharnessed her and ran his hands down her legs, and stopped at the left foreleg, and felt it, and his face changed.

"She's strained it," he said. "Pulled something in the leg." He looked up at Nell through the rain. "She's old, Nell. An old elephant doesn't heal like a young one. She saved your rig. It may have cost her the leg."


Chapter 27: Fire in the Cookhouse

A week later, on dry ground at last, the cookhouse burned.

It was nobody's fault, or everybody's — a tired cook, a banked fire, a grease tin too near the canvas, a wind that came up in the night. The cook tent went up in minutes, the way oiled canvas does, a roar and a tower of flame against the dark, and the men ran with buckets from the horse trough and beat at it with wet sacks and saved the wagons around it but not the tent and not, worse, the week's stores inside.

The flour, the coffee, the salt pork, the sacked beans, the sugar — a week's eating for sixty people and an elephant and a string of horses, gone to ash and ruined by smoke and water.

In the gray morning the company stood around the black ring where the cookhouse had been, and the smell of it was in everything, and nobody said much.

"We can't feed sixty people on nothing," Dewey said to August and Nell, low. "And we can't buy stores. The accounts are closed in every town Apex has touched, and Apex has touched all of them on this route. We've cash for maybe four days' food if we pay coin for everything and the merchants will sell to us at all."

August said nothing. He had aged a year in a month.

It was Madame Lorca, of all of them, who broke the silence. She had appeared at the edge of the burned ring in her shawl, and she held in her hands a cloth bag that clinked.

"I have taken fortunes from fools for thirty years," she said, "and I have not spent it on rum like a sensible person. Buy the food." She put the bag in Dewey's hands. "We will eat. We will eat, and we will think, and thinking is better done fed."


Chapter 28: A Contract of Her Own

Pratt's next move was the cleverest and the cruelest, and it came for Nell directly.

He found her alone — he was good at finding people alone — by the menagerie tent in the dusk, and he did not waste her time with elephants this time.

"I'll be plain," he said. "I've a contract here with your name on it. Not the act. You. The Apex hippodrome in Chicago wants the girl who flies without a net. Sixty a week. A two-year term. Indoor winters, your own dressing room, your face on the bills." He held the folded paper but did not offer it yet. "And here is the part you'll want to hear, and the part you'll hate me for. If you sign, the others follow. Bellamy's half gone already. Once the star leaves a sinking show, the rest stop pretending and save themselves. You'd be doing them a kindness, Miss Sparrow. You'd be opening the door."

"And the show?"

"The show dies either way," Pratt said, almost gently. "You know the arithmetic; I'm told you've done it yourself. The only question is whether the people on it scatter to poorhouses or come with me to good work. You can be the reason they land soft. All it costs is your loyalty to a dead thing."

Nell looked at the paper. Sixty a week. Her own dressing room. Her face on the bills. Eleven years of mud and cold dawns and unpaid Fridays, and here was the door out, held open by a clean hand.

"Keep your paper, Mr. Pratt," she said.

"You're certain."

"I'm certain."

He put it away without anger. "You're a romantic," he said. "It's a luxury. I hope you can afford it." He tipped his hat. "The offer stands a week. After that the price comes down, as all prices do."


Chapter 29: Tom Signs

Tom signed.

He came to tell her himself, before she heard it elsewhere, and she gave him that, the way she'd given it to him before. They stood by the dark trapeze wagon in the place they always stood.

"I took it," he said. "The Chicago contract. I signed this morning."

Nell had known it was coming and it still landed like a missed catch. "Tom."

"Don't." He held up a hand. "Don't make me say the speech I've been making to myself all week. I'm thirty-four, Nell. A flier's got maybe ten good years and I've used eight of them. I haven't been paid since May. I've got a mother in Dayton I send money to and I haven't sent any since spring." His voice cracked and steadied. "Forty a week. Indoor winters so my shoulders don't seize. A roof. I'd be a fool not to, and I've been a fool about plenty, but not about this."

"Who catches me?" Nell said. It came out smaller than she meant.

Tom flinched. "I've thought about that. I've thought about nothing else." He looked at the ground. "Bring up young Pernod's boy. He's got the hands for it, with training. Or — God, Nell — come with me. The offer's still both of us. Come fly where they pay you and there's a net and a roof and you're not killing yourself to save a man who already drowned."

"I can't," she said.

"I know you can't," Tom said. "That's why I have to go without you. Because if I waited for you to leave I'd be waiting under this canvas the day they tore it down." He put his hand on her shoulder, briefly, the catcher's grip she'd trusted with her life ten thousand times. "I'm sorry. You're the best I ever flew with. Bar none."

He left at the end of the week. Nell did not go to see him off. She climbed her rig instead and worked alone, releases into empty air with no one to catch her, until her arms gave out.


Chapter 30: A Town That Remembers

Then, in the worst of it, the road gave them one kindness.

They came to a small town called Marah on the Apex route, and August grew quiet driving in, and then he said, "Annie was from here. We courted here. We played here every August for fifteen years before — before things." He looked at the familiar steeple. "They knew her here."

They knew him too. The lot was the same lot they'd always used, and the man who owned it would take no rent. The merchants opened the closed accounts on a handshake, Apex or no Apex, because August Crane had brought the circus to Marah every summer of their childhoods and they were not about to take the syndicate's side against him now.

And the town came. Not a thin house, not a third-full tent — they came in their Sunday clothes on a Tuesday, fathers who'd been brought as boys now bringing boys of their own, and the benches filled and the overflow stood at the back, and August gave his spiel in his thin red coat and the gift came back into his face for the first time in weeks, because here, for one night, he was not a drowning man. He was the ringmaster of Marah's whole remembered childhood.

Nell flew with the net up — she would not risk the no-net stunt twice in a month — and even with the net the crowd of Marah roared for her as if she were their own, because in a way she was; they'd watched her grow up under this canvas.

The gate that night was the best of the season. Dewey counted it twice to be sure.

"It won't pay the note," he told Nell quietly. "Not a tenth of it. But it'll feed us a month, and it reminds a man why he bothers." He looked toward the tent where August was still shaking hands with farmers. "Look at him. That's the man I signed on with, twenty years gone. I'd forgot he was still in there."


Chapter 31: The Collapse

He collapsed on the second night, in the middle of the spiel.

He was three sentences in, the part about the ordinary barred at the door, and he stopped, and put his hand to his chest, and the gift went out of his face and something gray came into it, and he folded down into the sawdust in the center of the ring while a thousand people of Marah watched and did not at first understand it was not part of the show.

Nell was at the performers' entrance. She was over the ring fence and across the ring before she knew she'd moved. Silas was there a breath behind her. They got him up off the sawdust and out the back while the band, God bless them, struck up the screamer that meant trouble and sent the clowns tumbling in to hold the crowd.

The town doctor was in the third row and came at a run. He put his ear to August's chest in the dressing tent and listened a long time.

"His heart," the doctor said. "It's failing. Not all at once — it's been failing a while, I'd guess, and tonight it stumbled." He sat back. "He needs rest. Real rest, weeks of it, no excitement, no work. A bed that doesn't move." He looked around at the patched canvas, the road kit, the worn faces. "Which I don't suppose this is the place for."

"It's the only place he's got," Nell said.

August's eyes were open. His voice was a thread. "The show," he said. "Tomorrow's matinee—"

"Will go up," Nell said, taking his hand. "It'll go up fine. You rest."

"Who'll—" He couldn't finish it.

"I will," she said, surprising herself, surprising everyone. "I'll take the ring."


Chapter 32: Nell Takes the Ring

She had never wanted the ring. She was a flier; her place was forty feet up where the words didn't matter. But the next afternoon she put on August's spare red coat, taken in with pins by Madame Lorca through the night, and she walked into the center of the ring with a thousand strangers looking at her, and she opened her mouth and nothing came.

The silence stretched. She felt the crowd begin to shift.

Then she found, not August's words — she could never have August's words — but her own.

"My name is Nell Sparrow," she said, and her trained flier's pitch carried it to the back of the tent. "I have lived under this canvas eleven years. The man who usually stands here is resting, on his doctor's strict orders, and he sends his regrets and his promise that the show will be every bit as fine without him, which I think is a lie, but a kind one." A ripple of laughter; the crowd leaned in. "What I can promise you is this. Everyone you will see tonight has chosen to be here, in the rain and the mud, for love of the work and very little else. You'll not see a more honest two hours anywhere in this state. Ladies and gentlemen — the Hollis and Crane Combined Shows."

The band hit it. The horses came in. And she found she could do it — could read a crowd from the ground the way she read it from the bar, could feel when to hurry and when to let a moment breathe, could hold a thousand people in the curve of her voice.

When she came out the back, sweating, the coat heavy on her shoulders, Silas was standing there. He did not say much. He never did.

"You held them," he said. "Every one. August couldn't have done it better with that heart of his tonight." He paused, which from Silas was a paragraph. "He chose right, handing it to you."


Chapter 33: What Silas Knows

That night, late, when the lot was quiet, Silas told her the rest of it. He had been deciding for weeks whether to, and August's collapse decided him.

They sat by Juno's stall. The elephant favored her strained leg but she was eating, which Silas took as the only good news of the month.

"I told you I worked for Apex four years," Silas said. "I didn't tell you what I did." He looked at his hands. "I was a fixer. They'd buy the paper on a show, the way they've done to us, and when they wanted it broke they'd send a man to grease the wheel. I was the man for the animal end. I'd advise them which beasts were worth taking and which to leave. I'd find the buyers — the fairs, the zoos, the dealers. I'd handle the loading the day the sheriff came." His jaw worked. "I broke up six shows for them before I quit. Six. Old men's whole lives, loaded onto Apex wagons in an afternoon while they stood and watched. The animals didn't understand. That was the part I couldn't — the animals would look for their own handlers in the strange wagons and the handlers would be standing there ruined, and the animals would call for them, and we'd drive away."

Nell said nothing. There was nothing to say to it.

"So I know how they do it," Silas said. "I know every move on the board because I used to make them. The rival show. Buying off the star. Closing the accounts. Squeezing the rate. Pratt's running the same play he taught me." He looked up. "Which means I know where it's weak. There's one place these men are weak, Nell. They're greedy and they're careful, and a careful greedy man always, always takes a little more than the law allows. He can't help it. I've seen it every time. Find the place they cheated, and you've got a rope of your own."


Chapter 34: The Plan

"The interest," Nell said slowly. "Pratt raised it to twenty-two. He said the paper allowed it on default."

"Did you read the paper say it?"

"I read it once. I'm no lawyer."

"Neither am I. But I've read fifty of those contracts." Silas leaned forward. "Apex always writes a ceiling into the rate, because a court'll throw out a loan that charges too much — it's called usury, there's a law against it in most states, a hard limit on what a lender can take. They write the contract right up to the line and not over, on paper. And then they cheat. They charge over the line in fact, and dare the showman to sue, because the showman's broke and scared and doesn't know the law and there's a clause says no court needed anyway." He spread his hands. "But the showman could sue. If the rate they're actually charging is over the legal line, the whole interest could be void. Not the loan — they'd still get their eleven thousand back. But all the interest, the two thousand that's grown, the compounding, the twenty-two percent — gone. Thrown out. And then the number we have to find isn't thirteen thousand and growing. It's eleven thousand, flat, and shrinking as we pay."

Nell felt something she had not felt in weeks. "Can we prove the rate's over the line?"

"We'd need the law and the figures both. The law's at the courthouse — every state sets the line and records it. The figures—" He stopped. "The figures we'd need are what they've actually charged, month by month. And August's books are a drunk man's mess." He frowned. "But Annie kept books before him. Real books. If she ever recorded the early payments, the early rate—"

"There's a reporter following the show," Nell said. "From the Marah paper. He smelled the August story. What if we gave him a bigger one?"


Chapter 35: The Reporter

The reporter was a young man named Coyle from the county paper, and he had come sniffing after the human-interest story of the collapsed ringmaster and the girl who'd taken the ring. Nell gave him a better one.

She met him in the cookhouse over the coffee Madame Lorca's money had bought, and she told him, plainly, what Apex was: a syndicate buying up the small shows of the country by the slow strangle of debt, sending rival shows to bleed the gates, closing the accounts, squeezing rates past the legal line. She named no figures she couldn't prove yet. But she gave him the shape of it, the bigness of it, the dozens of dead shows behind this one.

Coyle wrote fast. "This is bigger than a county paper," he said, eyes bright. "If you've got proof — real proof, figures, the contract — I can get this to Chicago, to the city papers. People love a circus. They'll hate a syndicate that strangles one." He looked up. "Can you get me proof?"

"I'm working on it," Nell said.

She walked him to the edge of the lot at dusk. Two men were waiting by his buggy. They were not from the town; their clothes were too good and their boots too clean, and Nell knew clean boots now and what they meant.

"Mr. Coyle," one of them said pleasantly. "Long drive back to Marah in the dark. We'll see you safe to the county line." It was not an offer.

Coyle looked at Nell, frightened. There was nothing she could do; there were two of them and they were big and the law in this town was one old constable asleep at his stove. Coyle got in his buggy. The two men got their horses and rode out on either side of him into the dark, and that was the last Nell saw of the reporter, who wrote nothing, and whose paper, when she sent to it weeks later, said he had left their employ quite suddenly and gone east.


Chapter 36: The Note Called

Pratt called the whole note due in the first week of July.

The letter came by a paid messenger, formal and cold, on Apex stationery with the A in the circle at the top. The Syndicate, citing repeated default on interest, demanded payment in full of the principal and all accrued interest, the sum of thirteen thousand four hundred and twelve dollars, within thirty days of the date above. Failing payment, the Syndicate would exercise its right under the agreement to wind up the business and liquidate the pledged assets, the animals among them, without recourse to the courts.

Thirty days.

August read it from his sickbed in the wagon, propped on pillows, his face gray, and his hand shook holding it, and Nell took it from him and read it herself and felt the thirty days close around her throat.

"Thirteen thousand in thirty days," August whispered. "We cleared eighty dollars our best week before Marah. We can't—"

"Rest," Nell said.

"Nell, there's no—"

"Rest." She folded the letter. "You did the arithmetic the slow way and it crushed you. Let me do it a different way. Thirty days isn't time enough to earn it. But it might be time enough to break it."

She went out and called the company together — all of them, performers and roustabouts and the cook and Pernod and Madame Lorca and Silas — by the dead ring of the burned cookhouse, in the long July dusk, and she held up the letter so they all could see the A in the circle.

"They mean to take the show in thirty days," she said. "I'm going to ask you to do something foolish to stop them. But first I want every one of you to know exactly what we're standing in."


Chapter 37: The Benefit Week

What she asked was this: a benefit week. The whole company, every act, performing for nothing — no wages, not a cent — and every dollar of every gate, undivided, into the pot against the note. And not just the gate. She asked them to spread the word themselves, in every town and on every road, that this was the show fighting the syndicate, the small circus standing up to the combine, and to make the people of these small towns understand that buying a ticket was taking a side.

"It won't be enough," she told them honestly. "I've done the figures with Silas. Even a great week, even sold out every house, won't raise thirteen thousand. Not close. But it does two things. It buys us toward the number. And it buys us time and noise and a crowd at our backs, while Silas and I go after the other half — the part that doesn't take money. The part that takes proof." She looked around at them. "I won't lie to you and say it'll work. I'll only say it's a chance, and it's ours, and the other road is to scatter and watch them load Juno onto an Apex wagon in an afternoon."

She let that sit, the way Dewey had taught her without teaching her.

"You don't owe August your wages," she said. "You don't owe me anything. Anyone who wants to walk, walk now, with no shame and a handshake from me. But anyone who stays — we stay together, and we work for nothing for one week, and we find out what a small show can do when it stops being ashamed and gets angry instead."


Chapter 38: The Vote

They voted by lantern light, the old way, a show of hands.

Dewey raised his first, of course; he'd raised it before she finished asking. Madame Lorca raised hers, and the band raised theirs, and Pernod, and the cook who'd let the fire bank too low and had been working off the shame ever since.

The roustabouts were the question. They worked for cash and freedom, owed nothing to August's dream, and they'd nearly mutinied over a single missed payday. Nell watched the big canvasman who'd spat in the dark weeks ago, the one who'd said let the canvas lie.

He scratched his jaw. He looked at the dead ring of the cookhouse. He looked at the menagerie tent where Silas stood with the old elephant.

"A week for nothing," he said. "Then what?"

"Then if we've saved the show, you're paid back wages out of what's left, you have my word and August's," Nell said. "And if we haven't, you've lost a week you'd have lost anyway, because there'd be no show to pay you."

The canvasman thought about it. "I pulled stakes for forty shows in my life," he said. "Never once stood up to the money men. Always just moved on to the next lot." He raised his hand, slow, like it was heavy. "Reckon I'd like to see what it's like to stand up the once."

The other hands went up after his, one by one. Not all of them. Three men shook Nell's hand and walked, and she meant the handshake. But the rest stayed, and the count, when Dewey made it, was the whole show but three.

"Then it's settled," Nell said. "We open the benefit Monday. And God help Mr. Pratt, because the small towns of this county are about to find out they've got a side."


Chapter 39: Juno's Sickness

The benefit's first weight fell, as so much did, on Silas and the elephant.

Juno's strained leg had healed slow but it had healed; she could walk, could even do her gentle turn in the ring if Silas kept the act short. But in the second week of July she went off her feed, and then she went down in the night, lying on her side in the stall, which an old elephant must not do for long or her own weight will press the life out of her lungs.

Silas did not sleep for three nights. He got her up with ropes and the help of six men and kept her up, and he dosed her with what the horse-doctor in the town would sell him, and he sat with his hand on her trunk through the dark hours and talked to her, the low endless patter, and would not leave even to eat.

"If she dies now," he told Nell on the third gray dawn, hollow-eyed, "Apex doesn't even have to take her. The note comes due and the show's worth thirteen hundred less and there's one more reason to scatter." He pressed his forehead to the elephant's vast cheek. "But that's not why. You understand it's not why." He didn't finish; he didn't have to. He would have sat with her the same if she'd been worth nothing to anyone but him.

On the fourth day she ate.

She took an apple from his hand, and then a second, and then the grain, and Silas sat down in the dirty straw and put his face in his hands and his shoulders shook, once, and then he got up and went and washed his face in the horse trough and came back a handler again, steady, as if it had not happened.

"She'll do," was all he said. "She'll do now."


Chapter 40: Lorca Empties Her Purse

Madame Lorca came to Nell on the benefit's third night with the cloth bag again, and this time it held more than coin.

"I gave the food money freely," she said, "and I'd give it again. But there is more." She opened the bag on the cookhouse table. There were rings, a brooch, a heavy old necklace of garnets, a pair of earrings with small good stones. "Thirty years on the road. A woman alone learns to carry her wealth where the thieves can't bank it. These are real. The garnets came from a count in Bucharest who could not pay his fortune any other way." She pushed them across the table. "Sell them. Put them in the pot."

"Lorca, I can't take your—"

"You can and you will." The old woman's voice was iron. "I am sixty-three years old. I have no children but this show. What do I want with a count's garnets when I am dead in a poorhouse because I was too proud to sell them?" She closed Nell's hand around the necklace. "I read your cards in the spring. The tower falling, the buyer at the door. I will not sit in my booth and watch it come true while I hoard a dead count's stones. Sell them. And if anyone asks where the money came from, tell them an old fraud finally did something honest with her takings."

Nell sold the jewelry in the next town to a jeweler who knew good stones, and got a fair price because the jeweler had a daughter who'd cried at the girl who flew without a net, and the money went into the pot, and the pot grew, and it was still not enough, and everyone knew it was still not enough, and they kept feeding it anyway.


Chapter 41: Tom Comes Back

Tom Bellamy came back on the benefit's fifth day.

He came on the afternoon train and walked onto the lot in his city clothes and stood at the edge of it, and the company stared, and Nell came out of the menagerie tent and stopped.

"I read about it," Tom said. "It's in the Chicago papers — not the syndicate part, they've kept that quiet, but the benefit. The show that's fighting to buy back its own life. The girl who took the ring." He looked at the dead cookhouse, the patched canvas, the thin worn faces of people he'd left. "I read it in my own dressing room at the hippodrome. My own dressing room, Nell. The one Pratt promised me. And I sat there and I felt like the lowest thing that ever drew breath."

"You took a fair deal," Nell said. "Nobody blames you."

"I blame me." He held out an envelope. "It's what they paid me. The signing money, and five weeks' wages. Two hundred and eighteen dollars. It's the pot, or it's nothing — I don't want it, it burns my hand." He pressed it on her. "And I'll fly the benefit. For nothing, like the rest of you. I broke my contract to come — Pratt'll never hire me again and I don't care. Let me catch you one more week. Let me be on the right side of the thing the once."

Nell took the envelope. Then she did something she rarely did and put her arms around him, briefly, the way you embrace a partner who has caught you ten thousand times and missed only once.

"Welcome back," she said. "We open at two. Don't be late, you've gone soft in your city dressing room."


Chapter 42: The Record Crowd

The word had done its work. On the benefit's last Saturday, the crowd that came was the largest Nell had ever seen under that canvas.

They came from three counties. They came because the Chicago papers had told the story and the local papers had told it louder, the small show fighting the combine, the company working for nothing to buy back its own future. They came to take a side, the way Nell had hoped they would, and they paid their quarters and their halves and some of them paid a dollar and would take no change, and the benches filled and the standing room filled and Dewey turned people away at the door for the first time in a decade.

Nell flew with Tom catching, the act whole again for one afternoon, and the crowd that had read about her roared until the canvas shook. August watched from a chair at the performers' entrance, propped and pale but smiling, his hand in Nell's hand between her flights.

They counted the whole pot that night — the benefit gate, Lorca's garnets, Tom's two hundred and eighteen, the coin pressed into hands all week by farmers who couldn't spare it.

Dewey counted it twice. Then he set down the pencil.

"Six thousand, two hundred and forty dollars," he said. "It's the most money this show's seen in one place in ten years." He looked up, and the triumph went out of his face. "And the note's thirteen four. We're not halfway. Not halfway, Nell, with three days left on the thirty."

The triumph went out of the cookhouse with it. They had done the impossible and it was not enough.

"Then the money was never going to win it," Nell said. "Silas was right. We don't out-earn them. We out-prove them. Three days. We've got three days to find Annie's books."


Chapter 43: Pratt at the Gate

He did not even wait the three days. On the thirtieth morning Pratt arrived at the lot at dawn with two wagons, a clerk, and a county sheriff he'd brought from somewhere to keep the peace, and he came to take the menagerie.

The company woke to it and gathered. Pratt stood by his wagons with the contract in his hand and the sheriff beside him looking like a man who wished he were elsewhere.

"Thirty days are up at noon," Pratt said, "but I see no point in waiting for the formality. The Syndicate is exercising its rights. We'll begin with the livestock — they travel slowest." He nodded to his clerk. "The elephant first. Load the elephant."

Silas walked out of the menagerie tent and stood in the open gate of it, between Pratt's men and Juno, and he did not raise his voice.

"It's not noon," Silas said. "And you'll not load her before noon, and you'll not load her at noon either, because the note's in dispute."

"In dispute," Pratt repeated, amused. "By whom? With what? Mr. Crane signed a clear agreement."

"The interest rate's over the legal line of this state," Silas said. "Has been since the day he signed. That makes the whole interest void in law, and a note in dispute isn't a note in default, and you know it, Pratt, because you taught it to me. You don't move on a disputed note with a sheriff watching. It's the one thing your masters won't let you do, because it puts the whole game in front of a judge."

The sheriff, who had been bored, was no longer bored. He looked at Pratt with new attention.

Pratt's smooth face did not change, but something behind it did. "And your proof of this dispute?" he said softly.

"At the courthouse," Nell said, stepping up beside Silas, "by noon. The law of the rate, and the books that prove what you charged against it. You'll get your hearing, Mr. Pratt. You wanted no court. You're about to have one."


Chapter 44: Annie's Ledger

They had found it the night before, in the bottom of a trunk in August's wagon, under Annie Crane's folded shawls where it had lain three years untouched.

It was a real ledger, a bookkeeper's ledger, kept in Annie's small exact hand, and it ran up to the month she died. And in it — because Annie had recorded everything, every dollar in and out — were the first payments August had made on an earlier, smaller loan from the same Apex house, four years back, the loan he'd taken and repaid before this fatal one. Annie had written down each payment and beside it, in her careful figures, the interest charged.

Silas spread it on the table and ran his finger down the column and went very still.

"Twenty-four percent," he said. "Look. They charged August twenty-four on the first loan, four years ago, and Annie wrote it down to the penny, month by month. Twenty-four percent." He looked up at Nell. "The legal ceiling in this state is ten. I read it off the statute book at the county seat — Silas had copied the very page, the usury limit, ten percent per annum, plain. They've been charging more than double the legal rate for years. And here's a wife's own bookkeeping to prove the practice, and the new contract to prove they've never stopped."

"Will it hold?" Nell asked. "In front of a judge?"

"It's a dead woman's honest books against a syndicate's crooked paper," Silas said. "And the law's the law, written in the statute, ten percent and not a fraction more. If the rate's void, the interest's void. They get their eleven thousand back — that's fair, August borrowed it. But the two thousand of interest, the compounding, the twenty-two, the thirty days — all of it built on a rate the law won't allow." He closed the ledger gently, the way August had once closed it over the brand. "Annie kept her books to the penny. Three years dead, and she's about to save the show August nearly lost."


Chapter 45: Before the Clerk

They went to the county courthouse at noon — Nell, Silas, Pratt, the sheriff, and the clerk Pratt had brought, the whole strange procession in two buggies and a borrowed wagon — and they laid it before the county recorder and a justice of the peace who was hauled from his dinner and was not pleased about it until he understood what was in front of him.

Nell laid out Annie's ledger. Silas laid out the statute, the usury limit, ten percent, recorded plain in the state's own law. He laid the Apex contract beside it, and the demand letter with its twenty-two percent and its thirteen thousand four hundred. He spoke plainly, the way he did everything, and the justice followed it, and the recorder fetched the lawbook to be sure, and read it, and nodded slowly.

Pratt did not bluster. That was not his way. He stood smooth and calm and made the only argument he had.

"The contract was freely signed," he said. "Mr. Crane is a businessman. He agreed to the terms."

"A man can't freely sign away the protection of the law," the justice said, peering over his glasses. "That's the whole point of a usury statute, sir. So that a desperate man can't be made to agree to his own fleecing. Ten percent is the ceiling in this state. It doesn't matter what he signed. You can't charge above it, and what's more—" he looked at the demand letter again "—you've gone and charged more than double, and kept on charging it, and this woman's old account book shows you've made a habit of it. That's not a clerical slip, sir. That's a practice."

Pratt was silent.

"I'd be careful," the justice went on, warming now, "very careful, if I were the syndicate you speak for, about how I proceeded against this show. Because if this came before a real court, with this ledger in evidence, I don't think it'd be the circus that came out the worse."


Chapter 46: The Rate Voided

The justice would not, on a borrowed afternoon, dissolve a contract; that took a real court and time they could spend later. But he did the thing that mattered, the thing that broke Pratt's thirty days.

He found, on the face of the law and the evidence, that the interest rate stated in the Apex note was unlawful on its face, exceeding the statutory ceiling, and that therefore the note was in genuine dispute, and that no creditor could lawfully seize and liquidate property under a disputed note without first having the matter heard in court — which would take months, and which Apex, with its old crooked books now in evidence, would not want.

He wrote it out, signed it, and gave a copy to the sheriff.

"Sheriff," he said, "you'll not assist any seizure on this lot today, nor any day, until a proper court has ruled on this note. Do you understand me?"

"I understand you fine," the sheriff said, and he sounded relieved to understand it, because he had not liked his morning's work.

Pratt took his copy of the order. He read it once, folded it, and put it in his breast pocket with the care of a man filing a thing he intended to remember.

"You've bought time," he said to Nell, in the courthouse hall, low, just the two of them. "That's all. The principal's still owed. Eleven thousand. We'll come for it the lawful way, in a court, in our own time."

"Eleven thousand flat," Nell said. "Not growing. Not thirteen and climbing. Just the eleven August borrowed, that we always meant to pay." She looked at him steadily. "You taught Silas the game, Mr. Pratt. But you taught him too well. You should have charged a legal rate. You'd have had us, fair and square, and we'd have had no rope to pull."

Pratt almost smiled. "Greedy," he said, as if to himself. "We're always a little greedy. It's the one flaw." He put on his hat. "Pay the eleven thousand, Miss Sparrow, and the Syndicate will have no further interest in you. We don't litigate the unprofitable."


Chapter 47: Paying the Debt

It took the rest of the summer and the fall, but the number, at last, was a number a show could reach.

Eleven thousand flat, not growing. Against it, the six thousand two hundred from the benefit. And then, with the interest void and the seizure barred and the story in the city papers, the thing turned. Apex, embarrassed and unwilling to be dragged through a public court over its crooked books, let it be known quietly that it would take the principal and call the whole thing done. And the public, having read the story, came.

They came all the rest of the season, town after town, to the show that had beaten the syndicate, and the gates ran fat for the first time in years, and Nell put every dollar above costs into the pot and let the company go on half wages until the thing was paid, and the company agreed, because they were not working for August now or even for Nell. They were working for the proof that a small thing could stand up to a big one and win, which is a thing people will work very hard for.

A man Coyle had known, a real Chicago reporter, came and wrote the whole story, the syndicate and the dead shows and the usury and the dead woman's ledger that broke it, and it ran in three cities, and a benefit fund opened that Nell had not asked for and could not refuse.

By November, in winter quarters, Dewey counted the pot one last time and it was eleven thousand and forty dollars, and they paid Apex the eleven thousand by bank draft, and Pratt's house sent back a single line acknowledging the debt discharged in full, the A in the circle at the top of it, and Nell burned the letter in the cookhouse stove and watched the A curl and blacken and go to ash.


Chapter 48: The Ring and the Ledger

August was on his feet by then, slow and careful, his heart a thing he listened to now like a man listening for a far-off train. He would never again give the spiel two shows a day; the doctor had been plain. But he could sit in the ring's edge and watch, and he could teach, and he did both.

He called Nell to his wagon on a gray November afternoon when the show was bedded down for winter, and on the table he had set two things. The red coat, cleaned and mended, and Annie's ledger.

"I've thought a long time about this," he said. "Longer than my heart liked." He pushed the coat toward her. "The ring's yours. Not borrowed for a sick week — yours. You held a thousand strangers in Marah with my heart stopped in the dirt, and you held this whole company together when I'd have let it scatter from shame. I'm done being the man who lies and smiles. You're the one they follow now. So take the coat."

Then he pushed the ledger toward her.

"And take this," he said. "Annie's book. I kept the books after she died and I near killed us all with my keeping. I never had the head for it and I never had the honesty, when the figures went bad, to show them to anyone. You do." He tapped the ledger. "Keep the books, Nell. To the penny, the way she did. Show them to the company every season, good or bad. Never let it get to where one frightened man's signing things in the dark again." His eyes were wet. "That's the whole secret of it. Not the flying. The books, kept honest, and shown."

Nell took the coat. She took the ledger. She did not, for once, have anything to say, and August, who knew when words failed, only put his old hand over both of hers.


Chapter 49: Winter Quarters

They wintered together in a rented barn and a half-acre of frozen Indiana, lean but whole.

It was a hard winter, as winters were. Half wages, then no wages for a stretch, but they ate, and Madame Lorca read fortunes in the nearest town for cash and would not say whether she missed the garnets. Silas built Juno a proper warm stall, the warmest the old elephant had ever had, and her strained leg, given the long rest, healed clean, and she put on weight, and she would live a good while yet, Silas said, and he said it the way a man says a thing he has been afraid to hope.

Tom did not come back to stay — a flier needs the work, and a small show could carry only one act, and he had a mother in Dayton — but he wrote, and he sent the name of a young catcher he'd been training, a Pernod boy with good hands, and the boy came in February to learn the act under Nell, and was clumsy, and then less clumsy, and by March could catch a clean wrist.

Dewey kept the canvas mended through the cold. The roustabout who'd wanted to see what it was like to stand up once stayed the winter and the next, and stood up a second time and a third, because it turned out to be a thing a man could get a taste for.

And in the long evenings they sat in the barn by the stove, and August told the old stories, the flash flood of ought-three and the speech he'd made on a barrel about adversity, and the company laughed, and Nell kept the books to the penny by lamplight, and showed them, open, when anyone wished to see.


Chapter 50: The High Wire

In the cold spring of the new season, Nell Sparrow climbed the center pole again at dawn, in a field silver with frost, and rigged the trapeze.

The hemp was new this year. She'd bought it herself, out of the books kept honest, the first thing she'd bought when there was money to buy with — good new rope, spliced and tested, that would not part by July. Her hands knew the rungs without looking. Forty feet up there was only the gray sky and the bones of the rigging against it, the same as eleven years and a lifetime ago.

Below, the lot was waking. The cookhouse fire was lit and the smoke went up straight in the still air. Dewey was bawling at the canvas men. Silas led Juno out to drink, no chain at all, just his voice, and the old elephant's breath smoked in the cold. The new banner hung on the ticket wagon, painted fresh over the faded boards, and it still read HOLLIS & CRANE COMBINED SHOWS, because Nell had kept the name, but under it, small, in Annie's careful kind of lettering, it now read: A SHOW THAT BELONGS TO ITS OWN.

Nell sat on the bar and felt it take her weight, sure and new, and she looked out over the ring of wagons, the patched and the painted, the people who had stayed, the family she had nearly lost and had not.

Then she gripped the cable and stood, and pushed off into the cold bright air, and flew — out over the empty ring, in the gray dawn, with no crowd yet to see it and no one yet to catch her, only the new rope and her own two hands and the long sure swing of it.

It was enough. It had always, in the end, been enough.

She caught the return bar clean, and laughed, and the laugh went out over the lot, and the lot, waking, laughed back.
