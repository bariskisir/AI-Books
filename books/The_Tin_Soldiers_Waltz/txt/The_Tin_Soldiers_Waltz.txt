Chapter 1: The Soldier on the Shelf

The workshop slept under a roof of snow. Tib did not.

He stood on the high shelf where the old man had placed him years ago, painted blue coat, red cuffs, one boot black and shining, and below it nothing — the leg that had never been cast. He had stood there long enough to learn the room by heart. The lathe in the corner. The jars of glass eyes. The clock with the crooked minute hand.

He could not move while the old man watched. That was the rule he had set himself, the night he first found himself awake and afraid. So he watched instead.

Anselm Roe came in carrying a candle. He was stooped now, his beard gone the color of ash, his hands spotted like old paper. He set the candle on the bench and lifted a small box from the drawer where he kept it. The box was walnut, no bigger than a loaf, banded in brass.

"There you are," Anselm said, to no one. He turned the little key in its side. Three slow turns, no more. He had told himself, long ago, never to wind it all the way.

The lid came up. Inside, a brass cylinder studded with pins began to turn, and a comb of tiny steel teeth plucked out a waltz.

It was a simple tune. Tib knew every note. He could not have said how a thing of tin came to love music, but he did, and he loved this tune above all others, because the old man's face changed when it played. The hard lines softened. The eyes went somewhere far away and kind.

Anselm hummed along, half a beat behind. When the cylinder slowed and the last note thinned to nothing, he closed the lid gently, as if shutting a sleeping child's door.

"Goodnight, then," he said.

He took his candle and went up the narrow stairs to bed.

Tib waited until the floor above him creaked and settled. Then he let himself breathe — not air, he had no need of air, but the small inward motion that was the closest thing he had to it.

Below the shelf the workshop ticked and shifted in the cold. Outside, the snow came down on the clockwork city of Vellmark, on its turning weathervanes and its frozen fountains and its one great clock in the square, whose chimes Tib had heard every hour of his secret life.

He looked at the walnut box on the bench. He did not yet know what it held. He only knew the old man loved it, and so he loved it too.


Chapter 2: The Forgetting

It began with a tool.

Anselm reached for it without looking, the way a man reaches for his own hand. A small steel pick he used for setting springs. His fingers closed on the empty air beside it.

"The — " he said, and stopped.

Tib, on his shelf, went very still.

Anselm's hand hovered. He looked down at the rows of tools laid out in their felt slots, each in its place, and his face did a thing Tib had never seen it do. It emptied. For a moment there was no name in it for anything at all.

"The little one," Anselm said at last, and picked up the pick, and laughed at himself, a thin laugh. "Getting old, Roe."

He went back to work. He was mending a wind-up dog for a customer, a poor thing with a snapped tendon of wire. His hands still knew the work even when his tongue forgot the words for it. That was the mercy of it, Tib thought. The hands remembered.

But over the morning it happened again. A word here, a name there, slipping out of the old man like sand out of a cracked jar. He called the lathe "the spinning thing." He stood in the middle of the room once with a cup of cold tea and could not think why he had crossed to fetch it.

Tib watched and could do nothing. To move now would be to undo the only rule that kept him safe.

So he stood, and he learned a new kind of helplessness, which was worse than the old kind, because it had a face it loved at the center of it.

At noon a woman came to collect the dog. Anselm wound it for her and it trotted in a circle, barking its tin bark, and the woman smiled and paid and left. Anselm stood holding the coins.

"What was her name," he said softly. "She comes every week. What is her name."

He did not ask Tib. He did not know there was anyone to ask.

Tib thought: it is the box. Whatever is in the box is what he is losing. And he did not yet know if that thought was true, or only a hope wearing the shape of an answer.


Chapter 3: Three Turns of the Key

That night the old man wound the box again, and the waltz came out into the dark, and Anselm wept.

He did not know he was weeping. The tears came down into his beard and he sat with his hands open on his knees, and the music turned, and his face was full of something too large for him to hold and too far away for him to name.

"I had a brother," Anselm said suddenly. "Didn't I."

The cylinder turned.

"We danced in the kitchen. Mother played this. On a fiddle, not — not a box." He frowned. "Or was it the box. Was it always the box."

The tune slowed. He had given it only three turns. He never gave it more.

When it stopped he sat a long while in the dark, and Tib watched him from the shelf, and wished with everything in his small tin chest that he could climb down and put one hand on the old man's sleeve and say, I am here, you are not alone, the music remembers even when you don't.

But he could not. So he memorized the waltz instead, every pin and pluck of it, and held it in himself like a coal.

Anselm rose stiffly and shut the lid.

"Three turns," he murmured. "Mustn't wind it down. Mustn't ever wind it all the way." He said it the way a man says a prayer he no longer remembers the reason for.

He put the box back in its drawer and climbed the stairs.

Tib understood, then, what he had half-known since morning. The box did not merely play the old man's favorite tune. The tune was the old man's memory, wound up tight, paying itself out a little at a time. Each turn of the key was a fragment given back. And Anselm, fearing to spend it all, rationed it — three turns a night, never more — so that the well would not run dry.

But the well was running dry anyway. Tib had seen it all day, leaking out of the cracked jar.

He did not sleep. Tin does not sleep. He stood his watch over the drawer where the box lay, as he had stood his watch over the old man for years, and for the first time in his life he wanted, fiercely, to be more than a thing that stands.


Chapter 4: The Open Window

The storm came on the fourth night.

It came down off the northern roofs and threw itself at the workshop, and the loose pane in the high window — the one Anselm always meant to fix and never did — rattled in its frame. Tib heard it from the shelf. He heard the wind find the gap and worry it.

Anselm slept above. He had wound the box, three turns, and gone up early, more tired each day.

The pane gave at midnight. It swung in on its old hinge with a bang, and the storm came into the room, and the candles guttered out, and snow blew across the bench in a fine white veil.

And then there was a sound that was not the storm.

A scrape. A boot on the sill. A shape blotting out the gray square of window.

A man came down into the workshop. He came clumsily, knocking a jar, swearing under his breath in a voice cracked with cold. He was thin as a coat-rack and wrapped in rags, and where his right hand should have been there was a thing of brass and wire, a mender's hand, the kind broken men fitted to themselves when the flesh one was lost.

Tib watched from the shelf, frozen by his own rule, screaming inside the stillness.

The man cast about the dark room. His eyes were quick and wretched. He was not looking at the tools, not at the silver, not at the till.

He was looking for something small.

"Come on," the ragged man whispered to the dark. "Toymaker's house. There's always one. There's always a box."

And his eyes found the drawer.


Chapter 5: The Theft

The ragged man crossed the room in three strides.

He opened the drawer with his good hand. The brass one hung at his side, fingers twitching with a faint mechanical tremor he could not still. He lifted out the walnut box, turned it in the gray storm-light, and Tib saw something cross the man's ruined face — not greed. Recognition. As if he had been looking for this exact box his whole life and feared he would never find it.

"There," the man breathed. "There you are."

He tucked it inside his coat, against his chest.

Above, the floor creaked. Anselm's voice came down the stairs, thin and uncertain. "Hello? Is someone — is the window — "

The ragged man froze.

Tib made his choice in that instant. Not to move — he still could not, not with the man watching the room — but to be seen, if seeing him would slow the thief. He let himself tip. He had stood balanced on one boot for years by an act of secret will; now he simply stopped willing it. He fell forward off the shelf.

He struck the bench, bounced, clattered to the floorboards at the thief's feet with a bright tin clang.

The man flinched back. He stared down at the little soldier lying in the snow that had blown across the boards. For one held breath the two of them were very near, the great wretched man and the small fallen toy, and Tib looked up into the gray eyes and willed everything he had into stillness, into being only a toy, a fallen toy, nothing more.

"Just a soldier," the man muttered. "Just a broken little soldier."

Anselm's foot landed on the top stair.

The thief turned and went up and out the window the way he'd come, into the storm, the box against his heart, gone.

Tib lay on the cold floor. The window banged. The snow came in.

He had failed. He told himself he had not chosen to fail, that there had been no choice that worked — but he had fallen, and the box was gone, and that was the only fact that mattered.


Chapter 6: A Morning of Strangers

Anselm came down into a ruined room.

He moved through it slowly. The open window. The snow on the bench, melting now into dark stains. The drawer hanging open. He looked at the empty drawer for a long time.

"Something was here," he said. He pressed his fingers to his temple. "Something was here and now it isn't, and I — I can't think what it was."

Tib lay where he had fallen, and watched, and waited for the moment he could not be observed.

Anselm picked him up off the floor. The old hands were gentle even now. He turned the soldier over, checked the paint, the seams, the one good boot.

"You took a tumble, friend," Anselm said. "Drafty old place. I keep meaning to fix that window." He stood Tib back on the shelf, and patted the bench, and looked around the room as if it belonged to someone else. "Whose workshop is this," he said, very quietly, and it was not a joke. "Whose lathe. Whose tools."

He pulled a stool to the bench and sat. He did not start any work. He had forgotten what work was for.

A neighbor knocked, later, and brought bread, and called the old man by his name. "Morning, Anselm." And Anselm smiled and took the bread and did not know her, and she did not see that he did not know her, and she went away again into the snow.

That was the worst of it, Tib thought. Not the great forgetting all at once. The small forgettings, one by one, each so small that the world outside never noticed, until the old man would be a stranger to everyone, including himself.

Above the bench the broken window let in a thread of cold.

Tib waited for night.


Chapter 7: The First Step Down

When the house was dark and the old man asleep, Tib moved.

It was not as easy as falling.

He had fallen once and it had been simple — a surrender, a letting go. To climb down on purpose was another matter. He had one leg. He had stood by balance and will, but he had never walked, not once, in all his secret years.

He shifted his weight to the edge of the shelf. The drop to the bench was the height of a house, to him. He gauged it, the way he had seen the old man gauge a cut, and he lowered himself over the lip, hanging by his tin hands, and let go.

He landed hard. The single boot took the shock and the rest of him jarred. He lay a moment, taking stock. Nothing bent. Tin is tougher than it looks.

He got himself upright by gripping a chisel handle and pulling. Standing on one leg, on the bench, in the dark, he took stock of the impossible thing he meant to do.

He had to cross the room. He had to find a way down to the floor, and out the window, and into the city. He had to find a thin man with a brass hand and a stolen box, in a place vaster than anything Tib had ever seen, where everything was a hundred times his size and most of it could crush him without noticing.

He took his first step.

It was a hop, really. A small controlled fall forward, catching himself on the boot, arms wheeling. He nearly went over. He steadied. He did it again. And again. By the time he reached the far edge of the bench he had a rhythm to it — hop, catch, sway, set; hop, catch, sway, set — clumsy as anything, but his own.

He stood at the edge and looked down at the floor, miles below.

"All right," Tib said aloud, the first words he had ever spoken to the empty room. His voice was small and tinny and entirely steady. "All right. One thing at a time."

He had decided. That was the thing about him that the paint and the missing leg hid: once Tib decided a thing, the deciding was done forever.


Chapter 8: Waking Mr. Pim

He was not the only one who woke at night.

He had always suspected it. The toys on the lower shelves had a way of being not quite where Anselm left them, of facing the wrong way of a morning. Tib had never tested it. Now he needed help, and a one-legged soldier alone had little hope.

He hop-stepped to the edge of the bench above the toy crate and called down, softly, "Hello? Anyone? Is anyone there?"

Silence. Then, from inside the crate, a faint clash of cymbals, embarrassed and small.

"Who's that," said a voice. "Who's there. Show yourself or — or I'll bang my cymbals, I will, I'll wake the whole street."

"It's Tib. The soldier. From the high shelf."

A monkey's head poked over the crate's edge, brass-furred, with a red felt cap and a wide painted grin he could not change however he felt. He held a cymbal in each paw. His eyes were bright and frightened and kind.

"The high-shelf soldier," said the monkey. "I'm Pim. Mr. Pim, when there's company. You're not supposed to be down here. None of us is supposed to — to be, when it's like this. Awake."

"I know. Listen to me, Pim. The old man is sick. He's losing himself, a piece at a time. A thief took the box that holds his memories. I'm going after it. I can't do it alone."

Pim clutched his cymbals to his chest. "Outside? You mean to go outside? There's cats outside. There's wheels and boots and the cold that stops your spring. I've heard. I've heard the things that come back broken, the few that come back at all."

"Then don't come," Tib said. "I won't think less of you. But the old man wound you and painted you and set you down gentle every night, same as me. I'm going whether you do or not."

Pim looked at him a long moment. The grin stayed fixed. But something behind the painted face made a decision of its own.

"Wait," Pim said, and clambered out of the crate. "Wait for me. Somebody's got to make a racket if it all goes wrong."


Chapter 9: Coppelia Will Not Move

There was a third masterwork in the room.

She stood on the round table by the window where the best light fell, a clockwork ballerina in a stiff white skirt, one foot pointed, both arms curved over her head, frozen in the first instant of a turn she had never been allowed to finish. Anselm had made her the year before he made Tib. She was the most beautiful thing in the workshop and she knew it.

Tib and Pim crossed the floor to the table leg — a long, perilous journey across open boards, hugging the shadows — and climbed the carved leg hand over hand. Tib went last and slowest, hauling himself up by his arms where the others could push with two feet.

They came up onto the tabletop. Coppelia stood in the gray window-light, exactly as she always stood.

"Coppelia," Tib said. "We need you. The old man — "

"I know what's happened to the old man." Her voice was cool and precise, like a clock striking. She did not turn her head; she could not, posed as she was, but her eyes slid toward them. "I have stood at this window for a year. I see and hear everything. The thief. The box. The forgetting. I know it all."

"Then you'll help."

"No."

The word was small and final.

"I am the finest thing he ever made," Coppelia said. "Outside there is snow and salt and rust. Outside, beauty like mine lasts a season and ends in a gutter. I will not go." Her painted eyes glistened, or seemed to. "I am sorry for him. I am. But I was not made to be brave. I was made to be looked at."

Pim banged a cymbal, once, in disgust. The sound rang and died.

Tib only looked at her. "All right," he said. There was no anger in it. "Then tell us one thing, at least. Where would a man sell a stolen box of memories? Where do the menders gather?"

Coppelia was quiet. Then, low: "The Tin Market. Under the old gasworks, where the broken ones go to be bought for parts. Ask there. And — soldier." Her eyes found him. "I am ashamed. Remember that I told you the truth, even while I refused you. It is the only brave thing I have."

Tib nodded. "It's a start," he said, and meant it kindly. "Come on, Pim."


Chapter 10: Bootprints in the Snow

The window was the only way out, and the window was high.

They found the route the old way, the way the toys must always have used and never spoken of: along the bench, up a coil of rope, across the curtain rod, and out onto the snowy sill where the storm had left a drift packed hard against the frame.

The night had cleared. The city lay below them under a high cold moon, roofs heaped with snow, chimneys breathing pale smoke straight up into the still air. Somewhere a weathervane creaked. Somewhere the great clock ticked, so deep and slow Tib felt it more than heard it, a pulse under the whole frozen world.

"It's big," Pim said in a very small voice. "I didn't know it would be so big."

"It's the same world," Tib said. "Only more of it. Look there."

In the drift below the window, half-filled with new snow but still plain, ran a line of bootprints. One boot whole. The other dragging — the man had a bad foot, or a heavy load, or both. The prints went down off the sill ledge onto the slope of the porch roof and down again toward the street.

"He came this way," Tib said. "He'll have gone the easiest way down. So will we."

They went to the edge of the sill. Below, a drainpipe ran down the corner of the house, fat and frost-furred, all the way to the street.

Pim looked at it and made a thin sound. "I can't climb down that. I'll slip. I'll fall and burst my spring."

"You won't." Tib hop-stepped to the pipe and tested it with one hand. Cold burned through his tin palm; he ignored it. "We go slow. You hold my coat, I hold the pipe. One thing at a time. Always one thing at a time, Pim. That's the whole secret of it."

"That's a terrible secret," Pim muttered. But he took hold of Tib's coat.


Chapter 11: What Coppelia Could Not Do

They were halfway down the pipe when the window above them opened.

Tib looked up. Coppelia stood in the frame, in her white skirt, the moon behind her. She had moved. She had come down from her table and crossed the room and opened the window — things she had said she could not, would not do.

"Soldier," she called down, soft so as not to wake the house. "Wait."

Tib clung to the pipe. "We can't wait. The trail will fill."

"I know. I — " She stopped. Her hands gripped the sill. Tib saw what it had cost her even to come this far: she was trembling, fine and fast, her painted serenity cracked. "I cannot come with you. I tried. I got to the window and I could not put my foot on the snow. I am a coward to the bolt. But I could not let you go without — "

She drew something up onto the sill. A spool of fine silk thread, the kind Anselm used for the most delicate work.

"Take it. You'll need to climb things, bind things, lower yourselves. It's strong. Stronger than it looks." She paid the thread down the pipe to them, and Pim caught the end and wound it about his small brass shoulder. "And the brass crow," Coppelia said. "Listen. Madame Sorrel, the one who buys stolen memory — she keeps brass crows, runners with hooked feet. If you find the box at the market, you must reach it before her crows do, or it goes to her and you'll never get it back. I heard the menders speak of her once, through the glass."

"Why are you telling us this," Tib said, "if you won't come?"

Coppelia was quiet. The moon was very bright on her.

"Because telling you is the only part I am brave enough for," she said. "And because — if you bring him back to himself — I should like him to know I helped, in the end. In my small, cowardly way." She tried to smile and could not, posed as she was. "Go. Go quickly. And come back, soldier. All of you. Come back."

She drew in the window, and was gone.

Tib looked at Pim. "Strong as we are," he said, "we just got stronger by a friend. Even one who stays home."


Chapter 12: Down the Drainpipe

The last stretch of pipe was sheathed in ice.

Tib felt it before he saw it — the sudden glassy smoothness under his palm, the way his grip began to slide. "Stop," he said. "Ice. Hold to me. Don't grip the pipe, grip me."

"That's worse," Pim said. "You've only got one — "

"Grip me."

The monkey wrapped both paws and both feet around Tib's middle. Tib hooked his arm through a bracket where the pipe joined the wall, and they hung there a moment, swaying, the street still a long drop below.

The silk thread saved them.

Tib paid it out around the bracket and down, the way he'd seen sailors do it in a picture-book the old man had once shown a customer's child. They went down hand over hand, the thread biting, Pim's weight dragging, the ice-cold metal of the pipe sliding past, until the thread ran out and there was still a fall beneath them — but a small one now, into a snowbank heaped against the wall.

"Let go," Tib said. "It's soft."

"Soft," Pim wailed, and let go.

They dropped into the snow. It closed over them, cold and white and silent. For a moment Tib could see nothing, could feel nothing but the cold pressing in on every seam. Then his hand broke the surface, and he hauled himself up, and dragged Pim up after him by the felt cap, and the two of them stood at last on the floor of the world.

The street ran away in both directions, a canyon of snow and stone. The houses leaned over them. The cobbles, where the snow had been trodden, were each the size of a bed. A frozen gutter ran down the middle of everything like a black road.

"There," Tib said, and pointed.

The bootprints came down off the porch roof, into the street, and turned left, toward the low part of the city, toward the river and the old gasworks and the Tin Market under it.

"One thing at a time," said Pim, faintly.

"That's the spirit," said Tib, and they set off into the canyon of the street, two small bright things on a road built for giants.


Chapter 13: The Canyon of the Street

The street tried to kill them within the hour.

They kept to the wall, in the shadow of the buildings, because the open middle of the road was a place of terrors. Twice a sledge went past, vast as a moving house, runners hissing on the packed snow, and each time Tib pulled Pim flat against the stone and they waited, hearts ticking too fast, while the world shook and passed.

A horse went by. They had both seen horses, in the workshop window, far off and small. Up close it was a mountain that breathed steam and threw its great hooves down like falling anvils, each strike sending a tremor up through the cobbles into Tib's single boot. A clod of dirty ice the size of a wardrobe fell from one hoof and shattered an arm's length from where they crouched.

"I want to go home," Pim said, when it had passed. He said it simply, not as a complaint, just a true thing.

"I know," Tib said. "So do I. But the box isn't home. So we aren't either, yet."

They came to a place where a fallen boot lay against the wall — a child's boot, lost in some long-ago thaw, frozen now into the bank. It made a cave, dark and dry inside.

"We'll rest here a moment," Tib said. "Just to take stock."

They crawled in out of the wind. Inside the boot it was almost warm, in the way that out of the wind is almost warm. Pim huddled small. Tib sat with his back to the leather and looked out at the white canyon and the moon-shadows and the distant breathing city.

"Why do you do it," Pim asked. "The old man can't even see you move. He'll never know it was you. If we bring the box back he'll think it just — turned up. You'll get no thanks. You'll go back on your shelf and stand there forever and he'll never know."

Tib thought about it.

"I don't do it to be thanked," he said. "I do it because he's mine. He made me. He set me down gentle, every night, for years. I'd rather stand on his shelf knowing I went, than stand on his shelf having stayed."

Pim was quiet. Then he clashed his cymbals, very softly, once.

"All right," he said. "All right, soldier. Lead on."


Chapter 14: Soot

The cat came without warning.

One moment the street was empty. The next, a shadow detached itself from a doorway and poured across the snow toward them, low and silent and enormous — a black tom with one torn ear and eyes like green lamps, and Tib understood in a flash of cold certainty that to this creature they were not toys at all. They were prey. Small bright moving things. The oldest trap in the world.

"Run!" he shouted. "Pim, run — the gutter — get under the ice!"

They ran.

It was no kind of running, for Tib — it was the desperate hop-and-catch, flat out, faster than he had ever done it, his single boot striking the packed snow and his arms flailing for balance. Pim bounded ahead on all fours, cymbals jangling. The cat came after them in great soft leaps, gaining with every one, and Tib could hear it now, the huff of its breath, the faint creak of the snow under its weight.

A paw came down. Tib felt the wind of it. He threw himself sideways and the claws raked the snow where he'd been, four black furrows, and the cat overshot and skidded and turned.

"The grate!" Pim shrieked.

There was a sewer grate ahead, set in the gutter, its bars frosted but its gaps black and open. Pim dove through. Tib hop-flung himself after, and a paw caught his coat as he went, and for one sickening instant he was lifted, dangling, the cat's claw hooked through his blue tin coat and the green eyes huge and close and pitiless.

Then the coat tore. Tib dropped through the grate into the dark, and the cat's paw slammed the bars above, and a furious yellow yowl came down after him into the underworld.

He landed on ice, in blackness, beside Pim.

"Are you whole," Pim was saying, patting at him with frantic paws. "Are you whole, are you bent, did it get you — "

"It got my coat," Tib said, sitting up. There was a long rent down his side, the bright tin showing through the blue paint. "Nothing that matters. Just the coat."

But he sat a moment longer than he needed to, in the dark, while his spring slowed back to its ordinary pace. The cat's eyes had been the first thing in his whole life that had wanted to end him. He found he did not like it. He found, too, that it had not changed his mind by even a hair.


Chapter 15: Under the Ice

The sewer was a frozen river, roofed in stone.

It had not run in years — the water had stilled and frozen into a long pale road that wound away under the city. Light came down in thin shafts through the grates above, blue with moon and snow. The air was old and cold and smelled of rust and iron.

They walked along the ice. It was easier going than the snow, level and hard, and Tib found his hop-step almost graceful here, almost a glide. Pim went ahead, peering into the dark, his cymbals dim now and quiet.

Then they jammed.

Pim raised a cymbal to scratch his head with it — a thing he did when he thought — and the hinge of his arm seized with a small grinding crunch. He froze, paw up.

"Tib," he said. "Tib, I can't — my arm won't — "

Tib came to him. The cold had got into the monkey's works, into the fine joint of the cymbal-arm, and the ice had formed in the gears. Pim stood with his arm locked up, his painted grin suddenly horrible, a smile fixed over real fear.

"Don't force it," Tib said. "You'll snap the spring. Hold still."

He took Pim's arm in both hands and breathed on the joint — not breath, but that small inward warmth he kept, the same warmth that was the difference between a toy and whatever Tib was. He held the joint and warmed it, patiently, for a long time, while the cold pressed in and the dark ticked and far above them a city of giants slept.

The ice in the gear softened. The joint gave, all at once, and Pim's arm came down with a clash that rang away down the frozen river.

"Oh," Pim said, flexing it. "Oh, that's better. How did you — how did you do that?"

"I'm warm," Tib said, surprised to hear himself say it. "Inside. I don't know why. I think it's the same thing that lets me — be. Be awake. Be me." He looked at his own tin hands. "The old man put something in me he didn't put in the others. Or maybe I just got it on my own. I don't know."

"Lucky," Pim said.

"Maybe," said Tib. He thought of the cat. He thought of the old man forgetting his brother's face. "Or maybe it just means I can lose more than the rest of you. You can't grieve what you can't feel."

They walked on, down the frozen river, into the dark under the world.


Chapter 16: Old Borin

A light grew ahead in the dark — not moonlight, but a warm small glow, steady and gold.

They came around a bend in the frozen channel and found, set in a niche in the old stone, a figure seated on a fallen block. It was an automaton, man-shaped and man-sized — a giant, to them — made all of riveted brass gone green with age. In its hand it held a lamp, an old lamplighter's lamp, and inside the lamp a tiny flame burned that gave off no smoke.

The figure turned its head with a slow grinding of neck-gears and looked down at the two small things on the ice.

"Now then," said the automaton. Its voice was deep and rusty and slow, a voice that had not spoken in a long while. "What's this. Two of the little folk, off their shelves, down in the dark. That's a rare sight. That's a sight I've not had in years."

Pim shrank behind Tib. Tib stood his ground, looking up.

"I'm Tib," he said. "This is Mr. Pim. We're not afraid of you." This was not quite true. "Who are you?"

"Borin," said the automaton. "Once. The city lit its lamps by me, square by square, dusk to dusk, for ninety winters. Then they ran wires through the streets and gave the light to glass bulbs and copper, and they put me down here to keep the old pressure-gauge from freezing, where I'd not be in the way." He lifted his lamp a little. "I light this for company. Habit. A lamplighter wants a flame."

"You're not in anyone's way down here," Tib said.

"No." Borin's gears creaked, something like a sigh. "Not in anyone's way at all. That's rather the trouble with it." He set the lamp down. "Now. Two toys don't come down into my dark for the air. What is it you're after, little soldier?"


Chapter 17: The Name of Madame Sorrel

Tib told him everything. The old man. The forgetting. The box of memories, three turns of the key, the storm, the thief with the brass hand.

Borin listened without moving, the flame steady in his lamp. When Tib was done, the old automaton was quiet for a long moment.

"A memory-work," Borin said at last. "A music box wound full of a man's own life, paying it back to him a turn at a time. Aye, I've heard of such. Rare and dear and dangerous to make. And you say a mender took it — a man with a brass hand."

"You know him?"

"I know the kind. Broken men who fit themselves with broken hands and mend what they can to eat. Most aren't thieves. The one that is, that takes a memory-work in the night — " Borin's head turned, the gears grinding. "He'd not keep it. He'd sell it. And there's only one buyer in Vellmark for a stolen memory-work."

"Madame Sorrel," Tib said.

Borin's flame flickered. "You know the name."

"Coppelia told me. A clockmaker. She keeps brass crows."

"She keeps more than crows." Borin leaned down, close, his great green face filling Tib's sky. "Listen to me, soldier. Sorrel is a clockmaker, yes, but what she truly makes is a collection. She buys memory-works and winds them down to nothing, draining the lives out of them like wine from a cask, because she has discovered that a person's memories, taken into her own clock, slow her wearing. She does not age, they say, the way clockwork ages. She runs true. And she pays for it with other people's lives, a turn at a time." The flame steadied. "If your box reaches her, she will wind it all the way down. She will not ration it as your old man did. She will spend it in a night. And then there will be nothing left to bring home."

Tib stood very still on the ice.

"Then we have to reach it first," he said.

"Aye." Borin straightened, gears protesting. "And the market's the place. The Tin Market, under the old gasworks. That's where her runners come to buy. If your thief's selling, he'll sell there, and her crows will be circling." He picked up his lamp. "I can't go up into the streets — too big, too rusted, they'd take me for scrap. But I know the ways under the city better than anyone living or wound. I can take you up through the dark, fast, to the very floor of the market. Will you have a guide, soldier?"

"Gladly," said Tib. "And gladly the company."

Something moved in the old brass face that might, on a man, have been a smile.


Chapter 18: Through the Clockwork Guts

Borin led them off the frozen river into the workings of the city.

Vellmark, Tib learned that night, was not built on stone but on clockwork. Under its streets ran a vast machine — the great municipal works that turned the public clocks, raised the river-locks, drove the bellows of the heating-pipes that kept the city from freezing solid. Borin walked them through it on ledges and along still cog-teeth and over the backs of gears the size of millwheels, all of it ticking, turning, vast and patient in the dark.

"Mind your step here," Borin would say. "This wheel turns on the quarter-hour. We've time, but mind it." Or: "Down this way. The hot-pipe runs alongside; you'll warm your works." And they did, walking the length of a great copper pipe that breathed heat into the cold, and Pim's joints loosened and even Tib felt the warmth go into his seams like a kindness.

"You know it all," Tib said, marveling, as they crossed a chasm on a stilled chain, link by link.

"Ninety winters," Borin said. "A man learns a city in ninety winters. Even after they've no more use for him. Perhaps especially then." He paused on the chain, his lamp swinging. "You asked, before, why I light the flame. I'll tell you the rest of it. I light it because if I let it go out, I'm only old brass in the dark, waiting to seize. The flame's the part of me that still has a reason. You understand that, I think, soldier. You with your one leg and your hopeless errand."

"It isn't hopeless," Tib said.

"No," Borin agreed. "Hopeless things don't get a flame. Come on. We're nearly under the market. You'll hear it before you see it."

And they did. A rising din came down through the works — voices, the clink of metal, the rasp of bargains struck. Light filtered down through the cracks in a floor far above.

"The Tin Market," Borin said. "Up there. Step soft, soldier. From here, you're on your own legs. Leg." The old automaton's gears made their grinding almost-laugh. "I'll wait below the grate. I'll keep my flame lit for you. If you need a way down in a hurry, you'll find me."


Chapter 19: The Tin Market

They came up through a grate in the corner of the market floor, behind a barrel, into noise and light and the smell of hot oil and rust.

The Tin Market filled the floor of the old gasworks — a great brick hall, its iron roof hung with lanterns, its floor a maze of stalls and barrows. And every soul in it was broken in some way. Men with mended hands and patched coats. Old soldiers with peg legs. Tinkers, menders, scrap-women with sacks of springs. They bought and sold the bones of the clockwork city: gears, mainsprings, glass eyes, the salvaged guts of dead automata. And among the parts, here and there, sat the toys.

Tib saw them and his spring went cold.

Toys for sale. Wind-up toys, salvaged, mismatched, sad. A dancer with one arm. A drummer with a stove-in side. A whole barrow of them, tangled, some surely as alive as Tib and Pim and unable to show it, here among the strangers, waiting to be bought for parts.

"Don't look," Pim whispered. "Don't look at them, Tib, I can't bear it."

"I have to look," Tib said. "One of them might be the one we're after. Come on. Keep to the shadows under the barrows. Watch for a thin man with a brass hand."

They went down into the market, two small bright things in a country of giants, ducking under barrows, freezing when a boot came near, threading the forest of legs. Above them the bargains went on, the lanterns swung, the cold breathed in through the broken windows of the old gasworks.

And then Tib saw him.

At a food-stall in the corner — a barrow selling hot bread and thin soup to the broken people of the market — stood the thin man with the brass hand. The thief. He had his back to the room. He was holding out the walnut box to the stall-woman with his good hand, and with the brass one he was pointing at the bread.

He was trading the old man's memories for a meal.


Chapter 20: The Thief at the Stall

"There," Tib breathed. "There he is. The box."

Pim grabbed his arm. "We can't just walk up. He's the size of a tower. He'll step on us. He'll think we're salvage and sell us too."

"I'm not going to walk up to him," Tib said. "I'm going to talk to him."

He looked at the thief. Up close, in the lantern-light, the man was worse than he'd seemed in the dark of the workshop — gaunt to the bone, his rags frozen stiff, his face the gray of long hunger. The brass hand trembled at his side with that constant fine tremor. He was not a villain out of a storybook. He was a starving man selling the only thing he had stolen.

The stall-woman took the box. She turned it over, frowned, shook it. The waltz gave a single muffled note. She set down a heel of bread and a cup of soup, and held out her hand for the box to keep.

And the thief hesitated.

Tib saw it. The man's good hand closed on the box. For a moment he did not give it over. He looked at the box and his ruined face did the thing it had done in the workshop — recognition, hunger of a different kind, some old grief waking up.

"Wait," the thief said to the woman. His voice cracked. "Wait. Let me — one turn. Let me hear it once before I — "

He turned the key, one turn, with the brass hand's twitching fingers.

The waltz came out of the box into the din of the market. Anselm's waltz. The old man's tune. And the thief stood with the bread untouched in front of him and listened, and his eyes filled, and Tib, crouched in the shadow of the barrow, felt the whole errand turn on its hinge.

Because a brass crow had just landed on the rafter above the stall, and it had heard the music too.


Chapter 21: A Bargain Broken

It happened fast.

The brass crow dropped from the rafter. It was a horrible thing, all hooked joints and glass eyes, with a beak like shears and feet that ended in clutching wire. It came down at the box in a clatter of wings.

The thief saw it and snatched the box back from the stall-woman — "No!" — and the crow's feet closed on empty air. The stall-woman shrieked. Bread went flying. The crow wheeled up, came down again, and this time its beak caught the box's brass key and wrenched.

"It's Sorrel's!" someone shouted in the market. "Sorrel's crow! She's bought it! Let it take its due!"

The thief clutched the box to his chest with both hands, good and brass, and ran.

He ran straight toward the corner where Tib and Pim were hidden, blundering, the crow tearing at his shoulders. Tib had one instant to decide. He took it.

"Now!" he shouted, and threw himself out from under the barrow into the open, right into the man's path. "Stop! The box — the toymaker — Anselm Roe — that's his life you're carrying!"

The thief looked down. In the chaos, in the noise, he saw the small blue soldier standing in his path, one leg, arms spread, shouting up at him with a voice like a struck nail. He stopped dead. The crow struck again. And the box, wrenched between man and bird, slipped.

It fell.

It hit the floor of the market and burst its latch and skidded under the stalls, lid flapping, the cylinder spilling out one bent note. The crow dove after it. The thief lunged. And a runner's boot — a child of one of the scrap-women, darting through the legs — kicked the box without seeing it, and it spun away across the floor and was gone into the dark under the barrows.

The crow rose, screaming its metal scream, and shot up and out through a broken window into the night.

It did not have the box. But Tib, scrambling after the skidding walnut, understood with a falling heart that it knew now where the box was. And it would bring others.


Chapter 22: The Brass Crow

Tib reached the box first.

It had fetched up against a barrow wheel, lid open, the bent cylinder still trying to turn. He flung himself onto it, gripped the lid, and got it shut. The box was vast in his arms — taller than he was, heavier than he could lift. He could no more carry it off than he could carry the barrow.

Pim arrived, cymbals flailing. "We have it! Tib, we — how do we move it? It's the size of a house!"

"We don't carry it. We hide it. Quick — under here, behind the wheel — "

But there was no time. A second crow came in through the broken window, and a third, circling the lantern-light, glass eyes hunting. The first one had told the others. Tib could see how it would go: they could not lift the box, and the crows could, and the moment the market thinned the crows would take it up and away to Sorrel's house, and that would be that.

The thief came scrambling through the legs of the crowd. He dropped to his knees beside the barrow, his gaunt face level with Tib's for the first time, his gray eyes wide.

"You spoke," he whispered. "A toy that speaks. Anselm Roe — you said Anselm Roe. The toymaker on Candle Street."

"You took his memories," Tib said, holding the box, fierce and small. "Give them back."

"I didn't know what it was. I swear it. I only knew toymakers keep boxes, and a box might buy bread." The man's voice shook. "I didn't know it was his life. I — " He stopped. The crows circled lower. "They'll take it. Sorrel's already paid for it; her crows won't stop. They'll carry it to her and she'll wind it dry."

"Then help me get it away from them."

The thief looked at the box, and at the crows, and at the small blue soldier. Something moved in him — guilt, or the ghost of who he'd been before the brass hand.

"I can't," he said. "She'll know me. She's — she's taken things from me before." But he reached out with his good hand. "I can throw it. Down the grate you came up by. To the dark below, where the crows can't fly. It's the most I can do, and then I have to run, do you understand, I have to run."

The crows folded their wings to dive.

"Throw it," Tib said. "Throw it now."


Chapter 23: Over the Rooftops

The thief threw the box.

He had a good arm, the man, even half-starved. He scooped up the walnut box in his good hand and pitched it across the market floor toward the corner grate, and Tib and Pim flung themselves after it, and the crows dove screaming, and the box hit the grate's edge, teetered, and dropped through into the dark below — down to where Old Borin waited with his flame.

"Yes!" Pim shrieked.

But the crows were faster than toys. Two of them peeled off and shot down through the grate after the box. The third came at Tib and Pim where they stood at the grate's edge, and there was nothing to do but go down after the others, into the dark, into the chase.

They dropped through. Tib caught a cog-tooth and swung, Pim clinging to him, and below them in the lamplight he saw Borin — vast, green, slow — reach out one great brass hand and close it around the falling box, gentle as catching a bird.

"I have it," Borin called up, his voice booming in the works. "Soldier! I have it! But the crows — "

The crows were on them. They were made for tunnels as much as sky, hooked and quick. One struck Borin's hand and worried at his fingers, trying to pry the box loose. The old automaton was strong but slow; he could not swat a thing so fast.

"Up!" Tib shouted. "Pim, up — the hot-pipe — we lead them off the box!"

He didn't fully have a plan. He had the shape of one. The crows hunted by movement and by sound; if he and Pim made enough of both, the birds might leave the still box for the running toys. It was a terrible plan. It was the only one.

"Pim! Make your racket! Now!"

And Pim, brave Pim, planted his feet on the cog and crashed his cymbals together with all his strength, again and again, the sound ringing through the clockwork guts of the city like a fire-bell — and the crows turned from the box, and came for them.


Chapter 24: The Fall and the Catch

They ran along the hot-pipe with the crows behind them.

It was a nightmare of a run — the pipe round and slick beneath Tib's single boot, the heat shimmering up, the crows clattering close, their beaks snapping a hand's breadth from Pim's cymbals. Tib's plan, such as it was, held: the birds followed the noise and the motion, and behind them in the lamplight Borin tucked the box into a niche and stood guard over it, free.

But the pipe ran out over a chasm in the works — a black gap where a great gear turned far below — and there was a gap in the pipe where a section had been removed for repair years ago and never replaced. Tib saw it coming. Too late to stop.

"Jump!" he shouted, and jumped, and caught the far lip of the pipe, and hauled himself up — and turned to see Pim mistime it.

The monkey leapt, fell short, and caught the pipe's edge with one paw. His cymbals clashed once as the other arm flailed. His grip began to slip. Below him the great gear turned in the dark, patient, toothed.

"Tib!" Pim's painted grin was a horror over his terror. "Tib, I can't hold — "

Tib threw himself flat and reached down. Their hands met — tin to brass, soldier to monkey — and Tib's single boot found a rivet-head and braced, and he hauled, with everything that was in his small warm chest, while a crow dove at his back and raked his torn coat and shrieked.

He hauled Pim up. The monkey came over the lip and collapsed against him, both of them down, the crow wheeling away into the dark for another pass.

"I've got you," Tib gasped. "I've got you. I won't let go. One thing at a time, Pim, and the thing right now is that I have got you."

Pim clung to him, shaking. The crows circled, screamed, and — finding the noise stopped, the motion stilled, two small things gone quiet against a hot pipe in the dark — at last gave up, and rose, and were gone up through the grate into the night.

The works ticked on. Far off, Borin's lamp burned gold.

"We lost the crows," Tib said. "And Borin has the box. But that wasn't getting it home. That was just not losing it tonight."


Chapter 25: The Bell Tower Confession

Borin would not let them go on that night.

"You're bent, the both of you," he said, holding the box in the crook of his arm like a cat. "The monkey's spring is overwound from the racket, and you, soldier, you've a tear in you down to the tin. There's a place above the works, in the old bell tower, where it's dry and the city can't reach you. You'll rest. The box rests with me; no crow gets past me twice. And come morning we'll plan the long road to Sorrel's, for that's where it must go and where it must be won." He fixed Tib with his glass eyes. "Won't it."

"It will," Tib said. "Sorrel has it bought. She won't stop. The only way to keep the box from her is to take it where she can't follow — home."

So Borin carried them up through the works to a hollow at the base of the old bell tower, where a cracked bell hung silent and the wind came through a louvered window and the moon laid bars of light across the dusty floor. He set the box down, and set himself beside it, lamp lit, and the two small toys lay in the warmth of his flame.

Pim slept, or did the thing toys do that is like sleeping. Tib did not. He sat in the moonlight and looked at the silent bell.

"You don't rest," Borin observed, low.

"I never have," Tib said. "I think it's part of whatever I am." He was quiet a moment. "Can I tell you a thing, Borin? A thing I've never told anyone, because there's never been anyone to tell."

"Tell it."

"I want to dance." Tib said it to the moon-bars on the floor, not to the automaton. "Coppelia, the ballerina — she can dance, she was made to, and she won't, she's afraid. And I can't. I've got one leg. The old man never cast the other. I've stood on his shelf for years watching the music box play that waltz, and every time it plays I think — if I had two legs I would dance. I would dance to it. And I never can." He laughed, small and tinny. "It's a foolish thing to want, for a soldier."

Borin was quiet a long time. The flame burned.

"It's not foolish," he said at last. "Wanting to dance is just wanting to be more than what they made you. I lit lamps for ninety years and wanted, the whole time, to see the sun come up over a city I'd kept lit through the night. Never did. Always went dark before the dawn." His gears creaked. "Wanting more than your making — that's not foolish, soldier. That's the most alive thing there is."


Chapter 26: Soot Returns, Wounded

In the gray before dawn, something came up into the bell tower.

Tib heard it first — a dragging, an uneven scrabble on the stair, a low sound that was half growl and half something worse. He was on his feet at once, hop-stepping between the sound and the sleeping Pim, between the sound and the box.

It was the cat. Soot, the black tom with the torn ear — Tib knew it at once, those green lamp-eyes. But the cat that came up into the moonlit tower was not the smooth killer that had hunted them in the street. It came in low, dragging a back leg, and there was blood matting the fur of its flank, dark and frozen, and one eye was swollen half-shut.

It saw them. It stopped. It did not spring.

Tib stood his ground, small and ready, though there was nothing he could do against a cat even a wounded one. "We've nothing for you," he said. "Go on. Go on, then."

The cat made a sound. Not a yowl. A low broken cry, the sound of an animal in pain and fear, and it sank down against the wall, panting, the green eyes dimming, and Tib understood that the cat had not come up here to hunt. It had come up here to die where it was dry.

"Something's hurt it," Tib said slowly. "Something bigger than us. Bigger than it." He looked at the wounds — long, raking gouges, the marks of hooked wire. "Borin. Those are crow-marks. Sorrel's crows did this."

Borin's lamp lifted. "She's loosed the whole flock, then. Hunting through the city for the box. The cat got in their way."

The cat watched them with its one good eye. It was too spent to be a danger. Tib looked at it — at the torn ear, the frozen blood, the heaving sides — and he thought of how it had hunted them, and how it was only doing what cats do, and how it was now broken by the same enemy they were.

He crossed to it. Pim woke and made a strangled sound — "Tib, no, it'll — " — but the cat only watched, too far gone to strike.

Tib put his small warm tin hand against the cat's heaving flank, over the cold wound, the way he'd warmed Pim's frozen joint. And he held it there.


Chapter 27: An Unlikely Truce

The cat did not die.

Tib could not mend a living thing the way he'd freed a frozen gear — but his warmth was warmth, and warmth was what the freezing animal lacked. He sat against its flank through the last of the dark, his hand over the wound, and Pim, after a long while, crept close and warmed the cat's other side, and even Borin set his lamp near so the small flame's heat could reach.

By the gray dawn the cat's breathing had eased. The green eyes opened, both of them now, the swelling gone down. It lifted its head and looked at the small creatures pressed against it — the toys it had hunted — and Tib braced himself, because now it was strong enough to do what cats do.

It did not. It put out a rough pink tongue and rasped it once, clumsily, across Tib's tin head, the way a cat washes a kitten.

"Well," said Pim, faintly. "Well, I never."

"You came up here to die," Tib said to the cat, "and we warmed you instead. I don't think you've got the words for it, but I think you understand it. We're after the same thing you ran from. The crows. Their mistress. And we've a long way to carry a heavy box across a city of giants, and we're toys, and you're a cat that knows every alley in Vellmark."

The cat's tail twitched. It got up, tested its back leg, found it would hold.

"I don't suppose," Tib said, "you'd carry us."

It took a deal of doing. The cat would not be told; it had to be asked, and shown, and asked again. But in the end Tib climbed onto its back, between the shoulder blades, gripping the fur, and Pim climbed up behind him, and Borin lashed the walnut box across the cat's haunches with Coppelia's silk thread, snug and secure.

"Soot," Tib said, naming it, because a friend wants a name. "We'll call you Soot. Take us across the city, Soot. To Sorrel's house. We'll deal her trouble, and you'll have your own back on the crows that marked you."

The cat — Soot — flicked its torn ear, and turned, and carried them down out of the bell tower into the waking city, fast and low and sure, in a way no toy could ever go.


Chapter 28: The Frostbound Mansion

Soot ran the secret roads of the city — the alley-tops, the wall-ledges, the snowed roofs where no person walked — and carried them in an hour across a distance that would have cost them days.

Borin could not come. He had said his goodbye in the tower, pressing the box's safe-lashing one last time, lamp lifted. "I can't go up into the streets, soldier. But I'll keep my flame for you, below the market, and if you come back this way with the box, I'll light you home through the dark. Go on. Go and win it." And the old green automaton had watched them out of sight, the small steady flame burning in the dawn.

Now the city fell away beneath Soot's running feet, roof to roof, and the great clock in the square struck seven, and at last the cat slowed and crouched on the ridge of a high wall, and below them, across a frozen garden, stood the house of Madame Sorrel.

It was a tall thin house of black stone, ringed by an iron fence whose every spike was tipped with frost. Its windows were dark but for one, high up, where a cold blue light burned. And all over it — on the gutters, the sills, the chimney pots — sat the brass crows, dozens of them, still as ornaments, glass eyes catching the gray dawn.

From somewhere inside, faint through the stone, came a ticking. Not one clock. Many. A house full of clocks, ticking out of time with one another, a dry insect murmur that set Tib's spring on edge.

"That's where the stolen memories go," he said. "Listen. Every clock in there is somebody's life, wound into brass."

Pim shivered. "How do we get in past all those crows?"

"We don't go in past them," Tib said. He was looking at the frozen garden, at a low grating where warm air breathed out faintly into the cold — a cellar vent, a way under. "We go in under them. The same as we go everywhere. Underneath, in the dark, one thing at a time." He patted the cat's neck. "Set us down soft, Soot. And keep clear of the crows. We'll call for you when we come out."

The cat carried them down the inside of the wall, into the shadow of the frostbound house, to the dark mouth of the vent.


Chapter 29: The House of Stolen Time

Inside, the house ticked like a nest of insects.

Tib and Pim came up through the cellar vent into a low brick room, and from there up a coal-chute into the house proper, and what they found there stopped them both at the doorway.

A long gallery ran the length of the house, and both walls of it, floor to ceiling, were shelves, and every shelf was full of clocks. Mantel clocks, carriage clocks, music boxes, automata frozen in glass cases. And each one ticked or turned or murmured a thread of sound — a few notes of a song, a fragment of a voice, a sigh — and Tib understood that Borin had told it true. These were not clocks. They were people. Lives wound into brass and slowly, slowly running down, while their warmth bled into the house and its mistress.

"Listen to them," Pim whispered, appalled. "They're all — they're all saying things."

They were. Tib went close to one, a little gilt carriage clock, and from inside it came a woman's voice, very faint, repeating the same half-sentence over and over as the mechanism turned: "—and the apples were so red that year, so red, so—" and then a click, and the cylinder reset, and it began again. A life worn down to a single bright shred, played and replayed until even the shred would go.

"This is what she'd do to the old man's box," Tib said. His voice was low and hard. "Wind it down to one note. Then nothing." He turned from the shelf. "We don't have time to free them all. I wish to every bolt we did. But we have one box to find, and it's still on Soot's back outside, and what we're here for is to learn the house and find the way out before we bring the box in. Come on. Quiet now."

They went down the gallery in the shadow of the shelves. And as they went, Tib heard, threading up faint through all the other ticking, a tune he knew to his last rivet.

Anselm's waltz.

But the box was outside, on the cat. The waltz was coming from somewhere deep in the house.

"Pim," Tib breathed. "She's got another one. Another box of his. Or — " A cold thought. "Or she took a piece of him already, before. Listen. That's his tune. That's the old man's waltz, playing in this house, and it isn't our box."


Chapter 30: The Waltz in the Dark

Tib followed the waltz.

It led them up — up a service stair built for servants long gone, the steps each a cliff to climb, Coppelia's thread and Pim's strong arms hauling Tib's one-legged weight from tread to tread. The tune grew clearer as they climbed. It was Anselm's waltz, unmistakable, but slower than the old man had ever played it, dragging, as if the spring that drove it were almost spent.

They came out into an upper room, the room with the cold blue light. And there, on a table, under a glass dome, sat a music box.

It was not the walnut box. It was older, smaller, its case black with age — but when Tib crept close and looked through the glass, he saw the same brass cylinder, the same comb of teeth, and he knew the tune they played as he knew his own name.

"It's the same waltz," Pim whispered. "How can it be the same waltz in two boxes?"

Tib stared at the old black box turning under its dome, playing the old man's tune almost to a stop.

"Because it's the same memory," he said slowly. "Don't you see? This is where the waltz came from. The old man's mother played it. He said — that night — he said his mother played it, on a fiddle or a box, and he wasn't sure which. Borin said Sorrel's been at this for years, draining memory-works." He pressed his tin hands to the cold glass. "This is the old man's first memory-box. The one his mother made, maybe, or the one his life was first wound into when he was young. Sorrel took it years ago. She's been winding it down all this time. That's why he's been forgetting even before the thief came. The forgetting started here, in this house, long ago."

Pim's painted grin trembled. "Then there's two boxes. The old one she's almost finished, and the new one outside on the cat."

"Two halves of the same man," Tib said. He looked at the dragging, failing tune under the dome. "And if I don't get both of them home, he'll never be whole again."

The cold blue light flickered. And behind them, from the dark of the doorway, a voice spoke — low, elegant, unhurried.

"How clever," said Madame Sorrel. "A toy that reasons. I have wanted one of those for such a very long time."


Chapter 31: The Clockwork Hound

She filled the doorway, tall and narrow, in a gown the color of a winter dusk. Her face was smooth and ageless and her eyes were the pale gray of ice over deep water. In one hand she held a small silver key.

"Don't run," she said pleasantly. "Mind the dog."

Out of the dark beside her came the hound.

It was clockwork, like everything in the house — a low brass thing on four jointed legs, with no eyes at all, only a wide brass cone of an ear that swiveled toward the smallest sound. Where a living dog would have sniffed, this one listened. It turned its blind brass head toward Tib and Pim, and its ear locked onto them, and a low whir came out of it like a growl.

"It hunts by sound," Sorrel said, watching them with mild interest. "Quite deaf to everything but movement-noise and voices. Stand perfectly still and silent, and it cannot find you. Twitch a single gear, and it will have you in its jaws. I find it teaches my little acquisitions to be quiet." She smiled. "And I do so prize quiet."

Tib froze. Beside him Pim froze too, mid-step, one cymbal raised, his whole frame rigid with the effort of stillness.

The hound's ear swiveled. It came forward, a step, two, its jointed legs ticking, the brass cone questing left and right. It passed within a hand's breadth of Tib. He did not move. He did not let the smallest spring shift. He was a soldier; standing still was the one thing he had practiced all his life, on a shelf, watching an old man he loved.

The hound passed. Its ear found the doorway, the stairs, the small sounds of the house, and lost them in the general ticking.

"Hm," said Sorrel, almost disappointed. "Disciplined. Most things squeal." She crossed to the table and laid her hand on the glass dome over the old black box, where the waltz dragged toward its end. "You came for this, I think. Or for its twin — yes, I know about the twin; my crows lost it in the works last night, but my crows are patient and the city is not large. Both halves of one tedious old toymaker." Her pale eyes turned on Tib. "Why? What is a one-legged soldier to him, or he to a toy?"

Tib stayed silent. The hound's ear twitched toward Sorrel's voice and away.

"You may speak," Sorrel said. "Softly. The hound knows my voice; it will forgive a quiet word beneath it." She tilted her head. "Go on. I am genuinely curious. It has been a long time since anything in this house surprised me."


Chapter 32: Coppelia, Brave at Last

Tib spoke, low and even, beneath the ticking of the house.

"He made me," he said. "He set me down gentle every night for years. That's all. That's the whole of it. He's mine the way you're your own — and you'd cross a city for yourself, wouldn't you. So would I, for him."

Sorrel considered this. "Loyalty," she said, as if naming a rare specimen. "In a toy. He built better than he knew, your toymaker." She turned the silver key over in her long fingers. "I think I shall keep you. Not for parts. As a curiosity. A toy that loves — I have memories of love in a hundred boxes here, but never the loving thing itself, awake and walking. You'll go in a glass case, soldier, and I'll wind you down slowly, over years, and study how it fades."

"You'll have to catch me first," Tib said.

"The hound has already caught you. You simply haven't moved yet."

And that was true, and it was the trap of it: the moment Tib ran, the hound would hear, and have him. He stood frozen, and Pim stood frozen, and the waltz dragged toward silence under the dome, and Tib's mind went round and round for a way out and found only walls.

Then a window latch clicked.

Across the room, the casement swung inward on the cold dawn, and a small white shape climbed over the sill — a stiff white skirt, a pointed foot, arms that had been curved over a head for a year and were now, awkwardly, pushing open a window.

Coppelia.

She had come. She had crossed the whole city she was so afraid of, climbed the frostbound house past the crows, and now she stood swaying on the sill, trembling head to foot, terrified past speaking — but there.

"Soldier," she got out, in a whisper Tib barely heard. "I could not — I could not let you go alone. Not all the way. I am still afraid. But I am here."

The hound's ear snapped toward the window and the new voice.

And Madame Sorrel turned, and saw the clockwork ballerina on her sill, and her pale eyes lit with a collector's hunger.

"Oh," she breathed. "Oh, and a dancer, too. A matched set. How the morning improves."


Chapter 33: Caught

It happened too fast for Tib to stop.

Sorrel moved with a speed nothing so elegant should have had. She crossed the room in three steps and her long hand closed around Coppelia on the sill before the ballerina could so much as flinch. Coppelia cried out — a small bell-clear sound of pure terror — and the hound's ear locked onto it, and the brass dog lunged toward the cry.

"Still!" Tib hissed at Pim, and they held, frozen, while the hound passed them again, hunting Coppelia's cry, while Sorrel lifted the struggling ballerina up before her pale eyes.

"There, there," Sorrel murmured to Coppelia, as one soothes a caught bird. "Don't struggle. You'll bend. I should hate for you to bend; you're far too fine." She carried her to an empty glass case among the shelves — there were several empty, Tib saw now, waiting — and set her inside, and shut the glass, and turned a small key in its base. The case began, faintly, to tick. "There. You'll dance for me when I wind you, and weep when I let you run down, and it will be very beautiful, and I shall watch it for years."

Coppelia stood inside the glass, frozen in her unfinished turn, her painted eyes wide and brimming. Through the case she found Tib, and her look was not a plea. It was an apology. I tried, the look said. I came, and it was not enough, and now I have made it worse.

"Now," said Sorrel, turning back, the silver key in her hand. "The soldier. And the monkey. Where have they — ah." Her gaze settled on the two frozen toys. "Still playing dead. The hound will flush you eventually. Or I shall simply wait. I have a great deal of time, you understand. That is rather the point of me."

Tib stood frozen, his friend caged, the old man's waltz dying under glass, the brass hound circling, and the collector smiling.

And he thought, very clearly, beneath the panic: she has time, and we don't. So I must make her spend hers.


Chapter 34: The Soldier's Bargain

Tib spoke. He pitched his voice low under the ticking, the way Sorrel had shown him, and the hound only flicked its ear and let it pass.

"You want a toy that lives," he said. "Awake and walking. You said so."

Sorrel went still. "I do."

"Then here's my bargain. Let the ballerina go. Let the music box go — both halves, the old one under the dome and the new one when your crows bring it. Let them go home to the toymaker. And in trade, I'll stay. Of my own will. The living toy you've wanted all these years, who'll go into your glass case and not run, not fight, and you can wind me down slow and study how the life fades, for as long as it takes." He stood very straight on his one leg. "One living soldier, freely given, for two boxes and a dancer. That's a rich trade, Madame. Richer than you'll ever get again. There's only one of me."

The room ticked. Coppelia, in her case, made a small sound — "No, Tib, no" — and the hound's ear twitched toward it and away.

Sorrel looked at the soldier for a long moment, and something almost like respect moved in her ice-pale eyes.

"You'd do it," she said. "You'd truly do it. Spend your own little life to buy back an old man's." She came closer, crouched, brought her smooth ageless face down to his level. "Do you know, in all my years, no one has ever offered me a thing freely. They've only ever tried to keep what I came to take." She studied him. "Yes. I accept. The soldier, freely given, for the dancer and the two boxes."

She held out her long hand to seal it.

And Tib, who had learned the whole shape of her in an hour, knew with cold certainty that she was lying. She would take him, and keep the boxes too, and the dancer, and the monkey, because that was what she was: a thing that took, and never gave back, and could not.

But the bargain bought him one thing she could not lie her way out of. It bought him her attention, fixed entirely on him, her hand outstretched, her hound at ease.

It bought him a moment when no one in the room was watching the window.


Chapter 35: A Promise Meant to Be Broken

Tib reached up to take Sorrel's hand and seal the false bargain.

And as he did, he said — clearly, not low, but full and ringing, the first loud word he'd spoken in that house — "Pim. NOW."

Three things happened at once.

The hound's ear snapped toward Tib's shout, and the brass dog lunged at him.

Pim, who had stood frozen and waiting for exactly this, who had understood his soldier without a word, crashed his cymbals together with every ounce of his wound-up strength — a vast brazen CLANG that filled the room and shattered against the walls, an explosion of pure noise.

And the clockwork hound, whose whole self was that one great listening ear, took the full force of the cymbal-crash directly into its brass cone — and the delicate listening-mechanism inside it, tuned to hear a toy's smallest tick, could not bear the blast. Something burst inside the hound's head with a tinny shriek. It staggered. Its ear spun wildly, hearing everything and nothing, and it ran in a blind circle, snapping at the air, deaf and lost.

"My hound!" Sorrel cried, whirling.

Tib was already moving. He snatched his hand back from hers and hop-flung himself across the table to the glass dome over the old black box, and threw his whole small weight against it. The dome rocked. Pim leapt to help, and together they tipped it, and it slid off the table and shattered on the floor, and the old black music box stood free in the wreck of glass, the waltz still dragging, faint, alive.

"Pim, the box!" Tib shouted. "Carry it! I'll get Coppelia!"

The house had come awake around them. Far off, on the sills and gutters, the brass crows stirred and lifted their heads.


Chapter 36: The Ragged Man Returns

Tib reached Coppelia's glass case and hammered on it with both fists. It would not give; the glass was thick, the lock turned tight.

"The key!" Coppelia cried from within. "She has the key — the silver — "

But Sorrel had recovered. She turned from her staggering hound, and her face was no longer elegant; it was the bare cold thing beneath the elegance, and she came at Tib with the silver key clenched like a blade.

"Ungrateful little machine," she said. "I'll wind you down tonight."

And then the door of the room burst open, and the ragged man stood in it.

He was gaunt and gray and shaking, his brass hand twitching, his rags frozen — but he had come. He had followed them across the city, through guilt, up into the very house of the woman who frightened all of Vellmark.

"Sorrel," he said. His cracked voice shook, but he said her name. "You don't remember me. Of course you don't. You've got my remembering in a box on one of these shelves." He took a step into the room. "Twelve years ago. A young mender, a good one, with two good hands and a wife. You bought my memory-work off a debt I couldn't pay, and you wound it down, and you took her face away from me. I can't remember my own wife's face. I've a brass hand because I let a press take the flesh one, I was so far gone in the forgetting I didn't feel it close." His gray eyes were wet and furious. "I stole the old man's box because I'm a wreck you made. And I followed it here because I'm done being a wreck. Give them back. Give us all back."

Sorrel stared at him. For the first time, something crossed her face that was not control. "I take many boxes," she said. "I cannot be expected to—"

"No," the ragged man said. "You can't. That's the whole of you, isn't it." And he came at her, brass hand and all.


Chapter 37: What Sorrel Took

The ragged man was no fighter, and Sorrel was strong and quick, but he was desperate and large, and for a moment they grappled in the cold blue light while the hound ran blind and the crows gathered at the windows.

"The cases!" the ragged man shouted, holding Sorrel's wrist with his good hand, her silver key trapped between them. "The keys are kept under the cases — turn them widdershins — free them, free all of them — "

Tib understood. He dropped to the base of Coppelia's glass case. There, set in the brass foot, was a small keyhole, and a little winding key left in it. He gripped it with both hands and turned it backward — widdershins, against the wind — and the case's lock sprang, and the glass swung open, and Coppelia stumbled out into his arms.

"You came for me," she said, dazed. "You bargained yourself for me."

"It was a lie to buy a moment," Tib said. "Don't waste it. Pim's got the old box. Help him." Then, louder, to the room of shelves, to all the ticking caged lives: "There's a key in every case! Turn it backward! The wound-down ones can't free themselves — but the ones that can move, free the rest!"

And here and there along the great gallery, in cases Sorrel had thought long quiet, things stirred. A one-armed dancer. A drummer with a stove-in side. The salvaged, the bought-for-parts, the half-drained — those with movement left in them reached for their own case-keys, or for their neighbors', and one by one the locks began to spring, all down the murmuring hall.

Sorrel saw her collection waking and twisting free, and she made a sound of pure rage and threw the ragged man off her and lunged for the great clock at the end of the gallery — the tall black clock that was, Tib saw now, the heart of the house, the master mainspring into which all the stolen warmth had bled for years.

"If I cannot keep you," she said, "I will stop you. All of you. At once."

And she seized the great key in the clock's face and began to wind.


Chapter 38: Freeing the Dancers

Tib did not know what Sorrel's winding would do, but he knew it would be terrible, and he knew it would be soon.

"Everyone out!" he shouted down the gallery. "The window — the sill — there's a cat below, a black cat, he's a friend, he'll carry the small ones! Go, go now, don't wait to be free, free yourselves on the way!"

The gallery was chaos. The waked toys spilled from their cases — a dozen, two dozen, salvaged things that had been drained near to nothing, helping the ones still too weak to move, a one-armed dancer carrying a tiny seized music box under her good arm, the stove-in drummer kicking open a case with both feet. Pim staggered toward the window with the old black box clutched to his chest, near as big as he was, the waltz inside it ticking faint and slow.

"Soot!" Tib shouted out the window into the gray dawn. "Soot, to me!"

And the black cat came up the wall and over the sill in one long pour of muscle, and crouched in the window, green eyes taking in the wild scene, the crows massing on the gutters, the small bright things streaming toward him.

"Carry them down," Tib told the cat, gripping its torn ear, looking into the green eyes, trusting it to understand more than words. "All you can. The little ones first. Down to the wall, and wait."

Soot understood. The cat could not speak, but it lowered itself, and the small toys clambered onto its back, clinging to the black fur, and it bore them down the inside of the wall to safety and came back, and again, ferrying the freed across the deadly garden while the crows shrieked above.

Coppelia did not flee. She stood in the gallery, in her stiff white skirt, and helped the slowest of the drained dancers toward the window — a thing she would never have done a day ago, the fine ballerina lending her arm to the broken ones.

"You see," she said to Tib, almost laughing, almost weeping, "I am not so very afraid after all, when there's someone worse off than me to be afraid for."


Chapter 39: The Hound's Last Charge

Sorrel wound the great clock, and the house began to change.

A deep groan came up through the floor. The ticking of all the lesser clocks faltered, then surged — Tib felt it in his own spring, a wrongness, a pull, as if the great mainspring were trying to draw the life out of everything wound, all at once, into itself. The freed toys cried out and stumbled. Pim sagged under the old black box, his movements going syrup-slow.

"She's draining us," the ragged man gasped, on his knees now, the pull worst on him, the most drained of them all. "The big clock — it's pulling us all down at once — she'll wind us all to nothing and keep it for herself — "

The blind clockwork hound, still running mad and deaf, charged through the gallery — and by pure chance its blind charge took it straight at Sorrel where she stood winding, the one moving thing it could feel through the floor. It struck her legs. She staggered. The winding stopped for one breath.

Tib seized the breath.

"Pim! The box — give it to Coppelia — get out the window!" He hop-flung himself across the gallery, not away from Sorrel but toward her, toward the great clock. "The ragged man's right — it's the great clock — we have to stop the spring!"

He reached the foot of the towering clock as Sorrel kicked the blind hound away and turned back to her winding. And Tib, small and one-legged and entirely without a plan but the shape of one, did the only thing a tin soldier could do against a mainspring the size of a wheel: he jammed himself into it.

He thrust his own tin leg — his one good leg, his only leg — into the gap between the great gear-teeth, into the throat of the winding mechanism, and braced his arms against the frame, and held.

The gear came round and met him, and the soft tin of his leg crushed and folded — it did not break, tin folds before it breaks — and folded thick into the teeth and jammed them, and the great mainspring, wound near to bursting, ground to a shuddering halt against the body of a one-legged soldier.


Chapter 40: The Mainspring Jammed

The clock screamed.

The mainspring, wound to its limit and now stopped against Tib's crushed leg, strained and shrieked, the whole great clock shuddering on its base. The pull on the wound lives in the house cut off all at once. The freed toys gasped and steadied. Pim's movements eased. Even the ragged man drew a long breath and got to his feet.

But Tib was caught.

His leg was folded into the teeth, jammed solid, and the mainspring's pent force ground against him without let. He could not pull free. If the jam gave — if his leg tore loose or the tin sheared — the spring would let go all at once and the clock would do its draining work in a single instant.

Sorrel stood over him, the silver key still in her hand, staring down at the soldier jammed in the throat of her great machine. For the first time she looked, not cold, but old — as if without the slow theft of the clock she could already feel the years she'd cheated beginning to find her.

"You little fool," she said, very quietly. "You've jammed it with your own body. The instant you tear free, it lets go. You can't hold it forever. Tin fatigues. And the moment you fail, I have everything, and you have nothing." She crouched. "Or I could simply pull you out myself."

"You could," Tib said. His voice was steady, though the strain of the spring sang through his whole frame. "But the second you touch this clock, the ragged man's at your back, and the cat's at the window, and two dozen things you drained are free and angry and between you and the door. You've spent a lifetime taking, Madame. You never learned to do it with a roomful of witnesses who've got nothing left to lose."

Sorrel's pale eyes flicked to the gallery — to the freed toys gathered now, to the ragged man standing with his brass hand raised, to the green-eyed cat in the window, to Coppelia with the old black box held high.

"Pim," Tib said, not loud, not taking his eyes from Sorrel. "Is everyone down but us?"

"Everyone, Tib," Pim called from the sill. "Everyone but you and the box and me."

"Then go," Tib said. "Take the old box. Get it on the cat with the new one when the crows bring it — no. Wait." His jammed leg sang. A new thought, cold and clear. "The new box. Sorrel's crows still have to bring it. Where would they bring it?"


Chapter 41: Escape Across the Rooftops

"Here," Sorrel said, and laughed, a thin sound. "They bring everything here. To me. Your second box is on its way to this house even now, in the feet of my crows. You've freed my whole collection, soldier, and your prize is flying straight into my hands."

It was the truth, and it was the trap closing, and Tib had jammed himself into a clock with no way out and the second half of the old man still in the air over the city.

The ragged man crossed to the clock. "Let me," he said to Tib, low. "Your leg's the jam. If something else jams it — something that won't tear free when you climb out — " He looked at his own brass hand, the trembling mechanical hand that had cost him his flesh and his wife's face. "I've been wanting to be rid of this thing twelve years. Let it do one good turn."

And before Tib could answer, the ragged man thrust his brass hand into the gear beside Tib's leg, deep into the teeth, and braced it, and with his good hand worked Tib's crushed leg free of the mechanism. The brass hand held where the tin had held — wedged, locked, immovable, the spring grinding against it now instead of against the soldier.

"Go," the ragged man said through his teeth. "I'll hold it. A brass hand doesn't fatigue. Get the boxes home. Both of them. Tell the old toymaker — " he stopped. "No. Don't tell him anything. He doesn't know me. Just get him back his life. That's enough. That's the thing I came to do."

"Come with us," Tib said. "When we've the box. We'll come back for you."

"You won't get the chance, and we both know it." The man almost smiled. "Crows are coming. Go."

Tib went. There was no time for more, and the man had bought it for him. Pim caught Tib up, and Coppelia had the old box, and they went out the window onto the sill where Soot waited, and behind them Madame Sorrel screamed her rage at her jammed clock and her broken collection and the brass-handed wreck who held her ruin shut with the price of his own twelve years.


Chapter 42: Soot Holds the Line

They came down the wall into the garden, and the crows came down on them.

The whole brass flock, dozens of them, dropped out of the gray dawn with their hooked feet and shearing beaks, and they came not for the toys but for the old black box in Coppelia's arms — Sorrel's property, stolen back, to be retrieved at any cost.

"Get to the gate!" Tib shouted. "Soot — the box — guard the box!"

The cat understood. Soot put himself between the crows and the box, and the wounded tom who had limped into the bell tower to die became, in the gray garden, a black fury. He reared and struck, claws raking brass, and a crow came apart in the snow in a clatter of gears. He spun and caught another out of the air. The crows had marked him before, had driven him bleeding through the city; now he paid them back with interest, and the snow filled with broken brass birds.

But there were so many. They came in waves, and for every one Soot tore from the air two more dove at the box, and one got its hooked feet on the case and wrenched, and Coppelia would not let go, and was dragged off her feet across the snow.

Tib hop-flung himself onto the crow's back and drove his crushed tin leg — folded now to a blunt club — down into the joint of its wing, again, again, until the joint gave and the wing locked and the crow tumbled, and Coppelia tore the box free and clutched it and ran for the gate.

Soot held the line behind them, a black shape ringed in broken birds, buying them the gate, the wall, the street beyond. And when Tib looked back from the gate, the cat broke off and came after them in three long bounds, bleeding from a dozen new cuts, ragged ear flat, green eyes bright — and unbeaten.

"Up!" Tib gasped, and they climbed onto the cat's back, all of them, the old box lashed tight, and Soot carried them out of the garden of Madame Sorrel into the waking streets, leaving the brass crows wheeling and screaming over a house with a jammed heart.


Chapter 43: The Ragged Man Stays Behind

They did not have the new box.

Tib made himself say it, clinging to Soot's neck as the cat ran the rooftops. "We've the old box, the first one. But the new one — the walnut box, the one the thief took — Sorrel's crows still had it, flying it to the house. We never got it. We've only half of him."

"Then we've half of him and we're alive to get the rest," Pim said. "Which is more than we had an hour ago."

But Tib looked back across the roofs toward Sorrel's house, small now in the gray distance, the crows a dark smudge above it — and he thought of the ragged man with his brass hand jammed in the clock, holding the great spring shut, alone in that house full of stolen time.

"The crows will go back to the house," Tib said slowly. "They always go back to the house. They'll bring the walnut box there, to Sorrel — but Sorrel's clock is jammed, and the ragged man's holding the jam. As long as he holds it, the crows have nowhere to deliver. She can't wind it down. He's holding the whole house still."

"He can't hold it forever," Coppelia said softly. "He said so himself. A brass hand doesn't fatigue, but the rest of him will. And when he lets go—"

"When he lets go," Tib said, "the spring lets go, and the house finishes its draining, and everything Sorrel ever took is lost at once, the old man's new box with it." He gripped Soot's fur. "But while he holds it, we've a chance. He's holding the door open for us. He stayed behind to hold it open." Tib looked at his friends — the brave monkey, the no-longer-cowardly dancer, the bleeding cat. "I have to go back."

"Tib — "

"Not all of you. You take the old box home to Anselm — get half of him back, the surest half, the part we already have in hand. Coppelia knows the way; Borin will light you through the works. But the new box is in that house, and a man is holding the house still so that someone can fetch it, and I will not let that someone be no one."


Chapter 44: The Great Clock Chimes Dawn

They argued, briefly, on a snowy ridge while the cat panted beneath them. But Tib had decided, and when Tib had decided, the deciding was done.

"Take the old box home," he told Coppelia, settling it into her arms. "Wind it for him, three turns, no more, the way he always did — he'll have taught you the way of it, watching all those years through the window. Don't wind it down. It's half his life; spend it gentle." He turned to Pim. "Go with her. Make your racket if anything comes near. You saved us all with that racket, Pim. You're the bravest noise in Vellmark."

Pim's painted grin wavered. "You're coming back. Say you're coming back."

"I'm coming back," Tib said. "With the other half. And then we'll all of us go home together."

He did not know if it was true. But he had learned, somewhere between the shelf and the rooftops, that you say the true thing you mean to make true, and then you go and make it.

The great clock in the square began to strike.

It was dawn — real dawn, the first gray light breaking cold over Vellmark, and the city's great clock, the one Tib had heard every hour of his secret life from his shelf, struck the hour slow and deep, and the sound rolled out over the snowed roofs and the frozen river and the waking streets, and every lesser clock in the city seemed to still a moment to let it pass.

"That's Borin's hour," Tib said, listening. "He kept the city lit through ninety winters of nights and never saw the sun come up. Tell him — when you pass him in the works — tell him a soldier saw it for him. Tell him the dawn came up gray and the great clock rang it in, and it was worth keeping the dark for."

Then he slid down off the cat, alone, onto the snow, a small blue soldier with one folded leg, and turned back toward the house of Madame Sorrel, while his friends carried half an old man's life toward home.


Chapter 45: The Race for Home

Coppelia and Pim rode Soot down through the city as the dawn broke, the old black music box lashed and safe between them, and they did not stop, and they did not look back, because Tib had told them not to, and because looking back would have broken them.

Soot ran as the wounded run when there is something worth the wound — flat out, bleeding, sure. The cat carried them off the high roads down into the works through a coal-cellar Borin had shown them, and there in the gold of his lamp the old automaton was waiting, exactly as he'd promised.

"Half," Borin said, when they told him, his glass eyes turning toward the empty place where a one-legged soldier should have been. "He's gone back for the other half. Alone."

"He told me to tell you," Coppelia said, "that he saw the dawn. For you. The great clock rang it in, and it was worth keeping the dark for."

The old lamplighter automaton was silent a long moment in the ticking dark. The little flame in his lamp stood very straight.

"Did he now," Borin said at last, and his rusty voice had gone strange. "Did he say that." He took up his lamp and his great green frame creaked upright. "Then I'll light him home, and the dawn with him. Come. The works run quick at this hour, the gears all turning for the morning — there are roads through here that open only on the hour and shut on the quarter. We'll have your old man's half home before the tune runs down. Quickly. Carry the box gentle. It's a life, and it's half spent, and it's almost home."

He led them down into the turning heart of the city, lamp held high, and Coppelia carried the box, and Pim walked guard, and behind them the cat came limping, and ahead the long road home unrolled through the clockwork dark toward a workshop on Candle Street where an old man sat dim and forgetting.


Chapter 46: The Faltering Tune

The walnut box was where the crows had dropped it, after all — not delivered, because the jammed clock and the brass-handed man had given them no master to deliver to. Tib found it in the snow of Sorrel's garden, fallen from a crow's grip when Soot had broken the flock, half-buried, its lid sprung, its bent cylinder turning, turning, the new box paying out the old man's life into the empty cold of dawn.

He had crept back through the gate, past the wheeling crows now leaderless and confused, into the still and groaning house, and he had found the ragged man at the great clock, brass hand jammed in the gear, holding, his gray face slack with effort, alive.

"You came back," the man breathed. "You fool. You actual fool. I'd have died holding this so you wouldn't have to."

"I know," Tib said. "That's why I came back. The walnut box — it's in the garden, in the snow, winding itself out into nothing. Hold the clock one minute more. Just one."

He went and found the box and dragged it — too heavy to lift, but he could drag it, an inch at a time, his folded leg a useless club, his arms screaming — dragged it into the house, to the foot of the great clock, beside the man who held the spring.

The walnut box's tune was nearly gone. The cylinder turned slow and slower, plucking out the waltz one halting note at a time, with long silences between, the spring almost run down, the old man's newest memories nearly spent into the air.

"It's almost finished," Tib said, looking at it. "His new memories — the last years, us, the workshop — they're almost gone. If I can't get it home and wind it before the spring stops, the part of him that knows me will be — " He could not finish.

"Then don't talk," the ragged man said. "Run. I'll hold the clock till my arm comes off. Go. GO."


Chapter 47: A Workshop Gone Quiet

On Candle Street, in the dim workshop, the old man sat where he had sat for days.

Anselm Roe did not work anymore. He did not remember what the tools were for. He sat on his stool by the cold bench, in the gray dawn light, and his hands lay open and empty in his lap, and his eyes were quiet and far away and held nothing. The forgetting had nearly finished with him. He did not know the room, or the lathe, or his own name. He was a kind old emptiness in a chair.

The door opened.

It opened by no hand he could see — but had he been able to see, he would have seen a black cat slip in, and on its back a clockwork ballerina in a white skirt carrying a small black music box, and a wind-up monkey, and behind them, glimmering in the dark of the open door, the gold of a lamplighter's flame.

Coppelia climbed down. She crossed the workshop floor to the foot of the old man's stool, the box in her arms, and she looked up at the dim and empty face of the man who had made her — the man she had been too afraid to help, until a one-legged soldier had shamed her into courage.

"He doesn't see us," she said. "He can't. He's too far gone."

"Then we wind it where he can hear," Borin's deep voice came from the doorway, the old automaton too large to enter, his lamp reaching gold across the floor. "Wind it, dancer. Three turns. The way the soldier said. Three turns and no more."

Coppelia set the old black box on the floor in the bar of lamplight. She found the little key. And with her fine clockwork hands — the hands made for nothing but beauty, now doing the bravest plain work of their life — she turned the key. Three turns. No more.

She lifted the lid.


Chapter 48: The Music Box

The waltz came out into the quiet workshop.

It came out of the old black box, the first box, the mother's tune, the oldest part of him — and it filled the gray room, and the old man, who had sat empty and far away, went still in a new way. His open hands closed, a little. His head lifted.

"That tune," Anselm said. His voice was rusty with disuse. "I know that tune."

The waltz turned. The cylinder plucked the comb of teeth, note after note, and with each note something came back into the old man's emptied face — a flicker, a warmth, a name. His mother at a fiddle. A kitchen. A brother's hand in his, the two of them turning on a worn floor, small boys dancing to a tune their mother played.

"I had a brother," Anselm whispered, and this time he did not lose it. The memory took hold and stayed. "I had a brother, and a mother, and she played this, and we danced — " He pressed his hand to his mouth. Tears stood in his old eyes, but they were not the lost tears of the dim days; they knew their own reason now. "I'm Anselm Roe. I make toys. This is my workshop. I — I remember. I remember being a boy. How did — how is it playing — "

He looked down.

And he saw them. The lamplight had grown, or his eyes had cleared, and he saw the clockwork ballerina kneeling at his feet with her hand on the music box key, and the wind-up monkey beside her, and the black cat, and in the doorway the great green shape of an old lamplighter automaton holding up a lamp.

"Coppelia," the old man breathed, naming his creation. "You — you moved. You're winding it. You're — " His mind, returning to him, reached for the impossible and found it standing in his workshop in the gold of a stranger's lamp. "You're alive. You've been alive."

The door behind them all banged open on the cold.


Chapter 49: He Remembers

Tib came in dragging the walnut box.

He came hauling it over the threshold an inch at a time, the new box, the last years' box, its tune nearly stopped — and behind him, far behind, the city was waking to the news that Madame Sorrel's house had gone still and her crows scattered and her collection walked free, but Tib knew none of that yet and would not for hours. He knew only that he had a box to wind and a spring near dead and an old man who must not lose the part of him that held a soldier.

"Anselm!" he shouted — and stopped.

Because the old man was looking at him. Right at him. Seeing him.

"You," Anselm said. The walls of his memory had come back up, all of them, the old box's winding having opened the door for the new — and he looked at the small blue soldier with one folded leg dragging a walnut box across the floor, and his face broke open. "My soldier. My tin soldier. You went. You went out into the city. For me."

Tib stood very still, the way he had stood his whole life. But there was no hiding now, and no need.

"You're not supposed to know," Tib said. His tinny voice shook for the first time in the whole long night. "I made a rule. Never move while you watch. So you'd never know, so you'd never be afraid of me, so you'd just — have your soldier on your shelf, the way you wanted."

"Afraid of you." Anselm slid down off the stool, onto his old knees, on the cold floor, to bring his face level with the small one. "You crossed the city. You dragged my whole life home through the snow. I've made toys for fifty years and never once made one that — that loved." His old hand came down, trembling, and he touched the folded ruin of Tib's leg, where the soldier had jammed it into a clock to save a roomful of strangers. "What did you do to yourself. What did you spend, out there, for me."

"Wind the box," Tib said. "Please. The new one. It's almost out. Three turns. The part that knows me is in it, and it's nearly gone."

And Anselm Roe, who remembered now, took up the walnut box his soldier had dragged home, and turned the key.


Chapter 50: The Tin Soldier's Waltz

Spring came late to Vellmark that year, but it came.

The snow drew back off the roofs. The frozen fountains ran. The great clock in the square kept its hours, and far below it, in the works, an old lamplighter automaton named Borin sat by his flame and told the freed toys who sheltered with him about the morning a soldier saw the dawn for him.

The ragged man's name was Tomas. He had not died holding the clock; when the spring finally gave, with no one left in the house to drain, it had simply unwound itself harmlessly into the cold, and Sorrel — Sorrel, with her stolen years suddenly all coming due at once — had gone gray and stooped and old in a single night, an aged woman alone in a house of broken cases, harmless at last. Anselm took Tomas in. The toymaker could not give the man back his wife's face — that box was lost, wound down years ago to nothing — but he could give him work, and warmth, and a brass hand mended so it no longer trembled, and slowly Tomas began to make new memories to stand where the stolen ones had been.

Coppelia danced now. She danced in the workshop window where the best light fell, no longer afraid of being looked at, and the children who came with their broken toys pressed their faces to the glass to watch her turn. She had finished, at last, the turn she had held frozen for a year.

And Anselm Roe sat at his bench, in the long spring evenings, and cast a leg.

He took his time over it. He matched the tin, and shaped the boot, and painted it black and shining to mirror the other, and he fitted it to the small blue soldier with hands that no longer forgot. When it was done he stood Tib up on two legs for the first time in his life, on the round table by the window, beside the ballerina.

"There," Anselm said softly. "Now then."

He wound the music box. Three turns, no more. The waltz came out into the spring evening, the old man's tune, the one his mother played.

And the tin soldier, who had wanted only this, took the clockwork ballerina's hand, and on two good legs, in the gold of the lamplight, with the old man watching and remembering and glad — at last he danced.
