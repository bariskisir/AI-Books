Chapter 1: Summit Push

The headlamps cut tunnels through the dark. Tenzing moved first, his boots finding the ice by memory. Behind him, the line of climbers swayed like beads on a string. Two in the morning on K2. Air thin as a razor.

They had left Camp 4 in single file, the wind already working at their hoods. Russell complained about the pace. Pemba ignored him. Dr. Ashford checked her oxygen gauge twice before stepping onto the fixed line. Min-seo kept her eyes on Tenzing's boots and matched each step.

The Shoulder rose ahead, a blade of snow against a black sky. Tenzing paused at the anchor point and tested the previous rope. His left shoulder sent a spike of pain down his arm. He did not wince. He did not look back.

Behind them, the lights of Camp 4 grew smaller until they were indistinguishable from stars. Above them, the mountain waited. The wind carried the sound of ice settling, a deep groan that traveled through the rock. Tenzing started climbing again. There was nothing else to do. The mountain did not care about their plans or their fears. It was simply there, immense and cold, waiting to see what they were made of.

Chapter 2: The Bottleneck

The Bottleneck is a couloir of blue ice at 26,000 feet, steep enough that a falling body does not stop until the glacier. It is the only passage to the summit. Every climber on K2 must cross it.

Tenzing went first because that was his job. He placed his ice axe above his head and pulled. His shoulder protested. He set a new line of fixed rope through the anchors left by previous seasons. The ice was hard, the pick of his axe biting only shallow.

Behind him, Russell breathed in ragged gasps. Pemba moved with the economy of a man who had done this a hundred times. Ashford placed each tool with surgical precision. Min-seo climbed as though the mountain might forgive her if she was careful enough.

A serac the size of an apartment block hung above them. It had hung there for years, frozen in place by temperatures that never rose above minus twenty. But at this altitude nothing lasted forever. Tenzing looked up at it once and kept moving. The Bottleneck narrowed ahead, the walls closing in like a throat. He placed each tool with care, testing the hold before committing his weight. One mistake at this altitude and the fall would not stop until the glacier below. He had seen men fall. He had helped scrape the remains off the ice.

Chapter 3: First Light

Dawn came slowly, a gray seepage along the eastern horizon. The Karakoram range materialized from darkness: peaks stacked like teeth, ridges drawn in black ink. The altitude made everything flat and distant.

Tenzing stopped at a ledge no wider than a sidewalk. He took his ice axe and chopped a platform for the team to stand. One by one they came over the lip and collapsed against the slope.

Ashford removed her goggles and blinked at the view. Nanga Parbat floated on the southern horizon. Gasherbrum rose to the east. She had seen these peaks from airplanes, from books, from the hospital bed where her brother died. This was the first time she had seen them from a height that could kill her.

Min-seo was vomiting quietly into the snow. Russell was adjusting his regulator with thick fingers. Pemba shared a look with Tenzing. The look said: we are not fast enough.

Tenzing checked his watch. They were thirty minutes behind schedule. On an eight-thousand-meter peak, thirty minutes was the difference between summit and body bag. He signaled the team to move. On this mountain, delay was death. He had seen it happen more times than he could count.

Chapter 4: The Altitude

At 26,000 feet, the body drowns in air that contains no oxygen. Cells suffocate one by one. The brain swells. The lungs fill with fluid. Every climber knows the statistics. Every climber believes they will be the exception.

Ashford's regulator froze. She tapped it. Nothing. She exhaled and tried again. The intake valve was crusted with ice. She pulled her mask away from her face and the air hit her like a wall.

Tenzing saw her fumbling and was beside her in three steps. He took her regulator, put it in his mouth, breathed through it. The ice melted. He handed it back without a word.

She took it. Breathed. The world came back into focus.

She nodded. Tenzing was already moving again.

Min-seo watched the exchange and understood that she was in the company of people who would not let her die. She was not sure that made it better. The mountain was not interested in their loyalty to each other. The mountain would take them all if they made a single mistake. She had read about this moment in a hundred books but the books did not prepare her for the pressure in her skull, the way her thoughts came slow and syrupy, the quiet hum of her own mortality in every breath.

Chapter 5: Tenzing's Secret

Tenzing had fallen into a crevasse the previous spring on Nuptse. The fall was fifteen meters. He landed on his left shoulder and heard something tear. The sound was wet, like a boot pulled from mud. He climbed out, finished the route, and told no one.

At home in Khumbu, a doctor told him he had torn his rotator cuff. Surgery would take six months. Climbing would make it worse. Tenzing had clients booked. His family needed the money. He nodded at the doctor and booked a flight to Islamabad.

Now, at 26,000 feet, he placed his left hand on an ice screw and felt the joint shift. Pain dropped him to one knee. He stayed there for three seconds, breathing through the fire. Then he stood and drove the screw home.

Behind him, the team saw nothing. The secret was his alone. He had carried it across three peaks, through a dozen icefalls, past every doctor's warning. He would carry it to the summit or to his grave. He did not know which would come first. The pain was a constant now, a low fire that never went out. He had learned to work around it, to use his right arm for everything, to compensate without thinking. But the mountain was finding his weaknesses one by one.

Chapter 6: The Serac

They reached the traverse beneath the hanging serac at 7:22 AM. The ice above them was the color of old bone, layered with sediment from centuries of compression. A crack ran through it like a vein. The crack had not been there when Tenzing scouted the route the previous week.

He stopped and looked up. The crack was fresh. Water dripped from it, freezing mid-fall into beads that scattered across the couloir. The serac was waking up.

Ashford saw it too. She caught Tenzing's eye. Neither of them spoke.

Russell kept climbing. He did not look up. He did not want to know.

Min-seo looked. She watched the crack and listened to the ice talk. It made a sound like a ship groaning in heavy seas.

Tenzing made a decision. He would cross the traverse as fast as the rope allowed. He signaled Pemba. Pemba nodded. They both knew what the crack meant. The serac was a clock ticking down, and none of them knew the hour. Tenzing had crossed beneath a thousand hanging seracs in his career. He knew the numbers. One in a hundred collapsed while a team was beneath it. The odds were good. Until they were not.

Chapter 7: Min-seo's Doubt

Min-seo stopped at the edge of the traverse. Her crampons were planted on the ice but her body would not move forward. The serac hung above her, huge and patient. She could feel its weight in her chest.

Ashford came up beside her. "Look at my feet," she said.

Min-seo looked down.

"Just my feet. Not the serac. Not the drop. Just my feet."

Min-seo watched Ashford's boots move one after the other across the traverse. The rope between them went tight.

"Your turn," Ashford said.

Min-seo moved her right foot. Then her left. She did not look up. She counted steps. At thirty-seven she was across. Her hands were shaking so hard she could not unbuckle her harness.

Ashford touched her shoulder. "Good."

Tenzing was already moving up the next pitch. The crack in the serac had grown wider. Min-seo could see daylight through it now. A thin line of sky behind the ice. The serac was no longer solid. It was holding together by pressure and gravity and luck. She wondered if luck would be enough. She had never been a lucky person. She had worked for everything she had, trained through injuries, saved money for years to afford this expedition. Luck had never factored into her calculations. Now it was the only thing that mattered.

Chapter 8: The Collapse

The sound was not thunder. Thunder shakes the air. This was a sound that came through the ice, through the rope, through the bones of the climbers. A deep percussion that traveled upward from the mountain's core.

Tenzing felt it in his knees. He spun around. The serac was moving. Not falling yet. Shifting. A section the size of a bus leaned forward and dropped a curtain of ice fragments the size of cars.

Russell was in the middle of the traverse. He looked up and froze.

Pemba was above him. He had one hand on an anchor and the other reaching for Russell.

The serac let go.

The collapse was not fast. It was slow and enormous, a whole wall of ice tilting away from the mountain and disintegrating as it fell. The air turned white. Tenzing screamed something but the words were lost in the noise. He threw himself behind a rock rib and pressed his body into the mountain as the debris poured past like a river of stone. He felt the impacts through the rock, each one a shudder in his bones. He closed his eyes and pressed his face into the ice and waited for it to stop. He did not pray. He did not bargain. He just waited.

Chapter 9: Avalanche

The ice debris hit the couloir like a wave. Russell disappeared first. One moment he was there, reaching for Pemba's hand. The next, the rope went slack and he was gone.

Pemba clung to the anchor as the debris poured over him. His body was hammered against the ice. His leg twisted beneath him. He held.

Ashford pressed herself into the slope, her ice axe buried to the shaft. The debris streamed around her. A chunk the size of a suitcase struck her helmet. Her vision blurred. She tasted blood.

Tenzing had thrown himself behind a rock rib. He watched the avalanche pour past, white and gray and roaring. When it stopped, the couloir was smooth. All the anchors were gone. All the fixed rope. Russell was not visible anywhere.

Min-seo was alive. She was still standing on the far side of the traverse, untouched. The avalanche had split around her like water around a stone.

The silence after the roar was absolute. No wind. No movement. Just the sound of their own breathing, ragged and terrified in the thin air. Ashford's ears were ringing from the impact. She could taste blood in her mouth. She touched her helmet and found a dent in the plastic where the ice had struck. A few millimeters deeper and she would be unconscious. A few centimeters and she would be dead.

Chapter 10: Aftermath

Tenzing counted. He saw Ashford's blue jacket to his left. Min-seo's yellow pack above the traverse. Pemba was slumped over his anchor, still breathing.

No Russell.

Tenzing unclipped from the rope and moved down the couloir, searching the debris. He found a mitten, a piece of foam pad, a torn strap. No body.

Ashford called out to him. Her voice was thin, but it carried. "He's gone."

Tenzing knew she was right. At this altitude, on this terrain, there was no search and rescue. There was only continuing or dying.

He climbed back up to Pemba. Pemba's face was white. His leg was bent wrong below the knee. He looked at Tenzing, and the look did not need words.

Tenzing cut the damaged rope and retied what was left. He gathered the surviving team members. The four of them were alone on a mountain that had just shown them the full measure of its indifference. He looked at each of them in turn. Ashford's face was pale beneath her goggles. Min-seo was crying silently. Pemba's leg was already beginning to swell. Tenzing felt the weight of their lives pressing down on his shoulders. He had led them up. He would lead them down. Or he would die trying.

Chapter 11: The Broken Radio

Ashford reached into her down suit and pulled out the satellite radio. Its casing was cracked. A piece of ice had struck her squarely in the chest and the radio had taken the impact.

She pressed the power button. Nothing. She tried again. A flicker of a green light. Then darkness.

She tried to remember the protocol for water damage at altitude. The cold was supposed to help. But cold also killed batteries. She tucked the radio inside her suit, against her skin, and waited.

Tenzing watched her. "How long?"

"Maybe an hour. Maybe not at all."

"We need Base Camp."

"I know."

She kept the radio pressed to her stomach and kept climbing. Every ten minutes she tried the power button. On the fourth attempt, the screen lit up. She almost cried.

She keyed the mic. "Base Camp, this is Ashford. Come in."

Static.

"Base Camp, this is Ashford. Emergency. Come in."

Nothing but the hiss of the void. The radio was alive, but no one was listening on the other end. Ashford tried again. And again. She tried different frequencies. She tried the emergency channel. She tried the civilian band. Nothing. The mountain swallowed every signal and gave back only static.

Chapter 12: Rana Listens

Rana Thapa was making tea when the roar came down the mountain. He heard it as a vibration in the tent floor, a deep subsonic shudder that traveled through the glacier and into his boots. He set down the kettle and stepped outside.

The sky was clear. The summit pyramid of K2 was visible above the West Face. Everything looked the same. But the vibration told him something had changed.

He keyed the expedition radio. "Summit team, this is Base Camp. Come in."

Silence.

"Summit team, this is Base Camp. Over."

The static was the same static that had been there all morning. He tried every frequency. He tried the satellite handset. He tried the backup radio in the comms tent.

Nothing.

He stood in the middle of Base Camp, surrounded by tents and oxygen cylinders and the routines of a normal day, and understood that something was very wrong on the mountain above him. He was twenty-four years old. He had never managed a rescue. He picked up the radio and tried again. The static was the same. He tried every frequency in the book. He tried the satellite phone. He tried the backup set. Nothing. He sat down in the tent and stared at the equipment. Somewhere up there, people were dying. And he could not hear them.

Chapter 13: The Inventory

Tenzing called a halt on a ledge at 25,300 feet. The ledge was wide enough for all four of them if they sat with their knees drawn up. He unstrapped his pack and began counting.

Four oxygen bottles. Two of them full. One at half. Min-seo's was nearly empty. She had been running rich longer than the others.

Food: five energy bars, a bag of nuts, two liters of water that was already slush.

Medical kit: bandages, antiseptic, tape, ibuprofen, one syringe of morphine.

Bivouac gear: one emergency shelter, one sleeping bag, two headlamps with dying batteries.

Tenzing laid it all out on the ice. The others looked at the small pile. Five people's lives reduced to what fit in a single stuff sack.

"Two days," he said. "Maybe less if we move slow."

Ashford calculated in her head. Camp 3 was at 23,500 feet. Camp 2 at 21,000. The glacier at 15,000. The distances were not large by normal measurement. But on this mountain, with these supplies, they might as well be on the moon. Ashford did the math three times, hoping for a different answer. The numbers did not change. They had enough oxygen to get half of them to Camp 3. They had enough food to keep one person alive for a week. There were four of them.

Chapter 14: The Casualty

Min-seo found Russell's ice axe wedged in a crack below the traverse. She spotted the orange shaft against the white and climbed down to reach it. The axe was undamaged. The leash was still attached. The buckle was done up as though Russell had simply let go.

She brought it back to the ledge and showed Tenzing.

Tenzing took the axe. He had climbed with Russell for six days. Russell had been loud, impatient, dismissive of the Sherpa staff. He had also been brave in the way that rich men are brave — believing their money would protect them from consequences.

Tenzing set the axe upright in the snow. A marker. A grave post.

"How do we tell his family?" Min-seo asked.

Ashford answered. "We tell them when we are alive to do it."

The words came out harder than she intended. She did not apologize. There was no time for apologies on this mountain. There was only forward movement or death.

Chapter 15: The Descent Begins

They roped up in a new configuration. Tenzing in front, then Ashford, then Min-seo, then Pemba on a short leash because he could not weight his leg. The rope between them was the only thing connecting each person to the next.

The descent from the Bottleneck required down-climbing a forty-degree ice slope with no fixed rope. Tenzing cut steps with his front points. Each step took five kicks. Each kick sent a tremor through his shoulder.

Pemba moved by dragging his good leg and placing the bad one like a dead weight. He made no sound at all. Tenzing had known Pemba since childhood. Pemba never made a sound when he was hurt. It was the most frightening thing about him.

Ashford watched the time. They had covered two hundred vertical feet in an hour. At this rate, they would reach Camp 3 in fourteen hours. They had four hours of daylight.

Min-seo followed in silence. She had stopped thinking about the summit. She was thinking about the tent at Camp 3. A tent meant shelter. Shelter meant sleep. Sleep meant the possibility of tomorrow.

Chapter 16: Lost in White

The whiteout arrived without warning. One minute the sky was gray but clear. The next, the wind picked up and the world turned to milk. Ashford could see nothing beyond the end of her ice axe. The slope beneath her feet became a suggestion.

Tenzing stopped. He could feel the ground dropping away to his left. He could not see it. The fixed ropes they had been following were buried under fresh snow.

He turned to face the team. "Tight rope. No gaps."

They closed the distance between them until each person was ten feet apart. Ashford could barely see Min-seo, and Min-seo was directly in front of her.

Tenzing moved forward by feel. His crampons found the buried rope. He followed it as a blind man follows a rail. The wind screamed past them, horizontal, hard as gravel.

Pemba slipped. His bad leg folded. He went down on his back and slid three meters before the rope caught him. The sudden tension nearly pulled Tenzing off his feet.

Tenzing planted his axe and held. Pemba stopped sliding.

"Again," Tenzing said.

Pemba got up and kept moving.

Chapter 17: The Crevasse

The snow bridge collapsed without warning. One moment Ashford was walking on a surface that looked solid. The next, the snow fell away beneath her and she was dropping into darkness.

She fell a body length before the rope caught her. The harness bit into her thighs. She swung against the wall of the crevasse. Above her, a rectangle of gray light. Below her, nothing.

She heard Min-seo scream her name.

The crevasse was narrow. She could touch both walls with her outstretched hands. The ice was blue and smooth. She hung there, breathing, assessing.

"Ashford!" Tenzing's voice from above.

"I'm alive. I'm on the rope."

"Can you climb?"

She looked at the walls. No purchase. The ice was too smooth and too steep. She kicked her crampons into the wall and tried to stand. Her feet slipped.

"No. I can't get purchase."

Tenzing's head appeared in the rectangle of light. He was lying flat, spreading his weight across the snow. He looked down at her.

"Then I'll pull you up."

Chapter 18: The Rescue of Ashford

Tenzing anchored himself with two ice screws and a sling. He clipped Ashford's rope into a pulley system using a carabiner and a length of cord. The mechanics of the haul were simple: every inch he pulled took two inches of rope out of the system.

Min-seo anchored behind him and pulled on the tail of the rope. Together, they hauled Ashford out of the crevasse one meter at a time.

Ashford helped where she could, kicking her crampons into the wall to take weight off the rope. Her forehead was bleeding from where she struck the ice on the way down. The blood ran into her eye and she could not wipe it away.

Pemba sat on the snow, useless, his leg extended in front of him. He watched the rescue with the expression of a man calculating his own odds.

Ashford's gloves appeared over the lip of the crevasse. Then her forearms. Then her head. Tenzing grabbed her by the shoulder straps and dragged her onto solid snow.

She lay there, breathing hard. The blood from her forehead had frozen in a red stripe down her cheek.

Tenzing examined the wound. Superficial. He cleaned it with an antiseptic wipe and taped a gauze pad over it.

"You look terrible," he said.

Ashford laughed. The laugh was half sob. She did not care.

Chapter 19: Pemba's Signal

They continued down through the whiteout, slower now. Ashford's wound had stopped bleeding but her head ached. The altitude, the impact, the fear: they all pressed on her skull.

Tenzing searched for any landmark he recognized. The icefall between the Bottleneck and Camp 3 had shifted in the avalanche. The route he knew was gone. He was navigating by memory and intuition.

Min-seo saw it first. A flicker. Below them, to the left. A single point of light.

"Tenzing."

He stopped and looked. The light flickered again. A headlamp. Someone was alive below them.

Tenzing changed direction without a word. The team followed him across the slope, toward the light.

It took forty minutes to reach the source. They found a man half-buried in debris, his leg pinned between a serac block and the mountain wall. His headlamp was still on, pointing upward, signaling to anyone who might be above.

It was Pemba.

No. Pemba was right behind them. This was not Pemba.

This was Russell.

Chapter 20: The Injured Man

Russell was alive. The avalanche had carried him two hundred meters down the mountain and deposited him against the base of a serac. His leg was crushed between the ice and the rock. He was conscious, just barely.

Tenzing dropped to his knees beside him. Russell's eyes were unfocused. His lips were blue.

"Russell. Look at me."

Russell's eyes found Tenzing's face.

"I can't feel my legs," he said.

Ashford pushed past Tenzing and began her examination. She cut open Russell's climbing pants with a knife. The leg was swollen, discolored, distorted. Femur fracture. Possible pelvic involvement. Internal bleeding, likely.

"Can we move him?" Min-seo asked.

Ashford did not answer. The answer was obvious: no. Not without a stretcher. Not on this slope. Not with their supplies.

Tenzing knew what Ashford was not saying. He looked at Pemba, who met his eyes. Pemba shook his head once, almost imperceptibly.

Tenzing looked at the sky. The whiteout was thinning. The storm would pass. But that was not the problem. The problem was that Russell was dying, and there was nothing they could do to stop it.

Chapter 21: The Splint

Ashford built a splint from a tent pole and a foam sleeping pad. She worked quickly, her fingers clumsy with cold. The femoral artery was still intact. If she could stabilize the fracture, Russell might survive the next hour.

She pulled his leg straight. Russell screamed. The sound bounced off the ice walls and came back to them, distorted.

Ashford did not stop. She wrapped the splint, secured it with tape and cord, checked the circulation in Russell's foot. Pulse present. Weak but present.

"Can we move him now?" Min-seo asked.

"Not far. And not fast."

Tenzing calculated. Camp 3 was four hundred vertical feet below them. On good terrain, with an injured man, that was six hours. Russell would not last six hours in his condition.

"We carry him," Tenzing said.

"How?" Min-seo's voice was sharp.

"We take turns. Two of us supporting. One leading."

Ashford looked at Tenzing's shoulder. She knew. The secret was not a secret to her anymore. She had seen him favor his left side, seen the way he placed his ice axe with his right hand even when the line required a left-hand placement.

"Your shoulder," she said.

Tenzing met her gaze. "It will hold."

No one argued. There was no alternative.

Chapter 22: The Triage

They carried Russell for one hour. The progress was measured in feet. Every step was negotiation with the ice, with gravity, with their own failing bodies.

Tenzing took the left side of the improvised sling. Ashford took the right. They moved in rhythm, counting steps aloud. Min-seo led, finding the safest route. Pemba followed, dragging his leg, carrying the remaining gear.

At an ice step, the sling twisted and Russell fell. He hit his head on the ice. His eyes rolled back.

Ashford checked his pupils. Unequal. "He has a head injury. Bleeding inside the skull."

She did not say what this meant. But Min-seo understood. Russell was not going to survive.

"We should leave him."

The words came out of Min-seo's mouth before she knew she was speaking them. Everyone looked at her.

"He's dying," she said. "And we're dying with him."

Ashford opened her mouth to respond. Closed it. Because Min-seo was right.

Tenzing stood up. He looked at Russell, then at Pemba. Two injured men. Four rescuers who were themselves injured, exhausted, oxygen-deprived.

"We make camp here," he said. "We wait for the storm to clear. Then we decide."

But everyone already knew the decision.

Chapter 23: The Radio Lives

Ashford remembered the satellite radio in her suit. She pulled it out. The battery light was blinking red, but the device was alive.

She pressed the transmit button. "Base Camp, this is Ashford on K2. Do you copy?"

For a long moment, nothing. Then a voice, thin and distant, but unmistakably human.

"Ashford, this is Base Camp. Rana Thapa speaking. I copy you."

Ashford closed her eyes. She had never been so grateful to hear another person's voice.

"We have casualties," she said. "Russell is down with a leg fracture and head trauma. Pemba has a lower leg injury. Everyone else is functional but compromised. We are at approximately 24,500 feet. We need a plan."

Rana's voice came back. "Understood. Stand by while I assess resources."

The radio went silent. Ashford held it in both hands, as if it were a living thing.

Tenzing watched her. "Can they reach us?"

"We'll find out."

Chapter 24: The Situation Report

Rana sat in the comms tent with a notebook and a pen. He wrote down every word Ashford said. Then he read it back to confirm.

Russell Crawford: leg fracture, head trauma, unconscious. Surviving but deteriorating.

Pemba Nuru: lower leg crush injury, stable, ambulatory with assistance.

Tenzing Dorje: functional, unknown shoulder injury.

Ashford: minor head wound, functional.

Min-seo Park: functional, no oxygen remaining.

Location: approximately 24,500 feet, east side of the Bottleneck, near the ice step.

Supplies: two full oxygen bottles, five energy bars, two liters of water, medical kit, one emergency shelter.

Rana finished writing and looked at the page. The situation was worse than he had told Ashford. At Base Camp, he had two working radios, a satellite phone, a medical kit designed for altitude sickness not trauma, and a staff of four guides, only two of whom were fit to climb at this point in the season.

He looked up at the mountain. The summit was hidden in cloud. He picked up the radio and called Ashford back.

Chapter 25: The Satellite Call

Rana needed advice. He did not have a senior expedition leader to consult. He had a satellite phone and a list of emergency contacts.

He dialed his father.

Colonel Vinod Thapa answered on the second ring. His voice was steady, the voice of a man who had commanded troops in the Siachen Glacier at 20,000 feet, who had seen men die of cold and altitude, who had made the kind of decisions Rana was now facing.

"Tell me what happened."

Rana told him. The avalanche. The injuries. The four survivors at 24,500 feet. The two guides at Base Camp.

His father listened without interrupting. When Rana finished, there was a long silence.

"Son," the Colonel said, "I need you to hear something that will be difficult to hear."

Rana waited.

"You have people at Base Camp who can climb. If you send them up, you risk their lives. If you do not send them, the people above will die. There is no right answer. There is only the answer you can live with."

Rana held the phone so tight his fingers cramped.

"What would you do?"

"I cannot answer that. I am not where you are."

The line went quiet. Rana stared at the tent wall.

Chapter 26: The Colonel's Answer

Colonel Thapa spoke again. His voice was quieter now.

"When I was in Siachen, I lost two men to an avalanche. They were buried under forty feet of snow. We did not have the equipment to dig them out. I made the call to leave them."

Rana listened.

"I have lived with that call for thirty years. There is no day it does not visit me."

"Are you saying I should not send them?"

"I am saying that if you send them, some of them may die. I am saying that if you do not, the people above will die. I am saying you must decide and then carry the weight of that decision. No one else can carry it for you."

Rana looked at the radio. The guides were waiting outside. They knew what was happening. They were waiting for his order.

"Thank you, Father."

"Whatever you decide, call me afterward."

Rana ended the call and sat in the silence of the tent for a full minute. The walls of the tent flapped in the wind. He stared at the radio, at the list of supplies, at the photograph of his mother taped to the tent pole. Then he stood up and walked outside.

Chapter 27: Rana's Choice

The two guides were named Dawa and Pasang. They were brothers from a village near Namche Bazaar. They had climbed K2 three times each. They knew what the mountain could do.

Rana gathered them in the mess tent. He laid out the situation: four climbers at 24,500 feet, two injured, limited supplies, weather deteriorating.

"I need volunteers to go up."

Dawa spoke first. "The weather window closes tonight. If we go, we are climbing through the storm."

"I know."

"If we reach them, we carry them down through whiteout conditions. That is how people die."

"I know."

"If we do not go, they die."

"Yes."

Dawa looked at his brother. Pasang nodded once.

"We leave in thirty minutes," Dawa said.

Rana felt the weight lift a fraction. "I will maintain radio contact. If the weather turns worse than you can manage, I will order you down."

"You will not need to," Dawa said. "We will know when we cannot continue."

Rana watched them prepare their gear. Oxygen bottles, food, rope, medical supplies. They packed in silence. He was twenty-four years old. He had just sent two men to risk their lives on his authority. He did not feel like a leader. He felt like a boy pretending.

Chapter 28: The Volunteers

Dawa and Pasang moved through Base Camp with the efficiency of men who had done this work for a decade. Oxygen bottles, ropes, carabiners, headlamps, extra batteries, a first aid kit, a portable stretcher, a thermos of tea. They packed their bags without speaking.

Rana helped them sort the oxygen. Four full bottles for the ascent. Two to leave for the descent. The math was tight.

"Radio check," Rana said.

Dawa keyed his mic. "Base Camp, this is Dawa. Check."

Rana answered. "Loud and clear."

Dawa nodded. He shouldered his pack and stepped onto the glacier. Pasang followed without looking back.

Rana watched them go. The two figures grew smaller against the white expanse, merging with the ice until he could no longer distinguish them from the rocks.

He went back to the comms tent. He keyed the radio.

"Ashford, this is Base Camp. Rescue team is en route. ETA approximately eight hours."

Ashford's voice came back, thin and exhausted. "Understood. We will hold."

Rana sat down. The wait began. He looked at his watch. Eight hours. The longest eight hours of his life.

Chapter 29: The Descent to Help

On the mountain, time moved differently. Tenzing measured it in breaths. Each breath was one unit of existence. At this altitude, each breath contained less life than the one before.

Russell had stopped responding. His eyes were open but they did not track. Ashford checked his pupils again. One was fixed and dilated.

"He's herniating," she said.

"Herniating?" Min-seo did not know the word.

"Brain swelling. The pressure is building inside his skull. His brain stem is being compressed."

"How long?"

"Hours. Maybe less."

Tenzing made the decision fast. "We move. Now. All of us."

"Russell cannot move," Ashford said.

"I know."

The words hung between them. Russell was not going to survive. The question was whether his death would kill the rest of them.

Tenzing looked at Pemba. "Can you walk faster?"

Pemba's leg was swollen to twice its size. His face was gray. "I can try."

"Then we go. Ashford and Min-seo take point. I'll carry what gear I can."

"You cannot carry extra weight with your shoulder."

"I will manage."

Ashford wanted to argue. She did not have the strength. They left Russell wrapped in the emergency shelter, propped against a rock, with a bottle of water by his hand. He was unconscious. He would not use the water. Tenzing did not look back as they moved down the mountain.

Chapter 30: The Oxygen Debate

Min-seo's tank ran dry at 1:47 PM. She heard the change in the hiss of the regulator before she felt it in her lungs. The flow became shallow, then stopped.

She removed her mask.

The air hit her face like a chemical burn. She had been on supplemental oxygen for 36 hours. Her body had forgotten what the real air felt like. It was cold, dry, and utterly insufficient.

Her vision grayed at the edges.

Tenzing saw her remove the mask and was at her side in seconds. He took his own regulator and held it to her face.

"Breathe."

She shook her head. "Then you have none."

"I have more."

"You need it to lead."

"I need you to breathe."

She took the regulator. One breath. Two. The gray receded.

Ashford watched the exchange and said nothing. She checked her own tank. Half full. Two hours, maybe less if she dialed down the flow. She dialed it down anyway.

They were running out of everything. Oxygen. Time. Options. Tenzing looked at the slope below them. Camp 3 was out there, invisible in the cloud. He started walking. The others followed.

Chapter 31: The Hallucination

Min-seo saw the figure at the ridge. A man in a bright red jacket, standing on a promontory of rock, looking down at them. He was not moving. He was just standing there, watching.

"Someone's up there," she said.

Ashford looked. No one there. "There's no one."

"He's right there. In red."

The figure extended an arm. He was pointing. Min-seo followed the direction of his finger. There was nothing but drop, a sheer wall of ice falling away into the void.

"Min-seo. Look at me."

Min-seo turned. Ashford's face was close, her eyes sharp.

"There is no one on the ridge. You are seeing things because your brain is starving for oxygen."

Min-seo looked back at the ridge. The man was gone. Just rock and snow.

"I saw him," she said.

"I know. Come down from here."

Min-seo took a step. Her boot found nothing. The slope dropped away beneath her. She pitched forward.

Ashford grabbed her by the shoulder strap and wrenched her back onto the path.

"Watch your feet."

Min-seo looked down. She could not stop shaking. The figure was gone, but she could still feel his eyes on her back.

Chapter 32: Camp 3

The tents at Camp 3 appeared out of the mist like a promise. Two yellow domes half-buried in snow, their fabric whipped by the wind. Tenzing had never been so relieved to see a nylon shell in his life.

They reached the first tent at 4:13 PM. Ashford unzipped the fly and collapsed inside. Min-seo crawled in after her. Pemba sat down hard in the entrance, his leg extended, unable to bend his knee.

Tenzing stayed outside. He checked the tent anchors, dug out the buried guy lines, reinforced the windward side with a wall of snow blocks. The work was mechanical. His shoulder screamed through every motion. He finished the job and crawled inside.

Four people in a three-person tent. The warmth of their bodies raised the temperature above freezing. Moisture condensed on the walls. It was the most comfortable place any of them had been in three days.

Ashford distributed the remaining food. One energy bar each. Two sips of water each.

Tenzing lay back and felt his shoulder seize. The joint had locked. He could not lift his left arm above his waist. He said nothing. He closed his eyes. The wind howled outside.

Chapter 33: The Diagnosis

Ashford noticed Tenzing's arm hanging limp at his side. She noticed the way he used his right hand for everything, the way his left shoulder was held slightly elevated as if bracing against a blow.

She crawled over to him. "Let me see it."

"It is nothing."

"Let me see it."

He hesitated. Then he allowed her to unzip his jacket and pull his collar down.

The bruise covered his entire shoulder. Purple and black, spreading down his chest. The joint was visibly displaced, a lump of bone sitting where it should not be.

Ashford touched it gently. Tenzing's whole body went rigid.

"Torn rotator cuff," she said. "Probably a dislocation as well. How long?"

"Months."

"You have been climbing with a torn rotator cuff for months?"

"I have been climbing."

She wanted to be angry. But anger required energy she did not have. She pulled his jacket back up and zipped it.

"You need surgery."

"I need to get my team off this mountain."

"That too."

She found ibuprofen in the medical kit and gave him three. He swallowed them dry.

"Thank you," he said.

"Save your thanks for when we are down."

Chapter 34: The Second Wave

Dawa and Pasang climbed through the storm. The wind at Camp 2 was 60 kilometers per hour, gusting higher. The snow was driven horizontal, so dense that they could not see the ends of their own packs.

They moved on instinct. Dawa had climbed K2 in worse weather. He had been at 8,000 meters in a storm that killed six climbers on the south side. He knew the mountain's limits. He knew his own.

At the ice step below Camp 3, Pasang stopped. He was breathing hard. His oxygen regulator was icing over.

"Are you all right?"

Pasang tapped the regulator. "It's freezing."

"Change it."

"I only brought one spare."

Dawa considered. They had come to rescue people who might already be dead. If they lost their own oxygen, they would become victims.

"We continue," Dawa said.

Pasang nodded. He switched to the spare regulator. They continued upward.

Above them, the yellow tents of Camp 3 were barely visible through the snow. The wind was picking up, gusting harder. Dawa leaned into it and kept climbing.

Chapter 35: The Night

Night came down on Camp 3 like a lid closing. The wind did not stop. It increased, finding every gap in the tent fabric, every loose seam.

The four of them lay in the darkness without speaking. Pemba slept, his breath ragged. Min-seo shivered despite the shared warmth. Tenzing stared at the tent ceiling.

Ashford could not sleep. She listened to the mountain. The ice beneath them was alive, creaking, shifting, settling. The sound was not threatening. It was neutral. The mountain was not trying to kill them. It was just being itself. Survival was their problem.

She thought about her brother. He had died at 27,000 feet on this mountain. He had been climbing with a group that pushed too hard, too late in the day. He died of cerebral edema three hours below the summit. She had come here to be near where he died. She had not admitted this to anyone.

"Ashford." Tenzing's voice in the dark.

"Yes."

"Thank you for the diagnosis."

"You are welcome."

A pause. "We will make it down."

She did not answer. Promises at this altitude meant nothing. But she appreciated the attempt.

Chapter 36: The Wind

The storm intensified at 3:00 AM. The tent fly ripped free on the windward side. Fabric flapped wildly, a sound like gunfire.

Tenzing was awake instantly. He grabbed the edge of the fly and tried to pull it back into place. The wind tore it out of his hands.

"Dawa's team is out there," Min-seo said.

"They will find us."

"How do you know?"

Tenzing did not answer. He did not know. He wanted to believe it, but certainty was a luxury he could not afford.

The tent frame bowed inward. A pole flexed, almost to breaking. Ashford pressed her palm against the bending pole, pushing back against the wind. The pressure hurt her hand. She held.

Min-seo crawled to the far side of the tent and began piling gear against the wall. Packs, boots, empty oxygen bottles. Anything to add weight, to keep the tent from collapsing.

The wind screamed. The tent held.

They lay in the dark, listening, waiting. The fly snapped and tore. The poles groaned. They pressed their bodies against the walls and held the fabric down with their weight. This was survival now. Not climbing. Not descending. Just holding on.

Chapter 37: Pemba's Fever

Pemba woke Ashford at 4:30 AM. He was shaking. Not from cold. His skin was hot to the touch.

"Fever," she said.

She checked his leg. The wound had been wrapped in a bandage improvised from a cut-open sleeping pad. She removed the bandage carefully. The flesh beneath was swollen, red, weeping.

Infection had set in. The bacteria did not care about altitude or circumstance. They reproduced with the same efficiency at 23,000 feet as at sea level. They were faster, if anything, because Pemba's immune system was suppressed by the altitude.

Ashford cleaned the wound with the last of the antiseptic. She covered it with a fresh dressing. She gave Pemba the last dose of ibuprofen and one of the two remaining antibiotics.

"This will slow it," she said. "It will not stop it."

Pemba nodded. He understood.

"We need to get you lower. The infection needs oxygen. So do you."

"When do we move?"

"Dawn."

Pemba closed his eyes. The shaking continued. Ashford sat back and looked at the remaining supplies. One antibiotic left. No antiseptic. No painkillers. Three energy bars. She did the math and did not like the answer.

Chapter 38: The Rescue Arrives

Dawa's headlamp appeared at 6:12 AM, a single point of light in the gray dawn. He was fifty meters above Camp 3, descending through the broken ice.

Tenzing saw the light and crawled out of the tent. He stood in the wind and watched the light grow closer. Behind it, another light. Pasang.

Dawa reached the tents at 6:18. He dropped his pack and embraced Tenzing. The embrace was brief, professional.

"How many?"

"Four. Pemba is injured. Infection. Min-seo has no oxygen. Ashford is functional. I have a shoulder injury."

Dawa took in the information without changing expression. "We brought oxygen and a stretcher."

"We need help getting down. The route is destroyed."

"We know. We came through it."

Dawa began unpacking supplies. Oxygen bottles, food, water. He handed a bottle to Min-seo, who took it with trembling hands. Pasang worked on Pemba's leg, cutting away the old bandage, applying a fresh one.

Tenzing stood in the wind and watched his team receive the supplies that would keep them alive. He had not let himself hope before. Now he allowed a small amount.

Chapter 39: The Handoff

They loaded Pemba onto the stretcher. Pasang and Dawa rigged a harness system to distribute the weight. The stretcher was light, designed for exactly this kind of evacuation.

Pemba did not protest. He lay still, his face blank, his leg wrapped in fresh bandages. The fever had not broken. He was sweating despite the cold.

Tenzing tried to take a position at the front of the stretcher. Ashford stopped him.

"No. You are injured."

"I can carry."

"You are injured and you have been carrying this team for three days. Let them carry you now."

Tenzing looked at Dawa, who nodded. "She is right."

Tenzing stepped back. For a moment, he did not know what to do with his hands. He had been leading for so long that following felt foreign. He had spent his entire adult life in front of the rope. Being behind felt like losing control.

Ashford took the front position. Pasang took the rear. Dawa moved ahead to scout the route.

"Let's go," Ashford said.

The column began to move. Tenzing walked behind the stretcher, his good hand resting on the frame. He watched the mountain retreat behind them, climbing by climbing down.

Chapter 40: The Descent Resumes

The descent from Camp 3 to Camp 2 took eight hours. In good conditions, it took two.

Every step was negotiation. The route wound through a maze of crevasses, ice blocks, and debris from the avalanche. Dawa picked the path carefully. One wrong step meant a crevasse fall. One wrong decision meant the stretcher tipped.

Ashford's arms burned from the weight of the stretcher. She did not complain. She counted steps. At one thousand, she would switch with Min-seo. At two thousand, she would rest. The counting kept her moving.

Min-seo walked behind Tenzing, carrying the spare oxygen. She had stopped hallucinating. The oxygen had cleared her head. But the images stayed with her. A man in red, standing on the ridge, pointing into the void.

Tenzing's shoulder was a knot of fire. He walked without using his left arm, letting it hang. The ibuprofen had worn off. He did not ask for more.

They reached Camp 2 at 2:30 PM. The tents were intact. The wind had died. They stopped. They rested. The hardest part of the mountain was behind them. But the hardest part of the descent was still ahead.

Chapter 41: The Last Fixed Line

The fixed rope at the bottom of the ice wall was frayed. Tenzing saw it from ten meters away: a section of rope where the outer sheath had been cut through by a rockfall, exposing the core.

He stopped the column. "We need to cut and retie."

Dawa examined the damage. "We can cross one at a time. Carefully."

"No. The rope will not hold the stretcher."

They stood at the top of the wall and looked down. The drop was sixty meters. At the bottom, the glacier. To the left, a crevasse. To the right, a jumble of ice blocks.

Dawa took out a knife. He cut the damaged rope and tied a new knot below the frayed section. The knot reduced the rope's strength by thirty percent. He knew the number because he had been tying knots his whole life.

"I will go first," Dawa said.

He clipped into the rope and descended. The rope held.

One by one, they followed. Min-seo. Ashford. Pasang with the stretcher. Tenzing came last, moving down the wall with one arm.

At the bottom, they gathered. The crevasse yawned to their left. The glacier stretched ahead.

"The hardest part is done," Dawa said.

No one believed him.

Chapter 42: The Fall

Ashford was crossing a slab of black ice when her feet went out from under her. There was no warning. One moment she was upright. The next, she was sliding, her crampons scraping uselessly against the polished surface.

The slope was not steep. But it was steep enough. She slid toward the crevasse, picking up speed.

Tenzing saw her go. He was ten meters away, on good ground. He threw himself flat and extended his ice axe. The shaft caught her pack strap. The pick dug into the ice.

The rope went tight. Ashford stopped sliding. She was two meters from the crevasse edge.

She lay there, breathing.

"Don't move," Tenzing said.

"I wasn't planning to."

He crawled to the edge of the slab and extended his hand. She took it. He pulled her up with his good arm. She crawled onto solid snow and sat there, shaking.

"You saved my life," she said.

"I was the nearest."

"That is not a modest answer."

"I am not a modest man. I am a practical one."

She laughed. The sound was raw and strange in the thin air. It was the first time she had laughed in three days.

Chapter 43: Camp 2

Camp 2 looked like a miracle. Six tents arranged on a flat section of glacier, surrounded by a few scattered supply boxes and a stack of oxygen cylinders. It was a town after the emptiness of the upper mountain.

Ashford walked into the camp and sat down on the first flat surface she found. She did not care about decorum. She sat in the snow and let her legs extend in front of her. She had not sat properly in days. The relief made her dizzy.

Min-seo found the food tent. She opened a box of freeze-dried meals and began reading the instructions. The act was absurdly normal. Add boiling water. Wait five minutes. Eat. She could not remember the last time she had done anything so mundane.

Tenzing found the comms tent. He picked up the radio.

"Base Camp, this is Tenzing at Camp 2. We are all down. Repeat, we are all down from the upper mountain."

Rana's voice came back, crackling but clear. "Understood. Pemba's condition?"

"Stable. Infection in his leg. He needs evacuation."

"Helicopter is ordered. ETA at Base Camp is tomorrow morning."

Tenzing set down the radio. He sat in the tent and stared at the canvas wall. They had made it. They had made it through the storm, through the whiteout, through every mistake the mountain had thrown at them. He let himself feel it for a moment, then stood up. There was still work to do.

Chapter 44: The Debris Field

Rana's voice came through the radio at dawn. The helicopter could not land above 18,000 feet. The air was too thin for the rotors to get purchase. Pemba would have to be carried down to the glacier before evacuation.

"No more climbing," Ashford said. "Just walking."

"Walking is climbing," Tenzing said. "It is all the same."

They packed Camp 2 in an hour. The stretcher was lighter now that the remaining supplies were distributed. Pemba was heavier because the infection had spread. His leg was hot to the touch.

The route from Camp 2 to Advanced Base Camp crossed the icefall. The debris field from the avalanche had scattered house-sized blocks across the path. Dawa picked a route through them, moving from gap to gap.

Ashford walked with her eyes on her feet. She did not look at the seracs above her. She did not look at the crevasses. She looked at her boots and moved one after the other.

Tenzing walked beside her. He did not talk. They moved together in the rhythm of people who have shared something that cannot be undone.

Chapter 45: Pemba's Ride

The rescue sled broke at 16,500 feet. The left runner cracked when Pasang guided it over a pressure ridge. The sled tipped. Pemba rolled out onto the ice.

Pasang caught him before he slid. He held Pemba against his chest and checked his pulse. Strong but fast. The fever was high.

"Runner is broken," Pasang said.

Dawa examined the sled. The runner was a hollow aluminum tube, split along the seam. A field repair was possible but not ideal.

"Tent pole," Ashford said.

"What?"

"Use a tent pole as a splint." She was already pulling the poles from her pack.

They lashed the tent pole along the broken runner with cord and tape. The repair was ugly. It would not hold forever. But it did not need to hold forever. It needed to hold for two thousand vertical feet.

Dawa tested the sled. It tracked straight.

"Good enough," he said.

They loaded Pemba back onto the sled. He was semiconscious now, mumbling in Nepali. Tenzing listened. Pemba was reciting a prayer.

"From where?" Ashford asked.

"The Buddhist chant for the dying. He is not dying. He is just scared."

Ashford touched Pemba's forehead. Hot. Very hot.

"Let's move," she said.

Chapter 46: The Icefall at Night

Crossing the Khumbu Icefall at night was a special kind of terror. The shadows played tricks. A block of ice became a building. A crevasse became an abyss. The headlamps created small circles of light that pushed back the darkness but showed nothing of what was coming.

Dawa had crossed the icefall at night many times. He knew the rhythm. He knew the places where the ladders flexed. He knew which crevasses had grown wider since the morning.

The team followed him in single file. Ashford carried one end of the stretcher. Pasang carried the other. Tenzing walked behind, his headlamp trained on the sled.

The first ladder crossing was the worst. The ladder spanned a crevasse fifteen meters wide. The aluminum planks were lashed together, flexing under the weight.

Dawa crossed first. Then Min-seo. Then the sled. Ashford and Pasang moved in perfect sync, their steps matched to prevent the ladder from oscillating.

Tenzing crossed last. He was halfway across when a piece of ice broke free from the crevasse wall and fell into the darkness. The sound of the impact came back after a long delay.

He did not look down. He kept moving. The icefall groaned around them, a living thing shifting in the dark.

Chapter 47: The Collapse of Rana

Rana had not slept in 48 hours. He had been in the comms tent continuously, monitoring the radio, coordinating with the helicopter base in Skardu, updating the expedition company's headquarters.

His hands shook when he reached for the coffee cup. He had drunk so much coffee that his stomach burned. He could not eat. The thought of food made him nauseous.

His father called.

"You sound terrible," the Colonel said.

"I have not slept."

"You cannot lead if you cannot function. Sleep for two hours. I will monitor the radio."

"I cannot."

"You can. I will call you if anything changes."

Rana hesitated. "What if they need me?"

"Nothing will happen in two hours that cannot wait two hours. Sleep."

Rana lay down on the tent floor. He was asleep before his head touched the sleeping bag.

Two hours later, he woke to his father's voice on the satellite phone. "They are at the bottom of the icefall. The helicopter is inbound."

Rana sat up. His body ached. His head pounded. But for the first time in three days, the weight on his chest lifted.

Chapter 48: The Doctor's Confession

Ashford and Tenzing sat on a rock at the edge of Advanced Base Camp. Below them, the glacier spread out like a rumpled white sheet. Above them, K2's summit was visible through a gap in the clouds.

They had not been alone together since the icefall.

"My brother died here," Ashford said.

Tenzing did not react. He had been waiting for her to say this.

"I know."

"How?"

"The way you look at the summit. You see something different than the rest of us."

She picked up a piece of gravel and turned it over in her fingers. "I came here to see where he died. I thought maybe I would understand. Maybe I would forgive him for leaving."

"Did you?"

"I do not know yet."

Tenzing was silent for a long time. The wind moved around them, carrying the sound of the helicopter from far away.

"When I fell in the crevasse on Nuptse," he said, "I thought I was going to die. I thought about my daughter. About her growing up without a father."

"And you kept climbing."

"I keep climbing. It is what I do."

They sat together and watched the summit. The mountain was indifferent. But they were not.

Chapter 49: The Valley Floor

The helicopter arrived at 11:00 AM. The sound of the rotors echoed off the valley walls. Ashford looked up and saw the machine descending, tiny against the scale of the mountains.

Pemba was loaded first. Dawa and Pasang carried the stretcher to the helicopter and secured it inside. Pemba's eyes were closed. He had stopped talking an hour ago. But his heart was still beating.

The pilot gestured to Min-seo. She climbed in, taking the seat beside the stretcher. She looked back at Ashford and Tenzing, her face unreadable.

"There is room for two more," the pilot shouted.

Tenzing shook his head. "We have gear to pack. We will take the ground route."

Ashford looked at him. "Your shoulder."

"Will survive a walk to Base Camp."

The helicopter lifted off. The noise faded, replaced by the mountain silence.

Tenzing and Ashford stood on the glacier and watched the machine shrink to a dot against the sky.

"Well," Ashford said. "Shall we walk?"

"We shall."

They shouldered their packs and began the final walk down the valley. The ice crunched beneath their boots. The sun was warm on their faces. They did not speak. There was nothing left to say.

Chapter 50: The Mountain Stands

They reached Base Camp at dusk. The tents were lit from within, yellow and warm. Dawa and Pasang were waiting with cups of tea. Min-seo had already called from the hospital in Skardu. Pemba was in surgery. The doctors expected him to live.

Rana met them at the edge of camp. He looked exhausted, drawn, older than his age.

"Welcome back."

Tenzing shook his hand. "Thank you for sending them."

"I had to."

"Not everyone would have."

They stood in the fading light and looked up at the mountain. K2 was dark against the sky, its summit catching the last light of the sun. It looked exactly as it had looked before the expedition began. Indifferent. Immense. Unchanged.

Ashford sat down on a supply crate. She was too tired to stand. She looked at the mountain and felt nothing. No triumph. No grief. Just the quiet exhaustion of having survived.

Tenzing sat beside her. They did not speak.

The mountain stood above them, sharp and cold and permanent. It had been there before them. It would be there after.

They were the ones who had changed.
