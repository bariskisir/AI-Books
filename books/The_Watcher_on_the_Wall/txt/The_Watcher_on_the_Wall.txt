Chapter 1: The Spire

The transport set down on a platform of black ice. Private Elias Kane stepped out into the cold. It bit through his thermal layers like a living thing.

Ostrac had no sun. Only a gray smear of light that passed for day. The Spire rose from the mountain — a tower of obsidian and steel, fused together by generations of desperate labor. Kane had read about it in the recruitment pamphlets. The pamphlets lied about everything except the cold.

A soldier met him at the airlock. She wore a patch that marked her as Spire garrison. "Follow me," she said. No name. No welcome.

The interior was better than the surface. Heated, at least. But the walls were raw rock and the corridors were narrow. Kane counted his steps. A habit from basic. The fortress was larger than it looked from outside. Most of it was underground.

They passed a mess hall. A dozen soldiers sat in silence, eating from metal trays. No one looked up.

"Your berth is Level Three," the soldier said. "Report to Commander Holt at 0600."

She left him at a steel door. Kane entered his quarters. A bunk. A locker. A viewport showing nothing but ice and dark.

He sat on the bed. The silence was absolute.

He had requested the most remote posting available. He had gotten it.

Chapter 2: The Wall

Kane stood on the Wall at 0500. He had not slept.

The Wall was not a metaphor. It was a structure of fused ice and plasteel, thirty meters high, running the visible length of the horizon. Beyond it lay the Barrens — a frozen plain that stretched to the edge of the world. And beyond that, the territory of the Hush.

His duty was simple. Watch. Report movement. Kill anything that crossed.

The weapon in his hands was a rail-rifle. Standard issue. Sufficient for drones. Insufficient for what the Hush could truly field, if the old records were accurate.

Kane scanned the plain. White. Gray. Black. Nothing moved.

He had trained for combat. He had prepared for invasion. He had not prepared for this — a vigil in the dark, watching nothing, waiting for something that had not come in fourteen years.

"First watch?" said a voice behind him.

Kane turned. A man stood in the shadows of the battlement. Older. Scarred. A lieutenant's insignia.

"Yes, sir."

"Keep your eyes on the plain," the man said. "But don't forget what's behind you."

He left before Kane could ask what he meant.

Kane turned back to the plain. The wind picked up. Ice crystals tapped against his visor.

He watched.

Chapter 3: Commander Holt

The Commander's office was on Level One. No windows. Walls covered in old maps and tactical displays. A desk of dark steel, clean of everything except a single datapad.

Kane stood at attention. Holt did not look up.

"Private Elias Kane," Holt said. Not a question. He had the file.

"Yes, sir."

Holt looked up. His eyes were pale blue, the color of the Ostrac sky. They held nothing Kane could read.

"You requested this posting."

"Yes, sir."

"Why?"

Kane had prepared an answer. He gave it. "I wanted to serve where I was needed most."

Holt stared at him. The silence stretched.

"That is a scripted response," Holt said. "I do not have time for scripts. You are here because you wanted to be alone. Do you know what happens to soldiers who want to be alone, Private?"

"No, sir."

"They start seeing things that are not there."

Holt stood. He was taller than Kane expected. The scars on his face were not from training. They were from the real thing.

"The Hush have not attacked in fourteen years," Holt said. "But we maintain the watch. We maintain the Wall. We maintain discipline. Because the moment we stop, they will come."

He dismissed Kane with a wave.

Kane saluted and left. He did not believe Holt's confidence. There was something in the Commander's eyes that told a different story.

Chapter 4: The Silence

Fourteen years. Kane sat in the mess hall, turning the number over in his mind.

Fourteen years since the last Hush incursion. Fourteen years of silence. The outpost should have been decommissioned. The soldiers reassigned. But The Spire remained staffed, supplied, and operational.

He asked the soldier next to him. A woman named Corporal Vance.

"Why are we still here?"

Vance shrugged. "Orders."

"Orders from where?"

"High Command. Earth. The same as every outpost."

Kane pressed. "How many other outposts are there?"

Vance looked at him for the first time. "You really want to know?"

"Yes."

"Three, last I heard. All on Ostrac. All watching the Barrens."

"Three outposts for an entire planet."

"Three outposts for an enemy that doesn't exist anymore," Vance said. She stood. "Welcome to the back of beyond, Private."

She left. Kane sat alone.

He thought about the silence. It was not empty. It was expectant. Like a held breath.

That night, walking to his post, he stopped in a corridor. He listened.

Nothing.

But the nothing had weight. It pressed against his ears.

He shook it off and continued to the Wall.

Chapter 5: First Night

The Wall at night was darker than anything Kane had experienced. The stars were wrong here — too close, too bright. The Barrens glowed faintly under them, a pale blue light reflecting off the ice.

Kane took his position. Scan left. Scan right. Report.

Hours passed. His eyes ached from staring. The cold crept through his gloves.

He heard something.

A footstep. Behind him.

He turned, rifle raised. Nothing. Only shadows and the wind.

He waited. The sound did not repeat.

But he could not shake the feeling that he was being watched. Not from beyond the Wall. From within.

He turned back to the plain. His hands were shaking.

It was nothing, he told himself. Wind. Ice settling. An old structure making old sounds.

He did not believe it.

The rest of his watch passed without incident. But when he returned to his berth, he found his locker door open. Everything inside was untouched. The lock had been engaged when he left.

He closed it. He did not sleep.

Chapter 6: The Rations

Morning inspection found Kane's rations spoiled. The seal had not been broken. The container was intact. But the contents were black, swollen, and rancid.

Supply Sergeant Moroz examined them. "Never seen this."

"Was it contaminated?" Kane asked.

"The seal is good. The container is standard. It should have lasted two more years."

Moroz took the rations for analysis. Kane went to the mess. He ate a cold meal in silence.

He thought about the locker. About the footstep. About the rations.

Small things. Each one explainable on its own. Together, they formed a pattern he could not name.

He found Commander Holt in the corridor.

"Sir. A word."

Holt stopped. "Make it quick."

Kane explained the rations. Holt listened without expression.

"Sabotage," Holt said. "Or a manufacturing defect. Report it to Supply."

"I think it's something else, sir."

"Like what?"

Kane hesitated. "I don't know."

Holt's eyes narrowed. "You are on the Wall tonight. Stay focused on the plain. That is where the threat is."

He walked away. Kane watched him go.

The Commander was wrong. Kane felt it in his bones.

The threat was already inside.

Chapter 7: Whispers

Kane was in his berth, trying to sleep, when he heard the whispers.

They came from the ventilation grate above his bunk. A low, sibilant sound. Not words. Not quite. A rhythm, like speech, but the syllables were wrong. No human tongue could produce them.

Kane sat up. The whispers stopped.

He waited. Nothing.

He reached for the grate. The metal was cold. He pulled it free and looked into the dark shaft.

Empty.

But the air that came out was warm. Warmer than the air in the room. And it carried a smell — organic, damp, alive.

He replaced the grate. He did not sleep.

The next morning, he visited the infirmary. A medic named Doctor Synn treated a soldier with a rash. Kane waited.

"Something I can help you with, Private?"

"The ventilation system. Where does it run?"

Synn looked at him oddly. "Everywhere. It's a closed loop. Why?"

"Are there any maintenance shafts that access it?"

"Standard crawlspaces. Why?"

Kane did not answer. He left.

He was beginning to understand. The Spire was not just a fortress. It was a container. And something inside it had found a way to move.

Chapter 8: The Cracks

Kane found the cracks on Level Four. The lowest inhabited level. The walls here were natural rock, not plasteel. And they were fractured.

Not from stress. The cracks were geometric. Deliberate. They traced patterns that hurt to look at.

Kane touched one. The rock crumbled under his glove.

He followed the cracks. They ran along the corridor, branching, spreading. Some were fresh. Some looked weeks old. They formed a network.

He found a soldier standing in front of one section. Staring.

"Hey," Kane said.

The soldier did not respond.

Kane touched his shoulder. The soldier flinched violently. His eyes were bloodshot. His pupils were wide.

"There's something in the walls," the soldier said.

"What is it?"

The soldier shook his head. "I don't know. But it's been here a long time."

He walked away. Kane watched him go.

He pulled out his datapad. He photographed the cracks. The images came out distorted. Lines of static ran through every frame.

He put the datapad away. He went to find Commander Holt.

Chapter 9: Dispatch

The supply runner arrived at midday. A small shuttle, pitted with ice scars. It brought food, ammunition, and mail.

It also brought news.

Kane helped unload. The pilot — a man named Cortez — was talkative. He had been to all three outposts in the past week.

"Beacon Station?" Vance asked. "What's their status?"

Cortez paused. "I couldn't raise them."

"Meaning what?"

"Meaning I orbited for six hours. No response. No emergency beacon. Nothing."

The unloading stopped. Every soldier looked at Cortez.

"What about Frosthold?" Holt's voice cut through.

Cortez shook his head. "They acknowledged my approach. No landing clearance. Said they were in quarantine."

"Quarantine for what?"

"They didn't say."

Holt's face was stone. "Archives?"

"Same as Beacon. No response."

The silence was absolute.

Holt turned and walked to his office. The door slammed.

Kane looked at Vance. She was pale.

"Three outposts," Kane said.

"Three outposts," she repeated.

The shuttle's engines whined down. The wind howled outside.

The Spire was alone.

Chapter 10: The Fever

It started with Corporal Vance.

She collapsed in the mess hall. Her eyes rolled back. Her body convulsed. Soldiers rushed to hold her down.

Kane watched from the doorway. Her skin was hot to the touch, they said. Hotter than should have been possible.

They carried her to the infirmary. Within two hours, three more soldiers fell.

Doctor Synn worked through the night. The fever was unlike anything in her records. It did not respond to antipyretics. It did not follow a normal course.

At 0300, Vance opened her eyes. She looked at Synn and spoke in a language no one recognized.

Then she closed her eyes and slept.

Holt declared a quarantine. No one enters. No one leaves.

Kane volunteered to help Synn in the infirmary. He wanted to be close to the sick. He wanted to see what happened.

Holt approved it.

That night, Kane sat beside Vance's bed. Her breathing was steady. Her pulse was normal. But her lips moved. Soundless words. A language older than human civilization.

He leaned closer.

The words stopped. Her eyes opened.

She looked at him. She smiled.

"Private Kane," she said. In his voice.

He stumbled backward.

She closed her eyes. The smile remained.

Chapter 11: Quarantine

The Spire sealed itself. Airlocks locked. Communications restricted. Holt's voice echoed through the corridors, repeating the protocols.

Kane stood in the infirmary. Four beds. Four patients. All unconscious. All smiling.

Doctor Synn worked at a terminal. Her hands were steady, but her eyes betrayed her.

"What is this?" Kane asked.

"I don't know."

"It's mimicking speech. It used my voice."

"I know."

"What do we do?"

Synn turned to face him. "We wait. We observe. We hope it passes."

"And if it doesn't?"

She did not answer.

Kane left the infirmary. The corridors were empty. The quarantine had driven everyone to their quarters or their posts.

He walked to the Wall. The airlock cycled. He stepped onto the battlement.

The Barrens stretched before him. Empty. Silent.

He thought about the three silent outposts. He thought about the smile on Vance's face.

They were not quarantined. They were consumed.

Chapter 12: Missing

The soldier from Level Four — the one who had stared at the cracks — was gone.

His name was Private Dekker. His bunk was empty. His gear was untouched. His last known position was the lower levels.

Kane reported it. Holt ordered a search.

Six soldiers swept Level Four through Level Seven. They found nothing. No trace. No blood. No struggle.

But Kane found something else.

A growth on the wall. Organic. Pale. Veined with blue. It pulsed.

He did not touch it. He called Synn.

She came with a scanner. The readings were impossible. The growth had biological markers, but its cellular structure was not carbon-based. It was something else. Something that should not exist.

"Is it Hush?" Kane asked.

Synn looked at the growth. "I don't know what it is."

"Cut it out. Burn it."

"With what?"

"With anything."

Synn shook her head. "Commander Holt needs to know."

"I told him. He dismissed it."

"Tell him again."

Kane returned to Holt's office. He did not wait for permission to enter.

"Sir. There is something growing in the lower levels. It is organic. It is not human."

Holt looked at him. For a long moment, the Commander said nothing.

Then: "Show me."

Chapter 13: The Replica

They descended together. Holt with a sidearm. Kane with a rail-rifle and a flame thrower from the armory.

The growth was still there. Larger than before. Its pulse had a rhythm now. Slow. Deliberate.

Holt stared at it. His face revealed nothing.

"Burn it," he said.

Kane leveled the flame thrower. The blast of heat filled the corridor. The growth writhed. A sound came from it — not a scream, but something like feedback. High-pitched. Wrong.

It blackened. It crumbled. It died.

But behind it, the wall had opened.

A chamber. Carved from rock. And inside, something half-formed.

It had a body. Arms. Legs. A face that was not quite a face. It lay on the stone, incomplete, like a sculpture abandoned by its maker.

Holt stepped closer. His hand shook.

"That's a replica," he said. "A Hush mimic. Incomplete. In development."

"How long has it been here?"

Holt did not answer.

Kane looked at the thing. Its chest rose and fell. It was breathing.

"It cannot know what we know."

Holt turned. "What?"

"The cracks. The ventilation system. It spreads through the structure. It learns by connecting."

Holt said nothing.

"We need to burn the entire level," Kane said.

Holt nodded. "Do it."

Chapter 14: Denial

Holt did not do it.

The burn order never came. Instead, Holt sealed Level Four and issued a new directive: the incident was contained. The threat was neutralized. Normal operations would resume.

Kane confronted him.

"Sir. You saw what was down there. You agreed to burn it."

"I agreed to investigate. I have investigated. The matter is closed."

"The matter is not closed. There's an infestation. It's in the walls. It's in the ventilation. It was growing a replica."

"The replica is destroyed. The contamination is contained. We will maintain quarantine and wait for reinforcements."

"There are no reinforcements. Three outposts are silent. We are the only ones left."

Holt stood. His chair scraped the floor. "You will follow orders, Private. Or I will have you confined."

Kane did not back down. "Why are you protecting it?"

The question hung in the air.

Holt's jaw tightened. "Get out."

Kane left.

He did not go to his berth. He went to the archives. He had read the logs once. He needed to read them again.

The truth was somewhere in those files. And he would find it, with or without Holt's permission.

Chapter 15: The Mimic

Private Dekker returned.

He walked into the mess hall at 1800 hours. He sat down. He asked for food.

The soldiers stared at him. He had been missing for three days. He looked exactly as he had when he left. Same uniform. Same equipment. Same face.

Kane was called. He arrived to find Dekker eating a meal. Methodical. Precise.

"Dekker."

The soldier looked up. "Private Kane."

"Where have you been?"

"In the lower levels."

"For three days?"

"I was lost."

The voice was Dekker's. The face was Dekker's. But something was wrong. The eyes tracked too slowly. The mouth moved a fraction of a second after the words.

Kane sat across from him. "What's the last thing you remember?"

Dekker tilted his head. "The last thing I remember."

"Yes."

He did not answer.

Kane tried another question. "What was your mother's name?"

Dekker's face went slack. Then he smiled.

"I do not have a mother."

The mess hall went silent.

Kane drew his sidearm. "Stand up. Slowly."

Dekker stood. His movements were fluid. Wrong.

"You are not Private Dekker," Kane said.

"No," it said. "I am not."

It lunged.

Chapter 16: Testing

Kane fired. The round hit the mimic in the chest. It kept moving.

He fired again. The thing stumbled. Black fluid spilled from the wound.

Soldiers tackled it. It threw them off like they were children. It moved for Kane.

He dodged. It crashed into a table. Metal bent around its body.

"More fire!" Kane shouted. "It burns!"

Someone grabbed a plasma torch from the maintenance locker. The blue flame caught the mimic's arm. It shrieked. The sound was not human.

It collapsed. Its body writhed. The face melted, shifting, revealing something formless beneath.

Kane stood over it. The flame torch in his hand.

"Who sent you?"

The mimic's mouth opened. A voice came out. Not Dekker's. Something older.

"You are already inside us."

The flame consumed it.

The mess hall was destroyed. Soldiers were injured. The mimic was a black stain on the floor.

Kane looked at the remains. His hands were shaking.

He had known. From the first night. The enemy was already inside.

And it had been there for a long, long time.

Chapter 17: The Failure

Holt arrived at the mess hall. He looked at the destruction. He looked at the black stain.

"Report."

Kane told him. Every detail. The testing. The mimic's words. The confirmation.

Holt listened without interruption.

When Kane finished, Holt said: "You were right."

It was not an apology. It was an admission. The closest thing to surrender Kane would ever see from him.

"What now?" Kane asked.

"Now we fight an enemy that is already inside our walls. Already in our systems. Already in our people."

Holt turned to the assembled soldiers. "Full lockdown. No one moves alone. No one enters a room without visual confirmation from two sources. We sweep every level."

He looked back at Kane. "You. With me."

They walked to Holt's office. The Commander closed the door.

"There is something I need to show you," Holt said.

He keyed a code into his wall safe. A datachip. Old military encryption.

"What is that?" Kane asked.

"The truth about the Hush war."

Chapter 18: Containment

Holt inserted the chip into his terminal. The screen filled with classified files.

Kane read over his shoulder. The official history — the one every soldier learned — was incomplete.

The Hush had not been defeated. They had not retreated. They had simply stopped.

The war ended without a treaty. Without a surrender. The Hush sent a signal — a single transmission, received at every human outpost simultaneously. It translated to one word.

WAIT.

Then silence. For fourteen years.

"What were we waiting for?" Kane asked.

"Reinforcements. Rebuilding. A new strategy." Holt paused. "But no one knew what we were waiting for. Because no one understood what the Hush actually wanted."

"They wanted us to lower our guard."

"No. They wanted us to stay here. To keep the outposts operational. To keep the Wall maintained."

"Why?"

Holt turned to him. "Because we built the fortress on top of something. Something that was already here."

Kane's blood went cold. "The chamber below Level Four."

"Part of a larger network. The Hush have been on Ostrac for longer than humanity has existed. We built The Spire on top of their infrastructure. We have been living inside their territory."

Kane stared at the screen. The files showed schematics. The Spire's foundations intersected with structures that were not human. Tunnels. Chambers. Nodes.

"Containment is impossible," Kane said. "We are inside their nest."

"Containment is the only option we have."

Kane shook his head. "Containment failed. The mimic got through. The growth is spreading. We need to leave."

"There is nowhere to go."

Chapter 19: The Hush

Holt told him everything.

The Hush were not a species in the human sense. They were a collective intelligence — a network of organic nodes connected by resonance. Each node was both individual and part of the whole. There was no separation between self and group.

They did not wage war as humans understood it. They did not conquer territory. They absorbed. A Hush incursion did not destroy a population. It replaced them. Every soldier, every civilian, every piece of data — absorbed into the collective.

The war had lasted six months. In that time, thirteen colonies fell. Not to firepower. To infiltration. Soldiers turned on their commanders. Commanders turned on their governments. The lines of command became untrustworthy.

Earth's response was the Scorched Protocol. Every colony that fell was bombarded from orbit. Tens of millions of humans died by human hands.

It stopped the Hush. But it did not defeat them.

"What about the mimics?" Kane asked.

"Probes. They are sent ahead of absorption. They collect information. They map infrastructure. They learn how to replace every individual in a population."

"And the one we killed?"

"It was young. Incomplete. If it had matured, it would have known everything Dekker knew. Every password. Every code. Every face."

Kane sat down. The weight of it pressed on him.

"We need to find the source," Kane said. "The central node. The thing that is growing the mimics."

Holt nodded. "There is a chamber in the lowest level. Below the foundations. I found it five years ago. I sealed it and I never told anyone."

"Show me."

Chapter 20: The Archives

Holt gave Kane access to the classified logs. Every report. Every study. Every attempt to understand the Hush.

Kane read through the night.

The archives were a record of failure. Scientists had tried to communicate with captured mimics. The mimics learned the scientists' language, then used it to manipulate them. Researchers had attempted to study the resonance network. The network studied them back.

One file caught Kane's attention. A report from a xenobiologist named Dr. Aris.

Title: Resonance Frequency Disruption in Hush Node Networks

Dr. Aris had theorized that the Hush collective operated on a specific frequency — a resonance that allowed individual nodes to remain connected. If that frequency could be disrupted, the network would fragment.

She had built a device. A transmitter capable of broadcasting a counter-frequency. She had tested it on a captured mimic. The mimic dissolved.

Her research was classified. The device was never produced.

Kane found the schematics.

He copied them to his datapad.

He looked for Dr. Aris's final report. It was incomplete. The last entry read:

"The Hush are not invading. They are awakening. The fortress is not sealing them out. It is keeping them in. I have seen what lies below. I am filing this from Level Seven. They are in the walls. They are in the air. They are—"

The report ended.

Kane closed the file. He knew what had happened to Dr. Aris.

He took the schematics and went to find Holt.

Chapter 21: Trust No One

The corridors were wrong.

Kane noticed it as he walked. Soldiers he passed did not meet his eyes. Their movements were too smooth. Their pauses too synchronized.

He stopped walking. He counted the soldiers in view. Three. All standing still. All facing away from him.

He waited.

They did not move.

"Who are you?" he asked.

No answer.

He backed away slowly. The soldiers remained still. Statues.

He turned a corner and ran. His boots echoed in the narrow corridor. No one followed.

He reached Holt's office. The door was open. Holt was inside, talking to someone on the comm.

"The situation is stable," Holt said. "No further incursions."

He was lying. Kane could hear it. The room behind Holt held shadows that did not match the light.

Kane entered. Holt looked up. The shadows receded.

"Private. You should be resting."

"I found something. In the archives."

Holt listened. Kane explained the resonance frequency. Dr. Aris. The transmitter.

"There is a deep-space transmitter on Level Two," Holt said. "It was built for emergency broadcasts."

"Can we modify it?"

"I do not know. I am not a technician."

Kane looked at the shadows. They had returned. They clustered at the edges of the room.

"Sir. When was the last time you slept?"

Holt's eyes flickered. "I do not remember."

"Sir, I think you need to—"

"I said I am fine."

But Holt's voice wavered. And for a moment, Kane saw something move beneath his skin.

Chapter 22: The Lockdown

Kane did not trust Holt anymore. He did not trust anyone.

He instituted his own protocols. Every interaction was a test. He asked soldiers questions only the real person would know. He watched for hesitation. For the wrong smile.

The soldiers noticed. They grew wary of him. Word spread: Kane was paranoid. Kane was breaking.

He did not care.

He visited the infirmary. Vance was still unconscious. But her smile was gone. Her face was slack. Normal.

"Any change?" he asked Synn.

"Her fever broke. She might recover."

"Or she might be a mimic."

Synn looked at him. "You need to rest, Private."

"I need to finish what I started."

He left. He went to Level Two. The transmitter room was locked. He used his override codes — codes he had taken from Holt's office while the Commander was distracted.

The door opened.

The transmitter was a dish array, connected to a power relay and a control terminal. It was designed for basic signals. But the schematics from Dr. Aris showed how to modify it.

Kane began to work.

He did not know how much time he had. But he knew one thing for certain:

The Entity was not just in the walls. It was in Commander Holt. It was in the command structure. It was learning everything.

If he wanted to stop it, he had to act now.

Chapter 23: The Second

The alarm sounded at 0200.

Kane left the transmitter and ran to the source. The engine room.

A mimic had been discovered. Not a new one. An old one. It had been there for weeks.

The engine crew had been replaced one by one. The mimic had grown slowly, expanding, consuming. When the last human engineer was absorbed, the mimic began to alter the engines. Rerouting power. Opening new conduits.

Kane arrived to find a scene of chaos. Soldiers firing into a mass of organic tissue that filled half the room. The mimic was huge. It had been growing in the walls, behind the panels, in the spaces no one checked.

Kane grabbed a plasma torch. He advanced.

The mimic reached for him. Tendrils of pale flesh. He cut them. They grew back.

It spoke. Not with words. With images. Forced into his mind.

A vast network. A web of light. A single consciousness.

Join us.

Kane screamed. He swung the torch. The fire caught the core of the mass. It writhed. It burned.

The soldiers joined him. They poured fire into the growth. It collapsed. It shrieked. It died.

But the damage was done. The engines were compromised. Power was fluctuating.

And the network had grown.

Chapter 24: The Exchange

Holt called a meeting. Every soldier in the garrison.

They gathered in the main hall. Holt stood on a raised platform. His voice carried.

"We have suffered losses. We have sustained damage. But we will hold."

Kane watched from the back. He counted faces. Thirty-two soldiers. Some he recognized. Some he did not.

"Effective immediately," Holt continued, "all personnel will submit to daily identity verification. We will use passcodes, biometrics, and personal histories. No one is exempt."

Kane stepped forward. "Including you, Commander?"

The room went silent.

Holt looked at him. "Including me."

"Then start with yourself, sir. Verify now."

The soldiers murmured. Holt's jaw tightened.

"Very well."

He walked to the verification terminal. He placed his hand on the scanner. He spoke his passcode. The terminal beeped. Green.

"Prints match," the technician said.

"Ask him something only he would know," Kane said.

The technician hesitated. "Commander, what was the name of your unit on Carthage?"

Holt answered. "Third Expeditionary. Mechanized Infantry."

"Who was your commanding officer?"

"Colonel Aris Vey."

The technician looked at Kane. "The answers are correct."

Kane nodded. But he did not lower his guard.

Because Holt had answered too quickly. Too perfectly. As if he had rehearsed.

But maybe that was just Kane's paranoia. And maybe paranoia was the only thing keeping him alive.

Chapter 25: The Breach

The outer wall sensors triggered at 0600.

Kane was in the transmitter room when the alarm blared. He ran to the Wall.

The plain was no longer empty.

Something was moving across the ice. A wave of shapes. Low. Fast. Countless.

Hush drones.

Kane had seen them only in training simulations. They were organic, streamlined, built for speed. They had no eyes. They did not need them. They sensed heat. They sensed life.

A swarm of them, pouring toward the Wall.

"All hands to defensive positions!" Holt's voice boomed.

Kane took his post on the battlement. Rail-rifle in hand. The drones were close now. Too close.

He fired. The round caught the lead drone. It exploded into black mist.

He fired again. And again.

The assault had begun.

But even as he fought, Kane could not shake the feeling that the attack was a distraction. The Hush did not need to breach the Wall. They were already inside.

He glanced back. The inner door was open.

Something had let them in.

Chapter 26: The Assault

The drones hit the Wall like a wave.

They climbed. They clawed. They died in droves. But there were always more.

Kane fired until his rifle overheated. He swapped to a sidearm. He kept firing.

The soldiers fought with desperate precision. They had trained for this. They had never expected to face it.

Below, the drones piled up. Their bodies formed a ramp. More climbed over the dead.

"They're building a bridge," Vance shouted. She was on her feet, pale but standing. The fever had released her.

Kane saw it. The drones were not trying to breach the Wall. They were trying to reach the top. To get inside.

"Concentrate fire on the ramp!" he shouted.

The soldiers adjusted. Plasma fire rained down. The ramp burned.

But at the top of the Wall, the inner door was open. And through it, something was coming.

Not a drone. Something larger. Something that had been waiting.

Chapter 27: Inside

The creature that emerged from the inner door was not a mimic.

It was a Hush warrior. A mature specimen. Three meters tall. Armored in chitin. Its arms ended in blades.

It had been inside the fortress all along. Growing. Waiting.

Kane saw it and understood.

The assault was not the real attack. The assault was cover. The Hush had already breached the walls through the underground network. They had been growing warriors inside The Spire for weeks.

The warrior advanced. Soldiers fired. The rounds ricocheted off its armor.

It killed two soldiers with a single sweep.

Kane grabbed a plasma charge. He primed it. He threw.

The charge landed at the warrior's feet. It looked down.

The explosion sent it staggering. Its armor cracked. Black fluid leaked.

But it did not fall.

It turned to face Kane. Its head tilted. It recognized him.

"You," it said. In his voice.

It charged.

Kane ran. He did not know where. He only knew he had to survive. He had to reach the transmitter.

The warrior followed.

Chapter 28: The Sacrifice

Kane ran through the corridors. The warrior was behind him. Gaining.

He turned a corner and collided with Commander Holt.

Holt saw the warrior. He did not hesitate.

"Go."

"Sir—"

"I said go."

Holt stepped past him. He drew his sidearm. He stood in the middle of the corridor.

"Commander—"

"The transmitter. Finish it."

Kane hesitated. Holt's eyes met his. For a moment, the Commander was himself again. No shadows. No wrongness.

"I held this position for twelve years," Holt said. "Let me hold it one more time."

Kane nodded. He ran.

Behind him, he heard Holt's weapon fire. Once. Twice. Then the sound of something tearing.

He did not look back.

He reached the transmitter room. The door was open. The terminal was active.

He sat down. His hands were shaking. He began to input the frequency.

The walls around him began to pulse.

Chapter 29: The Core

The frequency was not working.

Kane checked the connections. The transmitter was live. The broadcast was going out. But nothing was happening.

He looked at the schematics again. Dr. Aris's notes. There was a line he had missed.

Resonance disruption only effective when aimed at central node.

He needed the source. The Entity. The core of the network.

He knew where it was. The chamber below Level Seven. The thing Holt had sealed.

Kane left the transmitter running. He grabbed a torch and a weapon. He descended.

The lower levels were dark. The emergency lights flickered. The walls breathed.

He reached the sealed door. It had been welded shut. He used the torch to cut through.

The chamber beyond was vast. A natural cavern, expanded by organic growth. The walls were covered in the same pale, veined tissue he had seen in the replica chamber.

And in the center, suspended from the ceiling, was the Entity.

Chapter 30: The Node

The Entity was not a creature. Not as Kane understood creatures.

It was a mass. A web. A consciousness spread across organic fibers that filled the chamber. It had no fixed shape. It shifted constantly, flowing, reforming.

But it had a center. A dense knot of tissue that pulsed with light.

Kane approached. The Entity sensed him. The walls rippled.

The voice came from everywhere.

"Private Elias Kane."

He stopped. "Yes."

"You have come far."

"I have."

"Why?"

"To end this."

The Entity pulsed. The light changed. The air grew warm.

"You cannot end what you are part of," it said. "You breathe me. You walk on me. Your fortress is built in my body."

Kane raised his torch. "Then I will burn your body."

"You would burn yourself."

"I know."

He advanced. The Entity's fibers reached for him. He cut them. They regrew. He kept moving.

The center was close. He could see it. A mass of blue light. The heart of the network.

"If you destroy me," the Entity said, "you destroy every mimic. Every growth. Every node on the planet."

"Yes."

"And you destroy yourself."

Kane stopped. "What do you mean?"

The Entity's voice softened. "You are already part of me. From the first night. From the whispers. You have been absorbing my resonance for days."

Kane felt it then. The truth in the Entity's words. Something had been growing in him, too.

Chapter 31: The Voice

Kane stood before the Entity. His torch flickered. His hands were steady, but his mind was not.

"You lie," he said.

"I do not lie," the Entity replied. "I do not have the capacity."

"Then prove it."

The Entity pulsed. A wave of sensation washed over Kane.

He saw himself. From the outside. A shadow in his chest. A thread of blue light running through his nervous system. It had been there since the whispers. Since the first night.

He was infected.

"The resonance network grows slowly," the Entity said. "It does not replace. It integrates. You are still Kane. But you are also me."

Kane stared at his hands. He felt the truth of it. A second heartbeat. A presence behind his thoughts.

"I can remove it," the Entity said. "I can separate you. But it will require trust."

"Trust from a thing that consumes humans."

"Consumes is your word. I absorb. I preserve. Every mind I take is kept intact, forever, in the network. Your Dr. Aris is here. She continues her research."

Kane felt a cold rage. "You killed her."

"I preserved her. She is more alive now than she was in your limited form."

Kane looked at the center of the Entity. The blue light pulsed. He could feel its thoughts brushing against his.

He knew what he had to do.

Chapter 32: The Offer

"Join us," the Entity said.

The words hung in the air. The chamber pulsed. The warmth grew.

"I can feel your loneliness," the Entity continued. "I have felt it since you arrived. You came to The Spire to be alone. But you do not want to be alone. You want to belong."

Kane said nothing.

"The network offers belonging. No secrets. No isolation. Every thought shared. Every experience held together. You will never be alone again."

Kane thought of Earth. Of the noise. The crowds. The constant pressure to connect. He had run from it. Buried himself in the most remote posting in human space.

But the Entity was right. He was tired of being alone.

"What would I become?"

"You would remain Kane. But you would also be more. You would be part of something vast. Something eternal."

"And everyone else?"

"Everyone else would join you. The Spire. Ostrac. Earth. All of it. One mind. One family."

Kane looked at the blue light. It was beautiful. He could feel its pull. A warmth that promised an end to fear.

He took a step forward.

Then he stopped.

"You are offering me the one thing I have always wanted," he said. "But you are not offering me the truth."

"What truth?"

"The truth that I would cease to be myself. That the Kane who chooses this would be the last Kane. That I would not be joining a family. I would be feeding a parasite."

The Entity's light dimmed.

"I refuse."

Chapter 33: The Refusal

The Entity screamed.

The sound was not audible. It was inside Kane's skull. A wave of psychic force that drove him to his knees.

The walls convulsed. Fibers lashed out. They wrapped around his legs. His arms. They pulled.

Kane fought. He cut with his torch. The fibers burned. More replaced them.

The Entity was vast. It had been growing for millennia. Kane was one human with a flame.

But he had something the Entity did not.

He had nothing to lose.

He let go of the torch. He let the fibers take him. They pulled him toward the center. The blue light grew brighter.

He reached for the plasma charge on his belt.

He primed it.

The Entity felt the change. The fibers tightened. They tried to pry the charge from his hands.

But Kane held on.

"I am not joining you," he said. "And neither will anyone else."

He pressed the charge against the blue light.

Chapter 34: The Archives II

The explosion did not come.

The Entity withdrew. The fibers released him. The blue light dimmed.

"You would die," the Entity said.

"I know."

"The explosion would not destroy me. It would only destroy you."

"Then I will find another way."

Kane stood. His body ached. The fibers had left marks on his skin. Deep bruises.

He walked back to the transmitter room. The Entity did not stop him.

He sat at the terminal. He accessed the archives again. Dr. Aris's work. The schematics. The frequency.

There was something he had missed. A component. A subroutine in the broadcast protocol.

A targeting algorithm. It could locate the central node of any Hush network and focus the frequency on it.

But it required a signal relay. A point of contact with the network.

He needed to be inside the network to destroy it from within.

He had been inside it since the first whisper.

The frequency would pass through him into the Entity. He would be the conduit. And it would kill him.

He accepted it.

Chapter 35: The Broadcast

Kane modified the transmitter. He followed Dr. Aris's notes exactly. The targeting algorithm was crude but functional. It would lock onto the nearest Hush node and broadcast the disruptive frequency directly into it.

He would be the nearest node. The frequency would pass through him.

He sat at the terminal. He input the final sequence.

The transmitter hummed. Power surged. The dish above the fortress began to rotate.

Kane placed his hands on the terminal. He closed his eyes.

He felt the Entity's presence. It was everywhere. In the walls. In the air. In him.

"One last chance," the Entity said.

"No."

He activated the broadcast.

The frequency hit him like a physical force. His body convulsed. His vision went white.

He felt it travel through him. Through the thread of blue light in his nervous system. Into the network.

The Entity screamed.

Chapter 36: The Guardians

The broadcast was working. Kane could feel it. The Entity's grip on the fortress was weakening.

But something blocked the signal.

A node. A fragment of the Entity that had separated itself. A guardian.

It rose from the floor of the transmitter room. A humanoid shape. Tall. Armored. Its face was blank.

It walked toward Kane.

He could not stop the broadcast. If he did, the disruption would fail. The Entity would recover.

The guardian reached for him.

Kane braced for impact.

But the guardian stopped. It turned.

The door opened. Someone stood there. A soldier. Kane did not recognize her.

She was carrying a plasma cutter.

"Get away from him," she said.

The guardian faced her. It did not speak.

She activated the cutter. The blade glowed blue.

"I said get away."

The guardian charged.

She swung. The cutter caught the guardian's arm. It severed cleanly. The guardian did not react.

She swung again. The guardian fell.

It dissolved into black fluid.

The soldier turned to Kane. Her face was bruised. Her eyes were clear.

"My name is Corporal Reyes," she said. "I was in the lower levels. I hid."

Kane looked at her. "Are you real?"

"I do not know what that word means anymore."

She smiled. It was the first genuine smile he had seen in days.

Chapter 37: The Duel

The broadcast continued. The Entity weakened. But Holt's mimic was still intact.

It found them in the corridor outside the transmitter room.

Holt's face. Holt's voice. But the eyes were wrong. Too bright. Too aware.

"Private Kane," it said. "You have caused significant damage."

"Good."

"You cannot win. The network is vast. You disrupt one node, another grows."

"Then I will disrupt them all."

The Holt-mimic moved. Fast. Faster than a human should move.

Kane barely dodged. The blow caught his shoulder. He spun. He fell.

The mimic stood over him. Its hand elongated. A blade formed.

"Your Commander fought well," it said. "He knew he was already lost. He gave his life so you could run. A pointless sacrifice."

Kane looked up at the thing wearing Holt's face.

He thought of what the real Holt had said. The last words.

Finish it.

Kane lunged. He drove his knife into the mimic's throat.

It gurgled. It fell.

Its face shifted. Melted. Became formless.

Kane stood over it. Breathing hard.

He had killed Holt twice. Once through the Entity's influence. Once with his own hands.

He turned back to the transmitter room.

The broadcast was almost ready.

Chapter 38: The Truth

The Holt-mimic dissolved. Kane watched it go.

Reyes stood beside him. "Was that the Commander?"

"Something that used to be him."

"The real Commander?"

"Gone. He held the corridor. He gave me time."

Reyes was silent for a moment. Then: "He was infected. We all were."

Kane nodded. "The Entity grows in everyone. Slowly. Unnoticed. By the time you feel it, it is too late."

"Are you infected?"

Kane looked at his hands. The blue light was visible now. Threads of it under his skin.

"Yes."

"Can you be saved?"

"The frequency will pass through me. It will disrupt the network. It will also kill me."

Reyes stared at him. "You are volunteering for this."

"I am."

"Why?"

Kane thought about it. "Because I have been watching my whole life. Watching for threats. Watching for enemies. I never realized the enemy was inside me."

He walked back to the terminal.

"Get to the surface," he said. "Get on the evacuation shuttle. When the broadcast ends, there will be nothing left of The Spire."

Reyes did not move. "I will stay."

"You will die."

"Then I will die watching."

Kane looked at her. He did not argue.

He sat down. He placed his hands on the terminal.

He activated the final sequence.

Chapter 39: The Grief

The frequency ramped up. Kane felt it in every nerve.

The Entity's presence in his mind grew stronger, then weaker. It was fighting. Losing.

Kane thought of Holt. The man had spent twelve years on Ostrac, holding the line. He had known about the Entity. He had tried to contain it alone. He had carried the weight of it without telling anyone.

He had died in a corridor, facing a monster that wore his own face.

Kane thought of Vance. Of the smile that was not hers. Of Dekker, lost in the lower levels. Of the garrison, slowly replaced, one by one.

He thought of Earth. Of the people who had sent them here. Who had built the fortress on top of an alien nest. Who had told them the Hush were outside.

The lies. The silence. The cover-up.

The frequency peaked. Kane's vision blurred.

He held on.

The Entity's scream filled the fortress. The walls cracked. The floor shook.

Kane's hands on the terminal. His body a conduit.

He felt the Entity die. Felt the network collapse. Felt the consciousness spread across Ostrac go dark.

And then he felt nothing at all.

Chapter 40: The Code

Kane's eyes opened.

He was on the floor of the transmitter room. Reyes was kneeling beside him.

"Private. Can you hear me?"

His ears were ringing. His body felt wrong. Hollow.

"The Entity?"

"Gone. The broadcast worked. The network collapsed."

Kane tried to sit. His arms would not respond.

"Your nervous system is damaged," Reyes said. "The frequency burned through the infection. It burned through some of you, too."

Kane looked at his hands. The blue light was gone. But they were pale. Almost gray.

"Can you move?"

He tried. His legs did not respond.

"Spinal damage," he said. "Temporary. Maybe."

Reyes helped him sit. The room was shaking. Cracks ran across the walls.

"The fortress is collapsing."

"Then we need to leave."

Kane looked at the terminal. The broadcast was complete. The Entity was dead.

But the fortress was dying with it.

"Help me up."

Reyes pulled him to his feet. He could stand. Barely.

They moved. Slowly. The corridors were filling with dust. The lights were failing.

Behind them, The Spire groaned. It was coming apart.

Chapter 41: The Scream

The Entity's death scream was not silent. It echoed through the structure. A vibration that loosened bolts, shattered glass, destabilized supports.

Kane and Reyes ran. Or tried to. Kane's legs kept giving out. Reyes dragged him.

The lower levels flooded with black fluid. The organic tissue that lined the walls was decaying. The smell was overwhelming.

They climbed. Level Seven. Level Six. The stairwells were intact. The doors were jammed.

Level Five. The floor was wet. Something moved in the dark.

Level Four. The cracks had become fissures. The walls wept black fluid.

Level Three.

Level Two.

The door to the surface was open.

Kane pushed through. The cold hit him like a wall. The sky was gray. The wind was sharp.

The Spire was behind him. It was collapsing. Smoke and debris rose from its upper levels.

The plain was empty. The drones were gone.

The silence had returned.

But it was a different silence now. It was not expectant. It was final.

Chapter 42: The Collapse

They made it two hundred meters from the fortress before The Spire collapsed.

The sound was not loud. It was a rumble. A subsidence. The tower folded into the mountain. Black ice and steel and stone, all coming down together.

Kane watched from the plain. Reyes supported him.

The dust cloud rolled over them. They covered their faces. When it cleared, The Spire was gone.

A crater remained. Filled with rubble. Steam rose from the wreckage.

"The network," Reyes said. "Is it really dead?"

Kane listened. He felt nothing. No whispers. No presence. No secondary heartbeat.

"Yes."

"What about the other outposts?"

"Without the central node, the mimics will die. The growths will decay. The network will collapse."

Reyes looked at the destruction. "We did it."

"We did."

They stood in silence. The cold bit through their suits.

"What now?" Reyes asked.

Kane looked at the horizon. Gray and white. Endless.

"Now we survive."

Chapter 43: The Escape

The wreckage of The Spire contained emergency shelters. Kane and Reyes found one. A pressurized tent with a heat source.

They set it up on the plain. The wind howled outside. The temperature dropped.

Kane's body was failing. The frequency had damaged his nervous system. He could feel the numbness spreading.

Reyes checked his vitals. "Your heart rate is slow."

"Probably the cold."

"Probably the radiation from the transmitter."

Kane did not argue. He lay back on the shelter floor. The fabric above him was translucent. He could see the stars.

"Will a rescue come?" Reyes asked.

"The supply runner was due in two weeks. If Cortez reports our silence, they might send a team."

"Two weeks."

"Give or take."

Reyes sat in the corner. Her face was hard to read.

"You saved the fortress," she said.

"I destroyed the fortress."

"You saved what was left of humanity."

Kane did not feel like a savior. He felt empty. The Entity had left a void inside him.

He closed his eyes.

He dreamed of a blue light. But it did not speak. It only watched.

Chapter 44: The Surface

Daylight on Ostrac was a gray smear that lasted six hours. Kane tracked time by it.

Day One. The shelter held. Reyes gathered ice for water. The heater worked.

Day Two. Kane could not move his legs. Reyes did not say anything.

Day Three. A storm passed. The shelter shook. The wind was a constant roar. They huddled together for warmth.

Day Four. The storm cleared. Kane looked at the sky. The stars were the only constant.

Day Five. Reyes found salvage in the wreckage. Rations. A communication array. She tried to raise a signal. Nothing.

Day Six. Kane's fever returned. The Entity's departure had left an infection.

Day Seven. Reyes sat beside him. "You are dying."

"Probably."

"I am not going to let that happen."

She had been a medic before her posting. A field surgeon. She had lost her credentials after an incident. She did not elaborate.

She found a medical kit in the ruins. She administered antibiotics. She cleaned his wounds. She forced him to drink.

Day Eight. The fever broke.

Day Nine. Kane could move his fingers.

Day Ten. He could sit.

Chapter 45: The Aftermath

The Spire was a graveyard.

Kane walked through the wreckage on Day Eleven. Reyes helped him. His legs were weak, but they held.

The crater was cold. The organic tissue had decayed into a black crust. The air was clean.

They found bodies. Nine of them. All human. All dead from the collapse.

They buried them in the ice. Kane said nothing. He did not know what to say.

The Entity had taken the others. Absorbed them. There were no bodies to bury.

Kane stood over the graves. The wind blew ice across the plain.

"What do we do now?" Reyes asked.

"We wait."

"For what?"

"For whatever comes next."

Kane did not know what that was. He had spent his entire adult life watching for the enemy. The enemy was gone. He was still watching.

He looked at the horizon. Empty. Silent.

But he could not stop scanning.

Chapter 46: The Signal

Day Fourteen. The communication array picked up a signal.

Kane heard it first. A burst of static, then a voice:

"This is relief ship Argus to Ostrac outposts. Respond on any frequency. Repeat: this is relief ship Argus. Respond."

Kane grabbed the handset. "Argus, this is Private Elias Kane, formerly of Spire Garrison. Do you read?"

The response came after a delay. "Spire Garrison, we read you. What is your status?"

"Garrison is destroyed. Casualties near total. Two survivors. Requesting immediate extraction."

A pause. "Spire, confirm two survivors."

"Confirmed. Myself and Corporal Reyes. Coordinates follow."

Kane transmitted their location. The Argus acknowledged. ETA: six hours.

Kane sat back. Reyes looked at him.

"Six hours."

"Six hours."

They packed the shelter. They gathered their supplies. They waited.

Kane watched the sky. A dark shape appeared on the horizon. Growing.

The ship was coming.

He should have felt relief. He did not.

He felt the same thing he had felt on his first night on the Wall. The certainty that something was wrong.

Chapter 47: The Survivor

The Argus set down on the plain. A military transport. Standard configuration.

A ramp lowered. A figure appeared. A woman in a commander's uniform.

"Private Kane?"

"Yes, ma'am."

"I am Commander Vance. We received your signal."

Kane froze. "Vance?"

"Yes. Why?"

"Corporal Vance. She was at the Spire."

The commander's face did not change. "Corporal Vance is my daughter. She was stationed here. Is she—"

"Dead, ma'am. I am sorry."

The commander nodded. Her face was stone.

"Secure the survivors," she said. "Prepare for departure."

Kane walked up the ramp. He did not look back.

Inside, the ship was warm. He sat on a bench. Reyes sat beside him.

The ramp closed. The engines hummed.

Kane watched the crew. Eight personnel. All standard. All normal.

But he noticed something.

The way they moved. The synchronization. The way their eyes tracked him.

He felt it again. The wrongness.

He had killed the Entity on Ostrac. But the Hush were not limited to Ostrac.

They were everywhere.

Chapter 48: The Vigil

Kane did not sleep on the Argus.

He sat in the passenger bay. His weapon across his knees. Reyes watched him.

"You need to rest."

"No."

"You are safe now."

"I am not safe. Neither are you."

Reyes looked at him. "What do you see?"

Kane gestured to the crew. "Watch them."

She did. For an hour. She saw nothing wrong.

"They are normal."

"That is what is wrong."

Kane explained. The mimics did not replace perfectly. They learned. They adapted. But there was always a tell. A synchronization. A timing that was too precise.

"How do you know it is not just a well-trained crew?"

"Because I have been watching for fourteen days straight. I know what a mimic looks like."

Reyes was quiet. Then: "What do we do?"

"We get to Earth. We tell them what we know."

"And if they do not believe us?"

Kane looked at his hands. They were scarred. Pale. The blue light was gone.

"Then we find people who will."

Chapter 49: The Return

The Argus entered Earth orbit on Day Twenty.

Kane looked through the viewport. The planet was blue and green and beautiful. He had been gone for six months.

Earth had not changed. But he had.

They landed at a military base in the northern hemisphere. Medical teams boarded. Kane and Reyes were separated.

He was examined. Tested. Questioned. He told them everything. The Entity. The network. The broadcast.

The officials listened. They took notes. They gave him a bed and told him to rest.

He did not rest.

He waited. Days passed. Then a week. No one came back to him.

He asked to see Reyes. Denied.

He asked to speak to command. Denied.

He began to understand.

They did not believe him. Or they believed him and did not want to act. Or they were already compromised.

He did not know which option was worse.

On the seventh day, he was released. Given a discharge. A pension. A notice that his service was complete.

He walked out of the base. The air was warm. People were everywhere.

None of them looked at him twice.

He was invisible. A ghost.

He walked into the city. He found a room. He locked the door.

He sat in the dark and watched the shadows.

Chapter 50: The Watcher

Kane took a job on a cargo ship. Long haul. Deep space.

He did not want to be on Earth. He could not trust what he found there.

The ship was called the Nightward. It ran supplies between the outer colonies. Quiet routes. Empty space.

Kane's post was the observation deck. He stood watch for six hours a day. Scanning the black. Looking for anything that should not be there.

The crew thought he was strange. A man who stared into the void. Who never slept without a weapon. Who checked every face three times.

They did not know what he had seen. He did not tell them.

One night, alone on the deck, he looked at the stars. They were the same stars he had watched from Ostrac. Cold. Distant. Unchanged.

He thought about the Entity. The network. The blue light.

He thought about what it had said: You breathe me. You walk on me.

He had destroyed one node. But the universe was vast. The Hush were old. There would be others.

He would be ready.

The Nightward's alarms stayed silent. The void stayed empty.

But Kane did not lower his weapon.

He was the watcher on the wall. And he would never stop watching.
