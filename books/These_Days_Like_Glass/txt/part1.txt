THESE DAYS LIKE GLASS

A Novel

---

ARC ONE: THE DISCOVERY

---

Chapter 1

The furnace breathed.

Elara Murano had been watching it for thirty-seven years—since before she understood that the fire inside was not a thing to be feared but a thing to be read. The flame leaned left when the humidity rose. It turned blue at the throat when the silica was ready. It whispered to her in a language older than Italian, older than Venice, older than the lagoon that gave this island its name.

She pulled the blowpipe from the glory hole and rolled it across the marver. The gather of glass glowed orange, the color of dying embers, the color of sunrise through smoke. She shaped it with a wet block of cherry wood, steam rising in a hiss that sounded like a sigh.

"Elara."

She did not turn. Marco Voss stood at the doorway of the workshop, his silhouette framed against the pale light of the canal outside. He was always at the doorway. Never inside. He said the heat bothered his lungs.

"The American is back," he said.

"Which American?"

"The one from last year. The collector."

Elara rotated the pipe, keeping the glass centered. The weight of it was familiar, a counterbalance to the weight of everything else. "I have nothing to sell him."

"He is not asking to buy."

"Then what is he asking?"

Marco was silent for a moment. He was a glassblower too, once. Before the tremor in his hands made it impossible. Now he ran the shop. Sold the pieces. Dealt with the people who came to Murano looking for souvenirs—or for something more.

"He wants to show you something."

Elara set the pipe down. The glass would cool. It could be reheated. Some things could not.

"Tell him I will meet him at the cafe. Tomorrow. Four o'clock."

"The Caffe dell'Arte?"

"No. The one by the boat stop. The one where the tourists do not go."

Marco nodded and disappeared. Elara heard his footsteps fade along the stone alley, the sound of a man who knew these streets the way she knew the fire.

She looked at the glass on the pipe, already losing its glow. She had been trying to make something new. A bowl with a rim so thin it would catch the light like a soap bubble. She had been trying for three weeks and had not succeeded.

Perhaps tomorrow she would try again.

Or perhaps tomorrow she would learn something that changed everything.

Chapter 2

The cafe was empty.

Elara arrived early, as she always did. She chose a table in the corner, her back to the wall, facing the door. This was not paranoia. This was the habit of a woman who had learned that the world could break a person the way a dropped bowl could break—suddenly, and into pieces too small to be repaired.

She ordered a coffee. She drank it slowly. She watched the door.

The American came at exactly four o'clock. He was tall, with gray hair that looked like it had been cut by someone who did not care how it looked. He wore a dark suit that was too warm for the weather but somehow did not make him sweat. His shoes were polished. His hands were empty.

"Signora Murano."

"Signor... I am sorry. I have forgotten your name."

"Call me what you like. Some call me the Collector."

"And what is it you collect?"

He sat down across from her. He did not order anything. He simply sat, his hands flat on the table, his eyes the color of old bronze.

"Your family."

The word hung in the air between them. Elara felt her chest tighten. She said nothing.

"I realize," the Collector said, "that this sounds alarming. Let me explain differently. I collect objects. Specifically, I collect glass. Murano glass. I have been doing so for a very long time."

"Many people collect Murano glass."

"Yes. But I do not collect it for its beauty, or its value, or its prestige. I collect it because each piece carries a story. And I have discovered that the pieces made by your family tell a single story. One that has been unfolding for four hundred years."

Elara stared at him. The coffee cup was empty in her hands. She set it down.

"Four hundred years," she repeated.

"Your family has been making glass on this island since the sixteenth century. Generation after generation. Father to son, mother to daughter. The techniques changed. The styles evolved. But the lineage never broke." He paused. "And I have acquired pieces from every generation."

"How many pieces?"

"Two hundred and seventeen. Spanning from your great-great-great—well, from your ancestor Matteo Murano, who apprenticed in 1612, to pieces made by your own father, Carlo Murano, who died—"

"Twenty years ago. He died twenty years ago."

The Collector nodded. He reached into his jacket and produced a photograph, yellowed at the edges. He placed it on the table between them.

It showed a vase. Deep blue, with veins of gold running through it like lightning frozen in time. The shape was simple, almost crude, but the craftsmanship was unmistakable. It was the work of someone learning, someone reaching for something they could not yet grasp.

"Matteo Murano," the Collector said. "Circa 1620. His first independent piece."

Elara picked up the photograph. Her hands were steady. They were always steady. That was what made her a glassblower.

"I have never seen this."

"No. You would not have. It left your family's possession in 1625, sold to a Venetian merchant. It passed through several hands before I acquired it in 1893."

"1893." Elara looked up. "That is before my grandfather was born."

"Yes."

"Then you are not the first Collector."

"Correct. There have been others. But I am the current one. And I have something I would like to show you."
