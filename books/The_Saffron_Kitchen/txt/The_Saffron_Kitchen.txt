Chapter 1: The Weight of the Tin

Nora Ellery knew the weight of a full saffron tin the way she knew her own hands. She lifted it from the spice shelf at four o'clock, before anyone else had tied an apron, and her thumb knew at once that it was wrong.

Too light. By a third, maybe more.

She set it on the steel and opened the lid. The threads inside were red enough. They smelled of hay and metal and something faintly medicinal, the way good saffron should. But there was not enough of it, and Saltmarsh did not run short on saffron. Not ever. The broth was the dish the whole menu hung from.

She made a note in her head and started the stock anyway. Bones, fennel, the white parts of leeks. The kitchen woke around her in stages — the low roar of the extraction fan, the click of the gas, the first knife finding its rhythm on a board.

Outside, the sea was the color of pewter and the wind came off it hard enough to rattle the back door in its frame. Saltmarsh sat on a hook of the Cornish coast where the land gave up and the water took over. People drove two hours for a table.

Nora toasted the threads in a dry pan until the air went gold, then bloomed them in a ladle of warm stock. The color spread like a stain in water. She watched it the way she watched everything: closely, without comment.

Sadie came in at four-thirty, cheeks pink from the hill, and hung her coat on the hook that was technically Theo's.

"Morning, chef," she said.

"It's afternoon."

"It's always afternoon in here." Sadie tied her apron. "What are we behind on?"

"Nothing yet. Stay that way." Nora nodded at the saffron tin. "When did we last get a delivery from Hassan?"

Sadie frowned, thinking. "Tuesday? I signed for something Tuesday."

"This is lighter than Tuesday."

"Maybe Daniel used it. He's been weird about the broth all week."

Nora capped the tin and put it back on the shelf, exactly where it had been, label out. She had a feeling she would want to know later what it had looked like before anyone touched it.

That was the first thing. She would remember it, after.


Chapter 2: The Critic Comes Tomorrow

Daniel Reyes came in at five with his collar up and his jaw set, and the temperature of the kitchen dropped a degree.

He was a handsome man going gray at the temples, with the kind of restless energy that made other people stand straighter. He had built Saltmarsh out of a failed pub and his own stubbornness, and on a good night he was the best company in the county. This was not a good night, and it had not started yet.

"Listen up." He clapped once, and the brigade stilled. "Tomorrow we get reviewed. The review. The one that matters. So tonight we run clean, and tomorrow we run perfect."

Theo, working the fish station, didn't look up. "Who's coming?"

"Doesn't matter who."

"It matters if I'm cooking for them."

"You're cooking for everyone the same," Daniel said. "That's the whole point. Heard chef?"

"Heard, chef," the kitchen answered, ragged.

Nora watched him. There was a tightness around his eyes she had not seen before, a man holding something heavy and pretending it was light. He caught her looking.

"The broth ready?" he asked.

"Building. It'll be ready for tasting at six, same as always."

"Good." He hesitated, which was not like him. "I'll taste it myself tonight. Don't let anyone else into the pot before me."

"You always taste it yourself."

"I know. I'm saying it anyway." He went into the office and shut the door, and a moment later she heard the lock turn, which he never did.

Imogen Vale glided in from the dining room in her good gray dress, clipboard in hand, and saw the closed office door.

"He's locked himself in," Nora said.

Imogen's mouth thinned. "He's been doing that." She made a mark on her clipboard and said nothing else, which from Imogen was a whole paragraph.


Chapter 3: Service

Service began at seven and the room filled in twenty minutes, the way it always did on a Friday, the bookings stacked three deep.

Nora ran the pass. It was where she was happiest — the tickets coming in, the calls going out, the plates landing under her eyes for a final look before they crossed into the warm noise of the dining room. She wiped a thumbprint of broth from a rim. She turned a scallop so its seared face showed. She sent nothing she would not eat herself.

The saffron broth went out forty times that night, golden in white bowls, and forty times she watched it leave with the small private satisfaction of a thing done right.

At nine, the monkfish came back.

It came back on a tray carried by a waiter who would not meet anyone's eye, and Imogen behind him with her cool face on. "Table six," she said. "Says it's underdone."

Theo took the plate and turned it under the light. "It's not underdone. It's rested. That's how it's meant to be."

"The guest disagrees."

"The guest is wrong."

"Fire another," Daniel said from the pass, not loudly. He had come out of the office at some point and Nora had not noticed when, which bothered her.

"It's not wrong, chef—"

"Theo. Fire another. Now."

Theo fired another. He slammed the pan a little harder than he needed to, and a little hotter, and the kitchen pretended not to notice. The second monkfish went out and did not come back, and the night rolled on toward its close, and nobody died, not yet.


Chapter 4: The Cold Room

By eleven the room had emptied and the brigade was deep in the long quiet work of breaking down. Pans soaking, surfaces wiped, the floor going gray under the mop.

Nora needed cream for the morning's prep list and went to the cold room for it. The cold room was at the back, past the dry store, a heavy insulated door with a lever handle and a light that came on when you stepped in.

The light was already on.

Daniel was on the floor between the racks, on his side, one arm flung out toward the lower shelf. A small steel cup lay near his hand, the kind they used for tasting, and a thread of pale liquid had run from it across the tile and stopped.

For a second she thought he had slipped. She said his name. He did not move. She knelt and her knees found the cold of the floor and she put two fingers to his neck the way she had been taught in a course years ago and never used, and there was nothing there. His skin was cold, but everything in that room was cold, so that told her nothing and everything.

"Daniel."

She did not shout. She would think about that later, that she had not shouted. She had said his name twice, quietly, the way you'd speak to someone asleep, and then she had stood up and backed to the door and held the lever without opening it.

Through the gap she could see Sadie at the sink, earbuds in, scrubbing a stockpot, the whole ordinary world still turning.

Nora opened the door.

"Sadie," she said. "Get Imogen. Don't run."

Sadie ran.


Chapter 5: The Scene

The police came in the small hours, blue light wheeling across the wet car park, and the kitchen Nora had spent six years memorizing became a place she was not allowed to touch.

A young officer put tape across the cold room door. Another took their names at the staff table, one by one, while the brigade sat in their whites and said almost nothing. Theo's leg jittered. Pavel held a mug of tea he did not drink. Sadie had stopped crying and gone to a worse, quieter place.

The detective arrived last. She was a broad, unhurried woman in a damp coat, with reading glasses pushed up into close-cropped gray hair and the look of someone who had been pulled out of bed and made peace with it.

"DS Okafor," she said to the room. "I'm sorry for your night." She let that sit, which was more than most would have. "I need to keep everyone a little longer. The kitchen's a scene now, so nobody touches anything — not a knife, not a tea towel. Understood?"

Heads nodded.

Her eyes went around the table and stopped on Nora. "You found him."

"Yes."

"You moved him?"

"I felt for a pulse. At his neck. I didn't move him otherwise."

"Did you touch anything in the room?"

Nora thought. "The door lever. The shelf, with my other hand, to steady myself. Not the cup."

"The cup."

"There was a tasting cup by his hand. I didn't touch it."

Okafor wrote something. "You noticed the cup."

"I notice things," Nora said. "It's the job."

The detective looked at her a moment longer than the answer needed, then moved on. But Nora had the feeling she had just been filed somewhere, under a heading she could not read.


Chapter 6: Prints and a Text

They let her go home at four and asked her back at noon, and at noon DS Okafor was waiting in the dining room with a folder and a second officer and two cups of bad coffee.

"Sit," Okafor said, not unkindly. "I want to walk through a couple of things."

Nora sat. The room looked strange in daylight, chairs up on tables, the sea flat and bright through the windows where last night it had been black.

"Daniel Reyes died sometime between ten and eleven," Okafor said. "We'll know more after the post-mortem. There was a bottle of wine open in the office." She slid a photograph across. "Your prints are on it."

"I opened it. He asked me to, around nine. He wanted a glass between courses." Nora kept her voice level. "He never drank during service before. I thought it was the nerves about today."

"You poured it?"

"I opened it and left it. I don't know if he drank it."

Okafor turned a page. "His phone shows a text he sent at 10:14. The last thing he sent anyone." She read it out. "It says: 'Nora — don't let them serve it. I'll explain.'"

The words went into Nora like cold water. "Serve what?"

"I was hoping you'd tell me."

"I never saw it. We're not allowed phones on the pass." She heard how it sounded. "I'd have told you that first if I'd seen it."

Okafor watched her with those patient eyes. "Don't let them serve it," she repeated. "And then he's dead in the cold room, and your prints are on the bottle, and your name's in his last message." She closed the folder gently. "You see how that reads."

"I see how it reads," Nora said. "It reads wrong. Like a dish that's been tampered with. Everything's there and it still tastes wrong."

"Tampered with how?"

"I don't know yet," Nora said. "But I will."


Chapter 7: Out of Balance

Okafor let her stand and stretch and look out at the water, and then asked the question she had clearly been saving.

"What did he mean, don't let them serve it?"

"The broth," Nora said. "It has to be the broth. It's the dish the menu's built on. It's what the critic would order first."

"Explain it to me. Like I've never cooked."

Nora turned from the window. "Saffron broth. You toast the threads, bloom them in stock, build it slowly. It's the thing he's known for. Forty covers a night order it. If something was wrong with the broth and a critic tasted it tomorrow, that's the end of the place."

"Was something wrong with it?"

"The tin was light yesterday," Nora said slowly, working it out as she spoke. "Saffron's the most expensive thing in the building. We weigh it, we lock it, we don't lose it. And the tin was a third light, and I don't know who used it."

Okafor wrote. "So maybe he found something wrong and the text means stop the service."

"Maybe." Nora frowned. "But you don't lock yourself in an office and drink wine alone and then die in a cold room because the saffron's short. That's not a balanced explanation. There's a flavor missing."

"You keep talking about flavor."

"It's how I think." Nora looked at her. "You taste a sauce and you know something's off before you know what. Salt, acid, a herb that's wrong. You chase it. That's what this is. Something's off and I'm going to chase it until I find what."

Okafor capped her pen. "Just be careful, Ms. Ellery, that you don't chase it somewhere it makes you look guiltier."

"I didn't kill him."

"People say that to me a great deal," Okafor said, "and about half the time it's true."


Chapter 8: The Refilled Tin

They sealed the cold room and the office but, oddly, not the spice store, which Okafor's team had photographed and apparently dismissed. So when the others had gone, Nora went back in.

She told herself she was getting her knife roll, which was true, and that she would only glance at the shelf, which was a lie.

The saffron tin was where she had left it the day before. Label out. She lifted it. Full weight now, or close to it.

She frowned and opened it.

The threads inside were red, but a different red. Brighter, more orange, fatter at the ends. She rubbed two between her fingers and lifted them to her nose. The smell was thin. None of the honey-and-iron depth of the real thing. It was the smell of saffron that had been cut, or worse, of safflower dyed to pass — the cheap fraud, the stuff that turned a broth bitter if you steeped it long.

Someone had emptied the good saffron and refilled the tin with this.

And they had done it after she left, because she had checked the weight herself at four o'clock and it had been light, and now it was full, of the wrong thing.

She did not touch anything else. She put the tin back, label out, and she took a photograph of it with her own phone, the threads spread on the lid, and another of the tin on the shelf, and then she stood in the cold humming room and let the fear move through her and out.

Someone in this kitchen had changed the recipe. And the man who would have tasted it first was dead.


Chapter 9: What Survives

Imogen was in the office doorway when Nora came out, holding the seal tape away from the frame to look in without crossing it.

"They told us not to," Nora said.

"I'm looking, not touching." Imogen let the tape fall and turned. Up close, in daylight, she looked older than her room voice. "Did you sleep?"

"No."

"Nor me." Imogen leaned against the wall. "I've been on the phone since seven. The bank. The insurers. Marcus." She said the investor's name like a word that tasted bad. "Do you understand what this does to us, Nora? A chef dead in his own cold room the night before the review. We won't get the review now. We'll get the story. 'Death at the celebrated Saltmarsh.' That's the headline. That's what survives."

"Someone killed him, Imogen."

Imogen went very still. "The police haven't said that."

"They will." Nora kept her voice low. "The saffron's been swapped. The good batch is gone and there's fake in the tin. He texted me to stop the service. None of that is an accident."

For a moment something crossed Imogen's face that Nora could not name — fear, or calculation, or grief wearing one of their masks. "Then you should tell the police," she said. "Not me."

"I have."

"Then let them work." Imogen pushed off the wall. "And Nora — be careful. Whatever you think you're doing. This place is hanging by a thread, and so, I'd say, are you."


Chapter 10: The Missing Page

Daniel had a flat above the harbor, ten minutes' drive, and he had given Nora a key two winters ago when he'd broken his wrist and needed someone to bring him knives and books. She still had it. She told herself the police would search the flat eventually and she was only getting there first, which was the truth and not a defense.

She took Sadie because Sadie should not be alone, and because two sets of eyes saw more than one.

The flat was tidy in the way of a man who was never in it. A bed made tight. A shelf of cookbooks, spines cracked from real use. On the table, his recipe ledger — a fat hardbacked book he carried everywhere, where he wrote his dishes by hand, crossings-out and all.

Nora opened it to the saffron broth. The recipe was there in his slanted writing, the quantities, the timings, the note in the margin she had read a hundred times: slow, slow, don't rush the bloom.

But the page after it had been torn out. Cleanly, close to the spine, so that only a thin stub remained.

"What was on it?" Sadie asked.

"I don't know. He kept variations there. Tests. Things he was working toward." Nora ran her thumb along the stub. "He never tore a page. He crossed things out. He never tore."

"So who tore it?"

"Either he did," Nora said, "or someone who knew what was on it and didn't want it found." She closed the ledger. "We take this to Okafor. All of it. We don't keep secrets from the police. That's how innocent people start looking guilty."

Sadie nodded, pale. "Nora. Am I in trouble?"

"Why would you be in trouble?"

Sadie opened her mouth, and shut it, and said, "No reason." Which was the second lie Nora had caught that day, and she filed it with the rest.


Chapter 11: Theo Taken In

They took Theo in on the Sunday.

Word came through Pavel, who had it from a waiter, who had seen the car. By the time Nora got to the restaurant the brigade was clustered in the car park in their off-duty clothes, looking wrong out of whites, like actors caught in the street.

"The monkfish," Pavel said quietly. "Someone told them about the row. That he slammed the pan. That he and Daniel had words."

"Everyone has words with Daniel," Nora said. "Had."

"Theo had more than most. He thought he should have had the chef de cuisine job last year, not you."

"I know what Theo thought." She had lived with what Theo thought for a year, the small cold currents of it under every shift.

Theo came out two hours later, gray-faced, and the others scattered to give him room. He came straight to Nora.

"They think I did it," he said. "They've got the monkfish, they've got me losing my temper, they've got nothing else but they think it's enough."

"Did you?"

"No." He said it fast and ugly and then quieter. "No. God, Nora, no. I wanted his job. I didn't want him dead. Those aren't the same thing." He gripped her arm. "You know me. Tell them. You ran the pass next to me for four years. Tell them I wouldn't."

She looked at him. She did not entirely know him — she knew his hands, his temper, his pride. But she knew the difference between a man who breaks a plate and a man who poisons a cup, the same way she knew salt from sugar by sight.

"I'll tell them what I saw," she said. "Which is a man with a bad temper and clean hands. That's all I can swear to."

It was not what he wanted. But he nodded, because it was more than nothing, and let her go.


Chapter 12: From the Bin

The cold room was sealed but the bins were not, and the kitchen bins had not yet been collected — the Sunday lorry didn't come till Monday, a small mercy of provincial scheduling.

Nora found the night's service waste still bagged in the yard. And in among it, in a smaller bag tied off separately the way they bagged liquid waste, the dregs of the broth pot from Friday night.

She should have called Okafor first. She knew that even as she peeled the bag open. But she had to know, and knowing came before procedure, always had.

She had no clean spoon, so she dipped a finger — a sin in any kitchen — and touched it to her tongue.

The taste hit the back of her throat and she spat into the drain.

Bitter. Metallic. A hard chemical edge under the saffron that should not have been there, that no amount of fennel or leek could explain. The real broth was round and warm and faintly sweet. This was the same broth turned against itself, soured, spoiled with something that did not belong.

She thought of the fake saffron in the tin and shook her head. No. Fake saffron made a broth thin and dull. It did not make it bitter like this, this aggressive wrongness. This was something else. Something added.

She tied the bag again, carefully, and this time she called the detective.

"DS Okafor? It's Nora Ellery. Don't throw out the bins. There's a bag of broth in the yard and I think you'll want it tested." A pause. "And yes. I tasted it. I know I shouldn't have. But now I know something you didn't, so we're even."


Chapter 13: Reconstruction

Okafor came herself, with a forensic kit and a hard look, and bagged the broth properly, and then sat Nora down in the empty dining room and made her go through it twice.

"You tampered with evidence," the detective said.

"I tasted a bin bag. The bag's still there, I tied it back exactly. And I just handed you a lead you didn't have." Nora met her eyes. "If you want to charge me with curiosity, go ahead."

Okafor almost smiled, which she clearly hated. "Tell me about the bitterness."

"It's not the fake saffron. Cheap saffron makes broth flat, not bitter. This is sharp. Chemical. Something was put in." Nora leaned forward. "I can prove it. Let me make the broth properly, here, while you watch. The real recipe. Then you'll taste what it should be, and you'll understand what you're looking for in the lab."

"That's not how forensics works."

"It's how cooking works. And someone used cooking to kill him, so maybe you need a cook." She held the detective's gaze. "I have nothing to hide. Watch me make it. Watch my hands."

Okafor considered her for a long moment, the way Nora considered a sauce. Then she nodded.

They had released the main kitchen, only the cold room and office still sealed. Nora tied on an apron and made the broth from memory under the detective's eyes — toasting, blooming, building, slow. It took an hour. When it was done she ladled a little into two cups and they drank.

It was round and warm and faintly sweet, the way it was meant to be, and something in Nora's chest unclenched at the rightness of it.

"That," she said, "is the broth. Now you know what was stolen from it."


Chapter 14: Pavel's Notebook

Pavel Novak made the bread and the desserts and he spoke to almost no one, but he wrote everything down. Nora had always known this in a general way. She had not known how completely until he set the notebook on the table in front of her, the Monday morning, with the slight ceremony of a man parting with a confidence.

"You're asking questions," he said. "I keep answers. Maybe they're the same."

The notebook was small, hardbacked, his writing tiny and exact. He kept times. He kept who came and went. Not gossip — facts, the way another man might keep a log of tides.

"Why?" Nora asked.

"In my country it was useful to know who was where," Pavel said, and did not elaborate. "Habit now. Look at Thursday."

She looked. Thursday, the night before the death. A list of entries in twenty-four-hour time. And there, at 21:40: Delivery, back door. Spice supplier. Signed for.

"A delivery at twenty to ten at night?" Nora said. "We don't take deliveries during service. Hassan comes in the morning."

"That's what I thought." Pavel tapped the line. "So I wrote it down. A van. One box. Someone signed."

"Who signed?"

"I didn't see. The light was bad and I had bread proving." He met her eyes. "But it wasn't on the rota. Nobody was rostered for the back door that night. So whoever signed for it wasn't supposed to be there."


Chapter 15: The Wrong Signature

The delivery book lived on a clipboard by the back door, and the police had not taken it, because nobody had told them it mattered.

Nora found the Thursday page. There was a signature against a late entry, a scrawl, and beside it the supplier's docket number and a single line: 1 x saffron, 50g.

Fifty grams. That was a fortune in saffron. And it had come at twenty to ten at night, off the books, signed for by a hand Nora did not recognize.

She compared the scrawl to the others on the page — her own neat initials, Sadie's round letters, Theo's slash. None of them matched. The Thursday scrawl was a deliberate illegibility, the signature of someone who did not want to be read.

"Whose is that?" she asked Pavel, who had come to stand at her shoulder.

"Not mine."

"Not anyone's I know." She photographed it. "Fifty grams of saffron comes in the night before the critic, signed by a ghost, and the next day the good saffron's gone from the tin and there's fake in its place." She tapped the docket number. "This came from Hassan's company. So Hassan knows what he sent and to whom."

"You'll go to him?"

"I'll go to him." She unclipped the page and stopped herself, and clipped it back. Evidence. Leave it. Photograph, don't take. She was learning. "First I'll tell Okafor about the docket. Then I'll go to Hassan before the police do, because Hassan is family, and family talks to family, and Hassan and Daniel hadn't talked in two years."


Chapter 16: The Brother

Hassan Reyes ran a spice import business out of a unit on a trading estate twenty miles inland, a world away from the sea. The air inside was thick with cumin and dried chili and, under it all, the dry hay note of saffron.

He was younger than Daniel, softer, with the same restless eyes gone tired. He knew who Nora was before she said her name.

"The sous-chef," he said. "Daniel talked about you. Said you were the only one who never let anything past you." He gestured at a stool. "Sit. I heard. I'm sorry. We weren't speaking, but he was my brother."

"Why weren't you speaking?"

Hassan looked at his hands. "Money. What else. He owed me. The restaurant's been bleeding for a year, did you know that?"

"I knew it was tight."

"Tight." He laughed without humor. "He owed me eleven thousand pounds for saffron alone. Last spring I told him no more credit. And you know what he did? He found a cheaper source. Started ordering off the books from a man I wouldn't trust to sell me sand." His face twisted. "My own saffron, the real Persian threads, and he's buying garbage from a stranger to save a few pounds. To spite me, maybe. We hadn't spoken since."

"Did you send a delivery Thursday night? Fifty grams?"

Hassan went still. "No. I don't deliver at night. And I hadn't sent Daniel anything in months." He pulled a ledger toward him — everyone in this story kept a ledger, Nora thought. "Look. Nothing from me since March."

"Then someone used your docket number."

"Someone," Hassan said slowly, "used my name to put something in my brother's kitchen." He looked up, and the grief in his face had a hard new edge to it. "I want to know who as much as you do."


Chapter 17: The Cheaper Source

"This cheaper source," Nora said. "Do you know who it was?"

"A name. Caldas. A middleman, works out of a lockup near Plymouth, sells whatever's going. Saffron, vanilla, whatever's been cut and dyed to pass." Hassan's mouth curled. "I told Daniel. I said, the man sells fraud. You'll put safflower in your famous broth and one day someone will notice. He laughed at me. Said his palate would catch anything bad before a customer did."

"His palate," Nora repeated.

"Best in the county, he always said. And it was. He could taste a dish and tell you the field the lamb grazed in." Hassan's eyes shone. "He tasted everything himself. First, every night. He didn't trust anyone else's tongue, not even yours, no offense."

"None taken. It's true." Nora thought of the steel cup on the cold room floor, the thread of pale liquid stopped on the tile. "He tasted the broth first, alone, every night, before service."

"Always."

"And if the broth was off — if the saffron was the fake stuff — he'd have caught it."

"He'd have caught it," Hassan agreed. "That was the whole point of him."

Which meant, Nora thought, driving home along the dark coast road, that whoever swapped the saffron either did not know Daniel would taste it first — or had counted on exactly that. Counted on him lifting the cup to his own lips before anyone else. The thought sat in her stomach like a stone all the way home.


Chapter 18: The Sale

Marcus Friel drove down from London on the Tuesday in a car that cost more than the kitchen earned in a season, and Nora found him in the dining room with Imogen, their heads bent over papers, their voices low.

She had come for her wages and her knife roll. She stopped in the service doorway, out of sight, because that is what you do when two people are speaking in the voices people use when they don't want to be heard.

"...can't keep it open with him gone," Friel was saying. "Be realistic, Imogen. The chef was the brand. No chef, no brand, no business. The kindest thing is a clean sale."

"To Lena Coyle." Imogen's voice was flat.

"To anyone who'll meet the number. Coyle's meeting the number."

"Coyle wants it because Daniel beat her. She wants to put her own name over his door. You know what that'll do to the staff."

"The staff," Friel said, "are an expense. Eight hundred thousand, Imogen. That's the offer. That clears the debts and leaves something for both of us. I'd take it before the inquest makes the place worthless."

Nora stood very still in the dark of the doorway. Lena Coyle. The rival from Porthcove. And eight hundred thousand pounds, available the moment Daniel was out of the way.

She had wanted a flavor that was missing, a reason that balanced. Here was a reason with a price tag on it.

She stepped back, quietly, and let them think they were alone.


Chapter 19: The Buyer

She caught Imogen alone an hour later, in the office doorway, the seal tape long since taken down.

"Lena Coyle's buying Saltmarsh," Nora said. No preamble.

Imogen did not pretend. "She's made an offer. Marcus wants to take it." She rubbed her eyes. "I don't. For whatever that's worth, which is less than Marcus's vote."

"You overheard him say it clears the debts. So the debts were real."

"The debts were drowning us." Imogen leaned against the frame. "Daniel kept the menu beautiful and the books a disaster. I held the rest together with my hands. And now—" She stopped. "Now a man I argued with for a year is dead, and the only people who profit are the ones who wanted the place sold. Including me, if I'm honest, since I'd rather have money than a corpse's restaurant. So put me on your list, Nora. I know you're keeping one."

"Are you on it?"

"Everyone's on it," Imogen said. "That's how lists work." She straightened. "But ask yourself who wanted him gone and the place sold, both. Because I wanted the second and not the first. And Lena Coyle—" She let the name hang. "Lena Coyle wanted both."


Chapter 20: Porthcove

Porthcove was an hour down the coast, a prettier harbor than Saltmarsh's, and Lena Coyle's restaurant sat above it like a glass jewel, all light and pale wood and good money spent well.

Lena received Nora in the empty afternoon dining room, between services, with coffee and a plate of small biscuits she had clearly made herself. She was a striking woman of fifty with silver hair cut sharp and a cook's forearms.

"The famous sous-chef," she said. "Daniel said you were the best palate he'd worked with. He said it like a complaint." She poured. "I was sorry to hear. Truly. We were enemies for sport, not for blood."

"You're buying his restaurant."

"I'm buying a building and a kitchen and a coastline. The name dies with him." Lena sipped. "You think that makes me a killer."

"It gives you a reason."

"It gives me a reason to want him to fail," Lena said. "Not to die. A failed Daniel I could enjoy for years. A dead Daniel I can only buy out cheap, and where's the pleasure in that?" She set down her cup. "But you didn't come here for my pleasures. Ask what you came to ask."

Nora looked at her. Lena was the kind of person who told the truth precisely because it was more disarming than a lie. "Did you and Daniel ever cook together?"

Something flickered in Lena's eyes. "Now," she said, "you're asking the right question."


Chapter 21: Whose Recipe

"We cooked together for six years," Lena said. "Before Saltmarsh. We had a place in Bristol, the two of us, partners in the kitchen and out of it. You didn't know that."

"No."

"Few do. We kept it quiet when it ended badly, which it did." Lena turned her cup in its saucer. "The saffron broth. His famous dish. The one the whole menu hangs from." She looked up. "It's mine, Nora. I created it. In Bristol, in a flat with no money, I worked out that broth over a winter. And when we split, he took it. Took the recipe, took the name, opened Saltmarsh, built a reputation on my broth, and dared me to say so."

Nora sat very still. "You never claimed it?"

"Who'd believe me? His name was on the door. Mine was just the woman who used to be with him." Lena's voice was even but her knuckles were white on the cup. "So yes, I wanted Saltmarsh. I wanted to put my name over the door and my broth on the menu and have everyone finally understand whose it was. That's my reason, since you're collecting them. It's an old one. Twelve years old. It doesn't make me kill a man the night before a review."

"Where were you Friday night?"

"Here. Full service. Forty staff and a hundred guests can place me at my own pass until midnight." Lena smiled thinly. "Check it. I'd want you to."

Nora believed her, mostly. But twelve-year-old grudges, she thought, do not always wait twelve years to be acted on. Sometimes they wait for the right night.


Chapter 22: Sadie Suspended

She came back to find Sadie sitting on the harbor wall outside Saltmarsh, coat zipped to the chin, eyes red.

"They suspended me," Sadie said before Nora could speak. "Imogen. She says I signed for the night delivery. The Thursday one. She says it's my signature in the book."

"Is it?"

"No!" Sadie's voice cracked. "I wasn't even — I left at nine on Thursday, I had the dentist Friday morning, you signed my early-leave yourself. I wasn't here at twenty to ten."

Nora remembered. She had signed the early-leave. Sadie had not been in the building when the delivery came.

"Then why does Imogen think it's you?"

"Because the scrawl's near where mine usually is on the page. Because I'm the easiest one to blame." Sadie wiped her face hard. "Because I'm the commis and nobody loses sleep over a commis."

"You're not nobody," Nora said, and meant it. "I signed your early-leave. That's a paper trail. You couldn't have signed for the delivery." She sat on the wall beside her. "But Sadie — back at the flat you started to say something. About being in trouble. What were you going to tell me?"

Sadie looked out at the gray water for a long moment. "Promise you won't be angry."

"I won't be angry."

"It's not about the delivery," Sadie said. "It's about something I saw. And someone I didn't want to get in trouble. Because I liked them. Because they were kind to me."


Chapter 23: Covering

"Tell me," Nora said.

Sadie hugged her knees. "Theo. The week before. He took something from the dry store and didn't write it in the book, and I saw, and he asked me not to say, and I didn't." She looked sick. "And then Daniel dies and there's all this about deliveries and missing things, and I thought — I thought it might be connected, and I'd already lied once by not writing it down, so I just kept quiet about everything. That's why I looked guilty. I wasn't covering a murder. I was covering Theo nicking a tin of something to take home."

"What did he take?"

"Anchovies. A tin of the good anchovies." Sadie laughed, a wet hiccup of a laugh. "That's it. That's my terrible secret. He took anchovies and I didn't tell, and I've been sick with it for a week thinking I helped someone get away with murder, and it was anchovies."

Nora let out a breath. It was the most innocent thing she'd heard all week, and it explained the lie, the flinch, the trouble Sadie thought she was in. A frightened girl protecting someone she admired over a stolen tin.

"That's not a crime worth losing sleep over," Nora said. "And it's nothing to do with Daniel. Theo's a thief of anchovies, not a poisoner." She stood and pulled Sadie up. "Come on. I'm taking you to Okafor, and you're going to tell her exactly what you told me, all of it, including the anchovies. Because the truth, even when it's small and embarrassing, is the only thing that gets you out of a list like this."


Chapter 24: Cause of Death

Okafor heard Sadie out, wrote it down, and to her credit did not laugh about the anchovies, though her pen paused.

Then she took Nora aside, into the cold bright dining room, and made a decision Nora could see her making.

"I shouldn't tell you this," Okafor said. "But you keep handing me things, so I'll trade. The post-mortem came back."

Nora waited.

"He didn't slip. He didn't have a stroke." Okafor consulted a slip of paper. "There was a cardiac drug in his system. Digoxin. Enough to stop a heart that was already weak — and his was. He had a heart condition. Did you know that?"

"No. He never said."

"Few knew, apparently. It was in his medical records but not his prescriptions — meaning he was managing it privately, or badly." Okafor folded the slip. "Digoxin's used to treat some heart conditions. In the right dose it helps. In the wrong dose it kills, and it kills faster if the heart's already failing. He had none prescribed. So someone gave it to him."

"How would you give it to someone?"

"That's the question. It's a powder or a tablet. Bitter, as it happens. You'd need to hide the taste." Okafor looked at her. "You tasted the broth from the bin. You said it was bitter. Chemical."

The pieces turned in Nora's mind and clicked. "You hide a bitter drug," she said slowly, "in something already going bitter. In a broth made with fake saffron, where a little extra bitterness wouldn't stand out."


Chapter 25: The Bitter Cup

"But here's what doesn't fit," Nora said, pacing now. "If the drug was in the service broth, other people would have been poisoned. Forty covers ordered it Friday. Nobody else got sick."

Okafor nodded slowly. "We checked. No reports. No other casualties."

"So it wasn't in the pot that went to the dining room." Nora stopped. "It was in his cup. The tasting cup. The one on the cold room floor."

"We bagged that cup. The lab has it."

"He tasted the broth first, alone, every night. Hassan told me. It was his ritual — he didn't trust anyone else's tongue. He'd ladle a little into a steel cup and taste it before service, by himself." Nora's mouth had gone dry. "So you don't poison the pot. You poison the cup he tastes from. The one only he uses. The dose meant for one man."

Okafor was very still. "That would take someone who knew his ritual."

"Everyone in the kitchen knew his ritual. It was a joke. 'The boss tastes first or the world ends.'" Nora pressed her hands flat on the cold table. "And the fake saffron and the bitter broth weren't even the murder. They were the cover. Two crimes, layered. Someone wanted the broth to taste wrong for the critic — and someone wanted Daniel dead. And I'm not sure yet they're the same someone."


Chapter 26: Access

That night Nora could not sleep, so she did what she always did when a dish would not come right: she broke it into its parts.

She took a sheet of paper and drew three columns. The cold room. The office. The spice store. The three sealed or secret places, the three points where the crime had touched the building.

Who had access to the cold room? Everyone. It was a working store; the whole brigade went in and out.

Who had access to the office? Daniel, who locked it that night. Imogen, who had a key — she ran the books from there. No one else.

Who had access to the spice store, where the saffron was swapped? The senior staff. Herself. Theo. Pavel, for his baking. Imogen, technically. Not Sadie alone — she was supervised. Not the waiters.

And who knew the tasting ritual, the cup, the bitter broth as a hiding place? Anyone who had worked a service. Which narrowed it not at all.

She crossed and uncrossed names. She thought about timing. The delivery came at 21:40 Thursday — the fake saffron arriving. The death came Friday between ten and eleven. A whole day in between, in which the good saffron was removed and the fake installed and the cup, somehow, was readied.

Whoever did this had not done it in a panic. They had done it over a day, in stages, the way you build a stock. Patiently. Which frightened her more than a crime of rage would have.


Chapter 27: Theo's Confession

Theo found her at the restaurant the next morning, where she had gone to fetch — truthfully this time — the rest of her belongings, since it was looking less and less likely Saltmarsh would open again.

"Sadie told me she told you," he said. "About the anchovies." He had the grace to look ashamed. "It's true. I took a tin. I take things sometimes. It's stupid. I earn enough to buy my own anchovies. I just—" He shrugged. "I've always felt like this place owed me. The job that went to you. The respect. So I took little things to even it up in my head. It's pathetic. I know."

"It's pathetic," Nora agreed, not unkindly. "It's not murder."

"No." He met her eyes. "And I want you to know — I doctored a plate once. Months ago. Sent out a dish for a guest I knew Daniel admired, and I oversalted it, just slightly, just enough to be wrong, so the man would think less of Daniel. That's the worst thing I've done in this kitchen. A pinch of salt out of spite." His jaw worked. "I'd never touch the broth. The broth's sacred. Whatever I am, I'm a cook. You don't poison the dish the place is built on. That's not a chef. That's just a murderer who happens to be in a kitchen."

It was, Nora thought, the truest thing anyone had said to her all week. Theo would cheat, steal a tin, salt a plate, nurse a grudge for a year. But there was a line in him, the line in every real cook, and the broth was on the far side of it.

"I believe you," she said, and was surprised to find she did.


Chapter 28: Late in the Office

Pavel's notebook again. She went back to it, because facts patiently kept were worth more than any confession, and Pavel had kept the most patient facts of all.

She read past the Thursday delivery to the Friday entries, the night of the death.

19:00 — service begins.
21:05 — D. Reyes to office. Door locked.
22:10 — I. Vale to office. Knocked. Admitted.
22:25 — I. Vale from office.
22:40 — D. Reyes from office to cold room.

Nora read it three times.

Imogen had gone into the office at 22:10, after Daniel locked it, and he had let her in, and she had come out at 22:25. And at 22:40 Daniel had gone to the cold room, where he died.

Fifteen minutes alone with Daniel, in the locked office, in the last hour of his life. And then he went to taste the broth from his cup, and never came out.

"You're sure of these times?" she asked Pavel, who had come to read over her shoulder.

"I check the clock. I always check the clock. It's the one thing that doesn't lie." He tapped the page. "She was the last person in that office with him alive. That's a fact. What it means is not a fact. But that it happened is."


Chapter 29: Confronting Imogen

Nora found Imogen at home, a small stone cottage above the town, books and ledgers everywhere, a woman who took her work to bed with her.

"You went into the office at ten past ten on Friday," Nora said. "After Daniel locked it. You were in there fifteen minutes. You came out, and twenty minutes later he was dead."

Imogen did not flinch. She had been expecting this, perhaps. She poured two glasses of wine and pushed one across, and Nora did not touch it, which Imogen noticed.

"You think I poisoned him in fifteen minutes."

"I think you were the last person to see him alive."

"I was." Imogen sat. "We fought. About the sale. He'd been on the phone to Marcus, agreeing terms behind my back — terms that cut me out, Nora. Out of a place I held together for six years. He was going to sell to Lena Coyle and take his share and leave me with the debts and my name on none of it." Her voice shook for the first time. "So yes, I went in and I shouted at him. I told him he was a coward and a thief and that he'd ruin all of us to save himself. And then I left. And he was alive when I left. Alive and furious and pouring another glass of that wine you opened."

"That's what you say."

"That's what happened." Imogen looked at her steadily. "I had a reason to hate him, Nora. I won't pretend otherwise. But I had no reason to kill him. Dead, he ruins us. Alive, I could have fought him in court for years and maybe won. You don't murder the man you're about to sue."


Chapter 30: The Alibi

"And anyway," Imogen said, "I can prove I didn't poison anything in that office. Look."

She turned her laptop around. A call log. A timed, recorded call to the bank's out-of-hours line.

"22:26 to 22:51," Imogen said. "Twenty-five minutes. The moment I left the office I called the bank to talk them out of pulling our overdraft, because Daniel had just told me he'd stopped trying to save the place. The call's recorded — they record everything. You can hear me in the corridor, then in the dining room. You can hear the room. I never went near the cold room. I never went near his cup. From 22:26 until staff started screaming about the body, I was on the phone, and the bank can place me to the second."

Nora studied the log. 22:26 to 22:51. Daniel had gone to the cold room at 22:40, while Imogen was demonstrably on the phone, her voice on a recording, the room's ambient noise around her.

"That covers the call," Nora said slowly. "It covers from twenty-six past to ten to. It doesn't cover the fifteen minutes you were in the office with him."

"He was alive when I left the office. The body was warm — warm-ish — when you found it after eleven. I'd have had to give him the drug in the office and have it act later. Does it work like that?"

"I don't know how it works," Nora admitted. "But Okafor will." She stood. "Your alibi's good for the cold room. It's not good for the cup, if the cup was readied earlier. So you're off my list for the act and on it for the means. Which is an improvement, Imogen. Take it as one."


Chapter 31: The Hidden Page

She had been so focused on people that she had half forgotten the thing: the torn page from Daniel's ledger. The recipe he'd kept after the broth. The variation, the test, gone.

She thought about who in the kitchen cared about recipes the way Daniel had. Theo cooked by feel. Sadie was learning. Imogen never cooked. But Pavel — Pavel wrote everything down. Pavel kept ledgers and logs and notebooks the way other men kept money.

She went to Pavel's station, which the police had cleared and released, and she searched it the way she'd search for a dropped ring. Under the bench. Behind the flour bins. In the gap where the steel met the wall.

And there, folded into eighths and tucked behind the loose end of the splashback, was a page of slanted handwriting. Daniel's hand. The missing page.

She unfolded it with shaking fingers.

It was not a recipe she knew. It was the saffron broth, but altered — the quantities changed, a new step, a note in the margin: cut saffron 40%, hold the bloom, cover with bitterness if needed. Trial for cost. Don't tell I.

Cut the saffron. Use less. Cover the cheaper, duller flavor with — with what? The page didn't say. But the intent was plain. Daniel had been planning to quietly degrade his own famous broth to save money. And to hide it from Imogen — don't tell I.

And the page was in Pavel's station.


Chapter 32: Pavel's Reason

She did not run to Okafor. She went to Pavel first, because she owed him that, and because some questions deserve to be asked to a face.

She put the folded page on his floured bench. He looked at it and his shoulders dropped, not in guilt exactly, but in something heavier — relief, maybe, that it was found.

"You took it," she said.

"I copied it. The original's in his ledger still, the stub — no. I tore it. I tore it and kept it." Pavel pressed his palms flat on the bench. "Let me explain it the right way."

"Explain."

"Thursday. Late. After service. I came back for my proving dough and the office light was on and the door open a crack, and Daniel was at his desk with this page in front of him and the saffron tin — the good one, the full one — and a bag of the cheap stuff. The fraud. He was decanting. Pouring out the real, pouring in the fake. Working out his cost-cutting trial." Pavel's voice was quiet and exact. "I watched a great chef adulterate his own broth to save money the night before a review. It broke something in me. So when he went out to the cold room, I took the page. I didn't want it to exist. I didn't want the proof that he'd do that to the dish. To us."

"You tore a page out of a dead man's ledger to protect his reputation."

"To protect the broth," Pavel said. "And maybe him. A great cook should be remembered for the dish, not for the night he ruined it. I wasn't stealing. I was — burying. Badly. I'm not good at hiding things. I'm only good at writing them down."


Chapter 33: Friel's Account

It checked out, in a way. Pavel had seen Daniel doing the very thing the torn page described, the swap of good saffron for fake — but that was Daniel's own hand, his own cost-cutting, the night before the death. It explained the fake in the tin. It did not explain the digoxin in the cup.

Two crimes, layered. Nora was surer of it than ever now. One man cheapening his own broth. Someone else using that cheapened, bitter broth as the perfect place to hide a poison.

Hassan called her that afternoon. "I found something," he said. "The night delivery. Fifty grams of saffron, Thursday, off my docket. I pulled the supplier records. The cheap stuff — the Caldas stuff — there's an order trail."

"And?"

"It wasn't ordered on Daniel's account. The cheap saffron Daniel was buying all year, that was on his account, in his name. But the fifty-gram night delivery, the one signed by your ghost — that was ordered and paid for through a company account." A pause. "Friel Holdings. Marcus Friel."

Nora gripped the phone. "The investor."

"The investor bought fifty grams of fraudulent saffron and had it delivered to the back door of his own restaurant at twenty to ten the night before the most important review of its life," Hassan said. "Now why would a man do that?"


Chapter 34: To Force the Sale

She drove to find Marcus Friel still in town, at the hotel up the coast, and he received her in the bar with a whisky and the unbothered face of a man who has lawyers.

"Why did you buy fake saffron and have it delivered to Saltmarsh?" Nora asked. No preamble. She had learned that the truth came faster when you didn't pad it.

Friel did not flinch, but his glass paused on its way to his mouth, which from a man like him was a confession.

"I want the place sold," he said, after a moment. "You know that. Everyone knows that. The restaurant's been losing money for a year and Daniel wouldn't face it. I had an offer from Coyle. A good one. But Daniel was going to fight it. He thought the review would save him." Friel set the glass down. "So I thought — if the review went badly, he'd have no fight left. He'd have to sell. I had Coyle's manager arrange a batch of the cheap saffron through the supplier, signed in, swapped for the good. The critic tastes a dull, off broth instead of the famous one. A bad review. Daniel folds. Sale goes through." He spread his hands. "Sabotage. Commercial sabotage. I'll admit that to you and deny it to a court. But I didn't kill anyone. I wanted the broth to fail. I didn't want the man to die. A dead chef tanks the price."


Chapter 35: Two Crimes

So there it was, laid out like a deconstructed dish, each element separate on the plate.

Daniel had been cutting his own saffron to save money — his hand, his ledger page, the fraud he'd told no one about, not even Imogen.

Friel had arranged a second batch of fake saffron, the night delivery, to sabotage the review and force the sale — separate from Daniel's own cheapening, a belt-and-braces ruin.

And someone — still unnamed — had put digoxin in the tasting cup, the cup Daniel used alone, the bitter cup hidden inside the bitter broth.

Three intentions, braided so tight that for days they had looked like one. Nora sat in her car in the hotel car park and pulled them apart, slow, the way you separated a split sauce by adding cold water and patience.

The saffron fraud was about money. Daniel's and Friel's both, two men cheapening the same broth from different directions for different sums.

But the poison was not about money. You do not need a man dead to make a broth taste bad. The poison was about Daniel himself — about ending him, specifically, the night before the night that mattered most to him.

Cruelty, that. Or grief. Or both. Whoever poisoned the cup wanted Daniel to die at the height of his hope, with the critic's table booked, the whole future open. That was not a sabotage. That was a punishment.

And punishment, Nora thought, comes from love gone wrong, or from a wound that won't heal. Not from a balance sheet.


Chapter 36: The Guest in the Back

Sadie called her, breathless, that evening.

"I remembered something. I keep remembering things, I'm sorry, it's like the more I calm down the more comes back." A breath. "Friday. Early in service, before the rush. I went out the back for the bins and there was someone there. In the yard. Not staff. A man in a good coat, just standing by the back door like he was waiting or — or like he'd just come out."

"What did he look like?"

"Older. Smooth. Silver-haired, sort of. A nice watch, I remember the watch catching the light. He smiled at me and said he was lost, looking for the toilets, and I pointed him round the front and he went. I didn't think anything of it. Guests wander. But he came from the back, Nora. From the kitchen end. Where the cold room is."

Nora's pulse quickened. "What time?"

"Early. Half seven, maybe. Before Daniel went to the office."

A man in a good coat, a nice watch, coming from the back of the kitchen during service, the night Daniel died. Not staff. Not a guest who'd lost his way to the toilets, because the toilets were nowhere near the yard.

"Sadie," Nora said carefully, "would you know him again?"

"I think so. The watch. The smile. Yeah. I'd know him."


Chapter 37: The Manager

She took the description to Lena Coyle, because a man in a good coat with a nice watch, working the edges of a rival's restaurant, sounded like money's errand boy, and money in this story had a name down the coast.

Lena listened, and her face changed.

"That's Gerald," she said quietly. "Gerald Pryce. My business manager. He handles — arrangements." She looked away, out at her own harbor. "I sent him to Saltmarsh that week. To look at the building, the figures, for the purchase. I didn't send him into the kitchen during service. I didn't send him near the cold room."

"He bought fake saffron on Friel's account and had it delivered," Nora said. "Hassan traced it. Friel arranged it; your man delivered it."

Lena was silent for a long moment. "Then Gerald did more than I told him to. Or someone told him to do more than I knew." Her jaw tightened. "Friel and Gerald, between them, sabotaging the broth. To force the sale. To get me the building cheap." She turned back to Nora, and there was real anger in her now. "I wanted to win, Nora. I wanted my name over that door and my broth — my broth — back. But I did not send a man to poison anyone, and if Gerald did, then he has ruined the one clean thing I had left, which was the truth about whose recipe it was."

"Did Gerald have digoxin?"

"How would I know what Gerald has?" Lena said. But the question had landed, Nora saw. It had landed in Lena like a stone in still water, and the rings were spreading.


Chapter 38: Hour by Hour

Okafor agreed, at last, to do it Nora's way — to reconstruct the night hour by hour, in the kitchen itself, using Pavel's log as the spine.

They stood in the released kitchen, Nora and Okafor and Pavel with his notebook, and walked the timeline like a recipe.

19:00 — service begins. The broth pot full and good — no. The broth pot full and faked, since Daniel himself had swapped it Thursday night, cutting his own saffron. The service broth was dull but not deadly. Forty covers, no casualties.

Half seven — Gerald Pryce in the yard, by the back door, near the cold room. Delivering, or doing something. The fake fifty grams from Friel.

21:05 — Daniel to the office, locked.

22:10 — Imogen in. 22:25 — Imogen out. The fight.

22:26 — Imogen on the phone to the bank, placed by recording until 22:51.

22:40 — Daniel from the office to the cold room. To taste the broth. To taste from his cup.

And there he died, between 22:40 and the hour, alone with the steel cup and the cold.

"The cup," Okafor said. "Everything comes back to the cup. When was the cup prepared? When was the drug put in it?"

Nora looked at the timeline and felt the missing flavor at last, the gap where the answer should be. "Before service," she said. "It had to be before. The cup he tastes from. Someone readied it before the night even started. Before any of these times. Hours before. Cold, and patient, and waiting for him to lift it."


Chapter 39: The Good Batch

"There's something else that's been bothering me," Nora said. "The good saffron. The real Persian threads that were in the tin. Daniel swapped them out Thursday night — Pavel saw him. So where did the good batch go?"

"Used in the trial?" Okafor offered.

"You don't use saffron by pouring it out. You weigh it into a dish. He poured it out into a bag, Pavel said. Decanted it. So there's a bag of the good saffron somewhere — fifty grams, a hundred, worth a small fortune. It didn't go in the bin. I checked the bins." She frowned. "It's gone. Someone took it away."

"Why would the killer take the good saffron?"

"Maybe they wouldn't." Nora paced. "Maybe taking the good saffron isn't the killer's move at all. Maybe it's a separate small theft, like Theo's anchovies, layered in with everything else. Or maybe—" She stopped. "Maybe whoever took it wanted to make sure the fake stayed in the pot. Wanted no chance of the good stuff going back in before the critic came. Removed the temptation. Removed the good saffron so the only broth possible on review day was the bad one."

Okafor considered this. "That's the sabotage, then. Friel's people. Make sure the bad broth's the only broth."

"Maybe." But Nora wasn't sure. The good saffron, removed and hidden, nagged at her like an under-seasoned dish. Somebody had wanted the bitterness to stay. And somebody had wanted Daniel to taste it.


Chapter 40: The Bitterness Stays

She lay awake that night and built it again, and this time a shape came clear.

If you wanted to murder Daniel Reyes with a bitter drug, you had a problem: Daniel had the best palate in the county. He would taste digoxin in a clean broth in a heartbeat. He'd spit it out, call for the dish to be remade, never swallow enough to harm him.

But what if the broth was already bitter? Already off, already cut with fake saffron, already wrong in a way he himself had made it? Then a little extra bitterness — the drug — would hide inside the bitterness he already expected to taste.

And here was the cruelty of it: Daniel would taste the bad broth, the broth he'd cheapened with his own hands, and he would think the bitterness was his own doing. His own cost-cutting. His own shame. He would frown, and swallow, and think I've gone too far with the cut, I'll fix it tomorrow — and the drug would go down with the guilt.

The killer hadn't needed to fool Daniel's palate. They'd needed Daniel to fool himself. To taste his own corner-cutting and blame himself for the wrongness, and swallow.

Which meant the killer knew Daniel had cut the saffron. Knew about the secret fraud. Knew don't tell I.

And only a handful of people could have known that.


Chapter 41: He Tasted First

She made a new list, smaller this time, the way a sauce reduces to its essential thing.

Who knew Daniel had secretly cut the saffron? Pavel knew — he'd watched it happen Thursday night. Hassan suspected — he'd warned about the cheap source. Friel knew the broth would be bad, because he'd arranged a second sabotage of his own. And Daniel knew, obviously.

Who knew Daniel tasted the broth first, alone, from his own cup, every night? Everyone in the kitchen.

Who knew both — the secret cut and the ritual cup — and had a reason rooted in the man, not the money?

Pavel had taken the page to protect the broth's honor. He'd had access. He'd known the cut, known the ritual. But Pavel's grief was for the dish, and his reason ran toward burying the shame, not killing the man. And Pavel kept facts; he had handed her the very timeline that could hang him. A man covering murder does not give the detective his notebook.

She crossed Pavel out, lightly, in pencil. Not certainty. But a strong doubt.

That left the knowledge of the cut. Who else could have known Daniel was cheapening his own broth in secret? Don't tell I. He had hidden it from Imogen above all. So Imogen, in theory, was the last to know.

Unless Imogen had found out. In the office. At ten past ten. In the fifteen minutes when they fought.


Chapter 42: The Ritual

Nora went back to Imogen one more time, in the morning, with the timeline in her hand and the new shape in her head.

"Your fight," she said. "In the office. Pavel logged it: ten past ten to twenty-five past. What did you fight about, exactly?"

"The sale. I told you."

"Only the sale?"

Imogen looked at her, and Nora watched the woman decide how much to give.

"He told me about the saffron," Imogen said at last, quietly. "In the fight. It came out. I accused him of running us into the ground, and he — he laughed, this awful tired laugh, and said he'd already started saving. That he'd cut the saffron forty percent. That nobody would ever know because his broth was famous and his palate was famous and the customers were sheep." Her voice was flat with old fury. "He told me he'd been serving them fraud for weeks and laughing about it. The dish I'd built the whole brand around. He cheapened it and didn't tell me. Don't tell I, the note said. I found the note later, on the desk, after." She pressed her lips together. "So yes. I learned, at twenty past ten on the night he died, that Daniel had been adulterating the broth and hiding it from me."

"And then he went to taste it. From his cup."

"He always tasted it," Imogen said. "First. Alone. Before service. He'd have tasted it that night the same as every night." She frowned. "But service had already happened. Why would he taste it at twenty to eleven, after?"


Chapter 43: After Service

It was a good question, and it cracked something open.

"He tasted before service, every night," Nora said slowly. "Half six, seven. Not at twenty to eleven. So why did he go to the cold room after service, after your fight, to taste the broth again?"

"I don't know."

"Because his text." Nora pulled out her phone, found the photo of Okafor's note. "10:14. 'Don't let them serve it. I'll explain.' He sent that to me at quarter past ten. During your fight, or just after. He'd decided something. He'd decided not to serve the bad broth to the critic. To come clean, maybe. To pull the fraud before it could be tasted by the one palate that mattered more than his own."

Imogen stared. "He was going to stop it."

"He went to the cold room after service to check the broth, to taste it again, to decide what to do for tomorrow. Not his usual ritual taste. A second taste, late, alone, after you'd shamed him and he'd lost his nerve for the lie." Nora's chest tightened. "And the cup was waiting for him. Whoever drugged it didn't even need the morning. They needed only for Daniel, at some point, alone, to lift that cup. And his guilt sent him to it early, in the dark, after service, where no one would see him doubt."

"Then the killer got lucky."

"No," Nora said. "The killer knew him. Knew that a man who'd just been caught cheapening his own famous dish, the night before judgment, would not sleep. Would go and taste it again, alone, and stand in the cold with his shame. The killer counted on the guilt. The guilt was the trap's spring."


Chapter 44: Old and Fresh

"So who," Okafor said, when Nora laid it all before her in the dining room, "knew the man that well? Knew he'd cut the saffron, knew he tasted alone, knew his guilt would drive him to the cup?"

"Someone with an old wound and a fresh one," Nora said. "The poison wasn't money. Friel and the sabotage, that was money — make the broth bad, force the sale. But the poison was personal. Aimed at the man. Timed for his worst, best night."

"Lena Coyle. The stolen recipe. Twelve years old."

"Old enough. And her man Gerald was in the building. But Lena was at her own pass, forty staff, a hundred guests, all night. And Gerald delivered fake saffron — sabotage, Friel's money — but would Gerald carry digoxin? Would he know Daniel's ritual, his guilt, the way the broth was already bitter?" Nora shook her head. "Gerald's an errand boy. He knows buildings and figures. He doesn't know how a chef tastes."

"Then who?"

"Someone inside. Someone who learned, that very night, that Daniel had betrayed the dish. Whose wound was both old — years of being cut out, overlooked, lied to — and fresh, freshly torn open at ten past ten in a locked office." Nora felt the answer arrive the way salt arrives in a sauce, all at once and undeniable. "The poison was in the cup before service. But the trap only sprung because of the fight. And only one person had both the old grievance and the fresh one."


Chapter 45: From Inside

"But the timing's wrong," Okafor objected. "You said the cup was drugged before service. Hours before. Imogen didn't learn about the saffron until the fight at ten past ten. She couldn't have drugged the cup in advance for a betrayal she didn't yet know about."

Nora stopped. The detective was right. That was the gap. That was the flavor still missing.

"The cup was drugged before service," she said slowly. "By someone who already knew about the saffron fraud before the fight. Who already had reason. The fight with Imogen wasn't the cause. It was the trigger that sent him to the already-poisoned cup early."

"So two people," Okafor said. "One who drugged the cup, earlier, knowing the broth was already bitter and would hide it. And the fight, separately, sent him to it."

"Or one person who knew before the fight." Nora pressed her hands to her eyes. "Who knew the saffron was cut before Friday night. Pavel saw it Thursday. Hassan suspected it for months. Friel knew the broth would be bad because he was sabotaging it too." She lowered her hands. "Gerald delivered the fake saffron Friday at half seven, before service, from the yard by the cold room. Gerald was at the cold room. Gerald had access to the cup before service. And Gerald worked for the person with the oldest wound of all."


Chapter 46: The Phone Again

But it wasn't right yet, and Nora knew it wasn't, the way you know a dish is close but not finished.

She went back to Imogen's alibi, because alibis that are too clean are themselves a kind of evidence.

"Your call to the bank," she said. "22:26 to 22:51. It places you in the dining room while Daniel was in the cold room."

"Yes."

"But it doesn't place you before service. Half six, seven, when the cup could have been drugged."

Imogen went still. "I was front of house. Setting the room. Twenty people saw me."

"Were you ever near the cold room before service? Near the cup?"

"I run the dining room, Nora. I don't go in the cold room." But something had shifted in her face — not guilt, but fear, the fear of someone who sees where a thing is pointing.

"Imogen." Nora sat. "I don't think you drugged the cup. Your alibi for the cold room is solid and you didn't know about the saffron until the fight. But you were the last to fight him. And you found the note after. And there's one thing you've never explained." She leaned in. "Why did you go into that locked office at all? He'd locked everyone out. He let you in. Why? What did you knock and say, to make a man who'd sealed himself away open the door?"


Chapter 47: What She Knew

Imogen was quiet for a long time. The sea moved gray beyond the windows.

"I knew he was selling," she said finally. "Not just suspected. Knew. I'd seen the email to Friel that afternoon, on the office screen, before he locked it. Cutting me out. So I knocked and I said, 'I've seen the email, Daniel. Let me in or I'll call Lena Coyle myself and tell her exactly what she's buying — a place drowning in debt run by a man who cheats his own customers.'" She looked at her hands. "And he let me in. Because I'd threatened him. Because I knew about the debt and I'd guessed about the saffron — I'd suspected for weeks, the broth had been tasting thinner — and I let him think I had proof. I bluffed. And in the office he confirmed it. He told me everything. He thought I already knew."

"So you knew about the saffron before the fight, in a way."

"I suspected. I didn't know until he said it." Imogen's eyes came up. "But Nora — suspecting isn't drugging a cup. I threatened the man's business. I wanted to ruin his sale, save my share, win. I'd have destroyed his reputation gladly. I never went near that cold room. The bank recording proves it for the time he died, and twenty waiters prove it for before. I am guilty of a great many cold things. I am not guilty of his death."

And Nora, watching her, believed it. Imogen had wanted to ruin Daniel. She had not wanted to kill him. The flavor of the room was wrong for murder. It tasted of grievance, not of poison.


Chapter 48: Not the Heart, the Stomach

So Nora did the thing she should have done at the start. She went back to the science, and she called Okafor with one question.

"The digoxin," she said. "The dose. Was it definitely enough to kill a healthy man? Or only enough to kill a sick one?"

A pause on the line. "The pathologist flagged that, actually. The dose wasn't massive. In a healthy heart it might have caused sickness, an irregular beat, a bad night. It killed him because his heart was already failing. The condition he was hiding. It pushed a weak heart over the edge."

"So whoever gave it to him," Nora said slowly, "may not have known his heart was bad. May have meant only to sicken him. To make him ill the night before the review. To make him fail, not die."

"That's possible," Okafor admitted. "A dose to incapacitate, not to kill. To put the famous chef in hospital the night before the critic came. Sabotage, again. Make the review impossible. Force the sale."

"Friel's logic," Nora breathed. "Make the broth bad. Make the chef sick. Either way the review's ruined, either way Daniel folds and sells. Gerald delivers the fake saffron and slips the drug into the tasting cup — a dose to make a man too ill to cook, hidden in the bitterness Friel knew would be there. Not murder. Sabotage that didn't know the man had a secret, failing heart."

"And the secret heart," Okafor said quietly, "turned sabotage into killing."


Chapter 49: Guilt Divided

It came apart in her hands at last, the whole split sauce, each part clear.

Daniel had cheapened his own broth in secret, hiding it from Imogen — money, and shame.

Friel had wanted the place sold and arranged sabotage — the fake saffron delivery, and, through Gerald, a dose of digoxin in the tasting cup. Enough, Friel and Gerald believed, to put a healthy chef in bed for the night. Ruin the review. Force the sale. They had not known about Daniel's heart. No one had, barely. The dose meant to sicken stopped a heart already failing.

Imogen had learned the worst of it in the locked office and shamed him, and her shame had sent him, early and alone, to taste the broth one more time — to the cup that was already waiting.

So who killed Daniel Reyes? Friel and Gerald, by the letter of it, with a dose they swore was meant to sicken. But Daniel's own hidden illness had made the dose fatal, and Daniel's own fraud had made the bitterness that hid it, and Imogen's own cruelty had timed his walk to the cup. Guilt shared. Guilt divided. A man undone by money and grievance and his own secrets, all at once, in a cold room, alone.

When Okafor arrested Marcus Friel and Gerald Pryce — for the saffron sabotage and the poisoning both, the charges to be argued for months — Nora felt no triumph. Only the quiet of a long service finally over. The wrongness, named at last. The missing flavor found.

It did not bring Daniel back. But it took her name off the list, and it took the lie out of the broth, and for a cook, that was something close to justice.


Chapter 50: One Quiet Service

They reopened Saltmarsh for one night, six weeks later, before the sale that would, in the end, still go through — to Lena Coyle, who had asked one thing: that the broth be served once more, correctly, under Daniel's name, before her own went over the door.

Nora cooked it. Of course she did.

She came in at four, as always. She lifted the saffron tin — Hassan's saffron now, the real Persian threads, paid for and full and right — and felt its proper weight in her hand. She toasted the threads until the air went gold. She bloomed them slow in warm stock and watched the color spread like a stain in water, and this time there was no fear in the watching, only the old plain pleasure of a thing done right.

The brigade was small that night. Sadie on garnish, steady now, no longer afraid. Pavel on bread, his notebook in his pocket, his conscience clear. Theo on fish, who had bought his own anchovies and brought a tin to leave on Daniel's old station, a private joke and a kind of apology. Imogen in the dining room in her good gray dress, running the room one last time, cleared of the worst of it if not the cold of it.

Forty covers ordered the broth. Forty bowls went out, golden in white, round and warm and faintly sweet.

At the end, when the room had emptied and the floor was going gray under the mop, Nora ladled a little of the broth into a steel cup and stood in the quiet kitchen and tasted it herself. First. Alone. The way he always had.

It was right. It was exactly right.

She rinsed the cup and set it on the rack, and turned off the lights, and let the kitchen breathe.
