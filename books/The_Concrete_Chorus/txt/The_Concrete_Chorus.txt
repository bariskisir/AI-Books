Chapter 1: The Stone Man of Aldgate

The body sat propped against the wall of the alley, legs splayed, arms at its sides. It could have been a homeless man sleeping off a night of cheap gin, except for the color. Grey. Every inch of exposed skin was the same dull limestone shade, from the weathered face to the motionless hands resting on the pavement.

Leo Ashworth read about it over his morning coffee. The article in the Metro was brief — three paragraphs buried between a parliamentary scandal and a football transfer rumor. "Strange Death in Aldgate: Police Puzzled by Unusual Condition." No name released. No cause determined. The paper speculated about a new street drug with horrific side effects.

He set the cup down and stared at the grimy window of his Camden flat. The morning light filtered through layers of London dust, casting everything in a sickly yellow glow. He told himself it was not his problem. He was out of that world now. Had been for three years, two months, and eleven days.

His office sat on the floor below, a cramped room above a kebab shop where he handled divorce cases and missing pets. The sign on the door read "Ashworth Confidential Investigations" — a name he'd kept because changing it cost money he didn't have. The cheap Venetian blinds were permanently crooked. The filing cabinet had a drawer that would not close.

He went downstairs at nine, unlocked the door, and sat behind his desk. The morning passed in the usual way: a call from a woman who thought her husband was cheating (he was), an email from a man who wanted proof his neighbor was stealing his mail (she wasn't), and a flyer for a new pizza place that had somehow been shoved under his door.

The second article appeared on his phone at lunch. The BBC news app pinged with a notification: "Met Police Investigate Second 'Stone Death' in Soho." Leo's thumb hovered over the link. He knew better. He had spent fifteen years building a reputation as London's most reliable occult investigator, and he had spent the last three dismantling it through sheer inactivity and self-contempt.

He clicked the link anyway.

The second victim was a woman in her forties, found in her flat above a shop on Old Compton Street. Same condition. Same grey stone skin frozen in what the reporter called "a mask of stark terror." The police were now consulting with pathologists from three different hospitals, all of whom were reportedly baffled.

Leo put the phone face-down on his desk.

His hand moved toward the bottom drawer of the filing cabinet — the one that would not close because it was jammed with old case files. He stopped himself. That drawer held everything he had tried to forget: photographs of creatures he had failed to protect, witness statements from beings the courts did not recognize as people, and a termination letter from the Accord's licensing board revoking his investigator's credentials.

"Not my problem," he said aloud, to no one.

The stuffed raven arrived at four o'clock.

It came via courier — a young man in a blue uniform who seemed confused by the package he was holding. "Says it's from a taxidermist," he said, reading the return address. "Weird, right?"

Leo signed for it and carried the box upstairs. It was about the size of a shoebox, wrapped in brown paper, tied with string. He cut the string with his pocket knife and lifted the lid.

Inside, nestled in wood shavings, lay a taxidermied raven. Its glass eyes caught the light with an unsettling gleam. A small envelope was tucked under one wing.

He opened the envelope. The note inside was written in Edgar Pillsworth's unmistakable hand — the cramped, spidery script of a man who had spent too many years writing labels for specimens in jars.

"Leo — the stones are not what they seem. The veils are thinning. Come to the shop tonight. Bring your old eyes. — E"

Leo read the note three times. Then he looked at the raven, which stared back at him with its unblinking glass gaze.

He had promised himself he was done. He had promised the Accord, the council, and most of all himself that he would never again step into the hidden world that had cost him everything.

But the stones were calling. And Edgar Pillsworth had never once led him wrong.

He put on his coat, checked that his old investigator's badge was still in the inside pocket where he had kept it for three years without ever looking at it, and locked the door behind him.

The London evening was damp and cold. He walked toward the Tube station with his hands in his pockets, past the neon lights of Camden Market, past the tourists with their cameras and the locals with their tired faces. The city hummed around him, oblivious to the fact that beneath its streets, something ancient was stirring.

He was going back in.

Chapter 2: The Taxidermist's Invitation

Edgar Pillsworth's shop occupied the ground floor of a Victorian townhouse in Bethnal Green, sandwiched between a laundromat and a boarded-up pub. The window display featured a fox mid-leap, a barn owl with outstretched wings, and a badger holding a teacup. The sign above the door read "Pillsworth's Curiosities — Est. 1873."

Leo had never understood why Edgar chose to run a taxidermy shop. The man was a cryptozoologist of considerable reputation, a former consultant to the Accord's historical division, and one of the only humans the creature community genuinely trusted. He could have been anything. Instead, he spent his days stuffing foxes and mounting deer heads for country pubs.

The bell above the door chimed as Leo entered. The shop smelled of sawdust, formaldehyde, and old books. Glass cases lined the walls, filled with birds and rodents arranged in elaborate dioramas. A mongoose fought a cobra. A squirrel sat at a miniature tea table.

"Edgar?"

The curtain behind the counter parted, and Edgar Pillsworth emerged. He was seventy-three years old, with a face like a crumpled paper bag and tufts of white hair sprouting from his ears. His cardigan had holes in both elbows, and his spectacles were held together with electrical tape.

"You came," Edgar said. It was not a question.

"Your note was cryptic even by your standards."

"I've been sending ravens to people for forty years. You're the first one who actually showed up." Edgar gestured toward the back room. "Come through. I've made tea."

The back room was part workshop, part archive. Shelves of specimen jars lined the walls, each containing something preserved in amber liquid. A half-finished otter lay on the workbench, its body stuffed with cotton wadding. Maps covered every available surface — Ordinance Survey maps, Tube maps, and hand-drawn charts of places that did not appear on any official document.

Edgar poured two cups of tea from a pot that had been steeping too long. Leo accepted one and waited.

"The Aldgate victim," Edgar said finally. "She was a naiad. Water nymph. Lived as a human for thirty-seven years. Had a job at an accounting firm. Paid her taxes. Had a cat named Marmalade."

"She?"

"The soul doesn't carry gender the way a body does, but in her human form, she presented as female. Point is, Leo — this wasn't a murder. This was an execution. And whoever did it knew exactly what she was."

Leo set his tea down untasted. "The Accord should be handling this. They have investigators. They have protocols."

"The Accord is in chaos. The Keeper hasn't made a public statement. The council is meeting in emergency session every night." Edgar moved to the workbench and picked up a magnifying glass. "I managed to get a sample from the first victim's remains. Before the police took the body."

He handed Leo a small glass vial. Inside was a fragment of grey dust, finer than sand.

"I had it analyzed by a contact at University College London. They think it's calcium carbonate — essentially limestone. But there's a trace element they can't identify. Something that doesn't match any known mineral on earth."

Leo held the vial up to the light. The dust shimmered faintly, catching the glow with an iridescence that had no business being there.

"This is Gorgon work," Edgar said quietly. "I've seen it before. Once. In a manuscript from the fifteenth century that described a similar petrification in Venice. A creature was turned to stone in a locked room. The Accord covered it up, but the record survived."

"Gorgons are extinct. The last one died in —"

"The last recorded Gorgon died in 1842. Yes. But bloodlines persist. Traits carry. And artifacts —" Edgar paused, adjusting his spectacles. "There are artifacts that can channel Gorgon magic without a living Gorgon present."

Leo set the vial on the workbench. "You need to take this to the Keeper. I'm not licensed anymore."

"I know you're not licensed. I don't care." Edgar's voice sharpened. "Three years is long enough to punish yourself. Whatever happened with the Lamont case —"

"We don't talk about the Lamont case."

"Fine. Then we talk about this." Edgar spread his hands over the maps on the workbench. "Two creatures dead in three days. A weapon that hasn't been seen in centuries. A creature community on the verge of panic. And a human investigator who knows more about the hidden world than anyone else alive."

Leo was silent.

"I'm not asking you to take the case," Edgar said. "I'm asking you to look at the evidence. One look. If you still want to walk away, I won't stop you."

Leo looked at the vial of shimmering dust. He looked at the maps of hidden London. He looked at Edgar's tired, earnest face.

"One look," he said.

Chapter 3: What the Accord Hides

Edgar led him past the workbench to a heavy oak door at the back of the shop. Three locks, each requiring a different key. Beyond it, a narrow staircase descended into a basement that extended far deeper than the building's foundation should have allowed.

The basement was a archive. Filing cabinets stretched in rows, their metal surfaces gleaming under fluorescent lights. A dehumidifier hummed in the corner. The temperature was cool enough that Leo could see his breath.

"The Accord doesn't know I have this," Edgar said, walking between the rows. "I've been collecting records for fifty years. Copies of council minutes, witness statements, autopsy reports. Everything they tried to bury."

"Why?"

"Because someone needs to remember the truth. The Accord's job is to maintain peace, and sometimes peace requires forgetting. But forgetting is dangerous."

Edgar stopped at a cabinet marked with a faded label: "Petrification Events — European Theatre."

He pulled the drawer open. Inside were perhaps a dozen folders, their edges yellowed with age.

"There have been more than two? I thought you said one."

"I said I saw one. I knew about several others." Edgar removed a folder and laid it on the nearest cabinet. "Venice, 1497. Prague, 1612. Bruges, 1745. Oslo, 1901. Each time, a single creature was turned to stone. Each time, the Accord investigated and found no culprit."

"Cold cases."

"Yes. But look at the pattern." Edgar opened the folder to reveal a map of Europe marked with small red dots. "Every petrification occurred within five miles of a major ley line intersection. And every victim was a creature who had chosen to live as a human rather than within the creature community."

Leo traced the map with his finger. London appeared at the center of a web of red lines, a nexus of ley energy that made it one of the most magical cities in the world.

"The Aldgate victim — Ione — was a naiad living as a human. The Soho victim — her name was Helena, a lamia — also passed for human. Whoever is doing this is targeting integrated creatures."

"Creatures who have chosen the human world over the hidden one."

Edgar nodded. "Exactly. This isn't random. It's a message."

The bell above the shop door rang, distant and muffled through the floors above. Edgar held up a hand and listened. Footsteps crossed the shop floor, heavy and deliberate.

"Stay here," Edgar whispered.

He climbed the stairs, closing the oak door behind him. Leo stood alone in the archive, surrounded by decades of hidden history. He could hear muffled voices above — Edgar's calm and measured, the other voice low and unfamiliar.

His phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number: "You're in danger. Get out of Bethnal Green. Now."

Leo looked at the message, then at the stairs. The voices above had stopped. He heard a single thud, like something heavy hitting the floor.

He moved.

Up the stairs, through the oak door, into the workroom. The shop beyond was silent. He crossed to the curtain and pulled it aside.

Edgar Pillsworth lay on the floor behind the counter, his eyes open, his mouth frozen mid-word. But he was not grey. He was not stone.

He was simply dead.

Leo knelt beside him, checking for a pulse he knew he would not find. The taxidermist's skin was still warm. A thin line of blood traced from his nostril to the corner of his mouth. No visible wound. No sign of struggle.

The front door of the shop stood open, swinging gently in the evening breeze.

Leo pulled out his phone and dialed the only number he had for the hidden world — a line he had not called in three years.

It rang four times. Then a woman's voice answered.

"Cassia Verney."

"Cassia. It's Leo Ashworth."

A pause. "I wondered when you would call."

"Edgar's dead. Someone killed him. And I think they were here for me."

Another pause, longer this time. "Where are you now?"

"Still in the shop."

"Stay there. I'm coming." The line went dead.

Leo looked down at Edgar's face, peaceful in death. The old man had dragged him back into this world, and now he was gone. The first name on Leo's list of victims had just grown by one.

He looked at the vial of shimmering dust still in his pocket. Then he looked at the open door.

He waited.

Chapter 4: The Sphinx at the British Museum

Cassia Verney arrived forty minutes later. She came through the back alley, bypassing the police car that had pulled up to the front of the shop — someone had reported the open door. She moved with the fluid grace of someone who had centuries of practice controlling her body.

She was tall, perhaps fifty in human years but ageless in the way of creatures that had never truly been young. Her hair was silver and gold in equal measure, pulled back in a tight bun. She wore a trench coat over a museum curator's tweed suit. Her eyes were the color of amber, and they missed nothing.

"I dealt with the police," she said, stepping into the back room. "A senior officer owes me a favor. They'll record it as a heart attack."

"He was murdered."

"I know." Cassia knelt beside Edgar's body and closed his eyes with a gentle hand. "But the human world cannot know that. The Accord will investigate internally."

"The Accord couldn't investigate its way out of a paper bag."

Cassia looked up at him, and for a moment her composure cracked. Something raw and ancient flickered in her amber eyes. "You think I don't know that? Edgar was my friend too. He was one of the few humans I trusted completely. And now he's gone."

Leo felt the weight of his own words. "I'm sorry. I didn't mean —"

"I know what you meant." She stood, brushing dust from her knees. "Come. We can't stay here. There are things you need to see."

She led him through the alley to a black car parked on the main road — a vintage Jaguar that looked like it belonged to someone with very old money. They drove in silence through the evening streets, past the lights of the City and into the quieter neighborhoods of Bloomsbury.

The British Museum loomed ahead, its neoclassical facade glowing in the floodlights. Cassia parked in a reserved spot and used a key card to enter through a side door.

The museum at night was a different place. The grand halls, empty of tourists, echoed with their footsteps. Statues watched from their plinths. The glass cases held their treasures in dim light, like sleeping animals.

Cassia's office was in the Greek and Roman department, a cluttered space filled with reference books, artifact fragments, and half-written cataloging notes. A nameplate on her desk read "Dr. Cassia Verney — Curator of Classical Antiquities."

She locked the door behind them and opened a cabinet that appeared to be solid wood but was actually reinforced steel. Inside, protected by foam padding, sat a fragment of stone about the size of Leo's hand.

"The lamia from Soho," Cassia said. "I had this piece removed before the police took the body. The Accord has a standing arrangement with certain coroners."

Leo took the fragment. It was warm to the touch, as if it still held some residual life. The surface was smooth, almost polished, and when he held it to the light, he could see traces of darker material within — veins of something that had once been organic.

"I've seen petrification before," Cassia said. "Basilisks. Cockatrices. The Medusa herself in the old stories. But this is different."

"How?"

She took the fragment back and placed it under a magnifying lamp on her desk. "Natural Gorgon magic doesn't leave traces. The victim becomes stone, completely and irreversibly. But this — look."

She pointed to the darker veins. Under magnification, they appeared to be moving — slowly, almost imperceptibly, like sediment settling in water.

"There's residual awareness. The victim is still in there, somewhere. Not alive, but not entirely gone. The magic that did this is impure. Modified."

"Someone's using an artifact."

Cassia nodded. "I believe so. The question is which artifact, and who is wielding it."

Leo sat down heavily in the chair across from her desk. "Edgar mentioned the Aegis of Perseus."

Cassia's face went still. "Edgar shouldn't have known about that."

"He seemed to know a lot of things the Accord wanted buried."

"He always did." She moved to her computer and began typing. "The Aegis of Perseus is a shield — technically a ritual implement — said to have been used by the hero Perseus to reflect Medusa's gaze and petrify her with her own power. It was stored in the Temple of Athena for centuries before being lost."

"Lost or hidden?"

Cassia did not answer immediately. She pulled up a catalog entry on her screen — a photograph of an empty display case, its velvet lining undisturbed.

"The Aegis was in our archives until six months ago," she said quietly. "I checked it myself during a routine inventory. It was there."

"And now?"

She turned the screen toward him. The empty case stared back.

"Now it's gone. And I never received a transfer request, a loan agreement, or a theft report."

Leo leaned forward. "Someone inside the museum took it."

"Someone with access to the most secure storage in the building." Cassia's voice was cold. "Someone who knew exactly what they were looking for."

She closed the catalog entry and faced him. "I have a contact who might know more. A creature who operates in the underground antiquities trade. But she doesn't deal with humans directly."

"You want me to go."

"I want you to go. You know the hidden world. You know how to talk to creatures who don't trust the Accord. And you have a stake in this now."

Leo thought of Edgar, dead on his own shop floor. Of the grey stone bodies in Aldgate and Soho. Of the shimmering dust in his pocket.

"When?" he asked.

"Tomorrow night. There's an auction in the Below. I'll get you an invitation."

The word "Below" hung in the air between them — the name for the hidden layer of London that existed in the cracks between human perception. Leo had not been there in three years.

He nodded. "I'll need supplies."

Cassia opened her desk drawer and pulled out a worn leather wallet. She tossed it to him. Inside was a badge — not his old investigator's license, but something new. A golden disk inscribed with the seal of the Accord and the words "Special Consultant — Full Authority."

"Consider it a temporary reinstatement," she said. "Don't make me regret it."

Leo looked at the badge. It felt heavier than it should have.

"I won't," he said.

But they both knew that was a promise he might not be able to keep.

Chapter 5: A Second Stone

The Soho crime scene had been cleared by morning, but DCI Tom Fletcher kept a copy of the photographs in his desk drawer. He had been looking at them for three days now, trying to find something the forensic teams had missed.

They showed a woman — Helena, forty-two, owner of a small boutique on Old Compton Street — sitting in her living room chair. Her face was frozen in an expression of pure animal terror. Her hands were raised slightly, as if she had been trying to ward something off. The grey stone that covered her body had preserved every detail: the wrinkles around her eyes, the individual strands of her hair, the pattern of her dress.

Fletcher was a large man with a boxer's build and a face that had seen too many late nights. He had been a detective for twenty-two years, and in that time he had learned that the official explanation was almost always wrong. The official explanation for this case was that some kind of industrial accident or chemical exposure had caused rapid calcification. The unofficial explanation was that something else entirely was happening.

He had met Leo Ashworth once, three years ago, on a case involving a series of disappearances in Whitechapel. The case had been classified before Fletcher could file his report, and Ashworth had vanished from the scene before Fletcher could ask him any questions. But he remembered the man's eyes — the way they seemed to see things that weren't there. Or things that were not supposed to be there.

When the second victim was found, Fletcher had requested Ashworth's file from the Met's internal database. It was thin — a few addresses, a terminated license, no criminal record. But the notes attached to the file were interesting. "Consultant on supernatural cases." "Recommended by Pillsworth, Edgar (deceased)." "Terminated following Lamont incident."

Fletcher looked up from the photographs. His office was small and cluttered, a glass box in the corner of the CID bullpen. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead. Someone had left a half-eaten sandwich on the desk next to his.

He picked up his phone and called the number he had found in Ashworth's file.

It rang five times. Then: "Who is this?"

"Detective Chief Inspector Tom Fletcher, Metropolitan Police. I need to speak with Leo Ashworth."

A pause. "Speaking."

"I'm investigating the Aldgate and Soho deaths. I think you might have information that could help."

Another pause, longer this time. "Why would you think that?"

"Because I've seen your file. And because I've been a detective long enough to know when I'm being fed a cover story. Three pathologists can't identify the cause of death. The bodies are chemically identical to limestone. And a man named Edgar Pillsworth, who your file lists as a known associate, died yesterday of an apparent heart attack."

"Heart attack."

"That's what the coroner says. I don't believe it."

The line was silent for a long moment. Then Ashworth spoke: "Where are you calling from?"

"New Scotland Yard."

"Meet me at the British Museum. Main entrance. One hour."

The line went dead.

Fletcher looked at the receiver, then hung up. He grabbed his coat and headed for the door.

The British Museum was quiet on a Tuesday morning. A school group clustered around the Rosetta Stone. Tourists wandered through the Egyptian galleries. Fletcher stood near the main entrance, watching the crowd, until a woman in a tweed suit approached him.

"Detective Fletcher?"

"Yes."

"I'm Dr. Cassia Verney. I'm a curator here. Mr. Ashworth asked me to bring you to him."

She led him through a staff-only door and down a corridor lined with storage rooms. The air grew cooler, the lighting dimmer. They stopped at an unmarked door, which she opened with a key card.

Leo Ashworth sat at a table in the center of the room. A fragment of grey stone lay on the surface before him.

"I'm told you don't believe in monsters," Ashworth said without preamble.

"I believe in what I can see."

"Then look at this." Ashworth pushed the stone fragment toward him. "This came from the Soho victim. Touch it."

Fletcher hesitated, then reached out and touched the fragment. It was warm. Warmer than it should have been.

"It's still alive," he said, and heard how absurd the words sounded.

"No. But it's — residual. Like heat from a light bulb that's been turned off." Ashworth sat back. "You're right to not believe the cover story. These weren't chemical accidents. They were attacks. And the person responsible is still out there."

"Who's the target?"

"Creatures." Ashworth said the word carefully, watching Fletcher's reaction. "Beings that aren't human. That have been living among humans for centuries."

Fletcher did not laugh. He did not roll his eyes. He had been a detective too long to dismiss anything out of hand. "What kind of creatures?"

"All kinds. The first victim was a water spirit. The second was a lamia — a serpent-woman from Greek mythology. They'd been living as humans for decades, hiding what they were."

"And the person killing them?"

"We think he's using an artifact called the Aegis of Perseus. It was stolen from this museum six months ago."

Fletcher looked from Ashworth to Cassia and back. "Why are you telling me this?"

"Because the police have resources we don't. And because when the truth comes out — and it will come out — you'll need to know what you're dealing with."

"And what exactly am I dealing with?"

Ashworth met his eyes. "A hidden war that's been brewing for centuries. And someone who wants to start the fighting."

Fletcher stood in the small room, surrounded by artifacts from ancient civilizations, and felt the ground shift beneath his feet. Everything he thought he knew about the world had just been quietly revised.

He sat down in the chair across from Ashworth.

"Tell me everything."

Chapter 6: The Scholar's Skepticism

Dr. Mira Chandrasekhar did not believe in the supernatural. She believed in evidence, in peer review, in the scientific method. She had spent fifteen years building a reputation as one of the world's foremost experts on classical mythology, and she had done it by treating myths as cultural artifacts rather than literal history.

When Cassia Verney appeared at her office door and asked her to consult on a "matter of some urgency," Mira assumed it was about the new exhibition on Greek hero cults. She was wrong.

"I need you to keep an open mind," Cassia said, leading her through the museum's lower levels. "What I'm about to show you will challenge everything you believe about the nature of reality."

Mira suppressed a sigh. She had heard variations of this speech before, usually from amateur archaeologists who thought they had found proof of Atlantis or alien visitations.

"I'm a scientist, Dr. Verney. I keep an open mind, but I require evidence."

"Good. I have evidence."

They entered a room that Mira had never seen before — a laboratory hidden behind a false wall in the museum's sub-basement. Leo Ashworth was waiting inside, along with a fragment of grey stone that looked like it had been chiseled from a statue.

"What is this?" Mira asked.

"Evidence," Ashworth said. "Touch it."

She hesitated, then reached out. The stone was warm. She frowned and touched it again. Room temperature stone should have been cool. She checked with the back of her hand. Still warm.

"Thermal retention is unusual," she said. "What's the composition?"

"Calcium carbonate. Essentially limestone. But with trace elements that don't match any known geological formation."

Mira leaned closer. The fragment had a faint shimmer, like mica, but more organic. She could almost imagine she saw something moving beneath the surface.

"This came from a human body?"

"A body that was once human, yes."

"Once human?"

Ashworth looked at Cassia, who nodded. He took a breath.

"The victim was a lamia. A creature from Greek mythology. She had been living as a human for forty years."

Mira straightened up. "You're asking me to believe in mythological creatures."

"I'm not asking you to believe anything. I'm showing you a piece of a woman who was turned to stone by a magical artifact. What you choose to believe is your own business."

Mira turned to Cassia. "You're a respected curator. You can't seriously —"

"Watch." Cassia held her hand over the stone fragment and closed her eyes. She began to speak in a language Mira did not recognize — ancient Greek, but older than any dialect she had studied. The air in the room grew thick. The lights flickered.

The stone fragment began to glow.

Mira stepped back, her heart pounding. The glow was faint, barely visible in the fluorescent light, but it was unmistakable. The stone was responding to Cassia's voice.

"She's still in there," Cassia said, opening her eyes. "Trapped. Aware. The magic that did this isn't complete. It's a perversion of the original power."

Mira's scientific mind raced to find explanations. A hoax. Some kind of sophisticated projection. A psychological trick. But she had worked with Cassia for two years, and she had never seen the woman do anything less than completely professional.

"What was that?" she asked, her voice steadier than she felt.

"A sphinx's trick. Words have power when you know how to use them."

"A sphinx."

"Yes."

Mira looked at the glowing stone. She looked at Ashworth's tired, earnest face. She looked at Cassia, who appeared to be exactly what she had just claimed to be.

"I need to sit down," Mira said.

She spent the next hour in a small office, drinking tea that Cassia had brewed, while Ashworth explained the situation. The hidden world. The Accord. The petrifications. The stolen artifact. Edgar Pillsworth's murder.

By the end, Mira's hands were shaking, but her mind was clear.

"Assuming I accept your premise," she said carefully, "what do you need from me?"

"Research," Ashworth said. "You're one of the world's leading experts on Greek mythology. The artifact was stolen from this museum. The killer is using modified Gorgon magic. If there's a pattern in the myths that could help us predict his next move, you're the person most likely to find it."

Mira considered. Her career was built on rational analysis. Accepting this case meant accepting that the foundations of her discipline were wrong — that myths were not symbolic stories but distorted histories.

But the stone fragment was still warm on the table. She could not explain that.

"I'll help," she said. "But I want to see everything. The artifacts, the records, the —" she struggled for the word " — the creatures. I want proof."

"You'll get it," Ashworth said. "But once you see it, you can't unsee it. And you can't tell anyone. The Accord protects the secret of the hidden world. If you break that trust, there are consequences."

"What kind of consequences?"

"Let's hope you never find out."

Mira looked at the stone one more time. Somewhere beneath years of academic conditioning, a deeper instinct was stirring — the same instinct that had first drawn her to mythology as a child. The sense that the old stories were trying to tell her something important.

She held out her hand. "I'll take the case."

Ashworth shook it. His grip was firm, his eyes tired.

"Welcome to the hidden world, Dr. Chandrasekhar. I hope you survive the experience."

Chapter 7: The Gorgon's Trail

They set up operations in Cassia's office, spreading maps and photographs across every available surface. The afternoon light filtered through the high windows, casting long shadows across the room.

Cassia had retrieved copies of the autopsy reports from her contacts in the coroner's office. The official documents were masterpieces of obfuscation — "rapid calcification syndrome," "unknown mineral deposition," "pending toxicology." But the real data was in the margins, handwritten by pathologists who knew what they were looking at.

"The magic signature is consistent across both victims," Cassia said, tapping the reports. "The same concentration of trace elements, the same pattern of calcification, the same residual energy. It's like a fingerprint."

Leo studied the photographs. The victims' postures were almost identical — arms raised, mouths open, eyes wide. They had seen death coming.

"The killer wants them to see it," he said. "He could have struck from behind, caught them sleeping. But he wants them facing him."

"Terror amplifies the magic," Cassia said. "The older Gorgon myths mention it. Medusa's power was strongest when her victims were afraid. It's why she was depicted as monstrous — terror opened the path for petrification."

Mira had been silent, reading through a stack of reference books she had brought from her office. Now she looked up.

"The Aegis of Perseus," she said. "The myths describe it as a shield or a breastplate, depending on the source. It was given to Perseus by Athena to help him defeat Medusa. Some versions say it had a reflective surface that allowed him to see Medusa's reflection instead of looking directly at her."

"That matches what we know," Leo said. "The killer is using it to channel and redirect Gorgon magic."

"But there's a problem." Mira held up a book open to an illustration of Perseus. "The Aegis was a protective artifact. It was designed to shield the wielder from harmful magic, not to amplify it. Using it offensively should be impossible."

Cassia nodded slowly. "Unless someone modified it. The Accord's records mention experiments in artifact modification — attempts to reverse the polarity of magical items. Most ended badly."

"Ashford," Leo said.

Both women looked at him.

"Who?" Mira asked.

"Just a name. Edgar mentioned it once, years ago. A human sorcerer who had been buying rare artifacts. I never followed up."

Cassia's face had gone pale. "Edgar mentioned Ashford to you?"

"Just in passing. Why?"

"Because Lord Ashford was my student. Decades ago. I taught him the Accord's deepest secrets. And then he disappeared."

The room fell silent. Leo watched Cassia's carefully maintained composure crack at the edges.

"He stole from the Accord's archives before he left," Cassia continued. "Manuscripts, maps, records of artifact locations. I assumed he was dead. I hoped he was dead."

"Does this fit? An artifact auction, modified magic, targeting integrated creatures?"

"It fits too well." Cassia turned away, her hands gripping the edge of her desk. "I created this. I trained the man who is now killing innocent creatures."

"You trained a student. What he did with that training is his choice."

"That's a generous way of looking at it." Cassia's voice was bitter. "But I'll take what I can get."

Mira cleared her throat. "If Ashford is the one doing this, we need to find him. Do we have any leads?"

Leo pulled out his phone and opened the message he had received before Edgar's death. He had not told the others about it yet.

"I got this just before Edgar was killed." He turned the screen toward them. "Unknown number. 'You're in danger. Get out of Bethnal Green. Now.'"

Cassia read the message, her brow furrowing. "Someone warned you."

"Someone who knew what was coming. And who knew how to reach me on a number I haven't used in years."

"That narrows the field. The number was known only to people in the hidden world."

"Or to people who had access to Accord records." Leo pocketed the phone. "Either way, it means someone inside the community is willing to help. Or someone who wants to play both sides."

Mira was still staring at the message. "Can we trace the number?"

"Already tried. It's a burner. No way to track it."

"Then we need another approach." Mira moved to the map of London spread across Cassia's desk. "The victims were both attacked in their homes. That means the killer knows where integrated creatures live. He has access to Accord census data, or he's been following them."

"The Accord's census is kept in a secure vault," Cassia said. "Only the Keeper and the council have access."

"Could Ashford have accessed it when he was your student?"

Cassia was silent for a long moment. "Yes. He assisted me with census updates during his training. He could have copied the records without my knowledge."

"Then the killer has a list of targets." Leo traced his finger along the map, from Aldgate to Soho. "And he's working through it systematically."

Mira picked up a pen and began marking locations on the map. "Aldgate. Soho. If the pattern continues, the next target will be somewhere along the ley line —" She drew a line across the map. " — here. Covent Garden. Or Holborn. Or the Strand."

"Too many possibilities."

"Then we need to narrow it down." Mira looked at Cassia. "You have access to the census. If we cross-reference integrated creatures living along that ley line with the ones who have been most vocal about human integration, we might be able to predict the next target."

Cassia nodded slowly. "I can access the records. But if anyone finds out I'm sharing census data outside the Accord —"

"You'll lose your position as Keeper."

"I'll lose more than that. I'll be exiled."

Leo met her eyes. "Then we make sure no one finds out."

Cassia held his gaze for a moment, then turned to her computer. Her fingers moved across the keyboard, accessing files that should have been locked behind layers of magical and digital security.

"I'll find our next target," she said. "And then we catch this bastard before he strikes again."

Chapter 8: The Night Porter's Toll

The entrance to the Below was beneath a Tube station that didn't exist on any map. Leo had been there before, three years ago, on a case that had ended badly. He had hoped never to return.

The entrance was disguised as a maintenance door on the Bakerloo line, accessible only to those who knew how to see it. Leo stood before it now, in the empty corridor of a station that had closed twenty minutes ago. The last train had departed. The platforms were silent.

He pressed his palm against the door. A pulse of warmth traveled through the wood — the door recognizing him, checking his credentials. Three years was a long time. He wasn't sure he would still be welcome.

The door clicked open.

Beyond it, a staircase descended into darkness. Leo had brought a flashlight, but he knew it would not work here. Light in the Below operated by different rules. He pocketed the flashlight and began to descend.

The stairs were old stone, worn smooth by centuries of feet. The air grew warmer as he descended, and the smell changed — from the damp of the Tube tunnels to something older, earthier, like the inside of a cave or an ancient tomb.

At the bottom of the stairs, a corridor stretched ahead, lined with doors on either side. Some were ordinary wooden doors. Others were arched, carved with symbols Leo recognized from a dozen different magical traditions. A few were simply holes in the wall, covered by curtains of beads or leather.

The Night Porter's office was at the end of the corridor, behind a door made of iron bands and rivets. Leo knocked three times.

The door swung open.

The Night Porter was a chimera — the head of a lion, the body of a goat, the tail of a serpent. He sat behind a desk made of petrified wood, his lion's eyes fixed on Leo with an unblinking stare.

"You," the Porter said. His voice was a rumble, like stones grinding together. "I thought you were gone."

"I was gone. I'm back."

"The Accord revoked your license."

"They reinstated me. Temporary consultant."

The Porter snorted, a puff of smoke curling from his nostrils. "The Accord changes its mind like the wind changes direction. One day you're in, the next you're out. But you always come back, don't you, Ashworth?"

"I need access to the auction houses. The illegal ones."

The Porter's tail twitched. "The auction houses don't deal with humans without an intermediary."

"You're the intermediary."

"I'm the Night Porter. I control who passes. That doesn't make me your errand boy."

Leo reached into his coat and pulled out the golden badge Cassia had given him. He set it on the desk. "This gives me full authority."

The Porter looked at the badge but didn't touch it. "Full authority means nothing in the Below. Down here, authority is about what you can offer. What are you offering?"

Leo had expected this. The Night Porter never gave anything for free. "A favor. Future consideration."

"Vague. I don't like vague."

"It's the best I can do. I'm working a case with no budget and no backup. If I survive, I'll owe you."

The Porter's lion eyes narrowed. "If you survive. That's an interesting condition." He leaned back, his goat's body shifting uncomfortably on the stone chair. "I've heard about the petrifications. Everyone in the Below has heard. The creatures are scared."

"They should be. The killer is targeting integrated creatures. Anyone who's chosen to live as human."

"The First-Born are claiming responsibility."

"I know."

"And you're going after them."

"I'm going after whoever is behind this. The First-Born might be involved, but they're not the mastermind."

The Porter considered this. His serpent tail coiled and uncoiled on the floor, a nervous gesture that Leo had never seen before.

"One favor," the Porter said finally. "To be named at a future date. You will honor it, or the Below will remember your broken word forever."

"I'll honor it."

The Porter reached under his desk and produced a brass key, old and tarnished. "This opens the door to the Attic Auction. It's held on the third level of the Below, in what used to be a Victorian ballroom. If your artifact passed through the Below, someone at the Attic will know about it."

Leo took the key. It was heavier than it looked. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Thank me when you're still alive next week."

Leo turned to leave, but the Porter's voice stopped him.

"Ashworth."

He looked back.

"The creatures you're trying to protect — they remember what you did. The Lamont case. They haven't forgiven you."

Leo felt the old wound reopen. "I know."

"Make sure you don't fail them again."

Leo walked back up the corridor, the brass key cold in his hand. He had three years of guilt to carry, and now he was adding more weight to the load.

The door to the Below swung shut behind him, and he was back in the human world, standing on an empty Tube platform, the last train long gone.

He checked his watch. The Attic Auction would begin in two hours. He had time to find a taxi and prepare himself for what he might find.

Chapter 9: The First-Born Manifesto

Cassia found the scroll rolled inside a hollowed-out book on her desk. It had been left sometime in the night, placed carefully among her reference texts as if it were just another volume on Greek pottery.

She did not touch it immediately. Instead, she stood very still, scanning her office for any sign of the messenger. The windows were locked. The door had been bolted from the inside when she arrived. The ventilation grilles were too small for anything larger than a rat.

But creatures could be very small when they wanted to be.

She approached the scroll slowly, her senses extended. No traps. No lingering magic beyond the scroll's own sealed wards. She reached out and lifted it from the book.

The seal was black wax, stamped with an image she recognized: a circle divided into three segments, each containing a symbol — a serpent, a flame, and a horn. The mark of the First-Born.

She broke the seal and unrolled the parchment.

The script was elegant, written in the old tongue that most modern creatures could no longer read. Cassia had learned it centuries ago, when she was young and the world was simpler. She read it now with growing coldness in her chest.

"To the Keeper of the Accord, and to all creatures of the hidden world:

For too long, we have hidden in the shadows of a species that is beneath us. For too long, we have bent our necks to laws written by humans who do not know we exist. The Accord was a treaty of necessity, not of justice. It was meant to protect us, but it has become a cage.

We, the First-Born, reclaim the birthright of all creatures. We do not ask for equality with humans. We do not ask for coexistence. We claim our place as the true inheritors of this world, and we demand that every creature of the hidden world renounce their human allegiances within seven days.

Those who refuse will face the fate of the two who have already fallen. They chose humanity over their own kind, and they have been punished accordingly. The stone they have become stands as a monument to their betrayal.

Seven days, Keeper. Then the harvest begins in earnest.

The First-Born"

Cassia read the scroll three times, memorizing every word. Then she sat down at her desk and stared at the wall for a long moment.

Leo arrived an hour later, having spent the night in the Below. He found Cassia still sitting at her desk, the scroll spread before her.

"When did you get this?"

"Sometime during the night. I don't know how they got in."

Leo read the scroll, his expression darkening. "Seven days. That gives us a week to find Ashford."

"Less, if they strike early."

Mira arrived moments later, carrying a laptop and a stack of books. She read the scroll over Leo's shoulder and let out a low whistle.

"First-Born. I've read about them in your archives." She set down her books. "They're extremists. They believe creatures are superior to humans and should rule openly."

"That's the simplified version," Cassia said. "The First-Born have existed in some form since the Accord was first signed. They've always been a minority, but they've grown bolder in recent decades. The older generation is dying off, and the younger creatures don't remember the wars that made the Accord necessary."

"And now they have a weapon."

"And now they have a weapon." Cassia stood and began pacing. "The timing is too perfect. The First-Born have been a fringe movement for centuries. They've never had the resources to mount a real challenge to the Accord. Suddenly they have access to a stolen artifact, modified Gorgon magic, and a strategy that threatens the entire integrated community."

"Someone is backing them," Mira said.

"Ashford. Using them as cover for his own agenda."

Leo folded the scroll and placed it back on the desk. "Then we need to meet the First-Born. Find out what they know about Ashford."

"Direct negotiation would be perceived as weakness," Cassia said.

"Not negotiation. Confrontation. If we can show the creature community that the First-Born are being manipulated by a human sorcerer, we might be able to split their support."

Mira frowned. "That's a risky strategy. They might not believe us."

"They don't have to believe us. They just have to have doubts. Enough doubts to slow them down while we find Ashford."

Cassia stopped pacing. "I can arrange a meeting. Through back channels. But it has to be tonight. Every hour we delay, the First-Born gain more support."

"Tonight, then." Leo turned to Mira. "I need you to focus on the genealogy. If Ashford is using Gorgon magic, he needs someone with Gorgon blood to power the Aegis. Find me a descendant."

Mira nodded. "I've already started. Medusa's bloodline is complex, but there are records. Three living descendants that I can trace."

"Then start crossing them off the list."

The team dispersed, each to their own task. Cassia began making calls through encrypted channels, reaching out to contacts she had cultivated over centuries. Mira returned to her books, diving into the tangled genealogy of Greek mythology's most famous monster.

Leo stood alone in Cassia's office, looking at the scroll, reading the ultimatum one more time. Seven days. In seven days, London's hidden world would either tear itself apart or unite against a common enemy.

He put the scroll in his pocket and walked out into the city, where the morning traffic hummed and the humans went about their lives, blissfully unaware of the storm gathering beneath their feet.

Chapter 10: The Nereid's Testimony

The nereid lived in a converted canal boat on Regent's Canal, near Camden Lock. She called herself Marina and worked as a massage therapist in a health spa. To her human clients, she was a friendly woman in her fifties with an unusual talent for deep tissue work. To the hidden world, she was a granddaughter of Nereus, an ancient sea-god whose daughters had danced in the waves since before humans learned to write.

Leo had arranged the meeting through the Night Porter, who had passed along a message through the Below's courier system. Marina had agreed to talk, but only on her own territory.

The canal boat was painted green and red, with flower boxes in the windows and a small deck where Marina sat feeding breadcrumbs to the ducks. She was a plain-faced woman with grey-streaked hair and the kind of hands that had spent decades working. She watched Leo approach with suspicion in her pale eyes.

"You're the investigator," she said. It was not a question.

"Leo Ashworth. I'm looking into Ione's death."

Marina's face tightened at the name. She threw the last of the breadcrumbs to the ducks and stood. "Inside. I don't want to talk about this where anyone can hear."

The interior of the boat was warm and cluttered, filled with seashells and driftwood and photographs of places that looked like they might be underwater. Marina poured tea from a kettle that had been simmering on the stove.

"How did you know Ione?" Leo asked.

"We were sisters. Not by blood, but by the water. We shared the same spring, once, before she chose to live on land full-time." Marina handed him a cup. "She was happy. She had a good life. A job she enjoyed, friends, a little flat in Aldgate. She told me once that she had never felt as free as she did walking through London."

"What happened in her final days?"

"She was scared. More scared than I had ever seen her." Marina sat down across from him. "She came to me three days before she died. Said someone was following her. Watching her from the shadows of the Tube stations."

"Did she describe who?"

"A man. Human, she thought. But she couldn't see his face clearly. He was always in the dark, always at the edge of her vision."

Leo made notes. "Did she mention anything else? Any specific phrase or symbol?"

Marina hesitated. "She said he was 'counting the veils.' I didn't know what she meant. I thought it was just fear making her speak in riddles."

The phrase struck Leo like a physical blow. The veils. The barriers between worlds.

"Did she say anything else about the veils?"

"Just that he was counting them. She kept repeating it, like it was important. 'He's counting the veils. He's almost done counting the veils.' I thought she meant the curtains, the walls, something physical. Now I wonder."

Leo set his tea down, his mind racing. "Thank you. This helps more than you know."

"Does it?" Marina's eyes were wet. "Will it bring her back?"

"No. But it might stop whoever did this from killing anyone else."

Marina nodded slowly. She walked with him to the door of the boat, and as he stepped onto the deck, she touched his arm.

"The man who killed Ione — he's not just hunting creatures. He's hunting something bigger. I can feel it in the water. The canals are restless. The river is holding its breath. Something terrible is coming."

Leo looked at the canal, dark and still in the afternoon light. The ducks had gone. The water was flat and motionless, as if the entire city was waiting.

"I'll find him," he said.

"Find him quickly. The water doesn't have much patience."

Leo walked back to the main road, the phrase echoing in his head. Counting the veils. He pulled out his phone and called Mira.

"Find anything on the genealogy?"

"Working on it. Three descendants, but one I can't track — she's off the grid completely. Why?"

"Because I just learned something. The killer is counting the veils. The barriers between worlds."

A pause on the line. "That changes things."

"I know. It's not just about the Aegis anymore. He's planning something bigger."

"How much bigger?"

Leo watched the canal, where the water had begun to ripple, though there was no wind.

"I think he's planning to open a door to Olympus."

The silence on the line stretched long enough that Leo checked to see if the call had dropped.

"That's impossible," Mira said finally. "The Olympian realm has been sealed since the fifth century."

"Someone forgot to tell our killer."

"I'll start researching. If he's trying to open an Olympian gate, there will be patterns. Ritual preparations. Something we can track."

"Do it. And keep me updated on the genealogy."

Leo hung up and stood by the canal, watching the ripples spread. Counting the veils. Ione had known what was coming. She had seen the killer's plan in fragments, and she had tried to warn someone.

But no one had listened until she was stone.

Chapter 11: The Museum Archives

The British Museum's archives occupied a warren of rooms beneath the main building, accessible only to staff with the highest security clearance. Mira had worked at the museum for six years without knowing that a third of these rooms existed.

Cassia led her through a series of locked doors, each requiring a different key or code. The air grew cooler and drier as they descended. The fluorescent lights gave way to older fixtures — gas lamps that burned with a steady blue flame.

"The museum was built on top of an older structure," Cassia explained. "A repository that the Accord established in the seventeenth century to house artifacts that were too dangerous to be known."

"Too dangerous for whom?"

"For everyone. Humans and creatures alike. Some artifacts cannot be destroyed — they can only be hidden."

They stopped before a door that was different from the others. It was iron, set into a stone archway, covered in inscriptions that Mira could not read. The language was older than Greek, older than Sumerian. Older than writing itself.

"This is the restricted vault," Cassia said. "The Aegis was kept here."

The door opened at Cassia's touch, and they entered a room the size of a small cathedral. Shelves stretched into the shadows, filled with objects from every civilization that had ever existed. Swords that glowed with faint light. Crowns that hummed with trapped energy. Scrolls sealed in crystal cases.

Mira stood in the center of the room, turning slowly, trying to absorb what she was seeing. "How much of this is real?"

"All of it. Every object in this room has genuine magical properties. The museum's public collection is mostly historical artifacts. This is the hidden collection."

"The Aegis was kept here?"

"It was. In this case." Cassia led her to a alcove where a pedestal stood empty. The velvet lining was undisturbed, but the air around it felt wrong — thin and cold, like a room that had been emptied too quickly.

Mira examined the pedestal. "The security?"

"Three layers. Magical wards, physical locks, and a monitoring system that alerts me to any breach. All three were bypassed without triggering a single alarm."

"Inside job."

"Almost certainly. The thief knew the protocols, the schedules, and the ward patterns. They had help from someone within the museum."

"Or someone who used to work here."

Cassia's face was unreadable. "Ashford was my student. He knew the museum's security systems intimately. If he stole the Aegis, he had decades to plan the theft."

Mira moved to a nearby shelf, scanning the artifacts. Her academic mind was struggling to reconcile what she was seeing with everything she had been taught. These objects were real. The magic was real. The hidden world was real.

"The Perseus myth cycle," she said, thinking aloud. "The Aegis was one of several artifacts associated with the hero. There's the helmet of invisibility, the winged sandals, the kibisis — the bag that could hold anything."

"The others were recovered by the Accord in the nineteenth century. They're in a different vault."

"But the Aegis was here, and now it's gone." Mira turned to face Cassia. "I need to see the records of the other artifacts. If Ashford is collecting them, there might be a pattern."

Cassia hesitated. "The other artifacts are classified even by Accord standards. Only the Keeper has access to their locations."

"And you're the Keeper."

"And I'm the Keeper." Cassia moved to a cabinet at the far end of the vault and withdrew a heavy leather-bound book. "This catalog lists every artifact in Accord custody, its current location, and its security status. I'm not supposed to share it with anyone."

"Then I won't tell anyone you showed me."

Cassia opened the book and began turning pages, her finger tracing the entries. Mira watched over her shoulder, reading the elegant script.

The helmet of Hades — secure, the Tower of London vault.
The sandals of Hermes — secure, the Louvre's hidden collection.
The kibisis — location unknown, missing since 1763.
The Aegis of Perseus — transferred to British Museum, 1952. Current status: STOLEN.

"And the security on these locations?"

"Comparable to what we had here. If Ashford could breach this vault, he could breach any of them."

Mira studied the catalog, looking for connections. "The helmet of Hades renders the wearer invisible. The sandals grant flight. The kibisis can contain anything, regardless of size. The Aegis reflects and redirects magic. If someone had all four —"

"They would be virtually unstoppable."

"And Ashford has at least one. Possibly more."

Cassia closed the catalog. "We need to check the other locations. If the helmet or sandals are missing, our situation is far worse than we thought."

"Can you do that without alerting the Accord?"

"I can make quiet inquiries. But it will take time."

Mira looked around the vault, at the thousands of artifacts that held powers the human world could not imagine. Somewhere out there, a man was using one of those powers to kill.

"Find out what's missing," she said. "I'll keep working on the genealogy. If Ashford needs Gorgon blood to power the Aegis, finding the descendant is our best chance of finding him."

They left the vault, locking the iron door behind them. As they climbed back toward the light, Mira's phone buzzed with a message from Leo.

"Meeting with First-Born arranged. Tonight. Below. Bring the catalog."

She showed the message to Cassia, who nodded grimly.

"Then we have work to do before sundown."

Chapter 12: The Collector's Gallery

The Attic Auction was held in a Victorian ballroom that had been transported piece by piece from a country house in Kent and reassembled three levels beneath Covent Garden. Chandeliers hung from a vaulted ceiling painted with clouds and cherubs. The walls were lined with crimson velvet. And the guests — a mix of creatures from every mythology — moved through the room with the practiced discretion of people who had been hiding for centuries.

Leo had not attended an auction in the Below since before his license was revoked. The feeling of being back was strange — like wearing a coat that no longer fit properly. He kept his hand in his pocket, touching the brass key the Night Porter had given him.

The auction had not yet begun. Guests stood in small groups, drinking wine from crystal glasses and examining the items displayed on velvet-draped tables. Leo moved through them, listening, watching.

A satyr in an expensive suit was arguing with a banshee about the provenance of a bronze mirror. A kitsune in traditional Japanese dress examined a scroll with predatory interest. A djinn hovered near the bar, his body flickering like a candle flame.

Leo found his target near the back of the room — a harpy named Voletta who dealt in stolen artifacts. She was a tall woman with sharp features and feathers woven into her hair, dressed in a gown that appeared to be made of crow's wings.

"Voletta."

She turned, her eyes narrowing. "Ashworth. I heard you were back."

"I'm looking for information."

"Information costs. You know that."

Leo reached into his pocket and withdrew a small pouch — silver coins, minted in the Below, each worth a favor in the hidden economy. He set it on the table beside her.

Voletta glanced at the pouch but did not touch it. "What do you want to know?"

"The Aegis of Perseus. It was sold through this auction house six months ago. I need to know who bought it."

Voletta's expression flickered — surprise, or fear, or both. "That's not the kind of information I trade in."

"It's the only kind I'm interested in."

"The buyer was anonymous. Paid in Olympian gold — the real stuff, not the diluted coins that pass through the Below. No name, no face, just a sealed bid that was opened and accepted."

"Who was the seller?"

"A harpy. Dead now. Disappeared a week after the sale."

"Where did she get the Aegis?"

Voletta leaned closer, her voice dropping. "She was a curator at the British Museum. Took it from the locked vaults, thinking she could sell it and retire to a private island in the Aegean. She didn't know what she was dealing with."

"The buyer tracked her down."

"The buyer didn't have to. The Aegis has a way of protecting itself. It killed her the moment she tried to sell it. Turned her to stone in her own flat in Canary Wharf. The Accord found her body and covered it up."

Leo's mind raced. A harpy curator, killed by the artifact she was trying to sell. That meant the Aegis had defenses beyond the security of the vault.

"Do you know where the buyer came from? Any accent, any distinguishing features?"

"Human, the auction master said. Paid in ancient currency. Dressed well. Spoke like someone who had been educated at the best schools." Voletta paused. "There was one thing. He had a scar on his hand — a burn, shaped like a circle with three lines through it."

Leo felt the confirmation like a punch to the chest. The symbol of the First-Born.

"Thank you, Voletta."

"You paid for it. We're even." She picked up the pouch and slipped it into a fold of her gown. "But I'll give you some advice for free. Whatever you're chasing — it's bigger than you think. The Aegis is just the beginning. The buyer was collecting more than one artifact."

"What else?"

"I don't know. But I heard whispers. Helmet. Sandals. Bag. He's putting together the full Perseus set."

Leo thanked her again and moved away, his mind churning. The full set. The helmet of Hades for invisibility. The sandals of Hermes for flight. The kibisis for containment. And the Aegis for offense.

If Ashford had all four, he would be capable of anything.

He made his way to the exit, passing through the crowd of creatures who had no idea that their world was about to change forever. Behind him, the auction began — the auctioneer's voice rising over the murmur of the crowd, the bidding starting at prices that would have bought houses in the human world.

Leo stepped through the door and into the darkness of the Below, the brass key cold in his hand.

He had confirmed the buyer. Now he needed to find the man.

Chapter 13: The Barghest's Warning

The streets of the Below were empty at this hour. The shops were closed, their windows dark. The gas lamps flickered in their iron brackets, casting pools of yellow light on the cobblestones.

Leo walked quickly, his footsteps echoing in the narrow alleyways. The Attic Auction had ended an hour ago, and he had stayed to gather as much information as possible. Now he was paying the price for lingering — the Below at night was dangerous even for those who knew its ways.

He sensed the barghest before he saw it. The air grew cold. The gas lamps dimmed. And from the shadows ahead, a low growl rumbled like distant thunder.

Leo stopped. He had faced a barghest before, years ago, and still carried the scars. They were creatures of the old world — spectral black dogs the size of horses, with eyes like burning coals and teeth that could tear through steel.

This one emerged from the alley to his left, its massive body blocking his path. Its fur was darkness made solid, absorbing the light around it. Its eyes fixed on Leo with an intelligence that was older and more malevolent than any ordinary animal.

"Someone sent you," Leo said, keeping his voice steady.

The barghest did not respond. It simply stood, watching, its growl a constant rumble.

Leo's hand moved slowly toward his coat pocket, where he kept a silver knife blessed in three different traditions. He had never needed to use it. He hoped tonight would not be the first time.

"You're a warning, aren't you? A message. Whoever sent you wants me to know I'm being watched."

The barghest took a step forward. Its claws scraped against the cobblestones. Leo could smell it now — the scent of wet earth and old graves.

"I'm not turning back."

The barghest lunged.

Leo threw himself to the side, rolling across the cobblestones, feeling the wind of the creature's passage. He came up with the silver knife in his hand, but the barghest had already turned, circling, preparing for another attack.

A voice cut through the darkness: "Asto!"

The barghest froze. Its ears flattened against its skull, and it let out a whine that was almost pathetic.

Cassia stepped out of the shadows, her hand extended, a word of power still hanging in the air. She spoke another word, and the barghest backed away, its tail between its legs.

"A barghest," Leo said, getting to his feet. "I had it under control."

"You were about to get yourself killed."

"I've survived worse."

Cassia's expression was unreadable. "The barghest was a message. The First-Born know you're investigating. They're testing you, seeing how far you'll push."

"How did you know I was here?"

"I've been following you since the auction. Making sure you made it out." She turned to the barghest, which was now sitting on its haunches like a chastened dog. "Go. Tell your master that Leo Ashworth is not so easily frightened."

The barghest vanished into the shadows, dissolving like smoke.

"I could have handled it," Leo said.

"Of course you could have. But handling it would have cost you time and energy we don't have." Cassia began walking, and Leo fell into step beside her. "I have news."

"Good or bad?"

"Both. The First-Born have agreed to meet. Tomorrow night. Neutral ground."

"Where?"

"The Threshold. A pub that exists between the Below and the surface. Neither fully in the hidden world nor fully in the human one."

"I know it. Used to go there years ago."

"It's changed. Like everything else." Cassia paused at the entrance to the tunnel that led back to the human world. "The First-Born are sending an envoy. A minotaur named Asterion. He's their most vocal leader — charismatic, intelligent, and dangerous."

"You've met him?"

"Several times. He was a moderate once. The First-Born radicalized him after his brother was killed in a hunting accident. Human hunters, mistaking him for a wild animal."

Leo felt the weight of the story. "That's how extremists are made. Grief, weaponized."

"Exactly. And that's why you need to be careful. Asterion believes he's fighting for justice. You can't reason someone out of a position they didn't reason themselves into."

They emerged from the tunnel into the empty loading dock of a Covent Garden warehouse. The human world was quiet, the streets wet with recent rain.

"Tomorrow night, then," Leo said.

"Tomorrow night. I'll pick you up at eight."

Cassia disappeared into the darkness, leaving Leo alone in the loading dock. He stood there for a long moment, listening to the distant sound of traffic, the hum of a city that had no idea what was lurking beneath its streets.

The barghest's growl still echoed in his ears. The First-Born knew his face now. They knew he was coming.

He wondered how many more warnings he would get before they stopped warning and started killing.

Chapter 14: The Second Veil

The third petrification happened at noon on a Thursday, in full view of hundreds of witnesses.

The victim was a harpy named Aello, a courier who worked for a magical delivery service that operated between the Below and the human world. She had been flying over the Thames, taking the usual route that kept her above the clouds but below radar, when something struck her mid-flight.

Witnesses on the ground reported seeing a flash of light in the sky, followed by a dark object falling toward the river. Some thought it was a meteor. Others thought it was a bird strike. No one realized, until the object hit the water, that it was a body.

Aello's stone corpse sank into the Thames, where it remained for three hours before the Accord's recovery team could retrieve it. By that time, the news had spread through every channel in the hidden world. A creature, petrified in mid-flight, in broad daylight, over one of the busiest rivers in the world.

Leo received the news via a coded message from Cassia. He was in his office, going through Edgar's files, when his phone buzzed with a single word: "Thames."

He turned on the small television he kept in the corner of his office. The BBC was reporting a "suspicious incident" on the river — a body had been recovered, but the police were not releasing details. The news anchor's tone suggested she knew more than she was saying.

Mira called five minutes later.

"I saw the news. Was it —"

"A harpy. Mid-flight. The killer is getting bolder."

"This changes things. Public incidents mean the Accord can't contain the story. The creature community will see this as an escalation."

"That's the point." Leo watched the television, where a reporter was now standing on the banks of the Thames, describing the recovery operation. "Ashford wants to create panic. Panic makes creatures turn to the First-Born for protection."

"And the First-Born will tell them the only protection is renouncing the human world entirely."

"Exactly."

There was a pause on the line. Then Mira spoke, her voice lower. "I found something. In the genealogical records. The third Gorgon descendant — Medea Gorgon — she's living in London. Brixton."

"How do you know?"

"Council tax records. She's registered as a resident at a flat on Acre Lane. But she's not answering any of my inquiries."

"She's probably scared. If Ashford is hunting Gorgon descendants, she's a target."

"Or she's working with him."

"That too." Leo grabbed his coat. "I'm going to Brixton. Can you send me the address?"

"I'll text it. But Leo — be careful. If she is working with Ashford, she's more dangerous than any creature we've faced."

Leo hung up and headed for the door. The address came through as he was locking up: 47 Acre Lane, flat 3B.

He was halfway to Brixton when he realized something. The harpy had been struck over the Thames, in broad daylight. Ashford wasn't just escalating — he was sending a message to the Accord. You can't protect them. You can't even see us coming.

The concrete chorus of London hummed around him — the traffic, the pedestrians, the endless noise of eight million people living their lives. Somewhere among them walked a sorcerer with a stolen artifact, turning creatures to stone.

Leo pressed harder on the accelerator.

He found the flat on Acre Lane three hours later, after circling the block twice and verifying that he wasn't being followed. It was a Victorian conversion, the kind of building that had been subdivided into flats decades ago and had been slowly declining ever since.

The door to flat 3B was at the end of a dim corridor on the third floor. Leo knocked. No answer. He knocked again, harder.

"I know you're in there, Ms. Gorgon. I'm not here to hurt you. I need to talk."

Silence. Then, a voice from behind the door, thin and frightened: "Who sent you?"

"No one sent me. I'm investigating the petrifications. I think you might know something."

"I don't know anything. Go away."

"You're a Gorgon descendant. The killer needs Gorgon blood to power the Aegis of Perseus. He's used it three times. He'll use it again. And when he does, he might come for you."

A long pause. Then the sound of locks being turned. The door opened a crack, revealing a sliver of a face — dark eyes, dark hair, skin that seemed to shimmer faintly even in the dim light.

"You're the disgraced investigator," Medea Gorgon said. "The one who let the lamia die."

Leo flinched. "That was three years ago."

"And now you're back. Trying to save people again." She opened the door wider, revealing a small flat filled with books and plants and the lingering scent of incense. "You'd better come in. But if you try anything, I'll turn you to stone myself."

Leo stepped inside. Behind him, the door clicked shut, and the locks turned again.

The hunt for Medea Gorgon had begun.

Chapter 15: The Faction Divided

The creature council convened in a chamber beneath the Royal Exchange, a circular room carved from living rock and lit by torches that burned with cold blue flame. Representatives from every major species sat in stone seats arranged in a spiral, their faces grim.

Cassia had called the emergency session. As Keeper, she had the authority to convene the council without the usual thirty-day notice. The petrification of Aello the harpy had made the necessity clear.

Leo and Mira waited in an antechamber, listening to the muffled voices of the council through the thick stone walls. They had not been invited to the session — no humans were allowed in the council chamber — but Cassia had promised to report back.

"It's taking too long," Mira said, pacing.

"They're arguing. That's what councils do."

"They should be uniting. There's a killer on the loose."

"There's a killer on the loose, but there's also a faction of extremists who see the killer as a hero. The moderate creatures are caught in the middle."

Mira stopped pacing. "Do you think the council will side with the First-Born?"

"I think they'll try to appease them. That's what moderates always do. They think if they give the extremists enough concessions, they'll stop pushing. But extremists don't stop pushing until they've won completely."

The chamber door opened, and Cassia emerged. Her face was drawn, her composure strained.

"They're divided," she said. "A third want to surrender to the First-Born's demands. A third want to call for human military intervention. And a third want to find a third path."

"Which third are you?"

"The third path. But I'm losing ground." She sat down heavily on a stone bench. "The moderates are scared. The First-Born are gaining support with every petrification. If we don't stop Ashford soon, the council will vote to surrender."

"When?"

"Three days. They've given us three days to present a viable plan. If we can't, they'll accept the First-Born's demands."

Leo did the math. Three days to find Ashford, stop the killings, and prove to the council that the First-Born were being manipulated by a human sorcerer.

"We can do this," he said.

"Can we?" Mira's voice was skeptical. "We have a suspect with no known location, an artifact that can turn people to stone, and a hidden world that's one step away from civil war."

"We have more than that." Leo pulled out his phone and opened the notes he had been compiling. "We know Ashford is a human sorcerer who was trained by Cassia. We know he stole the Aegis and is using it with Gorgon blood. We know he's collecting the full Perseus arsenal. And we know he's working with — or manipulating — the First-Born."

"That's a lot of knowledge. None of it tells us where he is."

"No. But it tells us where he's going." Leo laid his phone on the bench, showing a map of London he had marked with every known ley line intersection. "Counting the veils. Ione's last words. He's not just opening a gate to Olympus. He's finding the thinnest point between worlds. The place where the veils are weakest."

Cassia stared at the map. "Hyde Park. The Serpentine. There's an old gate beneath the lake."

"How do you know?"

"Because Ashford asked me about it, years ago, when he was my student. He was fascinated by the Olympian gates. I told him they were sealed and would never open again."

"You lied to him."

"I told him what the Accord wanted me to tell him. But the gate beneath the Serpentine was never truly sealed. It was locked, but locks can be picked."

Mira leaned over the map. "If Ashford intends to open the gate, he needs to do it at a specific time. The alignment of the ley lines, the phase of the moon —"

"The full moon," Leo said. "Seven days from the first petrification."

"That's tomorrow."

"No. The first petrification was six days ago. The full moon is tomorrow night."

The three of them looked at each other. The timeline had just collapsed.

"We need to find him before tomorrow night," Cassia said.

"Then we'd better get started."

Leo folded the map and stood. The council was still debating inside the chamber, oblivious to the fact that their deadline was a fiction. Ashford didn't care about their negotiations. He was following his own timetable.

And that timetable ended tomorrow night.

Chapter 16: Council of Ashes

Asterion, the minotaur envoy of the First-Born, arrived at the Threshold pub at precisely nine o'clock. He was massive — seven feet tall, with shoulders that barely fit through the doorframe, and horns that swept back from his temples like twin crescents. His eyes were the color of old copper, and they fixed on Leo with undisguised hostility.

The pub was a narrow room with low ceilings and a fireplace that burned despite the summer heat. The clientele was mixed — creatures who had chosen to live on the surface, humans who had stumbled into the hidden world and chosen to stay, and a few individuals who defied easy categorization.

Leo sat at a corner table, nursing a pint he had no intention of finishing. Cassia sat beside him, her posture relaxed but her eyes watchful.

Asterion crossed the room, his hooves leaving dents in the wooden floor. He did not sit.

"Ashworth. The Keeper." His voice was deep, resonant, the voice of someone used to being heard. "I expected more guards."

"This isn't an arrest," Leo said. "It's a conversation."

"Conversations are for equals. We are not equals."

"Then why did you come?"

Asterion's nostrils flared. "Because I want to see the face of the man who thinks he can stop what's coming."

Leo met his gaze. "I'm not trying to stop anything. I'm trying to understand it."

"Understand this." Asterion leaned forward, placing his massive hands on the table. "The Accord is a cage. It was built by creatures who were afraid of humans, and it has kept us in hiding for two thousand years. The First-Born will tear it down."

"By killing integrated creatures?"

"By removing those who have betrayed their own kind. Ione chose to live as a human. Helena chose to live as a human. Aello chose to serve humans. They made their choice, and they faced the consequences."

Leo kept his voice level. "They were innocent. They were living their lives."

"They were collaborating with the enemy." Asterion straightened. "You don't understand because you're human. You've never had to hide what you are. You've never had to pretend to be something you're not, just to survive."

"You're right. I don't understand. But I know that killing is not the answer."

"What do you know about answers?" Asterion's voice rose, drawing glances from the other patrons. "You failed the last creature who trusted you. You let her die. And now you think you can lecture me about justice?"

Leo felt the old wound twist. He did not look away.

"I failed her. I'll carry that for the rest of my life. But that doesn't make me wrong about this."

Asterion stared at him for a long moment. Then, unexpectedly, he sat down.

"You have courage," he said. "Stupid courage, but courage nonetheless. What do you want from me?"

"Information. The First-Born are claiming responsibility for the petrifications. But I don't think you're the ones doing the killing."

Asterion's expression flickered. "What makes you say that?"

"Because the killer is a human sorcerer named Lord Ashford. He's using the Aegis of Perseus, which he stole from the British Museum. He needs Gorgon blood to power it. And he's using the First-Born as cover for his own agenda."

The silence stretched. Asterion's face was unreadable.

"You're lying."

"I'm not. Ashford was trained by the Keeper. He knows the Accord's secrets. He's been manipulating the First-Born into providing cover for his attacks while he pursues his own goal."

"And what goal is that?"

"Opening a gate to Olympus. Capturing a god. Using its power to make the Aegis unstoppable."

Asterion's copper eyes narrowed. "That's impossible. The Olympian gates have been sealed for —"

"I know. Everyone keeps telling me that. But Ashford has been counting the veils. He's found the thinnest point. Tomorrow night, when the moon is full, he'll open the gate beneath the Serpentine."

Asterion was silent for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was different — less certain, more contemplative.

"If what you say is true — if Ashford is using us —"

"Then the First-Born are not the villains of this story. You're the pawns."

Asterion's fist clenched on the table. "I need proof."

"I'll get you proof." Leo stood. "But when I do, I need you to call off the ultimatum. Give the council time to deal with the real threat."

"And if you can't get proof?"

"Then I'll be dead, and it won't matter anyway."

Asterion stared at him for a long moment. Then he nodded slowly.

"Forty-eight hours. If you can prove Ashford is behind this, I'll call off the ultimatum. But if you fail —"

"I won't fail."

Leo turned and walked out of the pub, Cassia following close behind. The night air was cold, and the streets were empty.

"That was either very brave or very stupid," Cassia said.

"Probably both."

"He'll hold you to the deadline."

"I know."

They walked in silence through the darkened streets, the weight of the next forty-eight hours pressing down on them both.

Chapter 17: The Seven-Day Reckoning

Dawn broke grey and wet over London. Leo had not slept. He sat in his office, surrounded by maps and photographs and notes, trying to find a pattern that would lead him to Ashford before nightfall.

The full moon would rise at 9:47 PM. He had less than fourteen hours.

Mira arrived at seven, carrying coffee and a tablet loaded with research. Her eyes were red-rimmed from lack of sleep.

"I found something," she said, setting the tablet on his desk. "The Gorgon genealogy. Medea Gorgon is the key."

Leo looked at the screen. It showed a family tree stretching back centuries, from Medusa herself to the present day. Medea's name was at the bottom, attached to a long line of descendants.

"She's the only one in London," Mira continued. "The other two descendants are in New York and Tokyo. Ashford needs her specifically."

"Why her?"

"Because she's the only one with pure enough blood. The other two have diluted lines — too many intermarriages with humans. Medea's mother was a full Gorgon. Her grandmother was a full Gorgon. The bloodline is almost intact."

Leo studied the screen. "If Ashford needs her blood to power the Aegis, he would have approached her already."

"He did. According to my research, she's been in hiding for the past three months. She changed her address, her phone number, her name."

"Which means she's running from him. Which means she might be willing to help us."

"Or she might be too terrified to trust anyone."

Leo grabbed his coat. "Then I'll have to convince her."

He found Medea Gorgon exactly where he had left her — in her flat on Acre Lane, surrounded by books and incense and the lingering fear of a hunted creature.

She opened the door at his knock, her expression wary. "I told you everything I know."

"You didn't tell me Ashford already contacted you."

Medea's face went pale. "How do you know about that?"

"Because you've been in hiding for three months. Because you changed your identity. Because you're scared."

"I'm scared because a sorcerer wants to use my blood to power a weapon of mass destruction. That seems reasonable."

"It is reasonable. But hiding won't save you. He'll find you, eventually. The only way to stop him is to help us find him first."

Medea stared at him. "Help you. The disgraced investigator who let a creature die."

"You keep bringing that up."

"Because it's the only thing I know about you. That and the fact that you're here, asking me to trust you."

Leo met her eyes. "I made a mistake. I trusted someone I shouldn't have, and someone died because of it. I've spent three years trying to forget that. But I can't. And I can't make it right. All I can do is try not to make the same mistake again."

Medea was silent for a long moment. Then she stepped aside, opening the door wider.

"Come in. But if you betray me —"

"You'll turn me to stone. I know."

He stepped inside, and the door closed behind him.

They talked for two hours. Medea told him everything: how Ashford had approached her six months ago, offering her wealth and protection in exchange for regular blood donations. How she had refused, and how he had threatened her. How she had gone into hiding, moving from flat to flat, never staying in one place for more than a few weeks.

"Why didn't you go to the Accord?" Leo asked.

"Because the Accord is full of creatures who would sell me out for the right price. Ashford has money. He has influence. The Accord is corrupt."

"The Keeper isn't corrupt."

"Cassia Verney?" Medea's lip curled. "She trained Ashford. She's the reason he knows what he knows."

"She's also the reason he hasn't succeeded yet. She's on our side."

Medea shook her head. "You trust too easily."

"I trust carefully. There's a difference."

She looked at him with something approaching respect. "What do you need from me?"

"Your blood. Just enough to prove to the council that Ashford has been using Gorgon magic. If we can show them the signature —"

"You want me to be bait."

"I want you to be evidence."

Medea considered this. Then she went to her kitchen, opened a drawer, and pulled out a small glass vial.

"If I give you this, you'll leave me alone?"

"If I survive tonight, I'll never bother you again."

She filled the vial with a few drops of blood from a pinprick on her finger. The blood was dark, almost black, and it shimmered with the same iridescence Leo had seen in the stone fragments.

"If Ashford finds out I helped you —"

"He won't."

Leo took the vial and tucked it into his coat pocket. "Thank you, Medea. I won't forget this."

"You'd better not."

He left the flat and walked back to his office, the vial warm against his chest. The clock was ticking. He had twelve hours until moonrise.

Chapter 18: The Gorgon Genealogy

Mira had transformed Cassia's office into a war room. Maps covered the walls, marked with pins and string. Genealogical charts lay spread across every surface. The fragments of petrified victims sat in labeled containers on a side table.

She had not slept in thirty-six hours. Her eyes burned, and her hands trembled from too much coffee, but she could not stop. The patterns were forming, connections she had never expected to find in her academic career.

The Gorgon genealogy was a masterpiece of survival. Medusa's bloodline had persisted through two thousand years of war, persecution, and assimilation. The descendants had married into human families, diluted their blood, changed their names, and scattered across the globe. But the line had never broken.

Three living descendants. One in London. One in New York. One in Tokyo. All women. All carrying the same genetic marker that made their blood compatible with the Aegis.

Mira studied the chart she had compiled. Medea Gorgon was the London descendant — the most direct line, the purest blood. The New York descendant, a woman named Callisto, was fifty-three and worked as a librarian. The Tokyo descendant, a young woman named Yuki, was a student at the University of Tokyo.

Ashford needed Medea. But Medea had refused him. So where was he getting the Gorgon blood to power the Aegis?

She checked her notes again. Each petrification had required a fresh application of blood — the magic consumed it. Ashford had used the Aegis four times. If each use required a significant amount of blood, and if Medea had refused to supply him —

He must have another source.

Mira pulled out her phone and called Leo.

"Ashford has another Gorgon," she said without preamble. "Medea isn't his only source."

"What do you mean?"

"There have been four petrifications. Each requires fresh blood. Medea refused him six months ago. He would have run out of stored blood after the first or second attack."

A pause. "You're saying there's a fourth descendant."

"I'm saying there's a donor. Someone with enough Gorgon blood to power the Aegis multiple times. Someone who is cooperating with Ashford voluntarily."

"Or someone who is being forced."

Mira looked at her chart. "There's a gap in the genealogy. A branch that goes cold in the eighteenth century. I assumed the line died out, but what if it didn't? What if it went underground?"

"Find it. If there's another descendant Ashford is using, we need to know who they are."

Mira hung up and turned back to her research. The missing branch. She had dismissed it as a dead end, but now she wondered. The records from the eighteenth century were spotty at best — the Accord had been in chaos during that period, and many documents had been lost.

She pulled out the oldest volume in her stack — a handwritten genealogy from 1743, compiled by an Accord archivist who had interviewed every living Gorgon descendant in Europe. The handwriting was cramped, the ink faded, but the information was there.

She found the missing branch. A daughter born in 1721, recorded as "stillborn" in the official records. But the archivist had noted something strange — the child's body had disappeared before burial. The parents had claimed it was taken by "things that should not be."

Mira's pulse quickened. Stillborn. Body stolen. Or not stillborn at all.

She traced the branch forward, using census records, marriage registrations, and property deeds. The trail was faint, but it was there. A family that moved frequently, changed names often, and never stayed in one place for more than a generation.

Leadenhall Market. The trail ended at Leadenhall Market, where a shop had been in continuous operation since 1760. The current owner was listed as a man named Marcus Verney.

Verney.

The same surname Cassia had adopted.

Mira stared at the name. She picked up her phone again.

"Cassia. We need to talk."

Chapter 19: Stone and Dust

DCI Tom Fletcher had been suspended. The official reason was "failure to follow proper procedure in a sensitive investigation." The real reason was that he had been asking too many questions about the petrifications, and his superiors had decided he was a liability.

He sat in his small flat in Pimlico, staring at the case files he had smuggled out of the station. The photographs of the victims stared back at him — grey faces frozen in terror, bodies that had once been alive and now were nothing but stone.

His phone buzzed. Leo Ashworth.

"Where are you?" Leo asked.

"Suspended. At home. Going through files."

"I need your help. Off the books."

Fletcher almost laughed. "I'm already off the books. What do you need?"

"A Gorgon named Stheno. She was arrested three days ago for possession of illegal magical artifacts. I think she's connected to Ashford."

Fletcher pulled up the police database on his personal laptop. "Stheno Gorgon. Arrested at a warehouse in Deptford. Currently being held at a facility in —" He paused. "This is classified."

"The Accord's prison. It's not on any official map."

"Then how do I find it?"

"I'll send you the coordinates. But Fletcher — the Accord's prison is run by creatures. Human law doesn't apply there. If you go in, you're on your own."

Fletcher looked at the photographs on his table. He thought about the victims — creatures who had been murdered while trying to live peaceful lives. He thought about the system that had failed them.

"Send me the coordinates."

The prison was hidden beneath a decommissioned Tube station in South London — a maze of tunnels and cells that had been carved out of the earth centuries ago. Fletcher arrived at midnight, wearing civilian clothes and carrying a taser that would be useless against most of the inmates.

The guard at the entrance was a troll — twelve feet of muscle and bad temper, with a face that looked like it had been carved from granite.

"Human," the troll said, his voice like grinding stones. "You're not supposed to be here."

"I'm here to see Stheno Gorgon."

"Visiting hours are over."

"I'm not visiting. I'm investigating."

The troll stared at him. Fletcher stared back. He had been a police officer for twenty-two years. He had faced down armed suspects, rioters, and one very angry badger. He was not going to be intimidated by a rock monster.

"Call the warden," Fletcher said. "Tell him DCI Fletcher is here on Accord business."

The troll grunted and picked up a phone that looked like it had been installed in the 1950s. He spoke a few words in a language Fletcher didn't recognize, then hung up.

"The warden will see you. But if you cause trouble —"

"I won't."

The troll led him through a series of tunnels, past cells that held creatures Fletcher had only read about in mythology books. A cyclops. A harpy. Something that might have been a kelpie, though it was hard to tell in the dim light.

Stheno Gorgon was in a cell at the end of the corridor. She was a woman in her sixties, with silver hair and kind eyes. There was nothing about her that suggested the monstrous Gorgons of legend — just a tired old woman in a prison jumpsuit.

"Another visitor," she said when Fletcher appeared at her cell door. "The last one was a sorcerer. Brought me chocolates. Said he was my cousin."

"Lord Ashford?"

"I don't know his name. He had money, that's for sure. And he wanted me to do something for him."

"What?"

"Give blood. Said it was for a medical research project. Offered me ten thousand pounds."

"And you refused?"

"I'm not stupid. You don't give blood to strangers who show up at your prison cell with expensive chocolates."

Fletcher studied her. "Ashford framed you, didn't he? The artifacts in your warehouse — you didn't put them there."

Stheno's eyes widened. "How did you know?"

"Because you're not a criminal. You're a scapegoat. Ashford needed someone to take the fall while he continued his work."

"That son of a bitch." Stheno's voice was bitter. "He came to me first, before the other one. I told him no. So he framed me and found someone more willing."

"The other one. Euryale?"

Stheno nodded. "My sister. Euryale always was the believer. She bought into the First-Born ideology years ago. If Ashford needed Gorgon blood, he went to her."

Fletcher's mind raced. If Euryale was willingly supplying blood to Ashford, she was as responsible for the deaths as he was.

"Where can I find Euryale?"

"I don't know. We haven't spoken in years. But if she's working with Ashford, she'll be near him. She's always been loyal to whoever paid her attention."

Fletcher stood. "Thank you, Stheno. I'll do what I can to get you out."

"Don't bother. I'm safer in here than out there. At least the trolls don't want my blood."

Fletcher left the prison and emerged into the night air. He had a name — Euryale. Another Gorgon descendant. Willing to cooperate with Ashford.

He called Leo.

"Stheno's innocent. The real donor is her sister, Euryale. She's a First-Born sympathizer."

"Then we have two targets. Ashford and Euryale."

"And we have less than twelve hours."

Fletcher looked up at the sky. The clouds were clearing, and behind them, he could see the faint outline of the moon — already full, already rising.

"Then we'd better move fast."

Chapter 20: The Alibi of Bones

Leo met Fletcher at a cafe near Paddington Station at one in the morning. The place was nearly empty — a few insomniacs nursing coffees, a taxi driver reading a newspaper. They took a booth in the corner, away from the windows.

"Stheno was framed," Fletcher said, sliding into the seat. "Ashford planted the artifacts and tipped off the Accord. He needed her out of the way so she couldn't interfere with his work."

"Did she know where Ashford is operating?"

"No. But she gave me a lead — her sister Euryale. She's the one supplying blood to Ashford."

Leo nodded slowly. "Mira found a gap in the genealogy. A missing branch that went underground in the eighteenth century. Euryale might be from that line."

"Or she might be Medea's sister under a different name."

"Medea said she had no sisters."

"That we know of." Fletcher signaled the waitress for coffee. "The hidden world has a lot of secrets. Families hide branches, change names, disappear for generations. Euryale could be Medea's aunt, her cousin, her half-sister from a line that was written out of the official records."

Leo considered this. "We need to find Euryale before Ashford does. If she's the one powering the Aegis, she's the key to stopping the attacks."

"And if Ashford has her under guard?"

"Then we free her."

Fletcher's coffee arrived. He added sugar, stirred it, and looked at Leo across the table. "You know this is insane, right? We're two humans going up against a sorcerer with a mythical weapon and an army of extremist creatures."

"I know."

"And you're okay with that?"

"No. But I'm more okay with it than I am with letting thousands of innocent creatures die."

Fletcher nodded slowly. "That's what I thought you'd say."

He reached into his coat and pulled out a heavy revolver, sliding it across the table. "This is a Webley Mark VI. Standard British Army issue, 1915. But it's been modified."

Leo picked up the revolver. It was heavier than it looked, and the grip was engraved with symbols he recognized — warding sigils, protection marks.

"It's loaded with silver rounds blessed by a priest of the old faith," Fletcher said. "Won't stop everything, but it'll slow most things down."

"You carry this around?"

"I keep it for emergencies." Fletcher took a sip of his coffee. "This qualifies."

Leo tucked the revolver into his coat. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Just make sure you bring it back."

They finished their coffee in silence, then went their separate ways — Fletcher to dig up records on Euryale's known associates, Leo to meet Cassia at the museum.

The streets were empty as Leo walked through the predawn darkness. The city was holding its breath, waiting for the full moon to rise.

He thought about the gun in his coat, the vial of Medea's blood in his pocket, the weight of three years of guilt pressing down on his shoulders. He thought about Edgar, dead in his shop. About Ione, frozen in terror. About all the creatures who had trusted him once and learned not to.

He was not the same investigator he had been three years ago. He was older, slower, more careful. But the fire was still there, buried beneath the layers of cynicism and regret.

And tonight, he would need every spark of it.

Chapter 21: Blood and Glass

The fourth petrification happened in a glassblowing studio in Hackney Wick, at 3 AM on the morning of the full moon.

Leo received the news from Cassia as he was preparing to leave for the museum. The victim was a minor fae named Tamsin, a glass artist who had been creating sculptures for the hidden world for decades. She had been close to revealing Ashford's plans to the council — close enough that she had become a target.

The studio was still warm when Leo arrived. The furnaces glowed in the darkness, casting long shadows across the room. Tamsin's body stood in the center of the studio, her arms raised as if she had been reaching for something, her face frozen in a scream that would never be heard.

Cassia was already there, kneeling beside the body, her face pale in the glow of the furnaces.

"Fresh," she said. "Less than an hour ago."

"The killer is accelerating."

"Or sending a message. Tamsin was supposed to testify before the council today. She had evidence of Ashford's connection to the First-Born."

"Evidence that's gone now."

Cassia stood. "Not entirely. Tamsin was meticulous. She kept copies of everything. If we can find her archives —"

They searched the studio, moving through the half-finished sculptures and the tools of the glassmaker's trade. Mira arrived twenty minutes later, still wearing yesterday's clothes, her eyes wild with exhausted energy.

"I found the missing branch," she said, breathless. "Euryale Gorgon. She's not a descendant of the main line. She's from the hidden branch — the one that went underground in the eighteenth century. She's been living in London under a false identity for thirty years."

"Where?"

"Highgate. She runs a herbal shop on the high street. But she's not there anymore. I checked the property records — the shop has been closed for three months."

"She's with Ashford."

"Or Ashford has her."

Leo looked at the body of the fae, frozen in eternal terror. Tamsin had known something important, and she had died for it.

"Keep looking," he said. "There has to be something in this studio that tells us where Ashford is."

They searched through the night, sifting through papers and files, looking for anything that might lead them to the sorcerer's lair. It was Mira who found it — a sketchbook tucked behind a shelf of finished pieces.

The sketchbook was filled with drawings of the Serpentine — detailed maps of the lake, with annotations about water depth, underground tunnels, and the location of the Olympian gate. The final page showed a complex diagram of interlocking circles, with a date written in red ink.

Today's date.

"This is the ritual," Mira said. "He's going to open the gate tonight, at moonrise. The diagram shows the exact location — beneath the island in the middle of the Serpentine."

Leo studied the diagram. "Can we get there?"

"There's a maintenance tunnel that runs under the lake. It's used for the pump system. If we enter from the north side —"

"Then that's our way in."

Cassia looked at the sketchbook, her expression grim. "Ashford will have guards. The First-Born might be there. And he'll have the Aegis."

"I know."

"We could die tonight."

"I know."

Leo closed the sketchbook and tucked it into his coat. "But if we don't stop him, a lot more people are going to die. Creatures. Humans. The line between the worlds will dissolve, and there'll be nothing but chaos."

Cassia nodded slowly. "Then I suppose we'd better not fail."

They left the studio as dawn broke over London, the first light of the full moon day casting long shadows across the city. The countdown had begun.

Chapter 22: The Mentor's Confession

Before the final assault, Leo needed to know the truth about Edgar Pillsworth's past. The old taxidermist had been more than a mentor — he had been a link to a world Leo had never fully understood.

Edgar's funeral was held at a small crematorium in East London, attended by a handful of humans who had known him as a taxidermist and a handful of creatures who had known him as something more. Leo stood at the back, watching the service through a haze of grief and guilt.

Afterward, he approached Edgar's executor — a solicitor named Mr. Grimsby who handled the affairs of many creature-adjacent humans. Grimsby was a thin man with a mournful face and the air of someone who had seen too many secrets.

"Mr. Ashworth. I was told to expect you."

"Edgar left something for me?"

"He left several things. But one specifically for you." Grimsby handed him a sealed envelope. "He said you would know when to open it."

Leo took the envelope. "Did he say anything else?"

"Only that he was sorry he couldn't tell you in person. And that the truth about his past would not change what you meant to him."

Leo opened the envelope and pulled out a letter, handwritten in Edgar's cramped script.

"Leo — if you're reading this, I'm dead. I'm sorry I couldn't tell you this face to face, but I was a coward, and I thought I had more time.

I was a member of the First-Born. Forty years ago, when the movement was young and I was foolish, I believed in their cause. I thought creatures deserved to rule openly, that hiding was a form of cowardice. I attended meetings. I recruited new members. I believed I was fighting for justice.

Then I saw what the First-Born were willing to do to achieve their goals. A creature who refused to join them was killed — quietly, secretly, made to disappear. I was tasked with disposing of the body. And I realized I had joined a monster.

I left. I spent the next forty years trying to atone for my involvement. I helped creatures escape the First-Born. I fed information to the Accord. I became a taxidermist and hid in plain sight.

But I never stopped being afraid that they would find me. And now they have.

I'm sorry I couldn't tell you this myself. I hope you can forgive me.

Your friend,
Edgar."

Leo read the letter twice. The old guilt — the failure with the Lamont case — mixed with new grief. Edgar had been a broken man, carrying his own weight of regret, just as Leo carried his.

He folded the letter and put it in his pocket.

"The First-Born's hidden sanctum," he said to Grimsby. "Edgar mentioned it in his notes. Do you know where it is?"

Grimsby's face went pale. "I don't know anything about —"

"Edgar trusted you. I need to know."

The solicitor hesitated, then reached into his briefcase and pulled out a folded piece of paper. "I found this among his effects. I was going to destroy it."

Leo took the paper. It was a map, hand-drawn, showing a complex of tunnels beneath the Bank of England. The entrance was marked with a red X.

"The First-Born's headquarters," Leo said.

"If Edgar was involved with them —"

"He left them forty years ago. This map is his final gift."

Leo folded the map and tucked it into his coat alongside the letter. He had what he needed. The location of the First-Born's sanctum, where Ashford might be keeping Euryale, where the final confrontation would begin.

He left the crematorium and walked into the grey afternoon. The moon was already visible in the sky, pale and ghostly against the clouds.

Tonight, everything would end. One way or another.

Chapter 23: The Descent into the Vault

The Bank of England stood at the heart of the City of London, a fortress of stone and steel that had guarded the nation's wealth for three centuries. But beneath its famous vaults, there was another layer — older tunnels carved by creatures who had been in London long before the bank was built.

Leo, Cassia, and Mira approached through the underground service tunnels, following Edgar's map. The passages grew narrower as they descended, the walls changing from modern concrete to ancient brick to rough-hewn stone.

"This doesn't look like a bank," Mira whispered.

"It's not. The bank was built on top of an older structure — a Roman temple, according to the Accord's records. The First-Born made it their headquarters because it sits on a convergence of ley lines."

They reached a heavy iron door, covered in the same three-part symbol Leo had seen on the First-Born scroll. It was locked, but Cassia placed her hand on the surface and spoke a word of power. The lock clicked open.

"The Keeper's privilege," she said. "There's not a door in the hidden world that can resist the Keeper's voice."

Beyond the door, the tunnel opened into a vast chamber — a natural cavern that had been carved and shaped by centuries of occupation. Torches burned along the walls, casting flickering light on a scene that made Leo's blood run cold.

Dozens of stone figures stood in the chamber, arranged like statues in a gallery. Each one was a creature — a satyr, a dryad, a selkie, a brownie — frozen in poses of terror, their faces twisted into eternal screams.

"The victims," Mira breathed. "Not just the four we know."

"Ashford has been collecting them," Cassia said. "These are older petrifications. Decades old, some of them."

Leo moved through the gallery, counting. There were at least thirty statues. Thirty creatures who had been turned to stone and brought to this chamber as trophies.

At the far end of the chamber, a staircase descended into deeper darkness. Voices echoed up from below — voices raised in argument.

"The council," Cassia said. "Or what's left of it."

They descended the stairs, weapons ready. The voices grew louder, resolving into words.

"—cannot continue this way. The humans will notice. The Accord will intervene."

"The Accord is irrelevant. The First-Born will decide the future."

Leo reached the bottom of the stairs and stepped into a second chamber, smaller than the first, lit by a single large fire. Around the fire stood a dozen creatures — representatives of the First-Born's inner circle.

At their center stood a man in an expensive suit, his back to Leo. He turned as Leo entered, and Leo saw the face of Lord Ashford for the first time.

Ashford was handsome in the way of men who had never known hardship — smooth skin, perfect teeth, eyes that held no warmth. A scar ran across his right hand in the shape of a circle divided into three parts.

"Leo Ashworth," Ashford said, his voice smooth as silk. "I wondered when you would find me."

"I'm here to stop you."

"Stop me?" Ashford laughed. "You can't stop me. I have the Aegis. I have the blood of a Gorgon. And tonight, I will open a door to Olympus and claim my prize."

"Euryale," Leo said. "You're using her."

"She volunteered. Euryale believes in the cause. Unlike her sister, who chose to hide in a council flat and pretend the world wasn't changing."

"She's your prisoner."

"She's my partner."

Leo's eyes found Euryale, standing in the shadows behind Ashford. She was a woman in her forties, her face bearing the same faint shimmer as Medea's, her eyes empty.

"She's not your partner. She's your victim."

Ashford's smile never wavered. "Believe what you like. It changes nothing."

He raised his hand, and the torches guttered. The First-Born creatures moved to surround Leo and his team.

"You came too late," Ashford said. "The ritual has already begun."

In the distance, through the earth and stone, Leo heard the first notes of a sound he had never heard before — the deep, resonant hum of an Olympian gate beginning to open.

Chapter 24: The Architect Revealed

The chamber shook. Dust fell from the ceiling. The hum grew louder, vibrating through Leo's bones.

"The gate," Mira said. "He's opening it now."

Ashford stood at the center of the chamber, his arms raised, the Aegis glowing on his arm. The shield was smaller than Leo had expected — perhaps two feet across, made of polished bronze that reflected the firelight in strange patterns. The Gorgon's head on its surface seemed to move, its eyes tracking the people in the room.

"Your ancestors feared this power," Ashford said, his voice rising above the hum. "They built the Accord to hide from it. But I am not afraid. I will take what the gods have kept hidden for millennia."

"You're a madman," Leo said.

"I'm a visionary. There's a difference."

Cassia stepped forward. "Marcus. Stop this."

Ashford's composure cracked, just slightly. "Don't call me that. That name died when I left your tutelage."

"Then call yourself what you are — a thief, a murderer, a betrayer of everything I taught you."

"You taught me to be curious. You taught me to question. And when I questioned the Accord's authority, you tried to silence me."

"I tried to protect you. The Accord exists for a reason."

"The Accord exists to keep creatures weak." Ashford's voice was venomous. "I won't be weak anymore."

He raised the Aegis, and a beam of grey light shot toward Cassia. She threw herself aside, the beam striking the wall behind her, turning the stone to a grey, crumbling mass.

"Stop him!" Leo shouted.

Fletcher drew his revolver and fired. The silver round struck Ashford in the shoulder, but he barely flinched. The wound closed almost instantly, the bullet falling to the floor with a clink.

"Blessed silver," Ashford said, glancing at the bullet. "Charming. But I've been preparing for this night for thirty years. Did you think a single bullet would stop me?"

He raised the Aegis again, and this time the beam struck Fletcher in the chest. The detective was thrown backward, his coat smoking where the beam had hit. But the protection sigils on the revolver's grip flared, and Fletcher rose again, his face pale but alive.

"Expensive coat," he muttered.

Leo drew the silver knife he had carried since the Below. "You want to talk, Ashford? Then talk. Tell me why you're doing this."

"Why?" Ashford's eyes glittered. "Because the hidden world is broken. Creatures live in fear, hiding from humans who would destroy them if they knew the truth. The Accord is a cage. I'm going to tear it down and build something better."

"By killing innocent creatures?"

"By removing the weak. The creatures who chose humanity over their own kind are traitors. Their deaths are necessary."

Leo tightened his grip on the knife. "You're wrong."

"I'm not wrong. I'm the only one who sees clearly."

Ashford raised the Aegis one final time, and the beam of grey light shot toward Leo. He dove, rolled, came up running. The beam caught the edge of his coat, and the fabric turned to stone, crumbling away.

"I've studied you, Ashworth," Ashford called, turning to track him. "Your failures, your guilt. You think this is redemption? You think stopping me will bring back the creature you killed?"

"Shut up."

"I know about the Lamont case. The creature trusted you, and you let her die. She was my lover."

Leo stopped.

The chamber fell silent. The hum of the gate continued, but Leo barely heard it.

"Your lover?"

"Her name was Lamia. A lamia, ironically. She was working for me, gathering information on the Accord. And you —" Ashford's voice was cold " — you failed to protect her. She died because of your incompetence."

Leo's world tilted. The case that had haunted him for three years — the creature witness he had failed to protect — had been connected to Ashford all along.

"Every petrification I've committed, every creature I've turned to stone — I did it for her. And I wanted you to know that before you died."

Leo looked at the Aegis, glowing with stolen power. He looked at Ashford, twisted by grief and rage into something monstrous.

He understood now. Ashford was not a mastermind. He was a broken man, just like Leo.

And broken men could be stopped.

Chapter 25: The Bargain with Ashford

The hum of the Olympian gate grew louder. The walls of the chamber began to crack, thin lines spreading across the stone like veins.

"You don't have to do this," Leo said, his voice quiet.

"Don't have to?" Ashford laughed, the sound hollow. "I've spent thirty years planning this. My entire life has been leading to this moment."

"Lamia wouldn't have wanted this."

"Don't speak her name."

"She wanted to live in peace. She told me that. She said she was tired of running, tired of hiding. She wanted to be free."

Ashford's composure cracked again. "You didn't know her."

"I knew her for three days. But in those three days, she told me she had a partner. Someone she loved. She said he was kind, and gentle, and that he didn't know what she was."

Ashford's face twisted. "Stop."

"She protected you. She kept the hidden world from you because she loved you. And now you've become a killer in her name."

"I said stop!"

Ashford raised the Aegis, but his hand was shaking. The beam that shot toward Leo went wide, striking a support column, turning it to stone.

"You can end this," Leo said. "You can put the Aegis down. You can walk away."

"Walk away? To what? A prison? Execution?"

"To redemption. You're not beyond it. Not yet."

Ashford's eyes were wild, searching the room as if looking for an escape that didn't exist. The First-Born creatures around him watched uncertainly, their loyalty wavering.

"Don't listen to him," one of them said — a tall figure with horns and burning eyes. "He's trying to weaken you."

But Ashford was already weakening. His hand dropped, the Aegis lowering.

"I don't know how to stop," he said. "I've been doing this for so long —"

"Then let us help you."

Leo took a step forward. Another. The Aegis's glow dimmed.

And then the ceiling collapsed.

Stone and dust fell from above, forcing everyone to scatter. The Olympian gate's hum had become a roar, and the physical world was breaking apart under the strain.

Ashford stumbled, the Aegis falling from his grip. It clattered across the stone floor, its glow flickering.

Leo dove for it.

His hand closed around the Aegis's edge, and a shock ran through him — cold and hot at the same time, like touching a live wire. The shield pulsed with trapped magic, desperate to be released.

He lifted it, the weight immense.

"Don't," Ashford said, his voice broken. "It'll kill you."

Leo looked at the Aegis in his hands. The Gorgon's head on its surface was writhing, screaming silently. The souls of a hundred petrified creatures were trapped inside.

He could end this. He could turn Ashford to stone with a single beam. It would be justice. It would be revenge.

But it would also make him the same as the man he was trying to stop.

He set the Aegis down.

"I'm not you," he said.

And the chamber collapsed around them.

Chapter 26: The Escape from the Vault

The ceiling fell in sections, great slabs of stone crashing down around them. The First-Born creatures scattered, fleeing through side tunnels. Ashford stood frozen, staring at the Aegis on the ground.

"Move!" Leo grabbed Ashford's arm and pulled him toward the exit.

"Why are you helping me?"

"Because you're not dying here. Not like this."

Mira was already at the tunnel entrance, waving them forward. Cassia had Fletcher by the arm, half-carrying the wounded detective. Behind them, the chamber collapsed completely, the Olympian gate's hum cutting off with a sound like a dying animal.

They ran through the tunnels, following Edgar's map in reverse. The passages were shaking, the walls cracking, the entire underground complex destabilizing.

"Faster!" Cassia shouted.

They reached the iron door and burst through it into the service tunnels beneath the Bank of England. The shaking was less severe here, the stone walls giving way to concrete and brick.

Leo stopped, bending over, gasping for breath. Ashford stood beside him, his face blank, his expensive suit covered in dust.

"You saved me," Ashford said.

"Don't make me regret it."

"The Aegis —"

"Is still down there. Buried. And if you're smart, you'll leave it there."

Ashford looked at his hands. They were shaking.

"I don't know what to do now."

"You figure it out. You go to the Accord, you surrender, and you spend the rest of your life trying to atone for what you've done."

"Or you run," Fletcher said, his voice hard. He had his revolver trained on Ashford. "And I put a bullet in you."

Ashford looked at the gun, then at Leo.

"I'll surrender," he said. "I'll face whatever judgment the Accord has for me."

Leo nodded. "Then let's get out of here."

They emerged from the service tunnels into a back alley near the Bank of England. The sun was rising over the City of London, the first light of a new day painting the buildings gold.

The full moon had passed. The Olympian gate was closed. And Lord Ashford, the sorcerer who had terrorized the hidden world, was in their custody.

But as Leo looked at his team — Mira exhausted but alive, Cassia wounded but standing, Fletcher bleeding but defiant — he knew the fight was not over.

Euryale was still out there. The First-Born were still active. And the Accord was still divided.

But they had won this battle.

And tonight, they would rest.

Chapter 27: The Sphinx's Secret

Cassia's office at the British Museum had become a makeshift medical bay. Fletcher sat in a chair, his chest wrapped in bandages, refusing to go to a hospital. Mira was reviewing her notes, making corrections to the genealogy. Leo stood by the window, watching the sun climb over Bloomsbury.

Cassia sat at her desk, her face pale, her hands folded in front of her. She looked older than she had a week ago — the centuries weighing heavier than usual.

"I need to tell you the truth," she said. "About Ashford. About what happened."

Leo turned from the window. "You don't have to."

"I do. You've risked your lives for a world that cast you out. You deserve to know why."

She took a breath, and began.

"Ashford came to me forty years ago. He was a young man, brilliant and hungry, fascinated by the hidden world. I was the Keeper, and I saw potential in him. I took him as my apprentice."

"You taught him everything."

"I taught him the theory. I showed him the archives. I thought I was preparing a successor, someone who could take over when I was ready to retire." She shook her head. "I was wrong."

"When did you realize?"

"Too late. He had been stealing from the archives for years before I noticed. Manuscripts, maps, ritual texts. He was building his own library of forbidden knowledge."

"And you didn't stop him?"

"I confronted him. He denied everything. I believed him, because I wanted to believe him. I had invested so much in him that I couldn't accept the truth."

Cassia's voice cracked. "When he disappeared, I told myself it was for the best. I told myself he had found his own path. But I knew, somewhere deep, that I had created a monster."

"You didn't create him," Mira said. "He made his own choices."

"Did he? I gave him the tools. I showed him the weaknesses in the Accord's security. I taught him the words of power that let him breach the museum's vault." She looked at her hands. "Every creature Ashford turned to stone, I turned to stone with him."

Leo crossed the room and sat down across from her. "Then we're both responsible for failures that haunt us. The question is what we do next."

Cassia looked up. "What do you mean?"

"There are still creatures out there who need protection. The First-Born are still active. The Accord is still broken." He met her eyes. "We can sit here and drown in guilt, or we can keep fighting."

Cassia was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded slowly.

"You're right. I've spent too long carrying this guilt. It's time to put it down."

"Good. Because we have a lot of work to do."

Cassia straightened in her chair, some of the old fire returning to her eyes. "The First-Born. We need to find Euryale. She was Ashford's source of Gorgon blood, but she's also a victim. If we can bring her in, we might be able to break the First-Born's hold on her."

"And the council?"

"They'll need to see proof that Ashford was the real threat. That the First-Born were pawns in his game."

Leo pulled out the vial of Medea's blood. "We have evidence. We have testimony. And we have a captured sorcerer."

Cassia nodded. "Then let's present our case to the council. And hope they're ready to listen."

Chapter 28: The Third Suspect

Medea Gorgon was not at her flat when Leo arrived. The door was unlocked, the rooms empty, the incense still smoldering in its holder. Her books and plants remained — she had left in a hurry.

Leo called her phone. It went straight to voicemail.

He called Cassia. "Medea's gone."

"Gone where?"

"I don't know. But her flat is empty, and she left in a hurry."

"Check the garden."

Leo went to the back of the flat, where a small courtyard garden was visible through the kitchen window. Medea sat on a bench, her knees drawn up to her chest, her face wet with tears.

He opened the back door and stepped out. "Medea."

"I know." Her voice was small. "Euryale is my sister."

"You knew?"

"I suspected. When you told me about the other descendant, the hidden branch — I knew it could only be her. She disappeared years ago. I thought she was dead."

"She's not dead. She's been working with Ashford."

Medea looked up, her eyes red. "She was always the radical one. She believed the First-Born's rhetoric. I thought she would grow out of it, but she never did."

"Why didn't you tell me?"

"Because I was ashamed. My own sister, helping a killer. I thought if I kept quiet, it would go away."

Leo sat down on the bench beside her. "You can't control what Euryale does. But you can help us stop her."

"I don't know where she is."

"Then help us find her. You know her habits, her patterns, her hiding spots. You're our best chance of bringing her in alive."

Medea was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded.

"There's a place. An old mill in Walthamstow. We used to go there as children, when our mother was still alive. It's abandoned, but it's still standing."

"Would Euryale go there?"

"It's the only place she would go. She always said it was the one place she felt safe."

Leo stood and offered Medea his hand. "Then let's go get her."

They drove to Walthamstow in silence, the morning sun casting long shadows across the streets. The mill was on the edge of the wetlands, a crumbling stone building with a broken water wheel and windows that stared like empty eyes.

Medea led the way through the tall grass, her steps sure despite her fear.

"Euryale," she called. "It's Medea. I came to talk."

Silence. Then a figure emerged from the mill's doorway — a woman who could have been Medea's twin, except for the hardness in her eyes and the grey streak in her hair.

"You shouldn't have come," Euryale said.

"I came to save you."

"You came too late."

Euryale raised her hand, and Leo saw the Gorgon's mark on her palm — the same three-part circle that Ashford had worn. She had branded herself with the First-Born's symbol.

"Ashford is in custody," Leo said. "The First-Born are in disarray. It's over."

"It's not over." Euryale's voice was flat. "It's just beginning."

She took a step back, into the darkness of the mill. Leo moved to follow, but Medea held him back.

"She's not going to hurt us," Medea said. "She wants to talk."

They entered the mill together, stepping into the dim interior. The building had been stripped of its machinery, leaving only empty space and the smell of old grain.

"I know what you're going to say," Euryale said. "That I'm a victim. That Ashford manipulated me. That I should come quietly and face judgment."

"You're right. That is what I was going to say."

"It won't work. I knew what I was doing. I chose this."

"Why?"

Euryale's composure cracked. "Because I'm tired of hiding. I'm tired of being afraid. The First-Born offered me a future where I didn't have to pretend to be something I'm not."

"There are other ways. Better ways."

"There are no better ways. There's only this."

Leo stepped forward. "Euryale. Your sister loves you. She came here to save you, not to judge you. Let us help you."

Euryale looked at Medea, and for a moment, the hardness in her eyes softened.

"I'm sorry," she said. "For everything."

Then she turned and vanished into the shadows of the mill.

They searched for hours, but Euryale was gone. She had slipped out through a hidden passage, escaping into the wetlands beyond.

Medea stood in the empty mill, her face wet with tears.

"She's gone."

"She'll come back," Leo said. "Eventually."

"How do you know?"

"Because family always does."

But as they walked back to the car, Leo wondered if he was lying. Euryale had chosen Ashford, chosen the First-Born, chosen violence over peace. Some divides could not be bridged.

And some wounds never healed.

Chapter 29: The Harrowing of Medea

Medea did not sleep that night. She sat in her flat, staring at the wall, replaying every conversation she had ever had with Euryale, looking for the moment when her sister had slipped away.

Leo sat with her, keeping watch. The city was quiet outside, the full moon hidden behind clouds.

"She was always the strong one," Medea said. "When we were children, she protected me from everything. Bullies, monsters, our mother's rages. She was my shield."

"What changed?"

"When our mother died, Euryale took it hard. She started listening to the First-Born's rhetoric. They told her that our mother's death was the Accord's fault — that if creatures hadn't been forced to hide, she could have gotten proper medical treatment."

"Is that true?"

"Maybe. The hidden world doesn't have hospitals like the human world. Our healers are good, but they're not equipped for everything." Medea's voice was bitter. "The First-Born used our mother's death to recruit Euryale. They turned her grief into rage."

Leo nodded slowly. "That's how extremists work. They find the broken and offer them purpose."

"I should have seen it. I should have stopped her."

"You were grieving too."

"It doesn't matter now. She's gone, and she's not coming back."

"Don't give up on her yet."

Medea looked at him. "Why do you care so much? You don't know me. You don't know Euryale."

"Because I know what it's like to lose someone you love. And I know what it's like to blame yourself for it." Leo met her eyes. "I can't bring your sister back. But I can help you find a way forward."

Medea was silent for a long moment. Then she nodded slowly.

"There's something I didn't tell you. About the Aegis."

Leo waited.

"The Aegis can only be used seven times before it needs to be recharged. Ashford used it five times that we know of. The sixth use was tonight, at the glassblowing studio. That leaves one more use before he needs to recharge it."

"Recharge it how?"

"With the blood of an Olympian. A god, or a demigod, or even a fragment of divine essence. That's what he was trying to do with the gate — capture the echo of a deity and use it to power the Aegis permanently."

Chapter 30: The Fifth Strike

The Serpentine was black and still beneath the clouded sky. The park was empty at this hour, the paths deserted, the city's noise reduced to a distant hum.

"Lamia wouldn't want this!"

"You don't know what she would want."

The Aegis's glow dimmed. The gate's light flickered and died.

The Aegis clattered to the stone floor.

"I can't do this anymore," Ashford said, his voice barely a whisper. "I can't."

Leo stepped forward and picked up the Aegis. It was cold now, inert, its power spent.

"It's over," Leo said.

Ashford nodded slowly. "I know."

Chapter 31: The Human Cost

Chapter 32: The Museum Siege

Chapter 33: Counting the Veils

Mira had been working for three days straight, fueled by tea and adrenaline and the obsessive need to understand the phrase that had been haunting her since Ione's last words: "counting the veils."

The veils were not physical barriers. They were layers of reality, stacked like sheets of paper, each one separating a different realm. The human world was one layer. The Olympian realm was another. The Underworld was a third. And there were others — realms that had no names in any human language.

Ashford had not been trying to open a single gate. He had been trying to pierce all the veils at once.

"I don't know. Months. Years. A decade, at most." Mira met their eyes. "The hidden world is changing. We need to be ready."

Leo stood. "Then we prepare. We rebuild the Accord. We strengthen the veils. And we make
We make sure the next threat finds us ready."

Chapter 34: The Sixth Petrification

Chapter 35: Olympian Threshold

The secondary gate beneath the Serpentine was still active. Not fully open, but shimmering, waiting. Leo stood at its edge, looking into the silver pool of light that separated the human world from the Olympian realm.

Chapter 37: The Last Veil

The final veil was the hardest to cross.

"I failed him," she had written. "I saw the darkness growing in him, and I did nothing. I was afraid of confrontation. And now he is gone, and I do not know where he has gone, or what he will become."

He crossed it.

Asterion was silent for a long moment.

Then Medea stepped forward.

"I was like you," she said. "Bitter. Angry. Believing that violence was the only answer. But I saw what it cost. My sister died for the First-Born's cause. She died alone, in a garden, turned to stone by her own hand."

She took a breath. "I don't want you to die alone. I want you to choose differently."

Asterion looked at her. Something in his eyes shifted.

"I don't know if I can."

"Then try."

The chamber was silent. The council waited.

And Asterion, the minotaur who had led the First-Born's rebellion, nodded slowly.

"I'll try."

Chapter 47: The Shattering

The Aegis sat on the stone table in the center of the council chamber. Around it stood the leaders of the hidden world — the council members, the Keepers, the representatives of every species.

They had gathered to witness its destruction.

"The Aegis of Perseus has caused nothing but death and suffering," Mira said. "It was used by heroes and villains alike, twisted to purposes its creators never intended. Tonight, we end it."

She raised a hammer — ancient iron, forged in the fires of Mount Olympus itself. The only thing that could break the bronze.

"Any objections?"

The chamber was silent.

Mira brought the hammer down.

The Aegis shattered.

The sound was like thunder, like the collapse of a mountain, like the end of the world. The bronze split, cracked, and fell apart. And from within the fragments came a storm of light.

The souls of every creature the Aegis had ever petrified poured out — hundreds of them, thousands, a torrent of spirits that filled the chamber with their cries. They swirled around the council members, around the Keepers, around Leo.

And then they were gone.

The chamber fell silent. The fragments of the Aegis lay scattered on the floor, inert and harmless.

"It's over," Leo said.

Mira set down the hammer. "It's over."

The council members stood in silence, the weight of history settling around them. The Aegis was destroyed. The souls were freed. The hidden world could begin to heal.

And Leo Ashworth, the disgraced investigator who had failed so many, stood among them — not as an outcast, but as a hero.

Chapter 48: The Aftermath

The days after the shattering were a blur of reports, meetings, and negotiations. Leo spent most of them in the council chamber, helping Mira navigate the complex politics of the hidden world.

The First-Born, without Asterion's leadership, had splintered. Some chose to integrate. Others retreated deeper into the Below, vowing to continue the fight. But the immediate threat was over.

Medea took a position as the Accord's liaison to the Gorgon community, helping to rebuild trust between the descendants of Medusa and the wider hidden world. She and Leo spoke often, their shared experiences forming a bond that neither of them had expected.

Cassia stepped back from public life, spending her days in the museum archives, cataloging the artifacts that had been recovered from Ashford's collection. She had earned her rest.

Fletcher was formally reinstated to the Metropolitan Police and appointed as the official liaison between Scotland Yard and the Accord. His suspension was expunged, his record cleared.

And Mira — the scholar who had never believed in monsters — became the most powerful creature in the hidden world. She approached the role with the same methodical precision she had brought to her academic work, and she proved to be exactly what the Accord needed.

Leo walked through London on a cool autumn evening, watching the city that had once been his prison. The streets were full of people — humans and creatures alike — going about their lives, unaware of the world that existed just beneath the surface.

He stopped at the canal, where the nereid Marina had fed breadcrumbs to the ducks. The water was still, reflecting the lights of the city.

"Thank you," he said quietly.

No one answered. But the water rippled, just slightly, as if something beneath the surface had heard him.

Leo smiled and walked on.

Chapter 49: The New Accord

The integration ceremony was held in Trafalgar Square on a sunny autumn afternoon. Thousands gathered — creatures who had never before shown their faces in public, humans who had stumbled into the hidden world and chosen to stay, and representatives from every corner of the globe.

Mira stood at the podium, her bronze seal of office gleaming on her chest.

"Today marks a new beginning," she said. "For centuries, the Accord has kept the hidden world secret. We believed that secrecy was the only way to survive. But we have learned that secrecy breeds fear, and fear breeds violence."

She looked out at the crowd. "The new Accord will be built on openness. On cooperation. On the belief that creatures and humans can coexist, not as enemies, but as neighbors."

The crowd erupted in applause.

Leo stood at the back, watching. Beside him stood Cassia, her eyes bright with unshed tears.

"They're really doing it," Cassia said.

"They're really doing it."

"I never thought I would see this day."

"Neither did I."

They stood together, watching the ceremony unfold. The hidden world was coming into the light, one step at a time.

It would not be easy. There would be setbacks, conflicts, and challenges. But for the first time in centuries, there was hope.

And that was enough.

Chapter 50: The Concrete Chorus

The London dawn was grey and quiet. Leo walked through the empty streets, his hands in his pockets, his breath misting in the cold air.

He had been walking for hours, through the city he had once tried to leave. He walked past the British Museum, where Cassia was already at work in her office. He walked past the canal, where Marina was feeding the ducks. He walked past the taxidermy shop, still closed, still waiting for Edgar to return.

At the edge of Hyde Park, he stopped.

The Serpentine was calm, the water flat and grey. The island in the middle was covered in mist. The gate beneath it was closed, sealed, guarded.

Leo stood at the water's edge and listened.

At first, there was nothing — just the distant hum of traffic, the cry of seagulls, the rustle of leaves in the wind. But then, beneath it all, he heard it.

A chorus. A thousand voices, singing together in harmony. The voices of creatures — naiads and harpies, sphinxes and phoenixes, Gorgons and minotaurs — all living their lives in the hidden corners of the city.

The concrete chorus of London.

He had never heard it before. He had been too wrapped up in his own pain, his own guilt, his own failure. But now, standing at the edge of the Serpentine, he heard it clearly.

The hidden world was alive. It was beautiful. And he was part of it.

His phone buzzed. A message from Mira: "Council meeting at noon. The new protocols need approval."

He smiled and typed back: "I'll be there."

He turned away from the lake and walked back into the city, the chorus still singing in his ears.

Leo Ashworth, disgraced investigator, failed protector, broken man, had found his place.

He was the Accord's investigator. The bridge between worlds. The guardian of the veils.

And he was home.
