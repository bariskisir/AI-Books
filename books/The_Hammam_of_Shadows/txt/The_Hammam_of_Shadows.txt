Chapter 1: Before Dawn

The hammam opened its doors two hours before the first call to prayer. Fatma arrived earlier than that.

She moved through the coal cellar by feel — she had done this enough times that the dark was merely another texture. She could hear the furnace ahead, the low animal sound of it consuming the night's last load. The stoker Vasil would not appear for another half hour. Until then the building was hers.

She filled the first cauldron by hand, working the pump handle until her shoulder protested. The water was cold and smelled of the cistern — stone and old copper. By the time it reached the hammam floor it would be something else entirely: dense with steam, almost a substance rather than a liquid, the kind of air you could hold.

The private bathing chamber at the far end of the corridor was reserved for Nikos Papadakis on Tuesday mornings. He had kept this appointment for four years. He paid the premium rate without negotiation, arrived precisely at seven, and departed by eight-thirty. He was courteous in the restrained way of men who understand that courtesy is a form of economy — it costs nothing and purchases goodwill in quantity.

Fatma had attended him perhaps forty times. She knew the shape of his back, the scar on his left calf from some old injury he had never explained, the way he held his breath under the copper ladle before letting it go in a long exhale. She knew that he sometimes spoke to himself quietly in Greek, not prayer, something more like arithmetic. She knew he was afraid of enclosed spaces and that the hammam's open dome was the thing that made this bearable for him. He would look up at the skylight repeatedly, making sure it was there.

She prepared the private chamber now. Fresh linen on the massage table. Oil of almond in the cedar dish. The floor drain checked and clear. The stone lip of the pool wiped down with a cloth she wrung twice.

She did all of this in the gray hour before anyone arrived, because this was her work and she believed in doing it well, and because — though she would not have said this aloud — the hammam in those minutes belonged only to the steam and the stone and to her.

Outside, the city slept badly. She could hear it through the dome's small skylights: carts on cobblestones, a dog, a muezzin practicing in some minaret nearby. Istanbul in 1893 did not sleep so much as shift its weight from one discomfort to another. The old city and the new pressed against each other along the Golden Horn — Ottoman minarets, Frankish banking houses, Greek churches, Jewish schools, the train station being built near the water. Nothing quite fit and nothing quite fell apart. Fatma had lived in it for forty-two years and had long since stopped trying to understand it as a whole.

She understood the hammam. That was enough.

She lit the oil lamp in the private chamber and adjusted the wick until the flame was steady. The room filled with amber light and the smell of hot stone. She stood for a moment looking at the pool — a rectangular basin of pale marble, knee-deep, steaming steadily from below.

Then she went to prepare the rest of the building for its day.

Chapter 2: The Hands at the Stone

Papadakis arrived at seven, as always. He wore his usual suit — dark wool, slightly too heavy for the season — and carried his leather case. He checked in with Kadriye at the door, paid, and followed Fatma down the corridor to the private chamber.

"Good morning," he said, in Turkish that retained its Greek edges.

"Good morning," Fatma said. "The water is ready."

He paused at the threshold, looked up at the skylight as she knew he would. The dome overhead was pale with morning light. He nodded, as though this confirmed something, and stepped inside.

Fatma left him and went to attend the main hall, which was filling with the early customers — porters, a few minor civil servants, a tanner who came twice a week and always arrived first because he could not bear the smell of himself by morning. She worked through the crowd, scrubbing, pouring, answering questions in the short flat sentences of a woman who has answered the same questions ten thousand times.

An hour later she passed the private corridor on her way to the linen room. The corridor was dim, lit by one oil lamp at the far end. She was not looking at the chamber door. She was thinking about the linen count, which was short by three towels that she was certain had not been laundered.

Then she saw the hands.

The door to the private chamber was ajar, perhaps two finger-widths. Through the gap she saw the pool's stone lip and, gripping it from inside the water, two hands. The knuckles were white. The tendons stood up in the wrists. The grip was the grip of someone holding on, not the loose trailing hand of a man at ease in the water.

She stopped.

She heard no splash, no cry. The steam from the chamber rose in a pale column through the gap. The hands did not move. They held the stone with a ferocity that she recognized as effort — the specific effort of a body trying to hold itself up.

She should have pushed the door open. She knew this afterward. She stood in the corridor for perhaps three seconds — three seconds that she replayed for weeks — and then the door swung slightly on its hinge and she could no longer see through the gap.

She walked to the door and raised her hand to push it open.

Kadriye appeared at the corridor's end. "Fatma Hanım — the Konya gentleman is asking for you."

Fatma let her hand fall. She looked at the door. She looked at Kadriye.

"Tell him I'm coming," she said.

She went.

When she returned to the corridor forty minutes later, the lamp had gone out. She relit it, then pushed the door to the private chamber fully open.

Papadakis was face-down in the pool. The water around him was still and clear. His arms were at his sides.

Chapter 3: Eleven Minutes

Inspector Nazmi arrived at quarter past nine. Fatma had sent Kadriye to the police post on the corner — it was not far — and in the interim Mehmet Bey had come down from his offices above and stood in the private corridor with his arms crossed, repeating that this was a terrible accident, simply terrible, a tragedy.

Fatma said nothing. She stood at the chamber door and watched the body from there because she did not want to disturb it, though she understood that the person who had disturbed it most had already gone.

Nazmi was a compact man with a neat mustache and the careful eyes of someone who monitors the level of a room rather than any individual in it. He wore a blue uniform that was somewhat too large across the shoulders. He had two constables with him. One of them began to retch when he saw the body, stepped back, controlled himself.

Nazmi walked into the chamber without covering his shoes. He looked at the body for a moment from above. He walked around the pool's perimeter. He looked at the skylight. He bent down and examined the pool's drainage aperture — briefly, with no particular attention. He stood up.

Eleven minutes. Fatma counted.

Then Nazmi came to the door and looked at Fatma. "Who found him?"

"I did," Fatma said.

"When?"

"Approximately eight fifty-five."

"And when did he enter?"

"Seven o'clock precisely. He always arrived at seven."

Nazmi wrote this down. He looked at the room again, then at Mehmet Bey. "Accidental drowning," he said. "Heart, most likely, in men his age. I'll file the report today."

Mehmet Bey exhaled. "Of course. Yes. A tragedy."

Fatma looked at Nazmi. "Inspector. The man was a strong swimmer."

Nazmi's eyes came to her. They stayed for a moment. "Many strong swimmers have drowned," he said. "The steam is very heavy in these rooms." He put his notebook away. "I'll need a statement from you. Come to the post this afternoon."

He left with the constables. Fatma watched him go. She watched Mehmet Bey follow him into the corridor, saw Mehmet Bey lay a hand on Nazmi's arm briefly, saw Nazmi not pull away.

She turned back to the chamber and stood looking at the empty pool.

The body had been removed while she was giving her statement to one of the constables. The pool had been drained. The water had gone down through the drainage aperture and into whatever lay below. The chamber smelled of wet stone and the faint salt of a human being.

Fatma stood there until Kadriye came and told her the afternoon customers were arriving.

Chapter 4: Directions

Mehmet Bey called the staff together that evening after the hammam closed. He was a heavyset man with a large head and thick, careful hands — the hands of someone who had once worked with them and now used them primarily to gesture. He stood at the center of the main hall and looked at each of them in turn: Fatma, Kadriye, Vasil the stoker, Cemil the porter, two young women who handled the women's section on alternating days.

"There has been a tragedy here," Mehmet Bey said. "A death. The inspector has confirmed it was an accident. We must respect the ruling and not give rise to speculation."

He said this twice, in different formulations. What the city did not know could not harm the hammam's reputation. The hammam was one hundred and forty years old. It had survived earthquake and war and the slow ruin of changing fashion. It would survive this.

Fatma watched his hands while he spoke. They moved in broad, settling gestures — the kind of movements that mean everything is fine, trust me, calm down. His eyes visited each person in the room except Fatma. He skipped her each time with the precision of someone who has decided not to look at a specific thing.

She watched Nazmi, while Mehmet Bey spoke, in her memory. The inspector had stood in that chamber for eleven minutes. In those eleven minutes he had not looked at the stone lip of the pool. He had not asked where the steam outlet was or why the water was still so clear if a man had struggled in it. He had not asked Fatma what she had seen.

He had looked at the skylight. He had looked at the drain.

And then Mehmet Bey had put his hand on Nazmi's arm.

Mehmet Bey finished speaking. "Are there questions?"

Kadriye shook her head. Vasil stared at the floor. Cemil was very still.

"Good," Mehmet Bey said. "We open tomorrow as usual. The family of the deceased has been contacted. We will send our condolences through the appropriate channels."

He walked out. His footsteps on the marble were heavy and precise.

Fatma remained in the hall until the others had gone. Then she walked back to the private corridor and stood in the dark outside the chamber door. She put her hand flat against the wood.

The wood was cool. The furnace had been banked for the night.

She tried to remember the sound of the chamber when she had passed it — whether she had heard water moving. She could not recover it. There had been only the hands on the stone, the white knuckles, the tendons in the wrists.

Those she could remember perfectly.

Chapter 5: Wax on Stone

She returned to the private chamber alone the next morning, before the furnace was lit.

The room had been cleaned by Kadriye the previous afternoon — standard procedure after a death, at Mehmet Bey's instruction. The pool was dry. The linen and oils were fresh. The floor had been scrubbed with lye soap.

But the stone lip had not been replaced, because stone is not replaced. It is only cleaned.

Fatma took the oil lamp close to the lip and moved it slowly along the edge, watching the light change across the surface. The marble was pale gray veined with white, old stone that had been worn smooth by decades of wet hands. She knew its surface well. She had wiped it hundreds of times.

There. On the outer edge of the lip, near the left end of the pool: a dark smear, perhaps three centimeters long. Not wet. Not oil. She touched it with her fingertip.

Wax.

Not the wax of a candle — too dark for that, and harder, with a faint smell she could not name. Industrial, perhaps. The wax used to seal something.

She looked at it for a long time. Then she looked at the floor below it. The floor had been scrubbed clean. Whatever had been there was gone.

She thought: a man who was drowning would not carry a wax seal. A man who was drowned might carry one without knowing it.

She thought: or someone else left this.

She went to the coal cellar and looked at the base of the wall that ran beneath the private chamber. The wall was faced with old brick. She walked it slowly, holding the lamp. The mortar between the bricks was gray and undisturbed. She was about to turn back when she noticed, near the floor, a line of mortar that was slightly lighter than the rest — newer. A section of brickwork perhaps sixty centimeters wide and forty high.

She pressed her hand against it. Solid. But the mortar was definitely newer.

She replaced the lamp on its hook and went upstairs.

She said nothing about this to anyone.

Chapter 6: Kadriye's Calculation

Kadriye found her two days later in the linen room. Fatma was folding, and Kadriye stood in the doorway with the particular expression of a young woman who has decided to say something she has been holding back.

"I know what you're doing," Kadriye said.

"I'm folding linen," Fatma said.

"You went back to the chamber. You've been asking Vasil questions. You asked the man at the coffeehouse across the street whether he had seen anyone leave through the side passage on Tuesday morning." Kadriye's voice was careful, not angry. "I know because the coffeehouse man told Cemil, and Cemil told me."

Fatma set a towel down. "What did the coffeehouse man say?"

"That's not the point."

"It is to me."

Kadriye stepped into the room and closed the door behind her. She was twenty-eight, dark-haired, with the alert, calibrating expression of someone who had grown up without any margin for error. She had come to Istanbul from Bursa three years ago and had saved enough in that time to send money home monthly. She was good at her work because she paid careful attention to what the work actually required.

"If you start an investigation," Kadriye said, "you will lose your position here."

"I haven't started an investigation."

"If Mehmet Bey thinks you are questioning his directions —"

"I am not questioning his directions."

"Fatma Hanım." Kadriye sat down on the wooden stool beside the shelves. "A man died here. The police inspector came. He ruled it an accident. That ruling is what protects us from having police in this building every week, from customers going to other hammams, from Mehmet Bey firing everyone who was present that day."

"The ruling may be wrong," Fatma said.

Kadriye looked at her. "The ruling may be wrong," she said. "And if it is, there is a police inspector who decided it was right and a hammam owner who agreed with him. You are a tellak. I am a tellak. We do not have the standing to correct a police inspector."

"I know our standing."

"Then you know that nothing you find can go anywhere."

Fatma picked up the towel and finished folding it. "You said you knew what I was doing. You didn't say you were going to report it."

Kadriye was quiet for a moment. "I am not going to report it," she said. "But I want you to understand what it costs."

"I understand."

"I don't think you do." Kadriye stood. "I think you saw something and you can't let it go. That is admirable. It is also very dangerous and I am asking you to be careful."

She left. Fatma stood in the linen room for a while. Outside, the furnace had started up — she could hear the deep breath of it through the floor. The building was waking up around her.

She thought about the wax mark and the new mortar. She went back to work.

Chapter 7: The Greek Quarter

She went on her afternoon off, which fell on Thursday. She told no one where she was going.

The Greek quarter began where the old Byzantine walls gave way to the narrow streets running toward the water. The buildings were stone and whitewash, three and four stories, the upper floors overhanging the streets on wooden brackets. In the afternoon the light came in at angles, laying bright parallelograms on the cobblestones. Small children played in doorways. Old men sat outside a coffeehouse with their worry beads and their small glasses of tea.

Papadakis had lived two streets in from the water, in a house that was now shuttered. His wife, someone told Fatma, had gone to relatives in Thessaloniki immediately after the funeral. The house would be sold.

She found the coffeehouse where he had spent his evenings. The owner was a broad man named Christos, perhaps sixty, who looked at Fatma with frank curiosity when she introduced herself as a tellak from Çemberlitaş.

"He spoke of you," Christos said. "Well, not you specifically. He said the hammam was good. He trusted the people there."

"Trusted them to do what?"

Christos poured tea without being asked. "To be discreet. He valued discretion." He looked at his hands. "He was frightened in the last weeks. I told the police inspector this."

"What did the inspector say?"

"He wrote it down. He seemed very interested in writing things down." Christos's tone was dry. "He asked no follow-up questions."

Fatma drank her tea. "What was he frightened of?"

"He did not say, specifically. He said that he had information he shouldn't have, and that some people knew he had it." Christos paused. "He also said — this was perhaps two weeks before he died — that he had found a place to keep this information safe."

"Where?"

"He didn't say." Christos looked at her. "Why are you asking this?"

"Because I think the inspector's ruling was wrong."

Christos was silent for a moment. A cart passed outside, the wheels grinding on the cobblestones. Someone called out in Greek from an upper window.

"I think so too," Christos said. "Nikos Papadakis had swum in the sea since he was eight years old. He swam in the Bosphorus on New Year's Day for forty years." He shook his head. "He did not drown in a shallow pool."

Fatma set down her glass. "If anyone asks you whether I came here," she said, "tell them no."

Christos looked at her with something like respect. "I never saw you," he said.

Chapter 8: Roksanda at the Door

She arrived on a Friday, which was unusual — Friday was the men's busy day, the hammam most crowded in the morning before prayers. The women's section was quieter. Roksanda came alone, without the companion that respectable women usually brought to such establishments.

Kadriye took her booking at the door and then came to find Fatma with a particular expression. "Bulgarian woman. Says she was referred by a merchant family in Galata. Very specific about wanting the private women's bath."

"Give it to her," Fatma said.

"She asked whether you were working today. By name."

Fatma considered this. "Show her to the private bath. Tell her I'll attend her in twenty minutes."

She took twenty-five. She wanted to think.

Roksanda was perhaps forty-five, solidly built, with iron-gray hair worn back in a practical knot. She was sitting on the marble bench when Fatma entered, wearing the hammam's linen wrap with the ease of someone who had used many hammams. Her eyes were pale brown and careful.

"Fatma Hanım," she said. The Turkish was accented but sure.

"You know my name," Fatma said.

"Nikos Papadakis spoke of you." She used the past tense without any particular inflection, as though the fact of his death was already integrated into the way she thought about him. "He said you were reliable. He meant it as a compliment."

"He was a regular client. I knew him as I know all my regular clients."

"He is dead," Roksanda said, "and the police inspector says it was an accident, and I know it was not."

Fatma set down the basin she was carrying. "Tell me what you know."

Roksanda looked at her for a moment. "He was carrying documents. Not goods — he was in textiles, yes, but what paid was the other thing. Deeds. Certificates. Ottoman state papers that certain people wanted to move out of the country quietly, to sell to European investors." She said this without particular drama, as though explaining a market mechanism. "He had done this for three years. The hammam was a transfer point."

"For how long?"

"Since he began. The chamber was used as a drop point — not every week, but regularly."

Fatma thought of the wax mark on the stone lip. "And the hammam owner —"

"Knew," Roksanda said. "Was paid. I cannot tell you whether the inspector also knew from the start or whether he learned about it afterward and was then managed."

"Managed how?"

Roksanda's expression was the expression of a woman who has dealt with this question in many countries. "Money. Threat. The ledger that proves he is already implicated." She paused. "Nikos kept a ledger. He kept very precise records. This was his protection and also the reason he was killed."

Fatma sat down on the bench across the pool. The steam moved between them. She looked at Roksanda steadily.

"Where is the ledger?" Fatma said.

"That," Roksanda said, "is what I am trying to determine."

Chapter 9: Deflection

They sat together in the private chamber for a long time. The steam accumulated and thinned and accumulated again as the water cycled through the pipes below. Fatma did her work — she was still working, she had not stopped being a tellak — and they talked in the unhurried way that the hammam permitted.

Roksanda explained what she knew of the network. The documents moved through several hands: a customs official who provided access to the registry, a fixer who arranged the transport, Papadakis who handled the exchange with European buyers, and someone at the European end whose name Roksanda did not know. The hammam was used for a specific part of the transaction — not the goods themselves but the confirmation of payment, passed in the confusion of a public bathhouse where the presence of different men in proximity attracted no comment.

"And Papadakis," Fatma said, "became afraid."

"He learned something about the official. About how high the arrangement went. It was not just a customs functionary — there was a palace connection." Roksanda spoke carefully. "When he understood this, he wanted to stop. He told me this."

"But he didn't stop."

"He couldn't stop at that point. He knew too much and the men above him knew he knew it. He was trying to find a way out that didn't end with him dead."

"And failed."

"Yes." Roksanda folded her hands in her lap. "When you ask me where the ledger is — I have been looking for it since before his death. He told me he had hidden it in a place he trusted. I believe it is somewhere in this building."

Fatma did not react to this. She poured water from the basin over the bench, rinsing it, and said nothing for a moment.

"Why tell me this?" she said.

"Because I need someone who can move through this building at will, and because Nikos trusted you." Roksanda looked at her directly. "Also because you came to Christos's coffeehouse two days ago and asked about Nikos. You are already investigating. I am proposing that we do it together rather than separately."

Fatma wrung out the cloth she had been using. "Who killed him?"

"That I don't know. Not specifically. Fevzi — the fixer — could have done it, or had it done. But someone gave the order."

"And you think that person is still connected to this building?"

Roksanda paused. "I think the operation is still running. Nikos's death was meant to remove a liability, not stop the network. Which means someone is still using the hammam."

Fatma looked at the pool. The water was still, undisturbed.

"Come back next Friday," she said. "I need to think."

Chapter 10: What Halil Efendi Knew

Halil Efendi came on Tuesdays and Saturdays, as he had for as long as anyone could remember. He was a small, neat man of sixty-two, with a thin white beard and the manner of someone who had spent decades being the least conspicuous person in important rooms. He had retired from the palace administration four years ago and spent his mornings in the hammam and his afternoons in a house near the Hippodrome, which he shared with a cat and an exceptional library.

He did not ask questions. He told Fatma things, in the diffuse, circling way of men who have learned that directness is a form of vulnerability.

On the Saturday after Papadakis's death, he settled into the main hall's heated bench and said, while Fatma worked the soap cloth across his back: "The Greek merchant had a difficult final few weeks."

"I've heard that," Fatma said.

"He was not a man to make enemies. The difficulty was that he found himself in the company of people who considered him disposable." Halil paused while she moved to his shoulder. "You know, there are men who use a hammam purely for business. The steam makes them feel that secrets spoken there will dissolve with the water."

"Do they dissolve?"

"Rarely." He considered the ceiling. "On the morning of his death — I was in the main hall, not the private corridor — I saw three men I did not recognize leave through the side passage. Not the main entrance. The passage that connects to the coal cellar stairs."

Fatma's hands stilled for a moment, then continued.

"Three," she said.

"One was a tall man in a gray coat. One was shorter, thick through the shoulders, the kind of build that comes from labor rather than exercise. The third I saw only from behind." He paused. "The tall man I recognized. He is a customs official. I have seen him at the Sublime Porte."

"Do you know his name?"

"Arslan Bey. He is a second-level official in the customs registry. Not a prominent man, but one who controls significant paperwork." Halil tilted his head slightly. "I mention this only because I thought it odd that a customs official would use the service passage. Men of his standing generally prefer the main door."

Fatma finished the shoulder. She moved to refill the basin from the pipe.

"Thank you," she said. "For telling me."

"I am merely making conversation," Halil said. He looked at the dome above them, the light coming through the star-shaped skylights. "A beautiful building," he added. "It would be a pity if anything happened to its reputation."

He said nothing more specific. He did not need to.

Fatma wrung out the cloth and began again.

Chapter 11: The Schedule

She reconstructed the day from the booking ledger, which Mehmet Bey kept in his office and which Kadriye had access to for scheduling. Fatma asked to review it to reconcile the linen count — a legitimate enough request. Mehmet Bey waved her to it without looking up from his accounts.

She sat at the small desk outside his office and copied the entries for the previous Tuesday in careful, small script on a piece of paper she folded and put in her apron.

The morning appointments were seven through noon in thirty-minute intervals, with three private-chamber slots. Papadakis had the seven o'clock private chamber. The eight o'clock slot was listed under a name she knew: Arslan Bey, customs official. The eight-thirty slot was empty — rare; the hammam almost always ran full in the morning.

But Halil had said he saw three men leave through the service passage. Arslan Bey was a regular appointment. The other two were not in the ledger.

She went over it again. The ledger recorded paying customers, not visitors. A man could enter through the service passage without paying — if he had an arrangement with Cemil the porter, who managed the coal cellar door from the side street.

She thought about Cemil. He was a quiet man, twenty-six years old, from somewhere on the Black Sea coast. He had worked at the hammam for four years — the same length of time as Papadakis's weekly appointments. He did his job without complaint and ate his lunch alone.

She set the ledger back in its place and went down to the coal cellar.

The service passage connected the cellar to the side street through a wooden door. Cemil was sweeping the cellar floor. He looked up when she came in.

"Fatma Hanım."

"Just checking the coal supply," she said. "We've been running low on Fridays."

She checked the coal, made a note, and left. But while she was in the cellar she looked at the wooden door to the side street. The lock was a simple bolt. There were scrapes on the doorframe — fresh ones, pale wood showing through the dark paint — consistent with the door having been opened and closed more frequently than usual.

She went back upstairs. She did not speak to Cemil.

Chapter 12: The Nervous Bather

She knew Arslan Bey the way she knew most of the regular men — by his body and its peculiarities, which were the things that distinguished one man from another in the steam. He was perhaps fifty-five, lean, with a nervous energy that expressed itself in small movements: the leg that bounced when he sat, the way he checked his clothing three times before hanging it, the thumb that worried the edge of his towel.

He overtipped. Not dramatically, but consistently, and in a way that suggested the tip was not generosity but apology — he knew he was difficult, he compensated with money.

He came the week after Papadakis's death, on his usual Wednesday. Fatma attended him in the main hall. He was tenser than usual. He checked his clothing four times instead of three.

She said nothing unusual to him. She did her work. But she watched the way he sat on the bench after his bath, wrapped in his towel, looking at the wall. He was waiting for something. The wait had a specific quality — not patience but endurance.

Cemil appeared at the entrance to the changing alcove. He said nothing, but Arslan Bey saw him. Something passed between them — not money, not goods, just a look. Arslan Bey stood and went to the changing room.

Fatma carried a basin to the alcove adjacent and worked there for a few minutes. She could hear, through the wooden partition, the sound of Arslan Bey dressing. Then a smaller sound: something set on the wooden bench. Then, a moment later, the door opening and closing.

She waited until he had gone and went to the changing room. The bench was empty. But on the floor beneath it, behind the leg, she found a small leather strap — the kind used to bind a bundle. It had been cut, not untied.

She picked it up. It smelled of paper.

She put it in her apron and went to find Cemil.

Chapter 13: The Package

Cemil was not in the cellar. He was in the side passage, talking to a man through the open door to the street. They were speaking quietly. The man outside was not visible; she could see only Cemil's back, his posture deferential, listening.

She stepped back up the cellar stairs and waited. After three minutes Cemil came back through and closed the door. He did not see her. She watched him go to the corner of the cellar where the coal was stacked and reach behind the nearest pile. He withdrew a small square bundle, wrapped in brown paper.

He did not look at it. He simply put it inside his jacket and went back up the stairs.

Fatma stood in the cellar for a moment. The furnace breathed. She could hear footsteps above her on the marble floor.

She had not taken the package. She had not intercepted anything. She had simply observed. This distinction mattered to her — not because it would matter to anyone else, but because she needed to understand the exact nature of what she was doing.

She was not a police officer. She was not a judge. She was a tellak in a hammam in Istanbul in 1893, and the things she saw were the only tools she had.

She went back upstairs through the service door at the end of the corridor.

That evening she walked to the Spice Bazaar. She went slowly, as though she were shopping, stopping at stalls to handle cloth and ask prices she had no intention of paying. The bazaar was loud and warm, lit by oil lamps as the sun went down. The smell of spices — cinnamon, cumin, dried pepper — mixed with the smell of the harbor and the animals and the coal smoke from the city's thousand furnaces.

She found the textile warehouse on the second street in from the bazaar's eastern entrance. It was a building she had walked past perhaps a hundred times without noticing it. Nothing distinguished it from its neighbors except a small metal plate by the door, the kind that bore a company name. The name on the plate was in French.

She stopped and read it. Then she walked on.

Chapter 14: The Warehouse

The warehouse occupied the back half of a building whose street-facing portion was a fabric shop, legitimate enough — bolts of cotton, linen, a few imported woolens stacked on shelves behind glass. An old woman ran the shop and seemed to know nothing of what happened behind the interior door.

Fatma did not try the door. She walked past and found the alley that ran behind the building, where goods would be received. In the alley she found evidence of recent deliveries: fresh straw, the kind used to pack fragile goods, and the particular smell of ink and paper that she had associated, in the past, with printing offices.

She stood in the alley for a moment. A cat regarded her from a windowsill. Somewhere close by someone was hammering.

She thought about what Roksanda had told her. Ottoman state papers — deeds, certificates of debt. Documents that represented actual value: property rights, claims on state revenue, obligations of the government itself. In the hands of European investors, they would be worth something quite different than they were worth in the hands of the original holders. The original holders, perhaps, did not know they had been sold.

She walked back to the street. She had been in the bazaar area for an hour and her feet were beginning to hurt. She found a bench near a fountain and sat for a while, watching the water.

The warehouse belonged, she had learned from the French nameplate, to a company she could not read completely — the text was partly corroded — but she could make out the word Compagnie and a proper name that began with L.

This, she thought, was as far as she could go alone.

She needed Roksanda's return. She needed, perhaps, Halil Efendi's knowledge. She did not need to tell either of them about the other, not yet.

She went home. She lived above the hammam, in a room that overlooked the courtyard, small enough that her bed and her chair and her trunk filled it precisely. She sat on the bed and listened to the city settle into evening.

Chapter 15: Alliance

Roksanda came the following Friday, as Fatma had asked. She came to the women's private bath again, alone, and this time she brought bread and cheese wrapped in paper, which she set on the bench with the practical calm of a woman who understood that conversations of this kind required a certain amount of time and that time required sustenance.

Fatma told her about the warehouse. Roksanda listened without interrupting.

"Lindqvist," Roksanda said. "That would be Compagnie Lindqvist — a Swedish investment house with a Pera office. I know the name. They operate through local intermediaries and maintain a fiction of being purely a trading firm." She broke off a piece of bread and ate it. "They are buyers, not principals. The principals are in the palace."

"A name beginning with S," Fatma said. She did not say where she had heard this.

Roksanda looked at her. "Where did you hear that?"

"I am still gathering," Fatma said.

Roksanda accepted this with a nod. "I have been watching the warehouse for two weeks. The deliveries come on Tuesday and Thursday evenings, always through the alley. Small packages, well-sealed. One man receives them and a second man takes them to an address I haven't traced yet in Pera."

"What happens at the Pera address?"

"Presumably they go to Lindqvist, who then forwards them to buyers in Vienna or Amsterdam. The buyers are investors who think they are purchasing distressed Ottoman assets at low prices through a legitimate broker." Roksanda looked at her hands. "The assets are not legitimately obtained. The original holders were told the documents were being registered, not sold."

Fatma thought about this. "And the money?"

"The money goes back through the same chain, minus the commissions. A portion reaches the palace official who authorized the access to the registry. A portion goes to the customs official who provided the transport stamps. And the rest —" Roksanda paused. "The rest should have gone to Nikos. But he knew too much."

"So they stopped paying him by killing him," Fatma said.

"Yes."

The steam was thin now, the morning's heat almost used up. Fatma poured water from the basin over the bench. The warmth spread across the marble.

"I will help you," Fatma said. "But I want something in return."

"What?"

"The truth about whether there is a ledger and whether you have it."

Roksanda was still for a moment. Then she said: "There is a ledger. Nikos gave it to me three days before he died. He said it was too dangerous to keep in any one place. I have it."

"Why did you tell me you were looking for it?"

"Because I didn't know if I could trust you," Roksanda said simply. "Now I think perhaps I can."

Fatma looked at her for a long time. "Show me the ledger," she said.

Chapter 16: What the Ledger Contains

Roksanda did not produce the ledger immediately. She said she kept it in the place she was staying — a lodging house near the Sultanahmet mosque — and that she would bring it on her next visit.

In the meantime she told Fatma what was in it: three years of transactions, recorded in Papadakis's precise Greek script. Dates, document descriptions, buyer names (in abbreviated form), amounts paid. A column on the right-hand side listed initials, which Roksanda believed corresponded to the officials who had authorized each transaction.

"M.B.," Roksanda said. "Mehmet Bey. That appears consistently as a location marker — not a financial participant, but indicating the transaction passed through the hammam."

"How much did he receive?"

"A flat fee, monthly. Not large but consistent."

"And N.A.?"

Roksanda hesitated. "Nazmi. The inspector." She said it carefully. "Not as a participant in the actual fraud. But as — a guarantor of silence. A periodic payment."

Fatma nodded. She had suspected this, but hearing it confirmed was different from suspicion. She held the information carefully, turned it, set it down.

"Who is the palace official?" Fatma asked.

"The initials are S.P. I believe — I do not know for certain — that this is a man named Selim Paşa. A mid-level administrator who controls customs appointments." Roksanda looked at the wall. "He is not mentioned in the newspaper. He is not prominent. But he controls the paperwork, and paperwork in this city is the real currency."

Fatma had heard the name once, in a different context — from Halil Efendi, months ago, mentioned as an example of a man who had risen without appearing to rise. She filed this.

"The fixer," she said. "Fevzi."

"Fevzi handled the physical movement. He had two or three men who worked with him. He appears in the ledger as a line item — a large one."

"Is he dangerous?"

Roksanda looked at her with the particular expression of a woman who has encountered danger in several countries. "He has done dangerous things," she said. "Whether he is himself dangerous depends on what he is protecting."

Fatma stood and began to put the basin and cloths away. "I need to go to Pera," she said.

"Why?"

"I want to see the Lindqvist building. I want to count who enters."

Roksanda was quiet for a moment. "I'll come with you," she said.

"No," Fatma said. "You're known to these people — you're in the trade. I am a woman who scrubs backs for a living. No one will notice me standing on a street in Pera."

Chapter 17: The Religious Leader

Before going to Pera, Fatma paid a visit she had been postponing. She went on her day off, in the late morning, to the home of a woman named Şerife Hanım who led a small religious circle in the neighborhood near the Grand Bazaar.

Şerife Hanım was seventy, thin, and possessed of a stillness that was not peace but discipline — she had earned it, Fatma felt, the way a skill is earned. She received visitors in a room that smelled of rose water and old books.

Fatma explained the situation obliquely: a man had died in the hammam. The death had been ruled an accident. She believed it was not. She was trying to understand who had been using the hammam for improper purposes.

Şerife Hanım listened without expression. When Fatma finished, she said: "Why come to me?"

"Because you know this neighborhood and its people. Because you have known Çemberlitaş Hammam since before I worked there. And because —" Fatma paused. "Because I trust you not to repeat this to men who would find it useful."

Şerife Hanım smiled briefly — a smile that acknowledged the precision of this. "What do you want to know?"

"Whether the hammam has been used as a meeting point for any transaction you know to be irregular."

Şerife Hanım looked at the ceiling. "A woman who came to my circle — I will not say her name — has a husband who is a lower-level functionary in the customs registry. Six months ago she told me he had come home very agitated, saying that men from above had asked him to facilitate something he didn't want to facilitate. He was told it was a small thing — a matter of paperwork."

"Did he refuse?"

"He could not afford to." Şerife Hanım looked at her hands, which were folded in her lap. "A month later he was given a new office and a small increase in his pay. He did not speak of it again." She looked at Fatma. "The hammam's name was mentioned once. In the context of where certain meetings were coordinated."

Fatma absorbed this. "Thank you," she said.

"Be careful," Şerife Hanım said. "These things do not end well for the person who pulls the thread."

"I am not pulling the thread," Fatma said. "I am learning where it goes."

Şerife Hanım looked at her for a moment. "That distinction may not matter to the people holding the thread."

Chapter 18: Nazmi in the Steam

Inspector Nazmi came to bathe on a Wednesday, ten days after the death. He came in plain clothes, not his uniform — civilian trousers and a coat, the hat of a minor functionary rather than a police officer. He paid at the door without announcing himself and was assigned to the main hall.

Kadriye came to find Fatma immediately. "The inspector," she said. "Main hall."

Fatma considered this for a moment. "I'll take him."

"Fatma Hanım —"

"I'll take him," Fatma said.

She took the basin and the soap cloth and went to the main hall. Nazmi was already seated on the marble bench, wrapped in his towel, looking at the dome. He was alone in this section — the hammam had emptied somewhat for the noon hour.

"Inspector," Fatma said.

He turned. He had the look of a man who had not slept well. The careful eyes were bloodshot at the edges.

"Fatma Hanım." A pause. "I came for a bath, not an interrogation."

"Of course," she said, and began to work.

She said nothing for a while. She was good at silence — it was one of her skills, the ability to extend quiet without making it uncomfortable. Nazmi sat with his shoulders tight. Gradually the steam began to work on him, the way it worked on all men eventually, loosening the parts they held stiff.

"The Papadakis family has made inquiries," he said, after a while. Not to her specifically — to the steam.

"I see," Fatma said.

"The widow. She hired a lawyer." He paused. "The lawyers of wealthy Greek families in this city are persistent."

"I imagine they are."

He turned his head slightly. "Did the widow contact you?"

"No," Fatma said. "I gave my statement to your constable. I said what I saw."

"Which was?"

"I found the body," Fatma said. "As I told the constable."

Nazmi looked at the wall. She could not see his expression.

"The ruling stands," he said. "Accidental drowning. The medical examiner agreed." A pause. "There is nothing more to investigate."

"I am sure you are right," Fatma said.

She worked in silence for another few minutes. When she moved around to his front to hand him the basin for rinsing, she could see his face. He was frightened. Not of her — she was not a threat in any category he recognized. He was frightened of something else, something that had preceded his arrival here and would follow him home.

He left a good tip. She did not comment on it.

Chapter 19: The European Name

It was Kadriye who told her, which Fatma had not expected.

She came to the linen room three days after Nazmi's visit and stood in the doorway in the same way as before. But this time her expression was different — less resolved, more unsettled.

"I need to tell you something," Kadriye said. "And I want you to understand that I am telling you because I cannot not tell you, not because I have decided to be involved."

"I understand," Fatma said.

Kadriye came in and sat on the stool. "Three days ago. I was outside Mehmet Bey's office — I went to return a key. His door was not fully closed. He was on the telephone."

The hammam had acquired a telephone connection the previous year — a matter of some pride for Mehmet Bey, who had mentioned it several times in the year of its installation. He used it rarely.

"He spoke a name twice," Kadriye said. "I didn't understand the context. But the name was Lindqvist."

Fatma set down the towel she was folding. "Lindqvist."

"Yes. He said it in the middle of a sentence — 'if Lindqvist expects —' and then something I couldn't hear. And then later, 'tell Lindqvist the arrangement holds.'"

Fatma looked at her. Kadriye's jaw was set. She had made a decision and it had cost her something.

"Thank you," Fatma said.

"Please don't get me killed," Kadriye said, without any particular drama. She stood up. "And please don't let Mehmet Bey know I heard this."

"He won't hear it from me."

After Kadriye left, Fatma sat with this for a while. The telephone call confirmed what Roksanda had suggested: Mehmet Bey was not simply a passive recipient of payments. He was in active communication with the European end of the network.

The arrangement holds. That meant the network was continuing. That meant someone was still using the hammam, or planning to.

She went back to work, and she thought about Pera.

Chapter 20: The Building in Pera

She went on her next afternoon off. She dressed in her better clothes — not fine, but respectable, the clothes of a tradesman's widow, which is what she was. She took the ferry across the Golden Horn and walked up the hill from Karaköy into Pera, which was the part of Istanbul that had decided to be European and had done so with a thoroughness that always faintly unsettled her.

The streets here were wider. The buildings were taller, with the ornate facades that European architecture applied to even its commercial premises. Banks, hotels, telegraph offices, the French hospital, and between them the trading firms and investment houses that had proliferated since the Tanzimat reforms opened Ottoman markets to foreign capital.

Lindqvist Compagnie was on a side street off the main avenue. It was a modest building — two stories, stone-faced, with a small brass plate beside the door. No display window, no advertisement. The kind of firm that did not need to attract walk-in business.

Fatma walked past it on the opposite side of the street. She found a bench near a street vendor and sat and watched.

In the hour she sat there, four men entered and two left. She could not hear any of the conversations and could not see past the frosted glass of the ground-floor windows. The men who entered were unremarkable in dress — the clothing of the commercial class, neither wealthy nor poor, the uniform of men who handle other people's money.

But one of the men who entered, arriving at the building from the direction of the harbor, carried a small square package under his arm, wrapped in brown paper and bound with a leather strap.

The same leather strap, she thought, as the one she had found cut under the bench in the hammam's changing room.

She watched him go inside. She watched the building.

After another thirty minutes she walked back down the hill to the ferry. She had a name and an address. She had a connection between the hammam and the Pera office. She did not yet know who had given the order to drown Nikos Papadakis in a steaming pool, but she could see, now, the shape of the room he had been in.

Chapter 21: What Roksanda Explained

They met on a Friday, as before. Roksanda brought the ledger this time — a slim notebook, its covers dark with handling, the pages filled with a close Greek script that Fatma could not read but that Roksanda translated in a low voice, running her finger down the columns.

The pattern was clear once you could see it. Three years of entries. A transaction every two to three weeks, sometimes more frequently. The amounts varied — some were small, the transfer of a single deed; others were substantial, representing bundles of documents. The totals in the right column were in Ottoman kurus and in francs.

"The exchange rate," Roksanda said, "was unfavorable to the original holders. The documents were bought at distressed prices — the palace official represented them as worthless surplus, things the state was clearing from its registry. The European buyers paid what the official asked, believing they were getting a bargain. The original holders were never informed."

"And the original holders were?"

"Small landowners, mostly. A few merchants who had lodged claims with the state. People who had gone to the registry to document what they owned and had, without knowing it, signed their documentation into a chain that ended in Amsterdam." Roksanda turned a page. "Some of these claims are for property that no longer exists. But others are for property that does, and a European investor holding a registered claim on Ottoman land is in a very different legal position than a small landholder in Anatolia."

Fatma absorbed this. "And the palace connection ensured the claims were valid? That the registry stamps were genuine?"

"Yes. Selim Paşa controls the appointments of the registrars. He also controls the archive clerks. He could arrange for documents to be recorded legitimately and then transferred without the original holder's knowledge — using the original holder's own filing as authorization."

Fatma looked at the ledger. "Three years," she said.

"Three years. And the operation was expanding. Nikos told me, before he died, that they were moving into a new category — not just historical property claims but active concession documents. Mining rights. Water rights. These are worth far more and far harder to recover once transferred."

"Which is why he was killed," Fatma said. "He understood where it was going."

"He understood that once they began moving concession documents, there was no clean exit. The scale would attract attention eventually, and when it did, everyone in the chain would be investigated." She paused. "He wanted to stop before that happened. They decided to stop him instead."

Fatma looked at the ledger. "M.B.," she said.

"Yes."

"N.A."

"Yes."

She closed the ledger. She did not take it. "Keep this," she said. "Somewhere safe."

"I've kept it safe for two weeks."

"Keep it safer."

Chapter 22: The Hand-Off Point

She went back to the coal cellar on her next early morning, before Vasil arrived. She brought a chisel from the tool cupboard — a short one, used for maintenance — and a lamp.

The section of newer mortar was where she had found it. She pressed the chisel's flat edge against the mortar at the top of the section and worked it gently. The mortar crumbled. It was new enough that it had not fully cured.

Behind the brickwork, which came away in a single hinged section — a panel, she realized, not a wall — was a passage. Not large: a person would have to crouch. But it ran inward, toward the private chamber, and it had been used recently. The dirt on the floor was disturbed. She could see footprints, partial, in the dust.

She did not go in. She replaced the panel carefully, repacked the mortar with a rag dipped in water and gray dust from the floor, and left the cellar.

She understood now how it had been done. A man could enter from the side street through Cemil's door, descend to the cellar, and pass through this passage to emerge below the private chamber's drainage aperture. The drainage aperture — the one Nazmi had briefly examined — could be reached from below. The cover could be removed from the underside.

What this meant: the man in the private chamber was not alone when he died. Someone had come up from below, through the drainage aperture, into the pool. Had held him under. Had gone back down the same way. Had sealed the aperture from below and retreated through the passage.

The hands on the stone lip were not a man holding on. They were a man being held down, pushing up against a weight above him with the last strength he had.

She stood in the corridor outside the private chamber and looked at the door. The door had been ajar when she passed. Someone inside, finishing the work, had pulled it almost closed. The hands she had seen were in the last seconds of his life.

Three seconds. She had stood in the corridor for three seconds.

Chapter 23: Mehmet Bey's Fear

She went to him obliquely, as Roksanda had approached her. She knocked on his office door at midday, when the hammam was between the morning and afternoon rushes and she could be sure of twenty minutes.

Mehmet Bey was at his desk, going through papers. He looked up without enthusiasm.

"Fatma Hanım."

She sat down in the chair across the desk, which he had not invited her to do but which she did anyway. He registered this and said nothing.

"I want to speak with you about the private chamber," she said.

"The chamber has been in use since the week after the incident. I saw no need to close it indefinitely."

"I'm not speaking about the current bookings. I am speaking about its previous use."

Mehmet Bey set down his pen. He looked at her carefully. "Fatma Hanım, I do not know what you think you have learned —"

"I don't think," Fatma said. "I know. I know about the passage below. I know what the chamber was used for in addition to bathing. I know your name appears in a document that records payments made to individuals who facilitated the use of this building."

His face went through several expressions in rapid succession and then settled into something like exhaustion.

"You are a woman," he said, not as an insult but as a statement of what he believed was the relevant fact. "You have no standing to bring this to any court. Any magistrate in this city will take my word over yours."

"I know this," Fatma said.

He looked at her. "Then what do you want?"

"I want to know who you spoke to when you called Lindqvist," she said.

He was quiet for a long time. The sounds of the hammam came through the floor — water, the distant voices of clients, the slow breath of the furnace.

"There is a man in Lindqvist's office," he said finally. "A coordinator. I spoke to him twice. I was told to say that normal operations would continue, that there was no disruption."

"After Papadakis was killed."

"After the accident," he said, and then stopped, because he could hear how it sounded.

"Did you know he was going to be killed?"

A pause. "I was told the situation with Papadakis would be resolved."

"Resolved," Fatma said.

He had nothing to add to this.

Fatma stood. "I am not going to the magistrate today," she said. "I want you to think carefully about that word 'today.'"

She left.

Chapter 24: The Stoker's Knowledge

Vasil was sixty years old, Croatian, and had worked in the hammam's coal cellar for twenty-one years. He was a man of few words — not from shyness but from an economy of expression that came with age and physical work. He knew the building the way Fatma knew it: through its sounds, its temperatures, its daily rhythm.

She found him banking the furnace one afternoon and sat on the coal chest nearby. He glanced at her and continued working.

"The drainage in the private chamber was altered," she said. "When?"

He didn't stop working. "Six months ago," he said.

"Who did it?"

"Two men from outside. They came with Mehmet Bey's instruction. Plumbers, he said."

"You don't think they were plumbers."

Vasil shrugged. "Plumbers know how to fix drains. They know how to modify them." He tamped down the coal. "These men modified the drain cover so it could be removed from below. They also modified the access panel in the basement wall." He paused. "I didn't say anything because Mehmet Bey told me not to, and because I have worked here twenty-one years and don't want to lose that."

"I understand," Fatma said.

"I don't know what was done in the chamber," he said. "I don't want to know."

"You know a man died there."

"Men die in hammams," he said. "It happens. Old hearts, the heat." He looked at her for the first time. "But the drain being what it was — that was not for any legitimate purpose I can think of."

Fatma nodded. "Was the panel opened on the morning of the death?"

He looked back at the furnace. "I was not in the cellar that morning until half past eight. I can't say what happened before I arrived."

"And when you arrived?"

"The panel was closed. But the mortar at the base was cracked. Recently cracked." He was quiet for a moment. "I re-mortared it a week later. I thought perhaps the weight of the coal pile had shifted it."

She thanked him and left.

Chapter 25: The Hatch

She went back to the passage. This time she went in.

She brought the lamp and the chisel and an old cloth tied over her working dress. The passage was as she had guessed — low, requiring her to crouch, lined with the old brick of the building's foundation. The floor was packed earth. It was perhaps five meters long.

At the far end was a ladder of iron rungs set into the wall, rising to a wooden hatch above. She climbed it carefully, testing each rung. The hatch was heavy. She pushed it up and found it resisted — and then gave, swinging back on a counterweighted hinge. Someone had engineered this carefully.

Above the hatch was a small stone cavity, and above that, visible through an iron grate, the underside of the private chamber's pool. The drainage aperture she had examined from above. From here she could see the mechanism: the grate was not fixed but removable, set in a shallow groove. A person below could lift it, set it aside, and — in a steaming chamber where visibility was already poor — enter the pool from beneath without anyone above knowing.

She looked at the grate for a long time. The stone around it was pale marble, worn smooth. She could see, on the underside of the lip where Papadakis had gripped, a faint discoloration — the mark left by the wax she had found above. Wax from a seal, pressed against the stone.

She lowered the hatch carefully, climbed down, replaced the access panel, and went upstairs.

She knew now exactly how it had been done. She had no way to prove it that would satisfy a court, but she understood it completely. This was a different kind of knowing — the kind a woman in her position could have.

She thought: knowing is not nothing. Knowing is in fact quite a lot.

Chapter 26: Fevzi

Roksanda brought the name on the following Friday, as though she had been holding it for exactly this moment.

"Fevzi Çelik," she said. "He is known in the trade — not in the legitimate trade, but the other one. He coordinates physical movement of goods and sometimes other things. He is Bulgarian by birth, Turkish by function. He has worked in Istanbul for twelve years."

"What does he look like?"

"Thick through the shoulders. Forty years old perhaps. He dresses like a minor merchant but carries himself like a man who has used his body professionally." She paused. "He is careful but not cautious. There is a difference."

Fatma thought about the figure Halil had described leaving through the service passage: thick through the shoulders. Not the kind of build that comes from exercise.

"You said you knew him through the textile trade."

"He moves goods for traders who need to avoid customs inspection. He has done work for me — legitimate work, I should say, goods I needed to move quickly without paperwork delays." Roksanda looked at the steam rising from the pool. "I did not know about the other things he did until recently."

"You knew about the documents."

"I knew about Nikos's involvement. I didn't know the full scope." A pause. "Nikos told me about Fevzi three months ago. He said Fevzi had started asking questions about the ledger. Nikos didn't confirm or deny its existence."

"But Fevzi suspected."

"Yes. Nikos thought that was why the timeline accelerated. They needed to act before Nikos could use the ledger as leverage."

Fatma thought about the three men Halil had seen leaving through the service passage. Arslan Bey. Fevzi. And the third, seen only from behind.

"The third man," she said.

Roksanda looked at her. "What third man?"

Fatma explained about Halil's account. Roksanda was quiet, then said: "The palace connection. Selim Paşa, or a representative."

"A representative," Fatma said. She thought about what Halil had said — that the third man had been seen only from behind, that he had been the first to leave.

"A man who came to verify," Fatma said.

"And then left before it was finished," Roksanda agreed.

Chapter 27: Fevzi's Location

She went to the Grand Bazaar. She did not go often — the bazaar was the kind of place where you could spend an entire afternoon and accomplish nothing, and she had always approached it with a list and a purpose. Today she went without a list.

She found the rug merchant after twenty minutes of deliberate wandering. He was a man named Kemal who had sold rugs in the same spot for thirty years, and who knew — because a bazaar merchant worth his place knows everyone in adjacent trades — everyone who moved goods in and out of the bazaar district.

She did not ask directly about Fevzi. She asked about traffic near the Spice Bazaar, whether certain warehouses had been busy. She mentioned hearing that there was a man who handled quick shipping, not wanting to leave the city immediately. She described the build, the manner.

Kemal looked at his rugs for a moment. "There is a man who sometimes takes a table at the coffee stall by the eastern gate," he said. "I don't know his name. He is there most mornings."

"When does he arrive?"

"Early. Before the crowds."

"Thank you," Fatma said.

She went to the coffee stall the next morning. She arrived early, took a glass of tea, and sat where she could see the gate.

Fevzi arrived at eight. She recognized him from Roksanda's description — thick-shouldered, a minor-merchant air, the posture of a man who takes up space deliberately. He ordered coffee without looking around and sat with his back to the wall.

She watched him for twenty minutes. He spoke to no one. He drank his coffee. He read a newspaper. He left at eight twenty-five.

She made no move toward him. She simply confirmed his existence, the daily routine, the way he occupied a chair. She filed this.

She did not approach him. She was not ready for that and was not sure she would ever be.

Chapter 28: The Warning

Inspector Nazmi's summons came on a Tuesday. A constable appeared at the hammam's main entrance and asked for Fatma Hanım. Kadriye came to find her in the women's section.

"Police," Kadriye said.

"I know," Fatma said.

She went with the constable to the police post, which was three streets away. She had been here once before, to give her initial statement. The post smelled of tobacco and damp wool. The constable showed her to a chair in a corridor and left her to wait.

Nazmi appeared after fifteen minutes. He did not take her to an interview room — he stood in the corridor, which suggested either informality or the desire not to have the conversation recorded.

"You've been asking questions," he said. He did not sound accusatory. He sounded tired.

"What questions?"

"Around the bazaar. In the Greek quarter." He looked at the wall. "Someone noticed."

"Someone told you," Fatma said.

He looked at her then. "Someone told someone who told someone who told me." He paused. "I am not here to charge you with anything. You have done nothing illegal."

"Then why am I here?"

"Because I am telling you to stop." He said it without emphasis, not as a threat but as a statement of fact. "Whatever you think you know, whatever you are looking for — stop."

Fatma looked at him. His face was tired and the eyes were still bloodshot at the edges. He was a man in the middle of something too large, trying to manage it without being crushed.

"Who told you?" she said.

He didn't answer.

"The man who told you," Fatma said, "is he the same man who told you to rule the death accidental?"

A long pause. Nazmi looked at his shoes. "Go home, Fatma Hanım," he said. "Go back to your work. Leave this alone."

She stood. "Thank you for the warning," she said. And she meant it — not ironically. The warning itself was information.

She walked home and thought about what Nazmi's fear specified. He was afraid of the case being reopened. That meant it was possible for it to be reopened. That meant there was someone who could do it.

Chapter 29: What Fear Knows

On the walk back from the police post she thought about Nazmi.

He was not a bad man, probably. He was a man who had been placed in a position where his choices were all costly, and he had chosen the least costly one available to him. He had accepted payments — possibly not money, possibly just the assurance that his career would continue — and in return he had filed a report he knew to be false.

This was not uncommon. She had lived in this city long enough to understand that the city ran, in part, on the arrangement of such accommodations. The difference between a corrupt official and an honest one was often simply the size of the number that had been offered.

But Nazmi's warning told her more than he intended. He had said: whatever you think you know, stop. This meant he believed she knew something. If she had known nothing, the warning would have been different — he would have been gently dismissive, the way officials were dismissive of women who made claims. Instead he had treated her as a person with information.

And he had not arrested her. He had not threatened her. He had warned her.

A man who had been fully purchased would not warn. He would suppress. The warning suggested that some part of Nazmi was not entirely comfortable with what he had done — that his investment in the arrangement had limits.

She thought: if the case were reopened by someone above Nazmi's level, he would comply with the new investigation. He would not protect the network to the point of his own arrest.

She thought: who could reopen it?

She had no answer to this yet. But she noted the question, which was itself a kind of progress.

Chapter 30: The Funeral

The funeral was for a minor merchant named Bekir Efendi, who had run a small shop selling hardware near the Grand Bazaar and who had died of a stomach complaint at sixty-eight — an unremarkable death, the kind the city absorbed without flinching. He had been a client of the hammam for perhaps twenty years, one of those reliable presences whose absence was noted only gradually.

Fatma attended. It was customary — Mehmet Bey sent someone from the hammam to the funerals of long-standing clients. She wore black and stood among the mosque's outer courtyard with the other mourners, who were mostly the merchant community from the bazaar district.

She was looking for Arslan Bey. She had calculated that he would attend — the bazaar community was small enough that deaths were social occasions for everyone connected to it, and Arslan Bey moved in its circles. She was right.

He was standing near the gate, speaking to a man she did not recognize. The stranger was European in dress — not Ottoman, not Greek or Armenian, but the particular cut of northern European commerce. He was perhaps fifty, with pale coloring and a bearing that suggested someone more accustomed to offices than streets. He and Arslan Bey were deep in conversation, their heads close, the conversation rapid and quiet.

She maneuvered herself closer. She caught fragments — not enough to understand, but enough to hear the cadence and one word repeated: a name, spoken by Arslan Bey, twice. The name was not Lindqvist. It was a first name — Lars.

Lars was a Scandinavian name.

She filed this too: Arslan Bey knew the Lindqvist representative well enough to use his first name at a public gathering.

When the prayers began, the two men separated. The European left before the cortege assembled, which was itself notable — it meant he did not care whether his departure was observed. Men in legitimate business stay for the prayers.

She told Halil about this afterward, when they met on his next visit to the hammam. Halil identified the man from her description without hesitation: Lars Helgren, a representative of Lindqvist Compagnie, who had been in Istanbul for two years and was known to the business community in Pera as a negotiator.

Chapter 31: The Ledger

Roksanda brought the ledger on a Friday in early autumn. She had been carrying it in the lining of her traveling coat, and she put it on the bench of the private bath with the decisive care of someone relinquishing something that has weighed on them.

Fatma looked at it. She picked it up.

The cover was worn dark leather. The pages were dense with Papadakis's hand — small, consistent Greek script, the letters close together as though he had been conserving paper. She turned the pages slowly, looking at the structure even if she could not read the language. The columns were clear. Dates down the left. Document descriptions in the middle. Amounts in two currencies. Initials on the right.

"Show me the initials," Fatma said.

Roksanda moved beside her and pointed. M.B., recurring. N.A., less frequently. A.B. — for Arslan Bey, Roksanda confirmed. F.Ç. — Fevzi Çelik, appearing as a line item in the expenses column, paid consistently. And at the top of each year's section, in a different ink, S.P.

"He wrote it in a different ink," Fatma said.

"He added it later. After he understood the palace connection." Roksanda looked at the page. "He was afraid to write it in full. But he needed it there in case."

"In case of what?"

"In case he died and someone found this." She paused. "In case the ledger was the only testimony left."

Fatma closed the ledger. She sat with it for a long time.

The private chamber was quiet. The morning's heat was beginning to ease. Through the high skylight the light was white and still.

She thought: this is evidence. Not the kind that a court requires — she did not know how Greek shorthand would be treated as testimony, or how Roksanda would explain her possession of it — but it was the kind of evidence that could move things. It could be shown. It could be reproduced. It could be used to pressure the men whose initials appeared in it.

She thought: I am forty-two years old and I have no power in this city. What I have is this, and the knowledge of how a man was killed, and the names.

She set the ledger down. "Keep it," she said. "But I want to tell you something I intend to do with part of what I know."

Chapter 32: The Night Vigil

She sat with the information for a full night. She did not sleep. She sat in her room above the hammam with a candle and the paper on which she had copied the schedule and the other small notes she had gathered — the leather strap, the description of the package at Pera, the mortar sample from the access panel that she had kept in a cloth.

She listed what she knew: the method of the killing (the passage, the pool, the drainage aperture). The participants: Arslan Bey, Fevzi, the third man (unidentified, possibly Selim Paşa's representative). The infrastructure: the warehouse, the Pera office, Lindqvist and Lars Helgren, Cemil's role as the portal. The corruption: Mehmet Bey's payments, Nazmi's silencing.

She listed what she did not know: who exactly had held Papadakis under the water. Whether the third man was Selim Paşa himself or a surrogate. Whether Nazmi's corruption was reversible. Whether the network was still actively running or had paused after the death.

She listed what she could do. The list was shorter than the things she knew but longer than she had expected at the beginning.

She could give the information to a newspaper. This carried risk — the newspaper might be suppressed, or might publish something that destroyed the evidence chain and warned the network.

She could give it to a lawyer representing the Papadakis family. This was more structured but required trusting a lawyer she did not know.

She could give it to Halil Efendi and ask him to use his knowledge of palace politics to determine whether there was a faction that would benefit from exposing Selim Paşa.

She could do all three, in sequence, watching what each action produced before taking the next.

She blew out the candle as the dawn call came. She had not decided, but she had mapped the terrain. This was enough for now.

She went to start the furnace.

Chapter 33: Options

She did not sleep that night. She slept the following night instead, a long dreamless sleep that left her feeling scraped clean.

She had made a provisional decision: Halil first.

Halil Efendi came on Saturday. She waited until the main hall had emptied somewhat — the midmorning rush passing toward noon — and then she went to where he sat on the heated bench and said, quietly: "I need to speak with you privately. Not here."

He looked at her with his steady, unreadable gaze. "Walk with me to the gate when I leave," he said. "I will be ready in twenty minutes."

She walked with him to the hammam's main gate. The street was busy — a cart, two porters with loads on their backs, a group of students from the nearby school. Halil walked slowly, as old men do when they have nowhere particular to be.

"I have information about the Papadakis death," she said. "Specific information. I am trying to determine whether there is an official who could use it — within the government, not against it."

Halil was quiet for a moment. "You are asking whether there is a reformist faction investigating Selim Paşa."

She hadn't said the name. She kept her face still.

"You know about him," she said.

"I worked in the palace for twenty-three years," Halil said. "I know about most people worth knowing about." He paused. "Yes. There is such a faction. It is led by a magistrate — a senior one, not a young reformer — who has been building a case against the customs administration for eighteen months. He lacks documentary evidence."

"He would not lack it if someone brought it to him."

"No." Halil stopped at the gate. He put on his hat with the precise care of a man who has always put on his own hat. "If someone were to bring documentary evidence to this magistrate, it would need to be someone who could testify to its provenance. A lawyer, ideally, or a credible witness." He looked at her. "A hotel proprietress. A foreign national with business interests. Not —" He stopped.

"Not a tellak," Fatma said.

"The magistrate would need corroboration."

"He would have it," she said. "Through a Bulgarian textile trader with standing in her own country."

Halil nodded slowly. "I see." He looked at the street. "Tell me when you are ready. I will provide the introduction."

Chapter 34: Kadriye's Anger

Kadriye found out about the ledger the way Kadriye found out about everything: by being more observant than people expected.

She had seen Roksanda bring something in a coat lining. She had noted when Fatma came back from the private bath looking different — not excited, not frightened, but settled, the way a person looks when they have decided something. She had waited two days and then come to the linen room again.

"You have the ledger," she said.

"Roksanda has it."

"But you've seen it."

"Yes."

Kadriye sat on the stool. She looked at Fatma with an expression that had passed through several stages of emotion and arrived at a kind of exhausted clarity. "I told you I didn't want to be involved."

"You're not involved."

"I told you about the telephone call. I told you the name Lindqvist. That makes me involved, Fatma Hanım. Whatever you are planning, whatever magistrate or newspaper you are going to —"

"I haven't gone yet."

"When you do, I am in the chain. Any good investigator will find me." She pressed her hands flat on her thighs. "I have a mother in Bursa. I send money home. I cannot lose this position."

Fatma set down what she was folding. "Kadriye," she said.

"Don't tell me it will be fine."

"I was not going to say that." Fatma sat in the chair across from her. "You told me about Lindqvist because you couldn't not tell me. That is a true thing. But your name will not appear in anything I bring to anyone. You are not a witness, you are not a source. If anyone asks you, you heard nothing."

"What if they ask me under oath?"

Fatma was quiet for a moment. This was the real question, and it deserved an honest answer.

"I don't know," she said. "I will try to make it so that no one asks you. But I cannot promise that."

Kadriye looked at her for a long time. Then she stood. She went to the door and stopped. "If you get me fired," she said, "I will never forgive you."

"I know," Fatma said.

Chapter 35: Fevzi's Death

She heard about it from Halil, who had heard it from a former colleague in the city administration, who had read the constable's report.

Fevzi Çelik was found dead in an alley near the Spice Bazaar on a Thursday morning. He had been stabbed — three times, the report said, in a way that suggested professional intent rather than an altercation. His wallet was missing, which was why the constable's initial report cited robbery.

Halil told her this on his Saturday visit, sitting on the heated bench with his hands folded, looking at the dome.

"Robbery," he said, in a tone that did not suggest he believed it.

"No," Fatma said.

"The timing is notable," Halil said. "Three days after a customs official failed to appear for his regular appointments."

Arslan Bey had missed his Wednesday appointment. She had noticed his absence before hearing about Fevzi.

"They are cleaning," she said.

"It would appear so." Halil paused. "Which suggests a certain degree of panic in the network. Either someone has alerted them to an investigation, or they have identified a vulnerability and are moving to close it."

Fatma thought about Nazmi's warning. About who had told whom. "The inspector," she said.

"Possibly. A man in Nazmi's position would be highly motivated to prevent an investigation that would expose him." Halil looked at her. "Or the European end. Lindqvist has interests that extend far beyond this particular operation. They may have decided to cut their losses."

"Fevzi was a liability because he was the physical connection."

"Yes. The documents leave no fingerprints. The physical act of delivering them does." He was quiet for a moment. "You should move quickly now."

"I know," Fatma said.

She had already sent a message to Roksanda through the lodging house. She had also gone back to her room and moved the few things that mattered — the cloth with the mortar sample, the copied schedule — to Şerife Hanım's house for safekeeping.

Chapter 36: Acceleration

The death of Fevzi changed the mathematics. Before, she had been assembling information toward an eventual reckoning — something that could happen in weeks or months. Now the network was actively contracting, burning away the parts that could testify against it.

She went to Halil Efendi's house for the first time. She had not been there before; he had never invited her and she had never presumed. But she sent a note with a bakery boy and he replied by the same route: come at three.

The house near the Hippodrome was old stone, three stories, with a wooden bay window on the second floor. Halil received her in a ground-floor room that was, as he had said, full of books. The cat observed her from the top of the highest shelf.

She told him what she had: the method of the killing, the access passage, the ledger summary Roksanda had shared. She told him about the wax mark and the drainage aperture and what Vasil had told her about the plumbers.

Halil listened without interrupting. When she finished, he said: "The magistrate's name is Kemal Paşa. Not the same Kemal as the rug merchant — a different man. He is senior enough to reopen a criminal investigation and junior enough not to be managed by Selim Paşa's faction." He paused. "He has been building a case and needs a precipitating event — a visible piece of evidence that allows him to move without looking political."

"The ledger," Fatma said.

"The ledger would be ideal. But it needs to arrive through a credible channel."

"A foreign national," Fatma said, remembering their conversation at the gate. "Roksanda."

"A Bulgarian textile trader who can testify that the ledger was given to her by the deceased, and that the deceased told her it recorded criminal transactions." He looked at her. "This is the chain. Your name does not need to appear in it."

"My name will appear in it," Fatma said. "I am the one who saw the hands."

Halil looked at her for a moment. "Yes," he said. "I suppose it will."

Chapter 37: The Safe Place

She asked Halil to hold something.

She brought it in her apron — the cloth with the mortar sample, the copied schedule, the leather strap from under the changing bench. Small things, individually inconclusive. Together they placed a person at the scene and connected the physical facts to the testimony she intended to give.

Halil took them without ceremony. He placed them on the table beside his chair and looked at them, then at her.

"You should know," he said, "that someone has been asking about you."

"Who?"

"A man who came to the coffee house I frequent near the Hippodrome. He described a woman of your age, your position. He asked whether I knew her." He paused. "I said I did not."

"Describe him."

"Thick-shouldered. Forty years old perhaps." He paused. "Not Fevzi — Fevzi is dead. But the same type."

They had sent someone to watch her. Not to act — not yet. But to locate, to assess.

"I am going to move quickly," she said. "I am going to see the newspaper editor first. Not to give him the ledger — to tell him what to look for if something happens to me."

"Insurance," Halil said.

"Insurance."

She stood to go. Halil looked at the objects on the table.

"Be careful of the hammam's roof at night," he said. It was the same thing he had said before. She had not understood it then. Now she thought she did.

"I will stay below," she said.

She returned to the hammam as the afternoon faded. In the main hall she could hear the normal sounds of the building — water, voices, the furnace beneath. Kadriye was at the desk. She looked up when Fatma came in, looked at her face, and said nothing.

Chapter 38: The Search

She came back to the hammam after a long errand and found her room disturbed.

Nothing was taken. She knew her room precisely — she had lived in it for eight years and the position of each object was fixed. Her trunk was not where she had left it. The papers she kept in the drawer beside her bed had been shuffled. The small envelope where she kept her saved money was intact but had been moved a centimeter from where she kept it.

A professional search, or the attempt at one — someone who knew how to look through a room without destroying it, but who had not accounted for the fact that she was the kind of person who notices a centimeter.

She stood in the middle of the room and took stock. The mortar sample was gone — but she had already given that to Halil. The copied schedule was gone — also with Halil. Whatever the searcher had expected to find, they had not found it here.

She sat on the bed and thought about what this meant. They knew she had been gathering. They did not know what she had found. The search was a probe — an attempt to discover the extent of her knowledge before deciding what to do about her.

Kadriye appeared in the doorway. She looked at the room and then at Fatma.

"Was anything taken?" Kadriye asked.

"No."

Kadriye came in and closed the door. She sat on the trunk. Her face was very still. "You need to finish this quickly," she said.

"I know."

"They are watching the hammam. I saw a man in the street this morning, standing still for too long. I have seen him before — after the death."

"I know," Fatma said.

"What are you waiting for?"

"I am not waiting. I am sequencing."

Kadriye looked at her. "Then sequence faster," she said.

Chapter 39: The Newspaper

The editor of a Greek-language newspaper in Istanbul was named Alexandros. He was perhaps fifty, with the particular exhaustion of a man who had published inconvenient truths in a city that did not always welcome them and had survived so far through a combination of care and luck.

Roksanda had arranged the meeting. She knew him through the Greek community's commercial networks. They met at a coffeehouse in Pera, which was neutral enough ground.

Fatma told him the facts without numbers. She told him about the death, the ruled accident, the method she believed had been used, and the nature of the network. She did not give him the ledger. She gave him the structure.

Alexandros listened. He asked precise questions — about the customs official's name (she gave it), about the European company (she gave that), about whether she had physical documentation. She said there was documentation she was not yet ready to provide.

"What do you want from me?" he said.

"I want you to know enough that if something happens to me, you can tell someone where to look," she said.

He looked at her for a long moment. "You understand that I cannot publish on the basis of what you've told me today," he said. "Without documentation, without a source I can name, this is an allegation."

"I understand that," Fatma said. "I am not asking you to publish. I am asking you to remember."

He considered this. He was a careful man — not timid, but careful in the way of someone who had learned to distinguish between courage and recklessness.

"I will remember," he said. "And I will say this: if what you describe is true, the Papadakis family has retained a lawyer who has been trying to reopen the inquiry. The lawyer's name is Konstantinos. He is tenacious."

Fatma noted this. "Thank you," she said.

Roksanda was quiet on the walk back. Then she said: "Are you ready to give the ledger to the magistrate?"

"Soon," Fatma said. "One more step."

Chapter 40: Absence

Arslan Bey did not come the following Wednesday or the Wednesday after that.

This was significant. He had maintained his weekly appointment for three years without interruption. Even in the weeks after Papadakis's death he had come, bringing his nervousness and his over-tips and the small packages that moved through Cemil's hands. His absence meant one of two things: he had been warned to distance himself from the hammam, or he had already been arrested, or he was preparing to flee.

She went to the hammam's booking ledger again and looked at the pattern of his appointments. Consistent through the winter and spring. Then two missed appointments in the weeks before the death — which she had not noted before, or rather had noted as ordinary gaps and not as the thing they might have been. He had perhaps been uncomfortable, already sensing the tightening.

And now gone.

Cemil was still coming to work, which suggested he did not know about Fevzi. Or knew and had decided to continue as normal until someone told him not to. She watched him for two days without approaching him. He behaved normally — or what passed for his normal, which was quiet and careful. He ate his lunch alone.

On the third day she went down to the coal cellar and found him there, replacing the bolt on the side door. He looked up when she came in. His face did not change, but something in his body shifted slightly.

"The bolt," he said. "It's been stiff."

She looked at the door. The frame still had the scrape marks. The bolt was new.

"Good," she said. "Be sure to lock it when you leave tonight."

She went back up. Behind her she heard Cemil stop working. She heard the small sound of someone holding very still.

She did not look back.

Chapter 41: The Palace Thread

Halil gave her the connection in the same oblique way he gave everything — not directly but through adjacency, the way you read a text by its margins.

He had been speaking for some time about palace politics — the factions, the alignments, the slow pressure that certain reformist officials had been applying to the customs administration. He named no names except in the past tense or as abstractions. He spoke of trends. He noted that the current situation was unusually fluid.

And then, as though discovering a word in a sentence: "Selim Paşa controls appointments, as I mentioned. He also controls the archive clerks. But what he does not control, and cannot, is the magistracy. Kemal Paşa is a different chain entirely."

"You said Kemal Paşa needs a precipitating event."

"Yes. He has been building toward an indictment for eighteen months. He has accounts, testimony from minor functionaries, circumstantial evidence of systematic fraud." Halil paused. "What he lacks is a specific transaction that he can trace end to end. A single incident with documentation, witnesses, and a victim."

"He has all of that," Fatma said. "He simply hasn't been given it yet."

Halil looked at her. "The victim is dead," he said. "The witnesses —"

"I am a witness," Fatma said. "And there is a foreign national with the ledger. And there is a constable who can testify to the drainage alteration — Vasil saw the plumbers and can identify the timeframe. And there is Inspector Nazmi, who ruled the death accidental under circumstances that will not withstand scrutiny."

Halil was quiet for a moment.

"Nazmi will be the difficult one," he said.

"Nazmi will cooperate," Fatma said. "He is afraid of being on the wrong side of this and he is smart enough to know which way it is going."

"You have decided."

"I decided three weeks ago," she said. "I was waiting to understand the shape of it first."

Chapter 42: Confirmation

She sent a message to Halil through the bakery boy: I am ready.

He replied by the same route: Come Thursday.

She told Roksanda on Friday. Roksanda received this without surprise. She had been ready for some time, she said — she had been ready since Fevzi died, because after that it was only a matter of timing.

"The magistrate will question you," Fatma said.

"I know. I have given testimony before." Roksanda was matter-of-fact about this. "In Bulgaria, in the commercial courts. It is not pleasant but it is not mysterious." She paused. "I will need to leave Istanbul afterward. The people whose names appear in the ledger will not be pleased."

"Yes," Fatma said.

"And you? What happens to you after?"

Fatma considered this. She had thought about it on the sleepless night. She had come to a conclusion that surprised her with its flatness, its lack of drama.

"I continue to work," she said. "This hammam or another one."

Roksanda looked at her. "You are not afraid?"

"I am afraid," Fatma said. "But the alternative is to have seen what I saw and said nothing. To have let a man's hands mean nothing." She paused. "I am more afraid of that."

Roksanda nodded slowly. She looked at the steam rising from the pool.

"Nikos was a complicated man," she said. "He did things he should not have done. But he was trying to find a way out. That matters."

"It matters," Fatma agreed.

On Thursday she went to Halil Efendi's house. He walked with her to a building near the court district and introduced her to a clerk who showed her to a waiting room. The magistrate's assistant received her an hour later.

She gave her statement without notes. She had been living with it long enough that it was simply the truth, organized as clearly as she could organize it.

Chapter 43: The Editor

She went back to Alexandros the editor, two weeks after her first visit. This time she went alone, without Roksanda.

She told him she was ready to give him facts she was also giving to the magistrate. She told him the name Selim Paşa and the name Lars Helgren and the name Arslan Bey. She told him the structure of the network — the registry, the hammam transfer point, the Pera office, the European investors. She told him about Fevzi's death, which had been reported as a robbery, and about the alteration to the hammam's drainage system.

She did not give him the ledger. The ledger was going to the magistrate through Roksanda.

Alexandros listened and wrote. He asked precise questions and she answered precisely. When she finished, he looked at his notes.

"I can publish what you have given me," he said, "but I need to verify the palace connection before I name Selim Paşa."

"You will have verification," she said. "Within the week, I expect."

He looked at her. "How confident are you in that timeline?"

"Confident enough to give you my name as a source."

He paused. He had not asked for this. She saw him registering the weight of it — a woman who worked in a hammam, giving her name to a Greek-language newspaper in Istanbul. The courage in it, and also the cost.

"Your name," he said. "Fatma Hanım. The senior tellak at Çemberlitaş."

"Yes."

He wrote it down.

"I will not use it unless the story is published," he said. "And I will not publish until I have verification."

"Agreed," she said.

She walked out into the Pera street. The sun was in the west, the light golden and long. A tram went by. A dog trotted between the buildings.

She held the ledger still — Roksanda's name, not hers. Halil's introduction, not hers. But her statement had been given and her name had been given, and this was the first time in her life that an institution of any kind had asked for them in this context.

Chapter 44: The Reopening

Inspector Nazmi came to the hammam for the third time on a cold morning in late autumn. This time he arrived in his uniform, which meant something different from his previous civilian visit. He stood in the main entrance and asked for Fatma Hanım, and when she came he said: "I need to speak with you. Outside."

They stood in the street. He looked tired in a way that had passed beyond tiredness into something more fundamental.

"The Papadakis inquiry is being reopened," he said. "By the magistrate Kemal Paşa. I received the notification this morning."

Fatma said nothing.

"This is not my investigation now," Nazmi said. "The magistrate's office will be handling it. I have been — instructed to provide my documentation from the original ruling."

"And your ruling will be reviewed."

"Yes." He looked at the street. A cart went past. "I wanted you to know before the official notification reaches you. You will be called to give formal testimony."

"I know," she said. "I have already given a preliminary statement."

He looked at her sharply. "When?"

"Last Thursday."

He absorbed this. He was doing mathematics — she could see it in his face — calculating how much she had given, what it meant for his position.

"I told them what I know," she said. "Which is what I told your constable, and more besides."

He was quiet for a long time. Then he said, very quietly: "I made an error."

"Yes," she said.

"I was told it was a straightforward arrangement. That the merchant's death was indeed an accident and the other matters — the customs business — would be handled at a higher level." He stopped. "I was told it would not be investigated."

"And then it was," Fatma said.

"And then it was." He put his hat on. "I intend to cooperate fully with the magistrate's investigation."

She looked at him for a moment. "That is wise," she said. She meant it.

He left. She watched him go, then went back inside.

Chapter 45: Mehmet Bey's Confession

He came to her, which she had not expected. She was in the linen room, of course — she was always in the linen room — and he knocked on the door frame. He had never knocked on a linen room door frame in his life, as far as she could tell.

"Come in," she said.

He came in. He did not sit. He stood in the middle of the room with his thick, careful hands at his sides.

"I heard about the magistrate," he said.

"News travels," Fatma said. She continued folding.

"I want you to know something." He stopped. Started again. "I did not plan what happened to Papadakis. I was told the chamber would be used for a meeting. A business meeting, I was told, sensitive in nature, requiring privacy. I provided the access. I provided Cemil's assistance." He paused. "I did not know a man would die."

Fatma set down the towel she was folding and looked at him. She waited.

"I have not slept well since that morning," he said.

"No," she said.

"When I saw the body, I — I did not understand at first what had happened. I thought it was an accident. And then when I understood — I had already spoken to Nazmi. I had already told him it was an accident. And there was the arrangement, the monthly arrangement, and I knew that if the investigation went anywhere it would come back to me." He looked at his hands. "So I said nothing."

"And directed the staff to say nothing," Fatma said.

"Yes."

She looked at him for a long time. He was a coward, not a killer — she had decided this before he told her any of it. The cowardice was genuine: the refusal to act, the deliberate not-knowing, the accommodation that allowed other people to do the thing he would not. This was not the same as innocence, but it was not the same as murder.

"You will need to give this statement to the magistrate," she said.

"I know." He looked, for the first time, directly at her. "Is there — is there any chance that —"

"I don't know what the magistrate will decide about you," Fatma said. "That is not my area."

He nodded slowly. He turned to go. At the door he stopped.

"You have worked here for twenty years," he said.

"Yes," she said.

"I hope you will continue to work here," he said. "If the hammam is still here to work in."

She picked up the towel and resumed folding. She said nothing.

Chapter 46: What Fatma Decided

She believed him. This was the most uncomfortable thing.

She believed Mehmet Bey that he had not known a man would die. She believed that he had told himself, at each step, that what he was involved in was merely paperwork, merely access, merely a quiet arrangement that did not harm anyone directly. She believed that the morning of the death had shocked him genuinely, and that his response — silence, direction to the staff, the hand on Nazmi's arm — had been the reflexes of a terrified man rather than the calculations of a guilty one.

She did not believe this excused him. He had been paid. He had provided the physical access through which a man had entered to commit murder. He had then suppressed the investigation. These were not accidents or reflexes — they were choices.

But his guilt was not the same as the guilt of the man who had entered through the passage and held Papadakis under the water. That guilt was something the magistrate would need to reach, and she had given the magistrate the tools.

For Mehmet Bey, she thought: the rest of his life will be its own verdict. A man who has told himself the thing he accommodated was not so terrible, and who then watched a man drown in his pool, and who has not slept well since — he is already serving something.

She went back to her work.

The afternoon was busy. The hammam was full in that particular way it was full on autumn afternoons, when the cold made the steam more welcome and the smell of the water more acute. She moved through it without thinking about what came next. She prepared the chambers, she poured the basins, she worked the cloth across the backs of men who would forget her as soon as they walked out the door.

This was her work. She did it well. That was not a small thing.

Chapter 47: The Arrest

The newspaper reported it first, in a Saturday edition. Arslan Bey, a second-level official in the customs registry, had been arrested in connection with a customs fraud investigation. The article described a scheme involving the improper transfer of Ottoman state documents to European investors, facilitated by a brokerage in the Pera district. The arrest had been made by order of the magistrate Kemal Paşa.

Fatma read this in the coffeehouse across the street from the hammam, where she sometimes spent the first half hour of her morning off. She read it carefully. The article named Arslan Bey and the Lindqvist firm. It mentioned a palace administrator whose case was under investigation — it did not name Selim Paşa directly, but a person who knew the administrative structure could identify him from the description.

It did not name her.

The name Nikos Papadakis appeared in the third paragraph: a Greek merchant whose death had initially been ruled accidental had been identified as a victim of the fraud, and the original ruling was under review.

She folded the newspaper and set it on the table. She drank her tea, which had gone slightly cold while she was reading.

The coffeehouse owner said, from behind the counter: "Bad business, all that customs fraud. You see this?"

"Yes," Fatma said.

"Terrible. These officials, they think no one will notice." He shook his head. "Someone always notices."

"Yes," Fatma said. She stood and left the exact amount for the tea on the table, and went back to work.

Chapter 48: The Clerk

The clerk came on a Monday. He was young, perhaps twenty-five, with the slightly harried air of someone carrying many errands. He asked for Fatma Hanım at the door and Kadriye came to find her with wide eyes.

Fatma went to the main entrance. The clerk introduced himself as being from the office of the magistrate Kemal Paşa. He needed to take her official statement in relation to the death of Nikos Papadakis.

"I gave a preliminary statement," she said.

"Yes. This is the formal record, for the investigative file."

She led him to the small room Mehmet Bey used as an office — Mehmet Bey was not present — and sat across a table from him. He had a writing case and a pen that he uncapped with precise ceremony.

He asked her questions in a careful, formal sequence: her name, her position, her years of employment, her relationship to the deceased as a client. He asked what she had observed on the morning of the death, and she told him, beginning with her preparation of the chamber and proceeding through the moment she had seen the hands on the stone lip.

He wrote as she spoke. His handwriting was clear and small.

When she described the hands — the white knuckles, the effort in the wrists — he looked up from the page.

"Can you estimate how long you observed this?" he asked.

"Perhaps three seconds," she said. "Before the door swung."

"And your interpretation at the time?"

She thought about how to say it. "I understood it as the hands of someone holding on," she said. "The specific posture of effort — not leisure, not discomfort, but effort. A man fighting."

He wrote this down. "Fighting what?"

"Something from below," she said. "That was my reading of the posture."

He nodded slowly. He wrote more. He asked about the wax mark, which she described. He asked about Vasil's account of the plumbers, which she gave.

At the end he thanked her formally and closed his writing case.

"The magistrate's office will contact you if further testimony is required," he said.

"Yes," she said.

She watched him go. For a moment she stood in Mehmet Bey's office alone, looking at the writing case's impression on the table where it had rested. Then she went back to her work.

It was the first time in twenty years that an official of the Ottoman state had sat across a table from her and asked what she had seen, and written down what she said, and thanked her for it.

Chapter 49: The Ferry Landing

Roksanda left on a Tuesday morning. She sent a message to the hammam the night before: the ferry from Eminönü, eight o'clock.

Fatma went. She wore her good clothes — the same ones she had worn to Pera — and arrived at the landing as the city was still gray and cold. The ferries were loading on the European side for their morning runs, and the landing was crowded with the usual mixture of travelers: men with cases, women with bundles, a few civil servants with briefcases.

Roksanda was standing apart, near the railing, with a small trunk and her traveling coat. She looked as she always looked: solid, contained, alert.

They stood together for a moment, not speaking. The water of the Golden Horn was dark and slow. A gull moved overhead.

"The magistrate has the ledger," Roksanda said. "I gave it to him on Friday. He has the original. I kept a copy."

"Good," Fatma said.

"They will want me to return to testify if the case goes to trial. I told them I would come back."

"I believe you," Fatma said.

They looked at the water.

"I don't know if any of it will be enough," Roksanda said. "To convict the right people. These things go sideways."

"They do," Fatma said. "But Papadakis is named in the investigation now. The death is on record as something other than an accident. That doesn't disappear."

Roksanda nodded. She looked at the ferry, which was beginning to take on final passengers.

"He was a complicated man," she said again. She had said this before.

"Most people are," Fatma said.

Roksanda looked at her for a moment. There was something in the look that was not quite gratitude — it was more sober than that, more like recognition. The acknowledgment of two people who have shared a dangerous thing and survived it and will not forget it.

She picked up her trunk and went to the gangway.

Fatma stayed at the railing until the ferry pulled away. Then she turned and walked back through the city toward the hammam.

Chapter 50: The Work

The private chamber had been rebooked by the end of the month.

The new regular was a civil engineer, forty-three years old, who worked on the expansion of the city's water system and came on Thursday mornings. He was punctual and courteous in the same restrained way as Papadakis, though for different reasons — where Papadakis's economy had been that of a man in trade, the engineer's was the economy of someone who spent his days solving problems and had learned not to waste effort on anything outside the solution.

He looked up at the skylight on his first visit. Most men did.

Fatma prepared the chamber as she had always prepared it. Fresh linen. Oil in the cedar dish. The floor drain checked and clear. The stone lip of the pool wiped down.

She knew, now, what was beneath the pool. The passage had been sealed — the magistrate's office had requested access to the building, and two constables had come with a mason who sealed both the access panel and the drainage aperture from below. The mason had done it properly, with good mortar, and the work was solid. She could feel, when she pressed her hand against the cellar wall, that the difference in texture was almost gone.

Cemil had been taken in for questioning and released. He had cooperated — she did not know the full extent of what he had said, but he had not been arrested and had not been seen at the hammam since. Mehmet Bey had hired a new porter, a large silent man from somewhere in eastern Anatolia who asked no questions.

The autumn had come fully now. The water in the pipes was cooler at the start of the morning, requiring longer to heat. She had always preferred the autumn hammam — the steam was richer somehow, more necessary, and the clients were more appreciative of it, having spent the summer months needing it less.

She worked through the morning without thinking about the investigation or the magistrate or what would happen to Selim Paşa or Arslan Bey or Lars Helgren of the Lindqvist firm. These things were outside her reach. She had reached as far as she could and placed the information where it needed to go and now the mechanism of official consequence would proceed without her.

What remained was the work. The preparation and the attention and the reading of what each person needed. The knowledge she had accumulated over twenty years — not abstract knowledge but embodied, tactile, the knowledge of a woman who had spent two decades learning what lay beneath surfaces.

Kadriye was at the desk when Fatma came through the main hall. She looked up.

"New bookings for next week," Kadriye said. "Thursday is full."

"Good," Fatma said.

Kadriye looked at her for a moment with the particular expression of someone who has much to say and has decided none of it is necessary.

"Good," Kadriye said.

Fatma went to check the coal supply. Below her, Vasil was starting the furnace. The sound of it rose through the floor, the long, low breath of the building waking up, the heat beginning to move through the pipes toward the chambers above, toward the pool that was now simply a pool again, pale marble and clear water, waiting to do nothing more than warm the people who came to it.

She stood in the corridor for a moment.

Then she went to work.
