Chapter 1: Arrival at Dusk

The train from Geneva traced the northern shore of the lake. Claire pressed her forehead to the cold window. Mountains rose pale through the haze. Water the color of old pewter stretched to the south.

She had read about the Hotel du Lac in a monograph on Belle Epoque architecture. A palace built for the 1896 Geneva Exposition. White stone. Wrought-iron balconies. A lakeside terrace where kings had smoked cigars.

Now it was a relic.

The train slowed. Claire gathered her leather satchel and stepped onto the platform. The station was small, almost empty. A single taxi waited.

"Hotel du Lac," she said.

The driver glanced at her in the mirror. "You have business there?"

"I am a guest."

He nodded slowly and pulled away from the curb.

The road followed the lake. Plane trees lined the route, their branches meeting overhead. Through the gaps Claire saw the hotel rising on its promontory. Three wings. A central dome. Faded grandeur.

The taxi stopped at the iron gates. Claire paid and walked up the gravel drive. The flowerbeds were overgrown. A fountain stood dry.

The lobby was vast and dim. Marble floors. A reception desk of carved walnut. Brass fixtures tarnished to green. The air smelled of beeswax and dust.

An elderly clerk looked up from his ledger. "Mademoiselle Durand?"

"Yes."

"Welcome to the Hotel du Lac." He slid a key across the counter. "Room 307."

The key was brass. Heavy. Ornate. Unlike the plastic keycards she had expected.

"The third floor," the clerk said. "The elevator is out of order."

Claire took the key and climbed the stairs.


Chapter 2: The Key to 307

The stairs creaked under Claire's weight. The wallpaper was rose-patterned, faded to a blush. Portrait of forgotten dignitaries lined the walls.

She reached the third-floor landing. The corridor stretched ahead, dimly lit by sconces. The carpet was worn to threads in a path down the center.

Room 301. 303. 305.

She stopped at 307. The door was darker than the others. The paint was a deep burgundy, almost black. The number plate was brass, hand-engraved.

She inserted the key. The lock turned with a heavy click.

The room was large. A high ceiling with a plaster rose. Velvet curtains the color of dried blood. A four-poster bed with white linens. A writing desk by the window.

And a mirror.

It was tall. Floor to ceiling. Set into the wall opposite the bed. The frame was tarnished silver. The glass was old, with the faint ripple of pre-war manufacture.

Claire set down her bag. She walked to the window. The lake was gray and still. A single boat moved across the water, its wake invisible from this distance.

She turned back to the room. The light was fading.

There was a smell she could not place. Something floral. Something old. Like a pressed flower in a forgotten book.

She unpacked her notes. Hotel floor plans from the municipal archives. A 1924 photograph of the terrace. Guest ledgers from the Belle Epoque.

She spread them on the writing desk and began to work.


Chapter 3: First Impressions

Claire woke before dawn. The room was cold. The curtains glowed faintly with moonlight.

She had slept poorly. Dreams of corridors. Of doors that led to other doors. Of a woman's voice saying something she could not understand.

She showered in the narrow bathroom. The pipes groaned. The water was barely warm.

Dressed, she examined the room in daylight. The furniture was original to the hotel. Art Deco lines. Rosewood and mother-of-pearl. The bed was comfortable, the linens crisp.

The mirror drew her eye.

She stood before it. Her reflection looked back. Tired. Dark hair pulled back. The face of a woman who spent too much time in archives.

She touched the glass. It was cold.

Something was wrong with the reflection. Not with her. With the room behind her. The angle seemed wrong. The window appeared in a different place than it should.

She turned to check. The window was where it should be.

She looked back at the mirror. The reflection matched.

A trick of the light. Old glass warped by time.

She turned away and went downstairs for breakfast.


Chapter 4: The Hallway Without Doors

Breakfast was served in the empty dining room. A single waiter in a stained jacket. Coffee and a croissant. Claire ate quickly.

She had work to do. The hotel was being renovated next spring. The owners wanted a historical assessment before the work began. Document the original features. Identify what should be preserved.

She decided to walk the entire building. She took her notebook and a measuring tape.

The third-floor corridor ran east to west. She counted the doors. 301 through 310. She measured the distance from the stairwell to the end wall.

The math did not work.

The corridor was ten meters longer than the building's exterior allowed. There should have been a turn. A dead end. Something.

She walked to the end of the corridor. The wall was solid. Painted cream. A sconce cast a pool of light.

She pressed her palm against the wall. It felt wrong. Thicker than it should be.

She knocked. The sound was solid. Not hollow.

But she did not believe it.

She returned to her room and examined the floor plans. The 1924 plan showed a different configuration. A shorter corridor. A service staircase where the end wall now stood.

The modern plan showed the current layout.

Between them, space was missing.


Chapter 5: The Night Porter's Warning

That evening Claire could not sleep. She put on her coat and walked the corridors. The hotel was silent. The guests were few. Mostly elderly couples who retired early.

She found the stairs to the lobby. The clock above the reception desk read half past one.

A man sat in the corner of the lobby in a worn armchair. He was old. Seventy, maybe eighty. He wore a porter's uniform that had been modern sometime in the 1970s.

"You are awake," he said. His voice was rough. French accented with something else.

"I could not sleep," Claire said.

"The room does that."

She stopped. "What do you mean?"

He did not answer directly. He looked at the clock. "Room 307 has always been difficult. My father told me about it."

"Your father worked here?"

"Fifty years as night porter. Before me. I have been here fifty years more." He smiled. No teeth. "One hundred years of night porters in my family."

Claire sat in the chair beside him. "What did your father say about Room 307?"

"He said the woman in the mirror does not like to be watched."

The words settled in the dim air. Claire felt a chill that had nothing to do with the temperature.

"Who is she?" she asked.

The porter shook his head. "Some questions are better left unasked. Some doors are better left closed."

He stood slowly and walked away into the shadows.


Chapter 6: Archives and Photographs

The hotel archives were in the basement. A room of filing cabinets and bound ledgers. The smell of old paper and mold.

The management had given Claire access. She spent the morning going through photographs.

The hotel in 1900. The hotel in 1910. The terrace crowded with parasols and white suits.

She found the 1924 album. Leather-bound. The spine cracked.

She opened it.

The photographs were sepia-toned. Groups of guests on the lawn. Couples on the terrace. A portrait of the staff in their uniforms.

And one photograph that made her stop.

A group of five people on the terrace steps. Three men, two women. The women wore dropped-waist dresses. The men wore suits and straw hats.

One woman stared directly at the camera.

She was beautiful. Sharp cheekbones. Dark eyes. A mouth that knew how to keep secrets. She wore a silver dress and a string of pearls.

Claire studied the woman's face. There was something in her expression. Defiance. Or fear. It was hard to tell.

She turned the photograph over. On the back, in pencil: "Summer 1924. Marguerite D. and friends."

Marguerite. The woman in the mirror.

Claire copied the photograph. She returned to her room and pinned it above the writing desk.

The woman's eyes followed her.


Chapter 7: The Woman in the Mirror

Midnight. Claire was reading when the light changed.

Not the electric light. Something else. A shift in the quality of darkness.

She looked up.

The mirror was not reflecting the room.

It showed a different space. The same room, but wrong. The furniture arranged differently. The curtains a different color. A lamp glowing on the nightstand that did not exist in her room.

And a woman.

She stood in the center of the reflected room. A silver dress. Dark hair pinned up. The woman from the photograph.

She was not looking at her own reflection. She was looking at Claire.

Claire did not move. She could not.

The woman raised her hand and pressed her palm against the glass.

Claire's breath caught. She saw the woman's lips move. A word. A name.

Then the image dissolved. The mirror reflected the room again. Claire's own pale face stared back.

She sat for a long time. Her heart beat against her ribs.

She did not sleep that night.

In the morning, she checked the mirror. It was just a mirror. Dark glass. Tarnished frame.

But she knew what she had seen.


Chapter 8: Hotel Maps and Missing Rooms

Claire needed answers. She went to the municipal archives in Geneva.

The archivist was a young man with spectacles. He brought her the building permits for the Hotel du Lac. Original construction. 1894. Renovations. 1928. 1952. 1980.

She compared the floor plans.

The 1894 plans showed a regular layout. Identical floors. Four wings around a central dome.

The 1928 plans showed changes. A new wing added. Some rooms combined.

But there was something else.

The 1928 plan showed a room that was not on the 1894 plan. Room 307. Inserted between 305 and 309. As if it had been carved from the space between them.

She checked the 1980 plan. Room 307 was gone. The space was labeled "storage."

She checked the current plan. Storage. Not a guest room.

"But I am staying in Room 307," she said.

The archivist looked confused. "That room does not exist on the current plans."

"Then where am I sleeping?"

He had no answer.

Claire took copies of all the plans. She returned to the hotel as the sun set. The corridors seemed longer than before. The shadows deeper.

Room 307 waited for her.


Chapter 9: The Sound of Music

Two in the morning. Claire sat at the writing desk, the floor plans spread before her. She had been trying to reconcile the documents. Nothing matched.

Then she heard it.

Music. Faint. Coming from below.

A piano. A saxophone. A woman singing.

Jazz.

She stood. The music came through the floor. Through the walls. Distant but unmistakable.

She opened her door. The corridor was empty. The music was louder in the hallway.

She followed it.

Down the stairs. Through the lobby. Past the empty dining room. To the doors of the Grand Ballroom.

The doors were closed. They had been closed since the 1980s. The ballroom was used for storage.

But light bled under the door. Yellow light. Warm.

She pushed.

The doors swung open.

The ballroom was transformed. Crystal chandeliers blazed. The parquet floor gleamed. A jazz band played on the stage. Couples danced. Women in flapper dresses. Men in tuxedos.

Claire stood in the doorway. No one noticed her.

She stepped inside.

The air was thick with perfume and cigarette smoke. The year was 1924.

She turned. The doorway behind her was gone. There was only the ballroom. Music. Laughter.

She was no longer in the present.


Chapter 10: A Door Opens

Claire walked through the crowd. No one looked at her. She was invisible.

She touched a woman's sleeve. The woman did not react. Claire was there but not there.

The band played "Sweet Georgia Brown." The dancers moved in their contained choreography. Champagne flowed.

She found the terrace doors. She stepped outside.

The lake was the same. The mountains the same. But the hotel was young. The paint fresh. The gardens manicured.

A woman stood at the balustrade. Silver dress. Dark hair.

Marguerite.

She turned. She looked directly at Claire.

"You came," Marguerite said.

Claire could not speak.

"I have been waiting," Marguerite said. "Every night. For someone to see me."

"You are real," Claire said.

"I am as real as this night." Marguerite gestured at the lake. "I have been here since 1924. I cannot leave."

"Who are you?"

"Marguerite Delacroix. I came here with Victor. We were going to run away together."

She paused. Her expression shifted.

"But that is not the whole story," she said. "Nothing ever is."

The music swelled. The night was beautiful. But Claire felt the weight of something unsaid. Something terrible waiting beneath the surface.


Chapter 11: The Hotel Alive

The ballroom spun with color and sound. Claire stood beside Marguerite at the terrace door, watching the dancers.

"I do not understand," Claire said. "Where are we?"

"Tuesday night. August 12, 1924." Marguerite smiled. "The same night I have been living for one hundred years."

"Every night?"

"Every night. The music plays. The champagne flows. Victor arrives. We dance. We argue. He dies." She said it flatly. The facts of a story she knew by heart.

"You remember the repetition?"

"I remember everything. Every version of this night. Every word Victor says. Every note the band plays." Marguerite looked at the lake. "But I can never change anything."

"Why?"

"Because I am the one who holds the memory. The night is locked in me. As long as I remember, it plays again."

Claire tried to process this. "Can you leave?"

"I have tried. Every night I walk to the end of the terrace. Every night I reach the same point, and the music starts over." She turned to Claire. "But you are different. You came from outside."

"Can I help you?"

Marguerite studied her. "I do not know. No one has ever come through before."


Chapter 12: Meeting Marguerite

The band played "Stardust." Marguerite led Claire to a quiet corner of the terrace.

"I need to tell you the story," Marguerite said. "The real one. Before Victor arrives."

Claire nodded.

"His name is Victor Morel. He is charming. Handsome. He swept me off my feet in Paris." She paused. "I thought he loved me. But he loved what I could give him."

"What was that?"

"Access. I was the daughter of a banker. I knew everyone. The right families. The right parties." She shook her head. "Victor was not who he pretended to be."

"Who was he?"

"A thief. A confidence man. He had been working the resorts for years. Finding wealthy women. Taking what he could."

Claire remembered the photograph. The defiance in Marguerite's eyes. Fear.

"Did you know?" Claire asked.

"Not at first. But I began to suspect. He asked too many questions about the other guests. Their jewelry. Their travel plans."

Victor appeared at the terrace door. Young. Handsome. A smile that did not reach his eyes.

"Marguerite," he said. "Come inside. They are playing our song."

She smiled. A practiced smile. "Of course, darling."

She walked toward him. She did not look back.

The night was beginning.


Chapter 13: The Rules of the Dream

Claire tested the boundaries.

She walked away from the hotel into the gardens. The path was clear. The flowers bloomed. But at a certain point the world blurred. Colors ran together. The path curved back toward the hotel.

She could not leave the grounds.

She tried to speak to other guests. A woman in green silk. An elderly gentleman. They answered her questions but their eyes did not track her. She was a ghost in their world.

She entered the hotel kitchen. The chefs worked. Pots steamed. But when she opened a drawer, the contents were indistinct. A blur of things that did not resolve into detail.

The world of 1924 was detailed where it mattered. The dress. The music. The conversation. But the edges were soft. The unimportant things were vague.

She returned to the ballroom. Marguerite danced with Victor. She laughed at something he said. Her performance was flawless.

Claire watched Victor. He scanned the room as he danced. Noting the guests. The jewelry. The exits.

A professional.

A man in a dark suit stood near the bar. He was watching Victor too. He caught Claire's eye. He looked away.

Someone else was paying attention.

The night moved toward its conclusion. Claire felt the clock ticking. The shot was coming.


Chapter 14: Victor's Arrival

The evening unfolded as Marguerite had described.

Victor was attentive. He brought Marguerite champagne. He held her hand. He whispered things that made her blush.

But his eyes were elsewhere.

Claire followed him when he excused himself. He went to the library. A small room off the main lobby. Dark paneling. Leather chairs.

Another man was waiting.

He was older. Heavy. Expensive suit. He spoke in Russian.

Victor answered in French. "I have it."

"You have until midnight," the Russian said.

"The price has changed."

"The price is agreed."

Victor shook his head. "The circumstances have changed. The necklace is worth more than you offered."

The Russian stood. He was taller than Victor. "You are a fool. You stole from a countess. Do you think she will not notice? Do you think the police will not search?"

"By morning I will be in Italy."

"By morning you will be dead."

Victor laughed. "Threats. I have heard them before."

He walked out of the library. He passed within a meter of Claire. She saw the sweat on his forehead. The fear beneath the bravado.

He was not as confident as he pretended.

The Russian watched him go. He took out a cigarette. His hands were steady.

Claire followed Victor back to the ballroom. The band was playing. The dancers danced.

The clock moved toward midnight.


Chapter 15: A Walk on the Terrace

Marguerite found Claire on the terrace after dinner.

"I saw you watching," Marguerite said. "You followed him."

"Yes."

"He is meeting someone tonight. A Russian."

"I saw."

Marguerite gripped the balustrade. Her knuckles were white. "I have lived this night a thousand times. I know what happens. I know Victor steals the necklace. I know the countess discovers him. I know the shot."

"Then you know who fires it."

Marguerite was silent for a long moment. "That is the part I never see. The shot comes from somewhere. I run. I find him dead. And then the night starts again."

"You never saw the shooter?"

"I saw a shadow. Nothing more."

A breeze came off the lake. The water lapped against the retaining wall. The mountains were dark shapes against the sky.

"Tonight I will watch," Claire said. "I will find the truth."

"The truth will not change anything."

"Maybe not. But I will know."

Marguerite turned to her. In the moonlight her face was pale. "Knowing does not help. I have known the truth for a hundred years. I am still here."

"Then why do you want to leave?"

"Because I am tired." Marguerite's voice broke on the word. "I am so tired."


Chapter 16: The Argument

The clock in the lobby struck eleven.

Victor pulled Marguerite aside near the grand staircase. His face was tight. His voice low.

"We need to leave tonight," he said.

"Tonight? The train does not leave until morning."

"Then we will find another way."

"What happened? What did you do?"

"Nothing. I need you to trust me."

Marguerite pulled away. "Trust you? You have been lying to me since we met."

Victor's face hardened. "What are you talking about?"

"The Russian. The necklace. Did you think I would not find out?"

He grabbed her wrist. "You do not understand."

"I understand enough. You used me."

"I love you."

"Do not." She pulled her wrist free. "Do not lie to me. Not now."

The band played. The dancers danced. No one noticed the argument.

Victor's face crumpled. "I made mistakes. Bad choices. But I wanted to fix them. That is why I took the necklace. So we could start fresh."

"By stealing?"

"By surviving." He looked desperate. "I was drowning, Marguerite. And I did not know any other way."

She stared at him. Her expression was unreadable.

"Meet me on the terrace at midnight," she said. "We will talk then."

She walked away. Victor stood alone at the foot of the stairs.

Claire watched him from the shadows. The clock ticked.


Chapter 17: Marguerite's Confession

Claire found Marguerite in her room. Room 307.

The room looked different in 1924. The furniture was newer. The curtains were pale blue. A vase of fresh flowers stood on the writing desk.

Marguerite sat at the vanity. She was not looking at her reflection.

"I should have seen it from the start," she said. "The signs were there. The late nights. The whispered phone calls. The way he watched everyone except me."

"You loved him."

"I loved an idea of him." She picked up a brush. Set it down. "I wanted someone to save me. My father had arranged a marriage. A boring man with a boring fortune. Victor was exciting. Different."

"Did he love you?"

Marguerite laughed. "I do not think Victor loved anyone. He loved the game. The risk. The escape."

She turned to face Claire. "And I have been replaying this night for a hundred years. Watching him die. Watching myself lose everything. Because I cannot admit that I was a fool."

"You are not a fool."

"I stayed. Even when I knew the truth. I stayed because I was afraid to be alone."

Claire sat beside her. "What do you want me to do?"

"I want you to watch. Really watch. See what happens. Because I think I have been missing something."

"Missing what?"

"The moment between the argument and the shot. There is a gap. A few minutes I cannot account for."

"Where are you during that time?"

Marguerite hesitated. "I do not remember."


Chapter 18: The Shot

Midnight. The terrace.

Victor stood at the balustrade. He held a small velvet pouch. The necklace.

Marguerite walked toward him from the hotel. Her heels clicked on the stone.

"It is done," Victor said. "We can leave."

"I am not going with you."

He turned. "What?"

"You lied to me. You stole. You put us both at risk."

"I did it for us."

"Do not say that. Do not make me complicit in your choices."

Victor stepped toward her. "You do not mean it."

"I mean every word."

His face changed. The charm fell away. "You think you can walk away? You think I will let you?"

"Let me? I am not your property."

"You are my ticket out of this life. I will not lose that."

He grabbed her arm. She struggled. The necklace pouch fell. Diamonds scattered on the stone.

The shot came from above.

Claire looked up. A figure on the third-floor balcony. A flash. The shape of a person.

Victor fell. His hand released Marguerite. He crumpled to the ground.

Marguerite screamed.

Claire ran toward the hotel entrance. She took the stairs two at a time. She reached the third floor.

The corridor was empty. All the doors were closed.

But one door was not entirely shut. Room 307. A crack of light.

Claire pushed it open. The room was empty.

The mirror caught her eye. In the reflection, she saw Marguerite kneeling over Victor on the terrace. Frozen.

Then the world dissolved.


Chapter 19: The Loop Begins

Claire woke in her bed. Room 307. The present.

The ceiling was the same. The curtains were the same. But it was morning. Gray light filtered through the window.

She sat up. She was fully dressed. Her notes were spread on the writing desk unchanged.

She checked the mirror. It was just a mirror.

She checked the clock. 8:47 AM. The day after she had arrived. Not the day after the ballroom. The day after her first night.

Time had reset for her too.

She had crossed into 1924, lived through the night, and returned to the same morning. The same as it had been for Marguerite.

But she remembered. She remembered everything.

She went to the writing desk. The 1924 photograph was still pinned above it. Marguerite stared at the camera.

Claire studied the image. The other figures in the photograph. The man near the bar. The Russian.

And in the background, barely visible: a figure on the third-floor balcony.

She took out her magnifying glass. The figure was blurred. A shape. A person in dark clothing.

Was it the shooter?

She needed to go back. She needed to see more.

She looked at the mirror. Her reflection looked back.

Tonight, she would step through again.


Chapter 20: Research and Obsession

Claire spent the day in the hotel archives.

She found the original 1924 guest ledger. Handwritten. Leather-bound. She turned the pages carefully.

Marguerite Delacroix. Paris. Arrived August 5. Departed -- the entry was crossed out. No departure date.

Victor Morel. Marseille. Arrived August 5. The word "deceased" was written in red ink beside his name.

Countess Irina Volkova. St. Petersburg. Arrived August 3. Departed August 13.

Claire noted the countess's departure date. The day after the murder.

She searched for a police report. She found nothing. The hotel had no record of an investigation.

She searched newspaper archives online. A brief mention in the Geneva Gazette. "Tragic accident at Hotel du Lac. French national Victor Morel died after falling from a terrace. No foul play suspected."

Falling. Not shot. The official story was a fall.

Claire sat back. The cover-up had been thorough. The countess had connections. The hotel had a reputation to protect. A dead thief would damage business.

She found one more document. A letter from the hotel manager to the board of directors. Dated September 1924.

"The unfortunate incident has been resolved to the satisfaction of all parties. No legal proceedings will be initiated. The guest in question has agreed to a financial settlement."

The guest in question. The countess.

Claire copied the letter. The truth was buried in bureaucracy. But it was still there.


Chapter 21: Jean-Luc's Knowledge

Claire found Jean-Luc in the lobby that evening.

She sat beside him. He did not look at her.

"I have been to 1924," she said.

His hands stopped moving. He held a glass of water. Condensation dripped onto his trousers.

"I saw the ballroom. I saw Marguerite. I saw Victor die."

Jean-Luc set down the glass. "I was afraid of this."

"Your father knew."

"My father knew many things he never spoke of." He turned to look at her. "He was the night porter that summer. He saw everything. The theft. The argument. The shot."

"Did he know who fired it?"

Jean-Luc was silent. The clock ticked.

"He never told me," he said finally. "But I know it haunts him. Even in death."

"Your father is dead."

"His ghost still walks this hotel. I have seen him. In the basement. On the stairs. He is searching for something."

"What?"

"Forgiveness." Jean-Luc stood. "You are getting close to the truth, Mademoiselle Durand. Be careful. Some truths destroy the people who find them."

He walked away. His footsteps echoed in the empty lobby.

Claire watched him go. She thought about the figure on the third-floor balcony. The flash of the shot. The door left ajar.

Jean-Luc's father had been the night porter. He could have been anywhere in the hotel that night.

Including the third-floor balcony.


Chapter 22: The Thief's Trail

Claire researched Victor Morel.

He was born in Marseille in 1898. Father a fisherman. Mother a seamstress. He had left home at sixteen.

By 1920 he was in Paris. He worked as a croupier in a casino. He was fired for theft.

By 1922 he was running a small con. Fake antiques. Fake jewelry. Real profits.

He had a police record. Petty theft. Fraud. Two months in prison.

He had arrived at the Hotel du Lac with Marguerite in August 1924. He had been posing as a wealthy businessman.

The countess was his mark. She was traveling with a diamond necklace worth a small fortune. Victor planned to steal it and flee to Italy with Marguerite.

But Marguerite had discovered the truth. She had confronted him.

And someone had killed him.

Claire searched for any record of a criminal investigation. She found nothing. The Geneva police had closed the case in a week.

She called the Geneva archives. A clerk confirmed the file existed but had been sealed for one hundred years. It would be unsealed next year.

Next year. She could not wait that long.

She needed to go back to 1924. She needed to see the moment clearly.

The mirror was the only way.


Chapter 23: The Countess Returns

Claire found a photograph of Countess Volkova in a society magazine from 1924.

She was a striking woman. Forty years old. Dark hair. Cold eyes. She wore the diamond necklace in the photograph. Seven stones. Each the size of a grape.

Claire searched for the countess's history. She had fled Russia after the revolution. She had brought her jewelry. She lived in a suite at the Hotel du Lac for three months every summer.

The necklace had been a gift from her husband. A Russian prince. He died in 1919.

She never remarried.

Claire found a mention of the countess in a 1935 society column. She was living in Nice. She had sold most of her jewelry. She died in 1941. No children. No heirs.

The necklace was never mentioned again.

Claire wondered what had happened to it. Victor had stolen it. He had dropped it on the terrace when he was shot. The stones scattered.

Someone must have gathered them. Someone must have taken them.

She looked at the photograph of the countess. The woman's expression was unreadable.

Had she killed Victor? Had she ordered his death? Or had she been as much a victim as Marguerite?

Claire pinned the countess's photograph beside Marguerite's. Three women. One night. One truth.

She was determined to find it.


Chapter 24: Crossing Again

That night Claire prepared.

She took her camera. A small digital camera. She took a notebook. A flashlight.

She stood before the mirror. She touched the glass.

The surface rippled.

She stepped through.

The ballroom materialized around her. The music. The laughter. The champagne.

The same night. August 12, 1924.

She checked her camera. It worked. She photographed the ballroom. The band. The guests.

No one noticed.

She found Marguerite at the bar. She looked tense. The argument with Victor had not happened yet.

"Claire," Marguerite said. "You came back."

"I need to see everything. This time I will watch from above."

"The balcony?"

"There is a figure on the third-floor balcony. I saw it during the shot. I need to see who it is."

Marguerite's face went pale. "The third floor?"

"Yes."

"That is where I disappear. In the gap between the argument and the shot. I always find myself back on the terrace. I never remember the missing minutes."

"Stay with Victor tonight. Do not leave him. I will watch from above."

Marguerite nodded. Her hands trembled.

Claire left the ballroom. She climbed the stairs to the third floor. The corridor was dark.

She found a position at the end of the hall. A window overlooked the terrace below.

She waited.

The clock ticked toward midnight.


Chapter 25: The Second Time

Claire watched from the third-floor window.

Below, the terrace was lit by lanterns. Victor and Marguerite walked out together. The argument began. Claire could see Victor's anger. Marguerite's fear.

The necklace pouch fell. Diamonds scattered.

And then the figure appeared.

A man. On the third-floor balcony, no more than ten meters from where Claire stood. He was in shadow. But she could see his shape. His height. His movements.

He raised a gun.

The shot was loud. Terrifying. Claire did not flinch.

Victor fell.

The man lowered the gun. He stepped back from the railing. He turned.

For a moment, his face was visible in the light from the window.

It was Jean-Luc. Not the old man. A younger man. Thirty years old. In a porter's uniform.

Jean-Luc's father.

He looked down at the gun in his hand. He looked at Victor's body on the terrace below. He looked at Marguerite screaming.

Then he fled.

Claire ran after him. Down the stairs. Through the service corridor. She saw him disappear into the basement.

She followed.

The basement was dark. She heard footsteps. A door closing.

She found herself in the archives. The same room where she had spent the morning.

Jean-Luc's father was gone.

But on the table, in the dust, there was a small velvet pouch. Open. Empty.

The necklace.

He had gathered it. All seven diamonds.

He had taken them and hidden them.

And no one had ever known.


Chapter 26: The Third Man

Claire emerged from the basement. The ballroom was empty. The music had stopped.

The night was over. The loop was about to reset.

She found Marguerite on the terrace. She was alone. Kneeling beside Victor's body.

"I saw him," Claire said. "The night porter. He was on the balcony."

Marguerite looked up. Her eyes were empty. "He killed Victor?"

"He was there. He fired the shot. But I do not think he meant to."

"What do you mean?"

"The argument was between Victor and the countess. The porter was supposed to protect the countess. I think he was her bodyguard."

Marguerite stood. "Bodyguard?"

"The countess was afraid. Victor had threatened her. She hired the porter to watch over her."

"But he killed Victor."

"He was trying to intervene. The shot was meant to warn. Not to kill." Claire shook her head. "But I need more evidence. I need to be sure."

The world began to blur. The edges of the terrace dissolved.

"I will come back," Claire said. "I will find the whole truth."

Marguerite smiled. A sad smile. "I will be here. I am always here."

The world faded. The music started again.

Claire woke in her room.


Chapter 27: Marguerite's Prison

Claire sat at the writing desk. She had been back to 1924 three times now. Each time she learned more.

But Marguerite remained trapped.

Claire thought about the nature of the prison. Marguerite was not a ghost. She was not a spirit haunting a room. She was a person caught in a moment. A loop of memory and emotion.

The room was the anchor. Room 307. The mirror was the door.

But what held her there?

Claire believed it was guilt. Marguerite felt responsible for Victor's death. If she had not confronted him, he would not have been on the terrace. He would not have been shot.

But there was more. Marguerite was hiding something. The gap in her memory was suspicious.

Claire decided to confront her.

That night she stepped through the mirror again. The ballroom materialized. She found Marguerite before Victor arrived.

"Tell me what you are hiding," Claire said.

Marguerite looked away. "I do not know what you mean."

"The gap. Between the argument and the shot. You said you do not remember. But I think you do."

Marguerite's face crumbled. "I remember."

"Then tell me."

"I was in the room. Watching from above. I saw the porter come out. I saw him raise the gun."

"You were on the third floor?"

"Yes." Her voice was a whisper. "I saw everything. The shot. Victor falling. The diamonds scattering."

"And you have been pretending not to remember?"

"Because if I remember, I have to accept that I could have stopped it. I could have shouted. I could have warned him."

She began to cry. "I stood there. I did nothing."


Chapter 28: The Missing Necklace

Claire searched for the missing diamonds.

She had seen Jean-Luc's father gather them. She had seen the empty velvet pouch. But where had he put them?

She returned to the archives. She searched for any record of a valuable being hidden in the hotel.

She found nothing.

She asked Jean-Luc directly. "Did your father ever talk about the necklace?"

Jean-Luc's face went still. "What necklace?"

"The diamonds. Victor stole them from the countess. Your father picked them up after the shooting."

Jean-Luc was silent. The lobby was dark around them.

"I found a pouch in the basement," Claire said. "Empty. Your father was there."

Jean-Luc closed his eyes. "He never told me."

"But you knew. You have always known."

"I knew he kept something hidden. A secret from that summer. He made me promise never to look for it."

"Where?"

"In the walls." Jean-Luc's voice was barely audible. "He sealed them in the walls. Somewhere in the hotel. He said they were cursed."

Claire understood. The diamonds were still in the building. They had been there for one hundred years.

Hidden in the walls of the Hotel du Lac.

She looked at the wall of her room. The space between the rooms.

The place that should not exist.


Chapter 29: Jean-Luc's Father

Jean-Luc told Claire the story of his father.

"The night was hot. My father was patrolling the grounds. He heard voices on the terrace. The countess had asked him to watch her that evening. She was afraid."

"Of Victor?"

"Victor had threatened her. He wanted more money for the necklace. He said he would expose her if she did not pay."

"Expose her for what?"

"For selling her jewelry to survive. She was a countess with no money. She pretended to be wealthy, but she was selling everything she owned."

Claire absorbed this. "So Victor knew her secret."

"He knew. And he used it against her." Jean-Luc shook his head. "My father was loyal to the countess. She had been kind to him. He wanted to protect her."

"He killed Victor to protect her."

"No. He went to the balcony to warn Victor away. He did not mean to fire. The gun went off in the struggle."

"But he gathered the diamonds."

"He panicked. He thought the diamonds would be evidence of the theft. He hid them."

"And he never told anyone."

"He took the secret to his grave." Jean-Luc looked at Claire. "But I think he has been trying to tell me for years. His ghost walks the corridors. Searching. Waiting."

"For what?"

"For the truth to be known."


Chapter 30: The Truth Begins

Claire assembled her evidence.

The photograph of the three figures on the balcony. The floor plans showing the missing room. The letter from the hotel manager. The sealed police file. The empty velvet pouch.

She laid them out on the writing desk.

The story was clear now. Victor Morel was a thief. He targeted the countess. He stole her necklace. She hired the night porter to protect her.

The porter confronted Victor on the terrace. A struggle. A gunshot. Death.

The countess covered it up. She paid the hotel manager. She paid the police. She left the next morning.

Jean-Luc's father kept the diamonds. He hid them in the walls. He never spoke of it.

Marguerite was caught in the middle. She witnessed the death. She felt responsible. She could not leave the room where she had waited for Victor's love.

Her guilt held her there.

But there was one more piece. The gap in Marguerite's memory was her own shame. She had stood on the third floor and watched. She had not called out. She had not saved Victor.

She had wanted him dead.

That was the truth she could not face.

And that was why she could not leave.


Chapter 31: A Different Approach

Claire decided to change the plan.

She had been observing. Now she would act.

She prepared a package. The photographs she had taken. The copies of the documents. A letter explaining the truth.

She tucked them into her coat.

She stepped through the mirror.

The ballroom appeared. The music played. The guests laughed.

She found Marguerite at the bar.

"I have proof," Claire said. "Of everything."

Marguerite looked at the package. "What is that?"

"The truth. About Victor. About the countess. About the porter. About you."

Marguerite's face hardened. "I do not want to see."

"You need to see. It is the only way you can leave."

"I cannot leave. I am bound here."

"You are bound by guilt. Not by the room. Not by the mirror. By your own refusal to forgive yourself."

Marguerite looked at the package. Her hands trembled.

"Open it," Claire said. "Read the letter. Look at the photographs. Then decide if you want to stay."

Marguerite took the package. Her fingers were clumsy. She unwrapped it slowly.

She read the letter. She looked at the photographs.

Her face changed. The defenses fell. The mask crumbled.

"I wanted him dead," she whispered. "I stood on the balcony and I did nothing. I watched him die."

"I know."

"And now I have to live with that."

"You have been living with it for one hundred years. It is time to let it go."


Chapter 32: Photographing the Truth

Marguerite studied the photographs Claire had taken.

The ballroom. The guests. The figure on the balcony.

"This is me," she said, pointing to a blurry shape on the third floor. "I was standing there."

"Yes."

"I did nothing."

"You froze. Anyone would have frozen."

"But I wanted him dead. I hated him for lying to me. For using me." She set down the photographs. "And when I saw the porter raise the gun, I thought: good. Let him die."

Claire sat beside her. "That is a human thought. You were hurt. You were angry."

"It does not excuse what I did."

"It does not need to be excused. It needs to be understood."

Marguerite looked at the lake. The water was dark. The mountains were shadows.

"I have been replaying this night for a hundred years," she said. "Trying to find a version where I do something different. Where I save him. Where I forgive him."

"But you could not."

"Because the forgiveness needed to be for myself. Not for him." She turned to Claire. "How do I forgive myself?"

Claire did not have an answer. She was not certain forgiveness was a thing you could do. It was a thing that happened. Or did not.

But she knew one thing.

"You start by leaving," Claire said. "You walk away from this night. You let it end."


Chapter 33: The Companion

Claire searched the hotel for the hidden diamonds.

She studied the floor plans. The walls between rooms. The spaces that did not match.

She measured the corridor outside 307. She calculated the discrepancy.

There was a cavity. About one meter wide. Between the end wall of the corridor and the actual exterior wall.

She tapped the wall. It sounded hollow.

She found a maintenance closet. Inside, a panel at the back of the shelf was loose.

She pulled it open.

Behind the panel was a small space. Dust. A folded uniform from the 1920s.

And a box.

She lifted it out. It was wooden. Hand-carved. The lid was stuck.

She pried it open.

Inside, wrapped in yellowed silk, was the necklace.

All seven diamonds. Untouched for a century.

They caught the light. They glittered. They were beautiful and terrible.

Claire closed the box. She sat on the floor.

She had found the truth. The necklace. The secret. The ghost.

Now she had to decide what to do with it.


Chapter 34: Jean-Luc's Secret

Claire brought the box to Jean-Luc.

He was in the lobby. He looked at the box. He did not open it.

"You found them," he said.

"I did."

He nodded slowly. "I always knew where they were. My father told me on his deathbed. He made me promise to leave them."

"Why?"

"Because they were cursed. They had brought nothing but trouble. Theft. Murder. A woman trapped in time."

Claire set the box on the table between them. "What should we do with them?"

Jean-Luc was silent for a long time. The clock ticked. The lobby was empty.

"The countess's family still exists," he said finally. "A great-niece. She lives in Paris."

"Should I return them to her?"

"The necklace was stolen from the countess. Her family has never known what happened to it." He looked at Claire. "You could give them back. End the story properly."

"But Marguerite?"

"Marguerite needs something else. She needs to know her story is told. That someone remembers her as she was, not just as a ghost in a mirror."

Claire understood. The necklace was one thread. Marguerite was another.

Both needed to be untangled.

"I will return the necklace," she said. "And I will write the truth about Marguerite. Her real story. Not the cover-up."

Jean-Luc smiled. It was the first time she had seen him smile.

"Thank you," he said.


Chapter 35: Victor's Crime

Claire wrote the full account of Victor Morel.

She gathered all the pieces. His childhood in Marseille. His life as a confidence man. His arrival at the Hotel du Lac.

She wrote about the theft. How Victor charmed the countess. How he learned the location of the necklace. How he stole it while she slept.

She wrote about the confrontation. The countess discovering the theft. Victor threatening to expose her poverty.

She wrote about the arrangement. The countess paying Victor to keep quiet. Victor demanding more money.

She wrote about the final night. The argument on the terrace. The shot from above.

She wrote about the cover-up. The sealed police file. The bribes. The silence.

And she wrote about Marguerite. A woman who loved a man she did not know. Who discovered his lies too late. Who spent a century trapped in the moment of his death.

It was not a heroic story. No one was innocent. The countess was desperate. Victor was greedy. The porter was loyal to a fault.

And Marguerite was complicit in her own blindness.

But it was the truth.

Claire printed the account. She filed it in an envelope marked "Room 307."

She would leave it with the hotel archives. A record of what really happened.


Chapter 36: The Real Killer

The question of who killed Victor Morel was more complicated than Claire had thought.

Jean-Luc's father had fired the gun. But the circumstances were ambiguous.

Claire searched for more evidence. She found a letter in Jean-Luc's private papers. A confession. Written by his father in 1970.

"I went to the balcony to stop the argument," the letter read. "Victor had threatened the countess. She was afraid. I was afraid for her.

I did not mean to fire the gun. It was an accident. I was trying to separate them. Victor grabbed my arm. The gun went off.

I have lived with this guilt for forty-five years. I am an old man now. I will die with this secret."

Claire read the letter twice. She believed it.

The killing was not murder. It was a tragic accident. A struggle. A gun that should not have been loaded.

She showed the letter to Jean-Luc.

"I did not know he wrote this," Jean-Luc said.

"It is a confession. He wanted the truth known."

"He was a good man. A loyal man. He made a mistake."

"He made many mistakes. But he was not a killer."

Jean-Luc folded the letter carefully. "What happens now?"

"Now we tell Marguerite. She has been blaming Victor's death on herself. She needs to know the truth."

"The truth may not change her guilt."

"No. But it may help her understand."


Chapter 37: Marguerite's Grief

Claire stepped through the mirror one more time.

She found Marguerite in Room 307. The 1924 version. She was sitting at the vanity, staring at her reflection.

"I know everything," Claire said.

Marguerite did not turn. "What else is there to know?"

"The porter's father. He wrote a confession. The gun went off by accident. Victor's death was not my murder."

"Your murder?"

"Not murder. I did not kill him. But I wanted him dead."

Claire sat on the bed. "You have been punishing yourself for a thought. A thought everyone has at some point."

"You do not understand."

"I understand more than you think."

Marguerite turned. Her eyes were red. "I watched him die. I felt relief. Then guilt. Then I was trapped here. Punishment for my cruelty."

"Or punishment for your self-hatred."

"What is the difference?"

"The difference is that you can stop. You can forgive yourself. You can leave."

Marguerite stood. She walked to the window. The lake was dark.

"I have been here so long I do not know who I am outside this night."

"I can help you find out."

"How?"

"Step through. Into my time. See the world as it is now."

"I do not know if I can."

"There is only one way to find out."


Chapter 38: The Choice

Marguerite stood before the mirror. The surface was dark. It reflected nothing.

"I am afraid," she said.

"I know."

"What if I step through and there is nothing? What if I am not real?"

"You are real. You have been real for one hundred years."

"But I am made of memory. Of this night. Of this room." She touched the glass. "Outside this room, I may not exist."

Claire had no answer. She did not know what would happen if Marguerite left.

"They say the truth sets you free," Marguerite said. "But what if the truth destroys you?"

"Then you will be destroyed knowing the truth. That is better than living a lie."

Marguerite looked at Claire. Her eyes were clear.

"Will you stay with me?"

"For as long as you need."

Marguerite pressed her palm against the mirror. The surface rippled.

She stepped through.

The room was suddenly empty. The mirror reflected the bed. The curtains. The empty chair.

Marguerite was gone.

Claire stood alone in Room 307. She did not know if Marguerite had escaped or dissolved.

She waited.

The room was silent.

Then she heard a voice behind her.

"It worked."


Chapter 39: The Final Night

Claire turned.

Marguerite stood in the center of the room. She was solid. Real. She wore the silver dress from the photograph.

Her face was pale. Her hands trembled.

"I am here," she said. "In your time."

Claire approached her. She touched Marguerite's arm. She was warm. Alive.

"How is this possible?"

"I do not know. But I am here."

Marguerite looked around the room. The faded curtains. The modern lamp. The writing desk covered in papers.

"Everything is different," she said.

"One hundred years. A lot changes."

Marguerite walked to the window. The city lights twinkled in the distance. A car passed on the road.

"I have never seen a car," she said.

"You will see many things."

Marguerite turned to Claire. "What happens now?"

"Now you live. You explore. You find out who you are without the night."

"Will you help me?"

Claire nodded. "I will help you."

Marguerite sat on the edge of the bed. She looked tired. But she was smiling.

"I did not think I would ever leave that room," she said.

"You did not leave it alone. You left because someone finally saw you."

"Thank you."

"You are welcome."

They sat together in the quiet room. The ghost was gone. The woman remained.


Chapter 40: Letting Go

Marguerite spent the night in Claire's room. She did not sleep. She sat by the window and watched the world change.

At dawn, she spoke.

"I remember everything now. The good parts. The bad parts. Victor's face when he laughed. His face when he lied."

Claire listened.

"I loved him. I hated him. I wanted him dead." She paused. "Now I just want to remember him honestly."

"That is all anyone can do."

Marguerite stood. "The room feels different now. Lighter."

Claire felt it too. The oppressive weight was gone. The air was fresh.

"The loop has broken," Claire said.

"I think I broke it. When I stopped running from the truth."

They went downstairs together. The lobby was empty. The sun rose over the lake.

Marguerite walked to the front door. She opened it. She stepped outside.

The gravel crunched under her feet. The morning air was cold.

She looked up at the sky.

"It is beautiful," she said. "The sky. I had forgotten."

Claire joined her on the drive. "You are free."

"Yes." Marguerite's voice was quiet. "I am free."


Chapter 41: Dawn on the Lake

Claire and Marguerite walked to the shore of the lake.

The water was calm. The mountains were pink with sunrise.

"Thank you," Marguerite said. "For not giving up."

"I needed to know the truth as much as you did."

"Why?"

"Because I have been running from something too. Not a loop. A life. I have been hiding in archives. In other people's stories."

Marguerite looked at her. "And now?"

"Now I do not know. But I am not running anymore."

They stood together in silence. The sun rose higher. The light spread across the water.

"What will you do now?" Claire asked.

"Learn. Everything I have missed. Music. Books. Technology."

"You can stay with me. As long as you need."

"Thank you." Marguerite smiled. "I think I would like that."

They walked back to the hotel. The building was no longer menacing. It was just a building. Old. Beautiful. Full of stories.

One of those stories was over.

Another was beginning.


Chapter 42: The Room Changes

Claire returned to Room 307 alone.

The room was different.

The air was fresh. The musty smell was gone. The curtains seemed brighter. The bed seemed softer.

She checked the mirror. It was just a mirror. Her reflection looked back. Normal.

She touched the glass. It was warm.

The room was no longer a prison. It was just a room.

She sat at the writing desk. She looked at the photographs pinned above it. Marguerite in 1924. The countess. The men in their suits.

She took them down. She put them in an envelope.

The story was complete. The room could rest.

She packed her belongings. She had one more thing to do before she left.

She wrote a letter to the hotel management. She recommended that Room 307 be preserved. The original features. The mirror. The writing desk.

It should not be renovated, she wrote. It should be kept as it was. A record of the hotel's history.

A record of what had happened there.

She sealed the letter and left it at the front desk.


Chapter 43: The Necklace Found

Claire opened the wooden box.

The diamonds glittered. Seven stones. Each perfect.

She had found them in the wall. They had been there for one hundred years.

She had a choice. She could keep them. She could sell them. She could return them to the countess's family.

She had already decided.

She called the number Jean-Luc had given her. A woman answered. Her name was Simone. She was the great-niece of Countess Volkova.

"I have something that belongs to your family," Claire said.

"What is it?"

"A necklace. Your great-aunt's diamond necklace."

There was a long silence.

"That necklace was lost in 1924. My grandmother searched for it her whole life."

"I found it. In the Hotel du Lac."

"How?"

"It is a long story."

Simone agreed to meet Claire in Geneva. She would bring identification. She would prove her relation.

Claire hung up. She looked at the diamonds one more time.

They were beautiful. They had caused so much pain.

Now they would go home.


Chapter 44: Returning What Was Lost

Claire met Simone at a cafe in Geneva.

Simone was a woman in her sixties. Elegant. Gray hair. Sharp eyes.

She examined the necklace with reverence.

"This is it," she said. "I have seen it in photographs. My grandmother wore it in her wedding portrait."

"Your great-aunt never told her what happened?"

"She said it was stolen. She never said by whom."

Claire told her the story. The theft. The murder. The cover-up. The century in the walls.

Simone listened without interruption. When Claire finished, she was silent for a long time.

"All these years," Simone said. "And the truth was in a wall."

"Your great-aunt was a victim. She did not know the porter would kill anyone."

"She was also a woman trying to survive. I cannot judge her." Simone held the necklace. "What do you want in return?"

"Nothing. The necklace belongs to your family."

"Then I will thank you." Simone reached across the table. She took Claire's hand. "You have given closure to a story that has haunted my family for a century."

"You are welcome."

Claire left the cafe. She felt lighter. The necklace was gone. The truth was told.

One more thread tied.


Chapter 45: Jean-Luc's Relief

Claire returned to the hotel to say goodbye to Jean-Luc.

He was sitting in his usual chair. The lobby was empty.

"It is done," she said. "Marguerite is free. The necklace has been returned."

Jean-Luc nodded slowly. "I felt it. This morning, the hotel felt different."

"Your father's ghost?"

"I think he is at peace. Finally."

Claire sat beside him. "What will you do now?"

"The same thing I have always done. Watch over the hotel."

"You could retire."

Jean-Luc smiled. "And do what? Sit in a garden? I have been here fifty years. This hotel is my home."

"And the room?"

"Room 307 will remain closed. The management has agreed."

"And the mirror?"

"I will keep it. As a reminder."

"Of what?"

"That some stories need to be told. And that some people need to be seen." He looked at Claire. "You saw her. You saw Marguerite. You gave her peace."

"I gave her the truth. The peace was her own."

"That is the same thing."

Claire stood. She offered her hand. He took it.

"Goodbye, Jean-Luc."

"Goodbye, Mademoiselle Durand. Do not be a stranger."

Claire walked to the door. She looked back. The old porter was still sitting in his chair. Watching. Waiting.

The next night porter would have a different story to tell.


Chapter 46: The Final Archive Entry

Before she left, Claire made one final entry in the hotel archives.

She wrote the true history of Room 307.

Not the cover-up. Not the official story. The truth.

She wrote about Marguerite Delacroix. A woman who arrived at the hotel in love and left in pieces.

She wrote about Victor Morel. A thief who died for his greed.

She wrote about Countess Volkova. A woman who survived the revolution only to be haunted by her own fear.

She wrote about Jean-Luc's father. A porter who made a terrible mistake and spent the rest of his life trying to atone.

She wrote about the diamonds. Seven stones that bought silence and death.

And she wrote about the mirror. A piece of glass that had held a woman for one hundred years.

She sealed the entry in an envelope. She marked it: "For the historian who comes next."

The hotel had many stories. But this one was hers now. She had witnessed it. She had recorded it.

She had set the ghost free.

She closed the archive door and walked upstairs to pack.

Her work was done.


Chapter 47: The Last Night

Claire spent her last night in the Hotel du Lac in Room 307.

The room was quiet. Peaceful. She sat at the writing desk and wrote in her journal.

She wrote about the train journey from Geneva. The first glimpse of the hotel. The strange key. The first night.

She wrote about the music. The ballroom. The woman in the silver dress.

She wrote about the truth. The lies. The cover-up. The release.

She put down the pen. She stood and walked to the mirror.

Her reflection looked back. She studied her face. The tired eyes. The lines around her mouth.

She had arrived a historian. She was leaving something else.

She touched the glass. It was cool. Solid.

"Thank you," she whispered.

She did not know if she was speaking to Marguerite. Or to the room. Or to herself.

It did not matter.

She turned off the light. She lay in the dark. The lake lapped against the shore.

She slept without dreams.

In the morning, she packed her bag. She took the key from the nightstand. She ran her thumb over the brass surface.

Then she left the key on the desk. She closed the door behind her.

She did not look back.


Chapter 48: Closing the Room

The hotel manager met Claire in the lobby.

"Your research is complete?" he asked.

"Yes. The assessment is on the desk in Room 307. With my recommendations."

"Thank you, Mademoiselle Durand."

There was a pause.

"Room 307," Claire said. "I recommend you do not rent it again."

The manager raised an eyebrow. "The renovations will change the layout. That room may not exist in the new plan."

"Good."

He looked at her curiously. "You discovered something during your stay."

"I discovered a story. That is all."

"Will it be published?"

"That is not my decision. The story belongs to the hotel now."

The manager nodded. "Then it will remain in the archives. Where it belongs."

Claire smiled. "That is exactly where it should be."

She walked out of the hotel. The morning was bright. The lake glittered.

She got into a taxi.

"Geneva station," she said.

The taxi pulled away. The hotel shrank in the rear window.

It was just a building now. A beautiful building with a long history.

And one room that held a century of secrets.

The room was empty now. The ghost was gone.

But the story would live on.


Chapter 49: Departure at Dawn

The train pulled out of the station.

Claire found a seat by the window. The lake appeared between buildings. Gray water. White caps.

She thought about Marguerite. She had left her at a small hotel in Montreux. She had given her money. A phone. A list of contacts.

Marguerite would learn. She would adapt. She had a century of catching up to do.

Claire had offered to stay. Marguerite had refused.

"You have your own life," she had said. "Go live it."

They had promised to write. But they both knew the promise was for comfort, not for truth.

The train moved along the shore. The mountains rose on the far side of the lake. The water changed color as the sun climbed. Gray to blue. Blue to silver.

Claire took out her notebook. She wrote the date. She wrote: "The Ghost of the Grand Hotel."

She looked at the words. They felt right.

She closed the notebook. She watched the landscape flow past.

The historian had become part of the history.

The ghost had become a woman.

The room was empty now. But the story was just beginning.

For her. For Marguerite. For the Hotel du Lac.

The train carried her forward. She did not look back.


Chapter 50: The Photograph

Three months later, a package arrived at Claire's apartment in Lyon.

It was from the Hotel du Lac. The return address was Jean-Luc's name.

She opened it carefully.

Inside was a photograph. The 1924 photograph. The one she had pinned above the writing desk.

But it had changed.

The woman in the silver dress was smiling.

Marguerite had been somber in the original. Her expression had been guarded. Defensive.

In this version, she was laughing. Her head tilted back. Her eyes bright.

Claire held the photograph in shaking hands.

There was a note attached. Jean-Luc's handwriting.

"I found this in the archives. It was always there, but I never saw it clearly before. Perhaps you will recognize her."

Claire smiled.

She set the photograph on her desk. She looked at Marguerite's laughing face.

The ghost was gone. The woman remained.

She sat down and wrote a letter. She told Marguerite about the photograph. She told her about Jean-Luc. She told her about the hotel.

She sealed the envelope. She addressed it to the hotel in Montreux.

Then she went back to work. She had other stories to find.

But she carried this one with her always.
