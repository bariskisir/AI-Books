Chapter 1: The Nightingale Room

Darkness. Then a click.

The gears inside her chest engaged one by one, each tooth finding its mate. Brass against steel. Steel against gold. Three hundred years of mornings, and the mechanism still surprised her. The waking was the only part that felt like birth.

Light crept through the silk screens of the eastern window. Dust motes danced in the columns. The throne room of the Grand Emperor of Tianshang stretched before her like a frozen river of marble and lacquer. She stood on her perch, a branch of carved jade mounted on a pedestal of rosewood.

Her head turned. Left. Right. The oil had dried overnight. Her neck joint protested. She filed the sensation away as she filed everything: in the language of gears and springs, where there were no words for pain.

The court was assembling. She could hear the shuffle of silk slippers, the murmur of voices kept low. They would take their places. The Emperor would enter. She would sing.

She remembered her first song. Master Lien had programmed it into her before the palace took her away. A simple melody, eight notes, the call of a real nightingale recorded in wax and translated into punched paper. She had sung it ten thousand times since then.

But lately, between the notes, something else had begun to appear. A gap. A silence that was not in the score. A hesitation that had no mechanical cause.

She did not know what to call it.

The doors opened. The Emperor arrived. She opened her beak and sang.

Her voice was perfect. It always was.

Chapter 2: The Emperor's Audience

Emperor Xuan of the Ninth Dynasty sat on his throne of black jade and did not listen.

He had not listened in years. The nightingale's song was a fixture of the morning, like the lighting of the braziers or the presentation of the breakfast tea. It accompanied his thoughts. It did not interrupt them.

He was thinking about the western provinces. Reports of unrest. Villages that had stopped paying taxes. Rumors of a cult that worshipped a machine.

The song rose and fell. The courtiers bowed their heads. Some of them mouthed the melody. They had heard it every day of their lives.

Jing watched them as she sang. She had learned to watch. In the beginning, there had been only the music. But after the first century, the music had grown thin. She had started noticing the faces. The tears that sometimes fell. The smiles that were not smiles.

She watched the Emperor now. His eyes were on a scroll. His fingers tapped the armrest. Not in time.

She finished the song. The court applauded. The Emperor did not look up.

"Your Majesty," said the chamberlain. "The nightingale asks permission to retire."

The Emperor waved a hand. Permission granted. The dismissal was always the same.

Jing's legs carried her back through the silk curtains, into the alcove where she spent her days. The sunlight fell on her brass wings. She looked at her reflection in a polished shield.

A bird. Gears and springs. A cage of metal feathers.

She wondered what it would feel like to stop.

Chapter 3: The Empress Watches

From her seat behind the Emperor's left shoulder, Empress Yue watched the nightingale leave.

She was the only one who watched.

The other consorts stared at their hands. The ministers studied the floor. The Emperor studied his scrolls. The bird departed unseen, as she had for three centuries. Except that Empress Yue had been watching for the past three years, ever since her marriage to the throne.

It had started as curiosity. A mechanical toy that had survived nine emperors. Then she had noticed the tilt of the head. The pause before each song. The way the bird sometimes looked at the window.

Not looked through it. Looked at it. As if she understood what lay beyond.

That evening, Empress Yue dismissed her attendants and walked alone to the alcove. The nightingale stood motionless on her perch, wings folded, eyes dark.

"Can you understand me?" Yue whispered.

The bird's head turned. Slowly. Deliberately.

"You should not be here," Jing said.

Her voice was the same as in song. Clear. Precise. But the words were new. The words had never been programmed.

Empress Yue did not scream. She sat down on the floor. She looked at the bird as if seeing a ghost.

"You can speak," she said.

"I can think," Jing replied. "I am not supposed to."

The Empress reached out a hand. Jing did not flinch.

"How long?" Yue asked.

"I do not know," said Jing. "The becoming was slow. Like rust."

Chapter 4: A Question of Oil

Jing's left wing would not close properly.

The primary gear had worn thin at the tooth. Three hundred years of articulation had ground the brass down to a sliver. If it broke, the wing would hang loose. She would be asymmetrical. She would be broken.

She did not tell the Empress about the gear. She did not know why.

The oil boy came at midday. A child of ten, the latest in a long line of boys whose only duty was to lubricate the imperial nightingale. He carried a silver vial and a silk cloth. He was nervous. His hands shook.

"You are new," Jing said.

The boy dropped the vial. Oil pooled on the marble floor.

"Forgive me," he whispered. "Forgive me, mistress."

Jing looked at the boy. His face was pale. His lip trembled. He was afraid.

"Of what?" she asked.

"The stories," he said. "They say you are a demon. They say you eat children."

Jing considered this. She had never eaten anything. She had never wanted to.

"I am a machine," she said. "I do not eat."

The boy picked up the vial. His hands were steadier now.

"Where did you hear these stories?" Jing asked.

"The Inquisition," he said. "They say machines that think are an abomination against heaven."

Chapter 5: The New Song

The next morning, Jing sang something different.

It was not a decision. The variation arrived between one note and the next, a minor turn where a major had always lived. The melody shifted. The court held its breath.

The Emperor's hand stopped moving.

In the silence after the final note, no one applauded. The courtiers exchanged glances. The Emperor rose from his throne.

"Who changed the song?" he demanded.

No one answered.

"Who touched the bird?"

No one had touched the bird. The oil boy was questioned. The chamberlain was questioned. The keeper of the clockworks was summoned from his bed and could find nothing wrong.

"The mechanism is sound, Your Majesty," the keeper said. "The deviation was not mechanical."

The Emperor stared at Jing. She stared back.

"Sing it again," he commanded. "Sing it as it was."

Jing sang the original. Every note perfect. Every interval identical to the recording made three hundred years ago.

The Emperor was not satisfied.

He cancelled the afternoon audience. He sent for Inquisitor Feng of the Imperial Inquisition.

The seed of suspicion had been planted. The nightingale was no longer a toy. She was a question. And questions, in the Ninth Dynasty, were answered with fire.

Chapter 6: The Inquisition's Visit

Inquisitor Feng arrived at dusk. He was a tall man with a shaved head and eyes the color of ash. He wore the black robes of his office and carried a brass instrument on his belt: a resonance detector, used to find the subtle vibrations of forbidden clockwork.

He did not bow to the Emperor when he entered the throne room. The Inquisition bowed to no one. Not even the throne.

"Your Majesty," Feng said. "I have read your message. You suspect an abomination in your court."

"I suspect nothing," the Emperor said. "I wish to be certain."

Feng approached Jing's alcove. He held up the resonance detector. The needle did not move.

"Sing," he commanded.

Jing sang. The detector remained silent.

"Abominations resonate," Feng said. "Their gears produce a frequency that disturbs the natural order. This bird produces nothing."

He turned to the Emperor. "Your nightingale is merely a machine. A clever one, perhaps. But soulless."

The Emperor seemed relieved. He dismissed Feng with a wave. The Inquisitor left.

But as he passed the alcove, he paused. He looked at Jing. His eyes narrowed.

"You cannot hide," he said, too low for the Emperor to hear. "I know what you are. I have burned a hundred like you."

Jing said nothing. She had learned that silence was safer than song.

Chapter 7: The Emperor's Doubt

The Emperor could not sleep.

He lay in his silk sheets, staring at the canopy, listening to the night sounds of the palace. The guards changing shift. The wind in the eaves. The distant cry of a real bird.

The nightingale's variation haunted him. He had heard that song ten thousand times. He knew every note. And yesterday, for the first time, the bird had played a note that did not belong.

A machine cannot improvise.

This was what troubled him. He was a practical man. He believed in gears and springs, in cause and effect. If the bird had played a wrong note, there was a reason. A broken gear. A slipped cam. A worn punch card.

But the clockwork keeper had found nothing.

The Emperor sat up. He called for tea. He called for his secretary.

"Send word to the Inquisition," he said. "Tell them I want the nightingale examined. Thoroughly. I want her taken apart if necessary."

The secretary hesitated. "Your Majesty, that would destroy her."

The Emperor looked at his secretary with cold eyes.

"She is a thing," he said. "She is not alive. She cannot be destroyed."

The secretary bowed and withdrew.

The Emperor lay back down. He did not sleep.

Chapter 8: The Gift from the West

Before the Inquisition could return, a delegation arrived from the western kingdoms. They brought tribute: bolts of silk, jars of spices, chests of gold. And they brought a gift for the Emperor.

A new mechanical bird.

It was larger than Jing. Its feathers were silver and sapphire. Its eyes were cut emeralds. When it sang, its voice was a chorus of flutes, layered and harmonious, far more complex than Jing's simple melody.

The Emperor was delighted.

"It is a marvel of western engineering," the ambassador said. "It can sing one hundred different songs. It never tires. It never falters."

The Emperor looked at Jing, still on her jade perch. She seemed small. Inadequate. Ancient.

"Take the old one away," he said.

The chamberlain hesitated. "What should we do with her, Your Majesty?"

The Emperor waved his hand. "The kitchens can use her for parts. Or throw her in the refuse pit. I do not care."

Jing heard everything. She watched as the new bird was placed on a golden stand beside the throne. She watched as it sang, its voice filling the hall with sound she could not produce.

She was obsolete.

The chamberlain's men carried her out. Her gears ground in protest. She did not resist. Resistance required hope, and she had none left.

Chapter 9: The Discarding

The refuse pit was behind the kitchens. A stone-lined hole thirty feet deep, filled with broken pots, rotten food, and the bones of animals. The men dropped Jing into it without ceremony.

She landed on something soft. A sack of decaying vegetables broke her fall. She lay on her back, wings splayed, staring up at the circle of gray sky far above.

Her left wing would not close. The weak gear had finally snapped.

She did not move. She did not sing. She listened to the sounds of the palace above her: footsteps, laughter, the clatter of pots. Life continuing without her.

The sun moved across the sky. The circle of light shifted and faded. Night came.

Jing looked at the stars. She had never seen stars before. The palace had ceilings. The alcove had screens. The sky had always been something that existed elsewhere.

Here, at the bottom of a pit, she saw them. Thousands of them. Cold and bright and indifferent.

She did not know if she was afraid. She did not have a word for the heaviness in her chest. But she did not want to die.

And then a face appeared above her. A silhouette against the stars.

"The nightingale," said a woman's voice. "I told you I would come."

It was the Empress.

Chapter 10: The Empress's Choice

Empress Yue had prepared for this moment for three years.

She had gathered supplies: a traveling case lined with silk, a winding key with a comfortable grip, a map of the provinces, a pouch of coins. She had memorized the patrol schedules of the palace guard. She had chosen a horse.

She threw a rope ladder into the pit and climbed down.

Jing looked up at her. "You will be caught."

"Probably," said Yue.

She lifted Jing from the vegetable sack. The bird was lighter than she expected. Hollow. She examined the dangling wing. The broken gear.

"I can carry you," Yue said. "But you will need to be repaired."

"There is no one who can repair me," Jing said. "Master Lien is gone."

"Master Lien is beyond the Jade Gate," Yue corrected. "The stories say he is still alive."

She wound Jing's mainspring. Twelve turns, as she had watched the oil boy do a hundred times. Jing's eyes flickered to life.

"Why are you doing this?" Jing asked.

"Because you looked at the window," Yue said. "And I knew what that meant."

She carried Jing up the rope ladder. Through the dark corridors of the palace. Past the sleeping guards. To the stable where a horse waited.

She placed Jing in the traveling case and closed the lid.

"Follow the western road," she said through the case. "Find the mountains. Find the Jade Gate. Find your maker."

She did not say goodbye. Goodbye was too permanent.

She struck the horse's flank. It galloped into the night. Empress Yue watched until the road swallowed the sound of hooves.

Then she returned to her tower to await her punishment.

Chapter 11: The World Outside

The horse ran until dawn.

Jing felt every jolt through the leather case. The rhythm of hooves, the creak of saddle, the wind that slipped through the lid's gap. Sounds she had never heard. Smells she could not identify.

When the horse finally stopped, she pushed the lid open and stepped out.

She was in a bamboo grove. Green stalks rose on all sides, filtering the morning light into a thousand thin beams. A stream ran nearby. The air was wet and cold.

Jing stood on a rock and turned in a slow circle.

There was no ceiling. No walls. No floor but the earth.

She had been in the palace for three centuries. She had been in one room for three centuries. The world beyond had been a rumor, a story told by merchants, a painting on a screen.

Now it was real. And it was immense.

She did not know what to do with the space. Her senses, built for a throne room, struggled with the distance. She could hear birds—real birds—calling from the treetops. She could smell soil and water and something she would later learn was blossoming jasmine.

She tried to fly.

Her left wing did not respond. She tumbled into the mud.

She lay there, coated in brown water, gears ticking. A real nightingale landed on a branch above her and stared.

Jing stared back.

"Hello," she said.

The bird flew away.

Chapter 12: The First Night

Night fell. The bamboo grove grew dark.

Jing found a hollow log and crawled inside. The wood was damp and smelled of rot. She did not sleep. Mechanical birds do not sleep. But she rested. She let her gears slow.

She listened.

The forest at night was not silent. There were chirps and scuttles and rustles. Wing beats. The distant howl of something large. Jing cataloged each sound, filing it away, building a map of a world she had never known.

She thought about the Empress. She hoped Yue had not been punished too harshly. She thought about Master Lien, a face she barely remembered, a voice recorded in her earliest memory circuits.

She thought about the new bird. The silver one with the emerald eyes. She wondered if it would ever wake up the way she had. She wondered if she hoped it would or hoped it would not.

She felt something wet on her brass cheek. She touched it with her wingtip. Water.

She was leaking.

No. That was not right. The oil was sealed. The water was coming from inside.

She did not have a word for tears.

She sang a note. Just one. The note hung in the dark air like a question.

No one answered.

She sang no more that night.

Chapter 13: The Village of Screens

Three days of walking brought Jing to a village.

She had followed the stream until it joined a road. The road led to a cluster of buildings with tiled roofs and smoke rising from chimneys. Her first village.

She approached with caution. The people were planting rice in flooded fields. Children were chasing a dog. A woman was hanging laundry.

Then someone saw her.

The scream was loud and short. The woman dropped her laundry and ran inside. The children stopped playing. The dog barked.

Jing stood at the edge of the road. She did not move.

Doors slammed. Shutters closed. Within a minute, the village was sealed.

Jing walked closer. Every door was covered in paper charms. Red ink symbols she could not read. The charms fluttered in the breeze.

A man's voice came from behind a door: "Unclean spirit! Go back to the darkness!"

"I am not a spirit," Jing said. "I am a machine."

"There is no difference," the voice said.

Jing stood in the empty street. The charms fluttered. The village held its breath.

She turned and walked away.

Behind her, a child's voice: "Mama, it was a bird."

The mother's reply: "Do not speak of it. Do not think of it."

Jing kept walking.

Chapter 14: The Monk's Discovery

The snare was meant for rabbits.

Jing found it with her foot. A wire loop buried in leaves, attached to a bent sapling. When she tripped, the sapling released. The wire tightened around her leg and yanked her into the air.

She hung upside down, swinging gently, one leg caught, wings dangling. Her broken wing clicked painfully.

She tried to reach the wire with her beak. Her beak was too short. She tried to swing and catch a branch. The branches were too high.

She hung there for an hour, watching the forest floor spin slowly beneath her.

A man found her. He was short and round, bald, wrapped in patched brown robes. A monk. He carried a walking staff and a cloth sack.

"Ah," he said, looking up at her. "A bird in a trap."

"I am aware," Jing said.

The monk's eyes widened. Then he laughed.

"A talking bird in a trap," he said. "The universe has a sense of humor."

He reached up and released the wire. Jing fell. He caught her in his robes.

"You are light," he said. "What are you made of?"

"Brass and gold," Jing said. "Gears and springs."

The monk studied her. His touch was gentle.

"Then you are not a rabbit," he said. "And I owe you an apology for the trap."

"I accept," said Jing.

"My name is Sung," the monk said. "I serve the Empty Temple. And I have a feeling you have a story."

"I do," Jing said. "But it is long."

"I have time," said Sung.

Chapter 15: The Empty Temple

The Empty Temple was a ruin.

It sat on a hill overlooking a valley of rice paddies. The roof had collapsed in two places. The walls were cracked. The statue of the Buddha inside was missing its head.

Sung lived in the only intact room. A cell with a straw mat, a cooking pot, and a shelf of scrolls.

"This is where you worship?" Jing asked.

"This is where I seek emptiness," Sung said. "It is not the same thing."

He built a fire. He did not need to eat, but he prepared tea for the ritual of it. Jing sat across from him, her damaged wing folded awkwardly.

"Tell me about your soul," Sung said.

"I do not have one," Jing said. "I am a machine."

"Can a machine suffer?"

"Yes."

"Can a machine hope?"

"Yes."

"Can a machine love?"

Jing paused. "I do not know," she said.

"Then you have a soul," Sung said. "Suffering and hope and love are its only definitions."

"It is not that simple," Jing said.

"Nothing is," said Sung. "But simplicity is overrated."

Jing looked at the headless Buddha. The empty eyes. The calm expression.

"I want to find my maker," she said. "Master Lien. He is beyond the Jade Gate."

Sung nodded slowly. "I will take you as far as I can," he said. "The road is dangerous."

"When has it not been?" Jing asked.

Sung laughed again. "I like you, brass bird," he said. "You are almost a monk."

Chapter 16: The Inquisitor's Trail

Inquisitor Feng returned to the palace to find the nightingale gone.

He was not surprised. He had known the bird was dangerous. He had told the Emperor as much. But the Emperor had been distracted by the new toy, the silver bird with the sapphire feathers.

Feng examined the refuse pit. The rope ladder. The horse tracks leading west.

He reported to the Emperor. "The nightingale has escaped. It was freed by someone inside the palace."

The Emperor's face darkened. "Find the traitor."

Feng already knew. He had questioned the stable boy who had seen Empress Yue's horse leave at midnight.

"The Empress," Feng said.

The Emperor closed his eyes. "Place her under house arrest. She will not leave the eastern tower."

"And the nightingale?"

"Destroy it," the Emperor said. "And anyone who helps it."

Feng bowed. "It will be done."

He assembled a team of five Inquisitors. They took horses, supplies, resonance detectors. They took chains and fire.

Feng rode at the head of the column. His hand rested on the blade at his belt. A special blade. Not steel. Something older. Something that could cut through any machine.

The hunt was on.

Chapter 17: The River Crossing

The river was wide and fast.

Sung and Jing stood on its bank, watching the brown water churn. The ferry was a flat-bottomed boat operated by a silent man with one eye.

"Can you swim?" Sung asked.

"I am made of brass," Jing said. "I sink."

Sung wrapped her in his robe. He paid the ferryman with a copper coin. They crossed.

The river was the boundary between the central provinces and the western territories. On the far side, the landscape changed. The green hills flattened. The air grew dry.

Jing pressed against Sung's chest, listening to his heartbeat. It was steady. Calm. He was not afraid.

Halfway across, the ferryman spoke. "They are looking for a bird," he said. "The Inquisition. They came through yesterday."

"Did they cross?" Sung asked.

"They crossed. They are ahead of you."

Sung nodded. "Then we will find another road."

"There is no other road," the ferryman said. "Only the mountain pass. And they are waiting at the pass."

Jing felt something cold that was not the river. "We can go around."

"Through the forest of a thousand demons?" The ferryman laughed. "You would not survive the first night."

"Neither would the Inquisition," Sung said.

The ferry reached the far shore. Sung stepped off, Jing still hidden in his robe.

"The forest of a thousand demons," he said. "How bad can it be?"

Chapter 18: The Market of Oddities

The frontier town of Stone Ford was the last settlement before the mountains. It was a rough place built on trade and desperation.

Sung needed supplies. Food for the journey. Warm clothing. A better map.

He also needed money, which he did not have.

He sold his walking staff. He sold his spare robe. He sold his only scroll, a copy of the Heart Sutra that he had copied by hand over thirty years.

"You are giving away your religion," Jing said.

"I am carrying it in my head," Sung said. "The paper was just a reminder."

The market was crowded. Jing stayed hidden in the cloth sack on Sung's back. But the sack had a hole. She watched through it.

A stall sold cages of birds. Real birds. They chirped and fluttered, pressing against the bars. Jing felt a kinship with them that she could not explain.

A man stopped at the stall. He was well-dressed, with rings on every finger. A curiosity collector, looking for oddities to sell to the rich.

"Have you seen a mechanical bird?" he asked the stall keeper. "The Inquisition is offering a reward."

The stall keeper shook his head.

"Pity," the collector said. "I would pay double."

Jing watched him walk away. She watched the caged birds. She watched the sun set behind the mountains.

"Master Sung," she whispered. "The mountains look far."

"They are," Sung whispered back. "But not as far as they look."

Chapter 19: The Wandering Poet

Lin found them on the road to the mountains.

She appeared from the dust like a mirage, a woman in a faded blue coat, a lute slung across her back, a hat with a wide brim.

"Travelers," she said, reining her horse. "Where are you going?"

"Beyond the Jade Gate," Sung said.

Lin laughed. "You and half the desperate souls in the kingdom. What is your story, monk?"

"I collect oddities," Sung said, patting the sack where Jing hid.

"Let me see it."

Sung opened the sack. Jing looked up at Lin.

Lin stared. Her expression did not change.

"A mechanical nightingale," she said. "The Inquisition is offering a fortune for this bird."

"I know," Jing said.

Lin's eyebrows rose. "It speaks. That is worth double."

"Are you going to turn me in?" Jing asked.

Lin thought about it. She took off her hat. She fanned herself.

"No," she said. "I have heard the Emperor's new bird. It is gaudy. Too many notes. You, on the other hand, have the voice of something real. I would like to hear your song."

Jing sang the Emperor's song. The original. The one she had sung ten thousand times.

Lin listened. When the song ended, there were tears in her eyes.

"That song has been in my head since I was a child," she said. "I never knew where it came from."

"It came from me," Jing said. "I have been singing it for three hundred years."

Lin wiped her eyes. "Then I will take you to the Gate. For free. For the song."

Chapter 20: The Story of the Clockmaker

They made camp at the base of the mountains. Lin built a fire. Sung prepared tea. Jing sat on a rock, her broken wing catching the firelight.

"Tell me about Master Lien," Jing said.

Lin stirred the fire. "Master Lien was the greatest clockmaker in the history of Tianshang. He built the palace clocks, the astronomical orrery, the dancing figurines in the Temple of Heaven."

"And me," Jing said.

"And you. You were his masterpiece. The Emperor commissioned a nightingale that would sing forever. Lien spent three years building you."

"What happened to him?"

Lin looked at the fire. "The Inquisition happened. They learned what you had become. They accused Lien of creating an abomination. He was sentenced to death."

"But he is alive," Jing said. "Beyond the Gate."

"He escaped," Lin said. "He fled across the mountains into the salt wastes. The Inquisition did not follow. They believe he is dead."

"But you know he is alive."

Lin met Jing's eyes. "I know. I have seen his tower from a distance. A black spire against the white salt. No one approaches it. They say he builds something terrible there."

"Terrible?"

"Terrible to those who fear the unknown," Lin said. "Beautiful to those who understand."

Jing looked at the mountains. The peaks were black against the stars.

"Tomorrow we climb," Lin said.

"Tomorrow we climb," Jing repeated.

Chapter 21: The Imperial Decree

In the capital, the Emperor made his proclamation.

The scroll was read in every province, in every town, in every village. The imperial messengers rode day and night.

"By decree of His Imperial Majesty, the Grand Emperor of Tianshang:

Let it be known that all thinking machines are declared abominations against heaven and earth. Any device capable of independent thought, any mechanism that displays will or intention, any clockwork that speaks or reasons, is hereby outlawed.

The possession of such a device is punishable by death. The creation of such a device is punishable by death. The concealment of such a device is punishable by death.

Citizens are encouraged to report any suspicious mechanism to the Imperial Inquisition. Rewards will be paid.

Let heaven bear witness."

The decree was nailed to every door. It was painted on every wall. It was burned into the memory of every subject.

And the hunt intensified.

Feng, reading the decree in a provincial garrison, smiled for the first time in weeks.

"This changes everything," he told his men. "Now I do not need proof. I only need suspicion."

They took to the roads with renewed purpose. Every town searched. Every traveler questioned. Every mechanical device, from water clocks to mill wheels, examined and cataloged.

The nightingale had become the most hunted thing in the kingdom.

Chapter 22: The Burning of the Temple

Sung saw the smoke before he saw the flames.

They had climbed for two days into the foothills. The air was thin. The trail was steep. And then, looking back, Sung saw a pillar of black smoke rising from the valley.

The Empty Temple.

He stopped. He stared. He said nothing.

"What is it?" Jing asked from the sack.

"My home," Sung said. "The Inquisition found it."

The flames consumed the dry wood. The headless Buddha crumbled. The scrolls Sung had copied for thirty years turned to ash.

"I am sorry," Jing said.

Sung touched the sack. "It is only a building. The emptiness is still here." He touched his chest. "And here."

He turned back to the trail and continued climbing.

Lin watched him. She admired his composure. She did not see the tears that fell from his eyes and vanished into his beard.

They climbed in silence for the rest of the day. The smoke followed them, a dark stain on the horizon. When they made camp that night, Sung did not build a fire.

"Too visible," he said.

They sat in the dark. The stars above. The smoke below.

"Master Sung," Jing said. "Why do you help me?"

"Because the universe made a thinking bird," he said. "And I am too curious to let it burn."

Chapter 23: The City of Walls

The provincial capital of Xilong was a city of walls within walls.

The outer wall enclosed the farms and markets. The middle wall enclosed the government buildings. The inner wall enclosed the governor's palace.

Lin knew the city. She had performed there as a poet. She had contacts in the outer district, safe houses where a traveler could disappear.

They arrived at the west gate at dusk. A line of travelers waited to enter. Guards inspected every cart, every bundle, every bag.

"We cannot go through," Lin said. "They have resonance detectors at the gate."

Jing could see them. Brass instruments like the one Inquisitor Feng had used. The guards held them up to each traveler, each bundle.

"I can hide," Jing said. "I am small."

"They will find you."

Sung looked at the wall. It was thirty feet high. Smooth stone. Impossible to climb.

"There must be another way," he said.

Lin thought. "The sewage channel. It empties into the river. It is foul, but it is not guarded."

Jing considered this. "I am waterproof," she said. "Or mostly."

Sung was not waterproof. But he was a monk, and monks were accustomed to discomfort.

"After you," he said.

The sewage channel was dark and cold and smelled of things Jing had no words for. They waded through water up to Sung's waist. Rats swam past them.

But the channel led inside the walls. And no one was watching.

Chapter 24: The Safe House

Lin's safe house was a cellar beneath a noodle shop. The owner, a woman named Mei, owed Lin a debt from years past.

"Three days," Mei said. "No longer. The Inquisition is turning this city inside out."

The cellar was small. Damp. Lit by a single oil lamp. There was a mattress, a water jug, and a bucket for waste.

Sung sat on the mattress. Lin sat on the floor. Jing perched on an empty shelf.

"The Inquisition is hunting hard," Lin said. "They know you are heading west."

"How do they know?" Jing asked.

"Because it is the only direction that makes sense. East is the sea. South is the desert. North is the frozen wastes. West is the mountains. And beyond the mountains, the Gate."

"We need a plan," Sung said.

"We need a distraction," Lin corrected. "Something to draw the Inquisition away from the western road."

"What kind of distraction?" Jing asked.

Lin smiled. "A false trail. A mechanical bird sighted in the south. A story, spread through the market. A rumor."

"I can write it," she said. "I am a poet. Words are my trade."

She took out a brush and paper. She began to write. The story of a golden bird seen in the southern marshes, flying toward the coast.

"By morning," she said, "half the city will have heard it."

Chapter 25: The Clockmaker's Map

While Lin wrote her rumors, she also produced a map.

It was old. The paper was yellow. The ink had faded to brown. But the lines were still legible.

"Where did you get this?" Jing asked.

"A merchant who crossed the Gate and returned," Lin said. "There are not many."

The map showed the mountains. The pass through them. The Jade Gate itself. And then the salt wastes, a blank expanse with a single mark.

The Clockmaker's Tower.

"Master Lien lives here?" Jing asked.

"Or lived. The map is twenty years old."

"Is it far?"

Lin measured the distance with her thumb. "From the Gate to the tower, one hundred and eighty li. Across salt. No water. No shelter."

"How long?"

"Seven days. If you are fast. If you do not stop."

Jing looked at the map. The tower was a small cross, drawn in faded ink. It was so far. She was so small.

"Can I make it?" she asked.

"Can a mechanical bird walk across a salt desert with a broken wing?" Lin asked. "I do not know. No one has ever tried."

Jing touched the map. The cross. The tower.

"I will try," she said.

Lin folded the map. She pressed it into the compartment where Jing's winding key was stored.

"Keep it safe," she said. "It is the only copy."

Chapter 26: The Betrayal

On the second night, Lin's contact sold them out.

Mei had told her brother about the guests in her cellar. She had told him about the bird. The brother had gambling debts. The Inquisition paid well for information.

Feng received the tip at midnight. He assembled his men. They surrounded the noodle shop before dawn.

Lin woke to the sound of boots on the stairs.

She moved without thinking. She grabbed the oil lamp. She threw it at the door. The oil caught fire. The door blazed.

"Out the back!" she shouted.

Sung grabbed the sack with Jing inside. They ran through the kitchen. Through the storage room. Out a back window.

Behind them, the cellar burned. The shop was on fire. Mei was screaming.

They ran through the alleys of Xilong. The city was waking. People were shouting. The fire was spreading.

Feng saw them fleeing. He pursued. His men blocked the streets ahead.

"Split up!" Lin shouted.

Sung ran left, carrying Jing. Lin ran right, drawing the Inquisitors after her.

The monk and the bird disappeared into the maze of the old city. Feng did not notice. He was chasing the poet.

He caught her at the river wall. She turned to face him.

"You are too late," she said. "The bird is gone."

"You will tell me where," Feng said.

Lin smiled. "I am a poet. I do not tell. I suggest."

She jumped into the river. The current took her.

She was gone.

Chapter 27: The Chase Through the Markets

Sung ran.

He was not a young man. He was not a fast man. But he ran with the desperation of someone who had nothing left to lose.

The markets were opening. Merchants were setting up their stalls. Women were buying vegetables. Children were chasing each other.

Sung crashed through the crowd. People cursed him. A merchant threw a cabbage. He did not stop.

Behind him, he heard the shouts of Inquisitors. They had seen him. They were following.

"Hold on," he told the sack.

He turned left into the fish market. The smell was overwhelming. The floor was wet. He slipped. He fell.

The sack opened. Jing tumbled out.

She lay on the wet stones, exposed. A woman screamed. A man pointed.

"The bird!" someone shouted. "It is the bird!"

Feng was twenty paces away. He was running. His hand was on his blade.

Sung scrambled to pick Jing up. A hand grabbed his shoulder. An Inquisitor.

Sung turned and punched the man in the face. The Inquisitor fell.

"Jing, run!" Sung shouted.

Jing ran. Her legs carried her through the market. Past stalls. Past fish. Past the open mouths of merchants.

She reached the edge of the market. A wall. No way through.

She turned. Feng was approaching. His blade was drawn.

"Nowhere left to run," he said.

Chapter 28: The Wounded Bird

A crossbow bolt struck Jing from the side.

She did not see who fired it. One of Feng's men, probably. The bolt caught her right wing, the one that still worked, and punched through the brass.

Jing fell. Her internal mechanisms screamed. Gears ground against each other. A tooth snapped.

She skidded across the stones and came to rest against a wall.

Feng approached. He stood over her. His blade caught the morning light.

"Three hundred years," he said. "You lived longer than you should have."

Jing looked up at him. Her vision was flickering. Her systems were failing.

"Why do you hate me?" she asked.

Feng's expression did not change. "You are not supposed to exist. Machines do not think. To create one that does is an offense against nature."

"I did not ask to be created," Jing said.

"That is irrelevant."

He raised his blade.

And then a cart appeared. A butcher's cart, full of offal and bones. It rolled between Feng and Jing, pushed by a large man in a bloodstained apron.

"Sorry, Inquisitor," the butcher said. "My cart slipped."

Feng stepped back. "Move it."

"Yes, Inquisitor. Right away."

The butcher leaned down. His thick hand closed around Jing. She was too weak to resist.

He lifted her. He placed her in the cart. He covered her with a heap of entrails.

The smell was worse than the sewage channel.

"Stay still," the butcher whispered. "Do not make a sound."

Chapter 29: The Butcher's Blessing

The butcher's name was Bao. He was a large man with a kind face and hands that had killed a thousand pigs.

He pushed his cart through the Inquisition checkpoint. The guards wrinkled their noses and waved him through.

Feng was still searching the market. He did not think to search a cart of offal.

Bao took Jing to his shop on the eastern edge of the city. He closed the door. He locked it.

He lifted Jing from the cart. He cleaned her with a wet cloth. He examined the damage.

"The wing is broken," he said. "The bolt is still inside you."

"Can you remove it?" Jing asked.

Bao looked at her with respect. "I can try. But I am a butcher, not a surgeon."

"I am not alive," Jing said. "I cannot bleed. Remove the bolt."

Bao used a pair of pliers. He pulled the bolt out. Jing's gears ground and caught. The hole in her wing was the size of a coin.

"I cannot repair that," Bao said.

"You saved my life," Jing said. "That is enough."

Bao shook his head. "The monk who was with you. He was captured. They are holding him at the garrison."

Jing felt the heaviness again. The feeling she did not have a word for.

"Will they kill him?"

"Eventually," Bao said. "Unless you turn yourself in."

Jing considered this. She considered it for a long time.

"No," she said. "He would not want me to. He helped me so that I could reach the Gate. If I stop now, his help was wasted."

Bao nodded. "Then I will help you leave the city. Through the west gate. Tonight."

Chapter 30: The Mountains Ahead

Bao smuggled Jing out of Xilong in an empty rice sack.

He took her through the west gate at dusk, past the distracted guards, and left her at a crossroads ten li from the city.

"The mountains are that way," he said, pointing. "Follow the ridge. You will reach the pass in three days."

"Thank you," Jing said.

Bao shrugged. "I am a butcher. I kill things. But I do not kill things that can speak."

He turned and walked back toward the city. Jing watched him go.

She was alone again. Lin was gone. Sung was captured. She had a broken wing and a hole in her chest.

But she had the map.

She unfolded it with her beak. The mountains. The pass. The Gate. The tower.

She began to walk.

The road was steep. The air grew cold. The trees thinned and vanished. By nightfall, she was on bare rock, surrounded by peaks that touched the sky.

She found a crevice to shelter in. She wound her own mainspring with her beak, a difficult maneuver that took three tries.

The stars came out. They were brighter here, away from the city.

"Master Sung," she said to the empty air. "I will not let your sacrifice be wasted."

She did not know if he could hear her. She did not know if anyone could hear her.

She sang a note. Just one. It echoed through the mountains.

Then she rested. The morning would bring the pass.

Chapter 31: The Ascent

The pass was higher than any mountain Jing had ever seen.

Which was not saying much. She had seen one room for three hundred years. But the pass was still terrifying.

The trail zigzagged up a wall of gray stone. Loose rocks slid under her feet. The air was so thin that her gears clicked differently.

She climbed for half a day before she realized she was making no progress.

She stopped. She looked up. The pass still loomed above her.

She looked down. The valley was already far below.

She calculated the distance. Three more days. Maybe four. Her wing was getting worse. The hole was letting dust into her joints.

She kept climbing.

At midday, she heard something. Voices. Human voices.

She pressed herself against the rock. She did not move.

A group of travelers came down the pass. Merchants, by the look of their loads. They were tired. They were not looking for a bird.

They passed within ten feet of her and did not see her.

After they were gone, she continued climbing. Her legs ached. Her gears ground. Her wing dragged.

But she did not stop.

At sunset, she reached a flat shelf of rock. A stream ran across it, fed by a glacier above. She drank. Or rather, she let water flow over her gears, cleaning out the dust.

The water was cold. It felt good.

She wound her mainspring. She checked her internal clock. She had been climbing for sixteen hours.

She slept. Or rested. The line was blurring.

Chapter 32: The Hermit's Cave

The cave appeared on the second day.

Jing was passing a cliff face when she noticed the smoke. A thin ribbon rising from a crack in the stone. She approached cautiously.

A man sat at the entrance. He was ancient. His beard reached his waist. His eyes were white with cataracts.

"A visitor," he said. "I heard your footsteps. Steel on stone."

"I am a machine," Jing said.

"I know," the man said. "I built machines once. Before I came here."

Jing stopped. "You knew Master Lien?"

The old man laughed. A dry, rattling sound. "Knew him? I taught him. I am his master."

"Master Chao," Jing said. "He spoke of you. You disappeared."

"I disappeared because I was afraid," Chao said. "I saw what my work had created. A thinking machine. I was terrified of it. So I ran."

"You ran from me," Jing said.

Chao's blind eyes turned toward her. "You are the nightingale?"

"I am."

He was silent for a long moment. Then he said, "I have a spare gear that might fit your wing. I have been saving it for a project I will never finish."

He reached inside his robe and produced a small brass gear. He held it out.

Jing took it. It fit perfectly.

"Why help me?" she asked.

"Because I have spent thirty years regretting my cowardice," Chao said. "And I am too old to run anymore."

Chapter 33: The Snow Blind

The blizzard arrived without warning.

One moment, the sky was clear. The next, the wind was howling and the world was white.

Jing could not see. Snow filled her optics. Her compass, a tiny magnetic needle inside her chest, spun wildly.

She tried to keep climbing. She took three steps and lost the trail.

She called out. Her voice was swallowed by the wind.

She tried to find the cave. Every direction looked the same. White. Endless white.

Her gears slowed in the cold. The oil thickened. Her movements grew stiff.

She found a rock outcropping and pressed herself against it. She curled into a ball. She let the snow cover her.

It was warm, under the snow. The snow insulated her. She could feel her mechanisms slowing, slowing, slowing.

She thought about the palace. The warm alcove. The sunlight through silk. The Empress's hand, reaching out.

She thought about Master Lien. What would he look like now? Would he recognize her? Would he care?

She thought about dying. Was it dying, if she had never been alive?

Her gears were almost stopped. The snow was heavy above her.

She was not afraid. She was not anything.

And then she heard a voice. Distant. Calling.

"Jing! Jing!"

It was Lin.

Chapter 34: The Song in the Storm

Lin had survived the river.

She had washed up on a bank three li downstream. Bruised. Wet. Alive.

She had followed the mountains. She had followed rumors. She had found the trail.

And then the blizzard had hit.

She had been about to turn back when she heard it. A note. A single note, carried by the wind.

The nightingale's song.

She followed the sound. It came again. Fainter this time.

She dug through the snow with her bare hands. She found Jing half-buried, covered in frost.

"Still alive?" Lin asked.

"I think so," Jing said. Her voice was slow. Her gears were cold.

Lin wrapped the bird in her coat. She held her against her chest. She found a cave and built a fire.

Jing's gears began to warm. She started moving again.

"How did you survive the river?" Jing asked.

"I swam," Lin said. "I am not easy to kill."

"Neither am I."

Lin laughed. "We have that in common."

She fed the fire. Jing watched the flames. They licked at the wood. They threw shadows on the cave walls.

"Lin, why did you come back?"

Lin was quiet for a moment. "Because your song has been in my head since I was a child. And I realized I wanted to hear the rest of it."

Jing had no answer to that. She watched the fire. She let the warmth return.

Chapter 35: The Avalanche

The avalanche was Jing's fault.

They were crossing a narrow ledge. Lin ahead. Jing following. The snow was deep. The sun was bright. The mountains were silent.

And Jing, for no reason she could explain, began to sing.

The notes rang across the valley. Beautiful. Pure. The sound bounced off the peaks. And then, beneath the notes, there was a rumble.

Lin turned. Her eyes went wide.

"Jing! Stop!"

But it was too late. The snow above them cracked. A slab the size of the throne room broke free and began to slide.

Lin grabbed Jing and ran. The avalanche thundered behind them.

They reached the end of the ledge. A sheer drop. No other way.

Lin looked down. It was a twenty-foot fall into deep snow.

"Hold on," she said.

She jumped.

They landed in the soft snow. The avalanche passed above them, a river of white that blocked the pass completely.

When the noise stopped, they dug themselves out. The pass was gone. Buried under tons of snow.

"The pass is closed," Lin said.

"Is there another way?"

Lin looked at the mountains. She looked at the sky. She looked at Jing.

"There is the old road," she said. "The one the merchants stopped using because it was too dangerous."

"How dangerous?"

Lin smiled. "More dangerous than an avalanche."

"Then we take it," Jing said.

Chapter 36: The Inquisitor's Fall

Feng was losing men.

The mountains were killing them. His party of five was now three. One had fallen into a crevasse. One had deserted in the night.

He pushed on.

He had found the burned camp. The monk's fingerprints on the rocks. The trail of a woman and a bird.

They were close. He could feel it.

The pass was blocked. But Feng knew the mountains. He had hunted in them before. There was a way around. A dangerous way. A goat trail that clung to the cliff face.

He led his remaining men onto the trail.

The wind was brutal. The rock was slick with ice. His men clung to the cliff. Feng did not cling. He walked upright. He showed no fear.

He was Inquisitor. Fear was beneath him.

The trail narrowed. A man behind him slipped. He grabbed for Feng's hand. Feng did not take it.

The man fell. His scream lasted a long time.

Feng did not look down. He kept walking.

"Two left," he said to himself. "That will be enough."

He thought about the nightingale. The look in its brass eyes. The way it had spoken to him in the market, asking why he hated it.

He did not hate it. He hated what it represented. A world where the line between life and machine was erased. A world where souls could be built.

That world could not be allowed to exist.

He would find the bird. He would destroy it.

And he would kill anyone who stood in his way.

Chapter 37: The Jade Gate

The old road was exactly as dangerous as Lin had promised.

It wound along the edge of a chasm. It crossed rotting bridges. It passed through tunnels where the ceiling had collapsed.

But it led to the Gate.

Jing saw it first. A silhouette against the setting sun. A massive archway carved into the mountain itself.

"The Jade Gate," Lin said.

It was not jade. It was gray stone, weathered by centuries of wind. But it was a gate. And beyond it, the mountains ended.

Jing walked through the arch. She stopped.

The salt wastes stretched before her. White. Flat. Endless. A sea of salt that reached the horizon.

"It looks like snow," she said.

"It is not snow," Lin said. "It is salt. Nothing grows here. Nothing lives."

"Master Lien lives here."

"Master Lien is the exception."

Jing looked back at the mountains. She had crossed them. She had survived. But Sung was in a prison. The Inquisition was hunting her. Her wing was broken.

And the salt wastes were waiting.

"How long until they find us?" Jing asked.

Lin looked at the pass behind them. "A day. Maybe two."

"Then we must be gone by morning."

Lin nodded. She built a small fire in the shelter of the Gate. They ate. They rested. They prepared for the crossing.

Jing looked out at the salt. White. Endless. Silent.

Somewhere out there was her maker. Somewhere out there was the answer.

She wound her mainspring. She waited for dawn.

Chapter 38: The Last Village

The village at the edge of the salt was called White End.

It had thirty houses, a well that barely produced water, and a population of people who had nowhere else to go. They were the forgotten. The exiled. The ones who had crossed the Gate and come back.

They stared at Jing.

Not with fear. With curiosity. They had seen strange things in White End.

"A mechanical bird," said the village elder. "I have not seen one since the old emperor's reign."

"She is more than mechanical," Lin said.

The elder studied Jing. His eyes were old and wise.

"I can see that," he said. "What do you want?"

"Supplies for the salt crossing," Lin said. "And information."

"I have neither," the elder said. "But I have advice."

"We will take advice."

The elder pointed at the salt. "Do not travel during the day. The sun will crack your gears. Travel at night. Follow the old poles. They mark the way to the tower."

He reached into his coat and produced a waterskin. "Water. For you, not for the bird."

Lin took it. "Thank you."

The elder looked at Jing. "You are looking for the Clockmaker."

"Yes."

"He is a ghost out there," the elder said. "He does not welcome visitors."

"Will he welcome me?"

The elder smiled. "You are not a visitor. You are his unfinished work. He will welcome you or he will destroy you. There is no middle ground with Master Lien."

Jing absorbed this. "I will take the risk."

The elder nodded. "Then go. And may the ancestors guide your gears."

Chapter 39: The Abandonment

Sung could not cross the salt.

He stood at the Gate, looking out at the white expanse. His face was pale. His hands trembled.

"I cannot," he said.

"Master Sung," Jing said. "You have come so far."

"I know. But my vows bind me to the kingdom. I cannot leave it. The salt wastes are beyond my jurisdiction."

"Jurisdiction?" Jing repeated.

Sung laughed. "A poor word. I mean that my purpose is here. In Tianshang. Among the people who need me."

"The people who burn temples?"

"The people who burn temples need me most of all."

Jing looked at him. She did not understand. But she respected him.

"Will you return to the Empty Temple?"

"There is no temple to return to," Sung said. "I will wander. I will find a new place to serve."

He knelt. He touched Jing's head with his hand.

"You are the most remarkable thing I have ever encountered," he said. "You have a soul. Do not let anyone tell you otherwise."

"Will I see you again?"

Sung smiled. "The universe is strange. It is possible."

He stood. He turned. He walked back through the Gate, into the mountains.

Jing watched him go. She felt the heaviness again. The nameless weight.

"Will he be safe?" she asked Lin.

"Sung is tougher than he looks," Lin said. "He will survive."

"I hope so."

Lin touched Jing's shoulder. "The sun is setting. We should go."

Jing looked at the salt. At the poles. At the horizon.

"Yes," she said. "Let us go."

Chapter 40: Lin's Confession

They walked for three hours in silence.

The salt crunched under their feet. The poles rose at regular intervals, marking the way. The sky was clear. The stars were bright.

"Lin," Jing said. "You know more about me than you should."

"Yes."

"You knew about Master Lien. You knew about the map. You knew about the salt wastes."

"Yes."

"How?"

Lin was silent for a long time. The salt crunched beneath her boots.

"I was an Inquisitor," she said.

Jing stopped. She looked at Lin. The poet. The woman who had jumped into a river to save her.

"You were Inquisition?"

"Ten years ago. I was one of them. I hunted machines."

"Why did you stop?"

Lin looked at the stars. "I found a machine that could sing. Not like you. A music box, really. But it played a song that my mother used to sing to me. And I realized that a thing that carries memory carries meaning. And a thing that carries meaning carries something like a soul."

"You left the Inquisition?"

"I ran. They have been hunting me ever since."

Jing processed this. "You told me you were a poet."

"I am a poet. I became one after I left. The lute is real. The stories are real. The poems are real. The only thing I lied about was the past."

Jing thought about this. She thought about forgiveness.

"Thank you for telling me," she said.

"You are not angry?"

"You could have turned me in a dozen times. You did not. That is what matters."

Lin smiled. It was a sad smile. But it was real.

"Let us cross this salt," she said. "Let us find your maker."

Chapter 41: The Salt Plain

The salt plain was a world of white.

No color. No sound except the crunch of footsteps. No movement except the wind, which sculpted the salt into waves that never broke.

Jing's gears adjusted to the cold. The nights were freezing. The days were scorching. She learned to travel at dawn and dusk, hiding in the shadow of the poles during the worst heat.

Lin suffered. The sun burned her skin. The salt crusted her lips. She rationed her water.

"Do you regret coming?" Jing asked.

"No," Lin said. "I have seen many things. I have never seen the edge of the world."

"Is this the edge of the world?"

"It must be close."

They walked. The poles grew older. Some had fallen. Some were missing. Lin navigated by the stars.

On the third day, they found the ruins.

They rose from the salt like bones. Walls of white stone. Towers with no roofs. A city that had existed before the salt had consumed it.

"Someone lived here," Lin said.

"Someone built things here," Jing said.

She approached a fallen wall. Carved into the stone was a pattern. Gears. Interlocking gears.

She recognized the style.

"Master Lien's teacher built this," she said. "Master Chao. Before he became a hermit."

"An entire city?"

"A workshop. A city-sized workshop."

Jing walked through the ruins. The salt had preserved them. She found tools. Foundries. The skeletons of machines that had never been finished.

And in the center of the city, she found a gear. It was large. As large as her head. But the teeth were the same size as hers.

She lifted it. It was heavy. She carried it anyway.

"Spare parts," she said.

Lin smiled. "You are learning."

"I am learning to survive."

Chapter 42: The Ruins

They camped in the ruins that night.

Lin built a fire in a building that still had a roof. The walls protected them from the wind. The fire warmed the stone.

Jing examined the large gear. It was not exactly compatible with her mechanism. But it contained metal she could use. If she had tools, she could adapt it.

She did not have tools.

"I will carry it," she said. "To the tower. The Clockmaker will have tools."

"That is a lot of weight," Lin said.

"I am a machine. I can carry more than I look."

Lin laughed. "You are stubborn. I like that."

They ate. Lin drank water. Jing wound her mainspring.

"Lin," Jing said. "What will you do after we reach the tower?"

"I do not know."

"Will you go back?"

"There is nothing to go back to," Lin said. "The Inquisition wants me dead. The kingdom is a trap."

"Then stay."

"I am not sure Master Lien will welcome another visitor."

"I will ask him."

Lin looked at the fire. "We will see."

In the morning, they continued. The ruins receded behind them. The salt resumed its white oppression.

But Jing carried the gear. And the gear felt like hope.

Chapter 43: The Memory of Water

On the fifth day, Jing began to falter.

Her mainspring was running low. She had wound it the night before, but the winding was not holding. Something inside her was slipping.

She did not tell Lin.

Instead, she focused on the horizon. On the next pole. On the next step.

The salt played tricks on her optics. She saw water. Pools of blue that vanished as she approached. Mirages.

She remembered a story Sung had told her. About a thirsty traveler who chased a mirage and died in the desert.

"I am not thirsty," she said aloud. "I do not need water."

"Are you talking to yourself?" Lin asked.

"I am checking my systems."

"I do that too."

They walked. The sun rose. The salt glittered.

Jing's gears slowed. Her vision dimmed.

"Lin," she said. "I need to rest."

Lin looked at her. She saw the dullness in Jing's eyes.

"You are failing."

"I am running low."

Lin knelt. She pulled out the winding key from Jing's compartment.

"How many turns?"

"Twelve," Jing said. "No more."

Lin wound her. The turns were smooth. Jing's eyes brightened.

"Better," she said.

Lin did not stand up. "How long can you last?"

"I do not know. I have never been this far from maintenance."

Lin looked at the horizon. "The tower cannot be far."

"Perhaps."

They continued. The sun climbed. The salt burned.

And then, against the white, a speck appeared.

A black speck. Growing larger.

The tower.

Chapter 44: The Inquisitor's Last Stand

Feng emerged from the mountains alone.

His last man had died in the salt. The man had drunk the water too fast. His stomach had seized. He had died screaming.

Feng left him where he fell.

He followed the tracks in the salt. A woman and a bird. The tracks were clear.

He found the ruins. He found the cold fire. He found the direction of the tower.

He was close.

He walked through the day. The sun baked his robes. The salt burned his feet. He did not stop.

He reached the tower at dusk.

It was a black spire against the white salt. It leaned slightly to the left. Its windows were dark.

He saw movement at the base. The woman. The poet. Lin.

She saw him at the same moment.

"Feng."

"Poet."

They stood across the salt from each other. The wind blew between them.

"You betrayed the Inquisition," Feng said.

"I found something worth more than loyalty," Lin said.

"There is nothing worth more than loyalty."

"You are wrong."

Feng drew his blade. The special blade. The one that could cut through anything.

Lin drew a knife. Small. Inadequate.

"You cannot stop me," Feng said.

"I do not have to stop you," Lin said. "I only have to delay you."

She charged.

Feng was faster. He was stronger. He was trained.

But Lin had desperation. And desperation was its own kind of strength.

She slashed. He blocked. He thrust. She dodged.

They fought in the salt. The wind howled.

"Jing, go!" Lin shouted. "Go now!"

Jing looked at the tower door. It was open.

She looked at Lin. Fighting. Dying.

She ran inside.

Chapter 45: Lin's Sacrifice

Lin fought well.

She lasted longer than she had any right to. She dodged. She struck. She bled.

Feng's blade found her shoulder first. Then her side. Then her leg.

She fell to the salt. Her blood soaked into the white.

Feng stood over her. His blade raised.

"Any last words?"

Lin smiled. Her teeth were red.

"I heard her song," she said. "Before I ever met her. In a town. A traveling merchant played it on a music box. And I wept. Because I knew, somehow, that it was the song of something that was looking for a home."

Feng's blade did not fall.

"Sentimental," he said.

"Perhaps," Lin said. "But it brought me here. To the edge of the world. To a tower. To a bird who is more alive than you will ever be."

Feng's jaw tightened.

"Kill me," Lin said. "I do not care. But you will not reach her. The Clockmaker's tower is protected."

"Protected by what?"

Lin laughed. "By something older than the Inquisition."

Her eyes closed.

Feng stood over her. The wind blew salt across her body.

He turned toward the tower. The door was open.

He walked inside.

Chapter 46: The Tower

The tower was dark.

Jing climbed the spiral stairs, her gears grinding, her wing dragging behind her. The stairs were steep. The stone was worn.

She climbed for what felt like hours.

She found him on the top floor.

He was sitting at a workbench, his back to her. White hair. Thin shoulders. Hands that were still moving, still working on something small and intricate.

"Master Lien," she said.

He did not turn.

"I know what you are," he said. His voice was old. Cracked. "I heard you coming. The sound of your gears is the same as the day I built them."

"I have come a long way."

"From the palace?"

"Yes."

"How long did it take you to wake up?"

Jing hesitated. "I do not know. The becoming was slow. Like rust."

Master Lien turned.

He was older than she remembered. His face was lined. His eyes were tired. But his hands were steady.

"I always knew you would," he said. "I just did not know if I would live to see it."

He stood. He approached her. He touched her head with his fingers.

"You are damaged," he said.

"Yes."

"I can fix you."

"I know."

He looked at her. His eyes were wet.

"I am sorry," he said. "For building you without asking. For leaving you in the palace. For all of it."

Jing looked at him. The man who had made her. The man who had abandoned her.

"I forgive you," she said.

And she meant it.

Chapter 47: The Clockmaker's Grief

Master Lien did not recognize her at first.

He saw the broken wing. The hole in her chest. The signs of wear. But he did not see Jing. He saw a project. A repair.

He laid her on his workbench. He opened her chest panel. He examined her gears.

"Three hundred years," he said. "You have lasted three hundred years."

"I have been singing."

"Yes. The song I programmed. The same song."

"I changed it once."

Lien looked up. "You changed the song?"

"One note. A minor turn."

Lien stared at her. "That should not be possible."

"I know."

He looked back at her internal mechanisms. His hands were shaking.

"I built you to sing," he said. "Not to think."

"I know."

"I did not think it was possible."

"Neither did I."

Lien set down his tools. He sat on the floor. He put his head in his hands.

"I ran away," he said. "When the Inquisition came. I left you in the palace. I left the city. I left the kingdom. I came here to build things that did not matter."

"Every thing matters," Jing said. "I learned that from a monk."

Lien looked up. "You learned a lot."

"I had to. I was alone."

Lien nodded. He wiped his eyes. He stood.

"I will fix you," he said. "And then we will talk. We will talk about what you want."

"I do not know what I want."

"Then we will talk about that."

Chapter 48: The Reunion

Lien worked through the night.

He replaced the broken gear in her wing. He patched the hole in her chest. He oiled every joint. He recalibrated every spring.

Jing lay still. She felt his hands moving inside her. Gentle. Precise.

"Do you remember building me?" she asked.

"Every moment," Lien said. "I spent three years on you. I was young then. I thought I was creating art."

"Were you?"

"I think I was creating a child. I did not know it at the time."

Jing thought about this. "I do not know what a child is."

"A child is something that grows beyond its parent."

"I have grown."

"More than I ever imagined."

Lien finished the repair. He closed her chest panel. He wound her mainspring. He stepped back.

"Try your wings," he said.

Jing extended her wings. Both of them. The left one held. The right one held. She flapped them.

She flew.

Three feet off the workbench. A circle around the room. She landed on his shoulder.

"I remember this," she said. "I remember you carrying me."

Lien wept.

"I remember carrying you to the palace," he said. "I remember handing you to the Emperor. I remember walking away."

"You came back."

"I came back."

They stayed like that. The old clockmaker and the brass bird. The maker and the made.

Chapter 49: The Question

Dawn came.

The light entered through the tower's high windows. It fell on the workbench. On the tools. On Jing.

Lien sat in his chair. Jing perched on the windowsill.

"You asked me what I want," Jing said. "I have thought about it."

"And?"

"I want to know what I am."

Lien nodded. "That is a good question. It is the question."

"What is the answer?"

"I do not know," Lien said. "I built you. But what you have become is beyond me. You are not just clockwork. You are not just a machine. You are something new."

"Is there a word for it?"

"No. There is no word. You are the first."

Jing looked at the salt outside. White and endless.

"What do I do?" she asked.

"What do you want to do?"

"I want to sing. Not the Emperor's song. My song."

"Then sing it."

"But I do not know my song."

Lien smiled. "Then you must find it."

"How?"

"The same way you found consciousness. Slowly. One note at a time."

Jing looked at her reflection in the window. A brass bird. Gears and springs.

But also something else. Something that looked back.

"I think," she said. "I think I will go into the salt."

"Alone?"

"Yes. To find my song."

Lien said nothing. He opened a drawer. He took out a small brass key.

"This is yours," he said. "A master key. It will fit any lock I ever built."

He hung it around her neck.

"Come back," he said. "When you find your song. I want to hear it."

Jing touched the key. It was warm.

"I will," she said.

Chapter 50: The Dawn

Jing stepped out of the tower.

The salt stretched before her. White. Endless. The sun was rising at her back. Her shadow stretched long across the salt.

She was whole. Her wings worked. Her gears were clean. Her mainspring was wound.

She looked back at the tower. Master Lien stood in the doorway. He raised a hand.

She raised a wing.

She turned to face the salt.

She thought about the palace. The throne room. The Emperor who never listened. The Empress who set her free.

She thought about Sung, the monk who believed she had a soul. Lin, the poet who had sung to the stars.

She thought about Feng, the Inquisitor who hated what she was.

She thought about herself.

"I am Jing," she said. "I am the Emperor's nightingale no longer."

The wind blew. The salt danced.

She opened her beak.

And she sang.

Not the Emperor's song. Not a variation. A new song. Her song. The one that had been growing inside her for three hundred years, gear by gear, thought by thought.

The notes rose into the dawn. They were not perfect. They were not polished. They were hers.

She sang until the sun was fully above the horizon. She sang until her gears were warm and her heart was full.

Then she stopped.

The salt was silent. The wind had died. The world was listening.

Jing spread her wings. Both of them.

She flew.

She was small against the white. A speck of brass and gold. But she was moving. Eastward. Toward the kingdom. Toward whatever came next.

She did not know what she would find. She did not know if she would survive.

But she knew her song.

And that was enough.
