The Last Orchard
Barış Kısır

Chapter 1: The Last Spring

Old Seth's knife moved in the old way. He found the node where last year's wood met this year's bud and made a single clean cut. The branch fell. Wren caught it before it hit the ground.

"Don't let them touch the dirt," he said without looking at her. "The dirt carries everything now."

She held the branch away from her body. The cut end was pale and moist. It smelled of nothing.

They worked the north row that morning, the one closest to the wall. Seth pruned. Wren gathered. The sky was the color of old bone. No birds sang. That had stopped three springs ago.

"Here," he said, and placed her hand on a branch fork. "Feel that?"

She felt nothing but bark.

"The swell. The bud wants to push. You can feel it when you know what you're touching."

She pressed harder. Maybe there was something. A warmth. Or maybe she only wanted there to be.

Seth's fingers found her wrist and guided her hand higher. "This one's dead. See how the tip bends? That's rot pretending to be weight."

He cut it. The branch was hollow inside.

"The tree knows," he said. "It sheds what it can't feed. We have to help it."

Wren looked at the rows of trees stretching toward the wall. They looked the same as last year. The same as every year. But Seth's hands were slower now. His knife paused at every cut like it was asking permission.

A wind came over the wall. It tasted of dust and something chemical. Wren held her sleeve over her mouth.

Seth did not lift his arm. He breathed it in and kept working.

"Grandfather," she said. "The Factor came yesterday."

Seth's knife stopped. He looked at her then, his eyes clouded with age and something else. Fear.

"He came to the house?"

"No. Mother met him at the gate."

Seth turned back to the tree. He made a cut. Another. The branch fell.

"How many blossoms?"

"I don't know."

"Yes you do. You count everything."

Wren watched a drop of sap bead on the fresh cut. It was thin and clear, not thick like it used to be.

"Forty-three," she said. "On the trees that flowered."

Seth nodded. He reached for the next branch and his hand shook.

"Forty-three," he repeated. "Last year it was seventy."

The wall cast its shadow over them. Spring had come again, but it was thinner than before.

Chapter 2: The Factor's Visit

The horse came at dawn. Wren heard its hooves on the hard ground before she saw it, a rhythm out of place in the quiet.

She pressed her face to her window. The Factor rode alone. He never brought others on the first visit of the season. That came later, if the news was bad.

Dahlia was already at the gate. She had not slept. Wren knew because she had heard her mother walking the floor all night, counting steps instead of sheep.

The Factor dismounted without hurry. He was a tall man in a long coat. His boots were clean. Everything about him was clean.

"Dahlia." He said her name like he owned it.

"Factor." Her mother's voice was flat.

"I've come for the count."

"It's early."

"Spring is early. The settlement feels it."

Wren pulled on her boots and went to the door. She stood in the shadow of the frame and listened.

Dahlia did not invite him in. They stood on either side of the gate like it was a treaty line.

"The trees are stressed," Dahlia said. "The water table dropped again. We have forty-three blossoms."

The Factor's face did not change. "I need two hundred fruits this season."

"From forty-three blossoms? You know the math."

"I know the settlement's need. Two hundred."

"There won't be two hundred. There won't be half that."

The Factor looked past Dahlia at the orchard. His eyes moved along the rows, counting for himself.

"The old man still works?"

"He works."

"Then plant more. Graft harder. I don't care how you do it."

Dahlia said nothing.

The Factor reached into his coat and pulled out a pouch. He tossed it at her feet. It landed with a soft clink.

"Fertilizer. From the settlement labs. It helped the hydroponics."

"We don't use chemicals."

"You will this season."

He mounted his horse. The animal stamped, impatient.

"Two hundred, Dahlia. Or I send helpers to live among you and make sure the work gets done."

He rode away. The dust settled.

Wren stepped out of the doorway. Her mother was still looking at the pouch on the ground.

"Wren," she said. "Burn that. Don't touch it."

Wren picked it up with a stick and carried it to the fire pit. The pouch hissed when the flames touched it. The smoke was yellow.

Chapter 3: Counting Blossoms

Dahlia walked the rows alone after the Factor left. She carried a piece of charcoal and a flat stone and marked each tree as she passed.

The north row gave nothing. Not a single bud.

The east row had twelve. She counted twice to be sure.

The center grove, the oldest trees, held the rest. Twenty-nine blossoms on trees that used to flower by the hundred.

She stopped at the tree Seth had planted the year Wren was born. It had set three blossoms, fragile and small. She touched one and a petal fell.

Behind her, the house was silent. Seth would be at the workbench, sharpening tools he had sharpened yesterday. Wren would be watching the wall.

Dahlia sat on the ground between two roots. The dirt was dry and gray. She dug her fingers into it and brought a handful to her nose.

Nothing. The soil had no smell anymore.

Her mother had taught her to read soil by scent. Rich earth smelled of decay and life together. Good earth smelled of rain. This earth smelled of nothing but mineral dust and time.

She let it fall through her fingers.

Two hundred fruits. The math was impossible. Even if every blossom set and every fruit grew to full size, they would not reach two hundred.

She thought of the hidden pits. The winter stores she had told the Factor were gone. They were not gone. They were buried under the root cellar floor, three crates of dried apples from better seasons.

She would trade them. It was the only play.

But after the crates were gone, there would be nothing. No bank. No safety.

She stood and brushed the dirt from her hands.

Wren was waiting at the end of the row. She had been watching. She always watched.

"How many?" Wren asked.

"Enough," Dahlia said. It was not a lie. Enough to start a war. Enough to lose everything.

Wren looked at her hands. "You're bleeding."

Dahlia looked down. Her palm was cut where she had gripped a broken branch without noticing.

"Get the salve from the cellar," she said. "The yellow one."

Wren ran. Dahlia watched her go and did not look at her bleeding hand. The pain was useful. It kept her thinking.

Chapter 4: Wren's Wall

The wall was older than anyone remembered. Seth said his grandfather had built it, stone by stone, after the first die-off. It was twelve feet high and six feet thick at the base. On the other side was the world.

Wren had never been past it.

She climbed it today. The old footholds were still there, worn smooth by generations of children who had done the same. Seth said he used to climb it as a boy, when the other side still had grass.

She reached the top and pulled herself onto the flat capstone. The mortar had crumbled long ago. She sat and looked.

The valley spread out below her like a wound.

Gray hills rolled to a gray horizon. The river that had once run through the center was a dry scar, its bed filled with pale sediment. In the distance, the remains of a town stood with its roofs caved in. Nothing moved.

There was a haze over everything. Not fog. Something thinner and more patient that never burned off.

Wren had seen sketches of the world before. Books from the settlement library, printed in color. Fields of green. Water so blue it hurt to look at. But she could not connect those images to what she saw now.

She looked down at the orchard. From above, the trees made a green circle in the gray, like a coin someone had dropped in the dust.

"Wren!"

Her mother's voice. Sharp and afraid.

Wren climbed down. Her hands scraped on the rough stone. She was halfway when she lost her grip and fell the last few feet, landing hard on her back.

Dahlia stood over her, breathing hard. She had run from the house.

"What were you doing?"

"Looking."

"There's nothing to see."

"There has to be something."

Dahlia pulled her to her feet. Her grip was tight enough to hurt.

"There's nothing," she said. "I've seen it. There's nothing out there but poison and silence."

"Then why do we stay?"

Dahlia let go of her arm. She looked at the wall, and for a moment Wren saw something in her face that she had never seen before. It looked like doubt.

"Because the orchard is still alive," Dahlia said. "And while it lives, we stay."

She walked back to the house. Wren stayed at the wall, rubbing her shoulder where she had hit the ground. She looked at the top of the wall and thought about the world beyond it.

Chapter 5: The Root Cellar

Dahlia lifted the trapdoor in the kitchen floor. The root cellar stairs descended into dark. She took a candle from the shelf and lit it.

"Come," she said to Wren. "It's time you knew."

Wren followed her down. The cellar was cold and smelled of old potatoes and earth. Shelves lined the walls, mostly empty. A few jars of preserves. Dried herbs hanging from the ceiling.

Dahlia went to the back wall and pressed her palm against a stone. It moved. Behind it was a cavity she had cut into the dirt years ago.

Three wooden crates. Dahlia pulled one out and opened the lid.

Apples. Dried, dark, perfectly preserved. They filled the crate to the brim.

"Winter stores," Dahlia said. "From seasons when the harvest was good. I hid them."

"How many?"

"Three hundred. Maybe more."

Wren touched one of the dried apples. It was light and hard. "Does the Factor know?"

"He thinks we ate them all. He thinks every season we starve like everyone else."

Wren looked at the other crates. "This is what you trade when you need something."

"Yes."

"This is how we survive."

Dahlia closed the lid. "This is how I bought us time. Every apple buys a day. Every crate buys a month. But when they're gone, they're gone."

She put the stone back in place. The wall looked solid again.

"Why are you showing me this now?" Wren asked.

"Because you climbed the wall today. Because you're old enough to know what we're really protecting."

"The crates."

"No." Dahlia turned to face her. "The knowledge. Of how to hide. How to save. How to choose what to sacrifice and what to keep. That's what I'm showing you."

Wren looked at the wall where the crates were hidden. She thought about the dried apples, each one a day bought from the Factor.

"What happens when they're gone?"

Dahlia blew out the candle. The dark was complete.

"Then we find something else to trade."

Chapter 6: Seth's Cough

Seth was in the south row when the cough took him. He was shaping a graft on a young plum when his chest seized and he doubled over, the knife clattering on the stones.

Wren heard the noise and ran.

He was on his knees when she reached him, one hand on the tree trunk, the other pressed to his mouth. His shoulders shook. The cough was wet and deep.

"Grandfather."

He waved her off without looking up. She knelt beside him anyway.

The coughing stopped. He spat into his hand. The sputum was pink.

"Don't tell your mother."

"I have to."

"No. You don't."

He sat back on his heels and wiped his hand on his pants. His breath came in short gasps. She saw his fingers tremble against his thigh.

"It's the dust," he said. "Gets in the lungs. Sit with me a minute."

They sat together at the base of the tree. The plum's branches made a thin shadow over them.

"Your mother thinks she can hide enough. Barter enough. She's wrong."

"Grandfather—"

"The Factor wants the orchard. Not the fruit. The land. Once he has it, he'll tear out the trees and plant whatever the settlement labs cook up. We're caretakers now. That's all."

Wren looked at his hands. The hands that had taught her to feel the sap move. They were spotted and thin.

"I can learn the grafts."

"You can. You will."

"Then teach me."

He looked at her. His eyes were still clear, even if his body was failing.

"I will. But Wren?"

"Yes?"

"You need to learn more than the grafts. You need to learn when to leave."

She started to speak but he shook his head.

"Not now," he said. "But watch the wall. Watch what comes over it. When the day comes that the orchard can't be saved, you need to be ready to walk away."

He stood slowly, using the tree for support. She stood with him.

"Get my knife," he said. "We're not done with this row."

She picked up the knife. The blade had landed in the dirt. It was covered in gray dust.

Chapter 7: The Factor's Gift

The yellow smoke from the burning fertilizer pouch hung in the air for an hour. Wren watched it drift over the wall and dissipate into the haze.

Dahlia had not touched the pouch since it stopped burning. She stood at the fire pit, staring at the ash.

Seth came up from the orchard, moving slowly, his hand pressed to his ribs. He looked at the ash and knew.

"What did he give us?"

"Fertilizer," Dahlia said. "From their labs."

"It's poison."

"Probably."

Seth knelt and touched the ash. It was fine and pale, lighter than wood ash. It stuck to his fingers.

"This isn't for plants," he said. "This is for us. He wants to know if we'll use it. If we're desperate enough to put this in the soil."

Dahlia's jaw tightened. "Then we don't use it."

"No. We bury it. In the north corner, away from the well. If he sends someone to check, they'll see the disturbed earth and think we used it."

Wren watched them plan. This was how they survived. Not by fighting. By outthinking.

"There's a crate hidden he doesn't know about," Dahlia said quietly. "From the year the east trees bore heavy."

Seth looked up. "You never told me."

"I never told anyone."

He smiled. It was a thin smile, but it was real.

"You learned," he said. "Good."

Wren stepped forward. "What about the wild trees?"

Both of them turned to look at her.

"What wild trees?" Dahlia asked.

Wren hesitated. She had never spoken about what she had seen beyond the wall that day. The sapling growing in a crack in the stone, green and alone.

"Nothing," she said. "I thought I saw something."

Dahlia's eyes narrowed but she let it pass.

Seth stood, brushing the ash from his knees. "Bury the poison. Then we work the east row. Every blossom matters."

He walked back toward the orchard. Dahlia watched him go.

"Wren," she said. "What wild trees?"

"Later," Wren said. "When I'm sure."

She ran after Seth, leaving her mother alone with the ash.

Chapter 8: Grafting Lessons

Seth set up his tools on the flat stone by the workbench. Three knives, each different. Wax. Cloth strips. A bucket of water.

"Grafting is a wound," he said. "Never forget that. You're cutting two living things and forcing them to grow as one. The tree will try to reject the cut. Your job is to make the cut so clean the tree doesn't know it's there."

Wren watched his hands. They were steadier now than they had been in days. The work focused him.

"This is a bud graft." He picked up the thinnest knife. "The easiest. The most forgiving."

He cut a T-shape into the bark of a young rootstock. The bark peeled back, revealing the green cambium beneath.

"Now the bud wood." He selected a branch from a bucket of cuttings. "You want this year's growth. Last year's is too hard."

He sliced a bud from the cutting, a single smooth cut that left a sliver of wood attached to the green. He held it up for her to see.

"Perfect," he said. "Every time. If it's not perfect, the graft dies."

He slipped the bud into the T-cut, sliding it down until it sat flush against the cambium. Then he wrapped the wound with cloth, leaving the bud exposed.

"Your turn."

Wren picked up the knife. It was lighter than she expected. She found a clean section of rootstock and pressed the tip into the bark.

"No. Deeper. You have to cut through to the green or it won't take."

She cut again. The bark peeled.

"Better. Now the bud."

She selected a cutting and positioned the knife. Her hand was not steady. The cut was too deep, taking too much wood.

"Again."

She did it again. This time the slice was clean.

"Good. Now set it."

She slid the bud into the T-cut. It fit. She wrapped it with cloth.

Seth examined her work. He touched the cloth, checking the tension.

"It will live," he said. "You have the hands for this."

He turned to the next rootstock. "Now do it again. And again. Until you don't have to think about it."

She worked through the morning. By noon, her fingers were raw and she had grafted twelve buds. Seth checked each one and said nothing, which she knew was his way of saying they were good.

"You'll be better than me," he said finally. "Before I die."

"That's not hard," she said. "Your hands shake."

He laughed. It turned into a cough, but the laugh was real.

"True enough. But I taught you. That's what matters."

Chapter 9: Dahlia's Bargain

Dahlia rode to the settlement alone. She took one crate of dried apples, balanced on the back of their old mule. She did not tell Seth she was going.

The settlement was three miles east, a cluster of buildings around the old train station. The tracks had been torn up years ago for scrap. The station was now the Factor's headquarters.

People watched her ride in. They always did. The orchard family came rarely, and when they came, they brought news or trade.

The Factor's office was in the station's waiting room. The benches were still there, bolted to the floor. He had kept them.

He was at his desk when she entered. He did not look up.

"Dahlia. I didn't expect you so soon."

"I have a trade."

He looked up then. "You have nothing I want. You owe me a harvest."

"I have a crate of dried apples from last season. Perfect condition. Fifty fruits."

"And where were these when I asked for the count?"

"Hidden. For emergencies."

He leaned back in his chair. "And this is an emergency?"

"This is a negotiation."

He stood and walked to the window. The settlement square was empty. A dog scavenged near a dead fountain.

"One crate for two weeks," he said.

"One crate for a month."

"Three weeks."

"Done."

She did not let him see her relief. She had been prepared to take two.

"The crate is outside. I'll have it brought in."

"Send one of my men back with you," he said. "To confirm the count."

"No."

"Then no deal."

She held his gaze. "Your men don't set foot in my orchard. I counted the apples myself. Thirty-seven. You want to check, you come with me."

He considered. Then he nodded.

"I'll credit you, pending my visit. But Dahlia?"

She waited.

"Three weeks. Then I expect progress. Real progress."

She left without answering. Outside, she took a breath. The settlement air was worse than the orchard's. It tasted of metal and crowds.

She led the mule back toward the wall, counting the days she had bought. Twenty-one. Not enough. But it was something.

Chapter 10: Night Watchers

Wren could not sleep. The house was quiet, the kind of quiet that felt like waiting. She lay in her bed and listened to the wind.

Something moved outside.

She sat up. The window was dark. She pressed her face to the glass and saw nothing but her own reflection.

Then she saw them. Shapes at the fence line. Three of them, standing still, facing the orchard.

She slipped out of bed and went to her mother's room.

Dahlia was already awake. She was standing at her window, watching the same shapes.

"I see them," Dahlia said before Wren could speak.

"Who are they?"

"The Factor's men. Counting."

"Counting what?"

"Trees. Entrances. Us."

They watched together. The shapes did not move. They were patient, standing guard over the night.

"They're here to scare us," Wren said.

"No. They're here to learn. The Factor doesn't know the orchard. He needs eyes."

"Should we wake Grandfather?"

"No. He'll want to go out there with his knife."

They watched for another hour. The shapes stayed until the moon set, then they withdrew, melting into the dark.

When they were gone, Dahlia sat on the edge of her bed.

"They'll be back," she said. "Tomorrow. The next night. Every night until the harvest."

"Then what?"

"Then they'll come in the day."

Wren went back to her room but did not sleep. She sat at her window and watched the empty fence line. The orchard was dark and still.

She thought about the wild sapling beyond the wall. It was still there. She had checked yesterday. It was small and alone and untouched.

If the orchard fell, the sapling was all that would be left.

She made a decision. Tomorrow she would go to the wall. She would climb it and she would not come back until she knew everything growing on the other side.

The family needed options. The Factor had given them none.

Chapter 11: The Water Table

The well had been dug by Seth's father in the first year after the collapse. It went forty feet down, through clay and shale to the ancient aquifer that had fed the valley for millennia.

That aquifer was dying.

Dahlia lowered the bucket and felt the rope go slack sooner than it should have. She hauled it back up. The bucket was half full. The water was brown.

She brought it to Seth. He looked at it and said nothing. He took a cup and drank.

"Metallic," he said. "More than last week."

"It dropped another foot."

"The whole table is falling. Not just here. I heard the settlement has to drill deeper every year."

Dahlia poured the water onto the ground. It sank into the dust and left a dark stain.

"We can't irrigate with this. The trees will take up the minerals."

"Then we don't irrigate," Seth said. "We let the roots reach deeper."

"The roots are already dying."

Seth sat on the well's stone edge. His hands rested on his knees. They looked old.

"There's a deeper vein," he said. "My father told me about it. Fifty feet down, through a shale layer. Clean water, he said. Sweet."

"You never dug for it."

"Never needed to. Now we do."

Dahlia looked at the well. The stones were old. The rope was frayed.

"How long to dig?"

"Weeks. Months. With three of us? Maybe never."

"Then we get help."

"From who?"

She did not answer. They both knew.

The Factor had men. The Factor had equipment. But the Factor would never help without taking something in return.

Wren came around the corner of the house, carrying a bucket of water from the creek. The creek water was cleaner than the well water now. That was new.

"The creek's still running," she said. "But it's low."

"For how long?" Dahlia asked.

Wren shrugged. "It rained last night. A little. It might last a week."

"A week of water. Then what?"

Nobody answered. The question hung in the air like the yellow smoke from the burning fertilizer.

Chapter 12: Seth's Confession

The evening was cool. Seth sat on the porch, wrapped in a blanket despite the warmth. Wren sat beside him, shelling peas from the garden.

"There's something I haven't told you," he said.

Wren stopped shelling.

"The orchard is dying from within."

"I know. The blossoms—"

"Not just the blossoms. The roots. They're touching poison."

She waited.

"The water table is dropping. But what's rising is worse. The old contamination, from before the wall. It's been in the deep soil for decades, settling. But now the water is pulling it up."

"Can we dig through it?"

"No. The roots go where the water goes. And the water is poisoned."

Wren looked at the orchard. The trees were dark shapes in the fading light.

"Then why do we keep tending them?"

"Because they're still alive. Because they might adapt. Because we don't have anywhere else to go."

He coughed. The cough was dry this time. He wiped his mouth.

"I've been testing the soil for years. Every season, the same test. The contamination is spreading inward from the edges. The center trees are still safe, but the poison moves a foot closer every year."

"How long?"

"Five years. Maybe ten. Then the whole orchard is dead."

Wren put down the peas. She looked at her hands. They were covered in the green juice from the shells.

"Mother doesn't know, does she?"

"She knows. She tested the soil herself, three years ago. We never spoke of it."

"Then what are we doing?"

"Buying time. For you."

Seth turned to look at her. His eyes were wet.

"When I was young, there were orchards everywhere. Every valley had trees. When one died, you planted another. Now there's only us. And when we go, there's nothing."

He reached for her hand. His grip was weak.

"Wren. You asked me about the wild trees. What did you see?"

She hesitated. Then she told him about the sapling. The green leaves. The crack in the stone. The way it grew without anyone tending it.

Seth listened. When she finished, he was quiet for a long time.

"That sapling," he said finally. "It's more important than every tree in this orchard."

"Why?"

"Because it means life finds a way. In the poison. In the waste. Something is still growing."

He squeezed her hand.

"Protect that sapling, Wren. It's the future."

Chapter 13: The First Fruit

The first plum of the season ripened on the oldest tree in the center grove. It was early by three weeks. Wren found it when she was checking the east row.

It hung from a low branch, fully purple, heavy with sugar. She reached for it and stopped.

Something was wrong.

She leaned closer. The fruit was perfect on one side. On the other, a soft brown patch spread from the stem toward the cleft. Rot.

She did not pick it. She went to get her mother.

Dahlia came and looked at the fruit. She touched the soft patch with her finger. The skin broke. The flesh underneath was brown and wet.

"It's the water," Dahlia said. "The minerals are getting into the fruit."

"Can we cut it off?"

"The rot is inside. The whole fruit is contaminated."

Dahlia picked the plum and threw it into the weeds. It landed with a soft thud.

"Check every tree," she said. "Every fruit. If they're all like this, we have nothing to harvest."

Wren checked. She spent the rest of the morning walking the rows, examining every blossom and every tiny fruit. Most were still green, too small to show rot. But the ones that were ripening all showed the same soft brown patch at the stem.

She reported to her mother in the kitchen.

"Every early fruit has the rot."

Dahlia was sitting at the table, her hands flat on the wood. She did not move.

"The later fruits might be fine," Wren said. "They haven't had as much time in the soil."

"Or they'll rot before they ripen."

"We don't know that."

Dahlia looked up. Her face was tired. "We know enough."

Seth came in from the orchard. He had been checking the north row. He sat down across from Dahlia without speaking.

"The early fruit is rotting," he said.

"I know."

"It's the water."

"I know."

They sat in silence. Wren stood between them, watching their faces.

"We need a plan," Wren said. "We can't just sit here."

Dahlia looked at her. "What do you suggest?"

"Let me go past the wall. Let me look for clean ground."

"No."

"Mother—"

"I said no."

Wren looked at Seth. He said nothing.

The first fruit of the season was rotting in the weeds. And there was nothing they could do.

Chapter 14: The Sick Tree

Seth found it on a Tuesday morning. A core-tree in the center of the grove, one of the oldest, one that had born fruit for forty years. Black streaks ran up the bark from the root line to the first branches.

He called no one. He took his knife and cut a small wound in the bark. The sap that came out was dark and thick, like blood that had sat too long.

He knew what it was. The contamination had reached the heartwood.

He sat at the base of the tree and put his hand on the trunk. The wood was warm. Still alive. But the black streaks were spreading.

Dahlia found him there an hour later.

"What is it?"

He showed her the streaks. She touched them.

"How far up?"

"To the first branches. Maybe higher."

"Can we cut it?"

"No. It's in the core."

Dahlia sat beside him. They looked at the tree together.

"The whole grove will follow," she said. "One by one."

"Yes."

"How long?"

"A season. Two. The roots connect underground. They share the same water."

Dahlia stood. She walked around the tree, examining the streaks.

"I'm cutting it down."

"No."

"Seth. It's dead."

"It's not dead. It still has leaves."

"The leaves are poisoned. The fruit will be poisoned. We can't harvest from a sick tree."

Seth stood slowly. His joints cracked.

"If we cut it, we admit it's over."

"If we don't cut it, the rot spreads."

They faced each other over the tree's roots. Wren appeared at the edge of the grove. She had heard their voices.

"Mother. Grandfather. What's happening?"

Neither of them answered.

Wren walked to the tree and saw the streaks. She touched them. Her fingers came away stained dark.

"We have to quarantine it," she said. "Cut it and burn it."

Seth closed his eyes.

"Get the saw," Dahlia said.

Wren hesitated. Then she went.

Seth stayed with the tree, his hand on the bark, feeling the last of its life moving through the wood.

Chapter 15: Wren's Discovery

Wren did not go for the saw. She went to the wall.

The wild sapling was still there. She had lied to herself that it was nothing. One green shoot in a world of gray. But she had to see it again, had to confirm to herself that something still grew without permission.

She climbed the wall. The footholds were familiar now. She reached the top and dropped to the other side.

The ground beyond the wall was different. Harder. The soil was pale and cracked. Nothing grew here. Nothing lived.

But the sapling was where she had left it, tucked in a crevice where the wall met the bedrock. It had grown since she last saw it. New leaves at the tip. A second stem rising from the base.

She knelt beside it. The leaves were the same green as the orchard trees, but darker. Wilder. They had a shine she had not seen in the orchard's leaves for years.

She touched the bark. It was smooth. Young. Unmarked.

She looked around. The valley stretched away, dead and silent. But this one tree was alive. Somehow, in the poison, it lived.

Wren sat beside it. She thought about what Seth had said. Life finds a way.

She had to know if there were others. If this was the only one, or if the land was trying to heal.

She stood and walked farther from the wall than she had ever gone. The house behind her shrank. The orchard became a green dot.

She found nothing. Just gray earth and dead stone and the silence of a world that had stopped breathing.

But when she returned to the sapling, she noticed something she had missed. A trail of green, barely visible, leading away from the wall toward the riverbed. Tiny shoots. Baby plants, each no bigger than her finger, pushing through the cracked earth.

She followed them. They led to a small depression where the ground was darker. Damp. A spring, hidden under a shelf of rock, seeping clean water into a pool no bigger than her hand.

She knelt and touched the water. It was cold. Clear.

She tasted it. Just a drop on her finger.

It tasted of nothing. Clean nothing.

She looked up at the sky. The haze was thinner here. The sun was a white disk overhead.

She filled her canteen from the spring and went back to the orchard, carrying the knowledge like a secret.

Chapter 16: The Factor's Ultimatum

The Factor came with riders this time. Five men on horses, their faces hidden behind scarves. They stopped at the gate and waited.

Dahlia was in the north row. She heard the horses and knew. She walked to the gate slowly, wiping her hands on her apron.

The Factor did not dismount. He looked down at her from his horse.

"I've been patient, Dahlia."

"You've been three weeks patient. As agreed."

"The agreement was for progress. I sent men to check your trees. They tell me you have rot."

"One tree. We cut it."

"One tree today. Ten next week. A hundred by harvest."

She said nothing.

"The settlement needs food. My people are hungry. The hydroponics failed last month. Your orchard is all that's left."

"The orchard can't feed everyone."

"Then feed enough. What it can produce, I need."

The Factor leaned forward in his saddle.

"You have two options. Double the harvest by the end of the season, or I send workers to live here. They'll learn your methods. They'll make sure the trees produce."

"And if I refuse both?"

"I take the orchard by eminent domain. The settlement council has already signed the order."

He pulled a paper from his coat and held it up. It had seals on it. Official marks.

"You have until the full moon."

He turned his horse and rode away. The riders followed.

Dahlia stood at the gate, staring at the dust they left behind.

Wren came up beside her. "What is eminent domain?"

"It means they can take anything they want. If the law says it's for the common good."

"Is it?"

"Is it what?"

"For the common good?"

Dahlia looked at the paper in her hand. "It doesn't matter. The law belongs to whoever holds the most power."

She crumpled it and dropped it in the dirt.

"Wren. The wild sapling. Does it have fruit?"

"Not yet. But it's healthy."

"Can you get more? Seeds? Cuttings?"

Wren thought about the spring. The tiny shoots. The hidden pool.

"I can try."

"Do it. Quietly. Without telling your grandfather."

"Why?"

"Because if the orchard is taken, we need somewhere else to go."

Chapter 17: Cutting the Sick Tree

The saw was old but sharp. Dahlia had sharpened it the night before, running a file along each tooth until the edge caught her finger.

She carried it to the center grove. The sick tree stood apart from the others now. Seth had tied a red cloth around its trunk to mark it.

Seth was not there. He had not left the house that morning.

Wren came with her mother. She had never seen a tree of this size cut down.

"The roots first," Dahlia said. "You cut the roots so the tree doesn't tear when it falls."

She knelt and began digging around the base, exposing the thick roots. They were pale and wet, covered in the same dark streaks that marked the trunk.

She cut them one by one. The saw moved through the wood easily. Too easily. The tree was already dying.

When the roots were severed, she stood and made the first cut on the trunk. A wedge cut on the side facing the clearing. The saw bit into the wood. Sawdust came out dark.

Wren watched in silence. Each cut took the tree closer to the ground.

"Does it hurt?" Wren asked.

"The tree?"

"Yes."

Dahlia paused. "I don't know. I think it feels like ending."

She finished the cut and stepped back. The tree did not fall. It stood, held by its remaining wood.

"Now we push."

They pushed together, mother and daughter, their hands flat against the trunk. The tree groaned. The cut opened. The tree leaned, then fell, crashing into the clearing with a sound that echoed off the wall.

Birds that had not been there a moment ago rose from the other trees. Wren had not known there were still birds.

The tree lay on its side. The leaves were already wilting.

Dahlia wiped her face with her sleeve. "We burn it tonight. Everything. Branches, leaves, bark. Nothing goes into the soil."

"What about the stump?"

"I'll dig it out tomorrow. The roots can't stay in the ground."

Wren looked at the fallen tree. It had been alive that morning. Now it was wood.

"It feels wrong," she said.

"It is wrong. But letting it stand would be worse."

They stood together, looking at what they had done. The orchard felt smaller without the tree.

Chapter 18: The Wild Sapling

Wren went back to the wall that night, after her mother was asleep. She carried a small trowel and a piece of cloth.

The moon was thin. She climbed in darkness, feeling for the footholds. At the top, she paused, listening. Nothing moved.

She dropped to the other side and found the sapling by touch. It was taller than she remembered. Another day of growth.

She knelt beside it and began to dig. The soil was hard, cracked. She worked the trowel in small movements, loosening the earth around the roots without cutting them.

It took an hour. The roots were deeper than she expected. They had found the clean water below the cracked surface and they held tight.

She lifted the sapling carefully, keeping the soil ball intact. She wrapped the roots in the cloth and carried it back to the wall.

Climbing with one hand was difficult. She nearly fell twice. But she made it to the top and lowered the sapling to the other side before climbing down after it.

She carried it to the south corner of the orchard, the place Seth had shown her years ago. A pocket of good soil, protected from the wind, where the wall cast shade through the hottest part of the day.

She dug a new hole. She planted the sapling. She watered it with water from the hidden spring.

When she finished, she sat back and looked at it. In the dark, it was just a shadow among shadows. But she knew it was there.

"Grow," she whispered. "Please grow."

She went back to the house and washed her hands at the pump. The dirt under her nails was dark and clean.

In her bed, she lay awake, thinking about the spring and the green shoots and the clean water running under the dead world. There was life out there. Hidden. Waiting.

She would find more of it. She would bring it back. She would build a new orchard, one the Factor did not know about.

That was her plan. And she would not tell anyone until it was ready.

Chapter 19: Dust Season

The ash came on the wind three days later.

It arrived at dawn, a fine gray powder that fell like snow but did not melt. It came from the north, where the burn-falls had been running for weeks. Wren woke to find a thin layer of it on her windowsill.

By noon, it was thicker. The sky turned yellow. The air tasted of burning.

Dahlia gave orders. "Cover the well. Cover the seedlings. Wipe every leaf."

They worked through the day, going tree to tree, wiping the ash from each leaf with damp cloths. The ash stuck to the leaves like paste. If it dried there, it would clog the pores. The trees would suffocate.

Seth could not keep up. His hands were too slow, his lungs too weak. He worked the south row while Wren and Dahlia worked the center and east.

By evening, they had cleared half the orchard. The rest would have to wait until morning.

"We'll lose some," Dahlia said. "The leaves will burn."

"How many?" Wren asked.

"I don't know."

That night, Wren went to the hidden sapling. She wiped its leaves one by one. They were small and the ash had settled thick on them. She cleaned each leaf with her fingers, then with water from her canteen.

The sapling's leaves were still green when she finished. She sat beside it, her back against the wall.

"Stay alive," she said. "You're all we have."

In the morning, the ash was still falling. Thicker now. The sky was dark at noon.

They worked again. Wiping. Cleaning. Trying to save what they could.

Seth collapsed in the north row. Dahlia found him on his knees, his cloth still in his hand, trying to wipe a leaf he could not reach.

She helped him to the house and put him to bed.

"Stay inside," she said. "The ash will kill you."

"The trees—"

"Will die if we don't work. But we need you alive."

Seth lay back on his pillow. His breath was shallow.

"Wren," he said. "Come here."

She came to his bedside.

"The sapling. Did you move it?"

"Yes."

"Is it safe?"

"I think so."

"Good." He closed his eyes. "Good."

The ash fell for three more days. By the time it stopped, a quarter of the leaves had browned and curled. But the trees still stood.

And the sapling in the south corner was untouched.

Chapter 20: The Factor's Spies

The boy appeared at the fence on the fifth day after the ash stopped.

Wren saw him first. She was checking the sapling when she looked up and there he was, standing on the other side of the wire, watching her.

He was maybe sixteen. Thin. Dark hair falling into his eyes. His clothes were settlement-made, rough canvas that had been patched more than once.

"Who are you?" Wren asked.

"Nobody," he said. "I got lost."

"Nobody gets lost out here. There's nothing to get lost in."

The boy shrugged. He looked past her at the orchard.

"They're still alive. The trees."

"Most of them."

"How many?"

Wren did not answer. She studied him. He was not dressed like a scout. No boots. No weapon. His hands were bare and clean.

"You're from the settlement," she said.

"Yes."

"What do you want?"

He looked at her directly. His eyes were pale, almost colorless.

"I want to see something that's still alive."

He said it simply, without drama. Wren believed him.

She made a decision. She opened the gate.

"Come in. But if you touch anything, I'll break your fingers."

He smiled. It was a small, tired smile.

"Fair."

She led him through the orchard. He walked slowly, looking at every tree. He touched nothing. He just looked.

"Your family did this," he said. "Grew all of it."

"The land grew it. We just helped."

"How?"

"Grafts. Irrigation. Luck."

He stopped at the center tree, the one where they had cut the sick tree. The stump was still there, covered in ash.

"You lost one."

"Yes."

"More will follow."

"And?"

"And eventually you'll run out of luck."

Wren looked at him. "Is that what you came to tell me?"

He shook his head. "I came to see if it was true. The stories about the orchard. People say it's the last green place in a hundred miles."

"It is."

"I wanted to see it before it dies."

Wren felt anger rise in her chest. "It's not dying."

"It is. Everything dies. The settlement is dying too. The hydroponics failed. The water is going bad. The Factor tells us he's saving us, but he's just managing the end."

He looked at her.

"I'm not here to spy. I'm here because I wanted to see something beautiful before it was gone."

She did not know what to say to that.

They stood in the orchard, two children in a graying world, and watched the leaves move in the wind.

"You should go," she said finally. "Before my mother finds you."

He nodded. He turned and walked back to the gate.

"What's your name?" she called after him.

He paused. "Reeve."

"Reeve what?"

"Just Reeve."

He stepped through the gate and walked away.

Wren watched him until he disappeared into the haze. Then she went to check the sapling.

Chapter 21: Testing the Soil

Seth was well enough to sit up the next day. He asked for his soil testing kit, a worn wooden box he had kept for thirty years.

Wren brought it to him. He opened it on the bed. Inside were glass vials, a small scale, a bottle of reagent liquid, and a notebook filled with his small, precise handwriting.

"Get me a sample from the center grove," he said. "From where we cut the tree."

She brought it. He took a pinch of the soil and put it in a vial. He added the reagent. The liquid turned dark.

He held it up to the light.

"More contaminated than last month. The poison is moving faster."

He poured the soil out and took a clean vial.

"Now from the south corner. Where you planted the sapling."

She brought it. He tested it. The liquid stayed clear.

Seth looked at the two vials, side by side.

"The south corner is clean. The center is poisoned. The difference is fifty feet."

"What does that mean?"

"It means the contamination isn't everywhere. It's in pockets. Flowing along the old water paths."

He set the vials aside.

"There might be clean ground beyond the wall. If we can find it."

Wren thought about the spring. The green shoots. The clean water.

"There's a spring," she said. "Beyond the wall. The water is clean."

Seth looked at her sharply. "You found water?"

"A small spring. Under a rock shelf. The water tastes clean."

"Show me."

"You can't walk that far."

"Then draw me a map."

She took a piece of paper and drew the route she had taken. The wall. The crack in the stone. The dry riverbed. The shelf of rock.

Seth studied the map.

"This is on the old water table," he said. "The deep one. If the spring is clean, there's a clean aquifer below it."

"Is that good?"

He smiled. It was a tired smile, but it reached his eyes.

"It's everything."

He folded the map and put it under his pillow.

"Don't tell your mother yet. Let me think about this."

"Why?"

"Because a clean spring means we can start over. But we have to do it right. We can't run until we're ready."

Wren nodded. She understood.

She had started something. A hidden grove. A secret water source. A future her mother did not believe in yet.

She would keep building it. One sapling at a time.

Chapter 22: Wren's Oath

Wren found her mother in the kitchen, grinding dried herbs for medicine. She sat across from her and waited until Dahlia looked up.

"I need to tell you something."

Dahlia set down the mortar. "What?"

"I found clean water. Beyond the wall. A spring."

Dahlia's hands stopped moving. "How far?"

"Half a mile. Under a rock shelf."

"And you're sure it's clean?"

"I tasted it. Seth tested the soil from that area. It's clean too."

Dahlia was quiet for a long time.

"There's more," Wren said. "I found a sapling growing out there. Wild. Healthy. I moved it to the south corner."

Dahlia stared at her. "You moved a tree from beyond the wall?"

"It's growing. It's not sick."

"Wren."

"Mother. Listen. I can find more. There are shoots, tiny ones, near the spring. They're growing in the clean soil. I can dig them up and plant them here. We can build a new grove. Hidden. One the Factor doesn't know about."

Dahlia stood and walked to the window. The orchard was gray in the dusk.

"You're talking about starting over."

"Yes."

"It took Seth fifty years to build this orchard. Fifty years of work. You want to do it in a season."

"I want to start. That's all."

Dahlia turned. Her face was hard, but her eyes were wet.

"If the Factor finds out—"

"He won't."

"He will. Someone always finds out."

Wren stood. "Then we leave before he does. We take the saplings and go to the spring. We build a new orchard there."

"And Seth?"

"He can come. He said himself—he told me to learn when to leave."

Dahlia looked at her daughter. For a long moment, she said nothing.

Then she nodded.

"If you find more saplings, bring them. Plant them in the south corner. I'll help you dig."

Wren felt something loosen in her chest. She had been afraid her mother would refuse.

"I'll go tomorrow," Wren said. "At dawn."

"I'll come with you."

"No. You have to stay here. If the Factor sends someone, you need to be here. I'll go alone."

Dahlia shook her head, but she did not argue.

"Dawn," she said. "And you don't go past the spring."

"Mother—"

"That's the rule. Until we know more."

Wren nodded. "Dawn. Just to the spring."

She went to her room and lay down, but she did not sleep. She was already planning her route.

Chapter 23: The Boy's Betrayal

Wren found Reeve at the spring.

He was sitting on the rock shelf, his feet in the water, when she arrived at dawn. He looked up at her without surprise.

"Hello again."

She stopped. "You followed me."

"I followed the trail. You're not careful."

"You're not lost. You lied to me."

"I know." He did not look sorry.

She stood on the edge of the pool, her hands in fists. "You said you wanted to see something beautiful."

"I did. That part was true."

"And the rest?"

He stood. Water dripped from his feet onto the dry ground.

"My father sent me. He wants to know everything about the orchard. Where the water comes from. How many trees. Where the clean soil is."

"Your father."

"The Factor."

The word hit her like cold water. She stepped back.

"Your father is the Factor."

"Yes."

"He sent you to find this place."

"I found it myself. He just wanted a count."

She looked at the spring. The clean water. The green shoots. He had seen everything.

"Does he know?"

"Not yet."

"Why haven't you told him?"

Reeve looked at her. "Because I wanted to see if you were real first."

"Real?"

"The girl who tends the last orchard. The one who climbs the wall. I've heard stories about you my whole life. I wanted to know if they were true."

Wren felt her heart beating in her throat. "And now you know."

"Now I know." He stepped closer. "I'm not going to tell him."

"Why should I believe you?"

"Because I have nothing to gain. If he finds this place, he'll take it. He'll strip the land and build his own operation. And I'll still be his son, living in his shadow."

He looked at the spring.

"You found something clean. I've never seen water that clear. Not in the settlement."

"You live in the settlement. You grow hydroponics."

"The hydroponics failed. I told you the truth about that."

Wren did not know what to believe. She looked at his face. It was young and tired and hungry in a way she recognized.

"If you tell him—"

"He'll find out anyway. Someone will. It's not a matter of if. It's when."

He picked up his boots.

"But I'll give you time. As much as I can."

He walked past her, toward the settlement.

"Reeve," she called.

He stopped.

"Why?"

He did not turn around. "Because you showed me the orchard. Without asking what I'd pay."

He kept walking. She watched him go.

When he was gone, she knelt at the spring and touched the water. It was still clean. Still cold. Still hers.

For now.

Chapter 24: Drought Protocols

The creek stopped running on the seventh day of dry weather. The bed turned to cracked mud, then to dust.

Dahlia stood at the edge of what had been the water source for the south rows. The mud was hard and pale.

"We drill," she said. "Deeper than the old well."

Seth was too weak to help. He sat on the porch and watched them work.

Dahlia and Wren took turns at the hand auger. It was slow work, breaking through the hardpan, pulling up dry soil. By noon, they had gone ten feet. Nothing.

"We need to go deeper," Dahlia said. "The water tables have fallen everywhere."

They kept drilling. Fifteen feet. Twenty. The soil changed from gray to brown. Damp.

"Keep going," Dahlia said.

At twenty-five feet, the auger brought up wet sand. Wren put her hand in the hole. The sand was cold.

"It's here," she said.

"Pump it slow," Dahlia said. "Let it clear."

They set up the hand pump and began drawing water. It came up brown at first, thick with sediment. Then it turned clear.

Dahlia tasted it. "Metallic. But usable."

"We can irrigate?"

"Carefully. We mix it with stored water from the barrels. Stretch it as far as it goes."

Wren looked at the pump. It was a small thing, metal and leather, held together with wire and hope.

"How long will it last?"

"The well? A season. Maybe two. Then we drill again."

"And after that?"

Dahlia did not answer.

They pumped water that evening, barrel by barrel, carrying it to the trees that needed it most. The old trees in the center. The young trees in the south. The hidden sapling.

At midnight, they stopped. Wren's arms ached. Her hands were blistered.

She lay on the ground next to the pump and looked at the stars. They were hidden by the haze, but she knew they were there.

"Wren."

Her mother's voice. Soft.

"Yes?"

"When the time comes to leave—"

"Yes?"

"You'll know. You'll feel it. Don't hesitate."

Wren turned her head. Her mother was sitting against the well, her face shadowed.

"Where will we go?"

"The spring. If it's still clean. Or farther, if we have to."

"And Grandfather?"

"We carry him if we have to."

Wren looked up at the haze. The stars were not there. But she knew they were behind it.

She closed her eyes and dreamed of green.

Chapter 25: The Crossing

Dahlia made the decision at breakfast. Seth had not eaten in two days. The cough was back, worse than before.

"We're going beyond the wall," she said. "Today. All of us."

Wren set down her spoon. "What about the orchard?"

"The orchard will survive one day without us."

"Not all of us," Seth said from his chair. "I can't make the climb."

"I'll carry you."

"You're not strong enough."

Wren spoke. "There's a gap in the wall. South corner. The stones have fallen."

Dahlia looked at her. "You found a gap?"

"I found it looking for saplings. It's narrow, but we can get through."

Seth looked at the ceiling. "I'll stay."

"No," Dahlia said.

"Yes. I slow you down. Go. See what's out there. Come back and tell me."

Dahlia started to argue, but Seth raised his hand.

"I've seen everything I need to see in this world. Go."

They went.

Dahlia led. Wren followed. The gap was at the south corner, where the wall met the hillside. Stones had fallen inward, creating a ramp of rubble.

They climbed over the broken stones and stepped onto the other side.

The ground was gray and cracked. The silence was complete.

They walked toward the spring, following Wren's route. Dahlia stopped once to look back at the wall. The orchard was hidden behind it.

"Is it real?" she asked.

"What?"

"The orchard. From out here, it looks like nothing."

"It's real. I promise."

They reached the spring. Dahlia knelt and touched the water. She tasted it.

"Clean," she said. "How is this possible?"

"I don't know. But it's been here all along."

Dahlia looked at the green shoots around the spring. Tiny plants, pushing through the dead soil.

"This is where we start again. If we have to."

She stood and looked at the horizon. The gray hills. The dead valley.

"There's nothing else out here."

"Nothing," Wren agreed. "Just this."

"This is enough."

They stayed for an hour, exploring the area around the spring. Dahlia found more shoots, a patch of ground where the soil was darker, richer.

"Good drainage," she said. "This could hold trees."

She marked the spot with a stone.

"We come back tomorrow. With tools. We start preparing the ground."

Wren nodded.

They climbed back through the gap in the wall. Seth was waiting on the porch. He saw their faces and did not ask.

They had found the future. Now they had to build it.

Chapter 26: The Dead Forest

Beyond the wall, the world was gray and silent. Dahlia and Wren walked for an hour past the spring, following the dry riverbed north.

The trees they found were dead. All of them. An entire forest, bleached white and standing like bone. The trunks were smooth, stripped of bark. The branches reached up like hands.

Dahlia touched one. It crumbled at her touch.

"Chemicals," she said. "Whatever fell here, it stripped everything."

Wren looked at the dead trees. There were hundreds of them. Thousands. A whole valley of silence.

"Nothing grows," she said.

"Nothing."

They walked through the dead forest. The ground was covered in a layer of pale dust. It rose around their boots with every step.

At the center of the forest, they found something. A clearing where the trees had fallen in a circle, pushed outward from a central point.

Wren saw it first. A pool of water, dark and still. On its surface, a film of oil caught the light.

She knelt beside it. The water smelled of metal.

"This is the source," she said.

"Yes."

"Is it spreading?"

"No. I think it's receding. The dead forest is the edge. The spring is where the contamination hasn't reached."

Dahlia looked at the pool. "Yet."

"Maybe never."

"Maybe."

They stood on the edge of the dead forest and looked back at the wall. The orchard was invisible, hidden behind stone.

"If the contamination reaches the orchard," Wren said, "we lose everything."

"Yes."

"Then we need to leave before it does."

Dahlia did not answer. She was looking at the pool, at the oil on the surface.

"We have time," she said finally. "Not much. But some."

"How do you know?"

"Because the spring is still clean. The saplings are still growing. The land is healing, slowly. We just have to be faster than the poison."

She turned and started walking back.

"Come. We have work to do."

Wren followed. Behind her, the dead forest stood silent.

Chapter 27: The Factor's Claim

The Factor arrived with six men. They came at noon, when the sun was high and the shadows were short. He carried the paper with the seals.

Dahlia met him at the gate. This time, she did not open it.

"Factor."

"I've come for the orchard."

"The harvest is not ready."

"The harvest is irrelevant. The council has ruled. I'm here to take possession."

He held up the paper. The seals were fresh.

Dahlia did not move. "On what grounds?"

"Eminent domain. The settlement needs the land for food production. Your family has failed to produce adequate harvests."

"We've maintained this orchard for fifty years."

"Fifty years of declining output. The settlement can no longer afford your stewardship."

Dahlia looked at the men behind him. They were armed. Not with guns—guns were rare—but with clubs and knives.

"You're taking my home."

"I'm taking the land. You can stay as workers. I'll pay you in food."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then you leave. Today."

Dahlia looked at the orchard. The trees were green. The fruit was setting. It was not much, but it was hers.

"Give me three days."

"For what?"

"To pack. To say goodbye."

The Factor considered. "One day. Tomorrow at noon, I take possession."

He turned and rode away. The men followed.

Dahlia stood at the gate. She did not move for a long time.

Wren came out of the house. "I heard."

"Get your grandfather. We need to move fast."

"Where?"

"To the spring. We're taking what we can."

Wren ran to the house.

Dahlia looked at the orchard one last time. The trees she had grown up with. The trees she had grafted. The trees that had fed them.

Tomorrow, they would belong to someone else.

She went inside and began packing.

Chapter 28: Seth's Stand

Seth refused to leave.

He sat in his chair on the porch, his knife in his hand, watching the sun set over the orchard.

"Grandfather," Wren said. "We have to go."

"I'm not going."

"The Factor is coming tomorrow. He'll take everything."

"He can take the land. He can't take the trees."

"Mother says—"

"I know what your mother says. But I've lived here for seventy years. I planted the first tree when I was your age. I've grafted every branch on this land."

He looked at her. His eyes were clear.

"I'm not leaving the trees."

Wren sat beside him. "If you stay, they'll hurt you."

"Maybe."

"Grandfather—"

"Wren." He put his hand on her knee. "I'm dying. We both know it. My lungs are full of dust. I can't walk to the spring. I can't dig new holes."

He coughed. The cough was wet.

"If I'm going to die, I want to die here. Under the trees I planted."

Wren felt tears in her throat. She fought them.

"Then I'll stay with you."

"No." His voice was sharp. "You're the future. You and your mother. You take the saplings and you go. You build a new orchard. You survive."

She shook her head.

"Wren. Look at me."

She looked at him.

"I taught you the grafts. I showed you how to feel the sap. The knowledge is in your hands now. It's not in me anymore."

He lifted his knife and held it out to her.

"Take it."

She took the knife. It was warm from his hand.

"Every time you make a cut, I'll be there," he said. "Now go."

She stood. The knife was heavy in her hand.

"Goodbye, Grandfather."

"Goodbye, Wren."

She walked into the house. Behind her, Seth turned back to the orchard and watched the last light fade.

Chapter 29: The Bunker

Wren was packing the root cellar when she found the trapdoor she had never noticed. It was under a loose flagstone, hidden by a sack of dried herbs.

She pulled it open. Stone steps led down into darkness.

She lit a candle and descended.

The bunker was small, maybe ten feet square. The walls were concrete. Shelves lined the walls, filled with boxes.

She opened one. Papers. Old files, yellowed with age.

She opened another. More papers. Charts. Graphs. Photographs.

She held up a photograph. An aerial view of the valley, green and alive. No wall. No dead forest. Just fields and orchards stretching to the horizon.

She put it aside and kept looking.

At the back of the bunker, she found a metal cabinet. Locked. She tried the handle. It opened.

Inside were notebooks. A dozen of them, bound in leather, filled with the same small handwriting as Seth's soil tests.

She opened the oldest one. The first page was dated forty years ago.

"We have begun the engineering. The trees must survive what is coming."

She read. The words were technical, full of terms she did not understand. But the meaning was clear.

The orchard had been designed. Engineered. Seth had not planted it—he had inherited it from the scientists who came before.

The trees were made to survive the poison.

She took the notebooks and carried them upstairs. Her mother was in the kitchen, packing food.

"Mother. I found something."

Dahlia turned. She saw the notebooks.

"Where did you get those?"

"The bunker. Under the root cellar."

Dahlia's face went pale. "You found the bunker."

"You knew about it?"

"I knew. I never went inside."

Wren held up the notebooks. "The orchard was engineered. Designed to survive contamination. Grandpa didn't build it. He inherited it."

Dahlia took one of the notebooks and opened it. She looked at the diagrams without understanding them.

"Who built it?"

"I don't know. Scientists. Before the collapse."

Dahlia closed the notebook.

"This changes nothing. The trees are still dying."

"It changes everything. If the trees were engineered, there might be records. Instructions. A way to fix them."

Dahlia looked at her daughter. For the first time in weeks, hope flickered in her eyes.

"Keep reading," she said. "Find out everything."

Chapter 30: First Blood

The Factor came at noon. Six men. The paper. And a chain for the gate.

Dahlia was waiting. She had not slept. She had packed what she could and hidden the rest.

Behind her, the house was empty. Seth was on the porch, his chair facing the orchard. He had not moved since dawn.

"Where is the old man?" the Factor asked.

"On the porch."

"He needs to leave."

"He won't."

The Factor sighed. He motioned to his men. Two of them walked toward the porch.

Seth saw them coming. He did not move.

"Sir," one of the men said. "Time to go."

Seth said nothing.

The man reached for his arm.

Seth swung.

The knife was in his hand, the one he had kept sharp for fifty years. It caught the man across the forearm. Blood sprayed.

The man yelled and fell back. The other man drew his club.

Dahlia ran.

She grabbed the club before it swung, her hands closing around the man's wrist. He was stronger. He pushed her back. She stumbled.

The man raised the club again.

Something hit him from the side. Wren. She had thrown herself at him, her shoulder connecting with his ribs. He went down.

The Factor watched. His face was calm.

"Enough."

The men stopped. The wounded one was on his knees, holding his arm.

"Take the old man," the Factor said. "Gently. Put him in the house."

They lifted Seth from his chair. He did not resist. His knife had fallen. He was staring at the blood on the porch.

Dahlia stood. Her hands were shaking.

"Get off my land," she said.

The Factor looked at her. "This land is mine now. But I'll give you one hour to gather your things. One hour. Then my men clear the house."

He turned and walked toward the orchard.

Dahlia stood in the yard, her hands still shaking, her daughter beside her.

"We have an hour," Dahlia said.

"What do we take?"

"The notebooks. The saplings. Seth."

"Grandfather can't walk."

"We carry him."

Wren looked at the knife on the porch. She picked it up and wiped the blood on her pants.

Then she went to pack.

Chapter 31: Bunker Secrets

Wren read through the night. She sat in the root cellar with a candle and the notebooks, deciphering the old handwriting.

The scientists had been from a government program. They had anticipated the collapse. They had engineered trees to survive in contaminated soil, building resistance into the rootstock.

The notes were detailed. Grafting techniques. Soil amendments. Water filtration methods.

But the last notebook was different. The handwriting was Seth's.

He had continued the records after the scientists left. He had documented every season. Every graft. Every failure.

The final entry was dated three months ago.

"The center trees are showing signs of systemic failure. The contamination has reached the engineered resistance limit. The trees are dying from the roots up.

I have tried everything. The old techniques no longer work.

But there is hope. Beyond the wall, I have found wild growth. Unengineered. Natural. It is thriving where the engineered trees are failing.

The solution was never in the science. It was in the land itself."

Wren closed the notebook.

Seth had known. He had been watching the wild growth, the same way she had. He had been planning.

She went upstairs. The house was dark. Her mother was asleep, her hand on Seth's chest. He was asleep too, his breath shallow.

Wren went to the south corner of the orchard. The hidden sapling was still there. It had grown. New leaves. New branches.

She knelt beside it.

"You're the future," she whispered. "Not the science. Not the old trees. You."

The sapling's leaves moved in the wind.

She sat with it until dawn.

Chapter 32: The Compromise

The Factor sent the settlement medic at sunrise.

She was an old woman with steady hands and a quiet voice. She came alone, carrying a leather bag.

"I'm here to see the old man," she said. "The Factor sent me."

Dahlia stood in the doorway. "Why?"

"Because he wants him alive. He values the old knowledge."

Dahlia let her in.

The medic examined Seth. She listened to his chest. She looked at his hands. She took his pulse.

"He's not long," she said. "Weeks. A month at most."

"I know."

"There's something I can give him. For the pain. For the cough. It won't save him, but it will ease him."

"What do you want in return?"

The medic smiled. It was a thin smile, but not unkind.

"I want to see the orchard. Before it's gone."

Dahlia nodded. She led the medic through the trees. The woman walked slowly, touching the bark, looking at the leaves.

"You've kept them alive," she said. "Longer than anyone thought possible."

"We had the old knowledge."

"The old knowledge is dying with everyone who had it. The Factor wants to keep your father alive because he's the last person who knows how to make things grow."

Dahlia stopped. "The Factor wants to use him."

"He wants to learn from him. Then he'll let him go."

"That's not a compromise. That's theft."

The medic shrugged. "Everything is theft now. You take what you can and you hold it as long as you can."

She looked at Dahlia.

"Let me treat him. I'll give you medicine. Enough to keep him comfortable. In return, I'll tell the Factor the old man can't work. That buys you time."

Dahlia considered.

"One condition. You don't tell the Factor about the south corner."

"The south corner?"

"The young trees. They're not his. Not yet."

The medic nodded. "I didn't see anything."

They walked back to the house. The medic opened her bag and measured out a dark liquid into a small bottle.

"Three drops a day. In water. It will ease the cough."

Dahlia took the bottle.

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. I'm just buying us all a little more time."

She left. Dahlia watched her go.

That afternoon, she gave Seth the first dose. His coughing eased. He slept.

Wren watched from the doorway.

"Does this change anything?" she asked.

"It changes today," Dahlia said. "Tomorrow, we keep running."

Chapter 33: Wren's Archive

Wren began copying the notebooks at night, when the house was quiet. She used scraps of paper and a pencil she had sharpened to a nub.

She copied the grafting techniques first. Then the soil tests. Then the water filtration methods.

She copied Seth's final entries. The ones about the wild growth. The natural resistance.

Each night, she added to her pile. The pages grew. By the end of the first week, she had a book of her own, bound with twine.

She hid it in the root cellar, behind the loose stone where she had found the bunker entrance. If the Factor took the house, he would not find it.

Dahlia found her copying one night. She stood in the doorway and watched.

"What are you doing?"

"Making a record. In case the notebooks are lost."

Dahlia picked up a page. She looked at Wren's handwriting. It was small and careful, like Seth's.

"Good," she said. "Keep going."

She left. Wren kept writing.

She copied the old photographs too, tracing them onto paper. The green valley. The clean river. The world that had been.

She added her own notes. The location of the spring. The path through the dead forest. The clean soil near the south corner.

She was building a record. Not just of the orchard, but of everything they had learned. Everything they would need.

When she finished, she had forty pages. She bound them with twine and hid them behind the stone.

The next day, the Factor's men came to start the new irrigation system. They dug trenches through the orchard, cutting roots.

Wren watched from the kitchen window. Each cut was a wound.

"I saved the knowledge," she told herself. "That's what matters."

But she felt the cuts anyway.

Chapter 34: The Poison Spring

The irrigation water changed color on the third day of the Factor's work. It ran brown, then yellow, then a pale green that did not look natural.

Dahlia saw it first. She was at the well when the water came through the new pipes. She caught a cup and held it up to the light.

"Stop the pump," she said.

Wren stopped it. They looked at the cup together. The water had a chemical smell.

"What is it?"

Dahlia dipped her finger in and touched it to her tongue. She spat immediately.

"Poison. Something upstream."

"The Factor."

"Has to be. He's contaminating the water so we can't use it."

"Why would he do that? He just took the orchard."

"Because he doesn't want us to stay. He wants the land, not the fruit. If the water is poisoned, we leave. He doesn't have to force us."

Wren looked at the water in the cup. It was the color of sickness.

"The spring beyond the wall. Is it still clean?"

"We need to check."

They went through the gap in the wall. The spring was still clear. The water was cold and clean.

"For now," Dahlia said. "But if he's poisoning the upstream, it's only a matter of time."

"Then we need to move faster. Dig the new ground. Transplant the saplings."

Dahlia nodded. She looked at the wall, then at the spring.

"We start tonight. After dark."

They went back through the gap. The Factor's men were still digging. They did not look up.

Wren went to the south corner and checked the hidden sapling. It was still healthy. Still green.

But the irrigation trench was three rows closer than it had been that morning.

Chapter 35: Hand-Watering

The irrigation was dead. The pipes carried poison. The well was too shallow to reach the clean layer.

Dahlia and Wren carried buckets.

They started at dawn, filling buckets from the well and carrying them to the trees that needed water most. The young trees. The ones that could not reach deep roots.

Wren's hands blistered by the second hour. By the fourth hour, the blisters had broken. By the sixth, she could not feel her fingers.

"Keep going," Dahlia said. "The trees need more."

They worked through the afternoon. The sun was hot through the haze. The air was thick with dust.

Seth watched from the porch. He could not help. The medicine had eased his cough, but he had no strength.

"Water the south corner first," he said. "The saplings can't survive without it."

Wren carried a bucket to the south corner. The hidden sapling was drooping. She poured water around its roots. By evening, it had perked up.

She sat beside it, her hands wrapped in cloth.

"Hold on," she said. "We're going to move you soon. Somewhere safe."

She slept beside the sapling that night, her back against the wall.

In her dream, she saw the dead forest turning green. Trees rising from the gray soil. Water running clean through the riverbed.

She woke to the sound of digging. The Factor's men were working by lantern light, extending the trench toward the south corner.

She watched them from the shadows. They were getting closer.

At dawn, she went to her mother.

"We need to move the saplings tonight."

Dahlia looked at the trench. It was two rows away.

"Tonight," she agreed. "We dig them up and carry them to the spring."

Chapter 36: The Researchers

They arrived at dusk. Two figures walking up the road from the settlement, carrying packs and walking sticks.

Wren saw them first. She called Dahlia.

They watched the figures approach. They were thin, dressed in travel-worn clothes. One was a man, the other a woman. Both had the look of people who had walked a long way.

"Who are you?" Dahlia called.

The woman stopped. "Travelers. We heard there was an orchard here."

"Who sent you?"

"No one. We've been walking for weeks. We're botanists. Or we were. Before."

Dahlia studied them. "What do you want?"

"To see your trees. To take samples. We're trying to find plants that survived."

Dahlia looked at Wren. Wren nodded.

"Come in," Dahlia said. "But keep your hands visible."

The researchers entered. They walked through the orchard slowly, touching nothing. The woman knelt at the base of a plum tree and examined the soil.

"This is amazing," she said. "How have you kept them alive?"

"Carefully," Dahlia said.

"The contamination in this area is severe. The soil should be dead."

"The trees are resistant. Engineered, before the collapse."

The woman looked up sharply. "Engineered?"

"By a government program. The records are in the house."

The woman stood. "We need to see those records."

Dahlia hesitated. Then she nodded.

"Come inside."

That night, the researchers read the notebooks by candlelight. They took notes. They asked questions.

"There's valuable data here," the man said. "If we could replicate this—"

"Replicate it where?" Dahlia asked. "There's nowhere clean to plant."

"There's always somewhere. We just have to find it."

Wren spoke. "We found a spring. Beyond the wall. The water is clean."

The researchers looked at each other.

"Show us," the woman said.

Chapter 37: The Factor's Trap

The Factor arrived at dawn. He had a dozen men. They surrounded the house before anyone woke.

Dahlia found them at the gate. The Factor was in the center, his horse stamping.

"I've heard you're harboring fugitives," he said.

"They're researchers. Botanists."

"They're wanted by the settlement. They stole from the lab before they left."

"That's not true."

"It doesn't matter what's true. It matters what I say."

He motioned. His men moved into the orchard.

The researchers were in the house. They came out with their hands up. They did not resist.

"Take them," the Factor said.

"You can't do this," Wren said.

The Factor looked at her. "I can do anything, girl. This is my land now."

He turned his horse.

"The old man stays. The rest of you have three days to leave."

He rode away. His men followed, herding the researchers ahead of them.

Wren stood in the yard. The researchers were gone. The notebooks were still in the house. The saplings were still in the south corner.

But the Factor had seen them. She knew it. He had seen everything.

"We have to leave tonight," she said.

Dahlia nodded. "Tonight. We take the saplings and the notebooks. We go to the spring."

"And Grandfather?"

"We carry him."

The family had lost the orchard. But the future was still in their hands.

Chapter 38: Seth's Final Graft

Seth was bedridden now. He could not eat. He could barely lift his head.

But when Wren came to his bedside that evening, his eyes were clear.

"Wren. They took the researchers?"

"Yes."

"They'll be fine. The Factor needs them alive."

She sat beside him. His hand found hers.

"I'm not going to make it to the spring," he said.

"You will. We'll carry you."

"No. I'm staying."

"Grandfather—"

"Listen." His grip tightened. "I have one more thing to teach you. The last graft."

She leaned closer.

"It's not a tree graft. It's a knowledge graft. Everything I know, I'm giving to you now."

He closed his eyes.

"The trees will die. All of them. The old ones, the engineered ones. They were made for a world that no longer exists."

"The saplings—"

"The saplings are different. They grew naturally. They adapted. That's the future, Wren. Not the engineered trees. The wild ones."

He coughed. The sound was thin.

"When you plant the new grove, don't graft them. Don't train them. Let them grow wild. They know how to survive better than we do."

Wren nodded. She was crying.

"Do you understand?"

"Yes."

He relaxed. His hand went slack.

"Good," he whispered. "Now go. I need to rest."

She stood. She kissed his forehead.

"Thank you, Grandfather."

"Thank you, Wren. For the trees."

She left the room. Seth stared at the ceiling, listening to the wind in the orchard.

He died that night, his hand on the knife he had used for fifty years.

Chapter 39: The Blossom Watch

Wren checked the wild sapling every hour.

It had set fruit. Tiny things, hard and green, no bigger than her thumbnail. She counted them. Nine in total.

She did not tell anyone. Not even her mother. The sapling was hers. The risk was hers.

The Factor's men were still digging trenches. They had reached the young trees in the south corner. They had not found the wild sapling. It was too small, too hidden.

Wren watched it grow. Each day, the fruit got a little bigger. Each day, she checked for rot. For discoloration. For the brown patch at the stem.

There was nothing. The fruit was clean.

She touched one. It was hard. Not ready.

"Grow faster," she whispered.

But the fruit took its time. It did not care about the Factor or the poison or the deadline.

She sat with the sapling in the evenings, after the work was done. She talked to it. She told it about the spring and the clean soil and the new grove she would build.

"You can't stay here," she said. "I'm going to move you. Somewhere safe."

The sapling's leaves moved in the wind.

"It might hurt. Moving. But you'll live. You'll grow."

She put her hand on its trunk. It was thin. Vulnerable.

"I'll keep you safe," she said.

The fruit grew. One of them started to blush pink.

Chapter 40: Dahlia's Plan

Dahlia called Wren to the kitchen. She spread a paper on the table. A map, drawn from memory.

"The spring is here," she said, pointing. "The clean soil is here. We dig tonight."

"Tonight?"

"Our last night. The Factor's deadline is tomorrow."

Wren looked at the map. It showed the spring, the riverbed, the dead forest. And a new area, drawn in charcoal, south of the spring.

"What's this?"

"Where we plant the new grove. It's sheltered. The soil is deep. I tested it this morning."

"You went alone?"

"While you were checking the sapling."

Dahlia looked at her daughter.

"We take the young trees from the south corner. We take the wild sapling. We take the notebooks. We go through the gap at midnight."

"What about the house?"

"We leave it."

"The orchard?"

Dahlia was silent for a moment. "We leave it too."

Wren looked at the map. The new grove was a small circle on the paper. A promise.

"How long until it bears fruit?"

"Two years. Maybe three."

"Can we survive that long?"

"We have the dried stores. We have the spring. We'll find a way."

Wren looked at her mother. She had never seen her look so determined.

"We do it tonight," Wren said.

"Tonight."

They began packing. They took only what they could carry. Food. Water. The notebooks. Seth's knife.

The sun set. The moon rose.

At midnight, they started moving the trees.

Chapter 41: The Settlement Trial

A rider came at dusk. A boy, no older than Reeve. He carried a message sealed with the settlement's mark.

"Dahlia of the Orchard. You are summoned to the settlement council. Tomorrow at dawn. Failure to appear is forfeiture of all claims."

Dahlia read the message twice.

"Claims? I have no claims. The orchard is gone."

"The council wants to hear your testimony. About the bunker. The records. The old trees."

"Who told them?"

The boy shrugged. "Everyone knows."

Dahlia crumpled the paper.

"Tell them I'll come."

The boy rode away.

Wren came out of the house. "You're going to the settlement?"

"They summoned me. If I don't go, they'll send men to drag me."

"I'll come with you."

"No. Stay here. Guard the saplings."

Wren shook her head. "I'll come. If we're leaving, I want to see the settlement one more time."

Dahlia looked at her. Then she nodded.

"Pack for the journey. We leave at dawn."

They went inside. The moon was high. The saplings were hidden.

At dawn, they locked the house and walked to the settlement.

The trial would decide everything.

Chapter 42: The Council

The settlement council met in the old train station. The benches were full. People had come to watch.

Dahlia and Wren stood at the front. The Factor sat at a table on the raised platform, with four elders beside him.

The head elder spoke. "Dahlia of the Orchard. You have been called to testify about the records found in the bunker beneath your home."

"What records?"

"Do not play ignorant. The researchers who passed through spoke of notebooks. Scientific data. Government research."

Dahlia said nothing.

"These records belong to the settlement. They were left by the old government. They are common heritage."

"They're my father's records. He kept them for thirty years."

"Your father is dead. The records pass to the settlement."

Wren stepped forward. "The records are mine."

Everyone turned to look at her.

"I found them. I've been copying them. They're my work now."

The head elder raised an eyebrow. "And what do you intend to do with them?"

Wren looked at the Factor. He was watching her with cold interest.

"I intend to use them," she said. "To plant a new orchard. Beyond the wall. Where the soil is still clean."

Murmurs ran through the crowd.

"There is no clean soil," the Factor said.

"There is. I found it."

"And you expect us to believe you?"

"I don't expect anything. I'm telling you what I found."

The council looked at each other.

"We will deliberate," the head elder said. "You will have our answer by morning."

Dahlia and Wren left the station. Outside, the settlement was quiet.

"You shouldn't have told them about the clean soil," Dahlia said.

"Why not? If they know there's hope, maybe they'll help us."

"Or maybe they'll take it from us too."

Wren looked at the station. The council was still deliberating.

"We'll see," she said. "We'll see."

Chapter 43: Seth's Passing

Seth died at midnight. Dahlia found him in his chair on the porch, facing the orchard. His eyes were closed. His hand rested on a fallen apple he had picked from the ground.

She did not wake Wren. She sat beside him and held his hand until dawn.

When the sun rose, she went inside.

"Wren. Wake up."

Wren sat up. She saw her mother's face and knew.

"Grandfather?"

"He's gone."

Wren went to the porch. Seth was still in his chair. She touched his face. It was cold.

"The apple," she said. "He was holding an apple."

"Yes."

Wren took the apple from his hand. It was small and hard and perfect. No rot. No blemish.

"From the center tree," she said. "The one that's dying."

"It's clean."

"Yes."

They stood together, looking at Seth. The orchard was quiet.

"We bury him under the center tree," Dahlia said. "Where he planted the first graft."

They dug the grave together. It took them all morning. The ground was hard and full of roots.

They lowered him into the earth and covered him with soil. Wren placed the apple on his chest before the dirt covered it.

"Goodbye, Grandfather."

They stood in the orchard, the trees around them, the wall behind them.

Dahlia spoke first. "We go to the spring tonight. Seth would want us to go."

Wren nodded.

She looked at the tree above Seth's grave. It was old and tired and dying. But the apple he had held was clean.

Maybe there was still hope.

Chapter 44: The Wild Fruit

The fruit on the wild sapling ripened overnight.

Wren found it at dawn, blushing pink and gold in the morning light. Seven fruits. Perfect. No rot. No soft spots.

She knelt beside it and touched one. It was firm. Ready.

She picked it.

The fruit was small, no bigger than a plum. She held it up to the light. The skin was thin and perfect.

She bit into it.

The taste was sharp. Tart. Not sweet like the orchard fruit. But clean. So clean it made her eyes water.

She ate the whole fruit. The flesh was firm and juicy. The pit was small.

She picked the other six and carried them to her mother.

Dahlia was packing the last of the supplies. She looked at the fruit.

"From the sapling?"

"Yes."

Dahlia took one and bit into it. She chewed slowly. Her face changed.

"Clean," she said.

"I know."

"There's no contamination."

"The soil is clean. The water is clean. The fruit is clean."

Dahlia looked at the fruit in her hand. Seven fruits. Seven seeds.

"This is the future," she said. "Not the old orchard. This."

They saved the seeds. All of them. Wren wrapped them in a cloth and put them in her pocket.

The wild sapling had done what the engineered trees could not. It had survived. It had thrived. It had borne clean fruit.

Wren sat beside it and put her hand on its trunk.

"Thank you," she whispered.

The leaves moved in the wind.

She had a pocket full of seeds and a spring full of clean water. She had her mother and the notebooks and Seth's knife.

She had everything she needed to start again.

Chapter 45: The Verdict

The council ruled at dawn. Wren and Dahlia stood in the station as the head elder read the verdict.

"The council finds that the records discovered in the bunker at the orchard are the property of the settlement. They must be surrendered within three days."

Dahlia's face was stone.

"Furthermore, the council finds that the orchard, having been claimed under eminent domain, is the property of the Factor. His management is confirmed."

"However."

The elder paused.

"The council recognizes the right of the family to continue living on the land, as workers, under the Factor's authority. They may stay and tend the trees they planted."

Dahlia stepped forward. "And if we refuse?"

"Then you must leave the settlement's territory. You will be given three days to depart."

Wren looked at her mother. Dahlia's jaw was tight.

"We'll leave," Dahlia said.

"Dahlia—" the Factor started.

"We'll leave. Today."

She took Wren's arm and led her out of the station.

The settlement was waking up. People were going about their morning routines. They did not notice the two women walking to the edge of town.

"Mother. The records—"

"I copied them. Every page. You copied them too. The originals don't matter."

"And the orchard?"

"It's just trees now. We have the seeds. We have the sapling."

They walked past the last houses. The wall was in sight.

"Are we really leaving?" Wren asked.

"Yes."

"The settlement won't let us back in."

"I know."

Wren looked back at the station. The Factor was standing in the doorway, watching them.

"We'll build our own settlement," Dahlia said. "At the spring. With the seeds."

They reached the wall. The gap was still there.

Wren looked at the orchard one last time. The trees were green. The wall was gray. The sky was pale.

Then she turned and followed her mother through the gap.

They did not look back.

Chapter 46: Wren's Speech

The settlement gathered at the wall that evening. Word had spread. The orchard family was leaving. People came to watch.

Wren stood on the rubble of the gap, looking at the crowd.

Reeve was there. He stood at the front, his face unreadable.

"Go back to your homes," Dahlia said. "There's nothing to see."

Wren touched her arm. "Wait."

She climbed higher on the rubble.

"People of the settlement. I know you've heard stories about the orchard. About the bunker. About the records."

The crowd quieted.

"There are things in those records you need to know. The trees were engineered. Before the collapse. Scientists made them to survive the poison."

She pulled the copied pages from her pack.

"There are methods in here. Grafts. Soil treatments. Water filtration. Ways to make things grow again."

She held the pages up.

"The Factor wants these for himself. He wants to control the knowledge. But the knowledge belongs to everyone."

The Factor stepped forward. "That's enough."

"It's not enough." Wren looked at him. "You've taken the orchard. You've taken the records. But you haven't taken the seeds."

She reached into her pocket and pulled out the cloth. She held it up.

"These are seeds from a wild tree. Growing beyond the wall. Clean fruit. Clean soil."

The crowd murmured.

"The Factor says there's nothing out there. He's wrong. There's a spring. There's clean ground. There's life."

She looked at the crowd.

"I'm going out there. I'm going to plant these seeds. And I'm going to build a new orchard. One he doesn't control."

The Factor's face was dark. "You're a child. You don't understand."

"I understand enough."

She climbed down from the rubble. People were whispering. Some were looking at the wall with new eyes.

Wren walked through the gap. The settlement watched her go.

Behind her, Reeve started to follow. His father grabbed his arm.

"No."

Reeve stopped. But he watched until Wren disappeared into the gray.

Chapter 47: The Division

The settlement split that night.

Half the people believed Wren. They had seen her face. They had heard her words. They wanted to follow.

The other half stayed with the Factor. He had kept them alive. He had the power. He was safe.

Reeve was in the middle. He had seen the spring. He had tasted the water. He knew Wren was telling the truth.

But his father was the Factor.

He went to the wall at midnight. The gap was dark. He stood at the edge and listened.

Nothing. Just the wind.

He stepped through.

The land beyond the wall was dark and silent. He walked toward the spring, following the route Wren had taken.

He found her at the spring, sitting on the rock shelf.

"You came," she said.

"I came."

"Your father will be angry."

"He's always angry."

They sat together in the dark. The water was quiet.

"Half the settlement wants to leave," Reeve said. "They want to follow you."

"I have nothing to offer them. No food. No shelter. Just seeds and clean water."

"That's more than they have in the settlement."

Wren looked at him. "You want to stay."

"I want to help. If you'll let me."

She considered.

"Go back. Tell them to wait three days. Tell them to pack food and tools. Tell them to come at night."

Reeve nodded. He stood.

"Wren."

"Yes?"

"Your grandfather. I'm sorry."

She looked away.

"So am I."

He walked back through the dark. Behind him, the spring kept flowing.

Chapter 48: Night Exodus

The exodus began on the third night.

They came through the gap in the wall. Families. Old people. Children. They carried bundles and pushed carts. They moved in silence, afraid of being stopped.

Dahlia counted them as they arrived. Forty-seven people. More than she had expected.

"The Factor won't let this stand," she said.

"He can't stop them all."

"He can try."

Reeve was at the head of the group. He had organized them. Packed them. Led them through the dark.

"My father knows," he said. "He sent men to watch the wall. I bribed them."

"With what?"

"Promise of a future. Same as you."

Dahlia looked at the people. They were scared. They were hopeful. They were her responsibility now.

"The spring is this way," she said. "Follow me."

She led them through the dead forest, past the poisoned pool, to the rock shelf.

The water was still clean. The ground was still good.

"Here," she said. "We build here."

They set up camp. Tents. Fires. Shelters made from salvaged wood.

Wren stood apart, watching.

"Is this what you wanted?" Dahlia asked.

"I wanted a place to grow things. This is more."

"Are you ready for it?"

Wren looked at the people. The families. The children.

"I have to be."

She walked to the edge of the camp and looked back at the wall. The orchard was on the other side. The old trees. The old life.

She turned away.

The new grove would start tomorrow.

Chapter 49: The Factor's Last Move

The fire lit the sky at midnight.

Wren woke to the smell of smoke. She ran to the edge of the camp and looked back at the wall.

The orchard was burning.

Orange flames rose above the stone. The trees she had grown up with, the trees Seth had planted, were on fire.

"No."

Dahlia came up beside her. "The Factor."

"He's burning it."

"He'd rather see it dead than let us have it."

They watched the fire. The flames climbed higher. The smoke was thick and black.

Wren felt something break inside her. The old trees. The grafts. The history. All of it, going up in smoke.

"Seth's tree," she said.

"It's gone."

They stood together, watching the orchard burn. The fire spread. The wall glowed orange.

"We should go," Dahlia said. "The smoke will be toxic."

Wren did not move.

"Wren."

"I know. I'm coming."

She looked at the fire one last time. The flames had reached the center tree. The one where Seth was buried.

"Goodbye," she whispered.

She turned and walked back to the camp.

Behind her, the orchard burned through the night.

In the morning, nothing was left but ash and stone.

Chapter 50: The New Grove

Dawn broke gray and cold. The fire had burned itself out. The wall was blackened. The orchard was gone.

Wren stood at the edge of the camp with the seeds in her pocket. Seven seeds from the wild sapling. Seven chances.

Dahlia came to stand beside her.

"The soil is ready," she said. "I marked the spots."

They walked to the new grove. A patch of ground near the spring, where the soil was dark and clean. Dahlia had dug seven small holes.

Wren knelt at the first hole. She took one seed from the cloth and held it in her palm.

"Grow," she said.

She placed it in the earth and covered it with soil.

She moved to the second hole. The third. The fourth.

The people of the camp gathered to watch. They stood in a circle, silent.

Wren planted all seven seeds. When she finished, she sat back on her heels and looked at the ground.

Seven seeds. Seven trees. A new orchard.

Dahlia knelt beside her and put her hand on the soil.

"They'll grow," she said.

"How do you know?"

"Because you planted them."

Wren looked at the camp. The families. The children. The hope in their faces.

She looked at the wall. The blackened stones. The ashes of the old orchard.

Then she looked at the ground where the seeds were buried. They were small. Hidden. But alive.

She stood and brushed the dirt from her hands.

Seth's knife was in her belt. The notebooks were in her pack. The spring was flowing.

The old world was gone. The new one was just beginning.

Wren turned to the people gathered around her.

"Bring water," she said. "The seeds need to drink."

They moved. They worked. They built.

And in the ground, the seeds began to stir.
