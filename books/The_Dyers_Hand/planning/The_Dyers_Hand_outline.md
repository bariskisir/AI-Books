# The Dyer's Hand — Outline

## Premise

Fez, 1954. Idris ben Omar is sixty-eight years old and his hands have been blue for fifty-three of those years — since the day he first plunged them into an indigo vat as a fifteen-year-old apprentice. He is the last master dyer in the Fez medina still using the old formulas: indigo from the Sahel, madder root from the Atlas, saffron from Taliouine, and a particular shade of Moroccan blue that no one else has ever reproduced.

Morocco is on the cusp of independence. The French protectorate is crumbling. And a Paris fashion house has sent a representative to Fez with an offer: buy Idris's formulas outright, or watch them die with him.

Over the weeks that follow, Idris must confront the ghosts of his craft — the Jewish dyers who once shared the quarter, the son who refused to inherit the workshop, the wife buried in the cemetery above the city — and decide what a lifetime's knowledge is worth when no one is left to receive it.

## Core Cast

- **Idris ben Omar**, 68 — Master indigo dyer, widower, last of his line
- **Samira**, 35 — Idris's daughter, a schoolteacher in the Ville Nouvelle. Torn between the old city and the new
- **Henri Delacroix**, 42 — Representative of Maison Lafayette, Paris fashion house. Polite, persistent, genuinely appreciative of the craft he's asking to purchase
- **Yacob Cohen**, 71 — Jewish dyer who fled Fez in 1948. Returns for the first time in six years to visit his old friend
- **Driss**, 22 — Idris's young apprentice, a Berber from the mountains who showed up at the workshop door three years ago
- **Amina**, 55 — Idris's sister, who runs a bakery in the medina. The family anchor
- **Madame Dupont**, 58 — French colonial administrator's wife, Idris's best customer

## 50-Chapter Plan

### Part I: The Vat (1-10) — The blue hands, the workshop, the offer

1. The hands, blue as a winter sky at dusk. Idris washes them and the water runs indigo.
2. The workshop in the dyers' quarter — ancient stone vats, bundles of wool, the smell of fermenting dye.
3. Driss arrives at dawn, stokes the fire under the indigo vat, listens to the old man's silence.
4. A Frenchman in the medina — a tailored suit among the djellabas. He asks for Idris ben Omar.
5. Henri Delacroix presents himself, his card, his offer. Idris listens, offers tea, says nothing.
6. Samira visits the workshop after school. Her father tells her about the Frenchman. She is worried.
7. The indigo vat — how it works, the chemistry of fermentation, the color that appears only when the cloth rises into air.
8. Amina at her bakery — bread and memory. She was there when Idris's wife died.
9. Idris walks the medina at night, past the closed shops of the Jewish dyers, the empty synagogues.
10. Delacroix returns with a contract. Idris says he needs time. Delacroix says time is the one thing he cannot offer.

### Part II: The Color (11-20) — Memory, the Jewish dyers, the son who left

11. 1928. Idris is thirty-two, his hands are already blue, and his son Karim is born.
12. The dyers' quarter as it was — Muslim and Jewish workshops side by side, sharing formulas, sharing water.
13. Yacob Cohen taught Idris the secret of the indigo fermentation — a process that took twelve days.
14. Karim at fifteen — restless, embarrassed by his father's blue hands, drawn to the French schools.
15. 1948. Yacob leaves. "They say it's not safe," he says. "They say the same thing they always say." He is the last of the Jewish dyers.
16. Karim at eighteen announces he is going to Rabat to study law. Idris says nothing. His silence lasts five years.
17. The formula book — handwritten in Arabic and Judeo-Arabic, passed down five generations.
18. Idris's wife Fatima falls ill in the winter of 1951. The doctors in the Ville Nouvelle can't help. She dies in the spring.
19. After Fatima's death, Idris stops speaking for six months. He works. The vats are his only language.
20. Driss arrives in 1951 — a boy from the mountains with nothing but a note from a village elder who once bought blue wool from Idris. "Teach him," the note says.

### Part III: The Fading (21-30) — The offer deepens, Samira's choice

21. Delacroix brings samples — Paris dresses in Moroccan blue. "This is what your color can become," he says.
22. Samira reads the contract. It's complicated — rights, royalties, a lifetime buyout clause. She tells her father to get a lawyer.
23. Idris visits the Mellah — the old Jewish quarter. Most of the houses are empty now. A few Moroccan families have moved in.
24. Yacob Cohen writes from Marseille. He is coming to Fez. "I want to see the vats one more time," he says.
25. Driss tries to reproduce the master blue. His results are close but not the same. Idris watches but does not correct him.
26. Amina tells Idris he is being stubborn. "The Frenchman is offering money. The boy wants to learn. Why won't you let either of them in?"
27. Samira meets Delacroix at a café in the Ville Nouvelle. She wants to understand what he's really offering.
28. Idris dreams of Fatima. In the dream she is wearing a blue dress — the color of the sky over the Atlas.
29. Delacroix raises the offer. More money, better terms, an exhibition in Paris. Idris is unmoved.
30. Driss asks Idris why he won't teach him the secret. "Because you don't need the secret," Idris says. "You need the patience."

### Part IV: The Return (31-40) — Yacob's visit, the legacy

31. Yacob Cohen arrives at the Fez train station. He is thinner, older, but his eyes are the same.
32. The two old men embrace in the workshop. Driss watches, bewildered by the Hebrew-Arabic conversation flowing around him.
33. Yacob examines the vats. "You're still using my fermentation method," he says. "Thirty years and you never changed it." "It was perfect," Idris says.
34. Yacob tells Driss stories of the old dyers' quarter — the songs they sang, the rivalries, the shared meals on Shabbat and Friday.
35. Delacroix meets Yacob and realizes the old Jew knows formulas too. He sees a business opportunity. Yacob refuses politely.
36. Yacob and Idris walk through the Mellah at dusk. Yacob touches the mezuzah on his old doorway. The new Moroccan family has kept it.
37. Samira brings her daughter, Idris's granddaughter, to the workshop. The girl is six, named Fatima after her grandmother. She dips her fingers in a water basin and laughs.
38. Yacob tells Idris the truth: he is dying. A cancer. He came back to say goodbye. "Don't waste the time you have," he says.
39. Idris asks Yacob what he should do about the Frenchman. Yacob thinks. "Sell him the color. But keep the hands."
40. Yacob leaves for Marseille. Idris knows he will not see him again.

### Part V: The Blue (41-50) — Resolution

41. Idris tells Delacroix his answer: he will sell three formulas, not all of them. The Moroccan blue stays with him.
42. Delacroix negotiates. Idris does not. The Frenchman accepts the terms.
43. Idris begins teaching Driss the secrets — the timing, the temperature, the way to recognize the exact moment when the indigo is ready.
44. Samira watches her father teach and realizes he is finally letting go. She begins writing down the formulas herself.
45. Amina bakes bread for the workshop — a celebration. The neighbors come. The dyers' quarter has not seen a gathering like this in years.
46. Madame Dupont commissions one last dress in the old blue. Idris dyes the cloth himself, his hands working with the same certainty they had sixty years ago.
47. Idris writes to Yacob in Marseille, telling him about the sale, the teaching, the granddaughter who laughs at water.
48. The contract is signed. Delacroix returns to Paris with three vials of formula and a sample of blue wool.
49. Driss produces his first batch of true Moroccan blue. Idris examines it, nods once, and says, "Now you are a dyer."
50. Idris walks to the cemetery above Fez, to Fatima's grave. He tells her about the year — the Frenchman, Yacob's visit, Driss's triumph. The sun sets over the city. The sky is the color of his hands.
