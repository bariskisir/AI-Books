Chapter 1: The Station

The lorry stopped at the end of a lane lined with wet elms. Hannah Markham stepped out with her duffel bag and stood on the gravel, looking up at the house. It was a Georgian manor, sagging at the eaves, with blackout curtains hanging in every window. A sign beside the door read: ROYAL SIGNALS ESTABLISHMENT — NO ADMITTANCE.

Inside, the air smelled of floor wax and hot valves. A corporal led her past a room where women sat in rows with headsets, their hands moving across telegraph keys. The clicking filled the corridor like rain on tin. Her quarters were a narrow room with three bunks, a footlocker, and a window that faced the garden. An enamel basin sat on a stand. A single bulb hung from the ceiling.

She put her things down and walked to the radio room. The watch commander nodded at her. "Sergeant Markham. You'll take Station Four." She sat down and fitted the earphones over her head. The set was warm, still holding the heat of the last operator. She adjusted the frequency dial and heard the continuous hum of the European airwaves, voices layered on voices, war moving through wire.

She closed her eyes. The static was a language of its own.

For three weeks she had trained on a mock set in a hangar in Georgia. This was different. This was live traffic. Every pulse she sent might tip a scale somewhere. She opened her eyes and began to copy the morning intercept queue.

Her hands moved faster than she expected. The training held.

Chapter 2: The First Watch

Hannah worked the night shift from midnight to dawn. The room was dim, lit only by the green glow of dials and a single desk lamp. She shared the watch with two other WACs — Sergeant Joyce Collier and Corporal Alice Dunn. They sat at their stations in a row, backs to the wall, faces to the sets.

The traffic was light at first. Routine German logistics, routed through the intercept station at Bletchley and bounced here for relaying. Hannah copied each message onto a signal pad, checked the checksum, and passed it to the code room. The work was mechanical. It required precision, not thought.

At three in the morning, Joyce tapped her shoulder. "Tea." She held out a chipped enamel mug. The tea was strong, laced with powdered milk. Hannah drank it and watched the rain streak the blackout curtain.

Alice fell asleep at her station. Joyce prodded her and Alice jerked awake, blinking. "Sorry. Sorry." The watch commander, a lean warrant officer named Briggs, walked through once an hour, checking logs.

Hannah's eyes ached by five. She blinked and rubbed them, then went back to the frequency sweep. The war had no dead hours. Somewhere, always, a signal was moving.

She thought of Iowa. Of her father's silo, the sound of crows in the corn. It felt like another life. She wondered if she would ever go back to it, or if the war had already taken that from her too.

Chapter 3: A Ghost on the Line

It happened at 03:47 on her fourth night. Hannah had finished a batch of routine intercepts and was sweeping the upper bands for secondary traffic. She turned the dial slowly, past the usual military frequencies, past the Luftwaffe channels, into a stretch of air she rarely visited.

A signal broke the static. Short. Deliberate. Three bursts of Morse, repeated three times.

She checked her log. No scheduled transmission on this frequency. She copied the code without thinking, her pencil moving across the pad.

— .-. . ... .. ... - .- -. -.-. . / -- --- ...- . ... / -. --- .-. -- .- -. -.. -.--

She decoded it. "Resistance moves. Normandy."

Her hand stopped. This was not an intercept. This was a directed message. Someone was sending Allied intelligence from inside occupied France, on a frequency that should have been below the WACs' clearance.

She looked around. Joyce was asleep at her station. The room hummed with transformers and distant signals.

Hannah folded the paper and slipped it into her pocket. Her heart was beating hard. She had never hidden anything from a superior officer before.

"Station log," Briggs said, passing behind her. "Anything unusual?"

"No, sir," she said.

He walked on. The lie settled in her chest like a stone.

Chapter 4: Grey Wolf

The signal returned at 02:15 the next night. Hannah had been waiting for it, her fingers ready on the pencil. The same frequency. The same three bursts. She copied faster this time, her hand steady.

The message was longer. Fifteen groups of five-letter cipher. She filled two sheets of signal pad, her handwriting neat and precise. When the transmission ended, she sat back and let out a breath she did not know she had been holding.

She took the cipher sheets to the code room herself, claiming she wanted to practice decryption. The duty officer, a distracted lieutenant, waved her to an empty table. She worked through the cipher using the day's one-time pad — the same one used for all field traffic.

"We are Grey Wolf. We are inside German signals at Le Havre. Confirming D-Day preparations. Will transmit again tomorrow."

Inside German signals. A source inside the Wehrmacht's own communication corps, reaching out to the Allies on a blind frequency. She read the message three times. This was not a drill. This was an asset.

She had to report it. That was procedure.

She knocked on Colonel Reed's door at 06:00, still holding the paper. He opened it in his shirtsleeves, reading glasses on his forehead, a half-eaten biscuit in his hand.

"Sir," she said. "I have something you need to see."

Chapter 5: The Whisper

Colonel Reed read the decrypted message twice. He adjusted his glasses and read it a third time. Then he looked at Hannah.

"You pulled this off a blind sweep?"

"Yes, sir."

"Isolated transmission?"

"He said he'll transmit again tomorrow. Same time, same frequency."

Reed set the paper down. He turned to the window. The blackout curtain was pulled aside an inch, letting a slit of morning light fall across the floor.

"This is either the real thing or a German feint," he said. "I lean toward real. The intelligence office in London has been expecting contact from a source they code-named Le Havre. Word never reached us."

"Should I maintain contact, sir?"

He looked at her for a long moment. "Yes. But you log every transmission. Every word. Nothing goes outside this station."

"Yes, sir."

"And Sergeant — do not reply to him. Just listen."

She nodded. She knew she would reply anyway. The decision was already made, somewhere below conscious thought, in the part of her that had recognized his signal before her training knew what it was.

Chapter 6: The Reply

The next night, Hannah did not wait for Grey Wolf's signal. She keyed a short transmission of her own on the same frequency before his scheduled window.

"Grey Wolf. This is Operator Echo. Allied signals, Hampshire. We are listening. Confirm your chain of security."

She sent it blind, hoping the German receiver was tuned. Then she waited. The seconds stretched. She counted them by the flicker of the dial light.

He came back at 02:15, as promised. His cipher was tight, professional. He acknowledged her call sign. He confirmed his chain: a Free French courier who passed his messages to a fishing boat out of Dieppe. The operation was solid. The intelligence was real.

The transmission ended with two extra groups, not part of the intelligence. She decoded them last.

"Echo. Have you ever seen the sea at night?"

She read the words three times. The question was personal. It was not in the operational manual.

She did not log it. She folded the paper and tucked it into the lining of her footlocker, under the spare socks.

The sea at night. She thought of the English Channel, black and cold, invisible beyond the manor's garden wall.

She had never seen it. But she knew he was looking at it from the other side.

Chapter 7: Le Havre

Dieter Voss sat in the back of a radio truck parked behind a bombed-out warehouse in Le Havre. The truck smelled of fuel and old sweat. His headphones were warm against his ears. A rat scrabbled in the corner.

He had just sent his nightly report to London — via the frequency the Resistance had given him in Rouen, the one the British had confirmed through a chain of safe houses. His hand still rested on the key.

He was a lieutenant in the Wehrmacht's signals corps, assigned to the 711th Infantry Division. His official job was monitoring Allied radio traffic and reporting troop movements. His real job, the one no one in his unit knew, was passing everything he heard straight to the other side.

It had started in Rouen. He had seen an SS unit drag forty-seven people from a church and shoot them in the square. Women. Children. An old man holding a crucifix. The sound of the rifles had stayed in his ears for weeks.

He had a radio. He knew a man who knew a man in the Resistance. The network gave him a frequency and a cipher. They did not ask why a German officer wanted to help. They did not care.

Now he sat in the dark, listening to the British coast, knowing a woman named Echo was sitting somewhere on the other end. He had heard her key. He knew she was precise, patient, a little nervous on her first reply.

"You are a fool, Voss," he told himself.

He keyed the set anyway.

Chapter 8: The Crossing

Hannah and Dieter built their rhythm over six weeks. The intelligence traffic came first — troop movements, supply routes, codes Dieter had copied from his own command. Hannah passed it to Colonel Reed, who passed it to London. Allied bombers hit three of Dieter's reported targets with surgical precision.

But the second channel grew underneath the first. After the formal traffic ended, Dieter would key a few extra groups. Hannah would decode them and smile in the dark of the radio room.

"Piano is broken. Quartermaster will not requisition new wire. I play on the table instead."

"Iowa is corn and silence. I used to walk the fence lines at dusk. The sky goes purple."

They never said their names. They never said love. But the words accumulated, a private language built from Rilke and shorthand and the spaces between cipher groups.

Joyce noticed Hannah's moods. "You've been smiling at your set," she said one night.

"It's a good set."

"It's a B-mark-two with a shorted tube. It isn't that good."

Hannah said nothing. Joyce let it go, but her eyes stayed thoughtful. She knew something was changing in her bunkmate.

Chapter 9: Suspicion

Captain Braun reviewed the signal logs every morning at 06:00. It was a habit he had developed in North Africa, where a single missed transmission had cost a battalion twelve men and three armored cars.

Leutnant Voss's logs were clean. Transmission times matched scheduled windows. Cipher traffic was standard. Nothing was out of order.

But Braun had a feel for consistency. He had been a radio operator himself in the last war. He knew the shape of a clean log, and he knew the shape of a forged one.

He checked the power consumption records for the radio fleet. Voss's truck drew slightly more charge on certain nights than the logs justified. Not enough to prove anything. Enough to notice.

He mentioned it to Voss during a routine briefing.

"The truck battery is old, Captain. It loses efficiency in the damp."

Braun nodded. He did not press. But he told the duty NCO to keep an eye on Voss's radio truck during the night hours.

He watched Voss walk away. A university man. A pianist. The type who carried books in his kit bag.

Braun did not trust intellectuals in a war. They thought too much. They felt too much. They broke in ways that simple soldiers did not.

Chapter 10: The Cipher

Hannah lay awake in her bunk, listening to Joyce's slow breathing. The ceiling was dark. Rain tapped the window. Somewhere across the Channel, Grey Wolf was sleeping — or trying to.

She needed a way to keep their personal messages separate from the intelligence traffic. If Colonel Reed inspected her logs — and she knew he would, eventually — she needed clean records. The Rilke passages had given her an idea.

The answer came to her at 04:00, when she gave up on sleep and opened her copy of Duino Elegies, a battered paperback she had bought in a secondhand shop in Southampton.

She and Dieter both knew this book. He had quoted it in one of his early personal messages. The lines had felt like a handshake in the dark.

She transmitted a proposal the next night: use Rilke as a private key. Page and line numbers embedded in the intelligence groups. A cipher within the cipher.

Dieter replied within an hour.

"Page 14, line 3. Yes."

She opened the book. Page 14, line 3: "For beauty is nothing but the beginning of terror."

She smiled. It was the perfect key.

Chapter 11: Letters Unwritten

Hannah started a letter she knew she would never send. She wrote it in the dark of the bunkroom, by the light of a shielded torch, with the blanket pulled over her head.

"Dieter — I do not know if that is your real name. I do not know your face. I know you play piano on a table and you have seen SS shootings and you quote Rilke from memory. I know your Morse is sharp and your patience is long. I know the war is killing something in both of us."

She folded the paper and added it to the stack in her footlocker. There were six now. She did not read them again. She just kept them, folded tight, under the spare socks.

If she died, someone would find them and know she had loved a man from the wrong side of the war. If she lived, she might burn them. She had not decided which was worse.

Joyce rolled over in the bunk above. "You writing to that secret beau again?"

"Go to sleep, Joyce."

"That's what I thought."

The bunk creaked. Joyce snored.

Hannah tucked the torch under her pillow and closed her eyes. She imagined Dieter reading the words she had written. She imagined his face. She could not hold the image steady.

Chapter 12: The Drop

Major Delacroix arrived at the station on a Tuesday afternoon. He was a lean man in a Free French uniform, his face lined from years of exile and loss. He asked to see Sergeant Markham.

Colonel Reed brought her to the briefing room. Delacroix stood by the window, holding a folder.

"Your Grey Wolf," he said, "has given us a railway junction near Caen. We hit it last night. Fifteen cars of ammunition destroyed. The line is cut for at least a week."

Hannah said nothing. She kept her face still. But the knowledge that something she had relayed had caused destruction settled in her with a complex weight.

"The intelligence is good. London wants to expand the channel." Delacroix looked at her. "We want to put a dead drop in for him. Fresh papers. A new radio crystal. Can you transmit the coordinates?"

"Yes, sir."

That night, she sent the drop coordinates embedded in the Rilke cipher. Dieter acknowledged within the hour.

She thought of him walking through a French wood, finding a package wrapped in oilcloth under a stone. The intimacy of it — knowing where his hands would be, imagining the damp earth under his fingers.

Chapter 13: The Cipher Deepens

The personal messages grew longer. Hannah and Dieter developed their system into something fluid. Page numbers from Rilke encoded the emotional content. Line references signaled moods.

Page 22, line 7: "I am tired."

Page 6, line 11: "I am afraid."

Page 31, line 4: "I am thinking of you."

They never needed to write these things in plaintext. The page numbers said it all. It was a poetry only two people in the world could read.

Hannah found herself memorizing the Elegies. She could quote passages without looking. She heard Dieter's voice in the meter — even though she had never heard his voice at all. The words became a presence in her mind, a private liturgy.

One night, after a long silence, he sent a page number she did not recognize.

Page 41.

The book had only forty pages.

She decoded it anyway. The group was a false cipher. Embedded in it, using the emergency method they had agreed on, were three words in plaintext.

"I love you."

She read it four times. Then she turned off her set and sat in the dark, trembling. The radio tubes cooled. The static died. She was alone with three words that had crossed the Channel to find her.

Chapter 14: The Rendezvous

Major Delacroix drew the plan on a napkin in the briefing room. A field outside Evreux. A stone marker. A hollow beneath it.

"Your source retrieves the package. He signs back with the code word 'Crystal.' If we do not hear that word, we assume compromise and abort all future contact."

Hannah transmitted the details that night. She kept her hand steady on the key. She did not add anything personal. The operational discipline held.

Dieter's acknowledgment came two hours later, clipped and professional.

Then, ten minutes after the formal acknowledgment, a single extra group.

"Understood. Echo. Wait for me."

She closed her eyes. The set hummed. Joyce was watching her from across the room.

"Everything all right?"

"Fine."

Joyce did not look convinced, but she turned back to her station.

Hannah sat with her hands on the key, feeling the weight of those three words. Wait for me.

She had been waiting her whole life, she realized. She just had not known it until a voice in the static told her to.

Chapter 15: Braun's Net

Captain Braun tightened the perimeter. He ordered radio usage logs audited for the entire battalion, cross-referenced against fuel requisitions and battery charge records. He did this quietly, without announcing his suspicions to anyone but the NCO he trusted.

The results arrived on his desk at 07:00. Ninety-nine soldiers. Ninety-nine clean records. One anomaly.

Leutnant Voss's truck showed a power draw of 14.7 amp-hours on nights when his radio log showed only passive monitoring. Passive monitoring consumed 9.2 amp-hours. The difference of 5.5 amp-hours was transmission power.

Braun set the report down. He stared at the wall for a long time. He liked Voss. The man was competent, educated, polite. He played chess with the enlisted men. He never raised his voice. He brought books to read in the mess.

But the numbers did not lie. Transmission power meant sending, not receiving. Sending meant communication with someone outside the battalion.

Braun called the military police liaison. He did it quietly, off the books, using a telephone in an empty office.

He wanted to be sure before he destroyed a man's career. Or his life.

Chapter 16: The Drop Succeeds

Dieter walked through the wood at dusk. The field outside Evreux was overgrown, tangled with brambles and wild rose. He found the stone marker — a chunk of granite half-sunk in the earth, covered in moss so thick it looked like fur.

He knelt. The ground was soft from three days of rain. He dug with his hands, ten inches down, and felt oilcloth.

The package was small: a false identity card, a set of French papers, and a radio crystal wrapped in wax. He pocketed everything and pushed the soil back into the hole, covering it with leaves.

He walked back through the trees with his heart hammering. A German patrol passed within fifty yards. He stood still behind an oak until their footsteps faded and the forest went quiet again.

Back at the truck, he installed the crystal. The signal clarity improved instantly — the difference between listening through a wall and standing in an open field. He sent his acknowledgment.

"Crystal clear. Thank Echo."

He knew she would read it. He knew she would understand the double meaning. Thank you for the crystal. Thank you for being my echo.

He sat back and lit a cigarette. His hands were shaking. He did not care.

Chapter 17: Echo Answers

Hannah received the acknowledgment at 23:00. "Crystal clear. Thank Echo."

She decoded it with a steady hand and then sat back, letting the relief wash through her. He had the package. He was safe. The channel was alive.

Joyce came over with two mugs of tea and set one beside Hannah's elbow. "You look like you just won a bet."

"Something like that."

"Must be some bet. You've been tight as a drum for three days."

Hannah sipped the tea. It was too hot. She drank it anyway, letting the heat burn her tongue.

"Joyce, have you ever been in love?"

Joyce blinked. "What kind of question is that at eleven at night?"

"A real one."

Joyce thought about it. "I was engaged to a boy in Chicago. He sold aircraft parts. It ended when I shipped out." She shrugged. "It was love, I think. Not the kind you read about in books. The comfortable kind. The kind you settle into like an old chair."

Hannah nodded. She did not know what kind she had. It was not comfortable. It was not safe. It was not anything she had a name for.

It was the kind that came through static, in cipher, from the wrong side of the war.

Chapter 18: The Bond

The air raid siren went off at 01:00. Hannah had been on shift for four hours. She heard the drone of German bombers approaching from the east, the throaty pulse of Heinkel engines mixed with the higher whine of Dorniers.

The station shook. A bomb landed somewhere close — a quarter mile, maybe less. Dust sifted from the ceiling. The lights flickered and dimmed before steadying.

Joyce grabbed her arm. "We need to go to the shelter."

"I can't. I have traffic."

"Your traffic can wait."

Hannah pulled her arm free. She was already scanning the frequency, looking for Grey Wolf's signal. The bombers meant disruption. He might try to send while the British airwaves were confused by jamming and countermeasures.

His signal came through at 01:11, faint under the interference.

"Echo. Stay calm. I am here. Count the intervals with me."

She understood. He was giving her a rhythm to focus on. A technical drill. He keyed a steady pattern — one second on, one second off — and she repeated it back. The repetition was meditative. It anchored her.

The bombing continued. The station groaned. But their signal held, two people pressing back against the dark, one pulse at a time, a thread of human contact in a storm of metal.

Chapter 19: The Static Between

For three days, Grey Wolf's frequency was silent.

Hannah checked it every hour. She adjusted the dial, swept the bands, checked alternate frequencies. Nothing. The air was empty.

She did not sleep. She ate mechanically, chewing without tasting. Joyce brought her food and she pushed it around the plate.

Colonel Reed noticed. "Sergeant Markham, you look exhausted."

"I'm fine, sir."

"Take tonight off."

"I can't, sir."

He studied her. She did not look away. Her jaw was set.

"Very well. But I'm ordering you to sleep after your shift. That's an order."

The second day, she started checking the British casualty lists, looking for a German signals officer with no name. She did not know what she was looking for. She looked anyway.

The third day, she sat at her station at 02:15 — his usual time — and sent a single group.

"Grey Wolf. Are you there?"

No answer.

She sat in the dark until dawn, listening to static, her hand resting on the key like a hand she was holding.

Chapter 20: The Return

The signal came back on the fourth night. Hannah had just finished her tea and was about to sweep the band one last time before log-off.

His key cut through the static at 02:14.

"Echo. Echo. Apologies. Forward post reassignment. No radio access. Back now."

She decoded the message with shaking hands. She read it three times.

Forward post. He had been moved. Not captured. Not killed. He was alive and he had come back.

She keyed her response: "Grey Wolf. Acknowledged. Do not disappear again."

She meant it as operational discipline. She knew he would read it as what it was — a plea.

His reply came within seconds.

"I will not. I promise."

She sat back. Her hands were still shaking. She pressed them flat against her thighs until they steadied. The relief was so sharp it almost hurt.

Joyce looked over from her station. "He's back?"

"Yes."

"You want to tell me who he is?"

"No."

Joyce nodded. "All right. But someone's going to figure it out eventually."

Hannah knew she was right.

Chapter 21: The Leak

A high-priority message arrived from London on Hannah's station. It was marked EYES ONLY — COLONEL REED. She copied the cipher groups and prepared to transfer them to the code room.

But she also copied them into her personal log. She did it without thinking, a reflex built from weeks of hoarding Dieter's fragments, of wanting to keep every piece of the war that touched him.

The message contained details of the Allied deception plan for the Pas de Calais. Fake divisions. Phantom radio traffic. A fiction designed to draw German reserves north before the real invasion in Normandy.

Hannah read the decoded version in her bunk that night using the Rilke cipher.

She knew she should not transmit this. It was above her clearance. It was above Dieter's need to know. It could compromise the entire invasion.

She keyed it anyway.

She told herself it would help him stay safe. She told herself he deserved to know the real plan, so he could position himself correctly and avoid suspicion.

Both lies. The truth was simpler. She wanted to give him something valuable. She wanted to matter to him as more than a relay. She wanted to be indispensable.

She sent the message and folded her personal log into her footlocker.

Chapter 22: The Cross-Check

Colonel Reed had a habit of reading his operators' logs at random. He did not announce these inspections. He simply appeared at a station, pulled the logbook from its hook, and read it standing up.

He pulled Hannah's log on a Wednesday morning. She stood beside him as he turned the pages. Her hands were cold. The radiator hissed.

The log was clean. Every transmission logged. Every frequency noted. Times matched with the code room's records.

But Reed had been doing this job since 1939. He knew the difference between a clean log and a perfect log. A perfect log was a performance. It told him she had gone back and filled in gaps.

He looked at her. "You're a good operator, Markham. Best ear in the station."

"Thank you, sir."

"Good operators don't need perfect logs. They make small mistakes. They miss a checksum. They log a frequency one digit off."

He handed the log back. He did not say anything more.

But Hannah knew, watching him walk away, that he had seen the gaps. The transmissions she had not logged. The traffic that existed only in her memory.

The question was what he would do about it.

Chapter 23: The Interrogation

Reed called her to his office at 17:00. The briefing room was empty except for the two of them. He closed the door. It clicked shut with a sound of finality.

"Sergeant Markham, I am going to ask you a direct question. I expect a direct answer."

"Yes, sir."

"Are you in unauthorized communication with an intelligence asset?"

The word unauthorized hung in the air. She could lie again. She could deflect. She could claim operational necessity. But she was tired of carrying the weight of secrets.

"Sir, I have established a personal communication protocol with the source designated Grey Wolf. The protocol is not documented in my log."

Reed's jaw tightened. "Is he the real thing?"

"Yes, sir. His intelligence is verified. Major Delacroix has confirmed three successful operations based on his information."

"And the personal protocol?"

"It does not interfere with operations. But it exists."

Reed studied her for a long time. The radiator ticked. A truck rumbled past outside.

"Markham, if I report this, you will be relieved of duty and sent home. Possibly court-martialed. Do you understand?"

"Yes, sir."

"Get out of my office."

She left. Her hands were shaking. She did not know if she had just been pardoned or condemned.

Chapter 24: The Dead Man's Switch

Dieter's message arrived at 02:00, short and cold. The tone was different from his usual transmissions — clipped, urgent, stripped of personal warmth.

"Echo. Gestapo embedded in my command. If my cipher shifts by one offset in the middle of a transmission, I have been taken. Do not reply. Do not attempt contact. Burn all records."

Hannah read it twice. She went to the stove behind the mess hall and burned her personal letters, watching the paper curl and blacken. The ink flared and vanished. Forty-seven letters reduced to ash and smoke.

She told Joyce she had been writing drafts of letters home.

"To Iowa?"

"Yes."

"You never told me you wrote letters."

"I don't talk about it."

Joyce looked at the ash in the stove. She said nothing. But her eyes said everything.

Over the next three nights, Dieter's cipher was clean. No offset. No shift. He was still free.

But every transmission felt like a wire stretching thinner, a thread that could snap at any moment.

Chapter 25: The Visitor

Joyce cornered Hannah in the bunkroom. It was late. The other women were asleep, breathing softly in the dark.

"Sit down."

Hannah sat on the edge of her bunk.

"I've been watching you for two months now," Joyce said. "You skip meals. You don't sleep. You spend every night at that set like it's the only thing keeping you alive."

"It's not—"

"Don't." Joyce held up a hand. "I'm not asking for operational details. I don't want them. But you told me once you were in love with someone you'd never met. Was that true?"

Hannah was silent.

"It's the German, isn't it? The source."

"Yes."

Joyce let out a long breath. "Jesus, Hannah."

"I know."

"What are you going to do?"

"I don't know."

Joyce sat beside her on the bunk. The springs creaked. For a long time, neither of them spoke. The only sound was the wind against the window.

"I won't report you," Joyce said finally. "But I won't help you lie, either. If Reed asks me directly, I'll tell him what I know."

"I understand."

"It's going to end badly. You know that."

"I know."

Chapter 26: D-Day Approaches

The first week of June 1944 brought a spike in traffic that pushed every operator to the limit. Hannah worked twelve-hour shifts. Her fingers cramped from keying. Her ears rang with static even after the headset came off.

The coded messages flowing through her station were fragments of a larger machinery. Troop embarkation tables. Beach reconnaissance reports. Weather windows. She saw the edges of the invasion taking shape, a vast machine of metal and men.

Dieter's transmissions grew shorter. His command was on high alert. He sent intelligence in compressed bursts — German defensive positions, reserve divisions, artillery placements along the coast.

On June 3, he sent a single line: "It is coming. I can feel it."

On June 4: "Storms forecast. Delay possible."

On June 5: "If I do not transmit tomorrow, the invasion has begun."

Hannah sent back: "Understood. Be safe."

She stared at the words after she sent them. Be safe. What a useless thing to say to a man on the wrong side of an invasion, sitting in a radio truck that would soon be in the path of the largest amphibious assault in history.

Chapter 27: The Second Silence

June 6, 1944. D-Day.

Hannah did not sleep. She sat at her station with the frequency locked on Dieter's channel, listening through the chaos of signals that filled the air. The invasion traffic was overwhelming — hundreds of transmissions, landing reports, casualty counts, command orders. The entire airwaves belonged to the invasion.

At 02:15, Dieter's frequency crackled. His signal came through, weak but recognizable, buried under layers of interference.

The first group decoded cleanly. The second group shifted by one offset.

Hannah stopped decoding. She read the offset shift again. There was no mistake. She checked the cipher key. She checked the one-time pad. The offset was wrong.

He had been taken.

She keyed a single acknowledgment — the agreed signal that meant "message received" — and then she went silent.

She did not transmit again. She did not try to reach him. The protocol was absolute.

She sat at her set, staring at the dial, while the invasion rolled across the beaches of Normandy. Outside, history was happening. Inside, she was frozen.

Grey Wolf was gone.

Chapter 28: The Hunt

Hannah reported the offset shift to Colonel Reed within the hour. She did not mention the dead man's switch protocol. She simply said the source's transmission pattern had changed and she could not decode the traffic.

Reed called Major Delacroix. The Free French officer arrived within three hours, his face drawn, his uniform wrinkled from a sleepless night.

"The Gestapo raided a signals truck near Le Havre at 04:00 this morning," Delacroix said. "The operator escaped before they arrived. But the truck was captured. They know someone was transmitting."

"Did they identify him?"

"They found papers. A photograph. They have his name now. Leutnant Dieter Voss."

Hannah heard the name for the first time. Dieter Voss. She let it settle in her chest. It felt like a key fitting a lock.

"Is he alive?"

"We don't know. The Resistance network in Le Havre is compromised. We have no contact with his cell. He could be anywhere."

"Then I'll keep transmitting," Hannah said. "He knows the frequency. If he's alive, he'll find it. He'll find me."

Reed and Delacroix exchanged a look.

"Keep your station open," Reed said. "But I'm putting a time limit on this. Ninety-six hours. Then we move on."

Chapter 29: The Underground

For ten days, Hannah transmitted blind. Every night at 02:15, she keyed the same frequency.

"Grey Wolf. Grey Wolf. This is Operator Echo. Report status."

No answer.

She adjusted the time window. She tried 03:00. 04:00. 23:00. Midnight. She swept the adjacent frequencies. Nothing.

Joyce covered her duties when she could. She filed Hannah's logs, answered the watch commander's questions, made excuses.

"She's running a deep intercept analysis. Colonel Reed authorized it."

The lies held. Barely.

Hannah stopped eating properly. Her uniform hung looser on her frame. The circles under her eyes deepened to bruises. She was running on tea and willpower.

On the ninth day, Colonel Reed pulled her aside.

"Markham. He's gone. You need to accept that."

"Sir, with respect — I don't know that."

"You don't know anything else, either."

"I know he got out before the raid. He's alive somewhere. He's just not able to transmit. The Resistance is protecting him."

"That's wishful thinking."

"Maybe. But I'm his only line back to safety. If I stop transmitting, he has nothing. No one else knows his frequency."

Reed stared at her for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"Three more nights. Then we close the channel."

Chapter 30: The Voice Returns

On the tenth night, Hannah was asleep at her station — her head on her arms, the headset still on, a half-empty cup of tea beside her — when a signal broke the static.

"Echo. Echo. This is Grey Wolf. I am alive."

She jerked awake. Her ears were ringing. She thought she had imagined it. The signal was faint, barely above the noise floor.

The transmission came again.

"Echo. I am at safe house. Evreux sector. Resistance protection. Channel is secure."

She keyed her response with shaking hands.

"Grey Wolf. Acknowledged. What is your status?"

"Compromised. Cannot return to unit. Request extraction to England."

She read the words three times.

Extraction to England. He wanted to cross. He wanted to come to her. He wanted to stand on the same ground she stood on.

She sent one word: "Understood."

Then she went to find Colonel Reed.

Chapter 31: The Request

Colonel Reed listened to her report without interrupting. He sat behind his desk, hands folded, his face unreadable. When she finished, he took off his glasses and rubbed his eyes.

"He wants extraction?"

"Yes, sir."

"He's a German officer. Even if he's been working for us, the paperwork to get him into England will take weeks. Maybe months."

"He doesn't have weeks. His cell is compromised. The Gestapo knows his name."

Reed looked at her. "You understand what you're asking."

"I'm asking you to save a valuable asset, sir."

"I'm not talking about the asset. I'm talking about you." He leaned forward. "You have established an unauthorized personal relationship with an intelligence source. If this goes through official channels, that fact will come to light. You could be court-martialed."

Hannah did not answer. The risk did not matter.

"I've watched you fall apart over this man," Reed said. "If he comes to England, what happens?"

"I don't know, sir."

Reed put his glasses back on. "I'll call Delacroix."

He did not say yes. He did not say no.

Chapter 32: The Plan

Major Delacroix arrived the next morning with a map of the Cotentin Peninsula. He spread it across the briefing table, anchoring the corners with coffee cups.

"There's a field here, west of Evreux." He tapped a point near the Seine. "Flat. Eight hundred meters. Good for a Lysander landing. We've used it before."

"Night extraction?"

"Moonless. Weather permitting. We'll have a reception committee on the ground — Resistance fighters. They'll light the strip with torches."

Hannah studied the map. The route was risky. German patrols were thick in the region, still reeling from the invasion. One mistake and the whole operation would collapse.

"I'll be the ground relay," she said. "I'll keep the frequency open from here and guide the pilot in."

"That's above your pay grade, Sergeant."

"I'm the only one who knows his voice. I'm the only one who can authenticate him."

Delacroix looked at Reed. Reed nodded.

"She's right. She stays on the circuit until the package is secure."

Delacroix folded the map. "All right. We move in three days."

Chapter 33: The Betrayal

Captain Braun sat in his office at the Le Havre garrison, reading the Gestapo report for the third time. The paper trembled slightly in his hands.

Leutnant Dieter Voss — missing. Radio truck abandoned. Papers found in the truck linking him to a Resistance network.

Braun had not made the report himself. The Gestapo had beaten him to it. They had opened an investigation into the entire signals corps, interviewing every operator who had served with Voss. They had taken logbooks, duty rosters, personal effects.

Braun told them nothing useful. He gave them the logs. He gave them the power consumption records. He told them Voss had seemed like a competent officer, no different from the others.

But he kept one detail to himself.

He had found, in Voss's footlocker, a book of poetry by Rainer Maria Rilke. The spine was cracked. The pages were marked with pencil. A woman's name was written on the inside cover in ink that was not Voss's handwriting.

He did not know why Voss had kept the book. He did not know what it meant. But the Gestapo did not need to know about poetry.

He burned it in the stove behind his office and watched the pages turn to ash.

Some things, he decided, did not need to be in any report.

Chapter 34: The Night

The extraction was set for July 17, the moonless night. Hannah arrived at her station at 22:00, three hours early. She checked her equipment twice. Then a third time. The set was tuned to Dieter's frequency. The spare crystal was in her pocket.

Joyce came in at 22:30. "You look like you're about to jump out of a plane."

"I'm about to guide one in."

"Same difference."

At 23:00, Hannah opened the frequency. She keyed a short test: "Grey Wolf. Standing by."

His response came within seconds. "Evreux. Ready."

She sat back and began the long wait.

The hours passed. She listened to the static, to the distant chatter of other frequencies. Joyce brought her tea. She drank it without tasting it. Her senses were tuned to one thing only.

At 01:00, the Lysander took off from a secret strip in Dorset. Hannah heard the pilot's confirmation through the crackle.

"Leopard airborne. ETA thirty minutes."

She keyed the message to Dieter: "Thirty minutes. Prepare."

His reply: "Understood. Waiting."

She closed her eyes and listened to the drone of the engine, growing closer across the Channel.

Chapter 35: The Wait

Dieter sat in the farmhouse kitchen. The partisans had left an hour ago to secure the landing field. He was alone with an oil lamp and a pistol he did not know how to use properly.

He had not eaten in twelve hours. His stomach was hollow. His hands were steady.

He thought about Hannah. Her voice. He had never heard it, but he knew her rhythm on the key, the way her transmissions had a slightly longer gap between groups than standard protocol. A signature as distinct as a fingerprint.

He had built an entire woman from that rhythm. He had imagined her face, her hands, the way she might tilt her head when concentrating. He wondered how much of it was real, and how much was the loneliness of war.

The farmhouse clock ticked. A dog barked somewhere in the dark. He checked his watch. Twenty minutes.

He thought of Berlin. Of his mother's apartment, the piano in the parlor, the smell of coffee on Sunday mornings. He would never see it again. The decision to leave had been made months ago, in a village square in Rouen, watching people die.

The partisans' signal came through the window — a bird call, repeated twice.

The field was ready.

He stood up, checked the pistol, and walked out into the dark.

Chapter 36: The Engine

Hannah heard the Lysander's engine at 01:40, a low drone growing from the south. She keyed the pilot.

"Leopard, this is Anchor. Confirm visual on the landing zone."

"Anchor, Leopard. Field is dark. No visual yet. Over."

"Stand by. Reception committee is in position."

She relayed to Dieter: "Aircraft inbound. Light the strip."

Three seconds later, a line of torches flared in the field outside Evreux. The pilot saw them.

"Anchor, Leopard. Visual on the strip. Going in."

Hannah held her breath. The drone sank lower, then cut as the engine throttled back for descent.

She imagined the Lysander dropping out of the night, wheels touching wet grass, the pilot scanning for obstacles in the flare light.

"Leopard is down. Package is moving."

Dieter was running across the field. She could not see him. But she could feel him, like a signal on a frequency she had memorized.

Chapter 37: The Field

Dieter ran. His boots slipped on the wet grass. The Lysander was forty yards ahead, its engine idling, the pilot's silhouette visible in the cockpit. The torches flickered.

A shout behind him. German.

He did not stop. He did not look back.

The first shot cracked past his ear, a sound like tearing fabric. The second hit the ground beside him, spraying mud across his trousers.

He reached the Lysander. The side hatch was open. A hand reached out — the pilot's — and grabbed his coat. He was hauled inside before his feet left the ground.

"Go! Go!"

The engine roared. The plane lurched forward. Dieter was thrown against the fuselage as the Lysander bounced across the field.

More shots. The window cracked. A torch went out.

Then the plane was airborne, climbing steeply into the dark. The engine strained. The ground fell away.

Dieter lay on the metal floor, gasping. The pilot's voice came through the intercom.

"Welcome to England, sir."

Dieter closed his eyes.

Hannah heard the pilot's confirmation at 01:53.

"Anchor, Leopard. Package secure. En route to base."

She took off her headset. She pressed her hands to her face. The tears came silently, in the dark, with Joyce's hand on her shoulder.

Chapter 38: The Crossing

Hannah tracked the Lysander's flight by radio for the next forty minutes. The pilot checked in at waypoints — Evreux to the coast, coast to mid-Channel, mid-Channel to English airspace.

"Leopard crossing coastline now. ETA base twenty minutes. Weather clear. No intercepts."

Hannah keyed a message to Dieter — one she knew he could not read, not in the air, not without a set.

"You are safe. I am here."

She sent it anyway, hoping the transmission would reach him somehow, that he would feel it in the vibration of the aircraft skin.

When the Lysander landed at the secret strip in Dorset, the pilot's final confirmation came through as a laconic crackle.

"Leopard on deck. Package delivered. Tea time."

Hannah laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in weeks. It was a strange sound, unfamiliar in her own throat.

Joyce looked at her. "He made it?"

"He made it."

Joyce hugged her. It was brief and firm and exactly what Hannah needed.

"Now what?" Joyce asked.

"Now I wait."

Chapter 39: Debrief

Dieter was taken to a safe house in Pimlico, London. The house was narrow, terraced, with blackout curtains and a wireless that played BBC news all day in the kitchen. He was given civilian clothes — a brown jacket, white shirts, gray trousers — a ration book, and a fake identity as a Dutch refugee named Jan de Vries.

Major Delacroix visited him on the second day. They sat in the basement kitchen, drinking tea that was too weak.

"Your operator," Delacroix said. "The one who guided you in."

"Yes."

"Her name is Sergeant Hannah Markham. She is a WAC signals specialist stationed in Hampshire."

Dieter set down his cup. "Can I meet her?"

"Not yet. It's too dangerous. If you are seen together, the connection can be traced. The Gestapo has sources everywhere, even in England."

"By whom?"

"By anyone who is looking."

Delacroix left a newspaper and a pack of cigarettes. Dieter did not smoke. He read the newspaper from cover to cover, looking for any mention of a German signals officer who had disappeared from Le Havre.

There was nothing.

He was a ghost now. Officially dead to the army he had betrayed. He wondered if Hannah would still want him, now that he was just a man without a uniform, without a war to define him.

Chapter 40: The Gap

Hannah went back to her station. The traffic continued. D-Day had succeeded. The Allies were pushing through France. The radio room buzzed with victories and losses, progress and cost.

She transmitted to Dieter's frequency every night, but there was no reply. She knew he was in London. She knew he was alive. But he was a ghost in the system, with no radio access, no way to reach her without breaking his cover.

The silence was worse than when he had been captured. Then, she had hope. Now she had knowledge — knowledge that he was breathing the same air, walking the same streets, and she could not touch him.

She wrote him a letter, sealed it, and put it in her footlocker.

"Dieter. I am still in the same country as you. I am still breathing the same air. But there is a distance between us that transmission cannot close. I want to see your face. I want to hear your voice — your real voice, not the one I imagine when I read your Morse. I do not know if this is love or loneliness. But I know it is something I cannot end by signing off."

She folded the letter and added it to the stack.

Forty-seven letters now. Unread. Unsent.

Chapter 41: The Exception

Colonel Reed called Hannah to his office on a gray morning in August. The door was open. Major Delacroix was inside, standing by the window.

"Sit down, Sergeant."

She sat.

"We have a problem," Reed said. "Your asset — Voss — is in London. He's been debriefed. He's been cleared. But he's asking about you."

"About me, sir?"

"He wants to meet you."

Hannah's heart stopped. "Is that possible?"

Reed and Delacroix exchanged a look.

"It's against every protocol we have," Delacroix said. "A WAC operator meeting a former German officer who was her intelligence source. The security review alone would take months. The paperwork would bury us."

"But?"

Delacroix smiled. "But Colonel Reed has arranged a 48-hour pass to London. You will visit a cousin. The cousin does not exist."

"Where do I stay?"

"A flat in Pimlico. Number 14. The address is already in your file as a family contact."

Hannah looked at Reed. "Why are you doing this, sir?"

Reed leaned back in his chair. "Because I've been watching you for four months. You're a good soldier. The best operator I have. You deserve something the war cannot give you."

He paused.

"Don't make me regret it."

Chapter 42: The Door

Hannah took the train to London on a wet August morning. She wore her best uniform and carried a small bag with a change of clothes. The compartment was full of soldiers and nurses, all of them moving toward something or someone.

She got off at Waterloo and walked through streets still scarred from the Blitz. Empty lots where buildings had stood. Walls with faded advertisements for soap and tea. Pimlico was quiet, residential, almost untouched. Number 14 was a narrow townhouse with a black door and white steps.

She stood on the pavement for a full minute before she knocked. She had imagined this moment for months. Now that it was here, she did not know what to do with her hands.

The door opened.

He was taller than she had imagined. His hair was dark, his eyes were gray, and his face was thinner than it should have been. He wore civilian clothes — a brown jacket, a white shirt, no tie — and he looked at her the way she had looked at the sea for the first time.

"Operator Echo," he said.

His voice was low. It had an accent, soft and precise. It was real.

"Grey Wolf," she said.

He stepped aside. She walked through the door.

Chapter 43: The First Hour

They sat in the narrow front room. A coal fire burned in the grate. The curtains were open — a luxury they had never known during the war, having no blackout restrictions in a safe house.

Neither of them spoke.

Dieter poured tea from a pot that had been sitting too long. It was dark and bitter. Hannah drank it anyway. It gave her hands something to do.

"I practiced what I would say," he said finally. "I had four months to prepare. A whole speech. And now I have nothing."

"Neither do I."

He laughed. It was a small sound, surprised out of him. The tension in the room eased.

"I know your Morse," he said. "I know your pauses. I know you touch the key differently on sad days."

"You know my radio voice. Not me."

"Is there a difference?"

She considered the question. "I don't know yet."

They sat in silence again. It was not uncomfortable. It was the silence of two people learning to speak without cipher, without code, without the distance of the air between them.

Chapter 44: The Walk

After dark, they walked through the streets of London. The city was a patchwork of light and shadow — some buildings still standing, others reduced to rubble shaped like teeth. Searchlights swept the sky over the Thames, looking for a raid that did not come.

Dieter walked with his hands in his pockets. Hannah kept her arms folded against the cold.

"France is different," he said. "The damage is less visible there. England looks like a wounded animal still breathing."

"England looks like home now."

"Is it?"

She thought about Iowa. The cornfields in August. The grain elevator against the prairie sky. The quiet that went on for miles.

"No," she said. "But I don't know where home is anymore."

They turned onto a dark street. The buildings were intact here, Georgian terraces with blackout curtains drawn tight. No one was watching.

Dieter reached out and took her hand.

His fingers were warm. They were callused — not from work, but from the key. She knew those calluses. She had the same ones, on the same fingers.

They walked without speaking. Their hands fit together like a cipher mating.

Chapter 45: The Morning

Hannah woke in the narrow bed of the Pimlico flat. The curtains were thin and the morning light was gray, filtered through London clouds. She lay still, listening to the unfamiliar sounds — a lorry passing, a woman calling to a child, the clatter of a milk cart on the street below.

She was not in the bunkroom. She was not in Hampshire. She was in a flat in London, and Dieter Voss was making coffee in the kitchen. She could hear him moving, the clink of a spoon against a cup.

She got up. She put on her uniform — the skirt, the blouse, the tie. She checked herself in the small mirror above the dresser.

Her face looked the same. But it felt different. The eyes that looked back at her were the same gray-green, but they held something new.

She walked into the kitchen. Dieter was at the stove, heating a pan of water. He looked up.

"Good morning," he said.

"Good morning."

He poured the water into two cups. Instant coffee. The only kind available in wartime England. It was bitter and thin.

She took the cup. Their fingers brushed.

"I don't know what happens next," she said.

"Neither do I."

"But I know I don't want it to end."

He looked at her. His gray eyes were steady.

"Then let it not end."

Chapter 46: The Return

Hannah went back to the station. The train ride was quiet. She watched the English countryside roll past — green fields, stone walls, small villages with church spires. Ordinary England, doing its best to ignore the war.

Joyce met her at the gate. "You look different."

"I feel different."

"It was good?"

"It was everything."

Joyce smiled. "Good. Now get back to work. We've got a war to finish."

The work absorbed her. The traffic was relentless — supply lines, troop movements, casualty reports. The Allies were pushing toward Paris. The German army was fracturing.

She did not transmit to Dieter's frequency. She did not need to. She had his voice in her memory now, the real one, the one that said "Good morning" with a Berlin accent. She played it back when she closed her eyes.

Three weeks later, Paris was liberated. Joyce opened a bottle of gin in the bunkroom and they drank it out of enamel mugs.

"To the end," Joyce said.

"To the end."

Hannah drank. She thought of Dieter, somewhere in London, listening to the same news on the wireless.

The war was ending. She did not know what would survive of them when it was over.

Chapter 47: The Liberation

Paris fell on August 25. Dieter watched the newsreel in a cinema in London, sitting in the dark among strangers who cheered and wept. The images showed French crowds lining the Champs-Elysees, women kissing soldiers, the tricolor flying from the Eiffel Tower.

He felt nothing. Relief, maybe. But the joy on the screen did not reach him. He had seen too much of the war's other face.

He was assigned to a Free French unit preparing to enter Germany. The Allies needed German speakers. They needed men who understood the enemy's communications, who could read the traffic and predict the responses. His expertise was still useful.

He was given a new uniform — Free French, not British — and a new identity. He was officially Lieutenant Denis Vernier, a naturalized French citizen from Alsace.

He wrote a letter to Hannah and gave it to Major Delacroix to forward.

"I am going east. I do not know when I will return. But I will return. I have crossed one ocean of static to find you. I can cross a continent."

Delacroix took the letter. He did not promise delivery.

"You'll see her again," he said. "But not yet."

Chapter 48: The Long Silence

Autumn turned to winter. The Allies pushed into Germany. The radio traffic shifted from invasion logistics to occupation administration. Hannah's station was reassigned to relay duties between London and the advancing armies.

She scanned the casualty lists every morning. She looked for the name Voss. She looked for the name Vernier. She looked for any name that might be his, any fragment that might tell her he was still alive.

The lists were long. She never found him.

She wrote letters she did not send. The stack in her footlocker grew to sixty-three. Sometimes she read them, but mostly she just added to them, a chronicle of waiting.

In December, the Ardennes offensive sent a surge of casualties through the system. Hannah worked double shifts. She stopped eating meals. She stopped sleeping.

Joyce forced her to eat a sandwich. "You're no good to him dead."

"I don't even know if he's alive."

"Then assume he is. It's the only thing that keeps you going."

Hannah ate the sandwich. She went back to work.

Germany surrendered in May 1945. The war in Europe was over.

Hannah sat at her station and listened to the silence. There were no signals to intercept. No traffic to relay. The airwaves were empty.

Chapter 49: The Letter

The letter arrived on June 10, 1945. It was a standard military envelope, stamped and censored, addressed to Sergeant Hannah Markham at the Hampshire station. Handwritten. The postmark said Frankfurt.

The return address was a transit camp.

She opened it with shaking hands. She read it standing in the hallway, leaning against the wall.

"Hannah — I am alive. The war ended for me in a village outside Berlin, where I was processing surrenders for three weeks. I have been cleared for repatriation. I am in Frankfurt now, waiting for transit papers.

I do not know what happens next. I do not know if there is a place for a German in England, or if there is a place for me in your life. But I am asking — formally, in writing, the way we never could over the wire — if you will wait for me.

If you will meet me.

Frankfurt. The train station. Platform 3. I will be there on September 1, at noon, wearing a gray coat.

I love you. I have loved you since the night you told me the frequency was clear. I will love you until there is no more static.

Come find me.

— Dieter"

Hannah folded the letter. She put it in her pocket, next to her heart.

Then she went to request leave.

Chapter 50: Operator Echo

Hannah stood on Platform 3 of Frankfurt's Hauptbahnhof on September 1, 1945. The station was a ruin — half the roof gone, the walls pocked with shrapnel holes, the clock tower stopped at 11:47. Refugees milled across the concourse, carrying bundles and children and the hollow look of survival.

She wore her WAC uniform. It was pressed. Her shoes were shined. She had traveled for three days to stand on this platform, crossing the same Channel she had crossed with her signals so many times.

The clock read 12:03.

She waited.

A train arrived from the east. Steam billowed across the platform, dimming the faces. People stepped off — soldiers, civilians, displaced persons. She scanned every face.

Then she saw him.

He was thinner. His coat was mended at the elbow. His hair was longer than she remembered. But his eyes were the same gray she had seen in the Pimlico flat, in the farmhouse kitchen of her imagination, in every dream she had carried across the months.

He stopped when he saw her.

He did not say her name.

He said: "Operator Echo."

She said: "Grey Wolf."

The station noise faded. The refugees, the announcements, the clatter of wheels — all of it blurred into static, the familiar sound of distance collapsing.

She stepped forward. He met her halfway.

They did not need words. They had used enough words to cross a war. They had burned through ciphers and frequencies and the patience of everyone who loved them. They had spoken through static and silence and the long gaps between.

She took his hand. The calluses matched.

They walked out of the station together into a country that was rubble and dust and beginning.

The static was gone. The signal was clear.
