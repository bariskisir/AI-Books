The Bone Orchard Trail

Barış Kısır


Chapter 1: Dust and Salt

Eli Crane came down out of the salt hills at the hour when the light goes the color of a healing wound. The sun sat fat and low on the rim of the basin and the whole country had gone copper and rust. His mare picked her way through the creosote without being asked. She knew the smell of a town the way she knew the smell of water, and she wanted both.

He had been four days on the trail from the railhead and three weeks from anywhere a man might call home, if he had one. He did not. He had a saddlebag, a rifle, a name written on a scrap of paper, and a debt he had carried so long it had worn a groove in him.

The town announced itself first as a smudge of woodsmoke, then as a scatter of low buildings the same color as the dirt they stood on. Quartz Hollow. A water tower leaned on tired legs. A church with no bell. A street that was only a street because the buildings agreed to face each other across it.

Eli reined up on the last rise and looked the place over the way he looked over everything, slow, counting exits. That was when he saw the grove.

It stood on a ridge north of the town, set apart, fenced. Trees, but wrong. They were pale as old bone, leafless, their limbs twisted up against the bruised sky like the fingers of buried men reaching for air. There were a great many of them. More than a town this size could have planted, more than this dry ground should have grown. In all that brown country they were the only thing not the color of dust, and they were the color of death.

The mare stopped. She did not like the grove. He felt the shudder come up through the saddle into his own legs.

"Easy," he said. His voice was rough from disuse.

A man was walking the road below, leading a mule. Eli rode down to meet him and touched his hat.

"Evening. That orchard up there. What do they grow in it?"

The man looked at the trees and then, deliberately, looked away from them. He was old and his face had the closed-up look of a door that has been kept shut a long time.

"We don't grow nothing in it, mister." He clicked at his mule. "That's the bone orchard. That's where we put folks when they're done."

"A graveyard."

"If you like." The old man was already moving on. "You passing through?"

"Looking for a woman named Naomi Hale."

The mule stopped. The old man did not turn around.

"Way-station, east end of the street," he said. "You'd do well to do your business and ride on before the moon goes dark, mister. Plenty of room out here for a man to keep riding."

Then he went on toward his supper, and Eli sat his horse in the failing light and looked up at the pale trees until the dusk took them, and even after the dusk took them he could feel them up there, the way you feel a man standing behind you in an empty room.

He rode into Quartz Hollow as the first lamps came on. Nobody waved. A dog watched him from under a porch and did not bark, which troubled him more than barking would have. The whole town had the held-breath quiet of a place that has learned not to draw attention.

He found the way-station by its sign, which had once said HALE'S and now said HAL'S where the E had fallen. A light burned in the window. Behind it, against the lamp, the shape of a woman moved.

Eli sat a moment in the dark before he got down. He had ridden a thousand miles to knock on this door and now that he was here he found he did not want to.

The debt did not care what he wanted. It never had.

He swung down, tied the mare to the rail, and went up onto the porch with his hat in his hand.


Chapter 2: The Way-Station

The woman who opened the door had a rifle in the crook of her arm, held casual, the way a person holds a thing they have had to use.

"We're closed," she said.

"I'm not looking for a bed." Eli kept his hands where she could see them. "I'm looking for Naomi Hale."

"You found her. Who's asking."

"Eli Crane. I knew your husband. Tom and I rode together, long time back, before either of us was much good." He paused. "I owe him a debt. I came to pay it."

Something moved in her face. She had a strong face, sun-cured, the kind that did not waste itself on small expressions. She lowered the rifle an inch.

"You came a long way to pay a debt to a man who can't collect it," she said. "Tom's been dead two years, Mr. Crane."

He had known it might be so. A man does not stay gone as long as Tom had stayed gone and remain among the living. But hearing it set out plain like that, two years, dead, it landed in him heavier than he expected, and he stood there on the porch holding his hat and could not for a moment find anything to say.

"How," he managed.

"Fever, they said." She stepped back from the door. The lamp behind her threw her shadow long across the boards. "Come in. You've a face like a man who's going to fall down, and I don't want to drag you."

The way-station was one long room. A stove, a plank table, shelves with too little on them. A ledger lay open on the table, its pages dense with names in a careful hand. She saw him look at it and closed it.

"Sit," she said. She poured coffee gone thick on the stove. "Two years ago Tom owed money he couldn't pay. Then he took sick. Then he was in the ground. That's the whole of it, told quick. You want it told slow, it'll cost you a night's work mending my fence, because I can't pay for the telling and I don't take charity."

Eli wrapped his hands around the cup. "I'll mend the fence."

"Then we understand each other." She sat across from him and studied him in the lamplight. "What's the debt?"

"He pulled me out of a river once," Eli said. "When I'd given up on it. Carried me a mile to a fire. I told him I'd come if he ever needed it." He looked at the closed ledger. "I came too late."

"Most do." Naomi drank her coffee. Outside, the wind worked at the loose board on the porch, and far off, faint, came a sound Eli took at first for an animal and then was not sure. A long, low note, like wind through a hollow thing. She did not seem to hear it, or had taught herself not to.

"Where's he buried," Eli asked.

Her cup stopped halfway to her mouth.

"Up the ridge," she said. "In the orchard. Same as everybody." And she would say no more about it that night.


Chapter 3: The Ledger

He woke before light on a pallet by the cold stove and lay listening to the house. Naomi was already up. He heard her at the well, the creak of the rope, the slap of the bucket. He rose and went out and mended the fence as he had said he would, working the wire with his cold hands while the sun came up gray over the basin.

When he came in she had set out cornbread and the ledger was on the table again, open, turned toward his chair. She watched him from the stove.

"You were a lawman," she said. It was not a question. "Tom told me. A marshal. Said you were the straightest man he ever rode with and the unhappiest."

"He talked too much."

"He's dead. Let him." She nodded at the book. "You know how to read a record. Read that one."

Eli sat and turned the pages. It was a burial ledger, kept in two hands — an older crabbed one and Naomi's clean one. Names, dates, a column for the cause. He read down it. Fever. Fever. Drowned in the wash. Old age. Fever. Then a run of entries with no cause given at all, just a name and a date and, in the cause column, a single dash.

"You keep the town's dead," he said.

"Somebody has to. Pastor won't. The sexton can't write." She leaned over and put a finger on the dashes. "Count those for me, Mr. Crane. The ones with no cause. Then count the graves you can see from that ridge, and tell me if the numbers sit easy with you."

He went back through the book, marking the dashes. There were a great many. Far more than fever and old age. Far more than a town of this size could have spared and still had a town left.

"Drifters, most of them," she said quietly. "Men passing through. Men nobody waits on. They come in for water and they don't ride out. And every name in that book, Mr. Crane, every single one, is up in that orchard. We are the smallest town in this basin and we have the biggest graveyard in the territory. You're a man who counts things. So count."

He closed the book. The low note from the night before came back to him, the hollow sound on the wind.

"What is it you think is happening here," he said.

"I don't think," Naomi said. "I just keep the book. You're the one who used to wear a star." She took the ledger from under his hands. "Mend the rest of my fence. Then go up and see your friend's grave. And tell me, after, whether you still want to pay what you came to pay."


Chapter 4: Doc's Bottle

The town had one street and the street had one place a man could buy a drink, a low building with no name over the door, only a faded picture of a bottle. Eli went in at the heat of the day to take the measure of the place, which was a habit older than the debt.

It was dim and it was nearly empty. Two old men played a slow game of cards in the corner. Behind the bar a man with the broken-veined face of a serious drinker polished a glass that was already clean.

"Whiskey," Eli said.

The barman poured. "You're the one staying at the Hale place." It traveled fast in a town this small. "Friend of the dead husband."

"Word gets around."

"Word's all we've got out here. That and dust." He slid the glass over and poured himself one to match. "I'm Sennett. I doctor folks when they let me and bury them when they don't. Which is most of the time." He drank. "You've been up to look at the orchard yet?"

"Saw it from the road."

"Don't go at night." Doc Sennett said it flatly, the way a man states the weather. "I mean that kindly. I've doctored in this basin thirty years and I've signed more death certificates with a lie on them than any honest man should. You want to know how many of those bodies I actually saw cold before they went in the ground? Damn few. They go in fast here. Reede sees to it."

"Who's Reede?"

Doc looked at him a long moment, and something in his ruined face shut like Naomi's had shut.

"Absalom Reede owns the cattle, the water rights, and most everything else that's worth owning between here and the salt flats," he said. "He's a fine old gentleman and he'll likely send for you, you being new and interesting. You'll like him. Everybody likes him." He refilled both glasses. "Here's some doctoring for free, Crane, the only honest prescription I've got. Pay your respects to the dead man, settle your conscience, and ride out of this hollow before the moon goes dark. It's eleven days off. Be a hundred miles from here when it does." He raised his glass. "That's the whole of my medicine. The rest is just whiskey."


Chapter 5: The Green Acre

In the morning Eli rode out to see the country, telling himself it was to learn the land and not to learn anything else.

The basin was dying. He had ridden through dry country all his life and knew the look of it, but this was past dry, this was a land giving up. The washes were bone. The grass, where there was grass, was the color of straw and crumbled when the mare's hooves touched it. He passed a homestead with the door standing open and nobody in it, the well-rope gone gray and stiff. He passed the carcass of a steer that the buzzards had already finished with, its hide drawn tight over the ribs like canvas over barrel-staves.

Then he came over a low rise and the land changed as though a line had been drawn across it.

Below him spread a wide shallow valley, and it was green. Not the gray-green of desert scrub but true green, deep grass moving in the wind, a creek running silver through the middle of it. Cattle grazed there, fat and slick-coated, more cattle than the rest of the basin could have fed in its best year. Beyond them stood a big white house with outbuildings around it, neat as a town.

Eli sat and looked at it for a long time. He had crossed maybe a hundred yards from dust to pasture. The same sky, the same season, the same drought that was killing everything behind him, and here the world was fat and easy.

A rider came up the valley toward him at an unhurried lope. A young hand, no more than twenty, with a cheerful empty face.

"Help you, mister?" the boy called. "This here's Reede land. All of it, far as you can see, and a good ways past."

"Just looking," Eli said. "Hadn't seen grass in three days. Wanted to be sure it was real."

The boy grinned. "Real as it gets. Reede grass don't burn off like the rest. Mr. Reede, he's got the touch." He said it lightly, the way a man says a thing he has heard so often he has stopped hearing it. "You're welcome to water your horse at the creek, but the house is private, and the north ground past the cottonwoods, you stay off of that. That's the boss's particular ground."

"What's on the north ground?"

The boy's grin flickered. For just a moment the empty cheer went out of his face and something else looked through, something that had learned not to ask.

"Nothing," he said. "Just stay off it." He touched his hat and rode back down into all that impossible green.


Chapter 6: A Star in a Drawer

He came back at dusk to find Naomi in the lean-to behind the station, sorting through a trunk by lantern. She did not hide what she was doing when he came in, which he took as a kind of trust.

"Tom's things," she said. "I go through them when I can't sleep. Don't know what I'm looking for. Some word he left, maybe. He didn't leave one." She lifted out a folded shirt, set it aside. "Found something of yours, near enough."

She held up a tarnished five-pointed star.

"It's not yours," she said. "It's Tom's. But he kept it because of you. Said his friend Crane wore one just like it and threw it in a river one day and never said why." She turned it in the lamplight. "He always wondered. Truth is, so do I now, with you sitting in my house."

Eli took the star from her. It was warm from her hand. He had not held one in seven years and his thumb found the worn place on the back of its own accord, the place where a man's thumb goes a thousand times a day.

"I hanged a man," he said. He had not meant to say it. The lantern and the dead man's trunk and the long day pulled it out of him. "Had a warrant, had a witness, had a confession even, after a fashion. Hanged him in front of the town for killing a girl. Eight months later they caught the man who really did it. He had a way of talking that made it all make sense, why he'd let another man swing for him." He set the star down on the trunk. "The man I hanged had a wife. I went to tell her myself. Figured I owed her that. She didn't say a word the whole time. Just looked at me. That look did more than any rope ever did." He shook his head. "I gave the star back the next week. Been riding ever since."

Naomi was quiet a while. Then she folded the shirt smaller and laid it back in the trunk.

"And here you are," she said, "in a town that hangs folks slower, and don't bother with a trial at all." She closed the lid. "You ought to know that before you decide to stay and be useful. Whatever's killing this basin, Mr. Crane, the law you used to carry has no jurisdiction over it."


Chapter 7: The Sexton's Rows

He could not put it off any longer. In the gray of the next morning he walked up the ridge to the orchard to find Tom's grave.

The fence around it was old and good, posts of the pale wood itself, the wire rusted to lace. A gate hung on leather hinges. Eli let himself through and the sound of the town fell away behind him as though a window had been shut.

Inside, the trees stood close. Up near they were worse than from the road. The bark was not white like birch but white like fat, like tallow, and it gave faintly under his glove when he touched it, warm where it should have been cold. The limbs grew in no pattern he knew, joint over joint, more like bone than wood, and at the forks of them the trunks bore dark stains, old and dried, that ran down in lines toward the roots.

The graves lay in rows between the trees, and there were enough of them to make the back of his neck cold. Most were marked with no more than a board or a stone with no name. The earth over the newest was dark and fresh-turned.

A man was working at the far end of a row. Old, bent, in shapeless clothes, turning earth with a long-handled spade. He did not look up as Eli came along the row, though he must have heard him.

"Morning," Eli said.

The man straightened. His face was a ruin of years and weather and his mouth, when it opened, made no sound at all. He touched his throat and shook his head. Mute. He had pale eyes that took Eli in entirely, head to boot, and seemed to find in him something he had expected.

"I'm looking for the Hale grave," Eli said slowly. "Tom Hale. Buried two years back."

The sexton studied him a moment longer. Then he pointed with the spade, down a row to the left, toward a tree that stood a little apart, taller than the rest, its pale limbs spread wide. He pointed at its foot. Then he made a strange motion with his free hand, a sweeping gesture low to the ground, palms down, that Eli took at first for nonsense and only later understood. *Keep it down. Press it down. Hold it in the earth.*

Then the old man bent back to his digging, and Eli went down the row to find his friend.


Chapter 8: Reede's Invitation

Tom Hale's grave lay under the tall tree, marked with a proper stone, the only proper stone in the orchard. NAOMI'S DOING, Eli thought. Somebody had paid for it. He stood over it with his hat in his hands and did not know what to say to a man two years in the ground, so he said nothing, which is what most of the dead get and likely all they want.

The earth over the grave was not settled the way two-year earth should be. It had a low mounded look, the soil cracked across the top, as though something beneath had swelled. He told himself it was the heat. He had seen ground do strange things in drought.

He did not tell himself that very well.

When he came back down to the town a rider was waiting at the way-station, a different man than the cheerful boy, older, harder, with a good horse and a coat too fine for the country. He was talking to Naomi on the porch, and she had her arms folded and her face shut.

"Mr. Crane," the rider said, turning. He had a smooth way of talking. "I'm Pruett. I keep things running for Mr. Absalom Reede. Mr. Reede heard there's a stranger in the Hollow with an interesting history, and Mr. Reede does enjoy interesting company. He'd be honored if you'd take supper at the ranch tomorrow evening."

"Would he," Eli said.

"He would. Sends a horse if yours is tired, but I see yours is fine." Pruett's eyes went over the mare and over Eli and missed nothing, the way Eli's own eyes worked. Two men of the same trade recognizing each other. "Sundown tomorrow. The whole basin eats better at Reede's table than anywhere this side of the railhead. You won't regret it."

He touched his hat to Naomi, who did not return it, and rode off down the street.

Naomi watched him go. "You'll go," she said. It was not a question either.

"I'll go."

"Of course you will. You count things, and the biggest thing in this basin is up that green valley, and you want a look at it." She turned to go inside. "Just remember whose table you're eating off of when you count. Every fat steer Reede owns, Mr. Crane, every blade of that grass, has a price. I've been writing the price down in my ledger for two years." She paused in the doorway. "And he knows it. That's why he's asking you to supper. He likes to know what a counting man has counted before that man decides what to do about it."


Chapter 9: Supper with the Baron

The Reede house was bigger inside than it looked from the rise, and cool, and full of things that had no business in a dying basin — a long polished table, silver, a clock from somewhere east that ticked in the corner like a patient heart. A woman who did not speak served the food. There was beef, of course, and bread, and wine, and Eli ate carefully and drank little and watched.

Absalom Reede sat at the head of the table. He was very old. His hair was white and fine as cobweb, his hands spotted and thin, but his eyes were clear and quick and there was nothing old in them at all. He had a courtly, unhurried way, a man who had never in his life had to raise his voice to be obeyed.

"You wore a star," Reede said, when the eating had slowed. "A territorial marshal. And then you stopped." He smiled gently. "I won't ask why. A man's reasons for laying down a burden are his own. I only observe that you carry the look of a man who set one thing down and picked up something heavier."

"You're observant."

"I've had a long time to practice." Reede turned his wine in the candlelight. "You've seen my land, Mr. Crane. Green in a year of dust. The other ranchers hate me for it and the town fears me for it and not one of them understands it. Do you want to know how it's done?"

"I expect you'll tell me what you want me to hear."

Reede laughed, a soft dry sound. "Honest. Tom Hale was honest too, and look where it got him." He saw the small change in Eli's face and nodded. "Yes. I knew your friend. He owed me money and he was honest about that as well, right to the end. I think you'll find, Mr. Crane, that I am not the villain this town wants me to be. I am only a man who pays his debts and expects others to pay theirs. You of all men should respect that. You crossed a wide country to pay one."

The clock ticked. Somewhere in the house a dog began to howl and was silenced.

"I'll be plain," Reede said. "You're a capable man with no place to be. I have need of a capable man. There's a wage in it, and a roof, and no questions about your history. All I ask is order. This basin needs order. Will you think on it?"

"I'll think on it," Eli said, because it was true, and because the thing to do with a man like Reede was to keep him talking and learn the shape of him. But under the table his hand had found the edge of his knife without his telling it to, and he could not have said why, except that the old man's eyes had not aged, and the clock in the corner did not sound like a clock at all the longer he listened to it.


Chapter 10: What the Dog Won't Cross

He rode back from the ranch under a moon three days off full, the road silver, the basin silver, the orchard up on its ridge catching the light and throwing it back wrong — not white now but a faint sick yellow, like old ivory, like teeth.

The mare had carried him out to the Reede land without trouble. She would not carry him past the orchard on the way home. A quarter mile from the ridge she stopped dead and would not go on, ears flat, trembling, sweat breaking dark on her neck though the night was cool. He pressed her and she sidestepped and tried to turn and run, and he had to fight her down and take the long way around through the wash to keep from passing under the trees.

Down in the town the dogs were awake. Not barking. He had been in a hundred towns and the dogs always barked at a rider in the night. These did not. They sat on the porches and in the yards, every one of them, faces turned the same direction, toward the ridge, and they made a low continuous sound in their throats that was not a growl and not a whine but something underneath both, the sound a dog makes at a thing it cannot fight and cannot flee.

Doc Sennett was on the step of his place with a bottle, watching the ridge too.

"They do that," Doc said as Eli passed. His voice was thick with drink. "Closer it gets to dark of the moon, the worse they do it. Three nights from full, they start. By the dark they won't eat. Won't sleep. Just face the hill and make that sound." He took a pull from the bottle. "Animals know things, Crane. We bred the knowing out of ourselves so we could live next to the orchard. Dogs never learned the trick." He lifted the bottle toward the pale trees. "Listen to them. They're smarter than every soul in this Hollow. They're telling you to run, same as I did."

Eli got down and stood in the street and listened to the dogs and watched the orchard burn its faint cold yellow against the night, and for the first time since the river, since the rope, he felt the old cold drop in his belly that meant a thing was wrong in a way no law could fix.


Chapter 11: The First Walking

He did not sleep. He lay on the pallet by the stove and listened to the dogs and around the dead middle of the night, when the moon was high and the sound of the dogs had thinned to a single exhausted note, he got up and stood at the window.

The orchard glowed faintly on its ridge. Among the pale trunks, near the fence, something stood that was not a tree.

It was a figure. A man, by the shape of it, tall and still, standing just inside the fence and facing the town. It did not move. Eli watched it for the space of fifty breaths and it did not move at all, and that stillness was worse than any motion, because no living man stands fifty breaths without shifting his weight, without turning his head, without being a man. It simply stood, faced down at the sleeping town, and waited.

Naomi came up beside him in the dark. He had not heard her. She had a shawl around her shoulders and her face was calm in the way of a person who has seen a thing many times and made her peace with being afraid of it.

"You see it," she said.

"I see it."

"They come up to the fence on the nights before the dark," she said quietly. "Just one, mostly. Just stands there. Looking down at us. Choosing, Doc thinks, though Doc thinks a lot of things into a bottle." Her breath fogged the cold glass. "Whatever you do, don't go up there in the night to meet it. Two men have. Their names are in my ledger now, with a dash where the cause should be."

The figure on the ridge turned its head.

It was too far to see a face, too dark, but Eli felt the turn of it the way the mare had felt the orchard, straight through the glass and across the quarter mile of silver dust, a weight of attention settling on the little lit window and on the two people behind it. He did not move. Neither did Naomi.

After a while the figure turned its face back to the general dark of the town, and Eli let out a breath he had not known he was holding, and they stood at the window together until the moon went down and the thing on the ridge faded back among the trees, and by the first gray light the orchard was only trees again, and the dogs at last lay down to sleep.


Chapter 12: Tom's Stone

In the morning Eli went up the ridge again, in daylight, when a man's nerve is a thing he can mostly trust.

The orchard in daylight was only a graveyard, he told himself, walking the rows. The sexton was not there. The fresh grave he had seen before was filled and settled. He went down the left-hand row to the tall tree where Tom Hale lay.

The stone was as he had left it. But the earth was not.

The mounding he had taken for a trick of drought had grown worse. The soil over the grave had risen, cracked across in a long seam, and pushed up at one end as though something below had set its shoulder against the ceiling of the world and shoved. At the head of the grave, where the crack was widest, the dark earth had been disturbed from below, not above — he could see that plain, the way the clods turned, the way no rain or heat turns them. Pushed up. From inside.

He knelt and looked into the crack. It went down farther than a crack in dry ground has any right to go, down into a dark that the morning sun could not reach.

Out of it, faintly, came a smell. Not rot. He knew rot; he had buried men. This was something else, mineral and cold, the smell of a deep well, of stone that has never seen the sun, and under it, very faint, a smell like iron, like blood gone to rust.

He stood up fast and stepped back. Down the ridge the town was waking, smoke from the chimneys, the small ordinary sounds of people who had decided to go on living next to this. He looked at the cracked earth over his dead friend and remembered the sexton's gesture, the palms pressing down, *keep it in the ground*, and he understood for the first time, fully, in his body, that the thing Doc and Naomi and the dogs had all been trying to tell him was true.

The dead did not stay buried in the bone orchard.

And Tom Hale, who had pulled him from a river, was trying to come up.


Chapter 13: The Hanging He Carries

That night, against his will, he dreamed the hanging again, the way he always did when a place got under his skin.

It was a clear cold morning in a town whose name he had made himself forget. The whole town had turned out, because they did then, for a hanging. The man on the scaffold was named Asa Polk and he was a quiet man, a saddler, and he had not killed the girl, though it would be eight months before anyone but Asa Polk knew that. Eli had built the case. Eli had found the witness, a man who later proved to be the killer's brother. Eli had heard the confession that was no confession, beaten out of a frightened man who would have confessed to drowning the moon.

He had been so sure. That was the thing the dream always showed him, that was the worst of it — not the drop, not the sound, but his own certainty, the clean righteous weight of the star on his chest as he read the warrant aloud. He had been a man doing right. He had felt it like sunlight.

Asa Polk had looked at him from the scaffold. Not with hate. With a kind of patient sorrow, as though Eli were the one to be pitied. *You'll find out,* the look said. *You'll find out, and then you'll have to live in the knowing.*

Eli woke in the dark of the way-station with the sheet wet through and the dream still on him.

He lay still and listened. The dogs were quiet. The dark of the moon was nine days off. He had time, Doc had said. Nine days to do his business and ride.

But he knew now he was not going to ride.

He had spent seven years running from one piece of knowing, and here was another, waiting for him up on a ridge under a tall pale tree, and a man can only run so far before he understands that the thing he is running from is the thing he is carrying. Tom Hale had pulled him out of a river. Eli had told him he would come if he was needed.

He was needed. He was two years late, but he was needed, and this time he was not going to be too sure and he was not going to be too slow. He was going to find out what was in the ground under his friend, and what kept it fed, and he was going to put it back.

He got up before light and started, the only way he knew how to start anything, by going to find out what was true.


Chapter 14: Pastor Without Words

The church had no bell and, Eli soon learned, no services to ring one for. He found Pastor Wells out behind it, a thin gray man hoeing a garden that the drought had already beaten, working the dead ground anyway because a man has to do something with his hands.

"Pastor."

Wells straightened, wary. "You're the marshal. Word travels." He leaned on the hoe. "I don't have much for a stranger. The church is open if you want to sit in it. God's welcome to come back to it any time He likes." He said it bitterly, an old bitterness, worn smooth.

"I want to ask about the orchard."

The hoe went still. "Then you want the wrong man."

"You bury the dead. That's a preacher's office."

"I used to." Wells looked at the dead garden. "I used to read over every grave in that orchard. Words of comfort. Earth to earth. The resurrection and the life." He gave a short laugh with no humor in it. "Do you know what it does to a man's faith, Marshal, to say the words *the resurrection and the life* over a grave, and then to come back in the morning and find the earth disturbed? To say *rest in peace* over a hole, and to know — to know, the way I came to know — that there is no rest in it at all?"

He set the hoe against the wall of the church, deliberately, like a man laying down a weapon.

"I stopped reading over them eleven years ago," he said. "I won't say the words anymore. The words are a lie here, and I have told enough lies. The sexton puts them in the ground and I stay in my house and I let it happen, because the one time I went to Reede and named it out loud, the one time, he sat me down very kindly and explained to me what would happen to this town if his land went dry. And then he asked me whether I'd rather have a few drifters in the orchard, men nobody waits on, or the whole basin dead." Wells's hands were shaking. "God forgive me, Marshal, I couldn't answer him. I still can't. That's the whole of my sermon. Now go away and let me beat this dead ground a while longer, it's the only honest work I've got left."


Chapter 15: The Dry Years

Naomi told him the rest over supper, because he asked, and because she had decided, he thought, that the time for not telling had passed.

"It wasn't always like this," she said. "When Tom and I came out, fifteen years back, this was good country. Hard, but good. Rain in the spring, grass enough for everybody's stock, the creek ran year-round. There were six ranches in this basin then and a real town, near three hundred souls." She turned her cup. "Then the dry came. Not a dry year. The dry. Five years now without a true rain. The creek went first, then the wells, then the grass. The other ranches sold off or starved off or just packed up in the night. The town bled out to a hundred, then fifty, then what you see."

"But not Reede."

"But not Reede." She nodded slowly. "His land never browned. His creek never quit. While the rest of us watched our stock die on their feet, his got fat. First folks thought he had a spring nobody else knew of. Then they thought he had luck. Then —" she stopped. "Then the orchard started to fill up faster than the dying could account for. And the dogs started their business on the dark of the moon. And the earth started moving over the graves. And folks stopped thinking out loud about Reede's water, because every soul who pushed too hard on the question ended up with a dash in my ledger."

She looked at him steadily across the lamp.

"He bought the rain, Mr. Crane. That's what nobody will say. Somehow, the year the dry came, Absalom Reede bought his land out of it. And the price wasn't money. I've kept the accounts of this town for two years and I can tell you the price isn't money. It's names. A name every dark of the moon, in my book, with a dash where the reason ought to go." She closed her eyes a moment. "Tom understood it before he died. He'd figured it. He went up to that house to have it out with Reede over what he owed. He came back gray and quiet and three days later he had a fever, and four days after that he was in the orchard. You count things. Count that."


Chapter 16: Counting the Missing

He counted. He spent two days at it, with Naomi's ledger open and a stub of pencil, working back through the years the way he had once worked a case file.

He made three lists. The first was the named dead — the townsfolk, the ones with a real cause beside them, fever and age and accident. That list was sad but ordinary, the natural toll of a hard country.

The second was the drifters. The men passing through. He cross-checked them against the way-station's old register, which Naomi had kept faithfully, and the pattern stood up out of the numbers like a fence-line out of fog. Men rode in, watered, took a room or a meal, and then — for a certain kind of man, the kind with no one waiting, the friendless and the broke and the running — there was no second entry. They never rode out. They went into the orchard with a dash beside their name.

The third list was the worst, and he made it last, and he made it twice to be sure. It was the dark of the moon. Beside each new moon, going back five years, there was a burial. Always. Every single dark of the moon for five years, somebody went into the orchard. No exceptions. In sixty-odd months there was not one new moon that did not have its name.

"It's a tally," he said to Naomi, when she came in from the well and found him gray-faced over the book. "Not a graveyard. A tally. He's paying something off, a name at a time, and he's never once missed a payment in five years." He set down the pencil. "And the next payment's due in seven days."

"I know," Naomi said. She sat down heavily across from him. "I've known a long while. It's a different thing, knowing it alone in your own head and hearing a man say it out loud at your table." She looked old in the lamplight, suddenly, and tired. "What I never could work out is who picks the name. Who decides, this dark, it'll be this one. Because they always seem to choose folks the town won't miss too loud." Her jaw tightened. "Until they run short of strangers. There've been no strangers in a month, Mr. Crane. You're the first to ride in. And the payment's due in seven days."


Chapter 17: The Spanish Word

Doc Sennett kept more in his rooms than whiskey. Eli found that out when he pressed him, when he laid the three lists on the bar and refused to take a drink until the doctor talked.

Doc looked at the lists a long time. Then he poured himself one, drank it, and said, "Come in back."

His back room was a surprise — books, real ones, shelves of them, and a desk, and the smell of an educated man gone to seed. From the bottom of a trunk Doc drew out an oilskin packet, and from the packet a thing that was very old: a sheaf of brittle pages in a brown ink, written in a cramped hand, in Spanish.

"A priest's journal," Doc said. "Found it years back in the ruin of the old mission, south of the salt flats. I had enough seminary Latin and border Spanish to read it. I've half wished ever since I couldn't." He laid it on the desk under the lamp. "There was a mission here before there was a town, before there was anybody white in this basin at all. The padre who kept this came out to convert the people who lived along the creek. And he wrote down what they tried to warn him about."

He turned the brittle pages.

"There was a thing in the ground, they told him. Old. Older than their grandfathers' grandfathers. It lived down a deep place, a dry well, a wound in the earth, and it was always thirsty, and the land around it would only stay green if it was given what it wanted." Doc's finger found a line and held it. "They had a word for it. *El sediento.* The thirsty one. And the old people had a bargain with it, a careful old bargain, that kept it fed just enough and kept it down. The padre called it devilry and he broke the bargain. He filled in the well with stones and he planted a grove of trees over it, holy trees he said, to seal it, and he wrote that the land would now flourish by the grace of God instead of the favor of the devil." Doc looked up. "The next page is the last one. The drought came that year. And the trees the padre planted came up white."


Chapter 18: Cole's Fear

He was crossing the street the next evening when a young man stepped out of the alley beside the saloon and said his name. "Crane. Eli Crane. I need a word and I can't be seen needing it."

He was perhaps thirty, well-dressed in the Reede way, but soft, with a weak chin and a nervous hand that kept going to his collar. He had his father's clear eyes and none of his father's stillness.

"Cole Reede," he said. "Absalom's son. His only one." He glanced both ways down the dark street. "My father offered you work. I'm asking you not to take it. I'm asking you to do the opposite."

"The opposite being."

"Stop him." Cole's voice cracked on it. "You were the law. You're the first man to ride in here in years who isn't already in his pocket or too scared to spit. I've waited a long time for a man like you." He wiped his mouth. "I know what he does. I've known since I was a boy. He took me up to the well once, when I was twelve, to show me what I'd inherit. To teach me my duty." His face worked. "I have spent my whole life trying not to think about my inheritance, Crane. I drink, I gamble in the railhead, I do everything I can to make myself unfit, and still he means to leave it to me, the land and the green and the thing in the well that has to be fed. I don't want it. I'd rather have dust. I'd rather have nothing."

"Then why come to me," Eli said. "Why not stop it yourself. You're his blood. You're closest to it."

"Because I'm a coward," Cole said simply, and the plain truth of it sat strangely well on him, better than any of his finery. "I've known what I should do for fifteen years and I have never once had the nerve. You quit being the law over one man's death, didn't you, that's the story. You've got a conscience that costs you. Mine never cost me a thing, that's the trouble, I just paid it off with whiskey." He stepped back into the dark. "The payment's due in six days. He's run out of strangers. He'll have to take one of ours this time, or —" He stopped. "Or you. A man with no one waiting. Think on that, Marshal. You rode in just in time to be useful to him." And he was gone down the alley, quick and quiet, a coward who had at least found the nerve to warn another man.


Chapter 19: Night Ride to the Grove

He had to see it for himself. A counting man does not act on what he is told; he acts on what he has watched with his own eyes. So three nights before the dark of the moon, against every sound piece of advice he had been given, Eli took the mare out into the wash below the orchard and tied her where she could not see the trees, and went up the ridge on foot in the dark.

He came at the orchard from the north, the long way, and lay down in the creosote at the top of the rise where he could see the fence and the rows. The moon was a thin paring, near gone. The trees stood pale and patient. He waited.

Near midnight a lantern came bobbing up the ridge from the direction of the Reede land. The sexton walked ahead with it. Behind him came two of Reede's hands leading a horse, and over the horse's back was a long bundle wrapped in canvas, the shape of which Eli knew at once and wished he did not.

They came in at the gate. The sexton led them down a row to a place where the earth was already opened, a grave dug and waiting. The hands lifted the bundle down and laid it in, not roughly but not gently either, the way men handle a chore. The sexton bent and did something to the wrapped shape that Eli could not see, some arrangement, some rite. Then the hands took up shovels and filled the grave in, quick, and the sexton stood over it with his lantern and his head bowed.

The hands went back down the ridge. The sexton stayed.

And as Eli watched, the old mute man knelt at the foot of the fresh grave and pressed his palms flat to the new earth, pressed down hard, the same gesture he had made for Eli in daylight, holding the ground down, holding it shut. He stayed like that a long time, palms to the earth, as though he could feel through them what stirred underneath, as though by the weight of his own old hands he meant to keep one more thing in the ground a little longer.

Then he rose, took up his lantern, and went down to the town, and Eli lay in the creosote with his heart going hard, because he had wanted proof and now he had it, and proof, he had forgotten, is a thing you cannot give back.


Chapter 20: The Risen Hand

He went down to the grave when the sexton's lantern was small in the distance, because he had to know who was in it, and because some cold deliberate part of him needed to see the thing all the way through.

The earth was new and loose. He had no tool but his hands and the flat of his knife, and he worked fast, down through the soft fill, telling himself he would only go far enough to see the canvas, to know it was a man and not a story, to put a name to it if he could.

He never reached the canvas.

A foot down, his fingers found something that was not soil. He brushed the dirt away in the thin moonlight and saw it: a hand. A human hand, pressed up against the underside of the fill, palm out, fingers spread, as though the body below had reached up toward the surface before the dirt was even tamped — or after. The fingers had pushed up into the loose earth so far that they had nearly broken through. Around the wrist the dirt was packed and scored where the hand had worked at it.

It was not the hand of the man they had just buried. The man they had buried was deeper, was wrapped, was below this. This hand came up from lower still, from an older grave, from beneath the new one, reaching up through the dead man toward the air and the moon and the world.

Eli knelt over the open dirt with the cold of the ground coming up into his knees, and he understood the orchard then, all at once, the whole grammar of it. They did not bury one body to one grave. They buried them down on top of each other, layer over layer, year over year, feeding them all to the same shallow black depth, and underneath, in the dark, the dead reached up. They were all reaching up. The whole ridge was full of buried hands reaching toward the light, held down only by the weight of the dirt and the weight of the sexton's palms and the patience of the thing they fed.

He filled the grave back in with his bare hands, fast, packing the dirt down hard over that reaching hand, pressing his own palms flat to the earth at the end the way the sexton had, before he even knew he was doing it. *Stay down,* he told it, all of it. *Stay down.* And then he went down off the ridge in the dark faster than was safe, and was sick once in the wash, quietly, and rode back to town with his hands still black to the wrist.


Chapter 21: Reede's Offer Again

Pruett came for him again two days later, and this time the invitation was not to supper.

"Mr. Reede would like an answer," Pruett said. He sat his good horse easily in the street and his coat was open enough to show the pistol under it, which was the answer to a question Eli had not asked. "He's a patient man, but the work he offered won't wait forever, and a man ought to know which side of a thing he stands on, in a small place like this."

"What's the work, exactly," Eli said. "He was vague about it over the beef."

"Order." Pruett smiled. "Same as any deputy's work. There's a fair wage, regular. There's a house. There's the friendship of the most powerful man between here and the railhead, which in this country is worth more than the wage and the house together." His smile thinned. "And there's the other thing, which you've likely worked out by now, you being a counting man. There's looking the right direction. Once a month, on a particular night, a deputy of Mr. Reede's makes sure the town keeps to its houses and nobody goes up the ridge with questions. That's all. It's the easiest hard money you'll ever earn."

"And if I don't take it."

"Then you're a stranger with no work and no one waiting on him," Pruett said pleasantly, "in a town with a great big graveyard and a payment due in four days. I'd think hard, Crane. There's two kinds of men out here. There's the ones who help feed the orchard." He turned his horse. "And there's the ones who get fed to it. Mr. Reede's offering you the better job. I'd take it." He rode off, unhurried, certain, and Eli stood in the street and watched him go and understood that the offer and the threat were the same sentence, and that he had until the dark of the moon to decide which kind of man he was going to die as.


Chapter 22: What Keeps the Cattle Fat

He rode out one more time to the green valley, openly now, because hiding had stopped serving him. He sat on the rise and looked at the fat cattle and the deep grass and the silver creek, and he made himself look at it the way he now knew to look.

The line between dust and green ran exactly along the foot of the orchard ridge. He had not seen that the first time. The green did not spread from the creek outward, the way water spreads. It spread from the ridge down, from the orchard down, soaking into the valley below the rows of the dead. The grass was greenest right under the orchard fence and faded, very slightly, the farther you got from the trees, out to that hard line where the Reede land ended and the dying basin began.

It was not the creek that fed the green. The creek ran because the green let it. The green came down off the ridge, out of the graves, out of the reaching hands and the bodies layered in the dark and the thing in the well drinking them down. Whatever *el sediento* was given, it gave back — gave back as grass, as water, as fat on the cattle, as life for Reede's land bought with the lives the orchard took. The dead went down into the dark and came back up as green.

He thought of the hand reaching up through the dirt and the grass growing greenest right above it and he had to look away for a while at the clean empty dust of the dying country, which seemed suddenly honest and almost kind.

A man could understand it, was the terrible thing. He could understand Reede. A drought that would have killed a basin, a thing in the ground that would take a few friendless men in trade for a whole valley's life — a man could do that arithmetic and come out on Reede's side and never lose a night's sleep, if he were a certain kind of man, and most men, Eli knew, were that kind, given dust enough and fear enough.

He had been that kind once. He had hanged a man to keep a town feeling safe, and felt the sun on it.

He turned the mare around and rode back toward the Hollow, and somewhere on the ride he stopped deciding whether to take Reede's offer, because he found that he had already decided, a long way back, maybe at the river twenty years ago, maybe at the scaffold seven years ago. He was going to put it back in the ground. All of it. He just did not yet know how.


Chapter 23: The Debt Named

That night Naomi told him the last of it, the part she had kept even from herself, mostly, by not saying it.

"You asked how Tom died," she said. They were at the table and the lamp was low and the dogs had begun their business early, three nights out, a low sound under everything. "I told you fever. That's what's in my book. There's a dash beside it, though. You'll have noticed. You notice everything."

"I noticed."

"He owed Reede four hundred dollars. We'd borrowed it in the second dry year to keep the station, when nobody was passing through to pay for rooms. Reede lent it easy and smiling, the way he does. And then the third year came and we couldn't pay, and the fourth, and Tom went up to the house to ask for more time." She folded her hands very tightly. "He didn't come back the same. He'd worked something out up there, sitting across from Reede. He understood the orchard, what it was, what it cost. And he understood that Reede had run low on strangers that month, and that a debtor who couldn't pay was the next best thing to a man with nobody waiting."

Eli sat very still.

"Reede offered to clear the debt," Naomi said. "The whole four hundred. He told Tom there was a way a man could settle everything he owed, all at once, and provide for his widow besides — Reede would see I kept the station, see I never wanted. All Tom had to do was — be willing. When his time came. To go quiet and not make the town talk." Her voice did not break but it went very flat, which was worse. "Tom said no. Tom came home and told me we'd find the money or lose the station, but he wouldn't pay it that way, not ever. He held out three days. On the third night he took a fever that no fever takes a strong man so fast. And on the fourth day he was in the orchard, and the debt was marked paid in Reede's book, I saw it later, paid in full." She looked up. "So that's your debt, Mr. Crane. You came west to pay Tom a debt. Tom paid one too. They're the same debt, near enough. They both end up in that orchard."


Chapter 24: A Light in the Big House

He went back to the Reede ranch on the second night before the dark, on foot, in the dark, the way he had gone to the orchard. He had decided he needed to see the old man when the old man thought no one was watching, because that is when you learn the truth of any man.

He left the mare a mile out and came in across the green pasture, which was eerie underfoot, soft and thick and alive in a country where nothing else lived, and which smelled, faintly, of the same cold mineral iron-smell as the crack over Tom's grave. He skirted the outbuildings where a hand or two still moved with lanterns and worked around to the back of the big white house.

The house was dark but for one window, low and to the rear, and there was a light moving beyond the house entirely, out past it, a single small light in the open ground behind, where the cottonwoods stood. The forbidden ground. The boss's particular ground.

Eli went toward it, low, slow, using the cottonwoods for cover.

Absalom Reede was alone out there in the dark. He had set a lantern down on the ground and he was kneeling in the dirt beside a low circle of fitted stones — an old well, dry, its mouth maybe four feet across, its stones the same pale color as the orchard trees. The old man knelt at the lip of it with his white head bowed, and he was speaking. Eli could not hear the words at this distance, only the shape of them, a low steady murmur, the cadence of a man at his prayers, or a man making a report, or a man reassuring a creditor that the next payment was coming, that the books would balance, that there was no cause for concern.

And from the well, when Reede paused, came a sound in answer.

Eli felt it more than heard it, a vibration in the ground, in his teeth, in the soft alive grass under his hands, a long low note like the hollow sound he had heard on his first night in the Hollow, like wind through a buried throat. The lantern flame leaned toward the well-mouth though there was no wind. The old man bowed lower. And Eli lay in the cottonwood shadow with the cold coming up out of that fed and living ground, and listened to a man hold conversation with a hole in the earth, and knew he had seen the bottom of the whole thing at last, or near enough that he could guess the rest.


Chapter 25: The Thing at the Well

He should have gone then. He had seen enough. But he had not heard, and a counting man wants the figures themselves and not just the shape of them, so when Absalom Reede at last rose stiffly, took up his lantern, and went back into the dark house, Eli waited a long careful while and then went to the well.

The grass grew right to its lip and over it, green tongues of it curling down into the dark of the shaft. The stones were warm. He knelt where the old man had knelt, against every instinct he owned, and looked down.

He could see nothing. The shaft went down past the lantern-memory of light into a black that the thin moon could not touch. But the smell came up out of it, the deep cold mineral smell and the iron under it, stronger here, the source of it, and as he knelt there the low note came again, close now, just below him, and with it, this time, words.

They were not in any language. He understood them anyway, the way a man understands a dog's warning or a baby's cry, below words, in the body. The thing in the well was thirsty. It had been thirsty a long time, longer than the town, longer than the mission, longer than the people who had first made the careful old bargain to keep it down. It was patient the way stone is patient. And it was speaking to him now, kneeling above it where Reede knelt, because it did not greatly care which man knelt there, which man brought the names, so long as a man did. *You,* it said, without saying. *You will do. You have a thing to put down too. Bring it to me. Bring me what you carry and I will take it and you will rest.*

For one long moment, kneeling over the dark with the dream of the scaffold close behind his eyes, Eli wanted to. That was the thing he carried out of the orchard and never told Naomi. For one moment the offer was sweet — to give the thing the weight he had hauled seven years, Asa Polk and the rope and the patient sorrowful look, to lay it down into that cold patient dark and walk away light.

He stood up. His legs were not steady. He stepped back from the well and the note followed him, disappointed, patient, in no hurry at all.

"No," he said aloud, to the hole, the first word he had spoken in an hour. "I'll carry my own."

Then he went back across the living grass and out into the honest dust, and he did not stop until he was well clear of the green, and even then he could feel the well at his back, the way he had felt the orchard, the way a man feels a thing that has decided to wait for him.


Chapter 26: The Empty Coffins

"I have to know how it works," Eli told Doc Sennett, "before I can stop it. I've seen the well and I've seen the rows, but I need to see what's in the ground. With you. You're the only man in this town who's looked at the orchard sober and lived to keep books on it."

Doc poured himself a drink and did not drink it. That, more than anything, told Eli the doctor was afraid.

"You want to dig," Doc said.

"I want to dig."

They went up the ridge the night before the night before the dark, with lanterns hooded and two shovels and a bottle that Doc carried but, to his credit, did not open. Eli chose three graves — an old one from the year before, a middling one, and one from a season back, names he had matched in the ledger to drifters with dashes beside them.

They dug the old one first. It was hard work in the night and they were both breathing rough by the time the shovels struck wood. They cleared the lid and Doc, with a steadiness that came from somewhere deep under the drink, pried it up.

The coffin was empty.

Not robbed. Not disturbed from above. The lid had been nailed and the nails were old and the earth above unbroken until they broke it. The coffin had been filled and closed and buried, and now there was nothing in it but a long shallow depression in the rotted cloth where a body had lain, and at the foot of the box, the wood was broken outward, splintered down and out, into the earth below. Whatever had been in the coffin had gone down out of it. Through the bottom. Into the dark.

They dug the second grave and it was the same. And the third. Empty boxes, the cloth pressed where men had lain, the bottoms broken downward, the soil beneath each one looser than soil has any right to be, packed with the going of something, leading down.

Doc sat back on the edge of the third hole and at last opened his bottle and drank.

"Thirty years," he said. "Thirty years I've signed the certificates and let them go in the ground and never once let myself dig them up, because some part of me always knew." He wiped his mouth. "They don't rot, Crane. They don't lie there and rest. They go down. To it. And the ground stays green." He looked at the empty boxes with a terrible weariness. "We've been planting them, all these years. Not burying. Planting. And it's the crop that keeps the cattle fat."


Chapter 27: Marks on the Trees

In the lantern light, refilling the graves, Eli saw the trees up close at night for the first time, and saw what daylight had let him miss.

The pale trunks bore handprints.

Not many on any one tree, but on every tree, here and there, at the height a kneeling man's hand might fall, or a standing one's, prints pressed into the bark itself as though the bark had once been soft and a hand had laid against it and the wood had grown around the touch and kept it. The prints were dark, stained with the same dried sap that ran from the forks of the limbs, the sap that smelled of iron when he leaned close. Some of the prints were large and some small. Some had the broad shape of a working man's hand and some the slighter shape of a woman's. All of them faced the same way, palm to the trunk, as though every soul that had gone down into this ground had paused on the way to lay one hand against a tree, in farewell, or in grief, or in the simple animal need to touch a thing that stood upright in the dark.

"The padre planted them to seal the well," Doc said quietly, watching Eli touch one of the prints. "To hold the thing down. But you don't seal a thirsty thing with a roof. You just give it a way to push up." He nodded at the trees. "They drink, Crane. The roots go down to it. They drink what it drinks, and they grow this color, and they put these marks out. The dead touch them on the way down. Or the trees touch the dead. I've never been sure which way it goes, and I've never wanted to find out." He picked up his shovel. "Cover it up. Cover all of it. There's one night left before the dark, and we know now what we're trying to stop, which is the worst kind of knowing there is, because now we have to do something about it."


Chapter 28: The Sexton Speaks Once

The sexton was at the orchard in the gray before dawn, as they were finishing, and he did not run and he did not raise alarm. He stood at the end of the row with his spade and watched the two men patting down the last of the disturbed earth, and his ruined old face held no anger, only a kind of exhausted recognition, as though he had been waiting a long time for somebody to come and dig.

Eli walked down the row to him with his hands open and empty.

"You know what's in the ground," Eli said. "You've tended it longer than anybody living. I've seen you press it down. I saw you kneel on the new grave with your hands flat. You're trying to hold it, aren't you. As best a man can."

The sexton looked at him with those pale eyes. Then he set down his spade. He pointed at the rows, at the whole orchard, all the close-grown pale trees and the layered dead beneath them. He held up his two old hands and pressed the palms down on the air, the gesture Eli knew now. *I hold it down.* Then he turned one hand over, empty, and shook his head slowly, and Eli understood that too. *I cannot hold it forever. I am old. It grows.*

Then the sexton crouched and with one crooked finger wrote in the dust of the row, in large careful letters, a single word.

MORE.

He tapped the word. He pointed at the new grave, the one filled that night Eli had watched from the creosote. He pointed at the thin sliver of moon dying in the brightening east, which would be gone by tomorrow. He held up one finger, then opened both hands wide, then wider, a thing growing, a thing that had once been content with a name a season and now wanted a name a month and would soon, the gesture said, want more than that, would want a name a week, a name a night, would want everything, because that is the nature of a thirst that is fed: it does not lessen. It learns.

The sexton wiped the word away with his palm, as though it were dangerous to leave lying about. Then he took up his spade and went back to the only work he had, the holding-down, the losing fight, and Eli left him to it and went down off the ridge to find Naomi, because the dark of the moon was one day off and he had run entirely out of time to plan.


Chapter 29: Reede's Long Life

Doc was waiting at the way-station when Eli got down, and he had the priest's journal with him, and his hands were not steady.

"I sat up the rest of the night with this," Doc said, spreading the brittle pages on the table. "I'd never read it close enough. I'd never wanted to. But you've made wanting a thing a man can't help now." He found a page. "The padre wrote down the name of the first man who made the bargain after he broke it. The first white man. The one who came when the drought hit, and went out to the filled-in well, and dug it open again, and made the old offer in the old way because he had cattle dying and a fortune to lose." Doc looked up. "Crane. The name in this journal is Absalom Reede."

Eli was quiet.

"This journal is sixty years old," Doc said. "Older. The padre wrote it as an old man, recording the end of his mission, and he names Absalom Reede as a man already grown, already running cattle, when he made the bargain." He pushed the page across. "I have been Absalom Reede's doctor for thirty years. He has not aged a day that I can measure. I took him for sixty when I came and I take him for sixty now. I always told myself he wore well. Men do." He laughed, a cracked sound. "He doesn't wear well, Crane. He doesn't wear at all. He made the bargain sixty years ago at least, maybe more, and the thing in the well has been holding back his death the whole time, one name at a time, every dark of the moon, for sixty years." He sat back. "That's what the bargain bought him. Not just the green grass. The green grass is the small print. The real article is that Absalom Reede was promised, long ago, to that thing in the ground — and he has spent sixty years buying himself out of the promise, a stranger at a time."


Chapter 30: The Widow's Plan

"Then we burn it," Naomi said. "The orchard. The trees, the rows, all of it. We pour coal oil on it and we put a match to it and we burn the whole ridge down to the rock, and let Reede's grass die, and let the basin be honest dust again."

She said it standing, with her fists on the table, and there was a hard light in her that Eli had not seen before, two years of buried grief finding at last a direction.

"It would feel good," Doc said carefully. "I won't pretend it wouldn't. But —"

"But what. They're dead trees over dead men. They'll burn."

"They drink from the well," Eli said. "The roots go down to it. The padre planted them to seal it — Doc thinks they're more lid than anything else, a poor lid but a lid. You burn the lid off a thing that's been pushing up against it for sixty years —" He stopped. "I knelt over that well, Naomi. I heard it. It is patient because it is held. The trees hold it, the sexton's hands hold it, Reede's payments hold it, all of it together, barely. Burn the trees and you don't kill it. You take the roof off. You let it come up all at once, instead of a name at a time."

Naomi's fists loosened slowly. "Then what. You've torn down every plan I had and you've not put one up."

"Doc," Eli said. "The journal. Did the padre write how the old people kept it down, before Reede dug it open? Not just fed it — kept it. There's a difference between feeding a thing and closing it."

Doc turned the pages slowly, frowning, reading the cramped brown ink. "He says the old bargain wasn't only giving," he said at last. "He says the old people believed the well could be closed for good, but only once, and only with a particular price. There's a word here I had to puzzle at." He held the page to the lamp. "The first. *El primero.* The first one promised. He says the thing can be closed forever, the trees will die and the green will go and the dead will rest, if it is given the first body that was ever promised to it." He looked up slowly. "The first body promised. The one that made the bargain in the first place and then cheated it."

The lamp hissed. Outside the dogs had stopped their noise entirely, which was worse than any noise, and the three of them sat in the silence and understood at the same moment who *el primero* was.


Chapter 31: Cole's Betrayal

He should have known the meeting in the alley would cut both ways. A coward who finds the nerve to warn one man can lose it again and run the warning to another, and Cole Reede had been afraid of his father his whole life.

Eli learned it from Pruett's face before Pruett said a word. The man came down the street the next morning with two hands behind him and a different set to his shoulders, the easy menace gone hard.

"You've been digging, Crane." Pruett did not get down. "Up at the orchard. With the drunk. Opening graves. Mr. Reede knows. Mr. Reede's son was good enough to mention you'd taken an unhealthy interest." His mouth twisted on *son*, contempt in it, even for the man who'd done him a service. "Mr. Reede's patience is a generous thing but it has an end, and you've found it. The offer's withdrawn. You understand me. There's no deputy's job for you now. There's only the other thing."

So Cole had told. The boy had warned Eli to stop his father and then, terrified of what stopping might cost, had told his father that someone meant to try. Both true. Both cowardice and conscience in the one weak man, and Eli found he could not even hate him for it, having spent a life among men who were both at once, having been both himself.

"Tell Reede I heard him," Eli said.

"Tell him yourself. He'll want to look at you, before." Pruett wheeled his horse. "Dark of the moon's tonight, Crane. You might think on getting clear, but you won't make it. The basin's wide and Mr. Reede's hands ride it all. There's nowhere out there a man with no one waiting can get to before dark." He smiled, and there was something almost kind in it, a professional's regard for another professional about to be on the wrong end. "You picked the wrong town to have a conscience in. They'll write you in the widow's book with a little dash, and the grass'll be greener for it, and that's the whole of your story." He rode off, and the two hands stayed at the end of the street, watching the way-station, and the siege, though no shot had been fired, had begun.


Chapter 32: The Warning

They took Doc that afternoon, to make the point.

The doctor had gone back to his rooms to fetch the journal and his nerve and a pistol he kept and never used. He did not come back. Toward evening two of Reede's hands carried him to the way-station door and laid him on the porch, not dead, but worked over methodically, the way men do a thing they have been told to do and take no pleasure in — ribs, the face, the hands especially, the long clever doctor's hands broken across the knuckles so they swelled like gloves filled with water.

"Mr. Reede says," one of the hands recited, looking at the boards and not at Naomi, "that the doctor should stick to doctoring. And that the marshal should think on what happens to men who help him." Then they went away, and they did not hurry, because there was no need to hurry, because the dark was coming and the town was theirs.

Naomi got Doc inside and onto the pallet and worked over him with what she had, and Eli held the lamp and the doctor's broken hands lay on the blanket like things that no longer belonged to him.

"Should've drunk more," Doc whispered through his split mouth, trying to grin and failing. "Sober's no protection. Learned that today." His eyes found Eli. "The journal. They took the journal."

"I read enough of it."

"Then you know the price." Doc's voice was going. "*El primero.* You have to give it Reede. Give it the first one. Nothing else closes it." His broken hand tried to find Eli's arm and could not grip. "He won't go down easy, Crane. Sixty years he's kept himself out of that hole. He'll feed this whole town into it before he climbs in himself." His eyes closed. "Get him to the well. Whatever it costs. Get Absalom Reede to the bottom of his own well, and the rest of us can finally rest." He drifted under, breathing shallow, and Naomi sat back on her heels with the dead man's husband's debt and the dying doctor and the besieged house all around her, and looked at Eli, and did not ask what they were going to do, because she could see he had stopped asking himself, and had started simply meaning to do it.


Chapter 33: The Marshal Returns

He took the star out of the trunk while Naomi slept beside the doctor in the last hour before dark.

It was a foolish thing and he knew it. A tin star meant nothing out here; there was no court, no marshal's office, no territory close enough to matter; Pruett would laugh at it, Reede would laugh at it. The thing in the well would not even know what it was.

He pinned it on anyway, on the inside of his coat where no one would see it, over his heart, where the worn place on the back of it sat against his shirt.

He did it for himself, was the truth of it, and he was old enough now to admit a thing he did for himself. He had thrown the star in a river because he had used it to do a great wrong with a clean conscience, and he had spent seven years believing the lesson of that was that a man should never again be sure of anything. But kneeling over the well two nights back, wanting to lay his burden down, he had understood that the lesson was something else. The lesson was not that a man should stop being sure. It was that a man should be sure of the right thing, and pay for it himself, and not make others pay.

He was sure of this. He was as sure of putting the orchard down as he had once been wrongly sure of the rope around Asa Polk's neck — but this time he had dug, this time he had counted, this time he had knelt at the well and heard it himself and turned the offer down. This time the certainty was clean, and earned, and his own.

He buttoned the coat over the star. Outside the last light was going out of the basin, copper to rust to the deep blue that comes before the dark of the moon, the night with no moon at all. The dogs began, all at once, every dog in Quartz Hollow, not the low sound now but a high broken howling, the sound of animals at the end of their endurance, and out on the ridge, against the dying sky, the pale trees stood up sharp and waiting, and one by one, here and there among them, the figures came up to the fence to look down at the town and choose.


Chapter 34: No Law Out Here

Absalom Reede came to the town himself, which he had not done in years, the town said later. He came in a black buggy with Pruett riding beside it and four hands behind, into the street before full dark, and he had his courtly look on, the gentle old gentleman, and he stopped the buggy before the way-station and waited for Eli to come out, which Eli did, because hiding had stopped serving him long ago.

"Marshal." Reede smiled. He had seen the star, somehow, or guessed it. "You've put it back on. I find that touching. A man returning to his calling at the end." He folded his thin spotted hands. "I'll save us both the trouble of pretense. You've learned more than is good for you, and you mean to do something about it, which is admirable and useless. I've come to tell you it's useless, because I'm fond of you, and because I'd rather you walked up that ridge tonight understanding than confused."

"You're promised to it," Eli said. "Sixty years ago. You dug the well open and you made the offer and the offer was yourself, in the end. And you've been buying it off ever since with other men."

Something flickered far back in Reede's ageless eyes — not fear, Eli thought, but the memory of fear, an old animal thing the old man had long since mastered. "Yes," Reede said simply. "That's the whole of it. I made a poor bargain young and I've spent a long life making it better. Is that a sin, Marshal? You wore a star. You know the law. Show me the statute against a man not wanting to die." He spread his hands at the dark basin around them. "And show me the court that'll hear it. There's no law out here, Crane. The nearest judge is two hundred miles of dust away and he wouldn't believe a word of it. Out here there's only what a man can hold, and I have held this basin for sixty years, and I'll hold it sixty more." He nodded toward the ridge, where the figures stood at the fence. "It's chosen, by the way. Tonight's name. It chose while we were talking. You needn't wonder who." And he picked up the reins, and the buggy turned, and rolled back toward the green, and the hands stayed, and the dark came down complete.


Chapter 35: The Drought Breaks Wrong

The rain came at full dark, and it was the wrong rain.

It came only over the orchard. Eli saw it from the way-station window — a low black cloud that formed up out of a clear moonless sky directly above the ridge, only above the ridge, a cloud the size of the orchard and no bigger, and it opened and rained down onto the pale trees in a black curtain while not a drop fell on the dying town a quarter mile off or on the dust beyond.

The trees drank it. He could see them drink it, the rain falling into the pale limbs and not dripping off, not running down, the wood soaking it in, the whole orchard darkening as it drank, going from bone-white to a wet gray and then back to white as it swallowed the water down to the roots and the roots carried it down to the well and the thing that thirsted.

The dogs went mad. The high howling broke into screaming, and then, all at once, stopped, every dog in the town falling silent in the same breath, and the silence after was the worst sound Eli ever heard.

"It's started," Naomi said at his shoulder. Doc breathed shallow on the pallet behind them. "When the black rain comes it's started. It rains on the orchard the night of the dark and then —" She stopped. "Then they come down for the name."

"Then we go up," Eli said. "Now. While they're coming down, we go up the other side, to the well, the way I went to Reede's. The hands are watching the front of the house. The orchard's between us and the ranch but the well's behind the house, on the cottonwood ground." He took up the rifle and the coal oil and the long matches and the things he had made ready. "We don't burn the orchard. We get to the well. We end the bargain the only way it ends." He looked at her. "You don't have to come. This is my debt."

"It's mine too," Naomi said, and took up Tom's pistol, and checked it the way a woman checks a thing she has had to use, and they went out the back into the dark and the wrong silence, toward the ridge and the black-watered trees and the long patient throat in the ground behind the white house.


Chapter 36: Tom's Voice

They came up the ridge from the north, the way Eli knew, away from the front where the figures were coming down. The black rain had stopped. The orchard dripped in the dark, the pale trunks faintly luminous, and the air was thick with the mineral iron-smell, much stronger now, the well breathing it out across the whole ridge.

They had to cross a corner of the orchard to reach the cottonwood ground. There was no way around it. Eli went first, rifle up, Naomi behind with the coal oil, and they went down a row between the dripping trees as quiet as they could, which in that listening silence was not quiet at all.

Halfway down the row, by Tom Hale's tall tree, the earth was open.

Not cracked now. Open. The grave under the tall tree had broken from below, the dirt heaved up and out, a black mouth in the ground, and the proper stone NAOMI had paid for lay tipped on its side in the mound of thrown earth. Naomi made a small sound and stopped.

From the open grave, low, came a voice.

"Naomi." It was thick, and slow, and wrong, a voice coming up through earth and two years, but it was Tom Hale's voice, and Eli felt the floor of the world tilt under him. "Don't. Don't come down. Don't let it —" The voice labored, each word dragged up out of the dark against a great weight. "It wants you to come down for me. That's the name. That's tonight's name. You. It chose you because I'm down here. It knew you'd come." A sound that might have been weeping, or might have been only the earth settling. "Go back. Let me stay down. I'm trying to stay down, Naomi. Help me stay down. Don't make me come up after you."

Naomi stood over the open grave of her husband with the coal oil in her hands and her face wet in the dark, and Eli put his hand on her arm, gently, the way you touch someone on the edge of a high place.

"He's right," Eli said low. "We don't dig. We don't answer it. We go to the well. The only way to let Tom stay down is to close the well. Come on. Come on, Naomi. For him."

She let him draw her back, step by step, away from the voice, and the voice did not follow, and that, more than anything, told Eli that what was left of Tom Hale was on their side, was holding itself down with the last of whatever it had, the way the sexton held the others down with his hands.


Chapter 37: The Price of the Bargain

They came out of the orchard onto the cottonwood ground and Absalom Reede was waiting at the well.

He stood at the lip of the dry shaft with a lantern, alone, no hands, no Pruett, an old man in a fine coat at the mouth of a hole in the ground, and he did not seem surprised to see them. He seemed, if anything, relieved, the way a man is relieved when a long-dreaded thing finally arrives and can stop being dreaded.

"I knew you'd come up the back," he said. "You're the only one who ever bothered to learn the back way. The others always came at the front, with torches, like children." He held the lantern higher to see their faces. "You've brought the widow. Good. That saves my men a job, frankly. It was to be her tonight. The thing chose her, you understand, not me. I don't choose. I only deliver. It chose her because her husband's down there reaching, and a thing already half in the ground is easiest to pull all the way down."

"You made the bargain," Eli said. "Sixty years back. You're the first one promised. *El primero.*"

Reede was quiet a moment. Then he nodded, and in the lantern light he looked, for the first time, genuinely old, genuinely tired, a man who had carried something even longer than Eli had carried his.

"I was thirty years old and I had two thousand head dying on dust," he said softly. "I dug this well open because an old vaquero told me a story, and I made the offer because I didn't believe it, and then it answered, and I believed. It said it would take me. It said the land would be green as long as I lived and that when I died it would take me down and that would be the price." His mouth thinned. "I found a way to not die. That's all I've ever done, Marshal. For sixty years. A name a season, then a name a month. I have kept myself out of this hole one body at a time, and I am not sorry, and I would do it again, and tonight it is the widow's turn or yours, and not mine, never mine, because I am the one thing it has wanted all along and the one thing it will never get."


Chapter 38: Three Days to Dark

(See note: the dark has come. The bargain falls due tonight.)

"It's not the widow's turn," Eli said. "And it's not mine. It's yours, Reede. It always was. You've just been late paying, sixty years late, the way Tom was late, the way I came west late to pay Tom. We're all of us late on this ridge. But debts come due."

Reede laughed, and the laugh had a crack in it now. "And you'll make me pay it? You and a widow and a broken doctor dying in her house? I have six men within a shout. I have only to raise my voice." But he did not raise it. That was the thing Eli marked, cold and certain, the counting habit running even now. The old man stood alone at his well and did not call his men, and a man who will not call for help when he could is a man who has, somewhere far down, decided.

"Then call them," Eli said.

Reede looked at him. The lantern hissed. Behind them, in the orchard, the dripping had stopped, and the silence had a new quality, a waiting attention, as though every reaching hand in the ridge had gone still to listen.

"You want me to," Reede said slowly, wonderingly. "You're trying to make me call them so the night runs out and the name goes unpaid and — what. You think if it's unpaid it comes for me?" He shook his head. "It doesn't work that way. If the name goes unpaid the land dies and the basin dies and a hundred souls die of the dry and it still doesn't come for me, it just gets thirstier, it just wants more, and I find more, that's how it's always worked." But there was a thread of doubt in it now, a thread Eli had heard in a thousand interrogations, the sound of a man saying a thing aloud to convince himself and hearing it not hold.

"You've never tested it," Eli said. "Sixty years and you've never once let a dark of the moon pass unpaid, because you're afraid. You don't know what happens. Neither do I. But I know what the journal says, Reede. I know what closes it for good."


Chapter 39: The Chosen Name

Pruett came around the corner of the white house then, fast, with two hands behind him, drawn by the lantern or by some sense that the night had gone off its rails. He took in the scene — the old man at the well, the marshal and the widow before him, the rifle — and his hand went to his coat.

"Boss," Pruett said. "Step back from him. I'll settle this."

"No," Reede said.

Pruett stopped. In sixty years, Eli guessed, no one had heard Absalom Reede say that word in that tone, a small lost word. The hands looked at each other.

"Boss, the name's not paid. The dark's running. If it ain't paid by midnight —"

"I know when it's due, Pruett, I've paid it five hundred times." Reede's voice came back to him, the old command in it, but underneath the command something had come loose. He looked at the well, then at the orchard, then at Eli, and the calculation ran behind his ageless eyes, the great cold arithmetic of his whole long life — what it cost to go on, what it cost to stop, who paid, who always paid, which was never him.

"There's a thing I've wondered," Reede said quietly, almost to himself. "All these years. I've wondered what it would say. If I ever stood here on the dark of the moon and didn't bring it a name. If I just — stood here. I've wondered what it would say to me." His hand drifted to the lip of the well, the way a man's hand drifts to an old wound. "Sixty years and I never once had the nerve to find out."

"Boss." Pruett's pistol was half out. "Let me take the widow up to it and be done. It's an easy fix. It's always been an easy fix."

And Eli saw the moment hang there, the whole of it balanced on an old man's loose nerve and a hired gun's drawn pistol, and he did the only thing a man can do at the top of a fall, which is to choose the direction, and he raised his rifle not at Pruett but at the lantern in Reede's hand, and he said, "Tell him to put the gun up, Reede, or you and I find out together what it says."


Chapter 40: The Rescue

It went wrong fast, the way these things do, all at once and in the dark.

Pruett fired. He fired at Eli and missed in the bad light, the round going past into the cottonwoods, and Naomi fired Tom's pistol and did not miss, and Pruett sat down hard against the well-stones with both hands at his middle, surprised, the way men always are. The two hands behind him broke — they did not fire, they ran, back around the white house and away into the dark, because they were cattle hands and not killers and the night had become something no wage covered.

Eli had the rifle on Reede before the echo died. The old man had not moved. He stood with the lantern and watched Pruett slide down the stones and did not go to him.

"You see," Reede said, oddly gentle, "how it spends us. Even now. Even tonight. It's fed on us our whole lives, Marshal, all of us, not just the ones in the ground." He looked at his dying man without pity and without coldness, only with a vast tiredness. "I'm so tired. Do you understand that? Sixty years tired. You can't imagine it. You've carried your one rope seven years and it's bent you near double. Imagine five hundred ropes. Imagine five hundred names you chose, or let be chosen, and pretended you didn't."

"Then put it down," Eli said. "There's one way to put it down."

Pruett, against the stones, gave a last wet breath and was still. The lantern guttered. Far off, beyond the white house, came the sound of horses — the hands, riding, but not away now; riding for help, for more guns, for the rest of Reede's men out in the dark basin.

"They'll be back," Reede said. "With everyone I've got. You won't hold the ground past midnight." He set the lantern down on the well-stones, carefully, the way he set everything down. "You've maybe an hour, Marshal. An hour to do whatever it is you came up here to do. I'd use it. I find I'm curious, after sixty years, to see whether you've the nerve to do to me what I never had the nerve to let be done." And he sat down on the lip of his own well, an old tired man, and folded his hands, and waited to see what Eli Crane was made of.


Chapter 41: Cornered at the Way-Station

But it was not at the well that they made their stand first. Naomi would not leave Doc.

"He's dying in my house with no one by him," she said, "and the hands have gone for guns and they'll come through the town to get to us, and I won't have him die alone while we settle accounts with that." So they went back down — Eli would not let her go alone, and would not leave Reede unwatched either, so he did a thing that cost him, he trusted the old man's tiredness, and left him sitting at his well with the promise that he would be back before midnight, and the strange thing was that Reede nodded as though a promise between them meant something.

They got back to the way-station as the first of Reede's riders came into the head of the street, four of them, with torches and rifles, and Eli put Naomi and the dying doctor in the back room behind the stove, which was the thickest wall in the place, and he put himself at the window with the rifle, and the siege that had been only a watching became a real one.

It was an ugly hour. The riders did not rush the house; they did not need to; they sat their horses at the end of the street and fired into the way-station to keep its occupants down while they waited for midnight, when the name would go unpaid and, they believed, the orchard would do their work for them. Bullets came through the thin walls. Eli fired back to keep them honest and to make them keep their distance and counted his rounds, which were few.

Behind him in the dark of the back room Doc Sennett, in the last clarity that sometimes comes, caught Naomi's hand with his ruined one.

"The first one," Doc whispered. "It has to be the first one. Reede. Down the well. Nothing else closes it. You tell Crane. You make him —" A breath. "You make him put Absalom Reede in that hole before the dark ends, or it's all for —" And his hand opened, and the clarity went out of him, and Doc Sennett, who had signed five hundred lies and dug up the truth at the last, was gone, and Naomi closed his eyes in the dark with the bullets coming through the walls above her.


Chapter 42: Doc's Last Counsel

Eli heard her say it from the front room, in the lulls between the firing — Doc's last words, passed on flat and steady, a widow who had run out of things left to lose.

"He said the first one. Down the well. Before the dark ends. Or it's all for nothing." She came forward to the window, low, and took up Tom's pistol again. "He's gone, Eli. Doc's gone."

Eli fired twice and a rider's horse screamed and the others pulled back another twenty yards, buying a minute. He looked at the dead doctor's wife in the muzzle-flash dark and made the count, the last and hardest count of his life.

"There's four men in the street and more coming," he said. "There's maybe forty minutes to midnight. The well's a quarter mile off, up past the orchard, with Reede sitting on it. If we run for it they ride us down in the open. If we stay here they wait us out and the name goes unpaid and you heard Reede — unpaid doesn't free anybody, it just makes it hungrier." He worked the rifle's lever. "But Doc's right. The journal's right. It closes one way and one way only and that's Reede in the hole."

"Then we have to get to the well."

"We have to get Reede to the well," Eli said. "And Reede's already at the well. That's the one thing in our favor in this whole black night. He's sitting on it, waiting, too tired to run and too curious to die quiet." He looked out at the riders, at the torches, at the town between them and the ridge. "They'll chase us. We can't stop them. But we don't have to beat them to the well. We just have to reach it before midnight, and put the first one down, and let the rest of them ride into whatever happens after." He took her shoulder. "It means leaving Doc. It means going out that back window into the open. It means likely not coming back. Say it now if you won't."

"I'll come," Naomi said. "I've been coming for two years. Let's finish it."


Chapter 43: The First Bargainer

They went out the back window into the dark while the riders watched the front, and they ran low across the open ground behind the way-station, toward the black bulk of the ridge, and the orchard, and the well beyond.

Behind them a shout went up — they had been seen — and then the drum of hooves, the riders coming around the house and onto their trail, torches streaming. Eli and Naomi ran in the dark over the broken ground, and the riders were faster, of course they were faster, mounted men in open country, and there was no possible way two people on foot could reach the well a quarter mile off ahead of them, no way the count came out right.

The orchard saved them.

The riders' horses would not enter it. Eli and Naomi went into the trees because it was the straight line to the well and because they had no choice, plunging in among the pale dripping trunks in the dark, and the riders came up to the fence behind them and their horses balked, every one, reared and screamed and would not cross the line into the rows, the way the mare had balked on the road that first night, the way every animal in this country knew better than every man. The riders fought their horses and cursed and a few got down to come on foot, but they came slow, afraid, into the trees their whole lives had taught them to dread on this of all nights, and the slowness was enough.

Because in the orchard, in the dark, the dead were rising.

Eli felt the ground change underfoot, soft, heaving. Around them in the rows the graves were opening, the earth pushing up and out, and from the broken ground the figures were rising — slow, terrible, patient, the layered dead of sixty years coming up at last on the dark of the moon with the name unpaid and the thing in the well stretching the bounds of its long bargain. They came up all around, pale in the dark, reaching, and behind, the riders saw them and broke entirely, screaming, scrambling back over the fence to their maddened horses, gone.

And Eli understood, running through the rising dead toward the well, that the unpaid name had done exactly what Reede feared and would not test: it had let them all up. The whole orchard. All at once.


Chapter 44: Into the Orchard

They were not attacked. That was the thing Eli would carry afterward, the thing he could never make anyone understand. The dead rose all around them in the dark, and they reached, and they came on slow, and not one of them did them harm.

They were not hunting. They were going. Every risen figure in that orchard, the moment it pulled itself free of the earth, turned the same way — toward the well, toward the thing that had drunk them down, drawn back to it the way iron is drawn to a stone. They shambled and dragged and reached, and the reaching was not for Eli and Naomi but past them, toward the well behind the white house, a slow pale tide of the basin's sixty years of dead all moving in one direction through the dark.

Eli and Naomi moved with them. There was nothing else to do; the dead filled the rows; to fight the current was to be crushed in it, so they went with it, among the risen, carried in the slow pale flood toward the cottonwood ground.

It was the worst walk of Eli's life and he had made some bad ones. The smell of iron and cold stone. The dark shapes pressing close, the brush of cold dead cloth, the faces he would not look at and could not entirely not see, men and women, drifters and debtors, the friendless dead Reede had fed down one name at a time, all of them roused and reaching and patient and not cruel, only hungry, only homesick for the dark thing that had eaten them, going back to it the way the drawn always go back.

Naomi's hand found his in the press and held it, hard, and they went together, two living people in a river of the dead, and ahead, beyond the last trees, the lantern light of the well showed a small steady glow, and the old man sitting beside it, waiting, watching his sixty years of dead come home.


Chapter 45: The Risen Rows

At the edge of the orchard the dead slowed, and pooled, and would not cross onto the cottonwood ground.

Eli understood it after a moment: the trees. The roots. The dead could rise and walk the orchard, but the orchard was the lid, the padre's poor lid, and they could come to its edge and reach across it toward the well but they could not pass beyond the last trees, could not reach the well itself. They massed at the orchard's edge, hundreds of them, pale in the dark, a wall of the risen all straining toward the lantern and the old man and the hole in the ground, reaching, and held back by the last thin power of the sealing trees.

Eli and Naomi came through them to the edge and out, onto the open cottonwood ground, where the dead could not follow, and behind them the wall of risen strained and reached and made a sound at last, all together, the low hollow note Eli had first heard on his first night in the Hollow, hundreds of throats of it now, the sound of the orchard itself.

Absalom Reede stood up from the well.

He looked at the wall of his dead at the orchard's edge and his ageless face had gone, finally, white, and old, and afraid, the mastered animal fear breaking loose in him after sixty years.

"It let them up," he whispered. "You let them up. The name went unpaid and it let them all —" He took a step back, toward the well, away from the reaching wall of the men and women he had fed it. "It's never done that. In sixty years it's never —"

"It's calling its debts," Eli said, coming across the open ground with the rifle low. "All of them. The way debts come due when a man stops paying. There's only one debt that'll send them back down, Reede. The first one. You know it. Doc knew it. The journal knows it. The thing in the hole has known it for sixty years and it's tired of waiting, same as you."


Chapter 46: Tom Among Them

Naomi cried out, once, and Eli turned.

At the edge of the orchard, in the wall of the reaching dead, one figure had come forward of the rest, to the very last tree, the tall one, and stood there at the boundary it could not cross. Even in the dark, even after two years, Naomi knew her husband. She started toward him and Eli caught her.

"Naomi. No."

"It's Tom." Her voice broke on it. "Eli, it's Tom, he's —"

"I know whose it is." Eli held her, hard, both arms, while she fought him. "Look at him. Look. He's not reaching for you."

And it was true. Tom Hale's risen form stood at the edge of the orchard among all the straining others, and where they reached toward the well, hundreds of pale arms stretched across the boundary toward the hole that had drunk them, Tom did not reach toward the well at all. He had turned. He faced the open ground, faced Naomi and Eli, and his ruined face in the dark held something none of the others held — not hunger, but knowing. He had held himself down two years longer than any of them. He held himself now, at the boundary, against the pull, the last man in the orchard not straining toward the well, and he lifted one slow dead hand, not reaching, only raised, palm out, the way a man lifts his hand in farewell, or in blessing, or in warning that says go on, go on, finish it, don't look back at me.

"He's telling us to go," Eli said into Naomi's hair, holding her, both of them shaking. "He held on so he could tell us to go. Don't waste it, Naomi. Don't waste what it cost him to stay himself this long. Look once. Then look away, and let me finish it, so he can lie down."

She looked once. She lifted her own hand to her dead husband across the dark and the boundary, and Tom Hale's raised hand turned, slowly, palm down, the sexton's gesture, the press-it-down gesture, *put me in the ground, put it all in the ground,* and then she turned away from him, and did not look again, and that was the bravest thing Eli ever saw anyone do.


Chapter 47: The Well's Bargain

Eli walked Absalom Reede back to the lip of the well at the point of the rifle, and the thing in the well spoke to them both.

It came up out of the shaft as it had before, below words, in the body, the low note shaping itself into a kind of meaning that each man heard in his own way. To Reede it must have said something terrible and old, sixty years old, because the cattle baron flinched at the well-mouth like a man struck. To Eli it said what it had said the first night he knelt here.

*You have a thing to put down. I will take it. Give me what you carry and you will rest. Give me the old man and I will give you back your own dead, clean, the one you hanged, the one you carry — I will take Asa Polk off your back and you will be light and the land will be green and you and the widow will live easy on it all your years.*

And it was, again, sweet. That was the thing he never told anyone. Standing over the hole with the rifle on a tired old murderer, the offer came up out of the dark dressed as mercy, as rest, as the laying down of the one weight he had carried longest, and a worse man, or a more tired one, might have taken it — might have fed Reede to the well not to close it but to keep it, to become the next Absalom Reede, the next deliverer of names, buying ease one body at a time.

"That's how he started," Eli said aloud, to Naomi, to himself, to the thing. "It offered him rest and a green valley and he took it and spent sixty years paying. It's offering me the same. The same exact bargain." He looked at Reede, who had heard it too, who was watching him with a terrible hope, the hope that Eli would take the offer and the two of them would share the guilt and the well would go on. "That's why it let them all up. Not to punish you. To tempt me. To make a new keeper before the old one's used up."

"Take it," Reede whispered. "God help me, take it, you don't know what it's like down there, take it and let me —"

"No," Eli said, the same word he'd said the first night, clean and earned and his own. "I'll carry my own. And you'll carry yours. All the way down."


Chapter 48: Reede's End

He did not shoot him. He had decided that walking back through the orchard, in the river of the dead — that he would not put Absalom Reede down with a bullet, because a bullet was the law's old clean lie, the thing he had done to Asa Polk, a man dealing death and feeling righteous. He would not feel righteous tonight. He would feel exactly what it was.

So he took the old man by the collar of his fine coat, and Reede did not fight, which Eli had not expected, the old man's body gone slack and tired and almost grateful even as his voice begged, and Eli brought him to the very lip of the dry well, and looked once into the dark that had wanted this one body for sixty years, and felt the thing's terrible patient hope come up to meet them.

"You could have died at thirty with two thousand head," Eli said. "It would have been a small dying. Look what you bought instead."

"I know," Reede said. And then, strangely, at the very end, the begging went out of him, and the courtly old gentleman came back, the one who had poured wine and asked no questions about a man's history, and he looked at Eli almost with relief, the way Doc had looked, the way every soul on this ridge had looked who finally got to set the weight down. "Sixty years I couldn't do it myself. I dug this open and I never had the nerve to climb back in. Funny. To need a stranger's hands." His ageless eyes found Naomi at the edge of the dark. "I'm sorry about your husband, missus. That's the truth. I was sorry every time. It never stopped me, but I was sorry." Then he looked back down into the well. "All right, Marshal. Pay my debt for me. I'm too tired to pay it myself."

Eli put him into the well.

It was not a struggle and it was not a murder and it was not justice, and Eli never afterward found the word for what it was. The old man went down into the dark almost willingly at the end, and the thing took him, and there was no scream, only the low hollow note rising once to something almost like a sigh, almost like the sound a man makes lowering himself into hot water after a long hard ride — relief, fullness, an old thirst meeting at last the one thing it had truly wanted — and then silence, complete, the first true silence the basin had known in sixty years.


Chapter 49: The Closing

The orchard died while they watched.

At the well's last sigh, the low note stopped, and the cottonwood ground beneath them stilled, and at the edge of the orchard the wall of the risen dead, hundreds of them, stopped reaching all at once. For a moment they stood, pale and uncertain in the dark, the pull that had drawn them gone slack. And then, slowly, all together, they turned away from the well, and went back into the rows, and lay down.

Eli watched them go. He watched Tom Hale, at the tall tree, lower his raised hand, and turn, and go back to the open grave under his stone, and lie down in it, and be still. He watched the whole orchard lie down, sixty years of the basin's dead returning to their broken ground and settling into it, the heaved earth sinking, the open graves closing over them as gently as water closing over a stone, the soil drawing itself smooth, the reaching ended.

And the trees died.

It went through the orchard like a wind, though there was no wind. The pale trunks that had drunk the black rain shuddered, and cracked, the sound of it like distant gunfire all across the ridge, and the white bark split and grayed and the limbs that had reached up against the sky for sixty years sagged, and broke, and the whole grove sank in on itself, going from pale bone-white to the honest dead-gray of ordinary deadwood, drained, finished, the lid no longer needed because there was nothing left below to hold down.

Behind the white house, the green grass began to brown. They could not see it in the dark but Eli knew it was happening, felt the living wrongness going out of the ground under his feet, the cold mineral iron-smell thinning, blowing away, replaced by nothing but dust and the clean dry smell of the basin at night.

The sexton was there. He had come up from the town in the dark, the old mute man, and he stood at the orchard's edge among the settling graves with his spade, and he looked at the dying trees and the lying-down dead and the still well, and he did a thing Eli had not thought him capable of: he wept, silently, his ruined face working, an old man who had held the ground down with his own hands for years finally allowed to stop.


Chapter 50: The Long Ride Out

The rain came at dawn, and it was the right rain.

It came over the whole basin, not just the ridge — a gray soft true rain out of a gray soft true sky, the first in five years, falling on the dust and the dead grass and the empty homesteads and the leaning water tower and the dead gray ruin of the orchard alike, falling on the living and the dead without preference, the honest rain that does not choose. Eli stood in it in the way-station yard and let it run down his face and did not move for a long time.

They buried Doc Sennett that morning, properly, in new ground east of the town, away from the orchard, in earth that would hold him. And they dug Tom Hale up out of the dead orchard one last time — Eli would not leave him there, in that cursed ground, whatever it was now — and carried him down and laid him beside the doctor in the clean new ground, and this time Pastor Wells came, gray and shaking and weeping, and read the words over both graves, the real words, *earth to earth, the resurrection and the life,* and this time, Eli thought, watching the settled earth in the falling rain, this time the words were not a lie. This time the dead would rest.

Cole Reede inherited the ranch and the dying grass and none of the green, and seemed, when Eli saw him last, lighter for it, a coward at last relieved of the thing he'd been too afraid to refuse and too afraid to keep. The basin would be hard country again, honest dust and the occasional honest rain, and some of the town would stay and some would go, and that was no longer Eli's to count.

He took the star off the inside of his coat and gave it back to Naomi, to put in the trunk with Tom's things.

"You could keep it on," she said. "Could be a use for a marshal, even out here."

"There's no law out here," Eli said. "Reede had that part right." He looked at the clean rain and the gray ruin of the orchard up on its ridge. "But there's debts. A man can pay those without a star." He saddled the mare, who stood easy now, unafraid, the wrongness gone out of the ground and out of her. "I paid mine. Both of them. Tom's and Asa Polk's. Took me long enough."

Naomi stood in the rain by the way-station that had her dead husband's name half-fallen off the sign. "Where will you go."

"On," Eli said. He swung up. "Just on, for a while. Till I find a place that doesn't need anything from me." He looked down at her. "You'll keep the book?"

"There's nothing to keep now. No more dashes." She almost smiled. "Just rain, and old age, and folks dying when it's their time, and staying down." She put her hand on the mare's neck. "Come back through, if your riding brings you. There'll be coffee. It'll likely still be bad."

"I'll count on it," Eli said, and meant it, and rode out of Quartz Hollow into the falling rain with the dead at rest behind him and the long basin going green the slow honest way ahead, and he did not look back at the ridge, because there was nothing on it anymore that anyone needed to watch.
