Chapter 1: Before the Light

The pearl came free at twelve meters, where the pressure sat against Hina's ears like two flat stones. She felt the oyster's resistance — the muscle tension, the brief fight — and then it gave. She tucked it against her chest and let the current carry her up.

She broke surface in the grey hour before sunrise. The lagoon was flat. No wind. The atoll's palms were still dark against a sky the colour of ash. She floated on her back and held the oyster up. Water ran off her forearms in thin lines.

She had been diving this bed since she was nineteen. Fifteen years. She knew every head of coral, every drift of sand at the bottom. She knew where the current bent around the northern edge of the reef and pooled, and she knew that pooling made the oysters fat and slow. Her mother had shown her. Her mother was three years dead.

Hina swam the pirogue. It sat low in the water with the night's catch — six oysters in a woven basket, none of them guaranteed. The black pearl beds were not reliable. You dove fifty times and came up with nacre, or with nothing, or with a baroque shape that no trader would buy. Once in a season you brought up something like the one she now felt against her thumb: round, dark, with that faint iridescent bloom that made men spend money they did not have.

She did not open the shell yet. She set it in the basket and picked up the paddle.

The pass was narrow. She timed her exit between the swells, let the current help her through, and came around the outer reef just as the sun cut the horizon. Orange light moved across the water in one long sweep. She felt it on her wet shoulders.

The village was waking. Smoke from the cookfires. A dog somewhere. The smell of breadfruit on coals. She pulled the pirogue above the tide line and stood with the basket.

Her uncle Manea was already on the beach. He was fifty-eight and built like a breadfruit tree — wide through the chest, still strong, though his hair had gone entirely white. He was watching something out in the passage.

She looked. A schooner. Two masts. It had come in overnight, running before whatever wind there was south of the atoll. It sat now at anchor in the deep water of the pass, its hull dark, its rigging slack.

"What flag?" she said.

Manea didn't answer immediately. He squinted. "Tricolour."

She had known it would be. There had been talk for two seasons about a survey the colonial administration wanted to run through the Tuamotus. Charting the atolls. Taking stock. She had heard it from the copra trader who came through in April, and before him from a cousin in Takaroa who said a survey party had come through there in late 1872 and asked careful questions about the pearl beds.

"How many men?" she said.

"I count four on deck. More below."

She set the basket down and crouched to open the last oyster. The knife went in at the hinge. She pried. Inside, in the curve of the shell, the pearl sat like a small dark eye. She held it to the morning light and turned it once.

Round. Lustrous. Worth more than the village would earn from copra in a month.

She closed her fingers around it.

"They will want an interpreter," Manea said. He did not look at her.

"I know," she said.

Chapter 2: The Schooner

By mid-morning the survey party had come ashore. There were seven of them: a naval lieutenant named Moreau who wore his rank like a second skin, two enlisted men who carried equipment cases, a naturalist with a net bag of specimen jars, a cook, and two others whose function Hina could not immediately place — one of them young, with ink-stained fingers, who carried a rolled leather case she recognized as a map case.

They set up a camp at the north end of the beach, near the old copra shed that had stood empty since the last owner left for Tahiti. Moreau directed the enlisted men with short sentences. The young man with the map case walked the beach alone for a quarter hour before finding a spot that seemed to suit him. He set down his case and stood looking at the lagoon.

Hina watched from the shade of the pandanus grove. Teura came and stood beside her.

"The young one," Teura said. "He keeps looking at the water."

"They all look at the water," Hina said. "That's what they're here for."

Teura was twenty-six. She had their father's colouring — lighter than Hina, with a tendency to burn in the dry season. She had also inherited their father's ease with strangers, which Hina had not. Their father, Henri Aroha, had been a copra trader out of Marseille who stayed because their mother made staying easier than leaving. He had died of fever when Hina was eleven and Teura was three, and Hina sometimes thought that was why they were so different. She had known him. Teura had not.

"Do you know what survey means?" Hina asked.

"Mapping," Teura said. "Father Breton explained it. They draw lines and numbers."

"They draw lines and numbers and then the lines and numbers belong to someone else."

Teura made the small sound she made when she didn't want to argue. They stood in the shade and watched the camp take shape.

Moreau came ashore again in the early afternoon and sent one of the enlisted men to find the village chief. Manea went to meet him on the beach. Hina went with him, two steps back, as interpreter.

Moreau's French was official and quick. He said the survey was a government mandate. He said the party would stay three weeks, possibly four. He said the village would not be disturbed. He said this last thing with the certainty of a man who had never considered that his presence was itself a disturbance.

Manea listened. He waited for Hina's translation. Then he said, in Paumotu, that the village welcomed the surveyors and that he hoped the water would be calm.

Hina translated this faithfully.

Moreau nodded. He asked if there was anyone in the village with knowledge of the reef formation who might assist the surveyor.

Hina looked at her uncle. Manea looked at her.

She said, in French: "I can help."

Moreau's gaze moved to her. He took in her height, her build, the saltwater still dried in her hair from the morning's dive. He looked at her with the particular expression European men used when they were deciding something and did not want to show it.

"You speak French," he said.

"My father was French," she said.

Chapter 3: The Council

That night Manea called the elders to his house. They sat on mats in the lamplight — six men and two women, the oldest a woman named Vahine who had been diving the lagoon since before any of them were born.

Hina sat to one side. She was not an elder, but Manea wanted her there.

The talking went on for two hours. Most of it she had already anticipated. The elders who remembered the 1842 annexation were cautious. Those who had grown up under the colonial administration were more willing to accept the survey as simply a thing that happened.

Vahine said nothing for the first hour. When she spoke, everyone went quiet.

"The beds," she said. "They will want to know the beds."

"Not necessarily," one of the men said. "They said they are mapping the reef. The coral heads and the channels."

"Those things and the beds are not separate," Vahine said.

Manea looked at Hina. "You will be their interpreter."

"Yes."

"Then you must be careful about what you interpret."

She understood what he meant. There was the question of what to say in French, and there was the separate question of what to show them on the water. Those were not the same skill, and she would need both.

"There are three beds," Manea said. He said it slowly, to be sure she heard every word. "The three deep ones, in the channel north of the pass. They have sustained this village for four generations. If those beds appear on a French map, they will belong to France. Do you understand?"

"I understand."

"The shallow beds, along the east rim — those they can see. Everyone can see them. There is no hiding what a man in a boat can look down and find. But the deep channel, and what is in it, that is another thing."

"What if they send their own diver?" she said.

"Then we will have a different problem," Manea said. "But let us not create that problem before it exists."

Vahine nodded once and said nothing more.

On the way home, Teura walked beside Hina in the dark. "You're worried," she said.

"I'm thinking."

"Same thing, with you."

The frogs were loud. A frigate bird crossed the moon.

"They're not enemies," Teura said.

"They don't have to be enemies," Hina said. "They just have to do their job."

Chapter 4: Instruments

Émile Renard set up his plane table on the hard-packed sand above the tide line at first light, before anyone else in the camp was moving. He liked the early hours. The air was still and the light was level, which made triangulation easier. He worked by feel — legs of the tripod spread just so, the board levelled, the alidade laid across the paper.

The atoll's geometry was more complex than it looked from the schooner. The lagoon had multiple passes, and the reef rim was not the even oval the charts from Papeete had suggested. It curved and doubled back. There were sections where the coral rose so close to the surface that a boat's keel would touch at low tide, and other sections where the drop was sudden and the water went from turquoise to a dark, featureless blue.

He had been working an hour when he became aware that someone was watching him.

He turned. A woman stood at the edge of the palm grove, not hiding but not announcing herself either. She was tall for a Polynesian woman, with something in her face that he couldn't immediately place — some angle to the cheekbones or the bridge of the nose that suggested a complexity of ancestry. She held a length of netting that she appeared to have been mending.

He raised a hand in greeting. She didn't respond for a moment. Then she walked forward.

"What does that do?" she said. In French. Accented but clear.

He gestured to the alidade. "It takes angles. I sight along this edge to a point on the shore, and I mark the angle on the paper. Then I sight to another point. Where the lines cross, that's the position."

She looked at the paper. He had the northern reach of the reef sketched in, with the beginning of the lagoon passage. She studied it for a moment. He had the impression she was not seeing it as a drawing but as a fact — comparing it to something she carried in her head.

"The passage is wider than that," she said.

He looked at his sketch, then at the passage. "At low tide?"

"At any tide. The coral head on the eastern side — you've placed it too far north. It sits almost in line with the old standing stone on the beach."

He looked. She was right. He adjusted the sketch.

She watched him do it without comment.

"You're the interpreter," he said.

"Hina Aroha."

"Émile Renard." He hesitated. "I'll be charting the lagoon. The reef formations and the passages. The interior channels."

"I know what you'll be charting," she said. She folded the net over her arm. "My uncle asked me to help. If you need me, I'll be here."

She left without further ceremony. He watched her go and then looked back at his paper, where the corrected coral head sat in its proper position. He had been to three other atolls. None of the locals had noticed his errors before.

Chapter 5: Old Ground

Tupa found Hina at the net rack in the afternoon, where she was hanging the morning's lines to dry. He was a big man — wide in the shoulders, with the permanent squint of someone who had spent decades looking into sun-struck water. His hands were rough in the particular way that came from reef salt and rope.

He stood for a moment without speaking, which was his habit.

"Moreau's man," he said finally. "The one with the table and the drawing sticks."

"Renard," she said.

"You were talking to him this morning."

"I was correcting his chart."

Tupa picked up one of the net floats and turned it in his hands. "Manea asked you to interpret. Not me."

There it was. She had known it would come.

"Manea asked me because I speak French," she said.

"I speak enough French."

She looked at him. His enough French was trading-post French — prices and quantities and the words for common fish. It was not the French for legal mandates and survey methodology.

"Tupa," she said. "If you want to talk to Lieutenant Moreau about the weather or the price of copra, you have enough French. If you want to talk to him about what happens to this village's claim to the lagoon when it appears on an official chart, you need more."

He set the float down. "Your father's blood," he said. "That's what Manea really wanted."

"Yes," she said. "Probably."

He looked at her with something that was not quite contempt. It was older than contempt. It was the expression of a man who had watched the thing he was best at — diving the reef, knowing the water — slowly become secondary to things he had no way of learning. He had been the foremost diver on this atoll for twenty years. Then Hina had come into her ability in her mid-twenties and she was better. Faster, deeper, with a capacity to hold air that the older men talked about the way they talked about weather signs — with respect and a certain unease.

He had never forgiven her for it, because there was nothing to forgive. She had not chosen to be what she was.

"Just don't show them the channel," he said.

"I know that," she said.

He walked away. She went back to the nets.

Chapter 6: Father Breton

Father Breton had been on the atoll for twelve years. He had arrived in 1861 as a young Marianist missionary full of French theological confidence, and the atoll had done what atolls do to confidence over time. The church he had built with the villagers' help was tidy and white, with a tin roof that rattled in storms. He said mass on Sundays. He kept a garden of sweet potatoes and a goat he had named Voltaire, which he found quietly amusing.

He walked into the survey camp on the morning of the second day, carrying a wrapped parcel of sweet potato and dried fish.

Moreau received him with the courtesy due a representative of the church. Delacroix was not yet arrived, so Moreau was the unambiguous authority, and he was a man who understood the social utility of priests in remote postings.

Father Breton moved through the camp, talking to the enlisted men about their families and their boredom and their opinions of the food. He was good at this. He had spent years learning to speak to people who were not sure whether to trust him.

He found Émile at his table, sketching the western reaches of the reef.

"The cartographer," Father Breton said.

Émile stood, surprised. He had not heard the priest approach.

"Sit, sit," Father Breton said, waving him down. He looked at the sketch. He tilted his head. "Good lines. Reasonably accurate, from what I can see."

"You know the reef?" Émile said.

"Twelve years. I've fished it, sailed it, and pulled two men out of it." He set the parcel down on the edge of the table. "I brought you something to eat. The cook Moreau brought is, let me say, optimistic about his abilities."

Émile smiled. He unwrapped the parcel and broke off a piece of the dried fish. It was well-made — smoky, dense, not over-salted.

"Have you met the village yet?" Father Breton said.

"Some of them. The chief's niece is helping with interpretation."

"Hina." Father Breton nodded. "She is the person you most need to listen to. She knows this lagoon better than anyone here — better than I do, and I have twelve years on her."

Émile looked at him. "Better than Tupa? I heard he was the principal diver."

Father Breton considered this. "He was. He is still very good. But Hina — she knows the lagoon the way you know your own hands." He tapped the sketch. "If anything on your chart is wrong, she'll tell you."

"She already has," Émile said.

"Good." Father Breton looked out at the lagoon. The water was that specific colour that happened only in mid-morning, when the sun was high enough to penetrate the surface and the coral showed through in greens and yellows. "Be careful what you chart, young man."

Émile looked at him.

"Not for my sake," Father Breton said. "For theirs."

Chapter 7: First Correction

On the third day, Hina brought Émile to the reef's eastern edge in her pirogue. The morning was clear, with a light wind from the southeast. She had packed breadfruit and a water gourd because she intended to be out until the sun crossed its highest point.

He sat in the bow with his rolled chart and his compass. He was a neat person — she noticed that. He stowed his things precisely. He did not fidget.

"The eastern reef," he said. "The lieutenant wants the full perimeter."

"The perimeter I can give you. The inside depths will take longer."

He nodded. He was learning not to push for things in a single day. She had noticed that too.

They worked the eastern edge for two hours. He would sight along his compass and call out bearings, and she would tell him what each point was called in Paumotu and what it was called in her own private naming. He wrote both names down, which surprised her.

"Why write the Paumotu names?" she said.

"Because that's what the place is called," he said. "The French name I'll put in the margin. For the chart to be accurate, it needs to reflect what's actually here."

She thought about this. It was not the usual colonial logic, which tended to assume the French name would simply replace whatever had been there before.

"The point there," she said, nodding toward a broken coral formation to the south. "We call it Te Ua Nui. The big rain. A storm broke the coral into that shape forty years ago."

"Te Ua Nui," he said, writing it. "And in your margin?"

"Pointe du Corail. That's what Moreau will put on the official chart regardless."

He looked at her. "You've thought about this before."

"I've thought about maps before," she said. "My father had maps. Trader's charts. I was interested in them when I was small. The way a place becomes flat lines and numbers."

"Does it bother you?"

She considered the question honestly. "It depends on who holds the flat lines and numbers afterward."

He was quiet for a moment. Then he wrote something in the margin of his chart, in smaller letters. She could not read it from where she sat.

They moved south along the reef. She kept her paddle strokes even, conserving effort. He sighted and noted and asked occasional questions that showed he was paying attention — not just to the reef but to what she said about it.

At noon they stopped on a small coral islet barely large enough to stand on. He spread the chart on the flat rock and they ate.

"Your French is very good," he said.

"My father spoke it with me. Books, also. Father Breton has books."

"What do you read?"

"Voltaire," she said. "Some Hugo. Technical texts mostly. I found a manual on navigation — meridian sights and coastal pilotage. I liked that better than the novels."

He laughed. It was a real laugh, not the polite kind. "I was the same. I was supposed to study law. I read surveying manuals instead."

"But you became a cartographer."

"My father wanted a lawyer. I wanted to draw maps of places I hadn't been." He looked at the lagoon. "This is the farthest I've been."

"Do you like it?"

He looked at the water for a long time. "The place, yes. What I'm doing here — I'm less certain about."

She noted that he had said it without looking at her, which suggested he meant it.

Chapter 8: The Mandate

Moreau summoned Hina to the camp on the fourth day. He had a document open on his field table, held flat by stones at its corners.

He said: "The survey mandate covers the physical geography of the atoll. The reef, the passages, the lagoon depth. It also covers resources. Timber, copra production, fishery stocks. And mineral resources."

He paused. He used the pause deliberately.

"Pearl beds," he said.

Hina looked at the document. She could read it upside down and she was reading it. It was a standard colonial survey mandate, issued from the Governor's office in Papeete. The language was bureaucratic and careful. It said: complete enumeration of commercially valuable natural resources including but not limited to.

"There are pearl beds in the lagoon," she said. She kept her voice even.

"The chart will need to locate them."

"I know the shallow beds. I can take your man to them."

Moreau looked at her. He was not a bad man, she thought. He was a man who had a job and whose job was shaped by a system he had not designed. "The mandate is not limited to shallow beds, Madame Aroha."

"Then you'll need a diver who can show you the deep ones."

"You are a diver, I understand."

"I am."

"Then —"

"I will need to speak with my uncle before I can commit the village's cooperation to anything beyond the perimeter survey," she said. "We have customs about the reef. Protocols."

Moreau sat back. He was not pleased but he was not an idiot. He knew that forcing cooperation in a matter like this would produce bad maps. "How long?"

"A few days."

He nodded. "A few days."

She left the camp. On the path back to the village she walked quickly and thought faster. The mandate was what she had expected. The question was how to manage what came next.

Chapter 9: The Three Beds

Manea was up before dawn the next morning. Hina found him sitting on the beach with a cup of tea, watching the horizon.

She told him about Moreau's mandate. He listened without interrupting. When she finished, he was quiet for a moment.

"The three beds," he said.

"They'll want all of them."

"They cannot have the three." He said it without emotion, as a statement of basic fact. "The north bed, the middle channel bed, and the deep southern pocket — those three have fed this village in bad years. When the copra price fell. When the fish were poor. When the storm of 1869 took half the garden land. Those beds are a reserve. They are not a commodity."

"I know," she said.

"You know. But can you keep them from knowing?"

She watched a tropicbird cross the lagoon's surface, dipping without landing.

"The shallow beds along the east rim — those I can show them. I'll take Renard there myself. The depths are readable from a boat. Anyone can see there are oysters. Hiding them would only create suspicion." She paused. "The north bed is too close to the east rim. It might be visible from a boat with a glass."

"Then we lose the north bed," Manea said. "That is the sacrifice. We show them what they can find on their own, before they look for it. We control the revelation."

"And the middle and southern beds?"

"Those are yours to protect." He looked at her. "I am trusting you with that. Not because of your French blood. Because of your knowledge of the water. No one else can keep a boat in the wrong place convincingly."

She understood what he meant. She would need to take Émile to exactly the right wrong places — close enough to the deep beds to seem thorough, far enough to keep the deepest pools unmarked.

"And if they send their own diver?" she said.

"There is no one on that schooner who can dive twelve meters in a channel current," Manea said. "I asked the enlisted men. They cannot swim."

She almost smiled. "They are French."

He almost smiled back. "They are French."

Chapter 10: Red Calico

The bolt of fabric was vivid — a deep red with a narrow white stripe, trade cotton from Marseille or Lyon, the kind of material that arrived in the islands in exchange for copra and pearl shell. Hina saw it from fifty metres: her sister, standing at the edge of the survey camp, holding the bolt against her forearm to check the colour.

The sailor was a young man, twenty at most, with a sunburned neck and the pleased expression of someone who had got what he wanted. He had given Teura the fabric. In exchange, based on what Hina could see from the man's closed fist, Teura had given him two or three small pearls.

Hina waited until the sailor went back to the camp. Then she walked to her sister.

"How much?" she said.

Teura looked at her. "They were small ones. Imperfect. I wasn't going to get trader price for them."

"How much did he give you for them?"

"The fabric and a knife."

Hina looked at the red bolt. It was good fabric. Firm, tightly woven, the kind that would last. Under other circumstances she might have admired the trade.

"They're not all bad," Teura said. "Some of them are just young men a long way from home."

"I know that."

"You look at them like they're all Moreau."

"I look at them," Hina said, "like they're part of a system that doesn't know and doesn't care that we exist except as entries in a ledger."

Teura looked at her. "That young man's name is Pierre. He has a mother in Brest who writes him letters and a sister who is learning to be a seamstress. He told me."

"And tomorrow he will sail away and he will not think of you or the lagoon or the pearls you traded him. He will sell them in Papeete and that will be the end of it."

"Maybe," Teura said. "Maybe that's all right."

She turned and walked home with the fabric against her arm. Hina stood at the edge of the camp and looked at the schooner anchored in the pass. Its lines were neat. Its anchor chain hung straight in the still water.

She thought: the danger is not malice. The danger is efficiency.

Chapter 11: On the Lagoon

Émile asked her on the fifth morning. He had the pirogue question ready — she could see him working up to it across the beach, his leather map case under his arm. He was going to ask if she could take him out on the water to triangulate the inner formations.

She said yes before he finished asking, which threw him.

"I was going to say today or tomorrow, whenever —"

"Today," she said. "The wind is right."

He was ready in a quarter hour. She noticed he packed for a full day without being told — water, bread, his instruments, and a small sketchbook separate from his chart roll. The sketchbook was not official equipment. She did not ask about it.

They pushed off the beach and she paddled them toward the lagoon's interior. The wind was southeast, steady. The water was clear, the bottom visible at six metres in the shallower sections.

"The formation to the northwest," he said. "The charts from Papeete show it as a single mass. But from the beach it looks like two separate heads."

"Three, actually. The southern one is under a metre of water at high tide, so it doesn't show unless you know where to look." She angled the pirogue west. "I'll take you close."

She brought them over the shallowest coral head. He leaned over the gunwale and looked down. The coral was alive — branching and foliate, yellows and browns, with fish moving through it in slow schools.

"Beautiful," he said. It was not a professional observation. It was just what it was.

She watched him sight his compass across the heads and make notes. He was precise. He drew the formation exactly as it appeared, correcting his earlier guess from the beach. She fed him information — the name of each head, the approximate depths, the seasonal variability of the water level over them.

They worked the northwest formation for an hour, then moved south.

"Your uncle," Émile said. "Is he well?"

The question surprised her. "Why do you ask?"

"I saw him on the beach this morning. He looked — I don't know the right word. Burdened."

She considered how much to say. "He has responsibilities," she said finally. "He is responsible for this village and everyone in it. A survey party arriving does not make that easier."

"No," Émile said. "I imagine it doesn't."

He looked at his chart. "For what it's worth, I —" He stopped.

"Go on."

"I came here to make an accurate map. I didn't come here to make trouble." He set his compass down. "I know that those two things may not be as separate as I'd like."

She looked at him directly. He was looking at the water, not at her.

"That's an honest thing to say," she said.

"It's the truest thing I know about what I'm doing."

She picked up the paddle. They moved south along the lagoon, and for a while neither of them spoke, and the silence was not uncomfortable.

Chapter 12: What He Wanted

The tide was out by midday and they sat on a coral shelf that was dry enough to eat on. The shelf was flat and white, bleached clean. Around them the lagoon was a colour without a name in French — something between green and blue that shifted as clouds crossed the sun.

He was drawing in the small sketchbook, not charting — she could see that now. He was sketching the atoll's profile: the palms, the line of the village rooftops, the church spire.

She said: "You told me you wanted to be a painter."

He looked up. "I mentioned it."

"Is this —" she gestured at the sketchbook "— is this what that looks like now?"

He closed the book. Then, after a moment, opened it again and handed it to her.

The drawings were good. Not the mechanical accuracy of the chart, but something looser, with atmosphere — the way the light looked over the lagoon at a specific hour, the texture of the pandanus thatch, a quick study of Father Breton's face seen in three-quarter profile.

"These are skilled," she said.

"They're a habit. I draw to think."

"And the map is —?"

"The map is how I earn my position." He said it without self-pity, which made it more honest. "My father is a lawyer in Lyon. My grandfather was a lawyer. My family has a theory of what useful men look like. A map-maker was acceptable. A painter was not."

She handed the sketchbook back. "My father wanted me to work the copra trade. He thought diving was — he used the word temporary. A young person's activity."

"Was he wrong?"

She thought about it. "He never saw me at thirty. He never saw what I know at thirty. I think he would have been surprised." She paused. "I think he would have been proud."

Émile looked at the drawing of Father Breton's face. "My father has never been to a place like this. He could not imagine it. When I write him letters from the survey, I describe the administration and the logistics. I don't describe—" he looked at the lagoon "—this."

"Why not?"

"Because I don't have words for it that he would recognize as useful."

She looked at the water. "Then perhaps you should draw it instead," she said.

He laughed, quietly, and opened the sketchbook to a fresh page.

Chapter 13: What She Showed

The east rim beds were exactly where she had calculated they would be visible: shallow enough for a man with a glass to see from a boat, obvious once you knew what colour to look for in the water. She took Émile there on the seventh day, deliberately, so that the revelation would come from her rather than from his own observation.

She angled the pirogue over the first bed and let him lean to look.

Below: the sandy bottom at four metres, the clusters of dark-shelled oysters in their irregular rows, the way they caught light on the nacre edges.

"Pearl oysters," he said.

"Yes."

"How many beds along this rim?"

"Three visible from the surface. This one, one two hundred metres south, and a smaller one at the northern curve." She paddled easily, keeping the boat steady. "They're not the richest beds. But they're real."

He was making notes. She watched what he wrote — the latitude bearing, the depth estimate, the notation that the beds were visible from a surface boat. Good. Let the chart say that. Let the chart be complete about things that could not be hidden.

"Are there deeper beds?" he asked. He said it without looking up from his notes.

"There are deeper parts of the lagoon," she said. "Whether oysters are in them — the deep zones are harder to read. The light doesn't penetrate the same way."

All of this was technically true.

He looked at her. She kept her expression neutral — not closed, not open, just attentive. He was observant, this man. She had to be careful not to overplay the neutrality.

"I would need a diver to survey the deep zones," he said.

"You would," she agreed.

"Would you be willing to do that?"

She pretended to consider it. "That depends on what Lieutenant Moreau's mandate actually requires. Whether depth surveying is in scope."

"It is."

"Then I'll speak to my uncle."

She picked up the paddle and turned the pirogue south, toward the second visible bed. The timing was correct. She had given him the east rim. She had withheld the channel. And she had planted, without saying so directly, the idea that depth diving was a separate negotiation — which meant there were steps between now and the channel beds, steps during which things might change.

The morning light was high and clear. A brown booby sat on a coral head and watched them pass without alarm.

---

Chapter 14: Tupa's Move

She found out from Teura, who had heard it from Pierre the sailor, who had heard it from one of the enlisted men.

Tupa had gone to Lieutenant Moreau's tent.

The exact content of the conversation she couldn't know. Teura's chain of sources was long and each link had simplified things in the transmission. But the outline was clear: Tupa had told Moreau there were beds in the channel that Hina was concealing, and that he — Tupa — knew where they were.

She found Tupa on the south beach, mending a line.

She sat down beside him without preamble. "Moreau's tent," she said.

He kept working the line. "It's a public camp. Anyone can go."

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him the truth."

"Tupa." She kept her voice low. "What truth?"

He looked at her. "The truth that there are three deep beds in the channel, and that you are not going to show them."

The line went through his fingers. He did not look away from her.

She said: "Do you understand what happens if a company gets hold of those beds?"

"I understand what happens if no company does," he said. "The village stays exactly as it is. The copra price goes up and down. The fish are good some years and poor others. We never have money for a new boat or for medicine or for anything except getting through the next season."

"We have the pearls."

"We have whatever the trader will give us. Which is a fraction of what the same pearls sell for in Papeete. Which is a fraction of what they sell for in Paris." He tied off the line. "Manea's plan is to protect the beds forever. But the beds don't need protecting — they need to be worth something."

"They're worth more undamaged."

"They're worth nothing if we starve before we can dive them."

She looked at him. He was not wrong about the economics. That was the painful part. He was right about the copra price and the medicine and the boats. He was wrong about what Delacroix's company would actually do, but he didn't know that yet.

"Just don't do anything else," she said. "Give me time."

He picked up the next line. "You have some time," he said. "Not all of it."

---

Chapter 15: The Priest's Warning

Father Breton came to her house in the evening. He came alone, without the social pretext of visiting Manea or of pastoral duty. He sat on the step outside and she brought him tea.

"You know about Tupa," he said.

"Yes."

"He's not finished." Father Breton held the cup in both hands. The evening was warm but he always seemed to welcome heat. Twelve years on the atoll and he still drank hot tea in summer. "He's been meeting with Moreau's men in the evenings. Not Moreau himself — the men. Learning from them what company contracts look like. What terms are typical."

She sat across from him. The dark was coming in from the ocean side, the stars appearing from east to west.

"He's thinking about independent terms," she said. "Contracting himself directly."

"That's what I believe."

"He can't. He doesn't own the beds."

"He owns his diving," Father Breton said. "Under colonial law, a man who shows a company the location of a resource and agrees to dive it can receive a fee. The ownership of the resource itself is a separate legal question."

"One the company will resolve in its own favour."

"Yes." He drank. "But Tupa may not be thinking past his own payment."

She looked at the lagoon. The survey schooner's lamp burned at anchor. "Is there something you can do? He respects you."

"He respects the church," Father Breton said, with a rueful precision. "Whether he respects me personally is less certain." He paused. "I spoke to him two days ago. He listened politely and then said he was making a decision about his own livelihood and that God would understand."

"And what did you say?"

"I said I hoped God would explain it to me afterwards." He set down the cup. "I came to tell you because you need to know. Not because I have a solution."

"Thank you," she said.

He rose slowly. His knees troubled him. He had mentioned it once, only once, and not complained again.

"There is also the question of what comes after Moreau," he said. He said it looking at the lagoon, not at her.

"What do you mean?"

"The survey party has a mandate to chart. They chart and then they leave. What comes after them is determined by the charts." He turned to look at her. "I have been in this archipelago long enough to know that the next party is rarely the survey party. It is the party that uses the survey."

He walked away down the path toward the church. She sat in the dark for a long time and thought about what comes after.

---

Chapter 16: Delacroix

The second vessel appeared in the pass on the ninth day, without announcement. It was smaller than the survey schooner — a fast inter-island sloop, the kind that Papeete merchants used when they needed to be somewhere quickly. It came through the pass on the morning tide and anchored fifty metres from the survey schooner.

The man who came ashore was not like Moreau. Moreau had a soldier's bearing — upright, formal, organized by rank. This man was compact and unhurried. He wore good clothes without wearing them noticeably. He carried a leather satchel and shook Moreau's hand on the beach with the practised ease of someone for whom meetings were a tool.

Hina watched from the copra shed's shade.

He spoke to Moreau for ten minutes. She was too far to hear. Moreau's body language changed slightly — not submission, but a kind of adjustment, the way you adjust when someone senior to you arrives unexpectedly and you must recalculate the room.

Then the man turned and looked across the beach directly at her.

He walked over. She had not expected that.

"Madame Aroha," he said. "I am Delacroix. From Papeete."

His French was not the French of an official — it was the French of someone who had used language as an instrument for a long time and had worn it smooth with use.

"I know who you are," she said.

He paused. "What do you know?"

"Coastal trade company. Pearl interests in Gambier and the eastern Tuamotus. Here because the survey is approaching the beds."

He regarded her for a moment. "Thorough," he said. It was not a compliment exactly. It was the word you used when someone had done something you hadn't expected.

"What do you want?" she said.

"The same thing everyone wants," he said. "To understand what's in the lagoon."

"Then you should learn to dive."

He smiled. It was a thin smile, controlled. "That's what you're for," he said pleasantly, and walked back toward the camp.

---

Chapter 17: The Company's Letters

Manea was not a man who was easily shaken. He had led the village through three storms, a fever year, and the complicated 1862 negotiation over copra rights. He sat across from Delacroix in his own house and accepted the letters the company man brought with the careful courtesy of someone deciding how much weight to give a document they did not entirely trust.

The letters were from the Governor's office in Papeete. Hina read them over Manea's shoulder, slowly, catching every clause.

They were what Father Breton had predicted: letters of authority granting Delacroix's company first right of commercial exploitation over any pearl beds formally charted in the survey area. The language was specific about what "formally charted" meant. It meant beds that appeared by coordinate on the official survey documents with the countersignature of the survey's naval officer.

So: Moreau's signature was the trigger. Everything pointed at Moreau.

Delacroix explained this in simple terms, as if Manea were a man of limited intelligence. Hina translated without inflection, keeping Delacroix's condescension out of the Paumotu.

Manea listened. When Hina finished, he said: "Tell him I understand."

She translated.

Delacroix said: "The company is not here to dispossess the village. A share of harvest revenue can be arranged. These are not unusual terms."

Hina translated.

Manea looked at the letters. Then he looked at Hina with an expression she recognized — the expression that meant: translate not what he said but what he means.

She said to Manea, in Paumotu: "He means the beds will be harvested industrially. Divers brought in from outside. The village's share will be small and fixed, regardless of the size of the harvest. The beds will be worked until they are exhausted and then the company will leave."

Manea nodded slowly. He said nothing for a long moment. Then he said, in Paumotu: "Tell him I understand."

Hina turned to Delacroix. "My uncle says he understands."

Delacroix gathered his letters. "Good," he said. "Then we can proceed efficiently."

After he left, Manea sat in the quiet house for a time. Then he said: "How long before the chart is complete?"

"Ten days," she said. "Maybe twelve."

"Then we have ten days."

---

Chapter 18: What the Chart Said

He left the charts drying on a flat rock outside the camp tent, weighted by stones. She had seen him do it before — he needed them to dry flat after working with the pen, and the afternoon sun did it in an hour.

She was walking back from the lagoon. She had been out alone, checking the east rim beds, verifying there was nothing visible from the surface that she had not already shown Émile. There wasn't.

She stopped by the drying charts.

It took her less than a minute to read them. She had her father's ability with maps — his spatial sense, the way a flat surface became real space in her mind. She could see the atoll take shape in the pen lines: the outer reef, the pass, the east rim with its three marked beds, the village and its beach, the survey camp.

The interior of the lagoon was still light pencil — not yet inked, indicating work in progress. The northwest formations were detailed. The east rim was complete. The channel was blank.

So he had not yet charted the channel. Either because he had not gone there, or because she had successfully kept him away from it.

But she also saw something else. There was a note in the margin of the channel section, in his precise small hand: depth uncertain, access required.

He knew there was something there. He just didn't know what.

She stepped back from the charts and went to find Manea.

---

Chapter 19: After Dark

She told him she had looked at the charts. She said it directly, no preface.

He set down his pen. He had been working — a letter, she thought, to someone in Papeete. He looked at her without anger, which she had partly expected and partly feared.

"And?" he said.

"The channel is blank. He hasn't charted it. There's a note that access is needed."

He looked at the lamp on the table between them. "So he knows."

"He suspects. He doesn't know what's there."

"What did you see on the chart otherwise?"

"Complete. Accurate. He's good." She paused. "He put the Paumotu names in the margin."

Émile was quiet for a moment. He turned his pen in his fingers.

"I know what you're doing," he said. "You're keeping me away from the channel."

She had not expected him to say it directly. She had expected the game to continue longer.

"Yes," she said.

He nodded. He didn't seem angry about that either. "What's in the channel?"

She met his eyes. "What I said before. Deeper water. Current. I've told you it's difficult to survey without a diver."

"Is there a diver?"

"There's me."

"Are you going to take me there?"

She sat down on the folding camp stool across from him. The lamp was low — he had been working by diminishing light. "I need to know what happens to the chart after you make it," she said.

He looked at her. "Moreau countersigns it and it goes to Papeete."

"And then?"

"And then it's an official chart." He paused. "And then Delacroix's company files its exploitation rights based on what appears on it."

"Yes," she said.

They looked at each other in the lamplight.

"Tell me what's in the channel," he said.

"Pearl beds," she said. "Deep ones. Three of them. My uncle's family has managed them for four generations. They are the village's reserve against bad years."

Silence.

"If they appear on the chart," he said.

"They will be gone in ten years," she said. "The company will work them to exhaustion. I have seen what happened to the Gambier beds."

He looked at the lamp. A long silence.

"I am supposed to chart what is there," he said.

"I know."

"I cannot make an accurate chart of an area I have not surveyed."

"No."

"But I can note that I was unable to complete a survey of the interior channel due to access and depth limitations." He said it carefully, each word placed. "That would be accurate. The access was limited. The depth is significant."

She looked at him.

"I'm not telling you what I'll do," he said. "I'm telling you what options exist."

She rose. "I'll come back tomorrow morning," she said. "We can go to the channel and you can assess the access and depth limitations for yourself."

She left. She walked back in the dark through the palm grove and she thought: he is a better man than he knows. Or he is a better man than is good for him.

---

Chapter 20: Manea's Illness

The fever came on fast, as fevers in the tropics do. Manea was well at evening and shivering by midnight. By morning his temperature was high enough to worry and by afternoon Hina had sent Teura to ask Father Breton for whatever medicines the mission had.

It was not a dangerous fever — Father Breton said so, with the measured confidence of a man who had seen dangerous fevers and knew the difference. It was the ordinary kind: three or four days of misery and then improvement, assuming rest and water and no complications.

But it came at the worst possible time.

Without Manea, Hina was the village's effective voice. The elders were there and they were capable, but they deferred to Manea on questions of the French, and Manea had delegated that authority to her. She felt the weight of it.

Delacroix came to the house on the second day of the fever. She met him at the door.

He said Manea's illness was unfortunate and he hoped for a swift recovery. He said this with the particular smoothness of a man for whom other people's misfortunes were primarily logistical.

"You are representing the village now?" he said.

"I am helping my uncle," she said.

"Then you are representing the village." He smiled. "I have a proposal. Not for Manea — for you specifically. I've heard you are the most capable diver on the atoll. I'd like to discuss terms."

"My uncle will recover," she said. "Discuss terms with him."

"Of course," Delacroix said. "But while we wait for his recovery, perhaps you and I could speak informally. No commitment."

"No," she said.

He accepted this without visible reaction. He was a man who had learned long ago that the first answer was rarely the final one.

"When he recovers," he said pleasantly, and left.

She went back inside. Manea was asleep. His breathing was even. Teura sat beside him with a cloth and a bowl of water.

"He wants to use the time," Teura said. She had heard from behind the curtain.

"Yes."

"Will he?"

Hina looked at her uncle sleeping. "He'll try," she said.

---

Chapter 21: Delacroix's Proposal

He came to her a second time on the third day, not to the house but to the lagoon's edge where she was working. She was checking the pirogue's hull — a hairline crack in the bow she had been meaning to caulk.

He stood at a respectful distance, which told her he had thought carefully about approach.

"I'll be brief," he said.

She kept working the caulk into the crack. The caulk was a mixture of breadfruit sap and coconut fibre, the traditional method, effective and long-lasting.

"The company is going to obtain rights to whatever beds appear on the survey chart," he said. "That is fixed. The Governor's letters determine it." He paused. "What is not fixed is what the village receives in return."

She said nothing.

"Fifteen per cent of gross harvest value," he said. "Paid quarterly to the village council. That is more than any other atoll in the Tuamotus has received."

She pressed the caulk into the crack, working it even.

"And the beds?" she said.

"The beds produce what they produce."

"For how long?"

"The company's contract is for twenty years."

She looked up at him. "And in twenty years?"

He met her eyes. He did not look away. "In twenty years, the contract terms will be renegotiated."

She turned back to the caulk. "The Gambier beds took seven years to exhaust," she said. "At industrial rates."

A pause. "The Gambier beds were smaller."

"I know," she said. "I know their size. I know the rate of harvest. I know what the beds looked like in 1870 and I know what they look like now." She set down the caulk. "What do they look like now, Monsieur Delacroix?"

He was quiet for a moment. "The situation in Gambier was different."

"Because the village there did not resist," she said. "I know."

She picked up the caulk again. "My uncle will be well in two more days," she said. "Come to him then."

He waited a moment, as if deciding whether to press. He decided not to. He walked back toward the camp, and she watched him go, and thought about his fifteen per cent.

---

Chapter 22: The Wrong Question

He came back. She had expected that.

He came the following afternoon, and this time he did not approach with a proposal. He stood at the same distance and looked at the lagoon, and when he spoke his tone had shifted slightly — less of the salesman, more of the man.

"You asked why a company that could take everything is willing to share," he said.

She had asked exactly that, a week ago.

"What's the answer?" she said.

"Because taking everything requires cooperation." He looked at the water. "We are not a government. We don't have soldiers. We can obtain rights from Papeete, yes, but exercising those rights requires people on the ground who know the lagoon, who know where the beds are, who can direct divers." He paused. "If the village obstructs us, we can hire divers from outside. It costs more and produces less. The fifteen per cent is cheaper than obstruction."

She considered this. It was honest, which was unusual for him.

"So the fifteen per cent is not generosity," she said. "It's logistics."

"I said nothing about generosity," he said.

"And if the village cooperates and the beds are exhausted in ten years?"

"Then in ten years the village will have received fifteen per cent of whatever the beds produced. Which is more than they're receiving now."

"They're receiving one hundred per cent of what the beds produce now," she said. "At a rate that sustains the beds."

He turned to look at her. "You said I was asking the wrong question," he said. "When you asked why we share rather than take."

She had not said that. He had said it of her.

"I didn't say that," she said.

"No," he said. "I did. But I'll ask it anyway. What is the right question?"

She looked at the lagoon. The afternoon light was low, the water turning from blue to green to a deeper, richer turquoise at the far end.

"The right question," she said, "is what happens to the people here when the beds are gone."

He was quiet for a long time. "That is a question I can't answer to your satisfaction," he said at last. "And I think we both know it."

He walked away. She stood at the lagoon's edge and watched the light change.

---

Chapter 23: What Émile Knew

He showed her the reports at the mission house, where they met at Father Breton's invitation to review a translation question — a pretext she did not believe for a moment but was grateful for.

Father Breton made himself scarce, retreating to the garden with ostentatious purpose, leaving them at the table with papers between them.

The reports were internal company documents, survey reports from the Gambier and eastern Tuamotu operations. Émile had obtained them from the survey archives in Papeete before departing — professionally, as part of background research for the survey. They were not secret. They were simply not advertised.

The pattern was clear. First survey. Then exploitation rights. Then contracted divers brought in from Tahiti and Fiji, working fast and deep, at rates that produced volume but damaged the beds. Within seven years of the first industrial harvest in the Gambier beds, the annual pearl count had fallen by eighty per cent. The company's harvest reports recorded this matter-of-factly, without commentary.

She read through the tables slowly.

"This is what you meant," she said. "When you said you'd seen what happened."

"Yes."

"You've had these since Papeete."

"Yes."

She looked up at him. "Why didn't you tell Moreau?"

He looked at the table. "Moreau's mandate is a government mandate. The exploitation rights are a separate legal instrument. The survey results feed into the rights, but the rights exist independently. Showing Moreau evidence of depletion elsewhere doesn't change his orders."

"But it changes what he thinks."

"I don't know what it changes," Émile said. "He's a naval officer with a mission. His career depends on completing that mission."

She tapped the last page of the Gambier report, where the final harvest numbers showed a handful of pearls in a season that had once produced thousands. "Delacroix knows these numbers," she said.

"Of course he does."

"He offered us fifteen per cent."

Émile looked at the report. "Fifteen per cent of what those beds produced in their last two years of operation would not buy medicine for a winter."

"No," she said. "It would not."

She folded the reports. "Thank you," she said.

He took the papers back. "What will you do with that information?"

"I'll think about it," she said. "What are you going to do with it?"

He was quiet for a moment. "I've been thinking about that," he said.

She waited, but he did not say more. Outside, Father Breton could be heard talking to the goat.

---

Chapter 24: Tupa's Deal

She heard about it from Vahine. The eldest diver had seen Tupa leave the village before dawn and return at midday with money — French coins, not the trade goods that moved through the village's informal economy. She had said nothing to anyone, but she told Hina.

Hina found Tupa at his house in the late afternoon. He was eating alone, which he often did. She sat across from him without asking permission.

He looked at her. He did not pretend not to know why she had come.

"The middle channel bed," he said. "I dove it this morning with Delacroix's man. His man marked the buoy position. I gave them the depth and the estimated count."

She sat with this for a moment. "And the southern bed?"

"Tomorrow."

"How much?" she said.

"Enough to get to Tahiti," he said. "A stake."

She looked at him. He was forty years old. He had spent his entire life on this atoll, and she knew — she had known for years — that he was tired of it in a way that went deeper than weariness. He wanted something different, and the lagoon that had been his whole identity had become also his prison.

"You could have gone to Tahiti without selling the beds," she said.

"With what?" he said. "The pearl money? My share of what Manea's family permits me to take from beds I've been diving since before you learned to walk?"

The injustice in his voice was real. She did not dismiss it.

"Tupa," she said. "When the company comes, they will bring their own divers. You understand that."

"I know."

"Your money will run out in Tahiti in a year."

"Maybe," he said. "But I'll have had a year."

She said nothing. There was nothing to say about that.

"The beds are already marked," he said. "The middle channel bed. I can't un-mark it."

"No," she said. "You can't."

She rose. She stood in his doorway. "Was it enough?" she asked. Not bitterly. She genuinely wanted to know.

"It was enough to get out," he said. "That's all I needed."

---

Chapter 25: What He Owed

She told him she knew about the middle channel bed. She said it plainly, standing in the path between the copra shed and his house, where he was carrying equipment back to camp.

He stopped. He set the case down.

"You're going to tell me I betrayed the village," he said.

"I was going to ask you something first," she said.

He waited.

"When Manea asked me to be interpreter, and not you — I know that hurt. I know the history of it. Your years on this reef, and mine." She paused. "I've never thanked you for teaching me. When I was learning to dive. You were harder on me than anyone else, but you were honest about what I was doing wrong. I learned from that."

He looked at her. Something complicated moved across his face.

"I'm not saying that to soften the rest," she said. "I'm saying it because it's true and I've never said it."

A silence.

"The rest," he said.

"The rest is that you have made a decision that will hurt people who never hurt you, and you did it for reasons that I understand and cannot defend. The money is spent and the bed is marked and none of that changes." She met his eyes. "I'm not asking you to feel guilty. I'm asking you: can you undo any part of it?"

He picked up the case again. He was going to walk away. She had expected that.

But he stopped.

"The southern bed," he said. "I haven't dove it yet."

"No."

He stood with the case for a long moment. "I told them I'd go tomorrow."

She waited.

"I might be sick tomorrow," he said. He walked away.

She stood in the path. It was not undone. But it was something.

---

Chapter 26: Father Breton's Table

Father Breton's dinner table was a plank of salvaged timber supported by two rope-tied sawhorses. It seated six comfortably and eight in an emergency. He had used it for twelve years of meals and negotiations and confessions that had begun as meals and slid sideways.

He invited Hina and Émile to eat on the evening of the twelfth day. He cooked himself: a fish stew with sweet potato and coriander that he grew in the garden.

He opened a bottle of rum he kept for visitors and poured three glasses. The lamp was bright. Outside, the night insects.

"I find," Father Breton said, settling heavily into his chair, "that people think more clearly when they're eating someone else's food. Less need to defend territory."

Hina looked at Émile. He looked at the stew.

"Who are you expecting us to think clearly about?" she said.

"Each other. Your shared problem."

"We don't have a shared problem," she said.

"You both want the beds undamaged," Father Breton said. "That seems shared to me."

Émile said: "I want an accurate chart. Whether that's compatible with undamaged beds is not something I've resolved."

"I know," Father Breton said. "That's why I invited you to dinner."

They ate. The stew was good. The rum was rough but honest.

Hina said, after a while: "He is going to write a letter to his superior."

Father Breton looked at Émile.

Émile looked at his bowl. "I drafted it," he said. "I haven't sent it."

"What does it say?" Father Breton asked.

"It raises questions about the legality of the exploitation rights. Specifically whether the Governor's letters of authority were issued within the proper administrative mandate. There are procedural questions."

Father Breton considered this. "Procedural questions are not legal challenges."

"No. But procedural questions can delay a countersignature."

"For how long?"

"Months. The administrative response time from Paris to Papeete to here is —" He paused. "Long enough that the survey's completion might be held in abeyance."

Hina looked at him across the table. She had not known about the letter. The draft, yes. Not what was in it.

"That's a specific form of risk," she said. "For you."

"Yes," he said.

Father Breton poured more rum into all three glasses. "Drink this," he said, "and think about whether any of you believe in miracles."

He said it with enough dryness that all three of them almost laughed.

---

Chapter 27: The Letter

He showed her the letter two days later.

It was three pages, handwritten in the careful formal French of official correspondence. It was addressed to the Directeur du Service Hydrographique de la Marine in Papeete — his direct superior in the survey administration.

She read it slowly. It raised, as he had said, procedural questions. The questions concerned the 1873 revision of the colonial survey mandate, which had extended the scope of resource enumeration to include underwater natural resources. The letter argued — carefully, without accusation — that the extension of mandate had not been properly authorized at the ministerial level in Paris and that the Governor of French Polynesia had exceeded his authority in issuing exploitation rights based on a mandate that was itself of uncertain validity.

It was a technical argument. It was the kind of argument that could take a year to adjudicate.

She looked up.

"This would stop the survey?" she said.

"It would stop the countersignature of the survey. Which would stop the exploitation rights from taking legal effect." He was watching her read. "It doesn't stop Delacroix. He can still be here. His company can still negotiate. But without the official chart and the countersignature, his rights are unexercised."

"And if the argument fails? If Paris confirms the Governor's authority?"

"Then Delacroix has his rights in a year or two rather than in a month."

She set the letter down. "Is the argument valid?"

He paused. "My father's a lawyer," he said. "I grew up listening to legal arguments at the dinner table. This one —" he looked at the letter "— this one is not invented. The 1873 mandate extension was fast-tracked. There are questions about the Parliamentary authorization. I'm not certain the argument will succeed. But I'm certain it isn't fraudulent."

She looked at him. "Why are you doing this?"

He looked at the letter. He was quiet for a moment.

"Because I looked at the Gambier reports," he said. "And I looked at what's in this lagoon. And the map I'm making will be used to take one and produce the other." He paused. "I am not able to do that without at least trying not to."

She folded her hands in her lap. "Delacroix will find out."

"Yes," he said.

"Soon," she said. "He has people in Papeete."

"I know."

She picked up the letter and handed it back to him. "Send it," she said.

---

Chapter 28: Consequences

Delacroix found out in three days.

She did not know how. His network extended into the survey administration — that was clear — and the letter had been sent through official channels, which meant it had been read by at least two clerks before it left the island.

He came to the camp not to Émile's tent but to Moreau's. She was not present. She heard about it afterward from one of the enlisted men, a young Breton who liked to talk.

Moreau called Émile to the camp that same afternoon. Hina saw Émile go in. She waited at the copra shed.

He came out an hour later. He walked back to his own tent without looking her way.

She went to his tent at dusk.

He was sitting on his cot with the map case on his knees. He was not doing anything. He was just sitting.

"Moreau?" she said.

"Moreau was Moreau," he said. "He told me the letter was irregular and that a survey cartographer does not have administrative standing to challenge a mandate's legality. He told me to withdraw the letter."

"Delacroix?"

"Delacroix was—" He paused. "Delacroix told me something different." He looked at the map case. "He said that men who created unnecessary difficulties in colonial administration had a way of finding their next posting at a considerable distance from the one they preferred. He said this quietly, conversationally, as if he were discussing the weather."

"Did he threaten you directly?"

"He was much too careful for that." Émile set the map case down. "He said he hoped the survey would proceed smoothly and that he was confident I understood the importance of cooperation."

She sat on the folding stool. The lamp was not lit. They sat in the dimming light.

"The letter," she said.

"I withdrew it," he said. "Moreau required it."

She looked at him.

"But," he said, "the argument in it still exists. The legal questions are still there. Anyone who looked at the 1873 mandate could find them."

She understood what he was telling her. "Father Breton," she said.

"Father Breton writes to the Bishop in Papeete," Émile said. "The Bishop is not under colonial administrative authority. He is under the authority of the Church."

"And the Church has lawyers."

"The Church has lawyers," he said. "Very old and very patient ones."

---

Chapter 29: Teura's Choice

Teura told her at breakfast. She said it plainly, because Teura was a plainspoken person when she chose to be.

"I've agreed to work for the camp," she said. "Washing and mending. For wages."

Hina poured tea and did not respond immediately.

"It's fair wages," Teura said. "More than the copra work paid last season. And it's only for the remaining weeks of the survey."

"I know," Hina said.

"You're going to tell me not to."

"I'm not," Hina said.

Teura looked at her. "Really."

"You're twenty-six," Hina said. "You make your own decisions."

"You think it's a mistake."

"I think you have a right to make your own mistakes." She drank her tea. "I also think Delacroix will try to use whatever goodwill you build with the camp against me."

Teura was quiet for a moment. "I'm not a tool."

"No. But he'll try anyway. Be careful what you say to the men about the lagoon. About the family. About me."

"I'm not stupid, Hina."

"I know you're not."

Teura picked up her cup. "Pierre asked about you," she said.

"The sailor who bought your pearls."

"He said the cartographer — Renard — talks about you. In the evenings. When the men sit together."

Hina said nothing.

"He says he talks about the lagoon and the reef and the things you've shown him. But Pierre thinks —" Teura set her cup down. "He thinks it's not really about the lagoon."

Hina rose and took her cup to the wash basin. "Go do your washing," she said.

Teura smiled and went.

---

Chapter 30: The Formal Request

On the sixteenth day, Lieutenant Moreau issued a formal written request to the village. It was delivered to Manea — recovered now, moving slowly but clear-headed — by one of the enlisted men, in a sealed envelope with the naval survey's stamp.

Manea had Hina read it to him aloud.

The request was for a complete underwater survey of the interior lagoon, including the deep channel sections. It cited the survey mandate. It requested cooperation in designating a guide for the dive survey. It offered a fee — a specific amount, stated in francs — for the village's participation.

Manea listened to it twice.

"The channel," he said.

"Yes."

"They have the middle bed. Tupa's mark."

"Yes."

"They want the southern bed and the deep northern formation."

"That's what a complete survey would include."

He was quiet. His hands, folded on his knee, were still. He had the stillness of a man who had made peace with uncertainty.

"What are our options?" he said.

She had thought about this. "We refuse. They proceed without us, hiring divers from outside. That takes longer — maybe two additional weeks — but they'll find what's there eventually, given enough time and effort."

"Or?"

"We cooperate selectively. I dive the channel. I show them what I choose to show them. I'm the best diver they have access to. If I guide them wrong, they'll know eventually, but it will take time to correct."

"And?"

"And we build a legal argument that runs in parallel with whatever I do on the water. Father Breton's letter. The procedural challenge to the mandate. It doesn't stop the survey, but it delays the exploitation rights. Delay is not victory, but it's time."

Manea looked at the formal request. "Time for what?"

She said: "Time for the argument to develop. Time for the Bishop's lawyers to look at it. Time for whatever happens in Paris when someone asks the right question." She paused. "Time is what we have. We don't have law on our side, not yet. We don't have power. We have time and we have the water."

Manea folded the request and held it in both hands. "Respond formally," he said. "Tell them we will cooperate with the dive survey. And then dive the channel as you think best."

---

Chapter 31: The Dark Dive

She went alone, at night, three days before she was scheduled to dive with the survey.

There was no moon. The lagoon was black, the stars reflecting in it. She left the pirogue at the channel's northern edge and slipped in without a ripple.

The dark was absolute below two metres. She had a lamp — a small enclosed oil lamp in a glass jar, sealed against water, which cast a narrow yellow cone. It was not enough to illuminate the full channel. It was enough to see what she needed to see.

She dove the northern bed first. Down eight metres through the dark, following the slope of the channel wall. The current here was mild at night — less than in the day, which was why she had come when she had. She ran her hands along the bottom, reading it the way she read everything: by feel, by texture, by the slight variations in temperature that told her where the current ran and where it pooled.

The northern bed was intact. She counted by touch — the irregular clusters, the drag of shell against her palm. Dense. Well-stocked. A good year had been kind to this bed.

She surfaced. Breathed. Down again.

The middle channel bed — Tupa's marked bed — was deeper, at twelve metres. She knew it better than she knew most things in the world. She had been diving it since she was nineteen, this particular fold in the reef bottom where the current brought nutrients and the oysters congregated. Even now, knowing the buoy was above and the position had been given to Delacroix, she felt at home in it.

She did not stay long. It was already compromised.

The southern pocket was the deepest — thirteen metres, in a bowl formed by the collapse of an old coral structure. It required a longer hold. She paused at the surface, breathed the way her mother had taught her: deep and slow, three breaths, filling and emptying fully, not hyperventilating. Then down.

Thirteen metres. The lamp showed the curve of the bowl, the irregular ceiling of coral above. She moved along the bottom. The southern pocket was the smallest of the three beds but the densest — older shells, larger, the kind that produced the big round pearls that traders came specifically to find.

She stayed as long as she dared. Then she let herself rise.

At the surface, floating on her back in the dark, she looked at the stars and thought about what she had memorized. Every feature. Every distance. Every depth. She knew this bed the way Émile knew his charts — not as an image but as a body of knowledge that could be reproduced, applied, corrected.

She also knew exactly how to swim a survey pattern that would be thorough and accurate in all the ways that did not matter, and absent in precisely the ways that did.

---

Chapter 32: The Sketchbook

He came to the lagoon the next morning with the sketchbook, not the instruments. She had not asked him to come. He appeared on the beach while she was loading the pirogue and said he wanted to come out.

"No instruments?" she said.

"Not today."

She looked at him. He seemed different from the official survey mode — less armoured, more uncertain, as if the letter withdrawal had cost him something visible.

She paddled them to the northwest, away from the channel, to the outer reef where the water was clear and the coral spread in its full variety. She tied the pirogue to a coral head and they sat.

He opened the sketchbook. He drew the reef — not the technical version, but the way it actually looked: the fans and branches, the schools of fish turning as one body, the light filtering through the green-tinted water in long slanted columns.

She watched him draw. He did not ask her to hold still. He drew the scene as it moved.

After a while he said: "Will you sit facing me?"

She turned on the pirogue's thwart. He looked at her and then down at the page and back at her. He was drawing her face. She knew it from the way he looked — the cartographer's careful observation applied to something different.

"Go ahead," she said.

He drew for twenty minutes without speaking. The sun moved. A frigate bird circled overhead and left.

When he stopped, she held out her hand. He hesitated, then gave her the sketchbook.

She looked at herself. The drawing was skilled — she recognized herself, the angle of her face, the specific quality of attention in her eyes. He had not softened anything. He had drawn what was there.

"Is that how you see me?" she said.

"It's how you look when you're deciding something," he said. "Which is most of the time."

She handed the sketchbook back. "Most of the time I am deciding something," she said.

He took it back and closed it. They sat for a while on the anchored pirogue, the reef below them, the sun overhead.

"When does the survey end?" she said.

"Another week," he said. "Possibly less, if Moreau gets what he needs."

"What does Moreau need?"

"The channel survey." He looked at the water. "After that the chart is complete and we leave."

She thought about timing. One week. Maybe less.

"Émile," she said. She did not use his given name often. He looked at her when she said it.

"When you leave," she said. "What happens to the map?"

He was quiet for a moment. "The chart goes to Papeete. It gets countersigned and filed. Delacroix's company files its exploitation rights within thirty days of countersignature."

"And your letter?"

"Withdrawn," he said. "But —" He stopped.

"But the argument is still there," she said.

"Yes," he said.

The pirogue moved gently on the swell. Below them, through the clear water, the coral was alive with colour.

---

Chapter 33: The Marked Beds

Tupa dove the southern pocket on a Wednesday. Hina knew because she watched from the lagoon's edge, too far to stop it, close enough to see the buoy go down and stay.

Delacroix's man — a compact, experienced diver from Tahiti named Arii, hired specifically for this work — was in the water with him. They were out for forty minutes. When they came up, Arii had a slate board with coordinates written on it. He handed it to Delacroix who stood in the company's small dinghy, and Delacroix wrote the numbers into his leather ledger without expression.

That night she sat with Manea.

She told him the southern pocket was marked. He received this without visible response, which was not the same as receiving it without pain.

"So," he said.

"So."

"Both deep beds."

"Yes."

He was quiet. Outside, the sound of the lagoon — the small persistent movement of water over coral.

"The northern bed," he said.

"Still unmarked," she said. "Tupa hasn't dove it. I don't think Delacroix knows it exists."

"Yet."

"Yet," she agreed.

Manea looked at his hands. "The petition," he said. "Vahine and I talked about it. A petition asserting customary rights to the beds. Has it any legal standing under colonial law?"

"No," she said. "Father Breton's read the statutes. Customary rights are not recognized under the current administration."

"Then why do it?"

She paused. "Because legal standing and persuasive weight are different things. A signed petition from the village, with the chief's mark, goes into the official record. When anyone reviews that record later — in five years, or ten, or fifty — they will see that the village objected. That the village said: these are ours and we said so."

Manea considered this. "It won't stop anything now."

"Nothing will stop everything now," she said. "But it might stop some things. And it establishes a record."

He nodded slowly. "Draft the petition," he said. "I'll mark it. The elders will mark it. Get Vahine first."

She rose to go. "And Tupa?" she said.

Manea was quiet for a long time. "Let Tupa be," he said at last. "He made his choice. He lives in this village. He will have to live with it."

---

Chapter 34: After the Storm Warning

She told Manea in the morning. She had been up before dawn — she was always up before dawn — and she had felt the change in the air: the particular stillness that preceded a storm in the tropics, the way the birds flew differently, the altered quality of the horizon light.

By midday it was visible to everyone. The sky to the south had a greenish tint. The survey camp began securing its equipment. The schooner's crew took in the loose gear topside and doubled the anchor chain.

The storm hit that evening — not a great storm, but solid: gusts to thirty knots, heavy rain, the lagoon's surface churned to grey chop. The survey camp's tent held. Father Breton's mission was built to withstand this kind of storm and did.

The company's buoys were gone by morning.

Hina walked the beach after the rain stopped. The lagoon was littered with torn weed and small debris. Where the buoy lines had been in the channel, there was now nothing — the current had taken them, or the surge had pulled the anchors, or some combination of both.

The coordinates were in Delacroix's ledger. The positions were recorded. But the physical markers were gone, and without physical markers, Arii would need to re-dive to confirm positions. That meant at least another day's work, possibly two.

She stood on the beach and thought about how much could change in two days.

---

Chapter 35: The Bishop's Position

Father Breton came to her house on the second morning after the storm. He was carrying a letter. He had received it by the last post before the storm — a response from the Bishop in Papeete, written two weeks earlier and taking three weeks to arrive.

He sat down and read it to her.

The Bishop's letter was not a command. It was not a legal ruling. It was a statement of the Church's pastoral concern for indigenous communities under colonial administration, and it referenced specifically the depletion of natural resources in Gambier as a moral concern. It said the Church could not instruct secular authorities, but that the Bishop was prepared to make formal representations to the Governor's office opposing industrial exploitation of the Tuamotu pearl beds on grounds of community welfare.

Father Breton set the letter down. "It's not a veto," he said.

"No," she said. "But it's a voice."

"The Governor will listen to the Bishop as far as he must and no further."

"Further than he'd listen to me," she said. "Or to Manea."

He folded the letter. "I'm going to write back asking the Bishop to make those representations formally and in writing to the Governor. I'd like to attach to my letter a copy of the village petition."

She looked at him. "You knew Manea was drafting a petition."

"I may have suggested it," he said, with the mildness of a man who had been arranging things quietly for decades.

"Father Breton," she said. "How long have you been working at this?"

He picked up his teacup. "Since before Moreau arrived," he said. "I saw the survey request in the administrative bulletin six months ago. I've been corresponding with the Bishop since then." He drank. "I am not a fast-acting man, Hina. I am a patient one."

---

Chapter 36: The Altered Scale

She found out from the chart itself.

He had left the channel section on the drying rock again, this time with a small pebble at each corner. She stopped and looked — she always looked, it was now a habit she had not examined.

The chart was detailed. He had done the channel, working from her dive survey the day before yesterday. She had guided them through the channel with expertise and subtle misdirection — too far west on the southern pass, too far east on the northern entry, so that any diver following the chart would find the channel but miss the pockets where the beds sat.

She looked at the scale notation in the corner.

The scale for the channel section was marked differently from the rest of the chart. By a small but definite amount — roughly five per cent. She had surveyed enough maps to see it immediately.

If someone used the channel section coordinates from this chart and applied them to a corrected full-atoll scale, the positions would be off. Not by much — fifty metres, perhaps seventy — but in a lagoon where the beds sat in pockets no larger than twenty metres across, fifty metres was the difference between finding the bed and missing it entirely.

She stood for a moment. Then she picked up the pebble and set it back in its position and walked away.

She did not tell him she had seen it. He had not told her he had done it. That seemed correct.

---

Chapter 37: The Ledger Test

Delacroix showed her the ledger on a Thursday morning. He came to the beach where she was working, and he opened it on a flat rock without preamble — the coordinates of both marked beds, written in Arii's tight hand with Delacroix's additions in the margin.

She looked at the numbers. She knew the actual coordinates of both beds — she had been diving them since she was nineteen. The numbers in the ledger were close. Not exact.

He was watching her. She understood immediately: he had shown her the ledger to see if she would react to wrong numbers. This was a test.

She kept her expression neutral. She looked at the numbers as if studying them. She let two seconds pass.

"These are the positions from Arii's dive?" she said.

"Confirmed with the chart triangulation," Delacroix said.

"Mm," she said, and straightened up.

He was still watching.

"You've seen the channel?" she said.

"I've read the survey notes."

"Then you know the depths."

"I know what the chart says."

She met his eyes. He was testing her, but she could test in return. "The chart is accurate in the areas where I guided the survey," she said. "In the areas where Renard worked from the surface, there may be depth discrepancies."

He considered this. "Which areas?"

"You'd need to ask Renard."

He closed the ledger. He was not satisfied, but he had not found what he was looking for. The ledger numbers were wrong — she knew that — but she had not told him so. Which meant he still didn't know whether his numbers were right or whether she had successfully deceived him.

"Thank you, Madame Aroha," he said, pleasantly.

She walked away from the rock and did not look back.

---

Chapter 38: Three Days

The storm had bought three days. That was how she thought of it: a currency.

In those three days:

Father Breton completed his letter to the Bishop and dispatched it with the schooner's mail bag, which Moreau allowed once a week. The letter included the petition. The petition had Manea's mark, Vahine's mark, and the marks of six elders. It had, in a development she had not anticipated, Tupa's mark as well.

Hina went over the channel chart with Émile in what was presented as a verification session. The session was legitimate — she pointed out two genuine survey errors — and in the course of it she confirmed, without stating it directly, that the scale discrepancy she had seen was intentional and that she had observed it and was not going to speak of it.

Delacroix sent a dispatch to Papeete by the same mail bag that carried Father Breton's letter. She did not know what it said.

On the third day, the weather cleared fully. The lagoon went flat and bright. The survey resumed.

She sat in the evening at the lagoon's edge. The stars came out from east to west, the way they always did. The coconut palms moved in the night breeze. The schooner's lamp burned at anchor.

She thought: three days is not enough and it may be enough.

She thought: Émile leaves in a week.

She thought: when he leaves he will take the chart and the chart will do what charts do.

She thought: the lagoon will still be here.

---

Chapter 39: The Mission House

The second storm was smaller than the first — two hours of rain, wind, the fronds against each other. Father Breton opened the mission house to the survey camp personnel who had no adequate shelter. The mission house was solid stone, built by Polynesian hands in 1862, and it held.

Seven people crowded into the main room: Father Breton, Hina, Émile, two of the enlisted men, Delacroix, and Teura, who had been at the camp when the rain came and had no reason to leave.

They sat around the room in the positions of people who had not chosen each other's company. The enlisted men played cards at one end. Delacroix read by the lamp. Father Breton made tea.

Hina and Émile sat near the window that looked over the lagoon. The rain made the lagoon look different — greyer, more opaque. The depths were invisible.

"We leave in a week," he said. Not loudly.

"I know."

"The chart will go to Papeete. Moreau will countersign it."

"Yes."

"Unless Father Breton's letter reaches Papeete in time to create a formal intervention."

"The mail takes three weeks from here," she said. "You leave in one. The countersignature happens in Papeete."

He was quiet. "The timing is poor."

"No," she said. "The timing is what it is."

They sat. The rain came against the window. Delacroix turned a page. The card game at the other end of the room was ongoing.

"I don't know what to say to you," he said. "That isn't inadequate."

She looked at the rain on the glass. "You said you came here to draw a map of a place you hadn't been," she said.

"Yes."

"Have you drawn it?"

He looked at the sketchbook, which he had brought in under his coat. "The official chart, yes. Mostly."

"Not the chart," she said. "Have you drawn this place?"

He opened the sketchbook. He turned to a particular page and held it up.

It was a drawing of the lagoon at evening — the colour he could not quite name, the way the light moved, the line of the atoll against the sky. He had drawn it from memory, she could tell. It was truer than the chart.

"Yes," he said. "I've drawn it."

She looked at it for a moment. Then she looked at the rain.

"Then you'll take something home that Delacroix won't," she said.

---

Chapter 40: What Tupa Wanted

He came to the lagoon's edge at dawn, alone. He sat on the coral rock where fishermen sometimes sat when they were thinking about something they did not want to think about in their houses.

She found him there. She had not sought him out. She was going to the pirogue.

"You haven't asked me again," he said.

She stopped. "About?"

"What I wanted. What I was going to do. You asked once and you left it."

She sat on the rock beside him. The sun was not up yet. The lagoon was the grey of early light.

"You told me you wanted out," she said. "I believed you."

"I do." He looked at the water. "But wanting out and staying aren't the only two things. There are others."

She waited.

"The southern pocket," he said. "I dove it with Arii. I saw its condition. It's better than ten years ago."

"Yes," she said. "Good management."

"Manea's management," he said. "Your management." He was quiet. "The company diver couldn't see it. He was looking for oyster clusters and he found them. But the way the pocket is shaped — the overhang on the south side — he missed the second concentration entirely. It's under the overhang."

She said nothing.

"Arii's coordinates are for the main cluster," he said. "The secondary concentration, under the overhang — he never went there. It wasn't marked."

She sat with this. Tupa had seen it and had said nothing to Arii or to Delacroix. He had let them believe the main cluster was all there was.

"Why?" she said.

He picked up a piece of coral and turned it in his hands. "Because I dove that pocket when I was twenty years old with your mother," he said. "She showed me the overhang. She said it was a gift — that the south pocket had a secret inside itself."

She hadn't known that. Her mother had never mentioned Tupa diving with her.

"I marked my name on the petition," he said. "Teura told me. I thought you should know why."

He set the coral down and stood up. "I'm still leaving," he said. "Delacroix already paid me. But the overhang is yours."

He walked away along the beach. She sat on the rock until the sun came up.

---

Chapter 41: The Final Documents

Moreau spread the documents on the camp table. They were the completed survey documents: the atoll chart with all its detail, the resource enumeration, the depth survey notes. They were ready for countersignature.

Delacroix stood to one side. Émile stood to the other.

Hina was not in the camp. She was watching from the copra shed.

Moreau picked up his pen. He was going to sign. That was clear from his posture — the set of the shoulder, the forward lean.

Émile said something.

Moreau straightened. He said something back.

The conversation went on for several minutes. She could not hear it at this distance. She watched the shapes of it: Émile with his hands at his sides, speaking steadily; Moreau with his pen still in hand; Delacroix very still.

After ten minutes, Moreau set the pen down. He picked up the documents and walked into his tent.

Hina stayed where she was. Émile did not look her way.

---

Chapter 42: The Refusal

She found him that evening.

He was at his own tent, packing. The chart roll was on the cot. The sketchbook was in his coat pocket.

"What did you say?" she said.

"I said the depth survey of the interior channel was incomplete. That there were access issues I had documented and that the channel coordinates in the current chart were insufficiently verified." He folded a shirt. "I said I could not in good conscience sign a survey document with unverified interior coordinates."

"That was your refusal to countersign."

"I am the survey cartographer. My signature is required on the chart before Moreau can countersign it as naval officer. I refused."

She looked at him. "Moreau can force you."

"Moreau can order me," he said. "Whether that becomes force is a different question. He is not certain he has the authority, and I told him I was not certain he had the authority, and I used language close enough to what he's heard that he is now examining the mandate language." He paused. "He went to read the mandate language."

"For how long will that buy time?"

"Days," he said. "The mandate language is not actually ambiguous. He'll conclude that he can order me. Then he'll order me."

"Days," she said.

"Father Breton's letter is in the mail," he said. "Manea's petition is in the mail. What those documents trigger — I can't predict. But they're in motion."

She sat on the folding stool. "You've put yourself in a very difficult position," she said.

"I know."

"Delacroix will make sure it costs you."

"Probably." He folded the second shirt. "I find I care about that less than I expected to."

She looked at him folding shirts. He was a careful person — everything done with precision, each shirt flat, each edge aligned. She thought about the drawing in the sketchbook. She thought about the corrected scale in the channel chart.

"Émile," she said.

He looked up.

"Thank you," she said.

He held the shirt for a moment. Then he went back to folding. "Don't thank me," he said. "The documents are in the mail. What the mail does from here is not in my control."

"I know," she said. "Thank you anyway."

---

Chapter 43: The Petition

She walked into the camp the next morning with the petition. She had Manea behind her — he had insisted on coming despite his slow movement — and Vahine beside her, and two elders.

She did not arrange this. Manea had arranged it.

Moreau was at his table. Delacroix was present. Émile was present.

Hina placed the petition on the table.

It was a single page, written in French and Paumotu side by side, in her hand and Father Breton's. It stated that the community of Avatoru, Tuamotu Archipelago, asserted customary and established usage rights over the interior lagoon pearl beds as a collective resource managed across four generations. It named the beds. It stated the history of their management. It asked that the colonial administration recognize those rights before exercising exploitation authority.

It bore the marks of Manea, Vahine, six elders, Father Breton, and — at the bottom, in a slightly different hand — Tupa.

Moreau read it. He read it carefully, which she had not expected.

He said: "Customary rights are not recognized under the current administration."

"I know," she said. "The petition requests recognition."

"The administration cannot recognize what the law does not authorize it to recognize."

"The petition is addressed to the administration," she said. "Not to the current law. Laws change. Records persist."

Delacroix looked at the petition. He looked at Tupa's mark at the bottom. Something shifted in his expression — not visible to anyone who hadn't been watching him carefully.

"This will have no legal effect," Delacroix said.

"I know," she said. "Will you enter it into the official record?"

A pause. Moreau picked up the petition. "It will be attached to the survey documents filed in Papeete," he said. "As a supplementary exhibit." He looked at Delacroix.

"As you wish," Delacroix said, with the particular flatness of a man absorbing a setback he had not anticipated.

---

Chapter 44: Jurisdiction

Delacroix said it clearly, without anger. He said it in the way he did everything — as a matter of practical information.

"The Bishop has no jurisdiction over commercial surveying," he said. He said it to the room — not to Hina, not to Father Breton, but to the air, as if establishing a fact for the record. "Pastoral concern for communities is a matter for the Church. Resource rights are a matter for the colonial administration. Those are separate jurisdictions."

Father Breton, sitting at the table with his tea, said: "Jurisdiction is not the same as influence."

"In matters of law, it often is."

"In matters of law," Father Breton said. "I'm speaking of matters of policy. The Governor is not a judge. He makes decisions. Decisions are influenced by things other than legal mandates."

Delacroix looked at him. "The Governor will not reverse a commercial rights grant because a missionary in the Tuamotus wrote a letter."

"No," Father Breton said. "But he may hesitate to confirm it while a formal ecclesiastical representation is pending. Hesitation has its own effects."

Hina sat and said nothing. This was their argument, these two. She had introduced both letters into the world. Now the letters were speaking for themselves.

Moreau was present but silent. He had read the petition. He had read Émile's objection. He had read the mandate language. He was a man with a mission who had discovered that his mission was more complicated than he had been told.

Delacroix picked up his satchel. "The survey will be completed," he said. "The chart will be signed. The rights will be exercised." He said this calmly, as a prediction. "The timing may shift by weeks. Not more."

He left.

Father Breton drank his tea.

---

Chapter 45: The Bishop's Letter

Three days before the survey party was due to leave, a vessel came through the pass carrying mail from Papeete. The mail bag included a letter for Moreau, from the Governor's office.

Moreau read it alone in his tent. Hina knew this because the enlisted men were outside, not inside, and Moreau did not call anyone.

An hour later he called Délacroix.

She waited. She sat on the beach and she waited with the patience her mother had called the diver's second breath — the calm after the panic, when you had stopped struggling against the water and let it carry you.

She found out eventually, from Father Breton, who found out from the camp's enlisted man, who had heard through the canvas wall.

The Governor's letter was not a reversal of the exploitation rights. It was a request — from the Bishop, passed through the Governor's office as a formal inquiry — for clarification of the survey methodology, specifically regarding the interior channel depth survey, which the Bishop's letter had characterized as inadequately verified.

The letter requested that the countersignature be held pending a satisfactory methodological response from the survey team.

Pending.

The countersignature was pending.

---

Chapter 46: Tupa's Mark

She found Tupa sitting at the south end of the beach in the early morning, where he had been sitting when she found him before. He seemed to like the south end at this hour.

"The delay," she said, sitting beside him.

"I heard."

"Your mark on the petition."

He looked at the water. "What about it?"

"Delacroix saw it. He didn't expect it."

"No," Tupa said. "I don't imagine he did."

She looked at him. "Your mark on the petition and your testimony — that you dove the beds for Delacroix's diver, and that the channel survey was incomplete — that's the testimony that carries the most weight. You are not Manea's niece. You are not the village chief. You are the man who was inside the company's operation and decided to say something."

He was quiet.

"Moreau is going to ask you," she said. "Formally. Officially. He'll want a statement from you about what you saw when you dove with Arii. What the survey process actually looked like."

"And if I give it?"

"Then the methodological question has an eyewitness. The Bishop's inquiry has a source. The pending becomes more pending."

He picked up a shell from the sand. "Delacroix already paid me," he said.

"I know."

"He'll say I took his money and then betrayed him."

"Yes," she said. "He will say that."

Tupa turned the shell in his hands. She waited. She had learned, with him, to wait.

"He can say it," Tupa said. He set the shell down. "He can say whatever he likes."

He rose. He was going to give the statement. She could see it in the way he stood — resolved, in the way men were when they had decided something they could not un-decide.

"Tupa," she said.

He turned.

"The overhang in the southern pocket," she said. "When this is over — if it's ever over — I'll take you there again."

He looked at her for a long moment. He did not say anything. But something in his face changed.

---

Chapter 47: Moreau's Hesitation

Moreau sent for her on the twenty-second day.

He sat at his table with the survey documents in front of him. The chart was rolled. The resource enumeration was in its folder. The countersignature block was blank.

He said: "I have a formal inquiry from the Governor's office regarding the survey methodology. Specifically the channel depth survey."

"I know," she said.

"I have a statement from Tupa — a member of this community who participated in the dive survey — raising questions about the completeness of the channel survey. His statement says the survey missed significant formations."

"What are your conclusions?" she said.

Moreau looked at the documents. He was not a man who second-guessed easily — she had seen that in him from the beginning. But he was also not a man who wanted his career disrupted by a procedural failure that turned out to be substantive.

"My conclusions are that the chart as currently constituted may not satisfy the methodological requirements attached to the exploitation rights grant," he said carefully. "Which means that countersigning a chart of uncertain completeness would expose the survey — and myself — to questions that I am not positioned to answer."

She said nothing.

"I am going to delay the countersignature," he said. "Pending further review. The petition and the ancillary documents will be attached to the survey package as supplementary exhibits. The package will go to Papeete for administrative review." He looked up at her. "This does not resolve anything."

"No," she said. "It does not."

"Delacroix's rights are still in place. The beds are still in the survey area. When the administrative review concludes — and it will conclude — the chart will be countersigned and the rights will be exercised."

"I know," she said.

"Then why—" He stopped. "What have you achieved?"

She looked at the blank countersignature block. "Months," she said. "Maybe more. The administrative review in Papeete, the Bishop's formal representation, any questions that arise from the petition — those take time. During that time the beds are undamaged."

"And then?"

"And then we'll see," she said. "The world changes. Laws change. People in Paris who have never heard of this atoll may, in a year or two or five, become people who have."

He looked at her for a long moment. "You are a very patient woman," he said.

"My mother was a diver," she said. "She told me patience is what you do between breaths."

---

Chapter 48: Delacroix's Exit

He came to find her the morning of the survey party's departure. The schooner was loading. Equipment cases were being carried down the beach. The camp was already half-dismantled.

He stood at his customary distance. He had, she realized, always been careful about distance — never invading, never retreating, always at the precise range that gave him the most information while committing the least.

"You've won a few months," he said.

She had expected recrimination. What she got instead was the plain statement, which was worse because it was accurate.

"Yes," she said.

"The administrative review will conclude. The chart will be countersigned."

"Perhaps."

"Not perhaps," he said. "The law is clear. The rights are granted. The review is procedural." He paused. "I want you to understand something. I don't enjoy this."

She looked at him.

"This is not personal," he said. "I am not here because I want to damage your community. I'm here because pearl beds are worth money and the company has legal rights to that money. If there were a way to do this that didn't damage what you have —" He paused. "There isn't. That's not sentiment. It's the economics of extraction."

"I know," she said.

"Then you know also that when I come back —"

"If you come back."

He tilted his head. "If I come back — and I will — you will not have more options than you have now."

"No," she said. "But I'll have more time to find them."

He picked up his satchel. He was going to leave. At the waterline, his dinghy was ready.

He looked at her one more time. "You should have taken the fifteen per cent," he said. There was something in his voice that was not quite regret.

"And let the beds go in ten years," she said.

"Yes."

"No," she said.

He walked to the dinghy. She watched him go.

---

Chapter 49: The Last Morning

He came to find her the morning he left. The schooner was riding at anchor, the crew ready. The tide was running out through the pass — the best time to leave.

He walked down the beach to where she was standing at the water's edge. He had the map case under his arm and the sketchbook in his coat. He looked like what he was: a man about to leave.

"I'll write," he said.

She looked at the schooner.

"To the survey administration," he said. "From wherever I'm posted next. I'll raise the methodology questions formally, in writing, as part of the official survey record." He paused. "It probably won't change the outcome."

"No," she said. "Probably not."

"But it will be in the record. Everything is in the record now."

She looked at him. He looked at the lagoon. The morning was clear — the first clear morning in several days, the sky a clean pale blue with no cloud.

"The drawing," she said. "The one you did of this place. From memory."

"Yes."

"Don't lose it," she said.

He touched the pocket where the sketchbook was. "I won't," he said.

They stood. The schooner waited. Somewhere up the beach, the enlisted men were carrying the last of the equipment.

She thought about what to say. She found there was not much — not because there was nothing, but because the things she might say were too large for the beach and the morning and the time available. He was leaving. The chart was leaving with him. The petition was in the mail. The Bishop's letter was working its slow way through the administration.

"Thank you," she said. "For the scale."

He was quiet for a moment. "I don't know what you mean," he said.

"Yes you do," she said.

He almost smiled. "Goodbye, Hina Aroha."

"Goodbye, Émile Renard."

He walked away. She watched him until he was at the dinghy. He did not look back. She had not expected him to. Men who looked back, in her experience, were men who had not yet decided to leave. He had decided.

She stood at the water's edge for a long time after the schooner moved.

---

Chapter 50: The Water Still

She went out alone the next morning.

Before dawn. The lagoon flat, the stars still present in the east. She paddled the pirogue through the dark in silence, by memory, to the channel's mouth.

She tied the pirogue to the coral head she had been using for years, the one shaped like a hand with two fingers raised. She sat for a moment.

The sky was beginning to lighten. She could feel it rather than see it — a quality change in the dark, the black becoming grey in the east, the stars going first in the direction of sunrise.

She slid into the water.

The temperature was warm near the surface, cooler at five metres, cooler still at eight. She moved without urgency. She was not in a hurry. The dive was not for any purpose outside itself.

She went to the northern bed first. Down through the clear dark, her lamp illuminating the channel wall. The bed was as it had been — dense, undisturbed, the shells in their clusters along the sandy bottom. She moved along it with her hands just above the surface of it, reading.

She surfaced. Breathed. Down again.

She did not go to the middle channel bed. That bed was marked. What would come of the marking, she did not know. But she could not undo it, and going there now felt like an act of grieving she was not yet ready for.

She went to the southern pocket.

Twelve metres. Thirteen. The bowl of collapsed coral, the familiar ceiling, the light from the lamp moving over the familiar floor. She moved around the main cluster, counting by touch. Then she moved to the south wall.

Under the overhang.

Her mother had brought her here. Hina had been nineteen, just learning the deep dives, and her mother had taken her to this precise spot and guided her hand under the coral shelf. The secondary concentration — smaller shells, more densely packed, the kind that grew in each other's shelter.

She moved along the overhang now. The shells were there. Undisturbed. Arii had not found this. Delacroix had not found this. The storm and the delays and the scale and the petition — all of it had bought something.

She surfaced.

The sky was orange in the east. The atoll's palms were dark against it. The village was beginning to move — she could hear it, faintly, across the water.

She floated on her back and looked up. The last stars going out, one by one, as the light increased. The frigate birds beginning their morning circuits. The smell of the lagoon — salt, coral, the slight mineral sharpness of deep water.

The schooner was gone. Delacroix was gone. Émile's chart was on its way to Papeete, with the petition attached and the methodology questions filed and the Bishop's formal representation somewhere in the administrative machinery.

The beds were here.

She had not saved them. She did not believe in the word saved for something that was still in process, that would remain in process long after this morning. She had given them time. She had given the village time. She had put things into the record that had not been there before.

The rest was the rest.

She climbed back into the pirogue and picked up the paddle.

The water was still. The sky was brightening. The palms were beginning to catch colour. She paddled toward the village, toward Manea's house and Teura's red fabric and Tupa's packed bag and Father Breton's garden and the goat named Voltaire. She paddled toward the day's work, which was the only work that was ever available: the work of the day that was actually in front of you.

The lagoon spread wide around her. The coral heads caught the early light. Below the surface, in the dark of the channel, the oysters sat in their beds undisturbed.

The question open. The water still.
