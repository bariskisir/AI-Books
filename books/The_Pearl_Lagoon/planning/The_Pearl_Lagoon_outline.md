# The Pearl Lagoon — Outline

## Premise

1873, Tuamotu Archipelago, French Polynesia. Hina Aroha, 34, daughter of a Tahitian mother and a French trader father, is one of the most skilled pearl divers in the lagoon community of Avatoru. When a French colonial survey team arrives to map the archipelago's resources — including its pearl beds — Hina must navigate between the surveyor's charts, which will expose the lagoon's richest beds to corporate harvest, and her community's desperate need for trade revenue. Her guarded alliance with the team's cartographer, who has his own misgivings about empire, grows complicated as the arrival of a company agent threatens to upend everyone's plans.

## Core Cast

- **Hina Aroha**, 34 — Pearl diver and community negotiator, daughter of a Tahitian mother and French trader father. Divided loyalties, deep knowledge of the reef.
- **Émile Renard**, 31 — French cartographer attached to the survey team, privately conflicted about his role in colonial expansion.
- **Manea**, 58 — Hina's uncle and the village chief of Avatoru, cautious and traditional, keeper of the lagoon's unwritten laws.
- **Delacroix**, 47 — Company agent from Papeete, cold and methodical, determined to secure the pearl beds for commercial exploitation.
- **Teura**, 26 — Hina's younger sister, more trusting of the French presence, lured by the idea of trade and change.
- **Father Breton**, 62 — Marianist missionary who has lived on the atoll for twelve years; his loyalties run in complicated directions.
- **Tupa**, 40 — The island's most experienced diver before Hina surpassed him; resentful, unpredictable, and susceptible to manipulation.

## 50-Chapter Plan

### Act I: The Survey Arrives (Chapters 1–10)

1. Hina surfaces from a deep dive at dawn, a black pearl clutched in her fist, alone on the lagoon as the first light touches the coral heads.
2. A schooner anchors in Avatoru's passage — the colonial survey vessel — and Hina watches from the shore as strangers unload equipment.
3. Manea convenes the village elders; they debate whether to welcome or resist the surveyors, and Hina is asked to serve as interpreter.
4. Émile Renard sets up his plane table on the beach and works in methodical silence while Hina observes his instruments without approaching.
5. Tupa confronts Hina, angry that she was chosen as interpreter rather than him, and reminds her that her French blood is not a gift.
6. Father Breton visits the survey camp, blessing the work and smoothing the first awkward exchanges between the French officers and villagers.
7. Hina speaks to Émile for the first time, correcting his mispronounced place names on the chart; he listens without defensiveness.
8. The survey team's leader, Lieutenant Moreau, demands a full enumeration of the lagoon's pearl beds as part of the official survey mandate.
9. Manea privately tells Hina that the three richest beds — those the community depends on — must never appear on any French map.
10. Teura trades a necklace of small pearls to one of the French sailors for a bolt of red calico, and Hina sees the danger in her sister's delight.

### Act II: Alliances and Tensions (Chapters 11–20)

11. Émile asks Hina to take him out on the lagoon in a pirogue so he can triangulate the reef formations, and she agrees on her own terms.
12. On the water, Émile reveals that he joined the survey under pressure from his father and had hoped to be a painter instead.
13. Hina dives to show Émile the shallower beds, deliberately keeping the pirogue away from the deep channel where the best oysters grow.
14. Tupa speaks privately to Lieutenant Moreau, hinting that he knows where the richest beds lie and that Hina is concealing them.
15. Father Breton warns Hina that Tupa has been talking with the officers; he asks her what she plans to do about it.
16. Delacroix arrives on a second vessel, unannounced; his presence shifts the atmosphere of the survey camp from curiosity to something harder.
17. Delacroix meets with Manea, presenting letters of authority from Papeete that give his company first rights of exploitation over any charted beds.
18. Hina studies the partial charts Émile has left drying on the table while he eats; she understands now exactly what information is missing.
19. Émile discovers that Hina read his charts and instead of anger, he asks what she saw — a conversation that lasts past dark.
20. Manea falls ill; his illness is minor but it leaves Hina as the effective voice of the village at a critical moment.

### Act III: The Company's Hand (Chapters 21–30)

21. Delacroix approaches Hina directly with a proposal: guide him to the deep beds and he will guarantee the village a share of the harvest revenue.
22. Hina asks Delacroix why a company that could take everything is willing to share; he says she is asking the wrong question.
23. Émile confides to Hina that Delacroix's company has exploited pearl beds on three other atolls to exhaustion; he has seen the reports.
24. Tupa makes a private deal with Delacroix, agreeing to dive the deep channel and mark the bed coordinates in exchange for money and passage to Tahiti.
25. Hina confronts Tupa; he tells her she has always treated the lagoon as her private property and that he owes her nothing.
26. Father Breton hosts an evening meal and brings both Hina and Émile to his table; over rum and breadfruit, old alignments begin to shift.
27. Émile shows Hina a letter he has drafted to his superior in Papeete, questioning the legality of the exploitation rights granted to Delacroix's company.
28. Delacroix finds out about the letter; he summons Émile and makes clear, without direct threat, what the consequences of insubordination look like.
29. Teura tells Hina she has agreed to work as a laundress for the survey camp — for wages — and that Hina has no right to forbid her.
30. The survey team completes their above-water charts of the atoll; Delacroix requests that the underwater beds be formally surveyed next.

### Act IV: Depths and Decisions (Chapters 31–40)

31. Hina dives the deep channel alone at night, in the dark, to re-memorize every feature — the shape of the bottom, the current, the distance from the pass.
32. Émile asks to accompany Hina on one more lagoon excursion and brings his sketchbook rather than his instruments, drawing her face instead of the reef.
33. Tupa dives the deep channel with Delacroix's diver and marks the beds with buoys; by evening the positions have been noted in the company ledger.
34. Hina tells Manea what Tupa has done; the old man sits with it for a long time and then says the beds are marked but the lagoon is not yet lost.
35. Father Breton reveals to Hina that he has been writing reports to the Bishop in Papeete arguing against industrial exploitation; he asks if she wants him to do more.
36. Émile alters the scale of the chart covering the deep channel, making the coordinates slightly but measurably wrong — a small act of sabotage.
37. Delacroix shows Hina the company ledger with Tupa's marked positions; she realizes he already knows the correct locations and is testing her.
38. A storm blows through the lagoon, scattering Delacroix's buoys and damaging the survey vessel; three days pass without work.
39. During the storm, Hina and Émile shelter together in Father Breton's mission house; they talk through the night and nothing is resolved between them.
40. After the storm, Tupa approaches Hina and says he wants to undo what he has done; she asks him how and he has no answer.

### Act V: Resolution and Cost (Chapters 41–50)

41. Delacroix presents the final survey documents to Lieutenant Moreau for official countersignature; the exploitation rights will be lodged in Papeete.
42. Émile refuses to sign as the survey cartographer, citing errors in the charting process that require verification; Delacroix's timeline stalls.
43. Hina appears before Moreau and Delacroix with a counter-document: a petition signed by Manea and the village elders asserting customary rights to the lagoon beds.
44. Delacroix reminds the room that customary rights are not recognized under the current French colonial administration — and that the petition is without legal force.
45. Father Breton arrives with a copy of the Bishop's letter opposing industrial exploitation; Delacroix reads it, sets it aside, and says the church has no jurisdiction over commercial surveying.
46. Tupa, in a surprise move, adds his mark to the village petition; his testimony as the man who dove the beds for Delacroix's diver carries unexpected weight.
47. Moreau, uncomfortable with the conflict and aware that formal opposition has been lodged, delays the countersignature pending instructions from Papeete.
48. Delacroix leaves on the next vessel south, the documents unsigned but not abandoned; he tells Hina she has won a few months, not a future.
49. Émile stays on the atoll for two more weeks before his orders pull him north; on his last morning he and Hina walk the beach without speaking very much.
50. The schooner leaves; Hina returns to the lagoon, dives to the deep channel, and finds the oysters undisturbed — the harvest incomplete, the question open, the water still.
