Chapter 1: The Taste of Morning

Before the eye, the arms.

She woke the way she always woke, from the outside in, the suckers along her arms reporting the world before the rest of her agreed to have one. The crack in the limestone held her folded into itself, soft body packed into hard space, and the first thing she knew each day was the taste of the water moving past the mouth of the den — colder near dawn, threaded with the particular green bitterness of the weed beds to the east, and under that the old mineral flatness of the rock she lived in.

The second arm tasted something the others did not: a smear of crab on the stone where she had eaten the night before, gone sour now, not worth the keeping. The arm reported it and lost interest. She did not have to tell it to. Her arms knew a great deal on their own and bothered her with only some of it, and the bothering and the not-bothering was most of what it was to be her.

Light came down through the water in a slow grey wash, and now the eye opened, the horizontal slot of the pupil widening, and the den mouth resolved into edges. She did not see the green of the weed or the red of her own skin; color was not a thing the eye did. The eye did light and dark, the angle and the polish of light, the shiver of it off scales and sand. Color happened elsewhere, under the skin, where it belonged to thinking and not to seeing.

She unfolded one arm into the current and held it there, tasting the morning, deciding nothing yet, in no hurry. Hunger was a low note, not yet a chord. The reef was waking around her in its layers. She lay in her crack and let the day arrive through eight arms, and was, in the plain animal completeness of that, entirely herself and entirely content.

Chapter 2: The Crab

Hunger became a chord, and she went hunting.

She flowed out of the den — that was the only word for how a body like hers left a space, a pour, a boneless reorganizing — and crossed the open rubble below the rock with her arms spread wide over the stones, reading them. A crab leaves a taste. It leaves the chemistry of itself on everything it touches, and her arms picked the trail up off the rubble the way the eye might pick a moving thing out of stillness, and she followed it not with her eye but with her skin, low and flat, the color draining out of her until she matched the pale grit beneath her.

The crab was under a flat stone, a good one, broad-shelled, worth the work. She did not rush. Rushing lost more meals than patience ever did. She let two arms drift past the stone's edge, idle, harmless, while the rest of her went still and grey, and the crab, reassured by the stillness, came out to feed, and the moment its weight cleared the stone she was on it.

The strike was the whole body at once. The web between her arms ballooned down over the crab and the suckers had it and the crab's claws found her and pinched — a bright hurt, two arms flushing dark with it — but it did not matter, it was already over. She turned the crab in her arms, found the seam of it with the hard beak hidden at the center of her, and bit through. She backed under a ledge to eat, the way she always ate, hidden, holding the crab close while the suckers tasted its insides and the good warmth of the meal spread through her, and the dark flush of the pinched arms faded back to the easy mottled brown of a body that has fed and is, for now, satisfied.

Chapter 3: The Cold Below

Night changed the reef into another country, and some nights she went down into the cold to taste it.

The day reef was a place of ledges and hiding, of the eye, of matching the rock. The night reef was a place of the open, when the things that hunted by sight were blind and the water filled with small lives that glowed. She left the rubble and jetted out over the drop-off, the mantle drawing in and firing a slug of water behind her so that she shot forward in long glides, arms trailing, the reef falling away beneath her into blue that became black.

The cold came up to meet her. It had a taste, the deep cold, a cleanness, an absence of the reef's green clutter, and she liked it the way she liked the brass-bright pull of certain objects, without knowing why. Around her the small glowing things rose and pulsed, and she passed through them and they lit and faded, and once a larger shape moved far below her in the dark and she held still in the open water, every arm gone watchful, until it was gone.

She did not stay long in the deep. It was not hers; it was only a thing she tasted now and then, the way she sometimes pressed an arm into a crevice she had no intention of living in, just to know it. She turned in the black and rose, and the reef came back up around her, ledge by ledge, warmer and greener and busier, until she was home in the world she knew, and she folded into a crevice for the rest of the night with the cold of the deep still pleasant on her skin, like a color she had worn and put away.

Chapter 4: The Curved Clay

She found the wreck on a day she had gone too far west, hunting, and stumbled onto its edge by accident, which is how she found most things.

The sand here was different — finer, paler, undisturbed by the surge that worked the reef proper — and across it lay shapes she had no place for. They were curved. Not the curve of a shell or the curve of a stone worn round, but a made curve, the same curve repeated, long bellied forms half-sunk in the sand, some whole and some broken open, the color of old bone, furred with weed and crusted at the lips with the small white tubes of worms.

She stopped at the edge of the scatter and went still and read it from a distance with the chemistry on the current. It tasted of nothing alive and nothing dangerous. It tasted of clay and rust and time, of surfaces so old the sea had half-forgotten they were not itself. And yet something in the geometry of it pulled at her, the repetition of those bellied shapes, the way they lay in a long line as if poured from somewhere above and frozen — a pattern, and patterns were the one thing in the world that made her stupid with curiosity.

She did not enter the scatter that day. She lay at its edge for a long time with two arms extended onto the pale sand, tasting the nearest of the curved shapes, learning the cool smooth clay of it, the worm-tubes, the give of the silt. Then hunger called her back east to the rubble she knew. But she carried the shapes with her, lodged under the skin somewhere, and she knew already, in the wordless way she knew things, that she would come back.

Chapter 5: The Long Neighbor

She learned the moray the way one learns a hard rule, by breaking it once.

His crevice was in the reef's broken shoulder, a deep horizontal slot, and she did not know it was his. She was crossing the shoulder low and curious, arms spread, when one arm found the slot and slipped inside to taste it — a good space, she was thinking, deep, defensible — and the dark came out of the slot at her.

It came faster than anything she had a measure for. A long body, thick as her own mantle and longer than all of her stretched out, the jaw hinged back, the small eye flat and ancient. The strike caught the questing arm and tore it, a white shock of pain, and she did not think, there was no time to think; the mantle fired and she shot backward in a hard jet, ink blooming behind her in a dark false body, and she pressed herself flat and small and poured into a gap in the rock too narrow for the long body to follow.

She lay in the gap with the torn arm curled against her, its taste of her own blood in the water, and watched the slot. The moray had not pursued. He had only flowed back into his dark, his point made, his border drawn. She understood it the way she understood the surge and the cold — not as cruelty, simply as the shape of the world. The reef had a long neighbor in its shoulder, and the slot was his, and she would not put an arm in it again. The torn arm would heal; her kind grew their arms back, slowly, and she would carry the lesson in it as it regrew. She left him his dark and went home, wiser, down one good arm and up one rule.

Chapter 6: The Doorstep

A body like hers needs a door it can close, and so she built one.

She had outgrown the limestone crack — a body grows, and a den that fit last month binds this month — and she spent three nights testing new spaces along the rubble. The first was too open at the back; a thing could come in behind her, and an arm laid along the rear wall told her so and she left it. The second was good but the surge worked it, a pulse of cold water shoving through with each set of waves that would never let her rest, and she left that too. The third was right: a domed space under a tilted slab, one mouth, solid at the back, the surge broken by a shoulder of rock before it reached her.

She moved in and then she made it hers, which meant the doorstep. She hauled rubble to the mouth, stone by stone, choosing them — a flat shell, a knuckle of coral, a smooth water-rounded pebble she carried from a long way off because the eye liked the polish of it — and she built them into a low wall across the bottom of the den mouth, a midden, a threshold, so that she could draw the larger stones across the gap behind her and be enclosed.

There was a deep satisfaction in it that had nothing to do with safety, though it was also safety. It was the satisfaction of arrangement, of a space ordered to fit a body, of the stones lying where she had decided stones should lie. When it was done she packed herself into the dome with the door half-drawn and felt the particular ease that came only from enclosure, the world reduced to one taste-able mouth, and she rested there better than she had rested in weeks, behind a wall of her own building.

Chapter 7: The Grouper's Pressure

She lost the good den to a thing she never fought, because some things you do not fight, you only yield to.

The grouper came into her stretch of reef the way weather comes, without announcing itself, simply present one morning where it had not been. It was vast — a slab of a fish, wider than she was long, with a mouth that did not bite so much as engulf, a sudden cavern of suction that could take a thing her size whole if it caught her in the open. She felt it before she saw it, the pressure of its movement through the water, a heavy slow displacement that her whole skin read as a single word: large.

It took up a station on the rock above her den. Not hunting her, exactly — groupers hunted by patience and ambush, hanging still and then inhaling whatever passed too close — but its station was over her doorstep, and that was enough. She could not leave to hunt without crossing under that waiting mouth. She held in the den for a day and a night, hungry, the door drawn, while the great fish hung above her like a low ceiling.

In the end she did the only sensible thing, which was to give up the den. She waited for the grouper to drift to the far side of its station and she poured out of the mouth low and grey and fast, hugging the rubble, and she did not stop and she did not look back and she did not mourn the doorstep she had built stone by stone, because mourning was not a thing she did. She found a poor temporary crack two reefs over and packed into it, hungry and homeless, and began, without complaint, the work of scouting all over again. The reef gave and the reef took. She had learned that with her first torn arm. She simply started again.

Chapter 8: Storm-Light

The storm announced itself first as a change in the light, and then as a change in everything.

High up, where the water met the air she had never breathed and never would, something turned the bright ceiling to a churned grey, and the light that came down went strange and flickering, and the surge began to build. She felt it in her den, the new violence in the water, the back-and-forth growing until it was not a sway but a shove, sand lifting off the bottom in sheets, the whole reef gone milky with suspended grit.

She did the only thing there was to do. She found the deepest, tightest crevice within reach and she poured into it and she anchored. Two arms wedged into cracks at the back, the suckers locking, and she made herself heavy and small while the sea above her lost its mind. The surge tried to pull her out and she let it pull and did not move, holding by suction and by patience while the grit scoured past and the cold deep water churned up into the warm and the small noises of the reef were swallowed in the low roar of the water working.

It passed, as everything passed. The light steadied. The surge fell back to its old sway. She unwedged herself, stiff, and came out into a reef that had been rearranged in the night — a ledge collapsed, a familiar stone gone, the sand redrawn in new ripples, the smell of torn weed everywhere. The wreck, she would find later, had shifted too; the storm moved even the old buried things. For now she only flowed out into the changed reef and re-tasted it, patient, learning the new shape of her world the way she learned everything, one arm at a time, taking the sea exactly as it came.

Chapter 9: The Slow Ones

She saw the slow ones for the first time from under a ledge, and she did not understand them, and she never would, and that was the beginning of the only thing in her life that came close to wonder.

They came down from above, where things did not come from. Lights came first, hard white beams swinging through the blue, and then the shapes behind the lights resolved as they sank — enormous, far larger than the grouper, dark-bodied, trailing strings of silver bubbles that rose roaring back toward the bright ceiling. They moved wrong. Everything in her world moved with the water, fast or slow but always with it; these moved against it, slow and deliberate and clumsy, hanging in the open as if the open held no danger for them, kicking long flat limbs.

She pressed back under her ledge and went the still mottled grey of the rock and watched. Her whole skin was one held question. They were not predators in any shape she knew; they did not hunt; they hung in the water and swung their lights and made their roaring bubbles and disturbed nothing. One of them settled near the bottom not far from her and seemed, impossibly, to be simply looking, the great dark head turning slowly, the light moving over the reef and across her ledge and — she froze to absolute stone — over her, and past her, not seeing, and on.

After a long while they rose again, up the columns of their own bubbles, and were gone into the bright nothing overhead. She stayed under the ledge a long time after. She had no category for them. They were not food and not danger and not weather, and everything in her world had until now been one of those three. The slow ones were a fourth thing, and the fourth thing, once it had come, did not leave her skin.

Chapter 10: A Day Entire

This was a day, and her days were like this, and there were not so very many of them, though she did not know that yet.

She woke folded in the poor temporary crack, tasted the morning, found the hunger still low, and waited. When the hunger rose she hunted — a clam this time, prised from the sand with three arms working the shell while a fourth braced and the beak found the hinge — and she ate it under a ledge and was satisfied. She cleaned herself afterward, running the arms through one another, picking the grit from the suckers, a long idle grooming that was its own small pleasure.

In the warm middle of the day, with nothing needed, she explored. There was no purpose in it; she went to the western sand and tasted the curved clay shapes again because they pulled at her, and she lay at the edge of the wreck for an hour doing nothing but learning it a little more. She watched a wrasse work a cleaning station. She let a current carry her and jetted back against it twice, for no reason, simply to feel the firing of the mantle and the glide.

When the light began to slant she thought about a better den than the poor crack, and filed the thought, and ate again, smaller — a shrimp, barely worth the catching — and as the dark came down she found a crevice she half-trusted and drew a stone across part of the mouth and packed herself in. The fear came and went, as it always did, the low watchfulness of a soft thing in a hard world. The curiosity came and went. The hunger filled and emptied. She did not have words for any of it and did not need them. She was an intelligence the size of a single bright life, wanting and wary and curious by turns, and she lived her day entire and lay down in the dark at the end of it, and that was enough, and there would be more of them, until there were not.

Chapter 11: Inside the Wreck

She entered the wreck the way she did everything important, slowly, and from below.

The curved clay shapes that had pulled at her from the edge were, close up, a city of hollows. She moved in among them with her arms spread and her skin gone the pale fawn of the sand, reading each one as she passed. Some lay whole on their sides, mouths gaping, dark inside; some had cracked along their bellies and spilled their old silence into the silt. The clay was cool and slightly rough and tasted of nothing alive — of mineral, of age, of the long patient work of the sea unmaking a made thing.

She learned, that first proper night inside, that the hollow pots had voices. Not voices as the slow ones surely had voices; but when she poured into the mouth of a broken amphora and the current crossed its lip, the whole shape hummed, a low resonance she felt in her skin and in the water against her mantle, each pot tuned to its own size, so that the wreck in a moving current was not silent at all but full of these deep clay notes sounding under the reef-noise. She went from one to the next and let each one hum against her, fascinated, a creature made stupid by pattern moving through a field of buried instruments she could not know were instruments.

She did not take a den there yet. The wreck was too strange, too open, too full of dark mouths that might hold their own long neighbors. But she stayed until the dawn began, drifting among the amphorae, tasting the clay, learning the geography of the scatter, and when she finally went home east to her poor crack she did so reluctantly, already planning the next night's return, already half-belonging to the place.

Chapter 12: The Bright Heavy Thing

In the heart of the wreck she found the thing that would, in the end, be the last thing she ever looked at, though of course she could not know that, and would not have changed her course if she had.

It lay half-buried in silt where the densest scatter of amphorae gave way to a tumble of corroded shapes — the harder remains, the things that had been metal. Most of them were furred orange with rust and tasted of it, a sharp ferrous bite on the suckers. But this one was different. Where the silt had been disturbed — by the storm, perhaps, that moved even the buried things — a face of it showed clear, and the eye caught the polish of it: not the dull of clay or the fur of rust but a green-dark gleam, a surface that took the slanting light and bent it, threw it back changed.

She approached it the way she had approached the moray's slot, which is to say too boldly, and then more carefully. It was heavy; an arm laid across the exposed face felt the great cold mass of it sunk in the silt, far heavier than coral, heavier than anything its size had a right to be. It was made — she knew made things now, the curve of the amphorae had taught her made — but made into a shape she had no read for, edges and a long arc and the stubs of things broken off, crusted green at every join.

She touched the bright face and tasted it: a strange metal, not the rust-metal, something smoother and colder and faintly bitter. And the pull of it came up in her, the same dumb pattern-hunger the amphorae had woken but stronger, focused, a wanting with no object she could name. She drew back from it. It was too much, too heavy, too bright; she was not ready for it. But she marked where it lay, lodged it in the muscle-map of the wreck, and she knew she would come back to the bright heavy thing the way she had come back to everything that had ever pulled at her, which was: always, and soon.

Chapter 13: The Small Circle

The ring she found by accident, inside a broken pot, and the ring she kept.

She had poured into the belly of a cracked amphora to taste its insides — they sometimes held things, these pots, a trapped crab, a clutch of brittle stars, the good dark silt that small lives liked — and in the bottom of this one, under a finger's depth of silt, an arm found something small and hard and perfectly round. She drew it out into the light. It was a tiny circle of the bright metal, the not-rust metal, smooth all the way around, with a hole through its middle exactly the size of nothing she could think of, sized for no part of her, for no part of anything in her world.

She held it in the curled tip of one arm and turned it and tasted it. The same cold bitter brightness as the heavy thing, but small, light, manageable, a thing she could carry. The eye liked the way it caught the light; the skin liked the smooth weight of it in the sucker; some deeper part of her, the part that had built a doorstep out of chosen stones, liked simply that it was, that such a small perfect circle existed and was now hers.

She carried it home. Not east to the poor crack but to the edge of the wreck where she had begun, half-consciously, to think she might live, and she set the ring down at the base of an amphora and looked at it, and moved it, and set it again, and arranged it just so, the way she had arranged the pebbles of her lost doorstep. It did nothing. It was good for nothing. It was not food and not shelter and not safety. It was a small bright circle that pleased her, and she kept it, and in the keeping of a useless beautiful thing there was something that, in a larger and slower mind, might have been the very beginning of everything that mind would ever call its own.

Chapter 14: The Contest

She and the long neighbor wanted the same dark, and so they began the slow war that is fought by sitting still.

The best space in the whole wreck was a deep crevice in its collapsed side, where two great slabs of the old structure had fallen against each other and left a chamber beneath — one mouth, solid walls, deep enough to hold her with room to grow, sheltered from the surge, close to the amphorae and the bright heavy thing. She had found it on her third night in the wreck and known at once it was the den she had been scouting for since the grouper took her doorstep. But the moray had found it too. Perhaps it had always been his second hold; perhaps he had only just come to want it. It did not matter. They both wanted it, and there was one of it, and so the contest began.

It was not fought with strikes. She had learned the lesson of his strike and would not give him another chance at her arms, and he, for his part, was old and had learned the lesson of her ink and her jets and her boneless escapes. So they fought instead by presence, by occupation, by the simple patient mathematics of who was in the crevice when the other came. She would install herself in the chamber and he would arrive and hang his long body at the mouth, the ancient eye flat, and she would hold, color steady, papillae raised, until he flowed away to his old slot in the shoulder. Then she would leave to hunt, and return to find him in the chamber, and it would be her turn to hang at the mouth and wait him out.

It went on for nights. Neither struck. Neither fled, exactly; they took turns yielding, each yielding costing the other nothing but time. She had more patience than he expected, perhaps. She had the ring and the wreck and a wanting that had settled on this one dark, and she did not intend to lose it, and she did not hurry, because she had learned from the reef itself that the thing which holds longest holds the ground.

Chapter 15: The Map in the Muscle

She came to know the wreck the way she knew her own arms, which is to say without looking, from the inside.

There was no picture of it anywhere in her. The eye could not have held it; the eye held only what it was pointed at, a mouth, an edge, a moving gleam. The wreck lived in her differently — in the muscle, in the suckers, in the chromatic memory that flushed through her skin when she passed a place she knew. The long amphora that hummed lowest: an arm reached for its lip before the eye confirmed it. The tumble of rust-metal: the suckers braced for the sharp ferrous taste before she touched it. The bright heavy thing's exact angle in the silt: her whole skin warmed to a particular dark gold when she came near it, a private color that meant here, this, the one that pulls.

She moved through the wreck at night without hesitation now, the geography of it run down into her body, and the running-down had a feel to it, a settling, the way the poor crack had never settled into her and the doorstep den had begun to before the grouper. The wreck was becoming home in the only way home was ever made, which was not by deciding but by knowing — by the slow accumulation of a thousand touches until the place and the body fit each other like a sucker and the stone it has chosen.

The ring sat at the wreck's edge where she had left it. The crevice was still contested. The bright heavy thing waited in its silt. And all of it, the whole made strange field of clay and metal and time, was lodged in her now, a map written in muscle and color that she carried whole through the dark, never once seeing it, knowing it completely.

Chapter 16: The Cage That Was Not Right

A thing appeared on the sand near the wreck that tasted wrong, and the wrongness of it saved her.

It had not been there the night before, which by itself made it suspect; the reef changed by storm and by slow growth, not overnight. It was a cage — a box of hard straight lines, metal but not the old rust-metal, a new sharp chemistry on it that she had no place for, the same alien tang she had tasted faintly in the water after the slow ones passed. And inside the cage, behind the bars, was a crab. A good crab, a big one, moving, alive, exactly the meal her hunger was asking for, sitting in plain reach behind a fence of straight bright lines.

She went to it, of course. But she went the way the moray's slot had taught her to go, slowly, and she tasted before she took. She circled the cage low, reading it, and everything about it spoke against itself. The crab was too easy. The crab was too placed, sitting in the center of the box as if set there. The bars had a mouth, a way in, a swinging panel, and the panel had the same wrong new-metal taste, and the whole arrangement had the quality she had learned to fear most in her short life: it had been made, and not by the sea, and not by her, and not for her good.

She did not enter. Every hungry nerve in her arms wanted the crab and she did not enter, because under the hunger ran the older deeper current that had kept a soft thing alive in a hard world, the current that said a meal you do not have to work for is a meal that is working on you. She drew back from the cage and held at a distance and watched the easy crab move behind its bright bars, and she let the hunger go unanswered, and she went and found a clam instead, prising it the hard honest way from the sand, and ate it under a ledge, and lived.

Chapter 17: The Hinge

But she could not leave the cage alone, because the crab was still in it, and the puzzle of it pulled at her worse than the brass.

She came back to it the next night, fed already, the hunger quiet, so that it was not hunger that drew her but the other thing, the pattern-hunger, the need to know how a made thing worked. The crab was still inside, slower now, tiring. The swinging panel was still shut. She settled by the cage and began, with no plan and infinite patience, to take it apart with her arms.

She tasted the whole of it first, every bar, every join, until she found the panel and the place where the panel was held — a simple thing, a bent wire, a catch that the crab's pushing from inside could not reach but that an arm from outside could. She worked at it. The first arm found the catch and could not move it and a second arm came to help and between them they had no leverage and she shifted her whole body and braced against the sand and tried again. It took a long time. She was not frustrated in the way a hungry animal is frustrated; she was absorbed, the way she was absorbed by the humming pots, the work itself the point.

And then the catch gave. The bent wire slid, the panel swung, and the crab — stupid, lucky, undeserving — felt the opening and bolted out past her arms and away across the sand, and she let it go. She did not want the crab; she had wanted the hinge. She sat by the opened empty cage with the small triumph of it warming her skin a satisfied mottled bronze, having solved a thing made by the slow ones for reasons she could not guess, having beaten it not with hunger but with attention, and that — the beating of a made thing by sheer patient curiosity — was as close to joy as her kind came.

Chapter 18: The One Who Did Not Grab

The slow one came down alone, and moved among the amphorae as if he too belonged to the wreck, and she watched him from inside a pot and learned that they were not all the same.

She had seen the slow ones in their groups, with their swinging lights and their roaring bubbles, disturbing the water with their clumsy kicking. This one was different. He came down quietly — his bubbles smaller, slower, his limbs barely working, a long slow sink to the sand at the wreck's edge — and then he did the thing none of them had done. He became still. He settled near the bottom and folded himself small, as small as a thing his size could fold, and he hung there in the water above the amphorae and did nothing at all.

She was inside a broken pot a body's length from him, packed into its hum, gone the exact dark of its interior, one eye at the crack of the mouth. Every nerve told her to flee, and she did not flee, because the slow one did not hunt. He did not grab. The others, the rare times one had come near her, had reached — a great gloved hand swinging toward her, and she had jetted and inked and gone. This one reached for nothing. He hung above the wreck with his lights dimmed and his hands still and he turned his great head very slowly, looking, only looking, and the looking had a patience in it that she recognized, because it was her own patience, the patience of the contest, the patience of the hinge.

He stayed a long time and touched nothing and took nothing, and when he rose at last up his thin column of bubbles she came out of the pot and went to where he had hung, and tasted the water there, the strange warm chemistry of him already thinning into the sea, and she did not understand him, and she was not afraid of him, and the two of those things together were entirely new.

Chapter 19: Seen

The next time he came she let him see her, which she had never once chosen to do with anything, and the choosing of it changed her life in a way she had no measure for.

She felt him descend — she knew his particular slow sink now, the small bubbles, the quiet — and she did not hide. She came instead to the edge of the wreck, into the open at the lip of the amphora field, and she settled on a low rock in plain water with the eye toward where he would land, and she waited, exposed, every instinct she owned screaming against it, and she overrode them all, for no reason her body could give, because the not-grabbing one was coming and she wanted, obscurely, terribly, to be in the open when he came.

He landed and went still and saw her. She felt the moment he saw her — the great head stopped its slow turning, fixed, the dimmed lights steady. And under his looking she did the only thing she had to give, which was color. She flushed, without quite meaning to, a sudden deep red across the whole mantle, the red of alarm, of warning, the red that said large thing, danger, go — and then, because he did not move, because he did not grab, the red drained and she went pale, a cautious questioning fawn, and then a shifting mottle as the two impulses, the fear and the not-fear, ran their colors across her at once.

He watched all of it and did not move. He let her say it, the whole confused chromatic sentence of being seen by a thing she could not place, and he answered it the only way that would not have sent her bolting, which was by doing nothing, by simply remaining, large and still and harmless above the wreck. And then she had said it, and there was nothing more to say, and she moved away — not a panicked jet but a slow withdrawal, backward into the amphorae, the eye on him to the last — and he let her go, and did not follow, and that, the being seen and the being let go, was a thing she would carry under her skin beside the slow ones and the cold of the deep, in the place where she kept the world's true strangenesses.

Chapter 20: The Crevice Won

She won the crevice the way she had fought for it, by being there one night more than the moray could bear, and she moved her whole small world into the dark.

It came down, in the end, to three nights. Three nights running she installed herself in the deep chamber under the fallen slabs before he arrived, and three nights running she held the mouth against him, papillae raised, color steady and cold, refusing the wordless argument of his long body and his ancient eye, and on the third night something in the old neighbor shifted. He hung at the mouth a long while, and then he flowed away to his slot in the shoulder, and he did not come back the next night, nor the next. The crevice was hers. The contest was over. He had not been beaten; he had been outlasted, which between them was the only kind of winning either could afford.

She moved in the way she made anything home — by arrangement. She brought the ring from the wreck's edge and set it at the base of the chamber mouth. She hauled rubble for a doorstep, choosing the stones, building the low threshold she could draw across behind her. And she positioned herself so that, lying in the mouth of her new dark with the door half-built, she could see along the silt to where the bright heavy thing lay in its bed, the green-dark gleam of it catching the slant of light at the day's two ends, morning and evening, a small far brightness at the edge of her world that pleased her for no reason she would ever have.

The den was the best she had ever held. Deep, solid, sheltered, won. The ring at the threshold, the brass gleaming at the edge of sight, the humming pots all around, the whole mapped wreck run down into her muscle. She packed herself into the dark and drew the stone across and rested, fully home at last, in the collapsed heart of an old made thing, the long neighbor gone back to his shoulder, the not-grabbing one somewhere up in the bright nothing, the reef turning slowly through its days around the small bright center she had made of herself, and her arrangements, and her dark.

Chapter 21: The Touch

It was she who reached first, which she would not have believed of herself, and the not-grabbing one who held still and let her, and between the reaching and the holding-still a thing was made that had no name in her world or in his.

He had come again and gone still above the wreck, near her den now, close enough that she could have drawn the door and ignored him. She did not draw the door. She came out instead, slowly, into the open silt, and approached him along the bottom the way she approached a new amphora, cautious, color shifting, and he did the thing he always did, which was nothing — he hung in the water and let her come. One of his great limbs rested on the sand, the hand of it open, the fingers in their dark second skin curled loosely upward.

She extended one arm toward the open hand. Just one; the others held back, ready to fire her away, voting against this even as the one arm went forward. The arm crossed the last of the distance and the sucker-tip touched his glove.

He did not pull away. That was the whole of it and it was everything. A lifetime of touching had taught her that a touched thing either fled or struck; the moray struck, the crab fled, the world divided into the two. He did neither. He let the sucker rest on the dark skin of his hand and she tasted it — not flesh, a made covering, smooth and slick and faintly bitter, warm with the warmth of whatever was inside it — and she held the touch for one long beat, two, reading him through that single point of contact, the heat and the wrongness and the held stillness of him. Then she drew the arm back, unhurried, no jet, no ink, and he let it go, and they remained as they were, the two of them, a body's length apart on the floor of the wreck, having touched and not fled and not struck, which between her kind and his was as near to speech as either would ever come.

Chapter 22: The Given Shell

He brought her a thing, the next time, and set it down, and she understood the setting-down, and answered it, and that was the strangest morning of her life.

She felt him descend and came to the edge of her doorstep to watch. He sank to the sand near her den and reached not toward her but toward the bottom, and he set something down on the silt between them, deliberately, and then withdrew his hand and went still in his way. She waited a long time, the door half-drawn, until the stillness convinced her, and then she flowed out and went to the thing he had left.

It was a shell. A good one — a large smooth spiral, unbroken, the kind she might have chosen herself for a doorstep, clean and whole and pleasing to the suckers. It had not been there before; he had brought it from somewhere, from the bright nothing above, and put it down in front of her den. She turned it in her arms and tasted it and found it only a shell, only itself, no trap in it, no wrongness, a plain good shell given for no reason she could find.

She took it. And then, because the giving of it had moved something in her, the part that arranged, the part that had kept a useless ring, she did not simply carry it inside. She set it at the threshold of her den, beside the ring, in the place of honor where her chosen things went, and she positioned it just so, the spiral mouth turned outward, and she felt the rightness of it settle in her skin. The slow one watched her do it. She did not know what he made of it, could not know, would never know. But the shell sat at her door beside the ring from then on, his thing and her arrangement, a given object kept and placed, and the keeping of it was the only answer she had and she gave it, and across the gap between two kinds of mind that was, perhaps, enough of one.

Chapter 23: The Urchin Bed

She raided the urchin bed at the height of her powers, greedy and fast and brilliant, and the grouper nearly had her for it, and she did not care, because the raid was glorious.

The urchins grew thick on an open shelf of rock to the south, a field of black spines, slow and defended and full of good rich roe for a hunter quick enough and clever enough to crack them without being cracked. She was both, now, at the top of her strength, her arms long and sure, her color-changes fast as thought, and she went onto the shelf in the open day and took urchins one after another — not patiently, not one and away to hide, but greedily, cracking one against the rock and dropping it and taking the next, five of them in a fast bright spree, the roe warm and sweet, the spines pricking her arms and the pricking ignored.

It was the greed that drew the grouper. The same great slab of a fish that had taken her first den came off its distant station, pulled by the disturbance, the cloud of cracked urchin and the flash of her arms, and she felt the heavy pressure of it bearing down across the open shelf and knew she had stayed one urchin too long. The cavern mouth opened. The suction reached for her.

She fired. The mantle clenched and threw her sideways off the shelf and she hit the water already jetting, and behind her the grouper's strike closed on nothing but cracked shell. She ran for the wreck — there was no other thought, the wreck was safety, the wreck was hers — and she poured into the amphora field with the slab of the fish behind her, and she lost it in the maze. That was what the wreck was for, she understood, jetting between the pots, the grouper too large to follow through the gaps, its great body checked at the edges of the field while she threaded the humming hollows it could not enter. She came to rest deep in her own crevice, spines in her arms and roe in her belly and the wreck between her and the fish, alive, well-fed, and triumphant, the raid worth every danger of it.

Chapter 24: The Storm and the Brass

The second storm was worse than the first, and it remade the wreck, and when it had passed the bright heavy thing had turned its face more fully to the light.

She felt this one coming farther off and went to ground earlier, deep in the crevice with the door drawn and her arms wedged, and she rode it out as she had ridden out the first, heavy and small and patient while the sea above lost itself. But this storm reached deeper. The surge drove down even into the wreck, and through the walls of her den she felt the great clay shapes shifting, the low humming pots rolling, a grinding in the silt as the storm moved the old buried things the way only the biggest seas could move them.

When it passed and she came out, the wreck was changed. An amphora that had lain whole for longer than she had been alive had rolled and cracked along its belly, spilling silt and a small avalanche of brittle stars. The field's geography, run so carefully into her muscle, was wrong now in three places, and she would have to learn it again. And the bright heavy thing — she went to it at once, drawn, anxious for it the way she was anxious for nothing else — had shifted in its bed. The storm had scoured the silt from one whole face of it and tipped it slightly, so that the green-dark gleam she had only caught at the day's two ends now took the light more fully, a longer brightness, the slanting sun finding the polished arc of it and bending away changed.

She settled by it and tasted it and found it the same bitter brightness, unhurt, only turned. It pleased her enormously, the new angle of its gleam, though she could not have said why a buried made thing catching more light should please a soft creature of the reef. It simply did. She lay by the brass in the after-storm calm, her arms across its cold great mass, and watched the light bend off it, and was content in its company, and went home to her den only when the hunger finally called her, reluctant, as always, to leave the bright thing's side.

Chapter 25: The Eye and the Lens

He pointed a thing at her, a small dark thing with a glass eye, and she watched it watch her, and she did not hide, and the not-hiding lasted longer than any meeting they had ever had.

She knew him completely now — his sink, his stillness, his patience — and when he came that day and settled near her den she came out to him as she had learned to, into the open, unafraid. But he held something this time, a small box-shape in both hands, and he raised it slowly and turned its glass eye toward her, and the glass eye caught the light the way the brass caught it, a round dark gleam, and behind it she could feel his looking concentrated, focused, pouring through the little glass.

Her first impulse was the old red flush, the warning, and she let it rise and then let it fall, because she had learned this one did not grab and the box did not grab either; it only looked. So she did the thing that cost her the most and gave him the most, which was to stay, and to stay unchanged. She did not flush, did not pale, did not throw a pattern. She held her own color, a steady soft mottle, and she turned the eye — both pupils, the horizontal slots widening — toward the glass eye that watched her, and the two looked at each other across the silt, his look and hers, the glass and the slot, for a long suspended time.

She performed nothing. That was the gift and she did not know it was a gift; she only knew that she felt no need, under his patient looking, to be other than she was, no need to vanish into the rock or blaze a warning or pour away. She let herself simply be looked at, whole, herself, on the floor of her own wreck, and he looked, and the little glass eye held her, and when at last she did move away it was slowly and without fear, and she carried the strange new comfort of it with her, the comfort of having been seen entirely and having had no wish to hide.

Chapter 26: The Fast Violence

She learned that day that the slow ones were not all the not-grabbing kind, and that some of them came down to kill, and that the killing was large and fast and soon over.

It happened above her, near the surface, where she almost never looked. She felt it first as a wrongness in the water, a hard flat concussion that crossed the whole reef at once, a single pneumatic thud unlike anything the sea made on its own. She pressed flat against the rock by reflex, gone instantly to stone-grey, and turned the eye up toward the bright ceiling, and she saw the shapes of it playing out far above her: a slow one, but moving wrong, fast and purposeful, and a line, and at the end of the line a thrashing — the grouper, her grouper, the great slab of a fish that had hunted the reef as long as she had lived, caught now and fighting, a dark cloud spreading around it, hauled struggling up the water toward the air.

She watched it go up. There was nothing else to do and nothing in her that wanted to do anything else; this was weather, this was the world being large and indifferent, the way the moray's strike had been the world, the way the storm had been the world. The grouper thrashed and rose and was smaller and smaller against the bright and then was gone through the ceiling into the place where things did not come back from, and the line went with it, and the cloud thinned, and the reef was quiet, and the great fish that had taken her first den and nearly taken her on the urchin shelf simply was not there any more.

She stayed flat against the rock a long while after. She felt no triumph that the grouper was gone, and no grief; those were not the shapes of her response. She felt only the old deep watchfulness, deepened — that the slow ones came in kinds, and that one kind came down with the fast thudding death on a line, and that the bright ceiling she never watched could open and take even the largest thing on the reef without warning and without struggle she could affect. She filed it under her skin beside the cage and the trap, the made dangers, the wrongnesses, and she went on, smaller in her own reckoning of the world's safety, which is to say wiser.

Chapter 27: Play

In the long slack of a warm afternoon, with no hunger in her and no danger near, she played, and play had no reason, and that was the whole of what made it play.

A thing drifted across the wreck on the current — light, pale, a made thing of the slow ones' kind, a clear vessel that held nothing, tumbling slowly end over end in the water the way no living thing tumbled. It passed her den at a drift and she watched it go, and there was no food-thought in it and no danger-thought; it was only a thing moving oddly through her water. It went past, and the current turned it, and it came back.

She let it pass a second time, watching. And then, on the third pass, with no decision she could have named, she fired off her rock and jetted up into the open water and caught it. Her arms closed around the smooth clear shape and held it, and it was nothing, weightless, empty, no taste worth keeping, and she did not want it for anything. So she let it go. It drifted off, tumbling, and she hung in the water and watched it, and then she jetted after it and caught it again, and let it go again, and chased it again, up in the bright open afternoon where a soft thing had no business hanging exposed, doing a thing for which there was no purpose at all.

It fed her nothing. It sheltered her from nothing. It made her, for those minutes in the warm current, a little less safe than she might have been. And she did it anyway, three times, four, the catching and the releasing and the watching it go, until the current carried it off the reef for good and she let it leave and turned back to her den, satisfied in a way that had nothing to do with food or safety or arrangement — satisfied simply to have moved, and chased, and held a useless drifting thing in the sun for no reason but the doing of it, which in a mind as bright as hers was not so far from joy, and not so far from being free.

Chapter 28: The Patrol

At the top of her life, on a clear night, she made the round of her whole territory, and found every part of it in its place, and the wreck was hers, and she knew it.

She set out from the crevice after dark with no hunger driving her, only the wish to walk the borders of what she held, the way she had walked nothing before, because there had been nothing before that was so wholly hers to walk. She went east first, to the rubble where she had hunted as a smaller creature, and found it unchanged, the clams in their sand, the old limestone crack she had first denned in standing empty, too small for her now, a thing she had outgrown and left behind. She crossed the shoulder of the reef and passed the moray's slot, and the long neighbor was there in his dark, the ancient eye catching the faint light, and they regarded each other across the old border and neither moved, the contest long settled, the peace between them a known and stable thing.

She went to the urchin shelf, and the grouper's station above it was empty now, the great fish gone up the line, and she crossed the open shelf without fear that she could not have crossed in daylight a month before. She tasted the deep water at the drop-off, the clean cold she still liked. And she came back at last through the amphora field, the humming pots sounding their low clay notes around her, past the bright heavy thing gleaming faintly in the starlit silt, to her own crevice with the ring and the given shell at its threshold.

All of it was in its place. The neighbor in his slot, the empty shelf, the cold deep, the wreck, the brass, the den. She had a country and she had walked it and found it whole and held, and she packed herself into her dark at the end of the round with a settledness she had never owned before, full of the quiet authority of a creature at the height of its powers in the middle of the world it has made its own — not knowing, because there was no way for her to know, that the height of a life is also the place from which the only remaining direction is down.

Chapter 29: The Last Visit of the Warm

He came one more time while the water was still warm, and stayed the longest he ever stayed, and she took food from his hand and did not keep it, and the not-keeping was as much a part of it as the taking.

She felt his sink and came out to the open, and he settled near her den and did not raise the glass eye this time, did not bring a shell, only folded himself small and still and stayed. The afternoon went long around them. He hung in the water and she moved near him, easy now in his company, and she did the small idle things of a creature at rest near a thing it does not fear — groomed her arms, shifted a stone at her threshold, lay half out of her den in the warm slanting light.

Toward the end he offered her something from his hand. A pale fragment, food of some kind, a hard flat morsel held out on the open glove. She came to it and took it, lifting it from his palm with one arm, and she tasted it — strange, dry, a made food, nothing her sea had ever grown — and it was not bad and not good, not the warm sweetness of roe or the clean meat of crab, only an odd dry foreign thing. She held it a moment, and then she let it fall. She did not want it. She had taken it because he offered it, because the offering was the thing, the open hand and the crossing of the gap; the food itself was beside the point, and she let it drift down into the silt uneaten.

He did not offer again. Perhaps he understood; perhaps he understood nothing and only watched a creature drop his gift. It did not matter which. The transaction had been its own event, complete in itself — the offering and the taking and the letting-fall, a thing passed between two minds that shared no single thought, that wanted nothing from each other that either could name. Then he rose, slowly, up his thin column of small bubbles, and she watched him go up into the bright as she had watched the grouper go up, except that this going was gentle and expected and left no fear behind it, only the faint cooling absence of a thing that had been warm beside her, rising away as the warm season itself was, just then, beginning to rise away.

Chapter 30: The Turn of the Water

The water cooled by a degree, and the light came in at a lower angle, and she began to eat as she had never eaten, and something had changed in the chemistry of the world that she could not read and could not refuse.

It came on slowly, the way the storms came on, as a change in the texture of everything before it was a thing itself. The water against her mantle held a new coolness, slight but constant, the warm season tilting toward whatever came after it. The light that fell through the bright ceiling struck the reef more sidelong now, longer shadows, the brass at the day's two ends catching its gleam for longer and at a deeper slant. The small lives of the reef shifted their habits; the weed beds to the east changed their bitterness; the whole sea was turning over toward cold in the slow way it turned each year, the only year she would see it turn.

And in her the hunger changed. It was not the old clean chord of need, filling and emptying. It was a deeper, more constant pull, a wanting-to-eat that the eating did not fully answer, and she hunted now more than she ever had, taking crab and clam and urchin in a steady greedy procession, her body laying the food down somewhere inside her against a purpose she had no knowledge of. She did not understand it. She felt only the appetite, vast and patient and new, and obeyed it, hunting the cooling reef from end to end, building in her flesh some reserve her flesh knew the reason for though she did not.

She had no word for what was coming and no picture of it and no warning that the body lays down such reserves only once, and only for one thing, and only at the end. She knew only that the water had turned, and the light had dropped, and her hunger had become a strange deep engine that drove her up and down the reef in the shortening days — and she ate, and ate, and did not ask why, because asking why was not a thing she did, and the chemistry of her own ending moved through her quietly, dressed as appetite, and she followed where it led.

Chapter 31: Restlessness

A restlessness took her that no den could hold and no meal could quiet, and she gave herself to it because there was nothing in her that could do otherwise.

It began as a wrongness in the dark of her crevice. She would pack herself in at the end of the night, behind her doorstep, the ring and the shell at the threshold, the brass gleaming faintly at the edge of sight — everything as it should be, everything hers — and she could not rest. The dark that had always meant safety now meant only enclosure, walls, a stillness her body refused. She would draw the stone back and go out at strange hours, in the dead middle of the night, in the grey before dawn, and sweep the reef end to end with no hunger driving her and no curiosity either, looking for something she could not have described and did not find.

Her skin would not settle. In the old days her color had answered the world — grey for the rock, red for alarm, the satisfied bronze of a fed body. Now it answered something inside her instead. Patterns moved across her that she had never thrown, slow dark bars rising and fading along her mantle, a deep flush blooming and draining for no outward reason, a restlessness of color that matched the restlessness of the body, both of them driven by the same hidden engine she could not see the shape of.

She hunted, because the deep appetite was still in her too, and she ate, and the eating did not touch the restlessness. She returned to her den and could not stay in it and went out again. Something was building in her, something her flesh was preparing for with all its quiet greedy storing and its new strange colors, and she could not name it and could not refuse it, and the not-naming and the not-refusing drove her up and down the reef in the cold dark, a bright creature pulled by a tide that ran entirely inside her own body.

Chapter 32: Past the Borders

The restlessness pushed her past the borders of everything she knew, out of her own country, into reef she had never tasted, and she went without deciding to go.

She had always lived inside a known territory — the rubble, the shoulder, the urchin shelf, the wreck — and she had walked its edges and kept to them, because beyond them was the unmapped, and the unmapped held unknown dangers and no advantage worth the risk. But the engine in her did not weigh advantage. It pushed her past the moray's slot, past the long neighbor's dark, and the moray watched her go without moving, perhaps reading in her something he had read in others across his long life. It pushed her past the empty station where the grouper had hung, out onto open ground that fell away into reef she had never crossed.

She went carefully, because even driven she was still herself, still a soft thing in a hard world. She tasted as she went, every stretch of new rock, every current, the suckers reading the unfamiliar water for the chemistry of things that might eat her. The new reef was like and unlike her own — the same kinds of ledges, different in their particulars, strange weed, an unknown wrasse working an unknown station. She crossed it low and watchful, far from any den she could reach in a hurry, more exposed than she had let herself be since she was small, and the exposure frightened the old careful part of her even as the engine drove her on.

She did not know what she was looking for. Her body knew. Her body had laid down its reserves and turned its colors and driven her out of her safe country into the open precisely so that it might find the one thing it now needed to find, and she followed the body the way she followed her hunger and her curiosity, trusting it as she had always trusted it, out past every border into the unknown reef, looking for something she would know only when she tasted it on the current.

Chapter 33: The Chord on the Current

She tasted him before she saw him, a message in the water that struck her arms all at once like nothing she had ever felt, and she turned toward it before she knew she had turned.

It came on the current from somewhere ahead in the new reef — a chemistry, faint and then stronger, threading through the water and reaching her spread arms not as a single taste but as a whole sudden chord, every sucker reporting it at once, a signal pitched exactly to the engine that had been driving her, so that the instant it reached her the restlessness in her gathered and pointed, the way scattered light gathers through a lens. She had spent her life reading the chemistry of crab and clam and danger. This was none of those. This was a message meant for her, for what she had become in the turning of the water, and her body answered it before her caution could speak.

She moved toward it. There was no deciding; the deciding had been done by the appetite and the colors and the long drive out past the borders, and now there was only the moving, drawn up the gradient of the scent the way she had once drifted toward the curved clay and the bright brass, except that this pull was deeper than either, older, written into her below the level of the mind that kept the map and solved the hinge.

Her skin, as she went, did a thing it had never done. It bloomed into a pattern she had not chosen and had never worn, a display, a sudden chromatic statement thrown out toward the source of the scent ahead — not the grey of hiding or the red of warning but something new, meant to be seen, meant to answer the chord on the current with a color of her own. She crossed the unfamiliar reef throwing this new pattern before her like a held-out arm, following the scent in, toward the other of her kind that her whole changed body had driven her across the borders of the world to find.

Chapter 34: The Meeting

The meeting was slow and careful and spoken in color, and the thing it was for was brief, and then it was done, and the world had changed in her without her understanding how.

She found him among the rocks of the unfamiliar reef, another of her kind, larger-armed, his own skin already throwing the answering patterns, and they came together the way two wary intelligent creatures come together, cautiously, in stages, neither trusting, both driven. They did not touch at first. They hung a body's length apart and changed colors at each other for a long time, a slow exchange of pattern and counter-pattern, displays rising and fading across both their skins, a courtship conducted entirely in the language she thought in, color answering color across the gap, each reading the other, each deciding.

It was not gentle, exactly, and it was not unkind; it was simply the way of her kind, ritual and wary and old. There was a danger in it she dimly felt — that the other was large, that her kind were not always safe with one another, that the meeting could turn — and she kept her caution even as the engine drove her on, ready to jet, holding herself where she could break away. But the displays satisfied whatever they had to satisfy, and the wariness eased by degrees into something that allowed approach, and when at last they touched it was brief and particular and biological, the passing-across that her changed body had crossed the world for, an event of moments, and then complete.

And then it was over, and they drew apart, and the chord that had pulled her was answered and gone, and she hung in the unfamiliar water with something accomplished in her that she had no knowledge of, some change set in motion beyond recall. She felt no tenderness toward the other; that was not the shape of it. She felt only that a thing had been done that needed doing, that the long restless drive was ended, and that she could, now, turn back. And she turned back, toward her own country, toward the wreck, carrying inside her the consequence of the meeting, which was everything, which was the beginning of the end, which she could not see at all.

Chapter 35: The New Looking

She came home to the wreck and began to look at it in a wholly new way, examining every crevice and surface and depth with an urgency that had no precedent, and she did not know what she was looking for until she found it.

The journey back across the borders had tired her, and the deep appetite had quieted at last, replaced by something else, a new imperative as strong as the old restlessness but turned now to a single focused task. She moved through her territory not as its master walking the borders but as a creature searching, testing, measuring. She put her arms into spaces she had ignored for months. She tasted the back walls of crevices, the depths of the amphorae, the angles of the fallen slabs, weighing each against a standard she could feel but not articulate — a standard of safety, of enclosure, of a place that could be sealed and held and defended against the world for a long, long stillness.

Nothing in the open reef satisfied it. The old rubble dens were too shallow, too exposed. The amphorae hummed too open-mouthed. She went over the whole of her country again with this new looking, and it was the most particular she had ever been about anything, more particular than the doorstep, more particular than the winning of the crevice, because the thing she was choosing a place for was the only thing her body had left to do, and some deep knowledge in her flesh insisted that it be done right or not at all.

She did not find it in the open reef. She found it where she already lived. She came back at last to her own crevice — the deep chamber under the fallen slabs, the one she had outlasted the moray for, the one with the ring and the shell at its threshold and the brass gleaming in the silt beyond — and she went into the deep of it and tasted the back wall and the ceiling and the dark held heart of it, and her whole changed body said: here. This. The best place she owned, the place she had fought for, the place at the center of the world she had made. The new looking ended where her life had centered, and she began at once, that same day, to make it ready.

Chapter 36: The Making Ready

She set about remaking her den into something it had never been, and the work was the most absorbed and tireless labor of her life, and she did it for a purpose her body knew and her mind only obeyed.

She hauled rubble. She had built doorsteps before, low thresholds for drawing a stone across; this was more, and deeper, and stranger. She brought stone after stone to the mouth of the crevice and built the wall higher and tighter than any she had made, leaving only a gap she could just pass through, a den that could be all but sealed. She widened the inner passages where they were too tight, prising loose pieces of the old fallen structure and carrying them out, opening the chamber's deep places. She smoothed the back wall, running her arms over it again and again, clearing the silt and the debris, making a clean dark vault of the inmost part of the crevice.

She worked without rest, or nearly. The deep appetite was gone and she barely hunted now, taking a small meal only when the work flagged, grudging the time. Her whole self had narrowed to this labor, this preparation of a single perfect space, and the narrowing felt right, felt like the thing she was for, in a way that even the height of her powers had not. She had been, in her life, a hunter and an explorer and a solver of hinges and a keeper of bright useless things; now she was only this, a maker-ready of a dark, and she gave herself to it wholly.

When it was done the den was transformed. The wall at the mouth stood high and tight, the inner chamber clean and dark and deep, the back vault smoothed and ready. She moved through it checking everything, a finished thing, the best work she had ever done. The brass gleamed faintly through the narrowed mouth at the edge of the silt beyond, where it would catch the morning and the evening light as it always had, a small far brightness she would be able to see from inside her sealed dark. She had made the place. Now there was only the last thing to put in it, and her body, in its quiet certainty, was already bringing that, too, to its time.

Chapter 37: The Pale Strands

She laid the eggs in the smoothed dark vault of her den, long pale strands of them fixed to the ceiling, and she inspected each one, and then she sealed herself in, and the world narrowed to this.

It came over her in the readied chamber, the last imperative, and she gave herself to it as she had given herself to all the others, without resistance and without understanding, only the deep obedience of a body doing the one thing left in it to do. The eggs came in strands, pale and translucent, hundreds upon hundreds of them strung together like the smallest beads, and she fixed the strands to the clean ceiling of the back vault, one after another, working with a care she had never spent on anything, hanging her whole future in fragile pale ropes from the roof of the dark she had made.

When the strands were hung she went over them. Every one. She ran her arms across the clusters, tasting them, reading them, learning the feel of them, settling each strand where it should hang, removing a grain of silt that had fallen, adjusting, tending, already tending, before they were even fully laid. The chamber filled with them, pale ropes of small lives depending from the ceiling, and the sight of them on the eye and the taste of them on the suckers woke in her something as strong as anything she had ever felt, stronger than the brass, stronger than the chord on the current — a fixity, a total turning toward these strands, a sense that they were now the whole of what she was and what she was for.

Then she drew the last stones across the mouth of the den, sealing herself in with them, all but a narrow gap for the water to pass, and she settled herself among the hanging strands in the dark, the ring and the shell shut outside at the threshold, the brass a thin far gleam through the gap. The world she had walked and mapped and mastered was gone, shut beyond the wall. There was only the vault, and the pale strands, and her, and the long tending that would now begin and would not end until they did, or she did, whichever the dark decided first.

Chapter 38: The Defense

The moray came close, drawn by something, and she drove him off with a fury she had never owned, and in the driving-off she became, for the first time, a defender.

She felt him before she saw him, the long pressure of his body moving through the wreck toward her sealed den — the old neighbor, the long neighbor, with whom she had kept a settled peace for half her life. Perhaps he scented the eggs; perhaps the chemistry of a brooding female, or of the strands themselves, reached him in his shoulder and pulled him from his dark the way the male's chord had pulled her across the borders. Whatever drew him, he came, and he came to the mouth of her den, and he put his ancient head to the narrow gap in her wall, toward the pale strands hanging in the vault behind her.

And she answered him with everything at once. She did not jet; she did not ink; she did not yield the mouth as she had yielded it in their old contest. She filled the gap with herself, every arm spread and braced, every papilla on her skin standing erect so that she bristled, her whole surface roughened into a thing twice her size, and across that bristling surface she threw the most violent color she had — not the soft warning red of her youth but a hard pulsing alarm, dark bars strobing across white, a blaze of chromatic fury aimed straight at the old neighbor's flat eye. She made herself enormous and hideous and absolutely immovable in the mouth of her dark, and she held there, and she did not break, and every signal she owned said the same single thing: not these. Not here. Not while I live.

The moray held a moment at the gap. The flat ancient eye took her in, the bristling blazing barrier she had become. And then he drew back. He flowed away from the mouth of her den, back across the wreck toward his shoulder, yielding as he had yielded the crevice once before, and she held her furious display until he was gone and held it a while after, and only then let the papillae sink and the hard color fade, and drew back among her strands. She had defended them. She had become, in an instant, the thing a brooding mother becomes, and she settled in the dark over her hanging future with a new ferocity laid down in her beside all the rest.

Chapter 39: The Cessation

She stopped eating, and it was not a decision, and she did not feel it as loss, and that was the quietest and most terrible turn of her whole quiet life.

There was no moment of choosing. She did not resolve to fast, did not weigh the eggs against her hunger and elect the eggs; her kind did not work that way, and neither did she. It was simpler and stranger than choice. The hunger that had driven her up and down the reef her whole life, the clean chord that filled and emptied, the deep greedy engine of the cooling season — it ceased. A crab worked its way across the silt past the very gap of her den, a good crab, the kind she would once have taken in a bright pour of arms, and she watched it pass and felt nothing, no rising of appetite, no pull, the food-thought simply absent, a string gone slack.

She understood nothing of why. She knew only that she did not leave the den, and did not hunt, and did not eat, and felt no wrongness in the not-eating, because the part of her that would have called it wrong had been quietly switched off along with the hunger. Her whole self had turned to the strands. They were the appetite now; tending them was the only chord her body still sounded; and the cost of that turning, the slow cost that would be paid out of her own flesh over all the days of the tending, was a thing her body had agreed to long before, in the laying-down of its reserves, in the turning of the water, in the chord on the current, in every step that had brought her to this dark.

So she did not eat, and the prey moved past her gap unmolested, and she lay among her pale strands in the deep of the den and tended them, and her body began, very slowly, to spend itself. She did not feel herself beginning to fade. She felt only the strands, and the fanning of them, and the far gleam of the brass through the gap, and the deep rightness of being exactly where she was, doing exactly what she did, while the long slow arithmetic of her ending ran beneath it all, silent, unfelt, already underway.

Chapter 40: He Finds the Den

The not-grabbing one came down and found her changed den and stayed a long time outside it, and she watched him from within and barely moved, and the brass caught the light between them like a thing held up.

She felt his sink, the familiar slow descent, the small quiet bubbles, and some old part of her stirred toward it even now, even sealed in her dark and turned wholly to her strands. He came to the wreck and to her den, and he found it changed — the high tight wall, the narrowed gap, the whole transformed architecture of the brooding chamber — and he understood, she thought, or something in his stillness suggested he understood, that the creature he had visited all season had become something new and final, sealed in behind a wall against the world.

He did not try the gap. He did not reach for her, had never reached for her; he only settled in the silt outside her den, as close as he had ever come and no closer, and went still, and stayed. She watched him from within, one eye at the narrow gap, and she did not bristle as she had bristled at the moray, because the not-grabbing one was not the moray, because in all their meetings he had taken nothing and threatened nothing, and the deep new ferocity of her did not rise at him. She only watched, barely moving, fanning her strands in the dark, and let him keep his long vigil outside her own.

Between them, in the silt, the brass instrument lay and caught the slanting light, throwing its bent green gleam up into the water, and his looking and her watching crossed over it, the bright old made thing between the two of them like an offering neither had made. He stayed until the light began to go. Then he rose, slowly, up into the bright as he always rose, and she stayed in her dark as she would now always stay, and the brass dimmed as the day dimmed, and she turned back from the gap to her pale strands, and tended them, alone, sealed, spending, content.

Chapter 41: The Work of the Vigil

The den became the whole of the world, and the work of the den became the whole of her, and she gave herself to it with a steadiness she had never spent on hunting or exploring or any bright pursuit of her free life.

The work was simple and endless. She fanned the strands. Drawing water in through the mantle and pushing it out across the hanging eggs, over and over, a slow current of her own making washing the pale clusters so that they did not suffocate in the still dark — this was the first work, and the constant one, and she did it through the day and through the night without stopping, the rhythm of it become the rhythm of her. She cleaned them. Grain by grain she picked the settling silt from the strands, removed a fallen fragment, lifted away the smallest debris with the tip of an arm, tending each cluster in its turn and beginning again.

And she watched. From the gap in her wall she kept the one eye on the silt beyond, on the dim wreck, on the approaches to her dark, ready to become again the bristling fury that had turned the moray, ready to defend the strands against anything that came. Little came. The wreck went on around her sealed den, the humming pots, the slanting light, the turning of the cold season, all of it beyond her wall now and no longer hers to walk.

She did not miss it. That was the strange mercy of the turning that had taken her: she did not lie in her dark and long for the open reef, the urchin shelf, the chase of the drifting bottle, the cold clean deep. The part of her that had wanted those things had gone quiet along with the hunger. There was only the den, and the strands, and the fanning, and the watching, and within that narrowed world she was as complete and as absorbed as she had ever been at the height of her powers, more so, tending her hanging future in the dark with the patient totality of a creature doing the last and truest work of its life.

Chapter 42: The Narrowing Color

In the first long stretch of the fasting her skin still spoke, but it spoke in fewer colors now, the bright range of her narrowing toward something quieter, and she patched a gap in her wall with her own body and thought nothing of it.

She had been, all her life, a creature of enormous chromatic speech — the grey of the rock, the red of alarm, the satisfied bronze, the hard strobing fury of the defense, the new courtship pattern she had thrown across the borders, a whole vivid language run across her skin faster than thought. Now, sealed in her dark over the strands, spending her flesh slowly in the fast, she found the language thinning. The colors still came, still answered her moods, but the range of them drew in. The brightest blazes did not rise as readily. Her skin settled more and more toward a small band of quiet tones, a dim mottle, the chromatic equivalent of a voice going soft.

She did not perceive it as loss; she perceived it hardly at all. She knew only that the fanning went on, and the cleaning, and the watching, and that her body felt lighter and stranger as the days passed, hollowing as it spent itself on the strands and on the long current of her tending.

When a stone of her own wall shifted one night and left a gap too wide — a gap a small predator might pass, a gap that let the silt drift in onto her clusters — she did not haul a new stone to fill it. She had not the strength to spare for hauling now, and the strands could not be left unfanned while she labored. So she filled the gap herself. She set her own body into the breach, an arm and a portion of her softening mantle pressed into the opening, sealing it with her flesh, and she held there, part wall now, part watcher, fanning the strands with her free arms while her body itself patched the dark against the world. It seemed to her entirely reasonable. There was the den, and there were the strands, and whatever they needed of her she gave, including, now, the use of her own body as a stone.

Chapter 43: The Two Who Came

The not-grabbing one came again, and this time another came with him, and they hung outside her den a long time, and the other reached for the brass, and she did not move, because nothing now could move her from the strands.

She knew his sink, and felt, this time, a second descent beside it, a different pressure, a different rhythm of bubbles — another of the slow ones, larger or smaller she could not tell, but not him, not the patient one. They came down together to the wreck and to her den, and they hovered outside it, the two of them, and she watched from the gap, part of her body still set in the breach of her own wall, her free arms moving over the strands.

The two slow ones stayed and looked. She felt their looking, the concentrated attention of them turned on her changed den, on the high wall, on the gap where her dimmed skin and her watching eye showed in the dark. And then the second one, the stranger, reached — not toward her, but toward the brass, toward the bright heavy thing that lay in the silt between her den and the open water, the thing that had pulled at her since the first day of the wreck, the thing whose green-dark gleam she had watched from inside her dark all season. The stranger's great hand went toward it, closed near it, touched it perhaps.

And she did not move. A season ago she might have flared, might have bristled, might have felt the loss of the brass as the loss of a thing that was hers. Now she only watched, her free arms never ceasing their slow fanning of the strands. The brass was a thing of her free life, a bright pleasure from the days when she had walked the reef and kept useless beautiful things, and that life was over and shut beyond her wall, and she had no fury to spare for it. There were the strands. There was only ever, now, the strands. The slow ones could take the brass or leave it; her whole defended heart had narrowed to the pale ropes on the ceiling, and nothing beyond them could reach her enough to make her move.

Chapter 44: The Small Wanderer

A tiny octopus, new to the world, wandered past her den one day, and she watched it pass and did not know it for kin, and the not-knowing was not coldness but the plain truth of what she was.

It came across the silt outside her gap — impossibly small, a thing the size of nothing, a scrap of a creature with eight minute arms and a body she could have hidden under a single sucker, drifting and crawling in the halting way of the very young, tasting the wreck with arms that had only just learned to taste. It was perhaps two weeks into its life, whatever brood it had come from, whatever sealed den some other mother had spent herself in to make it. It passed within reach of her gap, busy, oblivious, a small bright spark of a life beginning exactly where hers was ending.

She watched it with the one eye and felt no pull toward it. It was not hers — her own strands still hung sealed and unhatched on her ceiling — and even had it been hers, she would not have known it, because her kind did not know their young, did not raise them, did not meet them; the mother sealed the den and tended the eggs and was gone before they hatched, and the young rose into the sea alone and made their own way, kinless, as she had made hers. The small wanderer was nothing to her. It was only another creature of the reef, the way the wrasse was, the way the crab was, a life she had no thread to.

And yet she watched it the whole way across her gap, every halting step of its small crossing, until it passed out of the eye's narrow view. Not with longing; she had no frame for longing toward it. But with something — attention, recognition of a shape, the dim sense of a pattern she herself had once been and her own strands would one day be. It crawled away into the wreck to begin its bright brief life, and she turned back to her ceiling of pale ropes, and fanned them, and the two of them, the new-begun and the near-ended, passed each other once in the dark of the same old wreck and went on, unkinned, each complete and alone in the only way her kind was ever anything.

Chapter 45: Memory as Color

Her past came back to her in the dark, not as pictures but as colors, each old thing of her life surfacing as the shade her skin had worn for it, and she lay among her strands and was washed through by the hues of everything she had been.

She did not remember the way the slow ones surely remembered, in scenes, in order, a life laid out behind them. Memory came to her as it had always come, through the skin, as color — and now, in the long stillness of the vigil, with the outer world shut away and nothing new to answer, the old colors rose in her unbidden, one after another, the chromatic record of her whole bright life surfacing in the quiet.

A deep red-brown bloomed across her dimming mantle, and it was the moray's first strike, the torn arm, the lesson at the edge of his slot — that exact shade, the color her young self had flushed in that first shock of pain and flight. It faded, and a particular cold shimmer rose in its place, the polarized silver of open water on the night she had jetted down into the deep to taste the cold, the clean far brightness of the lives that glowed. That faded too, and a satisfied bronze came up, the color of a crab cracked and eaten under a ledge in the good simple hunger of her free days. The dark gold of the brass. The steady mottle she had held while the glass eye watched her and she had felt no need to hide. The hard strobing white-and-black of the defense. Each old thing of her life rose as its color, surfaced, washed through her dimming skin, and sank.

She did not summon them and could not have stopped them. They came on their own, the colors of her life, fainter than they had once burned because her skin was fainter now, but unmistakable, the whole of what she had been written across what little of her remained, played out in the dark over the pale strands. It was not sorrow and it was not farewell, because she had no frame for either. It was only her life, returning the one way it knew how to return, in color, while she fanned her future on the ceiling and spent the last of herself in the tending.

Chapter 46: The Long Stillness Shared

He came alone again and sat outside her den longer than he had ever sat, more than an hour without moving, and she fanned her strands, and he watched, and a thing passed between them in that shared stillness that neither of their two kinds had a name for.

She felt his sink, fainter to her now, everything fainter now, but known, the patient slow descent of the not-grabbing one. He came to the wreck and to her den and he settled in the silt outside her gap, where the brass still lay, and he went still, and he stayed. The light moved across the wreck and he did not move. The slow current ran and he did not move. He folded himself small in the way he had, his dimmed lights steady, his great head turned toward the gap where her dim skin and her slow-moving arms showed in the dark, and he kept a stillness beside her that matched her own.

She did not come out to him. She had not the strength now, and the strands could not be left, and the part of her that had once reached out one arm to touch his glove was quiet. But she did not bristle and she did not withdraw deeper into the dark. She let him stay. She let his long looking rest on her, and she went on with her work under it, fanning the pale ropes, cleaning them grain by grain, and the two of them held their separate stillnesses on either side of her wall for the better part of an hour, the watcher and the watched, the rising kind and the ending kind, together in the dark of the wreck.

Something passed between them. She could not have said what; he could not have said what; their minds shared no single thought and never had. But across the gap, across the brass, across the wall she had built and the body she was spending, there moved some recognition that needed no thought to carry it — that they had known each other, in the only ways they could, across a season of the reef; that he had come and taken nothing and threatened nothing and was here now at the end keeping a vigil outside her vigil; that this, the long shared stillness of two creatures who would never understand each other, was its own whole and sufficient thing. Then he rose, slowly, the last time, up into the bright, and she stayed in her dark, and what had passed between them stayed, unnamed, complete.

Chapter 47: All Her Now

Her arms began to lose the half-lives they had always had, and the change should have frightened her and did not, because by now there was almost nothing left in her that was not the strands.

All her life her arms had been partly their own. The third arm had scouted ahead while she thought of other things; two arms had argued with the body over whether to take a meal; the fifth had wandered off to taste a thing the rest of her ignored; she had been not one mind but a loose committee of nine, the central self and the eight half-selves of her arms, and the negotiation among them had been so constant and so deep that it was most of what it felt like to be her.

Now that committee was dissolving, not into discord but into a strange simplicity. The third arm no longer scouted; it fanned the strands, or it did not, but it did not go off on its own errands any more. The fifth arm did not argue; there was nothing to argue about, no meal to weigh, no danger to flee toward or from, only the strands and the fanning, and on that one point all of her agreed completely. The half-lives quieted. The arms drew in toward a single purpose, and the many-minded creature she had been simplified, in the spending of herself, toward one thing, one current, one slow undivided attention turned wholly upward at the pale ropes on the ceiling.

It did not frighten her. The old self that might have feared the loss of its own multiplicity had thinned along with everything else, and what remained did not mourn the quieting of the arms because what remained was barely separate from the strands at all. She had been a committee of nine bright wanting selves walking a reef; she was becoming a single fading current of care in a dark vault, all of her, every arm, every dimming patch of skin, turned to the one task, simplified, unified, almost gone, and entirely at peace in the going.

Chapter 48: The Quickening

The eggs began to move, and she felt the moving before she saw it, and the last strength in her gathered toward them, fanning harder, because the strands that had been still so long were coming alive.

It came as the faintest change in the strands under her arms — a stirring, an internal urgency where there had been only stillness, the pale cases on the ceiling beginning, each one, to hold a moving thing. She put her arms to them and felt it, the small slow pulsing within the clusters, hundreds of tiny lives quickening at once in the dark she had kept for them, and the eye, when she turned it up to the strands, could just make out the shapes inside the cases now, curled, minute, eight-armed, perfect, each one a creature in waiting pressed into its pale shell.

The sight and the feel of it woke the last of her. Whatever reserve remained in her spent body, whatever final current she could still raise, she turned it now to the fanning, harder than she had fanned in weeks, washing the quickening strands with all the water she could still draw and push, because the moving things needed the water, needed the clean current over them, needed her, now, at the very end of her, more than they had needed her in all the still weeks of waiting. She had hung them and tended them and cleaned them and defended them through the long dark, and now they were stirring toward the moment she had spent her whole self to bring them to, and she gave the last of what she had to easing them toward it.

She was very nearly gone. The fasting had hollowed her; the color had drained to almost nothing; the arms had simplified to a single slow current of care. But the strands were quickening, and so she rose, one last time, to meet them — fanning, fanning, the dim worn remnant of a bright creature pouring its final strength up into the pale moving ropes on the ceiling of the dark it had made — and there was no fear in her and no grief, only this last fierce gentle work, the easing of her children toward the light she would not follow them into.

Chapter 49: The Rising

The hatching began, and the small perfect shapes broke free and spiraled up toward the light, and she fanned them loose into the water, and the world above her dark filled with her children going up.

It came all at once, or nearly — the cases opening, the moving things working free, the first of them slipping out of the strands into the open water of the den, tiny and complete, eight minute arms unfolding, the whole shape of what she was made impossibly small and new. And she fanned them. With the last current of her body she washed them loose from the ceiling, helped them free of the emptying cases, pushed them gently on the water toward the gap in her wall and the bright reef beyond, sending them out, one after another and then in a rising cloud, into the world she had walked and was leaving.

They rose. That was what her kind did at the beginning, before the reef and the dens and the long territories, before any of it — the newly hatched rose, up toward the bright ceiling, up into the open water to drift and grow and, the few that lived, to sink back down one day to reefs of their own. Through the gap in her wall she watched them go, a thin pale stream of tiny lives spiraling up out of her dark into the slanting light, the water above her den filling with them, hundreds of small rising things, the whole spent purpose of her body lifting away from her toward the surface she had never broken and never would.

She fanned the last of them loose. She had no strength now for anything but this, and she needed no strength for anything but this, because this was the last thing, the thing all the rest had been for — the hunting and the storing and the chord on the current and the sealed dark and the long fast and the simplified arms, all of it, her whole bright brief life, had been for this single rising of small perfect shapes up toward the light. She gave them to the water. They went up. The strands hung empty on the ceiling above her, their work and hers complete, and the den, for the first time since she had sealed it, held no future, only the spent and fading present of the creature who had kept it.

Chapter 50: A Color Without a Name

At the very end there was a color, and it was one she had never worn and had no name for and would not have named if she could, and it rose through her dimming skin as the light above filled with her children, and then there was the brass, catching the morning, and then there was the sea.

She lay in the mouth of her emptied den, the strands bare above her, the last of the small rising shapes spiraling up through the gap into the bright, and the worn remnant of her body let go, by inches, the way the season let go of the warm, the way the storm let go of the reef, without struggle, the long arithmetic of the fast come finally to its sum. Her arms, all her now, all one, rested still on the silt. Her skin, that had spoken her whole life in color, that had blazed and warned and courted and defended and remembered, dimmed toward the last of its language.

And a color came. Not the red of the moray's strike, not the silver of the cold deep, not the satisfied bronze of a fed body or the dark gold of the brass or the strobing fury of the defense — none of the colors of her life, which had all risen and washed through her in the long vigil and sunk. This was another color, one she had never thrown, rising now through the dimming skin of her own accord, unsummoned, unhurried, a shade that belonged to no warning and no hunger and no fear, a color she had no use for and had never needed until this single moment and would need only once. It rose through her and held, soft and strange and entirely her own, while above her the water was full of the small spiraling shapes going up into the morning light.

Through the gap in her wall the brass instrument lay in the silt where it had always lain, and the early light came slanting down through the bright ceiling and found the green-dark gleam of it and bent away changed, catching the morning as it had caught so many mornings, a small far brightness at the edge of a fading eye. The children rose. The strands hung empty. The brass caught the light. The nameless color held a moment longer in the still creature in the mouth of the dark she had made and won and filled and kept — and then the reef went on, turning slowly through its days, around the wreck, around the empty den, around the bright rising scatter of small new lives that did not know her and never would, the way she had never known the mother who had spent herself in the dark to send her, once, up into the very same light.
