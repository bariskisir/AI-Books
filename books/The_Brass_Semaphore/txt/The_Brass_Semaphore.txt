Chapter 1: Fog Arm

The brass semaphore moved too fast.

Ada had come to the museum expecting varnish problems, donor impatience, and a few missing screws. That was the usual work of late-career expertise: people called after deciding beauty mattered more than accuracy, then hoped the expert would bless the decision already made. She had nearly refused Olan's invitation until he mentioned the semaphore had been returned to motion. A moving signal instrument was not furniture. Once it moved, it spoke, and speech could be wrong.

She set her bag beside a covered crate and listened before anyone touched the handle. Old buildings carried sound poorly, but machines carried it honestly. The galleries hummed with ventilation, rain tapped the windows, and somewhere below a delivery cart thudded over a threshold. Then Lee took the handle, and the brass answered too quickly.

Ada Vey heard it before anyone else did. The sound was small, hardly more than a catch in the gear train as the left arm rose through the gray museum light. The gallery had not opened yet. Work lamps stood on the floor like dock lanterns. Dust showed in the beams. Beyond the tall windows, Lorn Harbor lay under morning fog, all masts and cranes reduced to blunt shadows.

Director Olan Marr stood with his hands folded behind him, the posture of a man trying to look patient with experts. "Beautiful, isn't it?"

"Restored," Ada said. "Not yet beautiful."

Olan's smile thinned. He had hired her as an outside authenticator because donors liked independent expertise, not because he wanted a retired signal officer to disagree with the room. Ada kept her eyes on the machine. The semaphore stood on a temporary oak plinth, its brass arms newly cleaned, its paint dark and even, its lenses polished to a wet shine. It should have pleased her. Instead the left arm climbed with a short, impatient snap, then settled three degrees above proper halt.

"You saw something," Olan said.

"I heard it first."

Calder Pike stood near the velvet rope. He wore a navy suit cut to suggest both money and nautical sacrifice. His great-grandfather's portrait, still wrapped in brown paper, leaned against the wall behind him. "The instrument is over a century old, Ms. Vey. A little temperament is allowed."

"Temperament is for patrons," Ada said. "Machines have causes."

The conservator operating the handle, a young technician named Lee, looked stricken. Ada softened her voice. "Run it again, please. From halt to warning."

Lee moved the handle. The right arm rose cleanly. The left followed with that same quick tooth, a click arriving half a beat early. Ada counted in her head from old habit: one, two, hold, return. The arm did not obey the public timing she had taught cadets until they hated her. A public signal officer would have noticed. A tourist would not. A smuggler might have preferred it.

Calder stepped closer. "My great-grandfather saved three ships with that machine."

"Then it deserves exactness."

"It deserves honor."

"Those are not opposites unless someone has made them so."

Olan cleared his throat. "We are three weeks from opening. The board preview is tomorrow. If this is a minor adjustment, I would rather we make it quietly."

Ada looked down at the counterweight, at the fresh polish that had not quite reached the old scratches near the hub, at the way the left arm wanted to hurry through fog no longer present. She had spent thirty years reading flags, lamps, blinker shutters, coded boards, and the faces of men who had made mistakes at sea. Wrong rhythm had a smell to it, metallic and dry.

"No quiet adjustment," she said.

The technician released the handle. The semaphore clicked again as it returned to halt. In the empty gallery, the old brass sounded less like a relic than a witness clearing its throat.

"You are being dramatic," Calder said.

"No," Ada said. "I am being early."

She took out her notebook and wrote the first line of what she suspected would become a very unpopular report: Left arm over-travels at halt; operating speed inconsistent with public harbor code.

Outside, a foghorn made a low note and vanished into the harbor. The sound reached the windows after the brass had already gone still.

Chapter 2: Museum Legend

The proposed label had been printed on thick cream stock and mounted in a sample frame. Olan handed it to Ada as if it were fragile evidence of competence. The heading read: Admiral Elias Pike, Voice of Lorn Harbor. Beneath it came fog, storm, rescue, courage, civic duty, and three ships brought safely home by the clarity of Pike's command.

The sample frame was heavier than it needed to be. Ada noticed that first. Museums often made uncertain statements physically substantial, trusting wood, glass, and cream paper to lend weight where sourcing had failed. The label had a small brass mock-up beside it, already polished for the preview. A visitor would see the shine, then read the certainty, and never know a question had been kept outside the frame.

Olan watched her read with the strained hope of a man waiting for a verdict he had scheduled a reception around. Calder stood near the window, not close enough to seem anxious, close enough to hear every word. Jonas kept his eyes on a stack of folders, but his pencil had stopped moving.

Ada read the whole label once. Then she read it again, slower, looking for the parts that had been left out.

"Where is the cargo case?" she asked.

Olan blinked. "The what?"

"Cargo records. Salvage reports. Customs forms. Insurance claims. If three vessels were rescued in heavy fog, every office in the harbor would have produced paper."

Calder answered before the director could. "This exhibit honors service, not bureaucracy."

"Signals are bureaucracy with arms."

A man at the archive table coughed into his sleeve. He was thin, bearded, and dressed in the resigned brown cardigan of a person who handled paper more honestly than people. Olan introduced him as Jonas Reed, chief archivist. Ada suspected the cough had contained approval.

The four of them stood in the temporary reading room behind the gallery, surrounded by crates and foam blocks. Through the open door Ada could see the semaphore on its plinth, the arms motionless now, innocent in the way objects were innocent until touched by records. Calder kept glancing toward it as if checking whether it still belonged to him.

"The Pike rescue is one of the harbor's central stories," Olan said. "The label summarizes what visitors need."

"Visitors need facts."

"Facts require selection."

"So does fraud."

The room went quiet. A cart squeaked somewhere in the corridor. Calder's face took on the stillness of expensive anger.

"That is an ugly word," he said.

"It is also a useful one when documents have gone missing."

Jonas looked up from a folder. "Some watch logs are incomplete."

Olan gave him a warning look. Jonas ignored it with the calm of a man who had spent years being warned by people who later needed his filing system.

"Incomplete how?" Ada asked.

"The public tower logs for three fog nights contain later copies, not originals. The signal book has gaps. The customs summary for the Maribel incident refers to an attached cargo schedule that is not attached."

Calder gave a short laugh. "A century of damp basements and civic neglect, and now my family is on trial because paper decayed."

"Paper decays in patterns," Jonas said.

Ada liked him immediately for that.

She placed the label on the table. Its language had the soft shine of donor prose. No one was responsible; everyone was brave; weather did all the dangerous verbs. It did not mention missing watch logs, disputed cargoes, private settlements, or harbor families who still used Pike as a curse in dockside arguments. Museum legends often excluded anyone who had filed a claim.

"Who wrote this?" Ada asked.

Olan hesitated. "The development office prepared a draft with historical input."

"And donor input."

"The Pike Foundation funded the restoration."

"Funding is not authorship."

Calder stepped toward the table. "You were asked to authenticate an instrument, Ms. Vey, not rewrite civic memory."

Ada slid the label back to Olan. "This is a speech. I was asked to authenticate an instrument. The instrument is behaving oddly, the records around it are thin where they should be thick, and your label has already decided what the evidence means."

Jonas closed his folder with a soft thump. "I can pull the signal books."

"Do that," Ada said.

Olan looked from Ada to Calder to the sample label, seeing the preview reception tilt out of alignment. "Let us be careful."

"Careful is excellent," Ada said. "Careful starts with the books."

Chapter 3: Signal Book

Jonas kept the signal books in a locked steel cabinet behind the public archive desk. The cabinet looked newer than the books and less trustworthy. When he opened it, the smell came out first: leather, dust, iron keys, old glue, and the faint salt that entered every object in Lorn Harbor no matter how far from the water it slept.

He made Ada sign the request slip though she was standing beside him and though the museum director had just told him to cooperate. She liked him better for it. Good archives did not run on trust alone. Trust was how paper walked away in briefcases and returned decades later as family legend.

"Apologies," Jonas said, turning the log toward her.

"Never apologize for custody."

He looked up, surprised, then gave the smallest possible nod. The archive room was narrow, with a public desk at one end and rolling shelves at the other. The windows had been painted shut before anyone now employed by the museum was born. Everything important smelled faintly trapped.

"We have harbor codes from 1879 through 1946," Jonas said. "After that the tower system was decommissioned and everyone decided radio would make human error obsolete."

"That worked out beautifully," Ada said.

He smiled without showing teeth and lifted a large volume onto a foam cradle. The cover was black, cracked at the hinge, with Harbor Signal Code 1917 stamped in gilt that had almost rubbed away. Jonas handed Ada a pair of gloves. She put them on and waited while he opened the book to the index.

The pages had been ruled by hand, the ink brown now, the signal numbers arranged in careful columns. Ada let her eyes move through them the way they had once moved across boards in rain. Distress. Pilot required. Quarantine. Customs inspection. Vessel dragging anchor. Fog drill. Cargo emergency.

Then the paper stopped.

Three pages had been removed with a blade. Not torn. Cut close to the binding with patient pressure. The stubs remained, narrow and clean, like old scars.

"Before your time?" Ada asked.

"Before my birth," Jonas said. "Not before my annoyance."

"Inventory notes?"

"The 1958 accession list says complete. The 1974 condition report says minor losses. It does not specify which losses, because apparently specificity was rationed."

Ada leaned closer. The missing pages fell in a precise range. Fog drills. Emergency cargo signals. Private-yard exceptions. Convenient absences liked company.

"Who used private-yard exceptions?"

"Repair yards, coal yards, sometimes naval contractors. Temporary signals for local coordination. They were supposed to be logged separately and expired after use."

"Supposed to be?"

"A phrase that keeps archivists employed."

Ada moved to the index. Near the bottom of one page, a signal number had been penciled in after the ink entries: 7-4-2. The hand was different. The graphite had smeared slightly under someone's thumb.

"Pike rescue signal?" she asked.

Jonas nodded. "According to the museum narrative. The number appears in a school pamphlet from the 1930s and in several anniversary articles. No one cites the original code."

"According to the book, nothing."

"According to the surviving book, nothing."

"Fair."

Jonas reached into a flat box and brought out a watch log. This one was a copy, made in a clerk's hand, with Public Tower Night Watch written across the top. Ada disliked copies when originals should exist, but copies had their own cowardly usefulness. They sometimes preserved what someone later wished had vanished.

On three fog nights, the same number appeared in the margin: 7-4-2. No explanation. No full signal text. Just the number, tight and slanted, beside times when horn intervals had been noted.

Ada felt old training wake in her hands. A meaningless signal repeated in clear weather might be error. A meaningless signal repeated in fog was not meaningless. It was private.

"Who had access to this cabinet?" she asked.

"In the last hundred years?"

"Start with the dead and work forward."

Jonas rested both hands on the table. "Pike family representatives funded two cataloging projects. They had supervised access. Museum directors had access. Harbor Board clerks before transfer. No full chain."

"And the missing pages?"

"No one admits to missing them."

Ada looked through the archive window toward the gallery, where Calder Pike stood near the covered portrait, speaking into his phone with his back to the room.

"People rarely admit to blades," she said.

Jonas lowered his voice. "Do you think the instrument was altered to send 7-4-2?"

"I think we have a machine moving wrong, a number with no official meaning, and fog nights where someone wrote it down anyway. That is enough to keep going."

"Good," Jonas said. "I hate those missing pages."

"Then let us give them company."

Chapter 4: Clockmakers Gear

Mara Quill arrived after lunch with a canvas tool roll, a square jaw, and the air of a person who would rather speak to springs than committees. She was the museum's contract clockmaker, brought in originally to make the semaphore move for the opening. Now she stood before it with both sleeves rolled to the elbow and a loupe clipped over one eye.

The rain had followed her in. Drops clung to the shoulders of her work coat and darkened the canvas roll where it rested against her hip. She did not remove the coat until she had walked once around the semaphore, close but not touching, as if greeting an animal known to bite. Ada had seen engineers do the same with engines, pilots with damaged panels, surgeons with difficult instruments. Respect began before contact.

Olan introduced her too formally. Mara ignored the courtesy and looked at the operating handle instead.

"Who polished the pivots?" she asked.

Olan looked at Lee, the technician. Lee looked at the floor.

"I did the exterior only," Lee said. "As instructed."

"Good. The exterior lies. The inside confesses."

Ada liked Mara too, though she had known her for less than a minute.

They moved the semaphore into the conservation room, where the light was cleaner and the air smelled of solvent, paper, and old wood. Calder insisted on observing. Olan allowed it, which told Ada something about how the museum's spine had been trained. Jonas came with a notebook. The room filled around the instrument until Mara turned and stared at everyone.

"If you breathe on my screws, leave."

Calder opened his mouth, then shut it. Mara removed the side plate.

The gear train was more delicate than the public would imagine. Brass wheels nested behind the case, each tooth cut to pass movement from the operating handle to the arms above. The metal was dark where polish had not reached. Old grease sat in the corners like dried honey.

Mara set a gear under the bench lamp. The loupe made one eye enormous. "Replacement gear."

"Modern?" Ada asked.

"Old enough. Wrong enough."

"Wrong how?"

"Tooth count." Mara took out calipers and a pencil. "The original wheel should have thirty-six teeth. This has thirty-four, and two are dressed by hand. See the file marks? Whoever did it wanted a faster lift through the middle of the throw."

Ada bent over the lamp. The marks were fine but visible, small planes catching light at a different angle. Not factory work. Not careless work either.

"How much faster?" she asked.

"Half a beat, maybe less, depending on the operator."

"Enough to distinguish one signal from another?"

Mara looked up. "For whom?"

"A trained watcher in fog, expecting the difference."

"Yes."

Olan rubbed the bridge of his nose. Calder crossed his arms.

"Could repair explain it?" Calder asked.

"Bad repair, yes," Mara said. "Clever repair, no."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only honest one. If a gear broke and someone needed the instrument working by dawn, they might fit what they had. But then they would accept roughness everywhere. This wheel is wrong in one useful way and careful in all the others."

Ada studied the linkage. The modified gear did not change the official positions. A visitor watching the arms settle would see the same vocabulary: halt, warning, cross, return. The difference lived in motion, not posture. Someone had wanted the arms to move faster without changing the public code. That meant a second code could live in timing.

"Can you reassemble it for testing?" she asked.

"Yes. But I want photographs first, and scrapings of the grease. Someone mixed something in."

"Graphite?" Ada asked.

Mara's large loupe eye narrowed. "You've seen this trick."

"I have seen quiet things made quieter."

Jonas wrote that down.

Calder stepped nearer despite Mara's earlier warning. "You are all building a scandal out of wear."

Mara put the gear down very carefully. "Mr. Pike, wear is random until it isn't. This is a decision."

No one spoke for a moment. Outside the conservation room, a school group passed in the corridor, their voices bright and unrelated.

Mara smiled thinly at Ada. "Your brass friend has habits."

"No," Ada said. "Its users did."

Chapter 5: False Drill

They ran the first test after the museum closed, when the public galleries had emptied and the building settled into its working noises: pipes ticking, carts rolling far away, rain against the high glass. Mara had reassembled the gear train with witness marks and temporary seals. A camera stood on a tripod. Jonas held a stopwatch. Olan stood in the doorway as if keeping one foot in administrative denial.

The lights were different after hours. Without visitors, the museum lost its polished voice and became a workplace: extension cords, dust cloths, paper signs taped crookedly to doors, staff shoes squeaking in service corridors. Ada preferred it. Public rooms lied less when they forgot to perform.

Iris had not yet entered the case, but Jonas had already begun behaving as if a judge might ask him what happened. He numbered the camera card, wrote the time, and made Lee initial a line in the notebook for operating assistance. Lee did so with the seriousness of a recruit accepting first watch.

Ada took the handle.

The old wood was smooth where hands had worn it. She paused before moving it. Instruments deserved a second of attention before use, especially instruments suspected of lying.

"Official rescue first," she said.

Mara nodded. Jonas lifted the stopwatch.

Ada moved the arms through the public rescue sequence from the surviving code: halt, warning, double rise, cross, return. She moved slowly, deliberately, the way a tower officer would signal through heavy weather when confusion could kill. The arms answered with solemn dignity. The camera clicked. Jonas called times.

"Now 7-4-2," Ada said.

Olan shifted. "If the number has no official meaning, what exactly are you performing?"

"A hypothesis."

She placed the handle at halt. Then she moved through the sequence suggested by the marginal number and the old signal taxonomy: seventh family, fourth arm pattern, second timing value. Halt, rise, cross, return. At ordinary speed it looked like nothing, a malformed drill, an operator correcting his own mistake.

"Again," Ada said.

This time she snapped through the modified gear's sweet place. The left arm cut upward half a beat early, the right crossed, the return came clean and quick. It was over before the room had decided to watch it.

Jonas lowered the stopwatch. "That was different."

"Yes."

"A watcher could catch it."

"Only if expecting it."

"And if not?"

"It looks like a clumsy correction."

Smuggling loved clumsy corrections. They let honest people explain away what they saw. A clerk heard an odd pause and called it weather. A customs man saw a blurred arm and called it poor maintenance. A captain waiting in the east lane saw permission.

Ada ran it again. The same quick flash appeared between the imagined beats of a foghorn. She could almost feel the harbor arranged around it: tower lamp above, customs pier blind below, a boat low in the fog with men holding their breath over covered cargo.

Olan watched from the door. "We cannot imply criminal use without proof."

"We are not implying. We are testing."

"The board will hear implication."

"Then invite them to learn the difference."

Jonas made a small sound that might have been a laugh. Olan ignored it.

Calder entered halfway through the next sequence, his shoes sharp on the gallery floor. "What is this?"

"A timing test," Ada said.

"On whose authority?"

"The museum director's, until he says otherwise."

Olan looked trapped. "Continue."

Ada moved the arms through 7-4-2 at the modified speed. Calder's face changed for less than a second. Not surprise. Recognition. His eyes went to the left arm first, then to the return, as if his body knew where to look before his manners remembered innocence.

Then the donor mask returned.

"Meaningless," he said. "Any machine can be made to look sinister if handled theatrically."

Ada set the handle back to halt. "You knew where the signal would finish."

"I know my family's artifact."

"That was not my question."

Olan stepped in. "Enough for tonight."

Ada let go of the handle. She had spent a career watching men pretend not to see flags: officers who ignored distress because rescue was inconvenient, captains who misread orders because profit waited elsewhere, politicians who called warning signals decorative. Calder's blink was not proof. It was not even evidence she could file. But it told her where pressure might later make a crack.

Jonas stopped the camera. "I will preserve the recording."

"Make two copies," Ada said.

"Three," Mara said. "I dislike disappearing things."

Calder looked at the covered machine, then at Ada. "Be careful with old stories."

"I am," she said. "That is why I don't trust them."

Chapter 6: Customs Question

Iris Senn arrived the next morning with three gray-tied bundles, a black coat wet at the shoulders, and the expression of a solicitor who had already decided politeness would be rationed. Olan introduced her in the archive room with visible caution. Calder, who had not been invited, appeared five minutes later and took a chair without asking.

The bundles came from offices that still used ribbon because ribbon made delay look dignified. Iris placed them on the table and did not untie them at once. She asked who was present, who represented whom, and whether the room was being recorded. Olan answered like a director. Calder answered like a man accustomed to being a category of his own.

"Then we will be clear," Iris said.

Ada liked her before the first document opened. Clarity, in Ada's experience, was not a temperament. It was a discipline, and Iris carried it like a blade kept sharp by use rather than display.

"I represent descendants of two cargo owners tied to the Pike rescue period," Iris said. "I also advise the Harbor Claims Cooperative when it can afford me, which is not often."

"The museum is not a court," Calder said.

"No," Iris said. "Courts have better rules about evidence."

Ada turned a laugh into a cough. Jonas did not bother.

Iris untied the first bundle. The tape left a pale line across the paper where no dust had settled. "Three cargo claims vanished after fog-night transfers. Tea from the Maribel. Copper wire from the Eastwake. Medical glass from the Orison."

"Vanished how?" Ada asked.

"Declared lost in fog confusion. Later compensated through private settlement. No surviving settlement agreement in public records. No full customs seizure record. No admiralty judgment. Just references to matters resolved."

"All during Pike's watch?"

"All during Pike command years. Two during weeks when the tower logs mention extraordinary fog drills. One on the night of the famous rescue."

Calder leaned back. "Lorn Harbor was dangerous. Cargo was lost. Families grieved and then embellished."

Iris looked at him over the rims of her glasses. "Families also kept receipts."

She opened the second bundle. Inside were copies of claims, letters, warehouse tallies, and a brittle newspaper clipping praising Admiral Pike's steady hand in weather that had frightened lesser men. Ada took Jonas's watch log and placed it beside the customs file. The dates aligned on two nights. In both margins, 7-4-2 appeared near the hour of the alleged transfer. On the third, the corner of the log page had been torn away exactly where a marginal note would have sat.

"Convenient tear," Jonas said.

"Your archive has many conveniences," Iris said.

"I dislike every one of them personally."

Ada traced the timeline with a gloved finger. "The cargo was reported aboard before fog. After fog, the manifests say confusion, delay, loss. Later the owners accept private money, but no court order explains why."

"Correct."

"And the cargo itself?"

"Pieces reappear in later sale records. Copper wire through Pike Yard agents. Medical glass through a hospital supplier whose director owed the admiral a favor. Tea through a wholesaler married into a Pike cousin line."

Olan sat down slowly. The chair creaked under the weight of institutional trouble.

Calder's face remained controlled. "Old commercial networks prove nothing. Everyone in Lorn Harbor sold to everyone else."

"Then the records will be easy to explain," Iris said.

Ada liked that too. Simple truth had a way of inviting paperwork.

Iris tapped the Maribel folder. "My clients' grandparents were told the sea took everything. They were poor enough to accept less than the cargo was worth and respectable enough to be ashamed of making noise. Pike was already being called a hero. No one wanted to accuse a hero during a war year."

"The sea takes many things," Ada said. "It does not alter signal books."

Iris's smile was brief. "That is why I came."

Rain ticked against the archive windows, thin and steady. Beyond the glass, fog pressed close enough to make the harbor disappear at the curb. Lorn had always been good at hiding shapes. Paper, less so. Paper failed in different ways. It yellowed, tore, migrated to wrong folders, survived the people who lied around it.

"What do you need from me?" Ada asked.

"A technical opinion. If this instrument could have transmitted a private signal, and if that signal corresponds to cargo movements, I can ask a judge to preserve everything before family memory improves itself."

Calder stood. "You are threatening litigation against a museum exhibit."

"No," Iris said. "I am threatening litigation against destruction."

Olan looked toward the gallery. "We should pause the preview."

Calder turned on him. "You should remember who paid for that restoration."

Ada closed the watch log. "That is becoming easier every hour."

Chapter 7: Foghorn Minute

Bram Vale met Ada at Pier Four, where the harbor smelled of diesel, wet rope, and fish boxes rinsed but never truly clean. His tug, the Nell Vale, sat low against black pilings, paint scraped from years of useful contact. Bram was broad, gray-bearded, and suspicious in a practical way. He looked at Ada's shoes before he looked at her face.

She had worn harbor shoes on purpose: old leather, flat soles, salt stains that no polish could quite remove. Bram noticed and gave nothing away. A gull cry cut through the fog, then the low clank of a chain from a nearby workboat. The museum, only four streets behind them, already felt like a different country.

Ada carried a notebook in her coat pocket and a recorder in her bag. She did not take either out yet. People who had been ignored for generations deserved a conversation before becoming a source.

"Museum woman?" he asked.

"Signal woman hired by the museum."

"Better. Museum people step where they're told. Signal people look up."

He led her down to the tug's galley. The ceiling was low enough that Ada had to duck. A kettle hissed on a burner. Laminated tide charts were taped to a wall beside a photograph of three women standing on a pier in oilskins, each with the same stubborn set to the mouth.

"My grandmother," Bram said, tapping the middle woman. "She kept fog logs because public horn records were often wrong and private survival depended on minutes. The harbor office wrote what it was supposed to do. Tug families wrote what happened."

He placed a notebook on the galley table. The cover had softened from handling. Inside, the handwriting changed over generations: ink, pencil, ballpoint, then ink again when someone sentimental took over. Bram turned to a marked page and pushed it toward Ada.

"My grandmother wrote what she heard."

Ada read the entries. Fog dense. Horn weak at public tower. Bell clear east. Pike lamp bright. Then times in a column. The public watch log placed the rescue signal at 02:10. Bram's family fog log placed a horn silence from 02:08 to 02:12.

"No horn, no official timing," Ada said.

"But the semaphore could still flash."

"To someone close."

Bram pointed through the small cabin window. The old signal tower rose above warehouses and fog, its top platform just visible. "A small boat in the east lane could see the arms under the lamp. Customs pier couldn't. Not with fog sitting low. My grandmother said it was like watching a man whisper from a church steeple."

"Did she write that?"

"She said it. My father said she said it. I wrote it down later, because the Vales have learned what happens when only Pikes write things."

Ada copied the page by hand before photographing it. She distrusted photographs alone; hand copying forced the mind to notice. Horn silence. Tower lamp visible. East lane traffic. At 02:11, a short notation: fast arms, not drill.

"She knew it was wrong," Ada said.

"She knew enough. But she was a tug captain's daughter in a harbor where Admiral Pike could get a man frozen out of contracts by lunchtime."

"Did your family ever file a statement?"

Bram snorted. "To whom? The same men eating supper at Pike's table?"

The kettle began to whistle. He poured tea into two mugs strong enough to tan leather. Ada accepted hers and let the heat settle into her fingers.

"Why bring this out now?" she asked.

"Because the museum put his portrait in the paper. Said children should learn courage from him." Bram looked through the window at the tower. "My grandchildren are grown, but I still dislike lies being polished for young eyes."

"The exhibit may change."

"May?"

"Will, if evidence keeps behaving."

Bram's laugh was short. "Evidence has been behaving. People haven't."

Ada could not argue. She looked again at the notebook. The public story needed storm chaos, horns sounding, officers shouting, ships nearly wrecked and saved by one heroic signal. Bram's page gave it a quiet minute instead: four minutes of horn silence, a lamp visible only to those placed correctly, a fast movement through fog.

Fog had been treated as absence. It was becoming a schedule.

"Would you testify to this if asked?" Ada said.

Bram took a long drink of tea. "I don't wear suits well."

"Courts have survived worse."

"Then yes. But I will bring the notebook myself. No courier. No museum drawer."

"Wise."

On deck, a deckhand shouted about a line. Bram rose, suddenly all work. Before leaving, he tapped the page with one thick finger.

"Don't let them call her mistaken."

Ada closed her notebook. "I won't. But I will prove why she wasn't."

Chapter 8: Brass Filings

Talia Hesk worked in the materials lab two streets behind the museum, above a chandlery that sold rope to tourists and real line to people who knew the difference. She had trained as a metallurgist, then drifted into conservation science because, as she told Ada, "old objects lie less creatively than living clients."

Her lab had no romance to it. Metal shelves, labeled solvents, two microscopes, a fume hood, a coffee cup banished to the only clear corner. Ada found the room reassuring. It did not flatter objects. It tested them.

Mara put the evidence envelopes down as if setting charges. Jonas placed the chain-of-custody form beside them and aligned it with the table edge. Talia noticed the gesture.

"Archivist?" she asked.

"Unfortunately for everyone careless," Jonas said.

Mara brought the bearing scrapings in labeled envelopes. Ada brought the chain-of-custody form Iris had insisted on. Jonas brought unnecessary pencils and then made himself useful by documenting every transfer. Talia watched them arrange themselves around her workbench with quiet amusement.

"This is either a serious matter," she said, "or you all enjoy paperwork as recreation."

"Both," Jonas said.

Talia opened the first envelope and tipped a dark smear onto a glass slide. Under magnification, the old grease became a landscape: black ridges, amber clumps, bright flecks of metal. She separated the filings with a fine probe and transferred them to a small tray.

"Brass filings inside the lower bearing," she said after the first scan. "Packed under old grease, not introduced by the recent exterior cleaning."

"Original material?" Ada asked.

"Some. Not all."

Talia moved to the spectrometer. The machine hummed with efficient indifference. Numbers appeared on the screen. She printed them, frowned, and ran the sample again.

"Two alloys. The first is consistent with naval brass from the original instrument. Copper-zinc-tin composition, trace lead where I expect it. The second has a slightly different zinc ratio and impurity profile. Cheaper repair stock. Common in private dockyards early last century."

Mara folded her arms. "Pike Yard used that alloy."

Calder was not present, which made the room more productive.

"Can you date it?" Ada asked.

"Not to a year. But it is period-appropriate. Not modern. Not from the current restoration."

The sentence settled over the bench. The modification had been made while the semaphore was operational, during the admiral's era, not by a careless restorer trying to make an old machine move for school groups.

Mara looked offended on behalf of time. "So someone altered it in service."

"Yes," Talia said. "And used it enough to produce wear."

She opened the second envelope. This sample came from inside the bearing seat, where Mara had noticed the strange dark sheen. Talia warmed it gently, tested a fleck, then touched it with a solvent that released a faint mineral smell.

"Graphite," she said.

Ada had expected it and still felt the small tightening of confirmation.

Jonas looked up. "In grease?"

"Mixed deliberately. It reduces friction and can quiet a fast movement. Not unusual in some machinery. Unusual here if the instrument was meant only for public signaling."

Mara nodded. "A public semaphore doesn't need to whisper. It needs to be seen."

"And heard by its operator," Ada said. "A loud gear tells you it moved. A quiet one tells someone else nothing happened."

Talia labeled the printouts. Her handwriting was precise and unfriendly to ambiguity. "I can state that the bearing contains evidence of historical modification using non-original brass and graphite-treated lubricant. I cannot state who ordered it."

"No one honest expects a filing to name its employer," Iris said from the doorway. She had arrived silently enough to make Jonas jump.

Ada wrote in her notebook: altered gear, quieted bearing, nonstandard timing. Then below it: period repair stock, private-yard profile, extended use.

Calder's legend had begun to acquire machinery. Not the heroic kind. The heroic version of the semaphore stood in storm and saved ships by speaking clearly. This version had been tuned to move quickly through a gap in sound, greased to reduce complaint, and repaired with metal from yards that profited from cargo.

Talia placed the filings in a small evidence vial. They looked like nothing: dust and shine, less than a pinch. Ada had seen cases turn on less. A missing comma in a log. A lamp wick trimmed too short. A flag folded in a locker where it should have flown.

"Can we compare the alloy to Pike Yard stock?" Ada asked.

"If someone produces a reference sample."

Mara gave a dry smile. "I know where three broken Pike Yard winches are stored."

"Legally?" Jonas asked.

"Museum accessioned. Poorly described. Beautifully neglected."

"Then let's neglect them usefully," Ada said.

Outside the lab window, fog pressed along the street at ankle height, turning parked cars into hulls. Lorn Harbor hid itself in layers. The filings, bright under glass, refused to hide.

Chapter 9: Patron Dinner

Calder hosted the board dinner in a private room above the Harbor Exchange, where the windows faced the water and the silver had been polished to accuse the guests of fingerprints. Ada would have preferred a sandwich in the archive, but Olan insisted she attend. "Visibility," he said. "Reassurance."

The invitation had called the evening informal. Ada had spent enough years around rank to know informal often meant rules would be enforced without being named. She wore a dark jacket, carried no bag, and put her notebook in an inside pocket where no one could mistake it for a prop.

The Harbor Exchange had once priced cargo and now priced memory. Framed bills of lading hung near the stair. Brass lamps lit oil paintings of ships no one in the room had loaded. At the top landing, a host took coats with the smooth pity reserved for people not rich enough to belong.

"You want me to reassure donors while investigating donor history?"

"I want them to see that we are measured."

"Then invite a ruler."

He did not laugh. She went because donors said useful things near wine and because Calder had recognized the false signal before he remembered not to.

The room filled with trustees, spouses, two city councilors, and a retired commodore who wore a club tie like a credential. Calder seated Ada halfway down the table, close enough to be watched and far enough to be managed. Olan sat at Calder's right. Jonas had not been invited. Ada envied him.

The first course was small and architectural. Conversation moved around the exhibit with the caution of boats in fog.

"The restoration is a civic event," Calder said when the wine had been poured. "Lorn Harbor needs reminders of what disciplined service once meant."

"It also needs working drains," a trustee said.

There was polite laughter. Calder smiled as if drains, too, had been funded by his ancestors.

"The exhibit will restore civic pride," he continued.

"History sometimes reduces pride," Ada said.

A trustee across from her laughed, then stopped when he saw she had not joked.

Calder turned slightly. "Ms. Vey has brought a rigorous eye to the instrument."

"And ear," Ada said.

"Indeed. Though I hope we remember proportion. A mechanical irregularity does not outweigh a century of testimony."

"It depends on who wrote the testimony."

Forks paused. Olan gave Ada the look of a man watching a vase tip in slow motion.

The retired commodore leaned forward. "Signals vary in bad weather. I have seen clean drills become nonsense in fog."

"So have I," Ada said. "That is why clean timing matters."

Calder's smile remained. "My great-grandfather commanded during war shortages, storms, labor unrest, and a harbor full of frightened merchants. If some repairs were improvised, that reflects necessity."

"Necessity usually leaves invoices."

"My family has supported this museum for decades."

"Then it can survive accurate labels."

The woman to Ada's left, a trustee named Helene, set down her glass. "What exactly would an accurate label say at this point?"

"That the semaphore contains non-original modifications affecting timing, that relevant signal-book pages are missing, and that a disputed private signal appears in records tied to cargo claims."

"That sounds accusatory."

"It is descriptive."

Calder leaned closer, lowering his voice though everyone nearby could still hear. "You are chasing dock gossip."

"I am following gear ratios."

His hand tightened around the stem of his wineglass. It was a small movement. Ada noticed hands. Signal work had trained her to read fingers at distance: steady, shaking, hurried, false calm. Calder knew enough to fear the difference between gossip and mechanism.

The second course arrived. Fish, of course. Lorn never missed a chance to serve itself. Ada ate because hunger was not improved by conflict.

At the far end of the table, one councilor asked whether the school programs would include "the controversy." Olan answered with soft phrases: ongoing research, interpretive balance, respect for stakeholders. Ada watched Calder listen. He disliked the word controversy less than he disliked research.

After dinner, guests moved toward the windows with coffee. Fog had thickened over the harbor. The old signal tower showed as a dark tooth against the pale water. Its lamp was unlit, but Ada imagined it burning above black roofs, the brass arms flashing privately while public horns slept.

Calder came to stand beside her. For a moment neither spoke.

"You have no roots here," he said.

"No."

"Then you do not understand what certain names hold together."

"Names are poor rope."

"You think truth is simple because you can measure a gear."

"No. I think gears are simple because people are not."

He looked out at the tower. "Be careful you do not damage what you cannot repair."

Ada turned from the window. "That advice came late to your family."

His face hardened. Then a trustee approached, and he became gracious again with a speed almost mechanical.

Ada left before dessert. On the stairs down to the street, she wrote one line in her notebook: Calder frames accuracy as damage. She underlined damage once.

Some instruments were built to speak. Others were taught to whisper. Families, she thought, were capable of both.

Chapter 10: Dock Notebook

Nadia Voss came to the museum through the staff entrance, carrying a biscuit tin under one arm and a dislike of institutions in every line of her body. She was in her sixties, small, silver-haired, and dressed in a dock jacket patched at both elbows. Iris met her in the archive room. Ada and Jonas waited at the table with clean weights, gloves, and the camera ready.

She refused the visitor badge. "I am not visiting," she said.

Jonas found a blank researcher tag and wrote her name on it instead. That satisfied her enough to keep walking. Ada watched the exchange with interest. Trust sometimes began with a noun.

The biscuit tin had a faded holiday scene on the lid and a dent near one corner. Nadia kept one hand on it even after setting it down, palm flat, as if the contents might be taken by vocabulary alone. The archive room seemed to feel its own shelves watching.

"I don't want it swallowed," Nadia said before anyone greeted her.

"By the museum?" Jonas asked.

"By anyone with shelves and excuses."

"Fair concern," he said. "We can examine it here. You keep custody unless you decide otherwise."

Nadia looked at him for a long second, then placed the biscuit tin on the table. Inside, wrapped in a flour sack, lay a narrow notebook with a cracked red cover. Ada smelled coal dust before she saw the first page.

"My aunt kept it," Nadia said. "Ressa Voss. She worked dock accounts when women were useful enough to trust with numbers and not important enough to credit. She hated Pike men and trusted shorthand."

Iris opened her laptop. "May we record?"

"Record everything. That's why I'm here."

The entries were dense: dates, initials, cargo marks, short columns of numbers, little symbols in the margins. At first the pages looked like private weather. Then Ada began to see the system. Dots clustered beside certain dates. Slashes crossed amounts. A small anchor appeared near times that matched Bram's fog log.

Jonas leaned in carefully. "These are cargo initials?"

"Some. Some are men. Some are boats. Aunt Ressa assumed everyone worth knowing already knew."

On a page dated within the Maribel week, Ada found 7-4-2 written beside east lane and Pike cutter. The words were abbreviated, but legible once Nadia supplied the key: E.L., Pk cut.

Iris exhaled. "Why bring this now?"

"Because the museum invited his portrait back."

"You knew about the exhibit?"

"The paper made sure everyone knew. There he was, Admiral Pike with his noble chin, coming back to teach civic pride. My aunt used to spit when his name came over the radio in anniversary pieces. I thought, enough."

Ada photographed the page. The camera's soft click sounded too modern for the anger on the table.

"Did your aunt ever try to use this?" Iris asked.

"She showed it to a lawyer in 1962. He said shorthand was not proof and old women should let old harbors sleep. She told him sleep was for honest men."

Jonas murmured, "I wish I had known her."

"You would have been afraid of her," Nadia said.

"Possibly with admiration."

They worked through the notebook for two hours. The key was partial. Dots meant fog. Double dots meant dense fog. Slashes meant unpaid duty or avoided customs. A small anchor meant private transfer. A triangle likely meant tower signal. Ada compared these marks to Jonas's watch log margins and Bram's horn silences. The documents did not merely agree; they touched at the joints. 7-4-2 appeared where the public record thinned, where cargo claims later vanished, where Pike Yard boats moved in fog.

Olan came in once, saw Nadia's expression, and retreated without speaking. Ada credited him with survival instinct.

Near the end, Nadia turned toward the archive window. Through it, the semaphore was visible in the conservation room, half disassembled, one brass arm resting on padded blocks.

"My aunt said brass remembers hands," she said.

"Brass records wear," Ada said.

"Same thing, in our family."

Ada considered that. Her own training preferred measurable language: abrasion, pressure, deformation, residue. But families were allowed their own forms of metallurgy. Sometimes memory took the shape of a notebook hidden in a biscuit tin because official places had failed to deserve it.

Nadia closed the cover. Her fingers stayed on it.

"If I lend this, it stays named as hers."

"Ressa Voss," Jonas said, writing carefully. "Creator of the dock notebook."

Nadia watched his pen. Some tension left her shoulders.

Iris gathered her copies. "This may matter in court."

"It mattered before court," Nadia said.

Ada nodded. "Court is only where some people start listening."

Chapter 11: Semaphore Test

Mara reassembled the gear train under camera, narrating every movement in a dry voice that made even screws sound accountable. The conservation room had been rearranged into a small theater of proof. Tripod at front. Side camera on the exposed gear. Stopwatch on the table. Evidence tags hanging from places visitors would never see.

Ada made them begin with silence. No one touched the handle for one full minute while the cameras recorded the object at rest. Jonas thought this excessive until Ada explained that rest was also a state. If anyone later claimed the arm had drifted, bounced, or been staged, the minute would answer.

"You learned this in the service?" Lee asked.

"I learned this from men who said instruments were fine before they failed."

Mara snorted. "Universal education."

Ada stood at the handles. "Official rescue."

Jonas lifted his pen. "Ready."

She moved the arms through the known sequence. The machine answered with public dignity: rise, hold, cross, return. Jonas called times. Mara watched the gear, not the arms. Olan watched everyone, trying to understand which detail would cost him most.

"Now 7-4-2," Ada said.

She moved slowly first. Nothing much happened. A ragged correction, awkward and forgettable.

"Again at modified speed."

This time the gear caught its altered place. The arm snapped through the half-beat and returned before the imagined horn could resume. The side camera caught the dressed teeth taking motion like a lock accepting a known key.

"Half-beat difference," Jonas said.

"Enough?"

"For a signal officer? Yes. For someone trained to watch that tower from the east lane, certainly."

Ada repeated the motion until no one could call it accident. The false signal looked like correction to an untrained viewer and intention to anyone waiting for it. Its genius was not complexity. It was plausible ugliness. It wore the clothes of error.

Mara stopped the recording and pointed at the bearing. "The graphite matters. Fast movement would make noise and bounce. This quiets both."

Olan watched the playback. On screen, the brass arm flicked with furtive grace. "This still may be coincidence."

Ada pointed to the quieted bearing, the altered gear, and the camera timer. "Coincidence does not grease itself."

Mara covered a laugh with a cough. Jonas did not bother hiding his.

Olan looked less amused. Money had asked for a hero. Brass was delivering a procedure.

"Can we say it was used for smuggling?" he asked.

"Not from this test alone," Ada said. "We can say it was capable of transmitting a private timed signal under cover of a public system."

"That sentence will frighten trustees."

"Good. Fear may improve their attention."

They ran the sequence again with the room lights lowered to the level a tower operator would have known before dawn. Shadows gathered in the exposed gear. The brass no longer looked ceremonial. It looked like part of a working system, one handspan of metal answering another hand at a distance.

Lee, the technician, stood near the sink and watched with his arms folded tight. "I cleaned around that bearing," he said. "I thought the dark stuff was old grease."

"It was," Mara said. "Old and useful."

"I should have noticed."

Ada turned from the handle. "Noticing is not magic. It is training plus permission. You had neither."

Olan flinched at that, which meant he understood some of the rebuke belonged to him. Lee looked relieved and ashamed at once.

Mara replayed the side camera footage frame by frame. On the monitor the altered teeth came together and parted with intimate precision. The left arm leapt, not randomly, not from wear, but from a planned transfer of force. Ada asked Jonas to mark the frame count between lift and return. He did, then checked his arithmetic twice.

"The same interval Bram's log leaves open," he said.

"Close enough for a waiting boat," Ada said.

Olan leaned over the screen. "How do you explain that to visitors?"

"We don't start with visitors," Ada said. "We start with the report."

He nodded, but his eyes remained on the flicker of brass. Ada could almost see the museum director in him making the unpleasant crossing from exhibit object to evidence object. It was a useful crossing. Many institutions drowned halfway across it.

Before they shut down, Mara placed a paper seal across the side plate. Ada initialed it. Jonas initialed it. Olan did too, slower than the rest. The machine stood open, witnessed, and newly difficult to pretend about.

Chapter 12: Watch Tower

The old signal tower smelled of salt, dust, and dead varnish. It stood at the end of a service lane behind the museum, built when harbor buildings were expected to work and endure without being loved. Its lower door opened with protest. Inside, the stair climbed tight around a central post, each tread worn hollow by boots.

Olan had offered to send maintenance staff with them. Ada refused. Too many helpful people turned an inspection into a procession, and processions missed corners. Jonas brought a key ring large enough to suggest institutional confusion. It took four tries to find the right one.

"We should label these," he said.

"You should label everything."

"We do. Then people add keys."

The door gave way on the fourth key and released air that had been waiting for years to leave.

Ada climbed slowly. Her knees disliked history in vertical form.

Jonas followed with the 1917 harbor chart rolled under one arm. "If you fall, I am not strong enough to carry you."

"Then take accurate notes."

"Comforting."

At the top, the harbor opened in gray layers: public channel, east lane, customs pier, old Pike yard, fog bell platform, breakwater. The city below looked less picturesque from working height. Roofs showed tar patches. Gutters sagged. A gull stood on a chimney with judicial contempt.

Ada unfolded a modern survey beside Jonas's old chart. The channel markers had changed, but the bones of the harbor remained. She took compass bearings through the tower windows, then through the empty place where the semaphore arms had once been mounted.

"Could the tower see the east lane?" Jonas asked.

"Under lamp, yes."

"Could customs?"

Ada moved to the west-facing slit and looked toward the customs pier. Warehouses blocked part of the line. Fog would erase the rest. "Not in heavy fog. Not unless customs knew exactly where to look, and even then they would see lamp smear, not arm detail."

She marked the chart. A private boat could wait behind the breakwater, invisible to customs, visible to the tower lamp. A fast signal would release it between horn calls. The geometry was simple enough to be elegant and ugly enough to be true.

Jonas handed her a flashlight. "There are scars on the sill."

Ada knelt. Four old screw scars marked the wood and stone near the lamp bracket. They were not random repairs. They formed a rectangle that would have held a shield, narrowing the beam toward the east lane and away from the public channel.

"Directional light," she said.

Jonas's face changed. "Private audience."

"Exactly."

She photographed the scars with a scale. Then she stood in the center of the platform and imagined an operator in wartime fog. Horn. Silence. Arm flash. Boat moving dark with cargo under canvas. Public rescue story above. Private transfer below.

"Do you ever hate being right?" Jonas asked.

"Only when being right gives other people work."

"Then you must suffer often."

Ada looked once more toward Pike yard. Its brick buildings had been converted into offices and restaurants, but the slip remained, black water sheltered between stone walls. Geography was stubborn. It remembered use.

Jonas unrolled a second sheet, a brittle fire-insurance map with rooflines tinted in faded washes. "The restaurant district was warehouses then," he said. "Lower roofs. Fewer signs."

"Better sight line from the tower."

"Worse from customs."

Ada took a bearing from the customs roof shown on the map to the east lane and then from the lamp bracket. The pencil lines crossed at a narrow wedge of water sheltered by stone. Not broad harbor. Not chance. A man waiting there would not have needed to see the whole semaphore. He would need only the fast left arm, the returning flash, and the lamp's narrowed angle. Fraud often lived in small audiences.

Wind came through the broken putty around the windows. It carried tar, salt, coffee from the street below, and the mineral cold of wet masonry. Ada could hear a truck reversing somewhere near the yard, the beeper flattened by fog. The tower had always been a place where sound arrived altered.

Jonas crouched near the sill again. "There are pencil marks under the paint."

Ada held the flashlight sideways. Numbers appeared faintly beneath peeling gray: 2:08, 2:12, then a short vertical stroke. Practice marks, perhaps. Or a bored operator measuring the same gap Bram's grandmother had written down.

"Photograph those," she said.

"Already doing it."

"With scale."

"I am not new."

"Good. I dislike training people on stairs."

He laughed quietly, then steadied the ruler against the sill. The marks were too faint to carry weight alone. Ada knew better than to love weak evidence because it supported a strong suspicion. Still, she noted them as context. Context was not proof. It was the room proof stood in.

On the descent, Olan called Ada's phone twice. She let it ring. The tower needed both hands.

Chapter 13: Gear Ratio

Mara's gear ratio chart covered half the archive table. She had drawn it by hand first because, she said, software made bad assumptions quickly. The chart showed two operating modes. Moved slowly, the semaphore behaved like a public instrument. Snapped through the altered teeth, it became something else.

The chart had grease at one corner and ruler marks down both margins. Mara had taped photographs beside the lines, then drawn arrows in pencil because ink felt too final before Ada had checked the motion. Ada liked that. Certainty earned in pencil could always be inked later.

Olan stood at the edge of the table, careful not to touch. He had begun to recognize that every object in the archive might become evidence without warning. Iris arrived with a legal pad and a sandwich she never managed to eat.

"The machine is bilingual," Jonas said.

"Do not say that near a grant writer," Ada said. "They will put it in the brochure."

Iris studied the chart with a lawyer's hunger for clean sequence. "Can we prove it was intentional?"

"Not with one object," Ada said. "With the gear teeth, graphite grease, lamp shield, repeated signal, and cargo nights together, we can prove design."

"Courts like together."

"So do conspiracies."

Mara placed enlarged photographs beside the ratio chart. The file marks on the gear teeth ran at a consistent angle. Tool pressure changed at the same points on each tooth. This was not a wheel grabbed from a box and forced to fit. It had been shaped for behavior.

Talia added her alloy report. The second brass matched samples from Pike Yard repair stock closely enough to exclude most other local sources. Not absolute, she cautioned. Nothing in old metal was absolute. But it was strong.

"Calder's family owned the yard," Iris said.

"And the yard serviced public harbor equipment under contract," Jonas added, producing a thin procurement file. "Which means they had legitimate access."

"Legitimate access is often where illegitimate use begins," Ada said.

Olan rubbed his forehead. He had begun doing that whenever evidence formed a sentence. "This exhibit is becoming expensive."

"It was always expensive," Ada said. "You just had the wrong cost center."

He looked at the chart for a long time. "Can we still show the semaphore?"

"Yes," Mara said before Ada could answer. "But not as a saint. As a machine with a criminal education."

Iris smiled. "That is not going in a pleading."

"Pity," Jonas said.

Ada gathered the chart, photographs, and sight-line measurements into a single folder. It had weight now. Not enough to finish the matter, but enough to make denial work harder.

Mara added a third sheet showing how an operator's hand would feel the altered portion of the throw. "This is important," she said. "The machine teaches the hand. After ten repetitions, you would know the catch without looking."

Ada took the handle and closed her eyes. Mara had isolated the gear on a test bench so the movement could be felt without moving the full arms. Ada pulled once slowly and felt the ordinary resistance. Then Mara guided her through the quick path. There it was: a small giving way, a place where the mechanism almost invited haste.

"A trained operator could repeat it in fog," Ada said.

"A trained operator could teach another one by touch."

Jonas wrote that down with visible dislike. "So the secret did not need a manual."

"Secrets with payrolls rarely stay only in paper," Iris said.

Olan looked at the folder. "How many people would have had to know?"

"Fewer than the rumor suggests," Ada said. "One officer authorizing the exception. One yard mechanic modifying the machine. One or two operators. Boatmen who received the signal. Clerks who later cleaned the money."

"That is many."

"It is a harbor. Work travels."

Iris asked Mara to describe the tactile catch in language suitable for testimony. Mara objected to the phrase suitable for testimony, then produced three sentences better than any lawyer would have written. The altered teeth created a distinct resistance change at the moment required for accelerated movement. The resistance change would assist repeatable operation. The modification was consistent with intentional timing, not general repair.

Ada copied the sentences verbatim into her own notes. Good technical language was like a good signal: spare, visible, hard to misread.

Chapter 14: Cargo Names

Iris did not speak of cargo as romance. She spread the claim files across the table and made each loss stand upright on dates, values, owners, and names.

She had warned them first. "This part will feel dry. It should not."

The files were tied with cotton tape and numbered in a hand older than everyone in the room. Ada saw water stains, rusted paper clips, brittle onionskin copies, invoices stamped paid and unpaid, and letters that had been folded so often the creases had become weak hinges. Cargo, in ledgers, looked obedient. In lives, it rarely was.

Nadia sat near the door, prepared to leave if the museum began speaking gently about loss. She stayed.

"Tea from the Maribel," she said. "Copper wire from the Eastwake. Medical glass from the Orison."

Each cargo had been declared lost in fog confusion. Each had been written down in the language of weather and regret. Each reappeared months later in private sale records tied to Pike agents, not whole enough to shout but visible in parts: crates of Assam blend sold through a wholesaler with Pike credit; copper wire entering a yard account under salvage adjustment; glass ampoules supplied to a hospital at a discount no one could explain.

Ada read the owners' names aloud once. "Marten & Vale Trading. Eastwake Electrical Cooperative. Orison Medical Supply."

"People should hear them," Iris said.

Olan stood by the window. "No one noticed?"

Nadia, who had come to answer questions about the notebook, gave him a look. "People noticed. They lost."

The sentence was plain enough to bruise.

Iris showed how the claims had been settled quietly, then buried in private correspondence. Families took less than they were owed because admiralty courts were expensive, wartime supply was chaotic, and Pike was already being polished into a hero. A heroic man could make doubt seem indecent.

"Cargo is never just cargo," Ada said.

Iris nodded. "It belonged to merchants, crews, insurers, warehouses, households. The Orison glass was prepaid by two clinics. The copper wire was meant for harbor repairs after the storm. The tea sounds trivial until you see the credit line behind it."

"Can descendants claim now?" Jonas asked.

"Maybe not all. Statutes close doors. Fraud opens some. Trust funds connected to stolen assets may open others. And public admission matters even when money limps."

Iris sounded like a woman willing to wind every stopped clock in the building.

Downstairs, the semaphore stood silent under conservation lights. Ada could see it through the glass whenever she turned. Its arms had moved more than messages through fog. They had moved risk away from one family and loss onto others.

She added the cargo names to her technical timeline. Machines did not care what they carried. People did. The report would have both.

Iris brought out copies of letters written by claimants after the losses. The handwriting changed from confident to cramped across pages as hope narrowed into negotiation. A merchant asked for reconsideration because his warehouse credit depended on the Maribel tea. A clinic administrator wrote that without the Orison glass, delayed shipments would affect winter treatment schedules. The Eastwake cooperative demanded access to tower logs and received a reply so polished Ada could smell contempt through the paper.

"Denied for administrative burden," Iris read.

Nadia gave a dry laugh. "Burden was their word for other people's hunger."

No one contradicted her.

Ada had spent most of her career separating emotion from operational facts. A distress flag did not become more accurate because someone suffered under it. But facts became dishonest when stripped of consequence. The cargo names made the mechanism human without softening the proof. They answered the old museum label's worst habit: weather did not lose those goods. People used weather.

Olan asked for permission to copy the letters for interpretive research, then stopped himself and looked at Nadia and Iris rather than Calder's absent checkbook. "With the families' consent," he added.

"Better," Ada said.

Iris sorted the letters into acid-free folders. "Some descendants may not want display. Some may want every word on the wall. We ask first."

Before Nadia left, she read the cargo names once more and added two pronunciations from family memory. Jonas corrected the catalog entries at once. Names, Ada thought, were bearings too. Let one sit crooked and the whole mechanism could begin to lie.

Chapter 15: Admirals Portrait

Admiral Pike's portrait had been unwrapped and hung temporarily in the back stairwell while the gallery plan changed around it. He stood in oil paint with one hand on a chart and the other near a brass telescope. His eyes fixed themselves beyond lesser men. His beard had been rendered with loving authority. Behind him, a storm behaved decoratively.

The back stairwell was not meant for contemplation. Staff used it to avoid visitor traffic and carry awkward things between floors. That made the portrait stranger there. Without a gallery wall and a label, Pike looked less like history and more like a man blocking the way.

Ada paused two steps below him. A cleaning cart waited on the landing. Someone had left a roll of packing tape on the floor beneath his painted boot. The indignity improved the picture.

Ada distrusted portraits that knew they were being historic.

She found herself stopping there after a long morning of cargo files. The painter had given Pike clean cuffs, firm mouth, noble fatigue. No one painted ledgers into the background. No one painted dock families waiting outside offices. Portraits were arguments in frames.

Calder found her there. "He saved this harbor."

"He may also have robbed it."

"You enjoy dismantling reputations."

"No. Reputations have poor screws."

He stepped onto the landing below her, one hand on the rail. For the first time he looked tired. Anger suited him better; fatigue made him human and therefore harder to dismiss.

"My family will not fund slander," he said.

"Then fund accuracy."

"You reduce a life to three cargo disputes."

"No. Your label reduced it to one rescue."

He looked at the portrait, not at her. "You have no idea what public memory requires."

Ada had spent thirty years sending clear signals because unclear ones killed sailors. She had watched families receive telegrams with too few details and admirals receive ceremonies with too many. Public memory, in her experience, required the same thing as navigation: correction after drift.

"Public memory requires maintenance," she said. "You let varnish do the work."

Calder's jaw tightened. "You think yourself immune to myth?"

"No. I think that is why records matter."

"Records can be arranged."

"So can portraits."

For a moment neither moved. Below them, a museum guard wheeled a crate past the stairwell door, humming under his breath. The ordinary sound made Calder's threat feel smaller and more dangerous at once.

"If this continues," Calder said, "the Pike Foundation will review every commitment to this institution."

"Evidence is not improved by hostage-taking."

"Nor is it improved by prejudice."

Ada turned back to the portrait. "Then help us find the missing pages."

He did not answer. That was answer enough for now.

Calder left angry. The admiral remained painted and useless, his telescope aimed at weather that could no longer excuse him.

Ada stayed after Calder's footsteps faded. Up close, the portrait's surface showed age cracks and old retouching. Someone had brightened the brass telescope decades after the original paint dried. Someone had deepened the storm clouds. Reverence, like fraud, could be revised in layers.

Jonas found her there ten minutes later with a folder under his arm. "Olan is asking whether the portrait can be moved to storage."

"Can it?"

"Physically, yes. Politically, it may develop weight."

"Then weigh it honestly."

He stood beside her and studied the admiral's painted hand. "My predecessor wrote three essays about him. All hero. No caveat."

"Your predecessor knew what he had."

"Did he?"

Ada considered the question. It mattered. Not for proof, perhaps, but for mercy. "He had missing pages, late copies, and dock complaints. Maybe he did not know the scheme. He knew enough to be less certain."

Olan appeared at the stairwell door. "The board wants the portrait in the opening."

"The board wants the old story in oil," Ada said.

"They say removing it looks punitive."

"Leaving it central looks obedient."

He came up two steps, then stopped below the painting. For once he looked at Pike not as donor icon but as evidence of institutional appetite. "What would you do?"

"Use it," Ada said. "Show how portraits create authority. Put it near the records that undermine it."

The portrait stayed on the landing for now, waiting to be demoted from shrine to object lesson. Ada found that better than exile. Hidden things had already done enough harm in Lorn Harbor.

Chapter 16: Maintenance Scratches

The scratches sat under the right arm hub where ordinary visitors would never look and an operator's eye would fall naturally in low light. Ada had noticed them on the first day and filed them under possible maintenance marks. Nadia's notebook changed that.

She had learned not to love first impressions. A scratch could be repair, haste, accident, boredom, or code. The only honest method was to make each possibility stand in line and dismiss it for cause. Still, when Nadia's key put shape to mark, Ada felt the old inward click of pattern becoming question.

Mara disliked the hub screws and said so for five minutes before removing them. Lee took notes. Jonas photographed the untouched surface, then the first angle of light, then the second.

Mara angled a lamp across the brass. The marks appeared sharply: short, long, short, cross. Then, near the handle stop, the same sequence repeated, fainter from wear.

"Say the notebook key again," Ada said.

Nadia stood with her arms folded. "Short-long-short is fog gap. Cross is east lane. Aunt Ressa used the same marks beside the Maribel entry."

Jonas adjusted the camera. "Cheat sheet."

"For crime, yes," Ada said.

Mara leaned close. "Or for a tired operator repeating a private signal in weather. Don't underestimate practical laziness. If a man has to remember a code under pressure, he marks the machine."

They photographed the scratches with raking light, then again with scale markers. Talia examined them under magnification. The cuts had age, oxidation, and tool consistency with wartime maintenance. Not modern vandalism. Not decorative damage. Use marks sat over and around them, proving hands had worked near them after they were made.

Olan came in halfway through and whispered, "Do they have to be in the exhibit?"

Mara looked at him as if he had suggested burning the museum for warmth. "They stay."

"Good," Ada said.

Restoration often erased inconvenient human behavior. A polished surface could become a lie told by kindness. This time the scratches would remain as part of the instrument's biography.

Nadia watched the monitor where the enlarged marks filled the screen. Her face had gone very still.

"There she is," she said.

"Your aunt?"

"Her anger. She knew these marks meant something. She wrote them down because no one could scratch them off her page."

Ada looked from notebook to brass. The instrument had not only moved. It had carried instructions on its own skin, small enough to survive because arrogance had thought them meaningless.

Talia made a silicone impression of the scratches, then another of nearby unrelated nicks. Under the microscope, the difference became stark. Accidental scratches wandered, crossed old polish, hesitated. These marks had depth, start points, and repeated pressure. A hand had cut them with purpose and then used them until the edges rounded.

"Same tool?" Jonas asked.

"Likely for the paired marks," Talia said. "Not for every scratch on the hub. We do not need every mark to be guilty."

Ada appreciated that. Overclaiming was how good cases injured themselves. She wrote likely same cutting implement, repeated operational wear, position visible to operator under low light.

Nadia watched the magnified image on the screen. The marks looked like trenches there, large enough to walk through. "My aunt drew them smaller," she said.

"She drew them from memory?"

"From anger, probably. She saw something once, then made herself remember. That was her habit. If a man shorted her account by a penny, she could tell you which sleeve he wore."

Mara looked up. "Was she ever in the tower?"

Nadia hesitated. "Family story says she delivered lunch to an operator who was courting her cousin. Saw more than lunch."

Jonas wrote family oral history, unverified.

"Do not make it sound weak," Nadia said.

"I make it sound findable," he replied.

By evening, the scratch photographs were printed and pinned beside the notebook key. The two images spoke across the board. Ada stood before them after everyone left and felt the case shift from suspicion into structure.

Chapter 17: Night Harbor

Bram took Ada into the harbor at night because the daytime sight-line test was too clean. "Fog at noon is scenery," he said. "Fog after midnight is evidence."

Olan's authorization letter was folded in Ada's pocket, already damp at the edges. It allowed temporary light tests, restricted vessel movement, and prohibited public demonstration. Bram read it once and said the museum had managed to make fog boring on paper.

"Lawyers helped," Ada said.

"I can smell them."

The Nell Vale's deck light made the pier boards shine black. Ada stepped aboard carefully. She trusted Bram's boat more than the museum's confidence, but water punished assumptions faster than donors did.

The Nell Vale moved slowly past dark pilings and moored workboats. Lorn Harbor had narrowed to sound: engine thrum, water slap, chain knock, a gull complaining in sleep. Fog lay thick enough to make the deck light a small room. Ada stood beside Bram in the wheelhouse with a recorder clipped to her coat.

"East lane," he said, easing the throttle back.

The old tower lamp glowed above them. Olan had signed the temporary test authorization with the expression of a man authorizing his own headache. Mara stood in the tower with Jonas and a radio, operating a replica handle linked to the restored lamp position. The semaphore itself was still in conservation, so they used a calibrated test arm matching the original angles and speed.

Ada called the time. "Ready for official rescue."

The arms moved above the fog. Or tried to. The official signal blurred into yellow smear, visible as motion but not legible from the tug. The foghorn sounded from the public channel, late and low.

"Now 7-4-2," Ada said.

Mara waited for the horn to die. In the quiet gap, the false signal snapped cleanly, one quick articulation under the lamp. It was visible for three seconds to the east lane and almost nowhere else.

Ada felt the old anger of professional clarity misused. Signals were supposed to reduce uncertainty. Here uncertainty had been engineered as cover.

"A boat here would see it," Bram said.

"And customs would hear nothing."

He cut the engine. Water slapped the hull. Fog made the city private. From this position, the customs pier vanished, but the tower remained, a single eye with arms.

"My family said Pike stole with flags."

"Your family was concise."

"Poor people often are. No one pays us by the word."

Ada recorded the sight line, the horn interval, the visibility. She described the lamp, the fog density, the distance to the breakwater. Bram added notes about current and how a small cutter would drift with oars muffled.

When they returned to the pier, her coat smelled of diesel and wet rope. Her notebook pages had softened at the edges. The harbor had testified, not in poetry but in angles, seconds, and blind spots.

Bram did not take the tug straight back. He let her drift near the old Pike slip with the engine idling low. The converted yard buildings showed tidy windows and expensive lamps now, but the water between the stone walls remained black and serviceable. A small craft could slide in there with crates under canvas, unseen by anyone who had not been told to look.

"My father said this place got rich twice," Bram said. "Once from ships. Once from pretending not to know ships."

Ada kept the recorder running. "Do you want that on record?"

"I want everything on record. I am tired of being colorful background."

That sentence stayed with her. Harbor families had been treated as atmosphere in the Pike legend, their knowledge reduced to dock talk when it challenged a man with a portrait. Bram's tug, his logs, his grandmother's minutes, all had been outside official history because official history had locked the door from the inside.

At the pier, Iris was waiting under a sodium lamp with her hood up and a thermos in one hand. She had come to receive the recording because she did not trust anyone to email evidence through a tired museum server. Ada handed over the card and watched Iris seal it.

"Visible?" Iris asked.

"From one place."

"Invisible?"

"From the places that would have mattered to customs."

Bram tied the line with sharp, practiced pulls. "That was always the trick. Make honest men look the wrong way and then call them blind."

On the walk back to the museum, Ada's shoes filled with the damp cold that came off stone after midnight. She felt tired, but it was clean fatigue, the kind earned by making a question smaller and sharper.

Chapter 18: Museum Hold

Olan agreed to delay the exhibit only after Iris filed notice of a preservation petition and delivered a copy to his office before breakfast. He read the first page twice, then looked at Ada as if she had personally invented civil procedure.

The office still held opening-week optimism: seating charts, sponsor lists, a sample menu, a box of name badges printed before evidence became inconvenient. Ada noticed Calder's badge on top. Gold border. Larger font. The small vanities of money had a way of surviving until someone threw them out.

Iris stood beside the desk and waited. She had the patience of a person paid to let silence work.

Olan turned the page and found the requested hold language. His face changed by degrees.

"The opening invitations are printed."

"Recycle," Iris said.

"The board preview is tomorrow."

"Then tomorrow can preview institutional caution."

Calder called within the hour. Olan put him on speaker by mistake, or perhaps from exhaustion. Calder's voice filled the director's office, cold and polished. Funding would be reviewed. Naming rights would be reconsidered. The foundation could not support a defamatory atmosphere.

Olan went pale. Ada almost pitied him, then remembered the label calling Pike the Voice of Lorn Harbor with no room for stolen voices.

"We are placing the object on conservation hold," Olan said into the phone.

"You are making a mistake," Calder answered.

"Possibly," Olan said. "But it will be documented."

That was the first sentence Ada had heard from him that morning with bone in it.

The semaphore was moved from public staging to conservation hold after lunch. Mara supervised the move as if transporting a sleeping patient with a history of biting. She attached tags in block letters: DO NOT POLISH. DO NOT OPERATE UNSUPERVISED. DO NOT ADJUST ARMS.

"That last one is for donors," she said.

Jonas locked the signal books in archival custody and logged the missing pages as an active concern rather than a historical shrug. Nadia's notebook went into a fire safe for overnight storage only after she inspected the safe herself. Bram's fog logs were scanned, copied, and returned to him before he would leave the building.

The gallery changed in one afternoon. The donor flowers were moved out. The heroic label came down. Evidence carts replaced champagne tables. The museum became less elegant and more honest.

Ada preferred it that way.

At closing, Olan stood in the half-stripped room. "The board will be furious."

"They will survive."

"Will the museum?"

Ada looked at the semaphore under its plain cloth. "That depends on whether it wants to be a museum or a chapel."

The answer did not comfort Olan. He stood with his hands in his pockets, looking at the empty places where opening-night furniture had been marked in tape on the floor. A caterer called twice. A florist left a message about delivery access. The museum's public calendar still promised a celebration of maritime courage, and the website team waited for language no one yet knew how to write.

"I became a director to keep the lights on," he said.

"That is a real job."

"It does not feel noble."

"Good. Noble gets people careless."

He looked at her, perhaps deciding whether to be offended. Then he laughed once, without pleasure. "Calder will say I let outsiders capture the institution."

"Outsiders are useful when insiders have learned where not to look."

In the archive, Jonas posted a custody sheet on the door and made every person sign in, including trustees who disliked being treated like undergraduate researchers. Mara placed the gear assembly in a locked cabinet with a transparent front so anyone could see it had not moved. Iris sent preservation notices to the museum, the Pike Foundation, the city archive, the harbor authority, and three private storage companies Calder's family had used.

By five o'clock, the hold had become a system. Ada trusted systems more than vows. A system made it harder for a frightened person to do a quiet foolish thing after dinner.

Lee stopped by with a roll of blue tape. "Do you need anything labeled?"

Mara pointed to a shelf. "Everything."

He set to work with visible relief. Small jobs steadied people. When the story cracked, give honest hands honest tasks.

Chapter 19: Court Petition

Iris's petition landed in Judge Leona Arda's courtroom on a wet Thursday, accompanied by declarations, photographs, timelines, and a proposed preservation order thick enough to annoy everyone named in it. It asked the court to preserve the semaphore, signal books, watch logs, donor archive, Pike family materials related to harbor signals, private yard repair records, and cargo settlement documents.

Ada had spent the night before cutting adjectives from her declaration. The first draft had contained three more than necessary. By midnight, the statement had become a clean sequence of observations: measured timing, altered gear, sight line, known code gap, limits. She slept four hours and woke thinking of the left arm's half-beat.

At the courthouse, Iris handed her a paper cup of coffee and a final copy. "Do not improve this aloud."

"I rarely improve things aloud."

"You sharpen them. Judges notice."

Calder's lawyers called it speculative. They wore identical dark suits and used the word heritage as if it conferred immunity.

Ada submitted a declaration on gear ratio, timing, maintenance scratches, and sight lines. She kept adjectives out of it. Courts, like seas, punished unnecessary flourish. Mara submitted a technical note. Talia submitted alloy findings. Jonas submitted an archivist's statement that managed to sound both dry and personally offended.

Judge Arda read for a long time. She had short silver hair, rimless glasses, and a face that suggested she had heard every expensive excuse in the county. The courtroom smelled of wet wool and floor wax.

"I have never enjoined a semaphore," she said at last.

"First time for every instrument," Iris said.

One of Calder's lawyers objected to the scope. Iris answered by walking the judge through the missing pages, the altered gear, the false signal, the cargo claims, and the donor archive risk. Ada watched Arda's pen. It moved most when Iris spoke of removable records.

"The court is not finding fraud today," the judge said. "The court is finding that materials may be relevant and must not disappear."

The order granted preservation and set a hearing. Calder had to produce private family materials related to harbor signals, Pike yard repairs, and cargo settlements. The museum had to maintain the conservation hold. No one was to alter the semaphore.

Outside court, Calder waited under the portico as rain stippled the stone steps.

"This is harassment," he said.

Ada answered before Iris could. "No. Harassment is repeated unwanted contact. This is discovery."

Iris smiled. Calder did not.

The brass would stay still until the documents moved. Ada found that satisfying. Instruments had done too much talking in fog. Now paper would have its turn.

Iris did not let anyone leave the courthouse quickly. Under the portico she assigned tasks with the briskness of a person who believed weather was no excuse for disorder. Jonas would prepare a supplemental archive inventory. Mara would produce a sworn condition statement for the gear assembly. Ada would expand the timing report into language a judge could follow without becoming an engineer.

"No insults," Iris added.

"To whom?" Ada asked.

"Everyone."

"Restrictive."

Calder watched from beside a stone column while his lawyers spoke in tight murmurs. Rain darkened the shoulders of his coat. For the first time, Ada saw calculation falter behind his composure. The court order had changed the scale of the matter. He could no longer manage it with invitations, dinners, and donor pressure. He had to produce paper, and paper was less loyal than guests.

Bram arrived late, having come only to see whether "the brass got arrested." Iris told him no. He seemed disappointed.

"Preserved," Ada said.

"That's less fun."

"More useful."

He handed her a photocopy of another fog-log page. "Found it last night. Different year. Same horn gap, same east lane note. Might be nothing."

Ada looked at the date. It fell six months after Maribel. Not nothing, then. A pattern was beginning to extend beyond the famous rescue, which meant the false signal might have been routine, not exceptional.

"Give this to Iris," she said.

"I made three copies. Learned from you people."

Chapter 20: Filing Cabinet

Jonas found the fog drill report in a cabinet labeled Lighthouse Fuel, which stood in a basement room no one had properly inventoried because the ceiling leaked in one corner and the light switch shocked people in damp weather. Archivists, Ada knew, developed affection for human misfiling only after it helped.

He had gone downstairs looking for lamp invoices, not revelation. That mattered to him. Discoveries made under a theory were useful; discoveries made while doing dull inventory had a different authority. No one could accuse a leaking basement of narrative ambition.

When he called Ada, his voice had the stiffness of a man trying not to run with paper in his hands.

"Are you alone?" she asked.

"With several bad decisions and one promising folder."

"Do not move it."

"I am offended you think I would."

He called her down with unusual urgency. "Bring Iris if she is nearby. Bring gloves if she is not."

Ada found him kneeling before an open drawer, his cardigan dusted gray. The room smelled of rust, paper, and old rain. On the floor lay a folder tied with cotton tape. Its label read Fog Drill, April 1917.

"In lighthouse fuel?" Ada asked.

"Apparently fog and lamps became conceptually entangled."

The report described a public drill on the same night as the Maribel cargo loss. It listed horn tests, tower lamp checks, operator assignments, and a phrase that made Iris, when she arrived, go perfectly still: private coordination with Pike Yard.

"Private coordination?" she asked.

"That phrase should itch," Ada said.

Attached to the report was a sketch of the tower lamp shield. Four screws. Angled side baffles. Narrowed beam toward the east lane. Same scar pattern Ada had photographed in the tower. The report did not accuse anyone. It did something more durable. It documented enough to make later lies harder.

Jonas held the paper gently. "Someone saved this by misplacing it."

"An old archival technique," Ada said.

He looked offended and pleased.

They carried the folder upstairs in a flat box. Olan met them in the corridor and read the phrase aloud, as if hoping it would change shape in his mouth. It did not.

"Private coordination with Pike Yard," he repeated.

"On a public drill night," Iris said. "With cargo later declared lost."

The filing cabinet had become the first honest witness with drawers. Ada looked back toward the basement stairs. Somewhere below, under mislabeled fuel and dust, a clerk's decision to keep one report out of the obvious file had lasted a century.

Jonas insisted on returning to the basement before the day ended, not to search further but to document the cabinet's surroundings before rumor turned the discovery into treasure hunting. Ada went with him because basements had a way of converting enthusiasm into broken spines and mishandled paper.

The room was worse under proper attention. Damp climbed the lower wall in a tide mark. A rusted pipe crossed above the cabinet. Someone had stored holiday decorations on top of municipal records, as if tinsel and fog drills belonged to one civic category.

"This is neglect," Jonas said.

"Neglect with consequences."

He photographed the cabinet label, drawer order, water stain, and the empty space where the folder had lain. "If a clerk hid it, why here?"

"Because anyone looking for signal papers would avoid fuel files."

"And anyone looking for fuel would find this useless."

"Good hiding is often administrative boredom."

He wrote that down, then crossed it out. "Not for the report."

"No."

In the drawer below, they found routine lamp invoices and a complaint about soot. Nothing dramatic. Ada was almost glad. Strong discoveries needed ordinary neighbors. Otherwise people called them planted or theatrical. A misfiled folder among dull invoices had the texture of life.

Upstairs, Iris read the drill report again and marked three phrases: private coordination, east-side visibility, temporary shield. "Temporary," she said. "Yet the screw scars remained."

"Temporary is a word people use when they want approval," Ada said.

Chapter 21: Counterweight

Talia found the counterweight because Mara disliked one screw.

The screw sat in a row of four and offended her by being almost correct. Its slot had been dressed to match the others, but the metal around it carried a different shine under oblique light. Mara called everyone over and made them look before she explained.

"A good faker understands symmetry," she said. "A better conservator distrusts it."

Ada bent near the base. The screw did not shout. It whispered in the same way the left arm had whispered on the first morning: a small timing error in the appearance of order.

"This one has been opened more than the others," Mara said, tapping the base plate. "The slot is dressed. Someone wanted it to look untouched."

They gathered in conservation with the cameras running. Ada stood back, hands in her coat pockets, while Mara removed the plate. Dust shifted. A small brass weight sat inside the hollow base, fixed on a short pin where no design drawing placed one.

Talia bent under the lamp. "Not naval issue."

On one side of the weight, almost hidden by tarnish, was a tiny ship mark above three letters: PYC.

"Pike Yard Company," Jonas said.

No one had to ask how he knew. Archivists carried whole shipyards in their heads when properly irritated.

Mara weighed the piece, then ran the arm motion with and without its mass. The extra weight helped the left arm snap back faster after the half-beat signal, making the false sequence clean instead of sloppy.

"So the gear hurries it out," Ada said, "and the weight hurries it home."

"Efficiently ugly," Mara said.

Ada imagined a yard worker installing it under orders, perhaps proud of the cleverness, perhaps frightened by what cleverness made possible. People who made tools for bad purposes did not always think of themselves as villains. Sometimes they thought of tolerances, delivery dates, a foreman's temper, a wage envelope.

At the next hearing Calder's lawyer called the weight routine repair. Talia answered without raising her voice. "Routine repairs are documented. Hidden weights are hidden."

Judge Arda looked over her glasses. "That sentence is clear enough for the court."

The counterweight did not yet prove Admiral Pike ordered theft. Evidence rarely moved that cleanly. But it placed family brass inside public equipment and gave the private signal its physical return. Ada liked evidence that understood geography. This one had crossed from Pike yard into public tower and stayed there, pulling the arm faster through fog.

Talia asked for access to the museum's accession photographs from the 1970s. Jonas found them in a binder with plastic sleeves gone cloudy at the edges. The semaphore had been dirtier then, its base still sealed, the arms packed in straw. No one had photographed the underside. No one had known to want it.

"Absence is not proof," Talia said.

"But it is an invitation to be careful," Ada said.

They compared the PYC stamp to marks on winch housings, pulley plates, and a broken yard capstan stored in the museum's industrial collection. The same little ship appeared there, struck with uneven pressure, bow deeper than stern. Talia overlaid the images and measured the defects in the stamp. A nick in the upper curve repeated across the counterweight and the capstan.

"Same die," she said at last.

Jonas made a small sound that would have been unbecoming in a reading room.

Mara looked at the base plate. "Whoever fitted this also polished around it. See the wipe marks? They cleaned the evidence they expected people to see and ignored the evidence they thought no one could reach."

Ada had known many dishonest acts with that shape. Great care at the surface, laziness where power assumed privacy. It was why drawers mattered. Undersides. Margins. The backs of panels. People lied best where they expected no audience.

Olan asked whether the counterweight could be removed for preservation.

"No," Mara and Talia said together.

He raised both hands. "I am learning."

"It stays with the machine," Ada said. "The story is mechanical. Separate the parts and someone will call each one incidental."

Chapter 22: Nadias Key

Nadia's notebook key emerged on a second visit, after two hours of patient page turning and one argument about whether museums made decent tea. It had been tucked into the back cover under a loose strip of cloth, folded so tightly that Jonas almost missed it.

The argument about tea mattered more than it should have. Nadia said the archive kettle made water taste like pipe. Jonas said the kettle was new. Nadia said new did not mean innocent. Ada watched them reach a compromise involving Bram's thermos and understood that trust was being negotiated by the cup.

Only after that did Nadia allow the back cover to be examined under stronger light. The cloth strip had always looked loose to her, she said, but Ressa had been a woman who hid things in plain sight and dared people to be observant.

"Do not pull," Nadia said.

"I am an archivist, not a barbarian."

"I have met both."

He worked the page free with a thin spatula while Ada held the light. The paper unfolded into a list of marks and names. Ressa Voss had not trusted memory entirely. Initials became ships. Dots became fog. Anchors became private transfer. A triangle meant tower signal. Slashes meant customs avoided or unpaid. Short-long-short meant horn gap. Cross meant east lane.

Ada felt the case tighten.

7-4-2 appeared beside Maribel, Eastwake, and Orison. It also appeared beside two lesser cargoes Iris had not yet traced, both marked with anchor and triangle.

"Your aunt knew," Iris said.

Nadia shook her head. "She knew enough to be angry and not enough to win."

That described many inherited archives. They came down not as proof but as stubbornness, as a refusal to let official forgetting have the last word.

Ada laid photographs of the maintenance scratches beside the key. Short-long-short, cross. Fog gap, east lane. The same code lived in notebook and brass. One had been made by a dock clerk writing against power. The other had been cut by an operator or mechanic using power. Between them lay the whole harbor.

Nadia touched the page with one gloved finger. "She would have liked this."

"Court?"

"Being right."

Ada understood. Being right after death was cold comfort. But it was not nothing, especially when paper carried the warmth forward. She looked at Iris. "This key should go into the petition supplement."

"Already drafting," Iris said.

Jonas placed the key in a sleeve. "Creator: Ressa Voss. Function: decoding aid for dock notebook."

Nadia watched the words become catalog. "Good," she said. "Make her hard to misplace."

They spent another hour matching the key to entries Jonas had once considered illegible clutter. The triangle beside a date became tower signal. A slash through a cargo mark became duty avoided. Double dots meant dense fog, not emphasis. Under the new reading, the notebook changed from grievance to map.

Nadia grew quieter as the pages opened. Anger had carried her to the museum. Recognition asked something different of her.

"She kept this for years in the kitchen dresser," Nadia said. "My mother used to put buttons in the same drawer. Imagine that. Buttons on top of all this."

"Good hiding place," Ada said.

"Or nobody thought a woman's notebook mattered."

"Also that."

Iris photographed each decoded entry and made Nadia approve the file names. Not for law, exactly, but for trust. Ressa_Voss_key_page_1. Ressa_Voss_Maribel_entry. Ressa_Voss_Eastwake_entry. Nadia watched the name repeat on the laptop screen and blinked hard once.

Jonas produced a loan form written in plain language. Nadia read every line. "This says temporary."

"Yes."

"This says no display without separate consent."

"Yes."

"This says you cannot clean it."

Jonas looked wounded. "I would not."

"The form says it, and now you say it. Good."

She signed with a heavy black pen. The stroke looked decisive enough to cut rope. Ada witnessed the signature. Iris did too.

When Nadia left, the biscuit tin went with her. The notebook stayed in a secured drawer for the first time by choice rather than distrust. In a case built on missing paper, custody had become a moral act.

Chapter 23: False Heroism

The famous rescue began to fall apart not all at once but by practical pieces.

That was what made it convincing and unpleasant. Ada had seen legends collapse theatrically only in plays. In archives, they failed by schedule discrepancy, receipt, payroll note, weather entry, and one bored clerk's copy. Each piece seemed too small to carry outrage. Together they removed the floor.

Olan wanted to gather everyone for a single meeting. Iris advised against it. "Let the evidence arrive in order. People trust sequences they can verify."

"People trust stories," Olan said.

"Then give them one that does not lie."

Jonas found a docking receipt for the first supposedly endangered ship. It had tied up forty minutes before the heroic signal. Bram produced a tug notation showing the second vessel was never in the public channel that night; it had waited outside until morning. Iris traced the third, the Maribel, and found it had been used as cover for a cargo transfer in the east lane.

Public history said Pike signaled three trapped ships through fog. The records showed one ship already safe, one never in danger, and one useful to theft.

Olan sat down when Ada showed him the timeline. "The whole exhibit..."

"Not the whole harbor," Ada said. "Do not mourn the wrong thing."

"I approved school materials."

"Then revise them."

He put both hands over his face for a moment. Ada let him have the silence. Institutions were slow to admit they had built rooms around convenient stories. Individuals could be slower.

Calder argued, through counsel and then in person, that rescue and irregular cargo movement could coexist. Iris agreed at once, which startled him.

"Of course they can," she said. "The question is why the irregular cargo enriched Pike agents, why the signal pages vanished, and why the machine was modified to transmit privately."

He had no good answer. He had indignation, family service, civic memory, and the weary claim that modern people lacked context. Ada had heard that last refuge often. Context was useful. It was not bleach.

The museum's heroic centerpiece became more complicated and less decorative. Ada preferred complicated. It made fewer demands on belief. The brass arms hung still above the table, caught between public salute and private theft.

Olan looked through the evidence again. "How do we tell this without turning the exhibit into a trial?"

"Tell what happened. Let the trial be a trial."

"And if we lose the patron?"

Ada looked at the stripped wall where Calder's donor panel had been planned. "Then the wall becomes more accurate."

The school materials were the first public casualty. Olan brought them to the archive in a blue folder: worksheets with neat questions about courage, leadership, and storm rescue. Students were meant to draw the semaphore arms and write a sentence about Admiral Pike's bravery. Ada put the pages down after reading one.

"These are not salvageable as written."

Olan did not argue. "I know."

Jonas took the worksheet and circled the phrase made the harbor safe. "The harbor was not safe for the people whose cargo disappeared."

"Or for clerks who knew too much," Nadia said.

Olan wrote revision notes in the margin. Ask who records history. Compare public and private documents. Identify missing evidence. It was a better lesson and a harder one.

"Teachers may complain," he said.

"Teachers handle harder things than donor discomfort," Ada answered.

Calder's counsel sent a letter objecting to any educational material that implied criminality. Iris replied by enclosing the court preservation order, the alloy report, the drill report, and an invitation to identify specific factual errors. No reply came that day.

At dusk, Ada returned to the conservation room and looked at the semaphore without operating it. The arms rested in neutral, but the machine no longer felt dormant. It had become crowded with people: Pike, yard mechanics, Ressa Voss, Bram's grandmother, cargo owners, museum directors, trustees, students not yet brought through the gallery.

False heroism had worked because it simplified the room. Real history made the room full. Ada trusted fullness more, even when it made walking difficult.

Chapter 24: Calders Archive

Calder's private archive arrived in banker boxes and resentment. Two paralegals brought it to the museum under court order, set the boxes on a rolling table, and behaved as if paper dust were a moral hazard. Calder came with them, formally cooperative and visibly aggrieved.

Iris met him at the loading entrance rather than the lobby. Ada appreciated the choice. Donors belonged in lobbies. Evidence came through loading doors, signed for and counted. Jonas stood with a clipboard, the custody log, and the expression of a man prepared to be disliked in alphabetical order.

"These are family materials," Calder said.

"These are court-ordered productions," Iris replied.

"Handle them respectfully."

Jonas looked at the boxes. "Respect begins with inventory."

The boxes contained family letters, yard accounts, anniversary speeches, clipped newspaper tributes, photographs of men in stiff collars, and a great deal of carefully curated hero material. Dates around the cargo claims were thin.

Jonas noticed the stubs first. In one correspondence file, three letters had been removed from a bound stack. The cuts were neat. The paper around them had not darkened enough to be old.

"Recently?" Ada asked.

"Not ancient," Jonas said. "After the binding had aged. Before this morning."

Iris photographed the stubs and added them to a list that made Calder's lawyer sigh.

One account book remained because no one had recognized its signal abbreviations. It was dull-looking, bound in brown cloth, labeled Yard Miscellany. Inside, a clerk had listed lamp work, counterweight fitting, shield adjustment, and repeated payments marked E. service.

"East service," Ada said.

Jonas checked Nadia's key. "East lane service, likely."

Calder claimed ignorance of technical language. "These are industrial notes. My family owned a yard. Yards repair things."

"Your ancestor did not seem confused," Ada said.

He looked at her sharply.

The archive omitted crucial dates, but omissions had edges. They pointed toward what someone feared. Iris requested sanctions for incomplete production. Judge Arda did not grant them yet, but she ordered a search certification and warned Calder's counsel that family history did not receive a lighter discovery burden.

By evening the banker boxes had been logged, sleeved, and arranged on secure shelves. Calder's family papers had entered the fog and come out damp.

Jonas locked the room. "I used to think archives were where things rested."

"No," Ada said. "They are where things wait."

The next morning, Jonas and Iris built a production log for Calder's boxes. Every missing sequence received an entry. Every clipped article was dated. Every envelope was checked for impressions where removed letters had once pressed against paper. Ada helped for an hour, then admitted Jonas was better at patient suspicion.

In a packet of anniversary speeches, they found Calder's own notes for the museum gala. He had crossed out "restored to working order" and written "restored to honor." He had crossed out "contested cargo claims" in a background memo and written "dockside folklore."

"That is not nineteenth-century fraud," Olan said when Iris showed him.

"No," Iris said. "It is modern suppression of notice."

Ada watched the phrase land. Olan sat down slowly. Modern guilt was harder for the museum to place at a safe historical distance. It had entered by email, board minutes, donor meetings, label drafts. It had worn a suit he recognized.

Calder's archive also contained a photograph of the tower interior taken during a 1968 commemorative cleanup. On the sill, before repainting, the old pencil times showed clearly. Jonas enlarged the image until the numbers filled the screen.

"Two-oh-eight," he said. "Two-twelve."

"Horn gap," Ada said.

The same cleanup file included a note: remove obsolete shield hardware. If the hardware was obsolete in 1968, it had survived long after the supposed temporary drill. Temporary again. Ada had begun to distrust the word more than some lies.

Iris added the photograph to her supplement. Calder had intended to control the story by releasing too little. Instead, the edges of what he released had begun to cut him.

Chapter 25: Public Hearing

Ada arrived early enough to watch the room decide what it wanted to be. Court staff arranged chairs, reporters tested pens, and harbor families claimed seats with the quiet firmness of people who had been standing outside official rooms for too long. The brass demonstration frame sat under a gray cloth near the witness table. It looked smaller than the rumors around it.

Iris placed folders in exact order. "We lead with movement, then documents."

"Good," Ada said.

"You hate surprises."

"I hate avoidable confusion."

Across the aisle, Calder's counsel unpacked binders with matching white labels. Calder himself had not yet arrived. His absence made the room breathe more easily, though no one would have admitted it.

When he did arrive, the air changed without becoming louder. His supporters straightened. A reporter lifted her pen. Calder nodded to no one in particular and took his seat as if choosing not to notice the harbor families behind him. Ada watched that choice. Power often showed itself first in what it declined to acknowledge.

The public hearing filled before nine. Reporters took the front benches. Harbor families took the middle. Museum staff stood along the walls. A cluster of retired men in old uniforms occupied the back with the air of people prepared to defend memory from evidence.

Judge Arda ordered the semaphore demonstrated on a portable frame built by Mara and inspected by both sides. The original gear assembly sat exposed behind a clear shield. Nothing about it looked heroic. It looked useful, which was more dangerous.

Calder's counsel objected to theater.

Judge Arda looked at the brass arms. "The object moves. We will observe the movement."

Ada appreciated practical judges.

Mara operated the official rescue signal first. Ada explained each position, the expected timing, the purpose of clarity in fog. The arms rose and crossed with sober slowness. Several people in the gallery leaned forward, disappointed perhaps by how ordinary truth could look.

"Now the disputed 7-4-2 sequence," Iris said.

Mara moved it slowly. A bad correction. A nothing. Then she reset and moved it through the modified teeth.

The false signal snapped through the room.

From the back, Bram said, "That's the one."

Calder's lawyer rose at once. Judge Arda allowed the statement only after Iris formally called Bram, swore him, and established the fog logs. Bram looked uncomfortable in his ironed jacket and entirely comfortable telling the truth.

"My grandmother described that movement," he said. "My father checked it against the tower. I saw it in the reenactment. Same quick arm. Same gap."

Even people who did not understand code understood secrecy when it moved in front of them. The half-beat had a guilty sound, though Ada knew guilt belonged to hands, not brass.

Iris then called Ada. The oath felt ordinary in Ada's mouth. She had sworn to tell the truth in inquiries before, after collisions, after equipment failures, after officers had found ways to blame weather for decisions. She sat with her hands flat on the rail and kept her answers short.

"What is the significance of the timing difference?" Iris asked.

"It creates a second message inside the public motion."

"Could an untrained observer mistake it for error?"

"Yes."

"Could a trained observer waiting in the east lane read it?"

"Yes."

Calder's lawyer rose with a folder of enlarged photographs. "Ms. Vey, you were not present in 1917."

"No."

"You cannot testify to Admiral Pike's intent."

"No."

"You cannot identify the hand that moved the semaphore on any fog night."

"No."

He seemed encouraged. "So you offer speculation."

Ada waited half a beat, the same discipline she used before answering signals. "I offer mechanical behavior, sight-line analysis, code comparison, and documentary correlation. Intent is for the court to infer from evidence."

Judge Arda wrote something down. Calder's lawyer looked less encouraged. A century of wear could loosen joints, slow arms, corrode pins, or make a bearing chatter. It did not invent a hidden counterweight stamped with a family yard mark, preserve a private signal number, and align itself with cargo claims. The lawyer had invited the sentence. She had merely let it arrive.

Chapter 26: Fog Reenactment

The reenactment order specified time, position, equipment, witnesses, and weather conditions with legal precision and meteorological optimism. Iris read the paragraph twice and said the court had written "fog if available" as if ordering stationery.

"Lorn will provide," Bram said.

It did. By four in the morning, the pier lights were weak halos and the deck boards were slick underfoot. Ada signed the equipment log with cold fingers. Mara checked the calibrated frame at the tower by radio. Jonas confirmed the old chart coordinates from shore, his voice thin through static.

"Everyone ready?" Iris asked.

"No," Bram said. "But we're leaving."

The clerk from chambers wore rubber boots under a formal coat and looked annoyed that the harbor had not arranged itself like a courtroom diagram. Ada respected her for getting aboard anyway. Some evidence required discomfort. The clerk sealed her notebook in a plastic sleeve, checked her watch against Ada's, and asked where she should stand to see without interfering.

The court allowed a harbor reenactment under controlled conditions, which made three lawyers argue for forty minutes about weather. Fog machines were proposed by one side and rejected by everyone who had ever been outside. Lorn supplied real fog by dawn.

Bram positioned the tug in the east lane at 05:12. A clerk from Judge Arda's chambers stood beside Ada with a clipboard sealed in plastic. Iris watched from the stern. Calder stayed on shore, a dark figure near the pier, perhaps because seeing the harbor from below would make denial harder.

Mara operated the tower arms using the restored lamp mount and calibrated frame. Jonas called intervals by radio. The public horn sounded, rolled low across the water, and died.

"Official signal," Ada said into the recorder.

Above them, the arms moved. The fog turned them into a pale blur.

"Disputed sequence."

Silence opened between horn calls. The false signal appeared, crisp and brief, visible for three seconds from the east lane. The customs pier remained swallowed.

"Private route confirmed," Ada said.

Bram pointed to black water beyond the pier. "A cutter could pass there with cargo light and no one wiser. Oars if careful. Engine if the horn covered it."

They repeated the test from three positions. The signal vanished from two and held from one. That mattered more than spectacle. It proved audience.

The reenactment did not prove who ordered the system. It proved the method worked. Methods mattered. They turned rumor into engineering.

When they came ashore, the clerk's hair was damp and her notes were dotted with fog. "The judge will appreciate the lack of drama," she said.

"Drama is for bad evidence," Ada answered.

The clerk asked to see the route from customs before returning to shore. Bram obliged and took the tug across the gray water. From that angle the tower lamp diffused into a dull stain. The arms vanished. The public horn, when it sounded, seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere.

"I would not have seen it," the clerk said.

"That is the point," Ada replied.

Iris leaned against the rail, coat wet at the hem. "Could customs have posted someone in the east lane?"

"Only if they suspected the east lane."

"And if Pike controlled the drill schedule?"

"Then suspicion would have to begin outside his paperwork."

Bram made a sound of agreement and turned the tug back. "Customs men trusted tower men. Harbor doesn't work if every man assumes the next one is crooked."

"It also fails if no one checks," Ada said.

"There is the trick."

They repeated one final run with the clerk filming from a handheld camera. The official sequence disappeared in fog smear. The false sequence cut through once, clean as a wink, then vanished. Ada disliked that it looked almost elegant. Efficient wrongdoing often did. That was why people mistook cleverness for merit.

Back at the pier, Calder had gone. His absence left a dark dry rectangle on the wet boards where he had stood under an umbrella. Ada noticed because absence, like presence, could mark a place.

Chapter 27: Maras Repair

Mara refused to call the work repair until everyone in the room understood what would not be repaired. The altered teeth would not be made innocent. The counterweight would not be removed. The scratches would not be polished into silence. Lee wrote the list on masking tape and fixed it to the bench where no one could pretend later that conservation had meant beautification.

"This is going to irritate visitors who like shiny things," he said.

"Good," Mara answered. "Shiny things have had a long turn."

Ada stood by the evidence cart and initialed the condition sheet. She had learned that signatures calmed nervous rooms. They made responsibility visible.

Mara made Lee read the condition sheet back aloud. It embarrassed him, but by the end he understood why every phrase mattered. "Preserved discoloration" meant one thing. "Stain removed" meant another. A future restorer, tired or vain, might obey the wrong sentence for decades.

Mara cleaned the gear teeth under a magnifier, enough to read tool marks and not enough to erase them. She worked slowly, stopping every few minutes to photograph a surface or mutter at a past mechanic.

"People think restoration means making a thing pretty," she said. "Mostly it means refusing to let ugliness hide under dirt."

Under old grease she found a second filing angle. The first altered speed through the lift. The second reduced the catch on return, working with the hidden counterweight.

"Two modifications," she said. "First speed. Then quiet."

Talia confirmed that the second filing carried the same repair-stock residue as the first. Jonas found a Pike Yard maintenance entry dated two months before the Maribel loss: tower action improved; operator complaint addressed. Ada disliked the neatness of it and appreciated it anyway.

She added the dates to the timeline. The machine had been prepared before the first disputed fog night. Not repaired after a storm. Prepared.

Olan watched silently. He had stopped defending the opening schedule and begun asking smaller, better questions.

"Can it still be displayed?" he asked.

"Yes," Mara said. "If you display what it is."

"A compromised public instrument."

"A public instrument modified for private use," Ada said. "Compromised makes it sound like the brass had a bad week."

Olan wrote the correction down.

That was the real restoration. Not brass shine. Function plus truth. The arms moved smoothly now, which made their history no cleaner. Ada had known officers like that: efficient, polished, rotten in the orders they chose to obey.

Mara asked Lee to assist with the final cleaning of non-evidentiary surfaces. She did not need him for skill. She needed him to learn the difference between care and erasure. Ada watched him take instructions without defensiveness.

"Leave that discoloration," Mara said.

"It looks dirty."

"It is information."

He stopped with the swab in the air. "How do you know which is which?"

"By asking what will be lost if you make it pretty."

Ada wrote that down though it belonged to Mara, not the report. It might help Olan with the exhibit text. It might help the museum with more than brass.

Talia arrived with a portable microscope and confirmed the tool marks after cleaning. The second filing angle showed more clearly now. Fine scratches ran parallel along the tooth face, then stopped abruptly where original machining resumed. A repairman had not replaced a broken part. He had edited the old one.

"Like revising a sentence," Jonas said.

"A sentence that moves metal," Mara said.

Olan asked whether the restored motion should be demonstrated in the exhibit. Mara surprised Ada by saying yes.

"People need to see how little difference a lie requires," she said. "Not a monster machine. Not a secret lever. A half-beat, a weight, a shield."

Ada agreed. The danger of the device was its modesty. Grand conspiracies invited disbelief. This had the small engineering of daily corruption.

Chapter 28: Customs Ledger

The county storage facility sat behind a vehicle depot and had the civic charm of a place no one visited unless obligated. Iris signed in at a plastic window. Ada signed after her. The clerk looked at the request form, then at the two women, then back at the form as if historical ledgers might be contagious.

"Harbor receipts are in Bay C," he said. "If the light flickers, hit the switch twice."

"Reassuring," Ada said.

Bay C held metal shelves, sagging boxes, and the sour smell of paper stored by people who believed dry enough was a standard. Iris found the ledger because she distrusted indexes and read the shelf labels backward.

"Backward?" Ada asked.

"People misfile forward," Iris said.

The method sounded ridiculous until it worked. Third shelf from the bottom, two boxes past the official range, a ledger with a cracked spine and a paper flag dated by a clerk who had long since become handwriting. Ada held the flashlight while Iris eased it free.

Iris found the customs ledger in a county storage facility that smelled of cardboard, mildew, and civic underfunding. The book had been misindexed under Harbor Miscellaneous Receipts, which was the kind of title that made wrongdoing feel bored with itself.

Duty payments had been rerouted through a family account under the name East Service. The amounts matched missing cargo valuations less a handling fee. The handwriting changed twice, but the pattern did not.

"Polite theft," Nadia said when Iris showed the page.

"Documented theft," Iris answered.

The ledger tied Pike Yard, the admiral's office, and private settlements. It did not show every coin. It showed enough. Maribel tea became a credit. Eastwake copper became a service adjustment. Orison glass became salvage reimbursement routed through an account no public office should have used.

At the next status conference, Calder's lawyers argued historical distance.

Judge Arda asked whether historical distance had deposited the funds.

The room enjoyed that more than lawyers prefer.

Ada read the account names later in the archive room. Numbers could seem bloodless until placed beside cargo, cargo beside people, people beside power. Signals moved arms. Ledgers moved profit. Both had to be read by someone willing to be disliked.

Olan stood over the ledger for a long time. "My predecessor accepted Pike money for the restoration."

"Money often arrives washed," Iris said.

"And leaves stains anyway," Nadia added.

Iris traced the East Service account into a private bank ledger held on microfilm at the county clerk's office. The reader made every page look like a ghost of itself, white letters on black. She and Ada sat shoulder to shoulder in a cramped room while the machine hummed and the clerk outside argued on the phone about copier toner.

"There," Iris said.

A deposit followed the Maribel loss by nine days. Another followed Eastwake by six. The amounts were reduced, rounded, then split. One share went to Pike Yard improvements. One to a household account. One to something labeled civic subscription.

"Respectability," Ada said.

"Bought wholesale."

They printed the frames, certified them, and carried them back in a folder Iris kept under her arm despite the rain. The ledger did not have the drama of a hidden drawer or moving brass. It had columns. Columns were how theft became furniture, scholarships, portraits, polished donor plaques.

At the museum, Olan read the bank entries and sat very still. "Civic subscription funded the first harbor memorial."

Jonas checked another file and confirmed it. Part of the stolen cargo money had helped build the stone memorial where Pike's name appeared above public sacrifice. The fact was so neat Ada distrusted it until three documents agreed.

Nadia did not speak for a long moment. "They made families pay twice," she said finally. "First with cargo, then with gratitude."

No one improved the sentence. Iris added the memorial fund to the restitution theory. Not because a court could easily unwind a century-old monument, but because public money trails mattered.

Chapter 29: Bram Testifies

Bram hated the courthouse before he entered it. He disliked the polished stone, the echo, the posted rules about hats, the guard who asked him to empty his pockets, and the fact that the fog logs had to pass through a scanner as if paper could be dangerous only in modern ways.

"It is all right," Iris said.

"It is not all right. It is procedure."

"Procedure can still get us where we need to go."

He grunted, unconvinced, but handed over the waterproof case only after Ada stood where he could see her. Trust, she thought, often worked by line of sight.

In the hallway, Nadia adjusted his collar without asking. Bram endured it with the stoicism of a man facing weather.

"You look like a summoned undertaker," she said.

"I feel like cargo."

"Then get delivered properly."

He smiled despite himself. The moment steadied him more than Iris's careful instructions had.

Bram testified in a jacket that looked recently attacked by an iron. He carried the fog logs himself in a waterproof case and surrendered them to the clerk only after confirming the receipt listed every volume.

Iris led him through his family history, tug work, fog practice, and the reason private logs existed.

"Public records tell what was scheduled," Bram said. "Water tells what happened. Families on water write that down if they want to keep eating."

He read his grandmother's entry for the rescue night. His voice roughened once, then steadied. "No horn at 02:08 to 02:12. Tower lamp visible east. Fast arms, not drill."

"What does that mean to you?" Iris asked.

"We heard no horn when the public log says horn. We saw tower flashes where no drill should be. My grandmother wrote it. My father believed it. I checked it."

Calder's lawyer rose for cross-examination with the confidence of a man about to mishandle a dockworker.

"Mr. Vale, harbor families disliked Admiral Pike, did they not?"

"Some did. He earned it."

Judge Arda looked down. "Answer only the question."

"That was my answer."

Ada kept her face still.

The lawyer tried bias, memory, family resentment, and the unreliability of fog. Bram answered each with a time, a place, or an object. Memory alone could be dismissed. Memory with timing became structure. Bram's logs matched Ada's reenactment, Nadia's notebook, and the watch margins.

When he stepped down, several harbor families nodded. Not applause. Recognition. The court officer did not have to quiet them. They had learned long ago how to make small public gestures carry.

On redirect, Iris asked Bram why the Vale logs had been kept after everyone involved in the original events had died.

Bram rubbed one thumb along the edge of the witness box. "Because work repeats. Fog repeats. Men with power repeat themselves if no one writes them down."

"Were the logs altered for this case?"

"No."

"How do you know?"

"Because my grandmother's ink is not available in modern shops, my father's spelling mistakes are consistent, and if I were inventing evidence I would make us look less poor."

That drew a warning glance from the judge and a rustle from the gallery. Calder's lawyer chose not to recross.

Afterward, in the courthouse hallway, Bram looked embarrassed by attention. A reporter asked for a statement. He said, "Read the logs," and walked away. Ada approved.

She found him outside by the steps, breathing as if the courtroom had been smaller than any engine room. "You did well," she said.

"I hate being looked at."

"You were heard."

"That is different."

"Yes."

He handed her a folded copy of his testimony notes. They were not notes at all, only dates and names written in a blocky hand: grandmother Mara Vale, father Tomas Vale, Nell Vale, east lane, horn gap. Anchors for memory. A man who disliked speeches had placed his family in the public record.

Chapter 30: Museum Rift

Olan called the emergency session for noon and then spent the morning regretting noon. By eleven-thirty, trustees were already arriving with expressions prepared in advance. Some wore concern. Some wore anger. One wore the bright fixed smile of a person hoping procedure would rescue him from judgment.

Ada took a chair against the wall. Iris sat beside her with a legal pad. Jonas stood near the door because he distrusted rooms where archives were discussed without archivists close enough to object.

"Do not let them say pause," Ada murmured.

Iris wrote the word on her pad and underlined it. "Pause usually means bury."

Olan opened the meeting with a timeline instead of a plea. Ada gave him credit for that. Dates on a screen did not solve cowardice, but they made evasions work harder. Calder donation pledged. Restoration begun. Mechanical irregularity noted. Signal pages missing. Preservation petition filed. Every line narrowed the room.

The museum board split in emergency session. The conference room had too little air and too much polished wood. Some trustees wanted to cancel the exhibit and return the semaphore to storage until tempers cooled. Others wanted to "contextualize" without naming theft.

Ada disliked contextualize when it meant soften.

Olan asked her, in front of them all, what an honest exhibit would require.

"Signal book. Modified gear. Cargo names. Family profit. Harbor claims. No donor wall."

Helene, the trustee from Calder's dinner, folded her hands. "No donor wall at all?"

"Not for the subject of the investigation."

Calder's representative stood. He had been introduced as a foundation adviser and behaved like a warning letter in human form. "The patron will withdraw."

Iris answered from the back of the room. "The patron is evidence."

That ended the polite portion.

Arguments followed. Budget. Reputation. School programs. Legal exposure. A trustee asked whether the museum should take positions on old wrongdoing. Jonas, who had been invited to answer archive questions and had remained quiet too long, said, "A museum is a position against forgetting."

No one improved on that.

The board voted to continue research and suspend Calder's naming rights. Not courage, exactly. Courage would have been cheaper if chosen earlier. But movement mattered.

Afterward, Olan stood in conservation beside the brass semaphore. "It was easier when it was a hero."

"Most lies are," Ada said.

Helene stayed after the vote, gathering coffee cups while trustees fled to other obligations. Ada recognized the gesture. Some people apologized by cleaning a room.

"I should have asked more at the dinner," Helene said.

"Yes."

The answer startled her, then she nodded. "Fair."

"Ask now."

Helene looked through the conservation glass at the covered semaphore. "What should the board fear most?"

"Not Calder."

"Then what?"

"The next donor who learns Calder almost succeeded."

Helene set the cups in a stack. "Policy, then."

"Policy before charm."

By afternoon, she had asked Jonas for a list of archive review requirements and Iris for conflict language. Olan looked exhausted when he learned this, then relieved. Institutions often needed one trustee to become tedious in the right direction.

The rift did not close. Two trustees resigned before supper, issuing statements about process and fairness. A third called Olan privately and asked whether the museum's insurance covered defamation. Calder's adviser sent another letter. The development office circulated a budget sheet with red cells multiplying like rash.

Ada watched the panic with limited sympathy. Money problems were real. So were roofs, salaries, school buses, conservation supplies. But fear of losing money had already nearly turned the museum into an accessory after the fact.

Chapter 31: Signal Scratch

The rain that morning made the conservation room feel shipboard. Water ticked in the vents, shoes squeaked on the sealed floor, and every metal surface held a faint chill. Ada liked the weather for scratch work. Raking light showed better when the world outside had stopped competing for brightness.

Mara set the hub under the lamp and turned off the overheads. The marks appeared one by one, not dramatic, not secret in any theatrical sense, simply waiting for the right angle.

"There," Nadia said.

Her voice changed when she saw them. It lost its public hardness and became private.

Ada did not answer immediately. Some recognitions needed a second to belong to the person who had made them. The scratches had existed for a century without Nadia in the room. Now they had reached her family line, and evidence became inheritance in reverse.

Ada matched the hub scratches to Nadia's notebook key under raking light while rain ticked against the conservation-room vent. Short-long-short-cross. Fog gap, east lane. The mark repeated near the handle stop, faint but present, exactly where a signalman would glance in low light.

"Operator reminder," Ada said.

Mara nodded. "Worn by use, not decoration."

The scratch was small enough to miss and too practical to be ornamental. Ada had seen similar marks on field equipment, shipboard lockers, radio dials, anything used under pressure by people who valued speed over rules. A hidden system always needed ordinary aids. Criminal cleverness still forgot things.

Jonas entered with the misfiled drill report in a sleeve and an expression close to triumph. "Margin note."

In the clerk's cramped hand, beside the lamp-shield sketch, appeared the same shorthand: short, long, short, cross. No explanation. None needed for the people who had used it.

Three independent surfaces: brass, notebook, paper.

Iris called it corroboration. Bram, when told by phone, called it "about time." Ada called it readable.

The brass had become more than object. It was a manual, altered by hands that assumed no one later would know how to read them. They had underestimated old officers, angry clerks, careful archivists, and families who kept notebooks in biscuit tins.

Ada added a new section to her report: Physical Encoding Marks. It sounded dull. Good. Dull headings made strong findings harder to dismiss as romance.

She made the section deliberately plain. No fraud, no conspiracy, no heroic reversal. Just mark location, mark form, comparable notation, visible angle, probable function. Iris read it and crossed out one adjective Ada had missed.

"You are usually more severe with yourself," Iris said.

"Fatigue."

"Dangerous in reports."

Ada accepted the rebuke. A single eager word could give Calder's lawyers a handle. She had seen strong findings weakened by the writer's desire to be understood emotionally as well as technically. Let the evidence carry its own anger.

Jonas brought the original signal book to the table and placed a facsimile beside it for handling. "If the missing pages return, the scratches become supporting evidence. If they do not, they remain a bridge."

"A bridge to what?" Olan asked.

"To the fact that someone used the same private shorthand in three places."

Mara pointed to the enlarged scratch. "And used it where the hand would need it."

Olan looked from brass to notebook to drill margin. "It feels like learning a language after the conversation ended."

"Yes," Ada said. "But the damage it caused is still speaking."

The room quieted at that. Outside, the museum's public lobby carried on: footsteps, a child asking about restrooms, a guard giving directions. Ada liked the contrast. Serious work should happen close to ordinary life. It kept people from believing history lived in a separate chamber, sealed from lunch breaks and wet coats.

Before she left, Nadia asked to see the scratch photographs again. She stood before them with her lips pressed tight.

"Aunt Ressa would have wanted to slap whoever made those marks," she said.

"Probably more than one person," Ada said.

"She had two hands."

For once, Ada let herself laugh.

Chapter 32: Patron Threat

The envelope arrived too thick to be useful and too expensive to be ignored. Olan brought it to the archive still sealed, as if opening it alone might trigger a clause. Iris slit it with a letter knife from Jonas's desk and spread the pages under weights.

"Never trust stationery that wants to be touched," Jonas said.

"Paper is innocent," Iris answered. "Usually."

Ada read the first paragraph over her shoulder. The letter tried to sound wounded before it sounded dangerous. That was a donor habit too: injury first, power second.

Olan asked whether he should call the board chair before responding. Iris said yes, but not from fear. "Call to inform, not to ask permission."

The distinction mattered. Ada watched him write it down. Permission had been the museum's old posture around Calder. Information would have to become the new one.

Calder's threat letter arrived on cream stationery with a watermark and a fury of expensive verbs. It accused the museum, Iris, unnamed activists, and "external consultants" of reckless revisionism, reputational injury, donor disparagement, and interference with philanthropic intent.

Iris read it aloud in chambers because Judge Arda wanted the dispute placed on record.

"Cease, desist, retract, preserve."

"Preserve is ours," Ada said.

The judge's mouth moved almost into a smile. "Ms. Vey, do not help unless asked."

"Understood."

Judge Arda refused to restrict the museum's factual investigation. She warned both sides against public overstatement and reminded Calder's counsel that preservation orders were not decorative.

Calder issued a statement anyway, accusing activists of rewriting maritime heritage. Lorn Harbor responded in the way of small ports with long memories. Copies of cargo claims appeared in shop windows. Bram taped his grandmother's fog entry beside the tug office coffee pot. Nadia's cousin put Ressa Voss's name on a chalkboard outside a bakery and misspelled admiral on purpose.

Lorn enjoyed a paper fight.

Ada disliked spectacle, but she liked records becoming public. Documents shut in drawers fed legends. Documents in windows made people ask better questions.

At the museum, the semaphore stayed under cloth. Its silence was now court-ordered, not donor-controlled. That difference pleased Ada more than it should have.

The threat letter changed the building's temperature. Staff who had been curious became careful. Development officers closed office doors. The front desk received three calls from men who wanted to know whether the museum had become political, which meant they had read Calder's statement and none of the documents.

Olan asked Ada to join the staff briefing. She stood at the back while he explained the preservation order, the investigation, and the difference between evidence and accusation. He did not do it perfectly. He said "sensitive history" once and "stakeholders" twice. But he did not blame weather, process, or unnamed errors. That counted.

A visitor services supervisor raised her hand. "What do we say if guests ask whether Admiral Pike was a criminal?"

Olan looked toward Ada.

"Say the museum is examining evidence that public equipment was used for private cargo transfers during Pike's command," Ada said. "Say the exhibit will show the evidence. Do not summarize what you have not read."

"That's a bit long."

"Truth often is."

Jonas, from the side wall, added, "We can make a staff sheet."

"Make it one page," the supervisor said. "People do not read three."

That was wisdom from the public floor. Jonas wrote it down.

Later, Calder's second letter arrived by courier, addressed to Olan and copied to half the board. Iris read it standing, then placed it flat on the table as if it were damp.

"They are trying to create fear of personal liability," she said.

"Will it work?" Olan asked.

"On some people."

"And on the facts?"

Iris smiled faintly. "Facts are harder to intimidate, though people keep trying."

Ada watched Olan fold the letter and put it in the case file rather than his desk. A small decision, but in institutions small decisions decided where memory would live.

Chapter 33: Tower Clock

The clock room had been ignored because it did not look like a gallery. That made it useful. It held dust, oil, maintenance slips, and the kind of ordinary precision that heroic labels rarely bothered to mention. Ada climbed the last stair and smelled metal before she saw the works.

Mara was already offended. She had found a bent access panel, three loose screws, and a maintenance brush stored bristle-down in a cup.

"People ask why machines fail," she said.

"And?"

"Usually because people are rude to them."

The clock face looked out over the harbor through glass that had gone wavy with age. From the floor below came the muffled sounds of visitors in unrelated galleries. Ada liked that the clock had kept its authority without applause. Time did not need admiration to govern a harbor. It needed maintenance and witnesses.

Mara repaired the tower clock because timing mattered and because broken clocks offended her. The clock occupied a cramped room below the signal platform, its face turned toward the harbor, its works dusty but intact. Pigeons had been there once. Mara took that personally.

"No creature with feathers respects an escapement," she said.

Ada held the lamp while Mara cleaned, oiled, and adjusted. Old photographs showed the clock four minutes slow during a 1932 ceremony, but maintenance logs from the cargo years showed it inspected weekly and accurate to the minute.

"Someone kept time very well," Mara said.

That mattered. The private signal depended on horn gaps, lamp timing, and arm speed. Sloppy time would make the system dangerous to its users. Precise time made it profitable.

The restored clock confirmed foghorn intervals. Ada ran the sequence on paper: horn, silence, private flash, horn. Then she ran it with Jonas reading old logs and Bram's grandmother's notes. The pieces met too neatly to ignore.

"It fits too cleanly," Jonas said.

"Clean mechanisms often serve dirty plans."

The clock face looked over the harbor with restored indifference. Time had been used as cover, then as proof. Ada found that elegant in the cold way evidence could be elegant.

Mara closed the case. "Do not let donors touch this either."

"I will put that in policy," Jonas said.

He probably would. The museum was becoming a place where even clocks received custody rules.

Mara found the old timing certificates in a drawer below the clock weights. They were brittle, signed by harbor inspectors who had cared about minutes because tide, cargo, and liability all depended on them. One certificate covered the week before the Maribel loss: Tower clock checked against harbor master chronometer. Variation under thirty seconds.

"So the public log's times were not approximate," Iris said.

"Not if the operator followed the clock," Ada answered.

"And Bram's grandmother's times?"

"She wrote by ear and habit. Her margin is within the same window."

Mara wound the clock one turn and let the escapement tick under the open case. The sound filled the small room with patient mechanical insistence. Ada counted ten beats before she stopped herself. Old training never left; it merely waited for rhythm.

Olan climbed up to the clock room carrying coffee in paper cups. "The board chair wants to know whether the clock is now evidence too."

"Yes," Ada, Mara, and Iris said together.

He handed out the coffee. "I assumed."

Progress, Ada thought, could sound like a tired director bringing bad coffee to the right room.

They mapped the horn intervals against the clock certificates, Bram's logs, the watch copies, and the pencil marks on the sill. The result was not a dramatic revelation but a tightening. The fog gap had not been an accident of broken equipment. It had been measured on a system capable of accuracy.

Jonas arrived last, out of breath from the stairs, with a photocopy of an operator payroll. Two names repeated on private coordination nights. One later worked for Pike Yard. The other disappeared from the public payroll after a dispute noted only as insubordination.

"Find the dispute file," Ada said.

"Already requested."

"Good."

The clock ticked between them. Time had been cover, then witness, and now assignment.

Chapter 34: Lost Cargo

The first draft of the cargo list looked too tidy, and Ada distrusted it on sight. Loss had not occurred in neat rows. It had arrived as unpaid invoices, delayed wages, failed repairs, bad credit, bitter inheritance, and family stories that hardened around names. Iris understood and still needed rows.

"Courts require columns," she said.

"People require context."

"Then we will build both and make everyone unhappy."

Nadia approved of that plan. She brought a thermos, sat beside Iris, and corrected every family name she recognized before the law could flatten it into an entity.

"Not Marten and Vale," she said, tapping one line. "Marten ampersand Vale. The ampersand mattered to old Mr. Vale. He said and was for people who trusted each other."

Bram, when consulted, confirmed this with embarrassment. Jonas corrected the entry. Ada found the smallness of the correction moving. Accuracy often entered through doors grandeur ignored.

Iris identified descendants, dissolved firms, successor companies, and family trusts tied to the lost cargo. Some claims were legally thin, ghosts with surnames and not enough paper. Others had enough fraud evidence to reopen settlements or at least force accounting from Pike foundation funds.

The restitution list began as a spreadsheet and became, in Ada's mind, a harbor map. Tea, copper, medical glass. Smaller cargoes too: lamp oil, machine belts, sealed crates of surgical tubing. Goods that had moved through hands and disappeared into fog.

At a hearing, Calder's lawyer tried impatience. "This is not about antique tea."

Nadia answered from the gallery, "It is when your family sold it."

Judge Arda warned the room, but not before the sentence had done its work.

Ada read the cargo list that evening. The money from those goods had built houses, funded campaigns, endowed prizes, bought respectability, and eventually paid for a museum restoration meant to praise the man who had arranged the loss. Historical theft did not become harmless because the thieves had portraits.

Iris filed the restitution list with careful caveats. It was imperfect. It had gaps, estimated values, contested successors. It was also the first list the harbor families had ever seen with their losses taken seriously.

Bram stood outside the courthouse after filing and looked uncomfortable with hope.

"Do lists help?" he asked.

"More than speeches," Ada said.

"Good. I dislike speeches."

"I had noticed."

The restitution spreadsheet grew columns no one liked: original owner, successor claimant, documented value, probable value, proof strength, limitation issue, moral claim. Iris disliked the last heading and kept it anyway because Nadia insisted some losses would never meet a court's appetite and still deserved to be seen.

"Moral claim is not a legal category," Iris said.

"Then invent a better place to put the dead," Nadia answered.

Ada expected Iris to object. Instead she adjusted the column width.

They worked through cargo one item at a time. Tea was easier than people expected because invoices survived. Copper was harder because it had been split and resold. Orison glass required a medical charity archive, a hospital ledger, and a retired nurse's grandson who kept photographs of the old dispensary in a shoebox. Each proof path had its own weather.

Olan asked whether naming weak claims would expose the museum to criticism.

"Everything exposes the museum to criticism," Ada said. "Try accuracy."

Bram attended one session and left after twenty minutes. "Numbers make theft look polite," he said in the hall.

"That is why Iris makes them impolite again."

He looked through the glass at the table where Iris was arguing with a century-old invoice. "She charges by the frown?"

"Likely by the wound."

Bram nodded as if that made sense and went to smoke in the rain.

By evening, the cargo list had become too large for one screen. Jonas printed it and taped the pages end to end along the archive wall. People walking past slowed, then stopped. A list had gravity when it took up space. Ada watched Olan read from one end to the other without speaking.

"It is bigger than the exhibit," he said.

"It always was."

Chapter 35: Hidden Drawer

The cabinet had been moved only six feet, but Jonas treated the shift as if the building itself had confessed. One corner lifted when it should not. A back panel clicked under pressure. Dust lay differently along the base, a pale crescent where no mop had reached for years.

"Do you see it?" he asked.

Ada knelt beside him. "I see that you are about to become impossible."

"I was always possible. Under certain conditions."

"This is not one of them."

He did not smile. His hand hovered near the warped panel and stopped. Training held. Excitement did not get to touch evidence first.

Ada called Iris before the panel opened. She called Mara too, because mechanical hiding was still mechanics. While they waited, Jonas stood with his hands clasped behind his back and stared at the cabinet as if it might change its mind.

"Breathe," Ada said.

"I am preserving oxygen."

Jonas found the hidden drawer because the signal-book cabinet did not sit square after being moved for conservation cleaning. One back panel bowed where it should have been flat. He noticed the shadow line, fetched a lamp, and then called Ada in a voice so controlled she knew he was near shouting.

"Do not touch anything yet," she said when she arrived.

"I am touching nothing. I am breathing judgmentally."

Behind the warped panel was a shallow drawer without a pull. Inside lay three folded pages tied once with dark thread. The paper matched the stubs in the 1917 signal book.

"I may faint," Jonas said.

"Sit after photographing."

They documented the drawer, the panel, the thread, the folds. Ada did not touch the pages until gloves, camera, scale, and witness log were ready. Paper deserved ceremony when it returned from concealment.

The missing pages listed private-yard exception signals. 7-4-2 appeared in a neat hand: east transfer clear. Beside the line sat initials: A.P.

Admiral Pike.

Iris arrived fifteen minutes later, hair windblown, coat buttoned wrong. She read the first line and closed her eyes.

"That is enough."

"Enough begins paperwork," Ada said.

The pages had not been destroyed. Hidden, perhaps by someone afraid to expose Pike and unwilling to erase proof. Cowardice and conscience could share a drawer. Ada looked at the cabinet, at Jonas's pale delighted face, at the old paper under light. The archive had kept its own counsel until the room was ready to listen.

They did not unfold the pages fully until Talia arrived with humidity controls and a portable support tray. Jonas hovered so intensely that Iris ordered him to sit in the corner for two minutes. He obeyed, badly.

"If anyone tears those pages," he said, "I will become difficult."

"Become?" Mara said.

The thread had stained the folds. The paper resisted at first, then yielded under gentle moisture. Line by line, the missing code returned. Private-yard exception signals. Temporary fog transfers. Shielded lamp acknowledgments. 7-4-2. East transfer clear. The letters A.P. were not flamboyant. They sat in the margin with administrative calm.

Ada felt no triumph. Triumph would have made the page too small. What she felt was the sober click of a lock opening after everyone had stopped pretending there was no door.

Iris photographed the initials from three angles. "We need handwriting comparison."

Jonas was already reaching for a file. "Pike signed tower orders, board minutes, and maintenance approvals. We have samples."

"Do not rush."

"I am hurrying slowly."

Nadia arrived before the comparison finished, summoned by Iris because the Voss notebook had helped make the drawer meaningful. She stood behind the table and looked at the page for a long time.

"So she was right," Nadia said.

"Yes," Ada said.

"Not just angry."

"Anger and accuracy can share a hand."

Nadia nodded once, then turned away to wipe her face with the heel of her palm. No one commented. Some confirmations arrived too late to comfort and still mattered enough to hurt.

By midnight, preliminary comparison showed the initials matched Pike's order hand. Iris sealed the pages for court. Jonas wrote recovered from concealed cabinet drawer in the accession note, pressing so hard the pen nearly tore the paper.

Chapter 36: Admirals Order

The order reached court in a flat evidence box, but it entered the room before the clerk carried it in. Everyone knew by then what the missing pages contained. Knowing did not reduce the force of seeing. The page had weight because it was small, because it did not rant, because it gave permission in the language of routine.

Ada watched Calder when Iris lifted the document. His face did not collapse. It tightened. That told her more than collapse would have. Shock performs innocence. Tightness protects a door.

"This is the recovered order," Iris said.

Judge Arda put on her reading glasses.

The room made the sound of people not moving. Paper could do that when it had waited long enough. Ada watched Judge Arda read the first line, then the second, then return to the first. The judge's face did not change, but her pen moved closer to her hand.

The admiral's order was brief enough to fit on half a page and careful enough to trouble everyone who read it. During heavy fog, tower may employ private-yard exception signals to manage east transfers. It named no smuggling. It did not mention tea, copper, glass, or settlements. It created the channel through which all of them could pass.

Iris placed the order beside the cargo ledger in court. Exception became transfer. Transfer became profit. Profit became legacy. She did not say it that way aloud; she was too disciplined. But Ada could see the chain in the arrangement of paper.

Calder's lawyer argued operational necessity. Wartime pressure. Crowded harbor. Limited visibility. Need for flexibility.

Ada took the stand and explained that official emergency codes already existed for every lawful need he named. Private-yard exception signals were unnecessary for safety and perfect for concealment. If the purpose had been public order, the code would have remained in the public book. If the purpose had been emergency coordination, customs would have been copied. If the purpose had been lawful cargo movement, settlement records would not have hidden behind family accounts.

Judge Arda asked, "Would a lawful transfer require missing pages?"

No one answered quickly enough.

The order did not show a villain announcing evil. Ada distrusted stories that required villains to behave theatrically. The page showed authority made flexible until theft could pass through. That was more believable and more damning.

After the hearing, Olan stood in the corridor with a copy in his hand. "It sounds so ordinary."

"Most bad orders do," Ada said. "That is why people sign them."

In the corridor, Calder's lawyers gathered around him like men trying to keep a tent from lifting in wind. Calder did not look at the copy in Olan's hand. He looked at Ada.

"You have your initials," he said.

"The court has them."

"Initials are not a soul."

"No. They are authorization."

He gave a short laugh. "You want a century to behave like a modern compliance file."

"No. I want powerful men not to hide theft inside weather."

For once, his answer did not come quickly. Around them, court staff moved carts, lawyers checked phones, and harbor families stood in small groups under fluorescent light. The grandeur Calder preferred had deserted him. The case had become paper, hallway, damp coats, and a judge's calendar.

Iris touched Ada's sleeve. "Enough."

Ada stepped back. She had no need to win a corridor exchange. The order had done more than any sharp sentence could.

That evening, she rewrote the relevant section of her report. The admiral's order did not prove every transfer, but it established authorized use of private-yard exception signals during fog. It connected the missing code pages to command authority. It made the altered gear less a curiosity and more an implementation.

She added a paragraph on limits. The evidence did not show Pike moving crates with his own hands. It did not identify every operator. It did not convert rumor into omniscience. But it showed a system he authorized and from which his accounts benefited.

Limits mattered. Without them, truth began to imitate legend. Ada had come too far to build a cleaner lie.

Chapter 37: Brass Witness

By the time the evidence chain reached the screen, the courtroom had lost its appetite for single dramatic proof. That helped. Ada had always distrusted cases that depended on one miraculous object. Real systems left distributed marks: metal, paper, money, memory, habit.

Iris dimmed the lights and did not raise her voice. The first image showed the gear teeth. The second showed the counterweight. The third showed the code page. Each slide carried a date, source, and custody note. Jonas had insisted on the custody notes. Ada had not argued.

"We proceed link by link," Iris said.

Judge Arda nodded. Calder stared at the table.

Talia took the stand first. She had dressed plainly and brought no theatrical enlargements of her own, only the lab report, photographs, and a reference sample sealed in a small bag. She answered questions as if every adjective had to pass through a scale. Ada admired that. Exactness was a form of courage when money wanted blur.

Talia linked the hidden counterweight to Pike Yard through alloy, stamp, and tool mark. Mara linked the weight to arm speed. Ada linked arm speed to the private signal. Jonas linked the signal to the missing pages. Iris linked the pages to cargo money.

"A chain," Judge Arda said.

"Yes," Iris answered.

The courtroom screen showed the evidence in sequence. Gear teeth. Graphite grease. Lamp shield. Hub scratches. Notebook key. Fog log. Cargo ledger. Hidden pages. No single item had to carry the whole weight. Together they made denial stagger.

Calder looked at the semaphore as if it had betrayed him. Ada thought betrayal required loyalty first. Brass had none. It carried wear. It received hands, pressure, grease, and time. It did not care whose name was on the foundation letterhead.

Talia's final slide showed the PYC stamp enlarged until the three letters filled the screen. In the gallery, someone whispered. The sound passed like a small wind.

"This mark was hidden inside the base," Talia said. "It was not visible to an operator or visitor. It functioned mechanically, not decoratively."

The instrument had become the best kind of witness: indifferent, specific, and difficult to flatter. The family mark had been there all along, pulling the arm faster through fog.

When court recessed, Bram passed Ada in the aisle. "Brass talks better than men."

"Only after someone learns the language," she said.

During the afternoon session, Calder's counsel tried to break the chain into harmless pieces. A hidden counterweight could be repair. A filed gear could be wear. A private signal could be drill shorthand. A ledger entry could be accounting convenience. A missing page could be old mishandling. One by one, each explanation sounded possible for less than a minute.

Iris did not object often. She let the lawyer build little shelters, then asked a witness to remove the roof.

"Could this counterweight alone prove smuggling?" she asked Talia.

"No."

"Could the gear alone prove smuggling?"

"No."

"Could the code page alone prove cargo transfer?"

"It proves a private signal meaning east transfer clear."

"And when placed with the ledger?"

"It supports use."

Ada watched Judge Arda's face. The judge disliked drama and liked sequence. Iris gave her sequence. The screen returned again to the enlarged PYC stamp, then to the gear teeth, then to Bram's horn gap, then to the East Service deposit. Not a thunderclap. A mechanism.

Olan sat behind Iris with both hands clasped, a museum director learning what an exhibit could become under oath. Jonas had three pencils aligned before him and used only one. Nadia sat beside Bram and did not look away when Pike's initials appeared.

At the break, a reporter asked Ada whether the semaphore was the smoking gun. She answered, "It is a brass instrument with documented alterations."

"That is less quotable."

"Good."

The reporter used it anyway. By evening, the phrase had reached the museum staff chat, stripped of context but not entirely of meaning. Ada disliked that and accepted it. Public truth traveled badly. The work was to give it enough structure to survive the trip.

Chapter 38: Injunction

The order arrived by email first, then by clerk-stamped copy an hour later. Iris trusted only the stamped copy. She carried it to the museum in a folder held flat against her chest, as if the paper itself might be jostled into ambiguity.

Olan gathered the department heads in the education room. No one sat. Standing made the decision feel less like policy and more like weather they all had to move through.

"Read the operative paragraph," Ada said.

Iris did. Preserve. Halt. Bar. Produce. Permit factual notice. The words were blunt enough to be useful.

The education coordinator asked whether school groups should be canceled. Olan looked at Ada, then stopped himself and answered. "Postponed until we have something true to teach."

That sentence did more for staff confidence than a longer speech would have. Ada saw shoulders lower around the room. People could work with postponed truth. They could not work with false celebration.

The injunction halted the exhibit opening, preserved all materials, and barred Calder from removing, altering, selling, or "curating" any family archive items related to harbor signals, Pike Yard repairs, or cargo settlements. Judge Arda's quotation marks around curating pleased Jonas deeply.

The order also allowed the museum to publish a factual notice of disputed history while the case proceeded. Olan read that paragraph twice and looked relieved.

"You are allowed to tell the truth now?" Ada asked.

"Ordered, even."

"Convenient."

The donor wall came down that afternoon. Staff removed brass letters from a panel that had never deserved them. No one cheered. The work was too awkward for that. Screws stuck. Adhesive tore paint. A facilities worker named Ren muttered that rich names always left holes.

Calder's statement called the order a misunderstanding of maritime practice. Bram taped a copy beside his tug's coffee pot and wrote ask me about practice under it.

The museum posted its notice by the closed gallery doors: Opening delayed pending preservation and review of newly identified evidence. It was plain, almost severe. Visitors stopped to read it. Some frowned. Some took photographs. One older woman touched the word evidence and nodded once before walking away.

The semaphore remained under cloth. Its arms would move again, but not for opening-night applause. Ada found that mercy of a kind.

The injunction also changed how people walked around the museum. Staff lowered their voices near the conservation room, not from reverence but from legal awareness. A registrar added a red line to the object file: subject to court order. Jonas made a duplicate preservation binder and hid it in a place he refused to name even to Ada.

"That seems excessive," she said.

"I have been reading about missing pages."

"Fair."

Olan drafted the public notice himself, then brought it to Ada, Iris, and Jonas. His first version still tried to soothe. Newly identified evidence has raised questions. Iris crossed out raised questions and wrote documents alteration and private signal use. Jonas crossed out heritage context and wrote disputed donor narrative. Ada crossed out unfortunate delay and wrote preservation.

Olan looked at the marked page. "You have left me no soft furniture."

"People can stand," Iris said.

He rewrote it. The final notice was short enough to read in one breath and plain enough to annoy everyone who preferred mist.

Calder's statement came two hours later. Misunderstanding. Activists. Maritime complexity. Family service. Ada read it once, then filed it under modern fog. Bram's annotation on the tug copy became a minor harbor attraction by supper. People stopped by to laugh and then asked him what the grandmother's log actually said. That was useful. Mockery opened the door; records had to walk through.

In the gallery, Ren patched the donor wall holes with compound. "You know," he said, smoothing one pale scar, "paint never matches after this."

"Let it show a little," Ada said.

"Museum won't like that."

"The museum is learning."

He considered the wall. "A little, then."

Chapter 39: Calder Breaks

Iris's office had bad coffee and excellent chairs, which Ada assumed was strategic. People who came to confess, bargain, or evade should be physically comfortable enough to keep talking. Calder arrived with one lawyer, one folder, and no Calder Foundation lapel pin. Ada noticed its absence immediately.

"This is a settlement discussion," his lawyer said.

"It is a production compliance meeting," Iris answered. "Settlement may occur if truth becomes less shy."

Calder looked at Ada. "Must she be here?"

"Yes," Iris said.

Ada said nothing. Silence made some men overexplain.

Calder chose the chair facing the window. Perhaps he wanted light behind him. Perhaps he wanted a view of the courthouse roof so he could remember exits existed. Iris let him choose. Then she placed the missing-page copies on the table where he could not avoid them without looking afraid.

Calder broke in the narrow way of men protecting what remained. There was no collapse, no confession with hands over face, no sudden moral sunrise. In a conference room at Iris's office, after the injunction and before sanctions could harden, he admitted he had heard rumors of private signals since childhood.

"Rumors," he said. "Dock talk. Family embarrassment. Nothing reliable."

Iris sat across from him with a yellow pad. "Why omit the dates from your archive?"

"To avoid unfair speculation."

"By removing fair evidence?"

He did not answer.

Ada watched him from the end of the table. Calder had not run the smuggling route. He had not filed the gear, mixed the graphite, or cut the signal pages. He had inherited money, myth, and a choice. He chose the myth because it kept the money clean and the family name mounted in brass.

She found that ordinary and therefore disappointing. People wanted corruption to announce itself. More often it arrived as maintenance: delete a date, soften a label, fund a gala, call questions divisive.

Calder agreed to produce remaining records, withdraw from museum governance pending review, and place disputed foundation funds in escrow. It was not repentance. It was containment.

Afterward, Jonas stood on the sidewalk in a cold wind. "I wanted him more dramatic."

"Most damage is administrative," Ada said.

"That is a terrible sentence."

"Unfortunately useful."

Calder's admission came with conditions, qualifications, and a lawyer's foot pressed firmly on every verb. He had not known. He had heard. He had wondered. He had inherited archives in disorder. He had relied on advisers. He had removed dates to prevent misinterpretation, not discovery. Every sentence tried to enter the room by a side door.

Iris let him talk until the talk weakened itself.

"Who told you the private signal rumors?" she asked.

"My father."

"When?"

"I was young."

"What did he say?"

Calder looked toward his lawyer. The lawyer looked at the table. "That fog made some fortunes possible," Calder said.

The sentence landed heavily because it sounded rehearsed by no one. Ada wrote it down.

"Did you search for documents after hearing that?" Iris asked.

"I found references."

"And removed dates."

"I removed inflammatory context."

"Dates are inflammatory?"

He did not answer.

That was as close as they came to confession. It was enough for practical movement and insufficient for moral satisfaction. Ada had learned not to expect the second from people bargaining to preserve the first.

Outside, Jonas kept pacing because stillness would have made him angrier. Olan joined them after signing a governance suspension notice. He looked older than he had at the first gallery test, but not weaker.

"He kept saying legacy," Olan said.

"People use legacy when they dislike inventory," Ada replied.

Jonas stopped pacing. "That should be on the archive door."

"No," Olan said. Then, after a moment: "Maybe inside the staff room."

The wind pushed grit along the sidewalk. Ada watched Calder's car pull away from the curb. No collapse. No moral sunrise. Just a man leaving with fewer boxes than he had brought.

Chapter 40: Restitution List

Restitution began on a whiteboard because paper had become too slow. Iris drew columns while Nadia objected to every word that sounded bloodless. Bram sat in the back and announced that if anyone said closure he would leave. No one said closure.

"We are not pricing grief," Iris said.

"Good," Nadia answered. "You could not afford it."

Ada stood by the window and watched rain move across the street in low sheets. She had no official role in compensation, but the mechanism she had read had made the list possible. That gave her a responsibility to witness the next, duller machinery.

Olan came in late and brought the museum's finance officer, who looked as if she expected to be blamed for arithmetic. Iris gave her a marker instead. "We need restricted funds, pledged funds, and money already spent on the restoration."

"That will be ugly."

"Good. Put it where we can see it."

Iris filed the restitution list with cargo owners' successors, family claimants, successor firms, and historical values adjusted conservatively. She refused inflated numbers. "Outrage is not accounting," she told Nadia, who disliked the sentence and then accepted it.

No one would be made whole. Wholeness was not available after a century. People had died with less money than they should have had, businesses had failed, opportunities had bent around a theft no court had named. But acknowledgement had numbers now.

The list included Maribel tea, Eastwake copper, Orison glass, and two lesser cargoes tied to later signals. Calder's family account had profited from each. Judge Arda ordered mediation over assets connected to the museum gift and Pike foundation funds.

Nadia signed as claimant witness for the dock notebook. Bram signed for the fog logs. A representative from the Eastwake successor cooperative signed with a fountain pen that leaked on his thumb. He stared at the ink and laughed once, not happily.

Ada signed nothing. Her work was the instrument. Still, when Iris handed her a copy, she kept it.

"For your files," Iris said.

"I have too many files."

"Then for your conscience."

Ada put the list in her bag. Signals should end somewhere visible.

The list did not satisfy anyone, which was one sign it had been made honestly. Claimants wanted more certainty. Calder's counsel wanted fewer names. The museum wanted smaller exposure. Iris wanted cleaner statutes. Nadia wanted Ressa alive long enough to see it.

"We have proof strength categories," Iris told the claimants at a community meeting in the union hall. "Strong, moderate, historical acknowledgement only. I will not promise money where the law will not carry it."

A man in the second row stood. "Then what is the point of acknowledgement?"

Nadia answered before Iris. "It means they stop calling your grandmother confused."

The man sat down.

Ada stood near the back beside Bram. The hall smelled of coffee, wet coats, and old floor polish. People held copies of the list with both hands, as if the paper might withdraw itself. Some found family names. Some found company names long gone. Some found nothing and looked angry at the absence.

"Gaps hurt too," Bram said.

"Yes."

"Can you put gaps in an exhibit?"

"You should."

Iris explained escrow, mediation, successor claims, and the limits of restitution after a century. She did not soften the difficulty. Ada respected that. False hope was another kind of bad signal.

After the meeting, an older man brought forward a receipt for lamp oil, folded in a tobacco tin. His family had never known whether it mattered. Iris took a copy. Jonas took his name. The list acquired one more uncertain line.

On the walk back, Ada looked at the harbor lights and thought about signals ending visible. Visibility was not completion. It was only the condition under which repair could begin.

Chapter 41: New Label

The development office sent one representative to the first label meeting and lost the room in eight minutes. She suggested the phrase unresolved maritime controversy. Jonas closed his eyes. Mara stared at the ceiling. Iris asked whether the controversy had filed teeth into a gear.

Olan dismissed the representative gently. "We will call you when we need layout."

After she left, the air improved.

Ada took the old label from its folder and placed it at the center of the table. Admiral Elias Pike, Voice of Lorn Harbor. The sentence looked smaller now, not because it had changed, but because evidence had grown around it.

"Keep it," Ada said when Olan reached to remove it.

"Why?"

"Because this is what we are correcting. Do not forget the shape of the error."

He left it on the table. By afternoon, the old label had become a warning object. Every new sentence had to answer it without imitating its certainty.

Ada and Jonas wrote the corrected label in the archive room because the development office had been politely disinvited. Olan tried to help for twenty minutes, inserted the phrase complex legacy twice, and was sent for coffee.

The first sentence took the longest.

This semaphore was altered during Admiral Pike's command to transmit private signals during fog.

Jonas read it aloud. "Too blunt?"

"No."

"Visitors may stop there."

"Then they will at least stop with the truth."

The label continued through gear modification, missing signal pages, cargo claims, hidden counterweight, and the later preservation case. Calder's name appeared only in the provenance section, tied to the failed donor narrative and the suspension of naming rights.

Museums loved passive voice. Ada removed every instance she found. Cargo was not lost; records declared it lost. Pages did not go missing; someone cut and hid them. Signals were not misunderstood; private operators used them.

Olan returned with coffee and read the draft. "It will upset people."

"Good labels often do," Jonas said.

Ada looked at him with approval. Archivists could learn.

Olan sat with the page for a while. "Leave in the cargo names."

"We intended to."

"I know. I am saying it out loud so I cannot retreat later."

That, Ada thought, was a useful form of policy.

The second sentence took almost as long as the first. Jonas wanted to explain private-yard exceptions immediately. Ada wanted the cargo names higher. Olan wanted visitors not to turn away before understanding the machine. Iris, reading from the end of the table, said everyone was trying to make one label perform the work of a book.

"Then use panels," she said.

"Visitors do not read panels," Olan answered.

"Some do. Write for them. The rest will read captions and remember the movement."

Mara came in halfway through, inspected the draft, and circled altered during Admiral Pike's command. "Say how."

"The next line says filed gear teeth and hidden counterweight," Jonas said.

"Make people see the hand work."

Ada agreed. Abstractions let blame evaporate. Filed teeth, shielded lamp, cut pages, routed funds. Verbs with tools in them.

They built the label cluster in layers: what visitors saw first, what they could read if they stayed, what documents supported each claim, what remained uncertain. Ada insisted on a section called Limits of the Evidence. Olan resisted because it sounded defensive. Iris supported it because it sounded honest.

"We cannot say every person who handled the machine knew the theft," Ada said. "We cannot say every fog signal moved stolen cargo. We can say enough."

"Enough is less satisfying than all," Jonas said.

"All is usually a warning sign."

By evening, the draft had lost every decorative phrase. No civic pride. No complex legacy. No voices of the harbor. The title became The Brass Semaphore: Public Signal, Private Use. It was not elegant. It was accurate enough to hold weight.

Olan taped the draft to the gallery wall. From a distance it looked severe. Up close, it finally let the object be complicated without lying on its behalf.

Chapter 42: Semaphore Restored

Mara began the final restoration meeting by placing two cards on the table. One read function. The other read evidence. She said if anyone proposed a treatment that harmed either card, they should expect a long afternoon.

"Can we add beauty?" Olan asked.

"If it behaves."

The answer pleased Ada more than it should have. Beauty was not the enemy. Beauty without accountability was. The semaphore deserved stable mounts, clean light, and enough polish to reveal form. It did not deserve to be made innocent for photographs.

Talia added a third card in smaller handwriting: future readers. Mara looked at it, then nodded. The room accepted the amendment without vote. That was how the best policy sometimes entered, not with ceremony but because a necessary phrase had finally been placed where everyone could see it.

Mara restored the semaphore to stable operation without returning it to false innocence. She refused requests to replace the modified gear with a replica of the original design. The altered gear stayed. The hidden counterweight stayed visible through a small inspection panel. The scratches stayed lit by raking light.

"Restoration as confession," Olan said.

"Restoration as evidence," Mara corrected.

They tested the final mount after hours. Talia sealed the bearing with modern documentation. Jonas catalogued the recovered pages and added a cross-reference so no future researcher would have to depend on luck and warped panels. Iris appended the injunction and restitution status to the case file.

Ada operated the arms once under supervision. Official rescue. Private 7-4-2. Halt.

The brass moved smoothly. It no longer whispered. Every movement would be explained by a guide, a label, a diagram, and the object itself. Visitors would see not only what the machine did but how people had taught it to lie.

"Does it feel different?" Mara asked.

"No," Ada said. "That is the point."

Objects did not feel shame. The shame belonged around them, in records, choices, money, and silence. The object had not become clean. It had become legible.

That was better. Clean things often lied by omission.

The restoration file became thicker than the old object file by noon. Mara documented every screw, every preserved mark, every non-reversible support. Talia added material reports. Jonas added provenance notes and cross-references to the recovered pages. Ada added timing diagrams stripped of drama and therefore more damning.

Lee built the inspection panel mount under Mara's supervision. His first version reflected too much light and made the hidden counterweight hard to see. Mara told him to fix the angle.

"Visitors will bend down anyway," he said.

"Do not make truth depend on flexible knees."

He fixed it.

They tested the demonstration script with museum guides. One guide moved the handle too quickly on the official signal, making the whole room wince. Ada corrected her and then softened her voice.

"The official signal is public. Give it time to be seen."

The guide repeated it properly. Halt. Warning. Cross. Return. Then the private timing, brief and unsettling. Several guides reacted the way the public hearing had: they understood secrecy before they understood mechanics.

"Do we call it smuggling?" one asked.

Iris answered. "In tours, say private cargo transfers tied to unpaid duty and misdirected claims. If asked whether that is smuggling, say the records support that conclusion, and point to the ledger."

"That is a mouthful."

"Law often is."

Ada watched them practice until the movement stopped looking like performance and began to look like instruction. The restored semaphore would not be allowed to charm without explanation. That mattered. Beautiful machines could make people forgive the hands that misused them.

At the end, Mara placed the old modified gear drawing beside the restored assembly. "It keeps its scar," she said.

"Good," Ada answered. "So does the harbor."

Chapter 43: Harbor Walk

Bram warned everyone before they left that the harbor would not behave for comfort. Shoes would get wet. Fog might hide the tower. Trucks would interrupt the best sentences. He said this with such satisfaction that two trustees looked toward the door as if reconsidering history.

Ada stood at the back of the group while Olan handed out route cards. The cards showed the tower, customs pier, east lane, Pike yard, and memorial stone. Jonas had added document references in small print. Bram said only archivists would read them. Jonas said that was enough to justify civilization.

Bram led the first public harbor walk in foul weather because, he said, fog history without damp was tourism. Ada approved. Twenty-six people came: harbor families, students, two trustees, a reporter, and one man who announced that his grandfather had admired Pike and then listened harder than Ada expected.

The route cards wilted in the mist almost immediately. Jonas looked offended on behalf of paper. Bram said useful paper learned humility outdoors. Ada folded hers into a pocket and walked without it; the harbor itself had become legible enough now.

They stopped at the tower, customs pier, east lane, and Pike yard gate. Iris explained cargo claims with a folder clipped against the wind. Jonas explained signal books and missing pages. Bram explained how boats moved when horns paused, using a coil of rope on the ground to show current and drift.

At the customs pier, someone asked whether Admiral Pike did any good.

Ada answered, "Likely. People can do good and still steal."

The group went quiet.

That was the sentence many histories resisted. Heroism was not disinfectant. It did not sterilize ledgers, gears, or stolen cargo. A rescue could happen in the same career as a fraud. A useful harbor officer could also make a private channel for profit. Moral arithmetic was not accounting, no matter how often families tried to balance books with medals.

At the east lane, fog thickened. Bram pointed upward. "There. You would see the arms."

The tower lamp had been lit for the walk. Through the gray, the signal frame showed faintly but clearly from that one place. People shifted, trying to see from other angles, and lost it.

Now everyone could understand audience. The harbor itself made the lesson.

The walk continued to the old Pike yard gate, where new brass numbers marked expensive offices and a restaurant had set small tables under an awning. Bram stopped the group at the threshold and pointed to the slip.

"A working boat could enter there without turning broadside," he said. "Cargo comes off fast if men are paid to keep quiet. Faster if they are afraid not to be."

A trustee looked uncomfortable. "Do we know men were threatened?"

"We know contracts vanished," Bram said. "In harbors, that speaks loudly."

Iris explained the difference between direct threat, economic pressure, and silence purchased by dependence. Ada watched the students in the group listen. They understood pressure better than some trustees did. Everyone who had ever needed a paycheck understood the shape of obedience.

At the memorial stone, Olan read the old inscription naming Pike among civic benefactors. Then he read the ledger line tying stolen cargo proceeds to the memorial fund. The group stood in rain-heavy air while traffic hissed behind them.

"Will the stone come down?" someone asked.

"No," Olan said. "But it will not stand alone anymore."

That was a better answer than Ada expected. Removal could become another simplification. Context, when done honestly, was not softness. It was fastening the easy story to the hard one so neither could walk away alone.

At the end of the walk, the man whose grandfather had admired Pike approached Ada. "He did save some ships, didn't he?"

"Likely."

"And stole."

"The evidence says his system did."

The man nodded slowly. "Harder to tell my grandson."

"Then he may remember it."

The fog thinned as the group dispersed. Bram looked pleased and cold. "Damp enough for history?"

"Almost too much."

Chapter 44: Olans Apology

Olan asked Ada whether an apology should be private first. She told him private apologies were for private injuries. The museum had printed invitations, drafted labels, courted donors, and nearly taught children a cleaned story. The apology belonged where staff could hear it and later decide whether policy followed.

"That sounds unpleasant," he said.

"So was the label."

He accepted that. To his credit, he did not ask her to help write the speech. He wrote it himself, crossed out four excuses, and brought the final page folded in his jacket pocket.

Before the meeting, he stood alone in the education room arranging chairs that did not need arranging. Ada watched from the doorway for a moment before entering.

"Nervous?" she asked.

"Appropriately."

"Keep that. It may improve the nouns."

Olan apologized at a staff meeting without saying mistakes were made. Ada respected that. Mistakes were made was a foghorn blown by no one.

He stood at the front of the education room with no slides, no donors, and no ceremonial tie. "I preferred the donor story because it was fundable," he said. "The museum failed by treating evidence as an obstacle. I failed by confusing support with authority."

Iris looked startled. Jonas looked satisfied. Mara looked as if apologies were less useful than maintenance schedules but still acceptable.

Olan committed to archive-first review for future gifts, written conflict rules for donor narratives, and conservation authority independent of development deadlines. Boring policy followed public shame, as it often did. Ada liked boring policy. It was where institutions proved whether remorse had legs.

Calder's funding gap remained. The museum would cut a gala and repair the archive roof instead of building a donor hall. This caused more internal grief than Ada thought sane, but the roof had been leaking onto nineteenth-century charts, and gala flowers had not yet saved a document.

"Less shine," Olan said afterward.

"More roof," Ada answered.

Museums, like ships, needed the unglamorous parts not to leak.

The staff questions after the apology were harder than the apology itself. A development assistant asked whether every donor archive would now be treated as suspect. Jonas said suspect was the wrong word; review was the right one. Mara said if review frightened donors, that was useful information. Iris said the policy should apply before money was accepted, not after a crisis.

Olan took each answer without hiding behind his title. "We will lose gifts," he said.

"Probably," Helene replied from the back. "We may also stop buying trouble at a discount."

Ada liked her more for that.

The archive roof repair became the meeting's unlikely argument. The gala committee had expected to postpone it again, as if water would respect sentiment. Jonas projected photographs of damp chart edges and a warped box of harbor permits. No one could make those images glamorous. That was their strength.

"These records outlived the people who neglected them," he said. "They may not outlive another winter."

The room voted for the roof. Not unanimously. One trustee wanted a naming opportunity for the repair campaign. Olan said no before Ada could speak.

Afterward, he found Ada by the loading dock. Rain fell in a clean sheet from the broken gutter, proving the meeting's point.

"I keep thinking about the first day," he said. "When you said no quiet adjustment."

"It was a good sentence."

"It was an expensive one."

"Truth usually bills late."

He looked at the water striking the concrete. "I should have heard the machine sooner."

"You hear it now."

That did not absolve him. Ada did not intend it to. But institutions, like people, needed a place to begin behaving differently after they had behaved badly.

Chapter 45: Patron Removed

The first list of places bearing Calder's name was longer than Olan expected. Gallery plan. Board roster. Donor archive. Invitation proof. Press packet. Website draft. Education sponsor slide. Storage crate labels. A museum, Ada thought, could spread a patron through itself like damp.

Jonas made the list into a removal checklist. "Documentation before deletion," he said.

"Even for his name?" Lee asked.

"Especially for his name. Erasure is not correction. We keep evidence of the error."

Ada approved. The museum would not pretend it had never bowed. It would show where it had bowed and when it stood up.

Lee found Calder's name printed on the back of a temporary wayfinding sign and held it up with disbelief. "Even here?"

"Especially there," Jonas said. "Temporary things become permanent by being ignored."

Ada thought of the temporary lamp shield, the temporary private-yard exceptions, the temporary omissions that had lasted generations. The word had earned suspicion.

Calder's name came off the gallery plan, the board list, the invitation archive, and the wall where the brass donor letters had left pale ghosts. It remained in the case file where it belonged.

He sold one harbor property to fund legal settlement reserves. The paper called it portfolio adjustment. Lorn called it tide turning. Ada did not care for poetic justice. She cared for documented consequence.

The Pike Foundation issued a careful statement about continuing civic commitment under revised governance. Iris marked it for tone and filed it under predictable.

Nadia visited the museum to see the new label proof. She stood before the mock-up with her hands in her jacket pockets and touched nothing.

"My aunt would still be angry," she said.

"Good."

"Good?"

"Anger kept the notebook."

Nadia nodded slowly. "Then I will keep being a little angry."

Ada considered that healthy. Archives often began with someone refusing to get over it. The world told people to move on when records were inconvenient; the useful ones moved paper instead.

Before leaving, Nadia signed the loan agreement for a facsimile of Ressa's key. Not the original. "She stayed with us this long," she said. "She can sleep at home."

Jonas accepted that without argument.

Calder's removal from the board required three votes, two abstentions, one procedural argument, and a closed session that left Olan looking as if he had been locked in a room with wet wool. The minutes used formal language. The hallway used better language. Suspended governance role. Terminated naming agreement. Records preserved under order. No one said disgrace in the minutes. Everyone heard it anyway.

The physical work was less abstract. Ren returned with a heat tool and a scraper to lift the remaining donor-plan vinyl from the gallery mock-up. Pale rectangles remained where Calder's name had been.

"You still want them showing a little?" he asked Ada.

"Not my gallery."

"That was not my question."

She looked at Olan. He looked at the wall for a long moment. "Let them show until repainting," he said. "Then photograph them for the file."

Ren nodded. "Learning."

Nadia came in carrying a copy of the loan agreement signed by two cousins who had argued with her for three days. "Family consensus," she said, "is a myth sold by people who never held a funeral."

Jonas took the papers. "But they agreed?"

"They agreed the facsimile can be displayed, the original cannot, and if anyone calls Ressa colorful I get to object."

"I will write precise."

"You had better."

Ada walked with Nadia to the exit. "You did well."

"I did paperwork."

"Often the harder part."

Nadia looked back at the gallery. "I thought I wanted his name gone. I do. But now I want hers present more."

"Better use of space," Ada said.

Chapter 46: Signal Lesson

Ada had not wanted to teach the lesson. She preferred reports, instruments, and rooms where silence could do part of the work. Iris told her that was precisely why she should teach it. Olan asked formally. Bram said if she refused, he would do it and include profanity. That settled the matter.

She arrived with no slides of her own. The object would be the slide. The documents would be the handouts. She placed the handle at halt and waited until the room stopped rustling.

"Before code," she said, "there is trust."

Several students wrote that down. Ada had not meant it as a quotable line, which made it safer. She continued before it could become decorative. Trust was not a feeling. It was a public agreement that a signal meant the same thing to the sender, receiver, and those affected by the action between them.

Ada taught the false code to a room of students, dockworkers, and museum guides two days before reopening. She did not begin with scandal. She began with purpose.

"Signals are agreements," she said. "A shape means one thing because a community has agreed to read it that way. Break the agreement and you do not merely confuse. You choose who gets clarity and who gets fog."

She demonstrated the official signal. Mara showed the gear motion on a camera feed. Talia passed around a replica of the hidden counterweight. Jonas showed facsimiles of the missing pages. Bram supplied insults when necessary, mostly aimed at men who mistook height for virtue.

Then Ada taught the private timing: 7-4-2, horn gap, east lane. She made the guides say back what the signal did and who could see it.

"Do not admire cleverness without asking who paid for it," she said.

The lesson ended with the arms at halt. No applause, at Ada's request. Applause made people think a hard thing had ended neatly.

"Signals are for clarity," she said. "When they conceal, the damage travels."

A guide asked if that would be on the tour.

"It had better be."

Olan, standing at the back, wrote it down. Ada trusted him more when he took notes.

The first training run failed in useful ways. A guide described the private signal as ingenious. Ada stopped her.

"Ingenious for whom?"

The guide flushed. "For the operators."

"For the people who profited. Not for the people who lost cargo, contracts, and claims. Do not let admiration outrun consequence."

They began again. This time the guide described the mechanism without praise. Filed gear teeth accelerated the left arm. A hidden counterweight sped the return. A shielded lamp narrowed the audience. Missing pages concealed the public record. The sentence was long, but it stood upright.

Bram taught the guides how to point across the harbor without romanticizing fog. "Do not say the fog hid everything," he said. "Say it hid some people from other people. That is different."

Mara showed them the inspection panel and made each guide find the counterweight with the mirror. "If you cannot see it, neither will visitors. Adjust the light, not the truth."

Jonas handed out facsimile packets: watch log, drill report, notebook key, ledger. "Do not wave these like props," he said. "They are documents. Let people read at least one line."

A young guide asked what to do if a visitor defended Pike.

Ada answered, "Ask which record they are relying on."

"And if they say family memory?"

"Ask whose family."

The room grew quiet in the useful way that meant people were thinking rather than waiting to speak.

At the end, Ada made them practice silence. After the private signal moved, they were to let the room hear the stop. No joke, no flourish, no apology for discomfort. The brass clicked into halt, and the guides stood still with it.

"Good," Ada said. "Let the machine finish its sentence."

Chapter 47: Fog Clear

The archive reopening drew fewer cameras than the hearing and more people who mattered to Jonas. Researchers carried pencils. Families carried envelopes. Staff carried anxiety. A security guard checked bags with more solemnity than usual, perhaps because everyone now understood paper could move money, memory, and reputation.

Nadia inspected the climate-control monitor before greeting anyone. "Numbers look good," she said.

"They are," Jonas answered.

"Numbers can lie."

"These are being watched."

She accepted that as a temporary treaty.

At the first table, a young researcher asked whether she could photograph the facsimiles. Jonas said yes, then handed her the citation format. "Use the creator names," he said. "Not just museum collection."

Ada heard Ressa Voss's name in the instruction and saw Nadia hear it too.

The harbor archives reopened with the signal books digitized and the originals available by appointment. Jonas looked both proud and worried, the correct archival expression. The missing pages were displayed as facsimiles. The originals rested in climate control, which Nadia inspected with the skepticism she gave all institutional promises.

Researchers came. Families came. One Pike cousin came and read quietly for an hour, then left without comment. Ada respected the silence. Not every reckoning had to perform itself.

She sat at a table and reviewed the final instrument report. The sections were plain: mechanical alteration, timing behavior, sight-line analysis, documentary correlation, limits of conclusion. She had included limits because truth without limits became another kind of vanity.

Outside, fog lifted in strips, revealing cranes, gulls, stained warehouse walls, and the ordinary mess of working water. History did not clear like weather. But records could be cleaned, ordered, and made available to people who had been told to rely on legend.

Jonas placed a new sign by the cabinet: Signal books are not neutral. Ask what they were used to move.

Ada did not object. It was almost concise.

"You could have used fewer words," she said.

"I am healing."

"Slowly."

Digitization did not make the archive clean. It made its disorder searchable. Jonas discovered three duplicate catalog numbers, two misdated photographs, and a box of harbor permits filed under educational puppets from a 1980s outreach program. He took the last insult personally.

"How does a permit become a puppet?" Ada asked.

"Through institutional sin."

"Precise."

Researchers began requesting files before the exhibit reopened. A graduate student asked for Pike Yard payrolls. A local teacher asked for school materials. A retired customs officer asked whether his grandfather's name appeared in the logs and then sat very still when it did, only as a witness, not a culprit.

Ada watched these encounters from a corner table while finishing her final report. Limits of conclusion remained the hardest section because everyone wanted certainty in the shape of a verdict. She wrote what the evidence showed, what it strongly supported, what remained unknown, and what could not responsibly be claimed. The last category mattered most. It kept the report from becoming the kind of story it had been written to correct.

Jonas read the sign again. "Signal books are not neutral. Ask what they were used to move."

"Still too many words," Ada said.

"I cut three."

"From where?"

"My soul."

She gave him a look.

"Fine. From a subordinate clause."

Outside, fog lifted and returned in thin drafts, as if the harbor could not decide whether to reveal itself. Ada no longer required full clearing. Full clearing was weather's promise, not history's. Better to have lamps placed honestly, records open, and people trained to ask where the blind spots had been made.

Chapter 48: Cargo Settlement

Iris did not celebrate by phone until she had the stamped order in hand. Ada admired that. Good news without paper was only weather. When the call came, Ada was in the tower checking the demonstration angles for the third time.

"Orison approved," Iris said.

Ada looked down at the harbor, where a workboat was backing from a pier in a wash of gray water. "How much?"

"Not enough."

"Real?"

"Yes."

"Then say that first when you tell them."

Iris was quiet for a second. "You could come."

"To the call?"

"To the museum. They will gather there."

Ada looked at the semaphore mount, the east lane beyond it, and the harbor that had carried loss longer than any docket. "I will come down."

The first restitution payment was approved for the Orison glass claim. It was modest and late by any humane measure, calculated through discounts, successor rights, and legal caution. It was also the first official admission that the cargo had not simply vanished.

The Orison representative arrived with a folder too new for the papers inside it. She had copied everything twice and marked the originals with slips in careful blue ink. Her great-grandfather's invoice sat on top, the amount small enough to seem almost absurd beside the century of effort required to acknowledge it.

"He wrote paid? in the margin," she said.

Iris looked. The question mark was faint but visible.

"We can include that in the file," she said.

"Please do. My family has been asking the same question for a long time."

Ada watched Jonas make the note. One punctuation mark had crossed generations. It deserved a place beside the stamped order.

Iris called Ada from the courthouse steps. "One down."

"Many partials to go."

"You always know how to celebrate."

"Practice."

Nadia brought a copy of the order to the museum and placed it beside her aunt's notebook in the case file. Bram brought harbor buns, which were edible enough if eaten warm and forgiven quickly. Jonas made coffee strong enough to hold them together.

Olan asked whether the settlement should be mentioned in the exhibit update. Iris said yes. Ada said briefly. Bram said put it where people cannot miss it. All three meant the same thing in different professions.

The settlement did not undo theft. It changed its public status. What had been fog became liability. What had been family grievance became docket entry. Ada had learned to accept imperfect signals when they pointed in the right direction.

The order's language was cautious: preliminary settlement, no admission beyond documented claim, funds held pending distribution. Iris read it aloud at the archive table and made a face at every qualification. Then she placed the certified copy beside the Orison file and smoothed it flat.

"It is ugly," she said. "It is also real."

Nadia studied the amount. "Less than they lost."

"Yes."

"More than they admitted before."

"Also yes."

Bram broke a harbor bun in half and inspected the inside. "This bread has the same legal philosophy."

"Dry but serviceable?" Ada asked.

"Late and dense."

For once, Iris laughed.

Olan drafted a small exhibit update noting the settlement. His first instinct was to put it near the exit. Bram objected. "Do not make restitution an afterthought."

They placed it beside the cargo list instead, a modest panel with the order date, the claim name, and an explanation of what the payment did and did not repair. Ada approved of the did not. Public institutions were tempted to close wounds narratively as soon as paperwork began. This one would not be allowed to pretend money had traveled backward through time.

The Orison successor representative came to see the panel before installation. She was a pharmacist with tired eyes and a precise handshake. "My great-grandfather kept the unpaid invoice in his desk," she said. "Not for court. For anger."

"Anger stores well," Nadia said.

The pharmacist looked at her, then nodded. "Apparently."

Ada watched the two women stand before the cargo list, neither touching it. Sometimes settlement meant no more than this: two descendants reading a line that power had failed to erase.

Chapter 49: Brass Quiet

The night before reopening, Ada asked for the tower key and received three warnings with it: alarm code, sticky latch, step light unreliable. She listened to all three because practical warnings were a form of affection in working buildings.

The tower stairs felt different now that the object below had been made legible. She climbed slowly, one hand on the rail. At the top, the harbor lay in evening gray, neither clear nor hidden. The east lane showed as a darker band near the breakwater. The customs pier lights came on one by one.

No signal moved. That was the point.

She stood there until the evening horn sounded from the modern buoy system. It was electronic now, precise and impersonal, but it still entered the body before the mind. Ada counted the interval after it faded. No private flash answered. No lamp narrowed itself for one waiting boat.

Ada closed the tower for the evening after checking the arms at halt. The city below had begun to light its windows. Fog waited beyond the breakwater, not yet entering, only gathering itself.

The key felt heavy in her pocket on the way down. Halfway along the stair, she stopped at the narrow window that faced the old customs pier. Modern lights burned there now, clean and indifferent. She thought of the men who had once stood below, hearing the horn and trusting the tower to mean what the public code said it meant.

Trust was rarely dramatic when it broke. It became a missing page, a late payment, a family insult repeated at supper until no one remembered the first injury. The work of repair was just as undramatic: label, log, testimony, gasket, roof, signature, light.

At the bottom, she wrote sticky latch on the key tag before returning it. Small warnings mattered. Someone else would climb those stairs after her.

Mara had left a note taped inside the service panel: do not overwork the left arm; old guilt still has old bearings.

Ada laughed despite herself.

She touched the handle once. Brass held the day's warmth and a century of hands. It had moved lies. Now it would move lessons. That was not redemption. Instruments did not need redemption. People did, and often settled for labeling.

From the platform she could see the east lane, the customs pier, the old Pike yard slip, Bram's tug light, the museum roof with its patched leaks. The harbor looked ordinary. That was how places survived scandal. They returned to work while paper tried to catch up.

She locked the panel and descended slowly. Her knees complained on the stairs. At the archive desk, Jonas was still working, one box open beside him and another waiting like a threat.

"Go home," she said.

"After one box."

"That is what you said two boxes ago."

"Time is complicated in archives."

Archivists and signal officers were both poor liars. Ada turned off the spare lamp anyway and left him one pool of light.

She did not leave the building at once. The gallery was dark except for the conservation lamps left on timers. The semaphore stood in its final position, no longer under cloth. Without visitors, labels, and voices, it became an object again: brass, paint, glass, screws, repaired wood. Ada preferred seeing it this way before the opening. Public meaning was necessary, but objects needed a last quiet hour before being asked to carry it.

She checked the inspection panel. The counterweight showed in the angled mirror, small and stubborn. She checked the hub scratches. The raking light caught them exactly as planned. She checked the handle stop, the gear cover, the title panel, the cargo names. Not because she distrusted Mara or Jonas, but because a signal officer checked.

In the lobby, Olan was sitting on a bench with his coat beside him.

"I thought you went home," Ada said.

"I tried. Got as far as the steps."

"Dangerous distance."

He looked toward the gallery doors. "Tomorrow people will decide what we have done."

"Some will."

"And the rest?"

"The rest will decide what they brought with them."

He considered that. "You make public work sound impossible."

"No. Just public."

They sat for a minute without speaking. The building clicked and settled. Somewhere upstairs, Jonas moved a box despite being told not to. Ada could tell by the rhythm of the cart.

"You will come tomorrow?" Olan asked.

"Yes."

"Operate the arms?"

"Once."

He nodded. "Once is enough."

Ada stood. "Often."

Outside, the fog had not entered the streets. It waited beyond the harbor mouth, patient as old weather. Ada walked home with the smell of brass still on her hands.

Chapter 50: The Brass Semaphore

Opening morning arrived without flowers. That was Olan's decision and Ada respected him for it. The gallery smelled of fresh paint, warm lights, and the faint oil Mara had permitted on the mechanism. Staff spoke quietly, not ceremonially. The first visitors waited outside the doors holding plain invitations.

Jonas adjusted one facsimile by less than a finger width. Mara listened to the gear without touching it. Iris checked the settlement update. Nadia stood with both hands in her pockets, looking at the case that carried her aunt's name.

"Ready?" Olan asked.

"No," Ada said.

He nodded. "Open it anyway?"

"Yes."

The exhibit opened without gala. Harbor families came first, by invitation written plainly on museum stationery with no embossed donor crest. The semaphore stood in the center of the gallery with its inspection panel open, its scratches lit, its false code explained by diagram, document, and moving demonstration.

The absence of gala changed the room. No flowers fought the smell of brass. No speeches delayed the first reading. People entered with wet coats and practical shoes, bringing the harbor in with them instead of leaving it politely outside. Ada saw a woman touch the cargo list with two fingers, then step back as if the names had answered.

Olan welcomed them with five sentences. He named the families, the archive, the court order, and the museum's failure. He did not thank Calder. He did not thank courage in the abstract. When he finished, no one applauded, and that restraint felt chosen rather than awkward.

Jonas opened the side case containing the facsimile pages. The motion was small, but several people leaned forward at once. Paper, after all the machinery and law, still had its own authority.

Calder did not attend. His absence improved the sight lines.

Ada stood near the back and watched people read. Some moved quickly, then returned when a detail caught them. Some stayed before the cargo names. A woman with a cane read them aloud: Maribel, Eastwake, Orison. Names mattered. They were signals too.

Olan stood beside the new label and answered questions without flinching. Jonas guarded the facsimile pages with the intensity of a chapel dog. Mara listened for gear noise. Bram complained about the coffee. Iris smiled only when no one looked. Nadia stood near the notebook case and told a young guide how to pronounce Ressa properly.

At noon, Ada operated the arms once. Official halt. Corrected halt. The brass moved cleanly and stopped where it should.

No private flash. No fog gap. No hidden east lane.

For a moment the room held still. Then people returned to reading, which was better than applause.

The instrument stood as witness now, not hero. That was honor enough. Ada looked through the gallery window toward the harbor, where fog had begun to thin over working water. Clear weather would not last. It never did. But the signal, at least, had been corrected.

After the demonstration, questions came slowly. A student asked why no one had found the hidden drawer sooner. Jonas answered without defensiveness. "Because archives reflect the care and cowardice of people. We are improving the care."

A dockworker asked whether Pike's portrait would stay. Olan pointed to the side wall, where the painting hung beside the ledger, cargo list, and label on manufactured memory. "It stays there," he said. "Not above the evidence. Beside it."

An older man asked if the harbor should stop using Pike's name on the memorial. Iris said that question belonged to the city, the claimants, and the public record now open. Ada was glad she did not pretend a label could settle every consequence.

Nadia stood by Ressa's notebook facsimile while two visitors bent to read the key. "She wrote small," one said.

"She had to fit a lot in," Nadia answered.

Bram took a group to the window and pointed toward the east lane. Mara corrected a guide's hand position at the semaphore. Talia watched visitors bend to see the counterweight and looked professionally satisfied. Lee replaced a smudged mirror before anyone asked. The museum had become, for one morning at least, a working system.

Ada remained near the back until the room no longer needed her. That was the best ending a signal could hope for: sent, received, no longer requiring the sender to stand waving.

On her way out, she passed the first label draft preserved in a small case for comparison. Admiral Elias Pike, Voice of Lorn Harbor. The old words looked embarrassed under glass. Ada did not hate them. They were evidence too, proof of the story the museum had almost told.

At the door, Olan caught up with her and handed over a copy of the final label, folded once. "For your files," he said.

"My files are dangerous."

"So are ours now, apparently."

That was the first joke he had made about the matter that did not sound like an apology wearing a hat. Ada accepted the page. Through the glass behind him, visitors continued to move around the semaphore in uneven circles, stopping, bending, reading, returning. No one knew how to behave perfectly around corrected history. That was fine. Perfect behavior would have suggested the lesson was too easy.

"What will you do next?" Olan asked.

"Read something that does not move."

"You will find fault with it."

"Likely."

He smiled, tired and real. "We will keep the roof repaired."

"Start there."

Outside, Nadia and Bram stood near the steps, arguing about whether the harbor walk route should include the bakery where Ressa's name had been written on the chalkboard. Iris listened with the expression of a lawyer deciding whether an argument had legal value. Jonas appeared at the door behind Ada and immediately complained that someone had placed fingerprints on the facsimile case.

The work, then, had not ended. It had merely become public enough for others to join.

Ada found that acceptable. Signals were never meant to live alone in the sender's hand. They crossed distance or they failed.

This one had crossed a century, late but legible, and that would have to be enough for the morning, and perhaps for the harbor itself.

Outside, the harbor sounded ordinary. Lines knocked against masts. Engines turned over. Someone shouted from a pier. Work continued, as it always had, but now one old machine had been made to tell the difference between clarity and concealment. Ada looked back once at the gallery windows and then walked toward the water.
