The Weavers of Oshun

A novel by Barış Kısır

---

ARC ONE: THE LOOM AND THE THREAD

---

Chapter 1: The Floating Market

Lagos floats.

Not the whole city — only the Marqet, the great market district that broke free from the mainland three generations ago and now drifts on the Lagos Lagoon like a woven island tethered by ropes thick as pythons. Wooden platforms rise and fall with the water. Canals run between stalls. The smell of smoked fish, ripe plantains, and indigo dye hangs in the salt air. Solar lanterns bob on the canals at dusk. Children leap from platform to platform, their laughter a physical pulse against the wood.

Damilola Akin sat at her mother's loom in the shadow of a baobab tree that had somehow taken root on a floating platform. The tree's roots plunged through the platform into the water below, anchoring it to the lagoon bed a hundred feet down. No one knew how the seed had arrived or why the tree had chosen this patch of floating wood to call home. The elders said it was a gift from Oshun. Damilola believed them.

Her fingers moved across the warp threads, finding patterns in the vibration. She could not hear the market — the cries of traders, the slap of water against hulls, the hum of the solar generators — but she felt it. Every footstep on the planks sent a ripple through the wood. Every pulse of the city's ancient engines thrummed up through the platform and into her bones. The Marqet spoke to her in a language older than sound: the language of contact.

She was seventeen. Deaf since birth. And she could see sounds in the loom.

It started three months ago. She had been weaving an aso oke fabric for a wedding — crimson and gold, the colors of joy — when she noticed that the threads held color differently when she was happy. The indigo turned deeper. The crimson seemed to pulse, like blood through a vein. She tested it: weaving while sad made the threads dim. Weaving while angry made them twist. Weaving while afraid made them fray. Mama Bisi, who ran the next stall over and had raised Damilola since her parents disappeared in the Great Sink, said it was the ancestors speaking through her fingers.

"Your mother wove the same way," Mama Bisi had said, pressing a palm to Damilola's cheek. "She could make cloth that remembered things."

Damilola had not understood then. She understood now.

The warp threads hummed under her fingertips. She pressed down on the treadle, passed the shuttle through the shed, and felt the weft thread settle against the others. A pattern emerged — zigzag lines like lightning, like the veins on a leaf, like the map of the lagoon's currents.

Something shifted.

The air thickened. The shadows around her loom deepened. And for a single, impossible moment, Damilola saw the fish seller drop a fish three seconds before it happened.

She blinked. The fish fell. The boy who caught it laughed.

Damilola looked down at her hands. The threads were warm.

She did not tell Mama Bisi what she had seen. Not yet. But she knew, with the certainty of a knot pulled tight, that something had changed in the weave of the world.

And she had done it.

Chapter 2: Threads of Silence

Silence was not empty. It was full.

Damilola had learned this as a child, pressed against the floorboards of the floating house, feeling the language of the world through her skin. The creak of wood was the market talking to itself. The vibration of footsteps was a conversation. The deep, slow pulse of the lagoon's tide was the heartbeat of the city itself. Rain was a thousand small impacts, each one a word. Fire was a crackling pressure against the cheeks. Thunder was a shudder that started in the ground and moved up through the spine.

She had her own language — a sign speech adapted by the floating market's deaf community that had grown over three generations, mixed with gestures that traders from a hundred tribes could understand. She spoke with her hands, her face, her shoulders. She listened through her eyes and her fingertips. When someone spoke to her, she watched the shape of their lips, the tension in their throat, the way their weight shifted as they formed words. Speech was a dance, and Damilola knew all the steps. She could read the mood of a room by the tension in the floorboards. She could tell if someone was lying by the way their weight shifted.

The loom was her voice.

When she wove, the cloth spoke. Her mother had left her the loom before the Great Sink — a broad frame of iroko wood, carved with orisha figures along its beams. Oshun, goddess of the river, danced across the right pillar. Ogun, god of iron and war, guarded the left. The crossbeam bore the face of Eshu, the messenger, his eyes cracked but still watching.

Damilola ran her fingers over Oshun's face. The carving was smooth, worn by generations of hands. Her grandmother had woven on this loom. Her mother. Now her.

She had tried to find others who could do what she did. The old women at the dye pits shook their heads. The babalawo, the priest of Ifá, had read the palm nuts and muttered something about "threads that should not be pulled." He had refused to speak further.

So Damilola learned alone.

She experimented in the quiet hours before dawn, when the market was still and the lagoon lay flat as glass. She wove patterns and watched for shifts in the world around her. A weave of tight herringbone made time stutter — she would see the same seagull cross the same patch of sky twice. A weave of loose, open netting made the future bleed through — she would glimpse a customer approaching her stall a full minute before they arrived.

But the zigzag pattern — the lightning weave from the day before — that was different.

That let her reach back.

She tested it again on the third morning. She wove the zigzag, focusing on the moment before. The fish seller's stall. The falling fish. She saw it again, clearer this time — the exact arc of the fish's tail, the surprise on the seller's face.

And when she looked at her hands afterward, she noticed a thread.

A single strand of silver, thinner than spider silk, trailing from her left palm.

She tugged it. It did not hurt, exactly. But she felt something pull — a distant ache, like a memory of a pain she had not yet felt.

She tucked the thread back into her skin and did not weave for three days.

On the fourth day, she returned to the loom. The silver thread was still there, coiled under her skin like a secret.

Chapter 3: The First Weave

The accident happened on a Tuesday.

The morning had started like any other. Damilola woke before dawn, splashed lagoon water on her face, and walked to the loom while the market was still asleep. She wove for an hour in the gray light, the threads cool and cooperative under her fingers. The fish seller arrived at six, slapping his catch onto the ice. The bread woman lit her oven at seven. Children ran to the floating school at eight.

At nine thirty-seven, the world broke.

A cargo barge had broken its mooring in the night. It was a heavy vessel, laden with palm oil drums and bags of cassava, and its mooring ropes had been old — frayed at the edges, weakened by months in the salt air. The Marqet's watchmen, drunk on palm wine at a wedding celebration on the north end, had not noticed until the barge drifted into the weaving district and crashed into the support beams. The impact collapsed a section of platform. The sound was not a sound to Damilola — it was a lurch, a deep rending groan that traveled through the iroko frame and into her chest, a physical shock that knocked the shuttle from her hand. Three stalls fell into the lagoon, their awnings sinking into the brown water. Two people fell with them.

Damilola was at her loom when the platform shook. The impact traveled through the floor — a deep, rending groan that traveled up through the iroko frame and into her chest. The warp threads trembled. A bobbin fell from its shelf and rolled across the floor. She ran outside and saw the chaos. The broken platforms splintered at angles, their edges jagged as broken teeth. The people in the water thrashed, grabbing at floating debris. The barge, still drifting, grinding against the remaining structure, its hull scraping against the support beams.

A child was trapped under a fallen awning, her leg pinned. The water was rising, slow but steady, creeping up the child's back. She would drown in minutes.

Damilola did not think. She ran back to her loom.

She sat down. Her hands found the threads by instinct — indigo for the water, crimson for the blood that might be spilled, and the golden thread she had spun from moonflower fiber, which glowed with a light of its own. She threaded the shuttle with a speed that surprised her. Her fingers had never moved this fast.

She began to weave.

The lightning pattern.

But this time she did not just see. She reached.

Her hands moved faster than they ever had. The shuttle flew. The heddles rose and fell like a heartbeat. The cloth grew beneath her fingers, and with each pass of the weft, the world around her began to shift. The air thickened. Time folded.

The fallen awning lifted. The child's leg slid free. The water receded as if it had never risen.

Damilola wove backward.

She saw it happen — the barge drifting away from the platform, the momentum reversing, the wood splintering back together. It was like watching a film run in reverse. The crash undid itself. The morning rewove.

Three minutes. She rewound three minutes.

When she stopped, her hands were shaking. The cloth on the loom glowed faintly, then dimmed. She looked down at her palms.

The silver thread had grown. It now ran from her wrist to the tip of her left middle finger, thin but visible, pulsing with a light of its own.

She wrapped her hands in cloth and walked outside.

The platform was intact. The barge was still drifting, but far away now, still a problem, but not an emergency. The child was standing with her mother, confused but unharmed. The fish seller was arranging his catch as if nothing had happened. The morning continued as if three minutes had never been unmade.

No one noticed what Damilola had done. They thought they had imagined the danger. They thought the barge had never come that close. The mother hugged her child and scolded her for running too close to the edge. The child nodded, not understanding that she had been saved from drowning by a loom.

But Damilola knew.

And so did the thread under her skin, pulsing with a light that only she could see.

Chapter 4: The Price of a Moment

She slept for fourteen hours.

When she woke, her body ached as if she had been beaten. Every joint throbbed. Her eyes were dry and gritty. Her muscles screamed when she tried to sit up. She had never felt anything like this — not after carrying dye buckets all day, not after walking to the mainland and back, not after any physical labor the market had demanded of her. This was deeper. This was cellular.

And the silver thread — she unwrapped her hands to find it had spread. It now branched across both palms like a river delta, faint but unmistakable. The pattern was delicate, precise, as if drawn by a fine brush. It extended up her wrists in thin tendrils.

She held her hands up to the morning light. The threads caught the sun, scattering tiny rainbows across the ceiling of her room. They were beautiful. They were terrifying.

"Pretty," she signed to herself, "but wrong."

She went to find Mama Bisi.

Mama Bisi was not her mother by blood, but she was mother in every way that mattered. She was a large woman with arms that had held a hundred children and a voice that could carry across the entire market. She sold indigo cloth — deep blue fabrics dyed in pits that had been in her family for centuries. She knew the language of the loom better than anyone Damilola had ever met.

Damilola found her at the dye pits, stirring a vat with a long wooden paddle. The air smelled of fermentation — the indigo leaves, crushed and aged, releasing their color into the water.

"I did something," Damilola signed.

Mama Bisi set down the paddle. She watched Damilola's hands carefully, the way she always did.

"I wove time," Damilola continued. "I undid a crash. Three minutes. I put it back."

Mama Bisi's face did not change. She took Damilola's hands in her own, turned them over, examined the silver threads.

"I wondered when you would tell me," Mama Bisi said.

"What?"

"Come."

Mama Bisi led her to the back of the dye shed, where a curtain of blue cloth hung against the wall. She pushed it aside to reveal a room Damilola had never seen.

A loom stood in the center. Old. Ancient. The wood was dark with age, carved with the same orisha figures as Damilola's own loom. But on this one, the figures were moving — not much, a subtle shift of posture, a blink of stone eyes.

On the loom, unfinished, was a tapestry of silver thread.

"Your mother wove on this loom," Mama Bisi said. "She could also see the threads of time. She called it the Sight of Oshun."

Damilola stared at the tapestry. The silver threads shimmered, and in their patterns she saw glimpses — a face she recognized as her own, a market that stretched beyond the horizon, a darkness that moved like ink through water.

"Every time you weave time," Mama Bisi said, "the thread of your life becomes part of the tapestry. The more you weave, the shorter your thread becomes."

Damilola looked at her hands. The silver threads pulsed under her skin. She had only woven three minutes, and already the cost was visible.

"The first weave costs almost nothing, a few days at most," Mama Bisi continued. "The second costs more. Each one takes more than the last, doubling if you weave the same pattern twice. Eventually, the tapestry is complete, and the weaver's thread runs out. Your mother's tapestry is still unfinished — that is why she is still alive, somewhere in the threads. She gave everything she had, but not quite everything."

"How long?" Damilola asked.

Mama Bisi shook her head. "That depends on what you weave. A small change costs a day. A large one costs a year. Your mother —" She stopped. "Your mother saved the market from the Great Sink. She wove for seven straight days. She stopped the sinking."

"But she disappeared."

"Yes. The loom did not kill her. But the cost — the cost left her somewhere between the threads. She is still weaving, Damilola. Somewhere. In the space between moments."

Damilola looked at the moving orisha carvings. At the silver tapestry. At her own thread-lined hands.

She understood now.

Her gift was a trade. Time for time.

Chapter 5: Mama Bisi's Loom

The hidden room became Damilola's second home.

Mama Bisi taught her the history she had been too young to learn. The story of the Weavers of Oshun — a guild of women who had served the goddess of the river since before the colonization, before the oil wars, before the floating market had ever drifted free from the mainland. They were the keepers of time. Every generation produced one weaver who could See. The others wove ordinary cloth, beautiful cloth, but without the silver thread. The Sight passed through bloodlines, skipping generations, appearing when the world needed it most.

Damilola's mother had been the last. Now Damilola was the next.

"You did not find the gift by accident," Mama Bisi said. "The gift found you. It always does."

The lessons were slow, meticulous. Mama Bisi taught her to read the silver threads like a map. Each branch indicated a weave completed. Each node showed a moment of decision. The longer the thread, the more time Damilola had spent altering reality. "Your mother's thread stretched from here to the edge of the market," Mama Bisi said. "She wove often. She wove deep."

The ancient loom had a name: Aago, which meant "time" in Yoruba. Its treadles were worn smooth by centuries of feet. Its warp beam was scarred where a hundred weavers had leaned against it, pulling threads tight, shaping the fabric of reality. The orisha figures on its frame were more animated than those on Damilola's loom — Oshun's hand moved in a slow, endless wave. Ogun's chest rose and fell with breath. Eshu's eyes followed whoever entered the room.

Damilola touched the beam. The wood was warm, alive.

"Can I weave on it?" she asked.

"Not yet. First, you must understand what you already have. You wove three minutes back. That cost you — let me see."

Mama Bisi took Damilola's hands again. She pressed her thumbs into the center of each palm, feeling the silver threads beneath the skin. Her eyes closed.

"Seven days," she said. "The first weave cost you seven days of life."

Damilola felt the number hit her like cold water. Seven days. For three minutes.

"It doubles each time if you weave the same pattern," Mama Bisi said. "Different patterns have different costs. The lightning weave — the one you used — is the most expensive. It reaches farthest into the past."

"Is there a weave that does not cost?"

Mama Bisi was quiet for a long moment.

"There is one. The Weave of Oshun. It is said that if you weave the goddess's own pattern — the dance of the river from source to sea — you can change time without cost. But the pattern is long lost. The last person who knew it died in the Middle Passage."

"The Weave of Oshun," Damilola repeated, feeling the words form in her mouth.

"Your mother searched for it. She wove herself into the threads looking for it. That is why she is gone."

Damilola looked at the silver tapestry. At the glimpses of her own face within it.

"Then I will find it," she said.

Mama Bisi smiled, but her eyes were sad.

"That is what your mother said too."

Chapter 6: Kofi's Indigo

Kofi the Dyer found her at the edge of the market, staring at the water. Her reflection rippled on the surface — a girl with silver threads glowing under her skin, her face young but her eyes already old. She had been sitting there for an hour, watching the hyacinth drift past, thinking about the price of the thread under her palms.

He was a tall man, thin as a shuttle, with hands stained blue from a lifetime of working indigo — the color had seeped into his skin so deep that it would never wash out. He was Mama Bisi's nephew, though he had spent most of his years traveling the coast, learning from dyers in Ghana and Senegal and as far east as Madagascar, where they made patterns that looked like woven dreams. He had returned to the Marqet only three months ago. His clothes carried the sharp, earthy smell of indigo ferment, the scent of leaves crushed and aged and transformed. His fingers, even clean, left faint blue smudges on everything they touched.

"You are the weaver," he said. Not a question.

Damilola turned. She watched his lips carefully. He knew sign — she had seen him using it with a deaf trader at the fish market. But he spoke aloud now, perhaps forgetting, perhaps testing. She did not respond.

"I am Kofi," he said, switching to sign. His hands were graceful, precise, each gesture flowing into the next like water finding its level. "My aunt told me about your gift."

Damilola still did not respond. She was not ready to speak of it to another person. The silver threads in her palms had begun to pulse — sensing something in this man, some resonance she did not yet understand.

"I am not here to pry," Kofi signed. "I am here to offer something."

He reached into the bag at his side and pulled out a bundle of cloth. Dark blue. Almost black. But when he unfolded it in the sunlight, the fabric seemed to glow from within. Tiny pinpricks of light, like stars, scattered across its surface, shifting and rearranging themselves as the cloth moved. Damilola had seen fine cloth before — Mama Bisi's indigo was prized across the lagoon — but this was something else entirely. This cloth was alive.

"This is indigo I dyed with a special process," Kofi said. "An old recipe. The dyers of the Ivory Coast call it adire ale — 'cloth that holds secrets.' When woven into a pattern, it amplifies the weaver's intent."

Damilola touched the cloth. The stars shifted under her fingers. They were warm, and they recognized her — she could feel it. The silver threads in her hands sang in response.

"What do you want for it?" she asked.

"Nothing. Everything. I want to see what you weave."

She looked at him. His eyes were honest, she decided. Or honest enough.

"Come to my stall tomorrow," she signed. "I will show you."

That night, she wove a small square of the star-cloth into the corner of a new tapestry. When she pulled the thread through, the silver lines under her skin pulsed in time with the stars. The cloth was alive. Or something near to it. She sat back and watched the stars move across the fabric, rearranging themselves into patterns she almost understood. The Orisha's Voice hummed approval. She was on the right path.

Chapter 7: The River's Memory

The lagoon remembered.

This was something Damilola had always sensed but never understood until she wove time. After her first weave, she could feel the water's memory pressing against her awareness like a hand against a window — the history of every wave, every current, every drop that had ever flowed through the floating market, all pressing to be known. It was not like her own memory, linear and clear. The lagoon's memory was simultaneous, all of its history present at once, like a cloth that showed every layer of its weave on the same surface.

The lagoon knew when Lagos was a fishing village of mud huts and dugout canoes. It remembered the first European ships arriving like floating islands. It remembered the slave forts built on the shore, the chains that clanked in the holds, the women who wove cloth in the dark to keep their spirits alive. It remembered the oil platforms that rose and fell. It remembered the day the market broke free and became the Marqet. All of it was there, suspended in the water, waiting, if you knew how to read it.

Weaving gave her the ability to read.

She sat at the edge of the platform, her feet dangling over the water, and let her fingers brush the surface. The silver threads in her palms tingled. Images flooded her mind — not her own memories, but the lagoon's.

She saw a woman weaving at dawn, a hundred years ago. The same loom. The same hands.

She saw the Middle Passage — a ship crossing an ocean, and below deck, a woman with a strip of indigo cloth, her fingers moving in the dark, weaving a pattern that kept her people alive through the crossing. The Weave of Oshun.

Damilola gasped and pulled her hand back.

The vision vanished. But the pattern remained, burned into her memory. Not complete — just a fragment. A corner of the river's dance.

She ran to her loom and began to weave.

The fragment was small — just six passes of the shuttle — but the feeling was unmistakable. The threads moved differently under her fingers. The air hummed. The silver threads in her hands did not grow.

She had found a piece of the Weave of Oshun.

She kept weaving until the fragment was complete on her cloth. When she stopped, the pattern glowed faintly, then settled into ordinary thread.

But Damilola knew what she had.

A map. A key. A way forward.

Chapter 8: A Crack in Time

The first crack appeared in the dye pits.

Mama Bisi woke to find the indigo had turned red. Every vat, every pit, every container of dye that had been blue for centuries had turned the color of dried blood. She called for Damilola at dawn, her voice carrying across the empty market. The red indigo moved in the vats as if alive, swirling without wind.

"The threads are shifting," Mama Bisi said. "You pulled too hard. The weave of time is like a fabric — if you tug one thread too strongly, the whole cloth distorts around it." She touched the red liquid with one finger. It came away clean, as if the color were only an illusion. "The dye remembers what you did. It is the memory of a wound."

"Is this because of what I did?" she asked.

"You reversed three minutes. But those three minutes contained a thousand events. A thousand threads. You changed the past, even slightly, and the present is adjusting."

"How do I fix it?"

"You don't. You can't. Time heals itself, but slowly. The more you weave, the more cracks will appear. The market will feel it. The lagoon will feel it. And eventually —" Mama Bisi stopped.

"Eventually what?"

"Eventually, something will notice. Something that lives in the cracks."

The warning came too late.

That night, Damilola woke to find her room cold. A figure stood at the foot of her bed — tall, featureless, made of darkness that seemed to drink the moonlight.

It had no face. But it had a voice.

"Little weaver," it said. The sound came from everywhere and nowhere. "You are pulling threads that do not belong to you."

Damilola could not move. Her hands were frozen at her sides.

"The orishas made rules," the figure said. "Time is a river. You are a fish. Fish do not redirect rivers."

She found her voice. "Who are you?"

"I am the consequence. I am what happens when the weave frays. I am the space between threads where the light does not reach."

It stepped closer. The cold intensified.

"Stop weaving, little weaver. Or I will weave an ending for you."

It vanished.

Damilola lay in the dark, her heart pounding, the silver threads in her hands glowing like embers.

Chapter 9: The Dye That Binds

Kofi found her the next morning, still shaken.

She told him everything — the figure, the warning, the crack in time, the indigo that had turned to blood. He listened without interrupting, his blue-stained hands folded in his lap.

When she finished, he stood up.

"Come with me," he signed.

He took her to the edge of the market, where the oldest platforms sagged into the water. Beneath one of them, half-submerged, was a canoe. He helped her into it and paddled out into the lagoon, away from the floating city.

They traveled for an hour. The Marqet shrank behind them until it was just a smudge on the horizon. The lagoon opened up, wide and brown and dotted with hyacinth.

Kofi stopped paddling.

"There is something I did not tell you," he signed. "About the adire ale. About the star-cloth."

He pulled out the bundle again. In the daylight, the stars were faint, but Damilola could still see them moving.

"The cloth holds secrets. But it also reveals them. When I dye it, I see visions. That is how I knew you were the weaver. The cloth showed me."

"What did it show you?"

Kofi hesitated. "It showed me the end of the Marqet. If you keep weaving without finding the Weave of Oshun, the cracks will grow. The market will sink. Everyone you know will drown."

Damilola felt the words hit her like a physical blow.

"That is why I came to you," Kofi said. "Not to help you. To warn you."

"Then why give me the cloth?"

"Because I also saw what happens if you find the weave. The market survives. You survive. The figure in the dark — it is defeated."

He held her gaze.

"I am not the one who decides your path. I am just one who saw both futures. The choice is yours."

Damilola looked at her hands. At the threads.

"Then I need to find the full pattern," she said.

"Yes."

"And I need to do it before the cracks destroy everything."

"Yes."

She picked up the paddle.

"Then we have no time to waste."

Chapter 10: The Orisha's Whisper

The dreams started that night.

Damilola slept and found herself standing on the bank of a river she had never seen. The water was silver, flowing uphill, defying every law she knew. The sky above was the color of deep water. The ground beneath her feet was not earth — it was woven cloth, stretching in every direction.

On the far bank stood a woman.

The woman was draped in yellow, the color of ripe mangoes, the color of sunset. Her skin was the deep brown of riverbanks after rain. She held a river in her hands — a tiny river, flowing from her palms and back into itself, a closed circuit of water that never spilled. The sound of it was not sound but presence, filling Damilola's chest like a held breath.

Oshun.

"You found a fragment of my weave," the goddess said. Her voice was music, was water, was the rustle of silk. "Well done, little one."

"I need the rest," Damilola said. "The cracks are spreading. A thing in the dark is coming."

"I know. I sent the cracks."

Damilola stared.

"Did you think time could be changed without consequence?" Oshun asked. "The cracks are my doing. They are tests. If you are strong enough to weave through them, you will find the full pattern. If you are not —" The goddess shrugged. "The river carries all things eventually."

"That is cruel."

"Nature is cruel. I am nature. I am the river that feeds and the river that floods. I am the birth and the drowning. You do not get to choose which part of me you worship."

Oshun stepped closer. The silver river flowed around her feet, never wetting them.

"I will give you a gift," she said. "One that my mother gave me, and her mother gave her."

She pressed her hand to Damilola's chest.

"Every time you weave, you will hear me. Not in your ears — you have no hearing there. But here."

She touched Damilola's heart.

"When you weave true, I will sing. When you weave wrong, I will be silent. This is the Orisha's Voice. Use it wisely."

Damilola woke with the goddess's hand still warm on her chest.

She went to the loom. She wove a simple pattern — a straight weave, nothing complicated. And she felt it. A hum in her chest. A vibration that was not a sound but was unmistakably music.

The Orisha's Voice.

She was not alone in the weave anymore.

ARC TWO: CRACKS IN THE FABRIC

---

Chapter 11: The River Speaks

The Orisha's Voice was not a sound. It was a knowing.

Damilola learned this over the following days. When she sat at the loom, the hum in her chest guided her hands. If she reached for the wrong thread, the hum faltered, dropping to a whisper of warning that made her fingers pause. If she chose the right one, it swelled, filling her ribcage with a warmth that spread to her fingertips until the threads themselves seemed to glow. She did not need to hear — she felt the goddess's approval like a warmth spreading through her ribs, a vibration that traveled from her chest to her hands to the loom. The Orisha's Voice became her second set of eyes, her silent teacher. She learned to trust it absolutely, the way a baby trusts its mother's heartbeat.

She tested it with the lightning weave. The moment her hands formed the pattern, the hum turned harsh, discordant, like a string snapped on a drum. Oshun did not want her reaching into the past. Not yet. Not without the full pattern.

So Damilola wove forward instead.

She wove cloth that showed the next day's weather — a scarf that rippled with rain before the clouds gathered, a blanket that turned gold when the sun would shine and gray when it would not. She wove a shawl that glowed with the exact shade of sunset that would come, as if the cloth remembered light that had not yet arrived. Customers came to her stall not for the fabric itself but for the prediction it carried — fishermen bought her weather-scarves to know when the lagoon would be safe.

Each forward weave cost almost nothing. A few hours of her life. A fraction of a day.

"Time is easier to go with than against," Mama Bisi observed, watching Damilola weave a tapestry that showed the coming harvest. "The river flows downstream for a reason."

But going forward could not fix the cracks.

The red indigo had not turned back to blue. More cracks appeared — a well that produced salt water instead of fresh, a tree that bore fruit in a single night and died the next morning. The market was fraying at the edges.

And the figure in the dark returned.

Chapter 12: The Shadow at the Edge

Damilola saw it first in reflections.

A dark shape in the corner of a dye vat, visible only when the indigo was still enough to show the surface. A silhouette in the polished brass of the market's old bell, gone when she turned to look directly. A shadow that moved against the light, wrong-way, defying the sun. She watched it slide across the surface of Mama Bisi's well, moving east while the sun cast west.

She told Mama Bisi. Mama Bisi told the elders. The elders consulted the babalawo, who threw the palm nuts and read the patterns they made.

"Eshu stands at the crossroads," the babalawo said. "The messenger brings both good and ill. A door has been opened that should have stayed closed."

"What door?" Damilola signed.

The babalawo, an old man named Papa Jide, looked at her with ancient eyes. His face softened.

"The door between then and now, child. When you wove backward, you made a hole in the wall of time. Something from the other side has stepped through."

"The figure said it was the consequence."

Papa Jide nodded slowly. "It is. In the old days, the Weavers of Oshun knew how to seal such doors. But those weavers are gone."

"Dead?"

"Some dead. Some... elsewhere. The weaver who taught your mother still lives, but she left the Marqet long ago. She dwells in the Badagry Mangrove, at the edge of the lagoon, where the water meets the spirit world."

Damilola leaned forward. "Can I find her?"

"Only if she wishes to be found. The mangrove is a maze of roots and mirrors. It shows you what you expect to see, not what is. But if you carry a thread from her own loom, she will feel you coming."

Mama Bisi produced a small spool of thread, aged and yellowed, from the hidden room. "This was your grandmother's," she said. "She was the one before your mother. She wove with the woman in the mangrove. Keep it close."

Damilola took the spool. The thread was warm.

"Then I go to Badagry," she said.

---

Chapter 13: The Mangrove Maze

The journey to Badagry took two days along winding lagoon channels.

Kofi paddled the canoe. Damilola sat in the bow, the spool of thread clutched in her hands, watching the lagoon narrow into channels that were not on any map. The water turned from brown to green to black as the mangrove closed around them. The temperature dropped. The sounds of the market — Kofi had described them to her once, the hawkers and the boats and the radios — faded into a thick, swallowing silence.

The trees grew from the water, their roots arching like the ribs of drowned ships. The air was thick and still. The usual pulse of the market had faded entirely — no footsteps on wood, no engines, no voices. Just the drip of water from leaves and the soft rustle of creatures moving in the dark. Mosquitoes hummed. Birds called in languages Damilola could not feel — their vibrations passed through the air, through the water, through the wood of the canoe, but she could not translate them into meaning.

She tried to read the water's memory, but the mangrove was confused. It remembered too many things at once — the slave ports, the colonial forts, the rituals of women who came here to pray, the bones of those who never left. The water was a palimpsest, layers of history written over each other, none of them fully erased. Damilola pulled her hand back from the surface. The weight of the dead pressed too close.

"Which way?" Kofi signed.

Damilola closed her eyes. She let the spool of thread rest in her palm. The silver threads in her hands reached out, sensing, tasting the air. They pulled her attention to the left, where the roots formed a tunnel so narrow the canoe would barely fit.

They paddled into the dark.

The tunnel twisted and turned. The roots above them formed a canopy that blocked the sun. Strange lights flickered in the water — bioluminescence from creatures that lived only in the mangrove's depths, glowing green and blue and occasionally the deep red of dying embers. Kofi paddled in silence. Damilola guided him with hand signals, her silver threads sensing the path ahead like antennae.

And then, without warning, they emerged into a clearing.

A hut stood on stilts in the center of the water. An old woman sat on the porch, weaving.

She looked up as they approached.

"I was wondering when you would come," the old woman said. Her voice was dry as leaves. "Your mother's thread has been calling to me for seventeen years."

She set down her shuttle and stood.

"I am Grandmother Adwoa. Last of the weavers of the old guild. And you —" She smiled. "You are the one who will finish what your mother started."

---

Chapter 14: Grandmother Adwoa

Grandmother Adwoa was older than the Marqet.

She had been born before the colonization, before the first European ships had charted the coast. She had seen the slave forts rise and fall. She had woven cloth for queens and commoners, for ceremonies and wars, for births and deaths. Her face was a map of wrinkles, each line a river of experience. Her fingers, gnarled with age, moved across her loom with a precision that belied her years.

She had also been the one who taught Damilola's mother.

"I sensed you the moment you wove your first thread of time," Adwoa said, offering them cups of bitter tea from a clay pot. The tea tasted of ginger and something earthy that Damilola could not name. "The fabric shuddered. I felt it all the way here, in my bones. Your first weave was like a bell ringing through the threads."

"Why did you leave the Marqet?" Damilola asked.

"Because I could not stop what I saw coming. The Great Sink. Your mother's disappearance. I knew the cost of what she planned, and I could not watch." Adwoa's hands stilled on her cup. "I am old, child. I have outlived every weaver I taught. I could not outlive another."

Adwoa's hut was filled with tapestries. Every surface was covered in cloth — hanging from the walls, draped over chairs, spread across the floor. Each one shimmered with silver threads, alive with the memory of the moments they had witnessed. A tapestry showing a queen's coronation. A cloth that held the memory of a child's first laugh. A shawl that had witnessed a wedding and a funeral, folded so both patterns could be seen. They whispered to Damilola as she passed, their threads vibrating with the stories they contained.

"The Weave of Oshun," Damilola said, the words feeling heavy in her mouth. "I found a fragment of it in the lagoon's memory. Just a corner — six passes of the shuttle. But it was enough to show me what I am missing. I need the full pattern to stop the cracks."

Adwoa nodded slowly. "The full pattern. Yes. I have been waiting for someone to ask for it."

She rose and walked to the far corner of the hut, where a loom stood covered in white cloth. She pulled the cloth away.

The loom was identical to Damilola's — iroko wood, orisha carvings — but the tapestry on it was complete. A river of silver thread, flowing from one end to the other, with patterns that shifted and changed as Damilola watched.

"The Weave of Oshun," Adwoa said. "The first complete version in three centuries."

Damilola's breath caught.

"But there is a problem," Adwoa continued. "I did not weave it. Your mother did, before she disappeared. The tapestry is finished, but it is not alive. It is a map without a traveler. To use it, you must weave it yourself."

"Show me," Damilola said.

Adwoa smiled.

"I was hoping you would say that."

---

Chapter 15: The Threads of Eshu

Learning the Weave of Oshun took four days.

Adwoa taught her in fragments — a crossing pattern here, a treading sequence there, a combination of colors that unlocked a new movement of the shuttle. Each fragment resonated in Damilola's chest, confirmed by the Orisha's Voice.

But on the fifth day, Adwoa stopped.

"There is one more component," she said. "The Threads of Eshu. The trickster's own signature. Without them, the weave is incomplete — you can travel time but not return."

"I have Eshu on my loom," Damilola said. "The carving on the crossbeam."

"Carving is not the same as living thread. To weave Eshu's signature, you need a piece of the messenger himself."

"How do I get that?"

Adwoa's face was grim. "You ask him. And he does not give gifts freely."

That night, Damilola performed her first ritual. Adwoa taught her the words, the movements, the offerings. She placed palm oil, kolanut, and a length of white cloth at the crossroads of three mangrove roots. She lit a candle. She called Eshu's name.

The candle flickered. The air grew heavy.

And a small boy appeared on the root above her, swinging his legs.

"You called me?" he said. His voice was old and young at once, full of tricks and wisdom.

"I need a thread of your spirit," Damilola said. "To complete the Weave of Oshun."

The boy — Eshu — tilted his head. "And what do I get in return?"

"What do you want?"

"A story. One I have not heard. Tell me a tale I do not know, and I will give you seven threads from my own cloak."

Damilola thought. She thought of the silent market, of the language of vibration and touch. She thought of how she could feel sound but never hear it.

"I will tell you the story of silence," she said.

And she did.

She told him of the world as she experienced it — the hum of the loom through her fingertips, the pulse of the lagoon through the soles of her feet, the way a laugh felt like a tremor in the chest. She told him of the language of hands, of the grammar of eyebrows and shoulders, of the conversations she could have without a single sound.

Eshu listened. For the first time in his eternal existence, he did not interrupt, did not joke, did not play tricks.

When she finished, he was quiet.

"I have heard every story," he said. "Every myth, every legend, every lie ever told. But I have never heard silence described."

He reached into his cloak and pulled out seven threads, each one the color of a crossroads at dusk.

"Take them. Weave well. And remember — the messenger always watches."

He vanished.

Damilola held the threads. They pulsed with their own light. She had the final piece.

---

Chapter 16: The Weave Begins

Back in the Marqet, the cracks were worsening.

A section of the floating market had drifted away overnight. Sixty stalls, two hundred people, gone into the mist. Damilola stood at the edge of the platform where the break had occurred, looking down at the frayed ropes and splintered wood. The mist had swallowed them completely — not the usual lagoon fog, but something thicker, whiter, something that did not burn off in the morning sun. The remaining platforms groaned under the strain. The lagoon had turned strange — fish with phosphorescent eyes that glowed in the depths, water that sometimes flowed uphill in defiance of gravity.

Papa Jide met Damilola at the edge of the market. His face was tired. He had the look of a man who had been arguing all night.

"The shadow figure appeared to the elders," he said. His voice was hoarse. He had not slept. The worry had carved lines into his face that were not there days ago. "It demanded you stop weaving. It said if you attempt the Weave of Oshun, it will tear the market apart. It said it will drown us all."

"It lies," Damilola signed.

"Perhaps. But the market is afraid. Some of the elders want to destroy your loom." He lowered his voice. "They came to my house last night. Adeyemi and four others. They asked me where you keep the threads of Eshu."

Damilola felt cold rage rise in her chest. "They cannot. The loom has been in my family for generations. It is not theirs to touch."

"They are afraid, child. Fear makes people do foolish things. It makes them forget who they are."

Damilola went to her loom. She sat before it. The seven threads of Eshu lay beside her, glowing with the light of crossroads and dusk. She took a breath and placed her hands on the warp. The wood of the loom was warm, alive. The orisha carvings watched her. Oshun's hand seemed to move, just slightly, encouraging her. Ogun's iron gaze was steady, a reminder that this was a battle as much as a craft. Eshu's eyes were closed, waiting.

She would not wait for permission. She would not ask for approval. The market was breaking. Her mother was still out there, somewhere in the weave. The threads of time were fraying, and she was the only one who could mend them.

She began to weave.

The first passes were the fragments she had learned from Adwoa. The river's memory. The crossing patterns. The dance of Oshun from source to sea.

The Orisha's Voice swelled in her chest, louder than ever before. It was not a hum now — it was a chorus. A thousand voices singing in harmony, the sound of every weaver who had ever sat at this loom, every woman who had pulled thread through cloth and changed the world.

She added Eshu's threads.

The world lurched.

---

Chapter 17: The Market Fights Back

The elders arrived at Damilola's stall as she wove. The sun had reached its zenith. The market held its breath.

They came with torches and cutlasses, their faces hard with fear. A crowd had gathered behind them — traders, fishermen, mothers with children at their hips. Some held stones. Others held ropes. Mama Bisi stood in front of the loom, arms crossed, refusing to move. She was sixty-three years old and had never backed down from anything in her life. A dye-stained woman of formidable size, she planted her feet and became a wall.

"You will not touch her," Mama Bisi said.

"The market is dying," the lead elder said. He was a man named Adeyemi, silver-haired and grim. "Every day she weaves, another piece breaks off. We cannot afford her experiments."

"They are not experiments. She is saving us."

"She is killing us! The platforms are breaking. The lagoon is turning strange. We have lost sixty stalls to the mist. How many more must we lose before you see what she is doing?"

Mama Bisi did not move. "I see exactly what she is doing. She is completing the Weave of Oshun. She is doing what your grandmothers' grandmothers could not do. And you come with torches to burn it all down."

"Stand aside."

"No."

Kofi stepped in beside her. He had no weapon, only his dye-stained hands. "And me," he said quietly. "You will have to move me too."

Adeyemi's face twisted. "You are not even from this market."

"I am from here now. And I stand with the weaver."

And then the silver threads in Damilola's hands flared.

A wave of light burst from the loom, washing over the crowd. The elders froze. The torches dimmed.

Oshun's voice spoke — not in Damilola's chest this time, but in the air itself, audible to everyone.

"Stand back. The weaver works at my command."

The elders dropped to their knees. The torches fell from their hands and sputtered on the woven platform. Some wept. Others pressed their foreheads to the wood. Adeyemi, the lead elder, looked up at Damilola with eyes that held both terror and awe. He had heard the goddess speak. He would never be the same.

Damilola wove on.

---

Chapter 18: In the Space Between

The world pulled apart.

Damilola felt herself leave her body. She was at the loom, and she was not. She was in the Marqet, and she was not. She drifted in a space between moments, where time flowed like liquid, where past and future were the same.

She saw the history of the loom.

She saw the first weaver, a woman named Olori, who had received the gift from Oshun herself a thousand years ago. She saw the loom passed from mother to daughter, through wars and famines and voyages across the ocean.

She saw her mother.

The woman was young, beautiful, with Damilola's own eyes. She was weaving the same pattern, the same river of silver. And she was calling out — not in words, but in a pulse of thread and color.

Come find me. I am not dead. I am in the weave.

Damilola reached for her mother.

The loom shuddered. The shadow figure appeared at the edge of the space, vast and terrible, its darkness bleeding into the silver.

"Stop," it said. "You weave yourself into death."

"You cannot stop me," Damilola said.

"I am not trying to stop you. I am trying to warn you. The Weave of Oshun does not grant passage freely. To bring your mother back, you must give something of equal value."

"What?"

The shadow figure gestured. Damilola looked down at herself.

The silver threads had spread. They covered her arms, her legs, her torso. They were no longer under her skin — they were her skin.

"The weaver becomes the weave," the shadow said. "That is the price."

Damilola looked at the threads. At her mother's face in the distance.

"Then I pay it."

---

Chapter 19: Mother and Daughter

She wove herself into the tapestry.

It was not like falling. It was like becoming. Each pass of the shuttle pulled a thread from her own being, a strand of silver that was her life, her years, her future. She felt herself thinning, spreading across the fabric of time like dye spreading through water. Her memories became patterns — the taste of mangoes became a golden thread, the feeling of rain became a blue one, the laughter of children became a rippling crimson. Her heartbeat became a rhythm in the cloth, steady and strong.

And then, through the shimmering weave, she saw her mother.

The woman was caught in the tapestry, suspended between moments. She had been there for seventeen years, weaving the same incomplete pattern, waiting for someone to finish it.

"Damilola," her mother said. "My daughter."

"I came to find you."

"I know." Her mother's eyes were full of tears. "I have been watching you. Every weave. Every mistake. Every triumph. I was so proud, and so afraid."

"I am going to bring you back."

"No. The cost is too high. You cannot trade your life for mine."

"I am not trading. I am weaving. There is a difference."

Damilola took her mother's hand. Together, they began to weave.

The tapestry grew. The river flowed. The Orisha's Voice became a chorus.

---

Chapter 20: The First Return

They emerged from the weave together.

Damilola collapsed at the loom, gasping. The silver threads had receded from her skin, but they still glowed beneath the surface, brighter than ever. Her lungs burned. Her eyes streamed tears she had not chosen to cry. Every muscle trembled.

And beside her lay a woman. Thin, exhausted, but alive.

Amina. Her mother.

The market was silent. The elders stared. Mama Bisi wept. Kofi stood frozen, his hands half-raised, unsure whether to touch Damilola or the stranger. The morning sunlight broke through the clouds and fell across Amina's face. She was pale from seventeen years without sun. Her hair had gone white at the temples. But her eyes — Damilola's own dark eyes — blinked open and found her daughter.

Amina opened her eyes.

"Damilola," she whispered. "You did it."

"I did not do it alone." Damilola's voice was barely audible. Her hands were still trembling from the weave.

Amina smiled, weak but genuine. "I know. I felt you. In the weave. You were never alone." She reached up and touched Damilola's face, her fingers tracing the line of her jaw as if memorizing it. "I saw you. When you were a baby, I held you. In the weave, I held you again. You were never alone."

The shadow figure was gone. The cracks in the market were healing — Damilola could see the wood knitting together, the gaps filling, the water settling, the indigo turning back to blue in slow spirals.

But the threads on her hands had grown thick, dark, heavy. They pulsed with a light of their own.

She did not look at them. She looked at her mother.

"It is good to see you," Damilola signed. Her hands were shaking.

Amina laughed, the sound rusty and strange after seventeen years of silence.

"It is good to be seen."

ARC THREE: THE WEAVER'S COST

Chapter 21: The Weight of Silver

Amina's return was a celebration. It was also a reckoning.

The Marqet threw a feast that lasted two days. Goats were roasted over open fires. Palm wine flowed through gourds passed from hand to hand. Musicians played drums whose rhythms Damilola felt through the platform, each beat traveling up through her bones and into her teeth. Children danced. Elders wept. Mama Bisi laughed until she choked.

But through it all, Damilola sat apart, watching her hands.

The silver threads had not receded. They had settled into a permanent network beneath her skin, visible in certain lights, pulsing with their own slow rhythm. When she held a cup, the threads pressed against the ceramic. When she touched cloth, they reached for it, hungry.

Amina noticed.

"Daughter," she said, sitting beside Damilola on the second night. The celebration continued around them, but they sat in a bubble of quiet. "Show me your hands."

Damilola extended them. Amina took them, turned them over, examined the silver network.

"How many weaves have you done?"

"I stopped counting after ten." Damilola looked down at her hands. The silver threads seemed to move as she watched, shifting like living things beneath her skin.

Amina's jaw tightened. "Ten major weaves in three months. That is... too many. Some weavers do ten in a lifetime. You have done ten before your eighteenth birthday."

"I know."

"The shadow figure — it spoke to you about the cost."

"Yes."

Amina stared at her daughter's silver-threaded hands. She had imagined this reunion a thousand times during her years in the weave — what she would say, how she would hold Damilola, how she would make up for lost time. She had not imagined this. A child dying for her mother's life. The weight of it pressed against her chest, heavy as wet cloth. Amina was quiet for a long time. Then she spoke, her voice barely audible.

"When I was caught in the weave, I could see the threads of everyone who had ever used the loom. Every weaver who came before us. The ones who wove wisely lived long lives. The ones who wove too much —" She stopped.

"The ones who wove too much died young," Damilola finished.

"Yes."

Amina pulled up her own sleeves. Her arms were also traced with silver, but faint, nearly healed. "I wove for seven days to stop the Great Sink. I nearly killed myself. When I came out, the threads had covered half my body. It took years for them to fade."

"Mama Bisi said you were caught in the weave."

"I was. But I chose to stay. The loom was not finished, and I knew you would come for me one day. I waited."

"You risked everything."

"I risked everything because I believed in you." Amina squeezed her hands. "But now you must promise me something. Do not weave the Weave of Oshun again. Not until you have recovered. Not until the threads fade."

Damilola looked at her hands. At the silver. At the hunger in the threads.

"I promise," she said.

She did not know if she could keep it.

---

Chapter 22: The Dying Indigo

The cracks in the market had healed, but the dye pits had not recovered.

Mama Bisi's indigo was still wrong. Not red anymore — it had settled into a muddy brown that would not take to cloth. She had tried everything. Fresh leaves. New pits. Different water. Nothing worked.

"The threads of time are still adjusting," Kofi said, stirring a vat with a wooden paddle. "The dye remembers the damage. It will take a season to forget."

"A season we do not have," Mama Bisi said. "The traders from the mainland come in two months. If I have no indigo, I have nothing to sell."

Damilola watched the brown liquid swirl. The threads in her hands tingled.

"Let me try something," she signed.

"Damilola," Amina warned.

"I am not weaving time. I am weaving cloth."

She sat at a small loom set up near the dye pits. She chose threads of white and pale blue. She began a simple pattern — straight weave, nothing complicated. But she focused her intent on the dye pits, on the indigo that would not return to blue.

The Orisha's Voice hummed, soft but present.

She wove an image of the dye pits as they had been. Deep blue. Alive. Correct.

When she finished, the cloth showed a picture of Mama Bisi's vats, full of indigo so dark it was almost black.

Mama Bisi looked at the cloth. Then at the vats.

The brown had begun to shift. A blue tinge appeared at the edges, spreading slowly, like ink in water.

"It is working," Kofi whispered.

Damilola held up her hands. The threads had not grown. This was not a weave of time — it was a weave of intent, of memory, of hope.

She had found a way to use her gift without paying the highest price.

---

Chapter 23: Kofi's Confession

Kofi found her at the edge of the market that night, watching the moon on the water.

"I lied to you," he signed.

Damilola turned. "About what?"

"When I first came to you. I said the star-cloth showed me the end of the Marqet. That was true. But I did not tell you how I got the cloth."

"How?"

Kofi looked at his hands. The indigo stains had faded, revealing the natural brown of his skin beneath.

"I stole it. From Grandmother Adwoa. Before she left the Marqet, I stole a roll of adire ale from her workshop. She knew I took it. She let me. It was meant for you."

"Why did you not tell me?"

"Because I was ashamed. I wanted to be the one who brought it to you. I wanted to be important to your story."

Damilola looked at him. At the shame in his posture, the way his shoulders curved inward. The water lapped gently against the platform below them. A fish jumped, splashed, was gone. The stars were just beginning to appear in the darkening sky.

"You are important," she signed. "Not because of the cloth. Because you paddled me to Badagry. Because you stood with me against the elders. Because you believed in me when I did not believe in myself."

Kofi looked up.

"Lying weighs heavy," she continued. "But so does the truth. You told me now. That is what matters."

They sat in silence for a long time.

Then Kofi reached into his pocket and pulled out a small bundle. "I have more of the star-cloth. I was saving it for something important — I did not know what, but I knew I would know when the time came." He pressed the bundle into her hands. "I think the time has come."

He placed it in her hands.

The cloth glowed. The stars on its surface moved, rearranging themselves into a pattern Damilola recognized — the river. The Weave of Oshun. The cloth was showing her the path.

"Thank you," she signed.

"Thank me when we are both old and the threads have stopped growing."

Chapter 24: The Second Shadow

The crack appeared without warning.

Damilola woke in the middle of the night to find the air cold. Frost had formed on the edges of the window — impossible in the lagoon heat, where the temperature never dropped below twenty-five degrees. Her breath clouded in front of her face. The mosquito net above her bed was stiff with cold. Her breath clouded in front of her face. The shadow figure stood at the foot of her bed, but it was different now — larger, more solid, its edges no longer blurry. She could see veins of darkness running through its form, like the pattern of threads in black cloth.

"You brought your mother back," it said. "That was not supposed to happen."

"I do not care what is supposed to happen." Damilola sat up. The cold bit through her thin sleeping cloth.

"You should. The balance of time is delicate. Every thread you pull weakens the fabric. Your mother's return created a paradox — she should be lost, but she is found. The universe is still adjusting."

"It healed the cracks."

"Those cracks were minor. This is not."

The shadow stepped closer. Damilola felt its cold reach into her chest.

"Your mother is a loose thread. The timeline has two of her now — the one who left and the one who returned. Reality is beginning to splinter around her."

"What do you want me to do?"

"Nothing. The paradox is beyond your ability to fix. I am here to tell you that the consequences will not be stopped by weaving. They will come, and you will have to face them."

"And if I weave anyway?"

The shadow's form flickered. "Then you will accelerate the unraveling. You will destroy not just yourself, but everyone you love."

It vanished.

Damilola sat in the dark, her heart pounding.

Her mother was a paradox. And the universe was coming to collect.

---

Chapter 25: The Thread That Should Not Be

She told Amina the next morning.

Her mother listened in silence, her face unreadable. When Damilola finished, Amina did not speak for a long time. The morning light crept across the room. Smoke from the breakfast fires drifted through the window.

"I knew," Amina finally said. "I felt it. In the weave, when I was trapped, I felt myself becoming wrong. The fabric of time did not want me back. It pushed against me like a current. Every moment I existed in the Marqet, I felt the timeline straining."

"Then why did you let me bring you?" Damilola asked. Her hands were trembling.

"Because I was selfish." Amina's voice cracked. "I wanted to see you. I wanted to hold you. I have missed seventeen years of your life — your first words, your first steps, the first time you sat at the loom. I was not there for any of it. And I would tear the fabric of reality itself to see you grow."

"But the shadow said —"

"The shadow is a guardian, not a master." Amina's voice steadied. "It enforces the rules, but it does not make them. The orishas make the rules. And there might be a way to satisfy both the rules and my existence."

"What way?"

Amina's eyes met hers. They were the same dark brown as Damilola's. "The Weave of Oshun, completed fully, does not just move through time. It rewrites it. It is not a patch on the fabric — it is a reweaving. If I weave myself back into the timeline properly, not as a paradox but as someone who was always meant to return, the universe will accept me. The timeline will absorb me."

"But that would require another weave of the full pattern."

"Yes."

"The cost —"

"Would be mine to bear, not yours." Amina said it firmly, as if the decision were already made.

Damilola shook her head. "I already have the silver threads. I am already marked. The threads know me. If anyone weaves, it should be me."

"You are seventeen years old. I am your mother. It is my duty to protect you."

"Duty does not matter when the universe is breaking around us." Damilola stood. "I wove you out of the threads. I will weave you back into them."

They argued for hours. The sun moved across the sky. Mama Bisi brought them food that neither ate. Kofi came to check on them and left again. In the end, they reached no conclusion. Amina refused to agree to Damilola's plan. Damilola refused to let Amina sacrifice herself.

But that night, when Amina finally slept — exhausted by the argument, by seventeen years of captivity, by the sheer weight of being alive again — Damilola sat at the loom alone, the seven threads of Eshu in her hands. The moon was high. The market was silent.

She would not let her mother pay the price.

Chapter 26: The Weave of Correction

She began at midnight.

The market was quiet. The lagoon lapped against the platforms. The moon was full, casting silver light across the floating city. Damilola had waited until everyone slept. Amina had finally closed her eyes at the third hour of the night, exhausted by their argument. Kofi had gone to his own stall. Mama Bisi was at the dye pits, praying over the red indigo.

Damilola threaded the loom with the Weave of Oshun pattern. She moved slowly, deliberately, feeling each thread settle into place. The threads of Eshu she kept separate, adding them one by one as the pattern required.

The Orisha's Voice hummed in her chest, but it was different now — cautious, warning.

"You are weaving against the current," the voice seemed to say. "This will cost you."

Damilola ignored it.

She wove her mother back into the fabric of time. Not as a paradox, but as a corrected thread. She pulled the memory of the Great Sink and reshaped it — made Amina fall into the weave rather than disappear from it, so her absence was intentional, not accidental. A weaver on a quest, not a victim.

The loom shuddered. The silver threads in Damilola's hands flared.

She felt the timeline shift around her. The past reknitted. The present adjusted.

And the cost hit her like a wave.

The threads surged up her arms, wrapping around her shoulders, spreading across her chest. She gasped, but did not stop weaving.

"You are killing yourself," the Orisha's Voice said.

"I am saving my mother," Damilola whispered.

She wove the final thread.

The loom went still.

Damilola collapsed.

---

Chapter 27: The Price Revealed

She woke three days later.

Amina was at her bedside. Kofi was in the corner. Mama Bisi was pacing.

"You are alive," Amina said. Her voice was raw.

"How long?" Damilola signed. Her hands felt heavy, strange.

"Three days. We thought you were dead."

Damilola looked at her hands. The silver threads had spread beyond her wrists. They covered her forearms now, dense and shining, like armor made of light.

"The weave worked," she said. "You are no longer a paradox."

Amina nodded slowly. "I checked. The timeline accepted me. The cracks in the market are gone. The shadow has not returned."

"Good."

"It is not good. You paid too much."

Damilola looked at her hands again. The silver had eaten into her skin. She could feel it moving, growing, reaching for more.

"How long do I have?" she asked.

Amina did not answer.

"Tell me."

"At this rate — if you do not weave another major pattern — perhaps a year. Maybe two."

Damilola felt the words settle into her chest like stones.

A year. Maybe two.

She had traded decades for her mother's life.

And she would do it again.

---

Chapter 28: The Dye That Remembers

Mama Bisi's indigo returned.

The vats turned blue overnight — a deep, luminous blue that was richer than anything she had produced before. Traders came from across the lagoon to see it. They called it "Damilola's Blue" and paid premium prices.

"You brought the color back," Mama Bisi said, pressing a bolt of the fabric into Damilola's hands. "This is yours. Weave something beautiful."

Damilola took the cloth. It hummed with the memory of the dye pits, of the correction she had woven, of the time that had been repaired.

She sat at her loom and began to weave.

She made a shawl for Mama Bisi, patterned with the history of the Marqet. She wove the floating market's birth, its growth, its survival through storms and droughts. She wove the faces of the people who had built it.

When she finished, the shawl was not just cloth. It was a living document. A book made of thread.

Mama Bisi wept when she received it.

"You are the greatest weaver since Olori," she said. "And I have wasted too much time not telling you that."

Damilola embraced her.

"I learned from the best," she signed.

---

Chapter 29: The Travelers from Beyond

A ship arrived at the Marqet.

It was not an ordinary ship. It had sails of white silk and a hull that seemed to shimmer, changing color as the light shifted. It anchored at the far edge of the market, and three figures descended.

They wore robes of silver thread. Their faces were masked.

The market gathered to watch. The elders stepped forward to meet them. The white-sailed ship shimmered at the edge of the Marqet, its hull cycling through colors like oil on water. Children pressed against the platform edges. Traders abandoned their stalls.

"We are the Weavers of the Gulf," the lead figure said. Her voice was amplified, carrying across the water without effort. It seemed to come from everywhere at once. "We have come to see the one who completed the Weave of Oshun."

Damilola stepped forward. The crowd parted around her like water around a stone.

"I am the one," she signed. Kofi translated, his voice steady but his hands trembling.

The lead figure removed her mask. She was an old woman, her face lined with scars that looked like threadmarks — silver lines that ran across her cheeks, her forehead, her chin. Her eyes were pure silver, without pupil or iris, and they fixed on Damilola with an intensity that made the air feel heavy.

"I am High Weaver Anaya. I have traveled from the floating city of Belede, across the gulf, to find you."

"Why?"

"Because the Weave of Oshun has not been completed in three hundred years. We felt it — a pulse in the fabric of time, radiating from this place. Like a drumbeat in the thread. We came to see if it was true. And you —" She looked at Damilola's hands. "You carry the marks."

"It is true."

Anaya looked at Damilola's hands. At the silver threads.

"So the cost was paid. As it always is." She bowed her head. "We request the honor of your presence in Belede. The Weavers of the Gulf would learn from you."

Damilola looked at her mother. At Kofi. At Mama Bisi.

"I will consider it," she said.

---

Chapter 30: The Third Path

That night, Damilola sat alone at the loom.

The silver threads on her arms caught the moonlight. She traced them with her fingers, feeling their texture, their weight.

Amina found her there.

"You are thinking about Belede," Amina said.

"I am thinking about time."

Amina sat beside her. "What about it?"

"How much I have left. How much I want to do with it. Whether I should spend it here, or —" She stopped.

"Or go where you are needed."

Damilola nodded.

Amina took her hand. "When I was caught in the weave, I saw many futures. In one of them, you go to Belede. You learn from the Gulf Weavers. You teach them what you know. You live a long life — not because the threads fade, but because you learn to manage them."

"And in the others?"

"In the others, you stay here, and the silver consumes you within two years."

Damilola was quiet.

"You are not choosing between two futures," Amina said. "You are choosing between dying here and living there."

"I do not want to leave."

"I know. But the weaver must follow the weave."

Damilola looked at the loom. At the orisha carvings. At Oshun's face, patient and eternal.

"I will go," she said.

But even as she spoke, she felt the threads in her hands pulse.

The journey was not over.

END OF ARC THREE

ARC FOUR: THE GULF AND THE LOOM

---

Chapter 31: The Voyage to Belede

The journey across the gulf took nine days.

Damilola traveled with Kofi, who insisted on coming. Amina stayed behind to help Mama Bisi with the dye pits. "This is your path," her mother had said at the edge of the Marqet, her hand on Damilola's cheek. "Walk it. And come back to me." They embraced, and Damilola felt her mother's heartbeat through the cloth between them — the same rhythm she had felt in the weave, the same steady pulse that had guided her through the darkness between threads.

The Gulf Weavers' ship was unlike anything Damilola had experienced. The white silk sails moved without wind, responding to patterns woven into the fabric. The hull shivered through the water, leaving no wake. It felt like sailing through a dream. The crew communicated in hand signals — not sign language as Damilola knew it, but a simplified system of gestures that she picked up within hours. For the first time in her life, she did not need a translator. She understood the crew as well as anyone, her hands moving naturally in the language of the sea.

High Weaver Anaya kept to herself for most of the journey, but on the third day, she found Damilola at the bow, watching the horizon. The gulf stretched to every edge, blue and endless. Flying fish broke the surface in silver arcs. The sky was the color of old bone.

"You have questions," Anaya said.

"Many."

"I will answer them. But first, tell me what you saw in the Weave of Oshun."

Damilola described the space between moments. The river of silver. Her mother, suspended in the threads. The shadow figure at the edges.

Anaya nodded. "You saw more than most. The first time I wove the pattern, I saw nothing. I was not ready."

"How many times have you woven it?"

"Three times. Each one showed me a new layer of reality. The first time, I saw the past. The second, the future. The third —" She paused. "The third time, I saw the weaver behind the weave."

Damilola frowned. "The weaver behind the weave?"

"There is a consciousness that exists in the space between threads. Older than the orishas. Stranger. The weavers of the old guild called it the Great Loom. It is the source of all time, all fabric, all existence."

"Does it speak?"

"It does not need to. It weaves. And we are the threads."

Damilola looked at her hands. At the silver threads.

"And if a thread breaks?"

Anaya's eyes met hers. "Then the pattern changes. The Great Loom adapts. But the broken thread is gone."

---

Chapter 32: The Floating City

Belede rose from the gulf like a dream of stone.

It was larger than the Marqet — a floating city of white buildings, connected by bridges of woven fiber that swayed gently with the current. Gardens grew on every platform, spilling flowers and vines in cascades of purple and orange and deep magenta. Waterfalls cascaded from the upper levels, powered by some mechanism Damilola could not identify — perhaps the same woven patterns that drove the ship's sails. The water fell in sheets that caught the sun and scattered rainbows across the lower platforms.

"The city was built by the first Weavers of the Gulf," Anaya said as they approached. "They came from across the ocean, fleeing a war that consumed their homeland. They brought the knowledge of the loom with them."

"How long ago?"

"Seven hundred years. The city has grown with every generation. Each high weaver adds a new platform. Each new platform requires a new pattern. Belede is not governed by laws — it is governed by thread."

The ship docked at a pier woven from reeds. Damilola stepped onto the platform. The surface was springy, alive. Beneath her feet, she felt the city breathing — a slow, rhythmic pulse, like a heartbeat. The woven fibers expanded and contracted with the tide, absorbing the motion of the water so the platforms remained stable.

"The city is woven," she signed to Kofi.

"Everything here is woven," he replied. "Look."

He pointed. The bridges, the buildings, the docks — all of it was made of interlocking fibers, dense and strong. Belede was not built. It was grown, like a coral reef, each generation adding new layers of woven structure.

A crowd had gathered to greet them. Weavers in silver-trimmed robes stood at the front, their hands raised in greeting. Children with threads woven into their hair ran between the adults, chasing each other through the crowd. Old women whose hands glittered with the same silver that marked Damilola stood at the back, watching with eyes that had seen centuries of weavers come and go.

They looked at Damilola with awe. She was the youngest weaver to complete the Weave of Oshun in three centuries. She was also the first to do it without the guild's training, without the proxy weave, without any of the safeguards they had developed over generations. She had done it alone, with nothing but her hands and her will.

"The one who completed the Weave," someone whispered. "The youngest in three centuries."

Damilola felt the weight of their gaze.

She was not ready to be a legend.

---

Chapter 33: The Guild of Silver

The Weavers of the Gulf initiated her on the seventh day. The days leading up to it were quiet — Damilola rested, ate the strange grain-based bread of Belede, and watched the city's rhythms from her window. Weavers passed below at all hours, their silver-threaded robes catching the light. Children played with looms the size of their bodies, learning the basics before they could read.

The ceremony took place in the Great Hall, a cavernous space woven from golden fiber. Light streamed through the ceiling, filtering through threads that shifted to create patterns on the floor — spirals, waves, the shapes of leaves and rivers. The patterns changed as Damilola watched, responding to the movement of the weavers below.

Anaya presided. The other high weavers stood in a circle around Damilola. They anointed her hands with oil that smelled of shea and honey. They wrapped her wrists in silver thread that came from a spool older than the Marqet. The thread was warm, and it bonded to her skin as soon as it touched.

"By the power vested in this guild by Oshun herself, I name you a Weaver of the Gulf," Anaya said. "You carry the Sight. You bear the thread. You will serve the loom until the loom releases you."

Damilola felt the threads in her arms pulse in response. The silver on her skin seemed to glow brighter, answering the ceremony, accepting the bond.

"The initiation is complete," Anaya said. "You are one of us now."

The circle broke. The weavers embraced her, one by one. But as they did, Damilola noticed something strange.

Some of their hands had no silver threads. Their skin was clear, unmarked.

"How is that possible?" she asked Anaya later. "The senior weavers have no silver. But they told me they have woven the pattern."

Anaya's face was guarded. "They have. But they used a different method."

"What method?"

"The Weave of Oshun can be completed at a distance. If you weave through a proxy — another loom, another thread — the cost transfers."

Damilola stared at her. "The cost transfers to someone else."

"Yes."

"That is monstrous."

"It is survival. The guild has existed for seven centuries because we share the burden. No single weaver carries the full weight."

"But I have been carrying it alone."

"You have. And you are dying because of it." Anaya's voice softened. "I am not proud of how the guild operates. But it is the reason Belede still stands. We made a choice, long ago, to spread the cost so that no one weaver would be consumed."

Damilola looked at the silver on her arms.

"I want to learn the proxy weave."

"Are you sure? It is not an easy technique. It requires binding another person to your loom — a person who will carry part of the cost."

"I will find someone willing."

Anaya nodded slowly. "Then I will teach you."

---

Chapter 34: The Proxy Weave

Learning the proxy weave took two months. Damilola practiced every day in a small room beneath the Great Hall, where the light came from threads woven into the walls and the air smelled of old wool. Anaya guided her through the fundamentals — how to extend her silver threads without losing herself, how to find another person's rhythm and match it, how to share the burden without transferring it entirely.

It was not a weave of time — it was a weave of connection. Damilola learned to extend her silver threads outward, linking her loom to another, sharing the burden of the pattern.

But every proxy had to be willing. The connection could not be forced.

"I will be your proxy," Kofi said.

"No. The cost is too high. You could die."

"Then I will die for something that matters."

Damilola shook her head. "I will not let you."

"You cannot stop me. This is my choice."

They argued for a week. In the end, Damilola relented.

The binding ceremony was simple. They sat at facing looms. Anaya guided them through the thread patterns. Damilola extended her silver threads. Kofi opened himself to receive them.

The connection snapped into place.

Damilola felt him in her chest — a second heartbeat, a second breath. She could sense his emotions, his thoughts, the rhythm of his blood.

"It worked," she whispered.

Kofi smiled. The indigo stains on his hands had begun to glow.

"I can see it," he said. "The weave. I can see the threads of time."

He had the Sight now. Not fully — not like Damilola — but enough.

The proxy weave had made him part of her gift.

---

Chapter 35: The Tapestry of the Lost

In the guild's archive, Damilola found a tapestry older than Belede itself. The archive was a vast underground chamber lit by threads woven into the walls — they glowed with a pale, steady light that never flickered. Shelves of rolled cloth lined every wall, each roll labeled in a script Damilola did not recognize. Some were so old they crumbled at the edges.

It hung in a sealed chamber at the deepest level of the Great Hall. The fibers were so old they had turned to dust in places. But the pattern was still visible — a river of silver, flowing from a source she could not identify, splitting into a thousand tributaries.

"The Tapestry of the Lost," Anaya said. "It shows the fate of every weaver who completed the Weave of Oshun before us."

Damilola studied it. Each tributary represented a weaver's life. Most ended abruptly — a thread cut short.

"Why did they die so young?"

"Because they wove alone. The guild had not yet discovered the proxy weave. Every weaver before that discovery carried the full cost of the pattern."

"But you have the proxy weave now. Why are there still weavers dying?"

Anaya was silent for a long moment.

"Because the proxy weave has a limit. It can spread the cost, but it cannot eliminate it. Every time the pattern is woven, the total cost increases. Each generation, the burden grows heavier. Eventually —" She gestured at the tapestry. "Eventually, the cost becomes too great. The threads break."

"And then?"

"And then the Weave of Oshun dies with the last weaver. Unless someone finds a way to break the cycle."

Damilola looked at the tapestry. At the thousands of lives woven into its pattern.

"I think I know why I was able to complete the pattern," she said.

"Why?"

"Because I did not know it was impossible. I did not know the cost was supposed to destroy me. I wove because I had to."

Anaya looked at her. "Ignorance is not protection."

"No. But determination is."

---

Chapter 36: The Dream of the Great Loom

The dreams returned.

Damilola slept and found herself in the space between threads. But this time, the shadow figure was not there. Instead, she saw the Great Loom. It was infinite. Its frame stretched beyond the horizon, beyond the edges of reality. The warp threads were made of light — every color that existed and some that did not. The weft threads were made of darkness, but not the empty darkness of the shadow — the deep darkness of night skies, of wombs, of the space between stars.

And across it, weaving with hands that contained galaxies, was a figure so vast she could not see its face. It was taller than mountains. Wider than oceans. Its fingers moved across the threads with the slow precision of tectonic plates.

"You are the broken thread," the figure said. Its voice was creation itself. It was the sound of the first cell dividing, the first wave breaking, the first breath.

"I am Damilola."

"I know. You are the one who wove against the current. You brought back a lost thread — your mother — and corrected the pattern."

"Yes."

"That was not supposed to be possible." The figure did not sound angry. It sounded curious, like a weaver examining an unexpected knot. "The pattern is fixed. The river flows one way. Cause and effect. Action and consequence. This is the law I wove into the foundation of time."

"Then how did I do it?" Damilola's voice was small against the vastness of the Great Loom, but she did not let it shake.

The figure paused its weaving. It looked at her — a gaze that spanned millennia. Galaxies reflected in its eyes. It saw her entire life at once: the infant pressed against Amina's chest, the child learning sign language on the floating platforms, the girl discovering silver threads in her palms, the young woman sitting at this loom, unbroken.

"Because you are not a thread. You are a weaver. And weavers can break the rules." The figure's hands resumed their work. "That is the secret the guild forgot. They learned to obey the pattern, but they forgot they could change it."

It gestured, and Damilola saw a vision.

The Weave of Oshun, but different. Richer. Deeper. A version of the pattern that did not consume the weaver, but sustained them. The threads moved in a cycle — taking from the weaver but also giving back, like a river that both receives rain and releases vapor.

"The full pattern was never meant to be destructive," the figure said. "The guild lost the true version when they crossed the ocean. The trauma of the crossing — the Middle Passage, the ships, the deaths — it erased the memory of the complete pattern. What they have now is a copy, a shadow, mostly accurate, but missing the key element."

"What element?"

"Reciprocity." The figure's voice deepened. "The weave must give back as much as it takes. The weaver must receive time, not just spend it. The river must flow both ways."

"How? How do I restore it?"

"Find the source. Oshun's beginning. The spring where time is born. Weave there, and the pattern will restore itself. The water remembers what the guild forgot."

The vision faded. Damilola woke gasping, her hands reaching for the loom.

She knew what she had to do.

---

Chapter 37: The Source of the River

The source of Oshun's river was not on any map.

Damilola searched the guild's archive for three weeks, reading every record of Oshun's worship, every myth, every traveler's account. She unrolled scrolls of cloth so old they crumbled at the edges. She traced patterns with her fingers, reading the stories embedded in the weave. She consulted Anaya, who brought out maps drawn on leather and maps drawn on dried leaves. She wrote to Grandmother Adwoa via messenger bird — a small weaver bird that carried a thread from the mangrove hut to Belede and back.

No one knew where the river began. Some said it was in the sky. Some said underground. Some said it began in the womb of the first woman and flowed outward from there.

But the Tapestry of the Lost knew.

She returned to the sealed chamber. She laid her hands on the ancient fibers. The silver threads in her arms reached out, connecting with the tapestry, reading its memory.

The tapestry showed her a mountain. A spring. A place where the water emerged from the earth, untouched, pure, the first moment of the river.

It was in the highlands of Yorubaland, on the mainland, a week's journey from the coast.

"I need to leave Belede," Damilola told Kofi.

"Where are we going?"

"To the source of Oshun's river. To find the true Weave."

Kofi did not ask questions. He began packing.

---

Chapter 38: The Road to the Highlands

They left Belede at dawn. The floating city receded behind them, its white towers catching the first light, its bridges swaying gently in the morning breeze. The woven walls of the guild hall caught the sunrise and scattered it across the water in fragments of gold.

The guild gave them a small boat with a single woven sail, provisions for two weeks, and maps drawn on treated leather. Anaya stood at the pier as they departed. Her silver eyes were unreadable. Her hands were clasped at her waist. Behind her, the other high weavers had gathered in silence, their silver-threaded robes rippling in the morning wind.

"Be careful," she said. "The highlands are not safe. There are things there that predate the orishas. Older spirits. Older rules."

"I know."

"You may not return. The source of Oshun has claimed more than one weaver."

"I know." Damilola looked at Kofi. He was checking their supplies, counting rations, his profile sharp against the morning sky.

Anaya placed a hand on Damilola's cheek. "You remind me of myself, when I was young. Before the guild taught me to be afraid. Do not let them teach you to be afraid."

"I will not."

The journey to the mainland took three days. The boat carried them across open water, past fishing villages built on stilts, past islands where the only inhabitants were birds. Kofi caught fish and grilled them over a small fire in a sandbox on the deck. They slept under the stars. The silver threads on Damilola's arms glowed in the darkness, visible even through her sleeves.

From the mainland, they traveled by foot, following the river upstream. The water grew clearer, colder, more alive, as they climbed into the hills. The villages they passed were small, traditional — mud-walled compounds, thatched roofs, women pounding yams in wooden mortars.

The villages they passed were small, traditional. People stared at Damilola's silver-threaded arms. Children reached out to touch her.

"She carries the mark of Oshun," the elders whispered.

On the sixth day, the river narrowed to a stream. On the seventh, they reached the mountain.

It rose from the earth like a sleeping giant, covered in forest. Somewhere on its slopes was the spring — the source of Oshun's river.

They began to climb. The path was steep, overgrown with vines that caught at their ankles. The air grew thin and cool. Birds with iridescent feathers watched from the branches. Kofi went first, clearing the path with his cutlass. Damilola followed, her silver threads sensing the way, pulling her toward the source like a river drawn to the sea.

On the second day, they found it.

---

Chapter 39: The Spring of Oshun

The spring was guarded.

They found it on the second day of climbing — a pool of water so clear it seemed to float above the rock. It emerged from a crack in the mountain and flowed downhill, becoming the river they had followed. The air around the pool was different. Cooler. Thicker. It hummed with a presence that was not quite sound and not quite silence.

But around the pool stood figures made of water. Human-shaped, translucent, watching with eyes that were not there. Their bodies shifted and flowed, never holding the same shape for more than a heartbeat. One had a woman's face, then a bird's, then a pattern of ripples.

"River spirits," Damilola whispered.

Kofi's hand went to his knife.

"Do not," Damilola said. "They are not enemies."

She stepped forward. The spirits turned to face her. The air pressure changed. The water in the pool began to swirl in a slow circle.

"I am Damilola Akin, weaver of Oshun's thread," she signed. "I have come to weave at the source."

The spirits did not respond in any language Damilola knew. But one of them — the tallest, its form rippling with golden light — extended a hand, pointing to the center of the pool.

Damilola understood.

She walked into the water. It was cold, impossibly cold, but she did not stop. The cold reached through her skin, through her muscles, into her bones. It should have been painful. Instead, it felt like truth. She waded to the center, where the spring emerged from the rock in a steady pulse — the heartbeat of the river.

She sat down in the water. It came to her chest. The cold settled around her like a second skin.

She opened her bag. She brought out her shuttle, her threads, the seven threads of Eshu.

She began to weave the Weave of Oshun.

This time, it was different. The pattern came alive in a way it never had before. The threads glowed. The water swirled. The spring seemed to pulse with light.

The Orisha's Voice filled her chest, overwhelming, ecstatic. It was not a hum anymore — it was a song. A melody that had been waiting in the stones of this mountain for millennia, waiting for someone to come and remember it.

"You found it," the voice said. "The beginning. The source. The spring where time is born."

Damilola wove. The pattern corrected itself under her fingers. The cost that had been consuming her — the silver threads that had been eating her life, day by day, weave by weave — began to reverse. She felt years flowing back into her. Not all of them. Not the ones she had already spent. But the flow stopped. The river gave back.

---

Chapter 40: The Restored Weave

She wove for three days.

Kofi waited at the edge of the pool, watching. The river spirits stood guard, their translucent forms circling the perimeter like sentinels. The mountain rumbled, as if approving the work. On the second day, rain fell — not from clouds, but from the waterfall behind the pool, which had somehow reversed direction and was falling upward into the sky.

When Damilola finished, she rose from the water. The spring had gone quiet. The pool was still. And on her body, the silver threads had changed.

The silver threads on her arms had changed. They were still there, but they no longer consumed. They had become channels — not taking from her, but flowing through her, connecting her to the river of time.

"The weave is restored," she said. "The true pattern."

She felt different. Stronger. More herself.

But the silver had not faded entirely. She looked at her arms.

"The proxy weave is still necessary for the largest patterns," she said. "But now it works properly. The burden is shared, not transferred. It flows in a cycle — taking from each weaver, yes, but also giving back. The river completes its loop."

Kofi embraced her. His arms were strong. She could feel his heartbeat through his chest, steady and warm.

"Does this mean you are not dying anymore?"

She pulled back and looked at her hands. The silver threads were still there. They would always be there. But they had stopped growing. The hunger in them had quieted.

"It means I am not dying as quickly." She smiled. "That is enough. That is more than I had yesterday."

They began the journey back to Belede, carrying the restored Weave.

But as they descended the mountain, Damilola felt a presence at the edge of her awareness.

The shadow figure was still there. Waiting.

The war was not over. It had only changed shape.

ARC FIVE: THE FINAL WEAVE

---

Chapter 41: The Shadow King Returns

The shadow figure met them at the gates of Belede.

It had grown again. Now it was the size of the city itself, a mass of darkness that blotted out the sun. The sky behind it was pure black, as if night had decided to arrive early and stay. The white towers of Belede were gray silhouettes against the dark. Birds had fled. Fish had gone deep. The water around the city was still and flat, reflecting nothing.

The weavers of the guild stood on the walls, their hands raised, silver threads extended against the dark. They formed a net of light across the city's perimeter, a barrier of woven brilliance that held the shadow at bay. For now.

"You have restored the weave," the shadow said. Its voice was everywhere. "But you have not undone the damage you caused."

"I undid the paradox," Damilola said. "My mother is no longer a loose thread."

"There are other threads. Thousands of them. Every time you wove, you created ripples. The Weave of Oshun is restored, but the fabric of time is still torn."

"Then I will mend it."

"You cannot. The tears are too many. They spread across centuries."

Damilola stepped forward. The silver threads on her arms blazed.

"Then I will weave time itself back together. Every thread. Every tear. Every moment I broke."

The shadow's form flickered. "That would require weaving across the entire timeline. You would enter the Great Loom itself — the space beyond time. No weaver has ever returned from such a journey."

"Then I will be the first."

Kofi grabbed her arm. "Damilola, you cannot. That journey would consume you."

"The shadow is right. The tears are everywhere. I caused them. I must heal them."

"Then I come with you."

"No. The proxy connection cannot reach into the Great Loom. You would be severed. You would die."

"Then I die."

Damilola looked at him. At his stubborn, indigo-stained hands. At his eyes, which held no fear.

"Stay alive," she said. "Someone needs to remember me."

She turned and walked toward the shadow.

---

Chapter 42: The Looms of the Ancestors

The shadow stopped her. It parted around her, letting her pass.

She walked through darkness for what felt like hours. Days. Years. Time had no meaning here. There was no sun, no moon, no change in the quality of the dark. Just the sensation of walking forward, one step after another, into an absence that pressed against her from all sides. She was in the space between — the place she had glimpsed during the Weave of Oshun, but deeper, stranger. The air here tasted of copper and old stone.

Ahead, lights appeared. Not stars. Looms.

Looms. Hundreds of them. Thousands. Each one was tended by a figure in white — the ancestors, the weavers who had come before. They moved in silence, their hands never stopping, their threads stretching into infinity.

"The Great Loom," Damilola whispered.

An ancestor looked up. An old woman with silver eyes.

"You are the one from the future," the ancestor said. "The one who restored the weave."

"I am."

"We have been waiting for you. The tears in time are beyond our reach — they are in your timeline, not ours. Only a weaver from your era can mend them."

"Show me."

The ancestor gestured. Damilola looked down and saw the fabric of time spread before her like a vast cloth. It was beautiful — a tapestry of infinite complexity, every thread a life, every pattern an era.

But there were holes. Gaps where the threads had been pulled loose. Dark spots where the fabric had frayed entirely.

"Each hole is a moment I broke," Damilola said.

"Yes. Each one spreads, unraveling the cloth around it. If they are not mended, the entire tapestry will collapse within a generation."

Damilola knelt before the fabric.

"Then I will mend them."

---

Chapter 43: The First Tear

She reached for the nearest hole — a small one, from her first weave, the falling fish that had started everything. It was no larger than her thumb, a dark spot in the bright fabric where the threads had pulled apart. But even a small hole, if left unmended, could unravel an entire garment.

Her fingers touched the frayed edges. The moment opened before her. She was back in the Marqet, watching herself at the loom, watching the fish fall through the air.

She stepped into the moment.

She did not change what happened. She did not stop the weave. Instead, she reinforced the edges of the torn fabric, weaving new threads around the hole, binding it closed.

The hole sealed.

She felt the cost. A day of her life, drawn from her like a thread pulled from cloth. But the true weave gave back — a half-day returned.

She was managing the cost. Finally.

She moved to the next hole. And the next. Each tear she mended, each frayed edge she sealed. Some were small — moments she had barely altered, a few seconds here, a glance there. Others were larger, gaping wounds where her weaves had rippled through days and weeks, changing the lives of people she had never met. Each one took time. Each one cost her more years of her life, pulled from her like thread from a spool, each year a length of silver that she fed into the fabric.

She was becoming part of the fabric.

---

Chapter 44: The Mother's Thread

She found her mother in the weave.

Not the real Amina — the thread of her, the pattern she had left in time. It was tangled, knotted from the paradox of her disappearance and return. The knot was dense, layered, each loop of thread wrapped around another in a spiral that seemed designed to resist all untangling. Seventeen years of paradox wound into a single tight core.

Damilola sat with the knotted thread. She did not cut it — that would destroy the pattern. She did not pull it — that would create new tears. She untangled it, thread by thread, with the patience of a weaver who had all the time in the world. Her silver threads extended from her fingers, working their way into the knot, loosening one loop at a time.

As she worked, she felt her mother's presence. The memory of her hands, the rhythm of her breathing. The knot pulsed with Amina's heartbeat — a rhythm Damilola had never heard but recognized in her bones. She remembered being held as an infant, pressed against her mother's chest, feeling that pulse through skin and cloth.

"You are saving me again," the thread seemed to say.

"I will always save you," Damilola replied.

The knot came loose. The thread settled into place. The tapestry brightened.

She moved on.

---

Chapter 45: The Battle in the Fabric

The shadow appeared in the weave.

It was not the figure from the Marqet — it was older, deeper, a manifestation of the Great Loom's own resistance to change. It stood before Damilola in the space between threads, vast and formless. Its edges merged with the darkness beyond the tapestry. Its presence pressed against her like a weight on the chest.

"You are mending the tears," it said. "But you are also changing the pattern. Every repair alters the design."

"The design was flawed. I am correcting it."

"The design was the will of the Great Loom. You are a thread. Threads do not correct looms."

Damilola stood her ground. The silver threads on her arms blazed like captured starlight. "I am a weaver. Weavers correct looms."

The shadow lunged.

It was not a physical attack — it was an assault on her existence. The shadow tried to unmake her, to pull her apart into individual threads, to scatter her across the fabric of time. She felt herself fragmenting. Her memories separated from her body — she saw her childhood as if from a great distance: the floorboards of the floating house, Mama Bisi's hands, the smell of indigo. Her identity began to dissolve. The shadow was pulling her apart, thread by thread, and she was spreading across the fabric like water spilled on cloth.

But the Orisha's Voice roared in her chest. Not a hum now. A roar. The sound of a river in flood.

"I am Damilola Akin," she said. "Deaf weaver. Daughter of Amina. Weaver of Oshun's thread. I am not a thread to be pulled."

She grabbed the shadow and wove it.

The darkness resisted, thrashing, but her hands were steady. She wove the shadow into the tapestry — not as a threat, but as part of the pattern. A guardian. A border. A thread that held the edges together.

The shadow screamed. Then it quieted. Then it became part of the weave.

She had won.

---

Chapter 46: The Last Tear

One hole remained.

It was the largest — the tear from her greatest weave, the correction that had brought her mother back. It gaped like a wound in the center of the tapestry, surrounded by pale, stretched threads that had been trying to hold together for months. The edges were dark, frayed, reaching outward like the legs of a spider. Damilola could feel the emptiness radiating from it, a cold that seeped into the surrounding pattern and weakened everything it touched.

Damilola approached it. The edges were ragged, frayed across decades. The threads around it were pale, stretched thin.

"This is the cost of bringing me back," her mother's voice said, from somewhere in the fabric.

"Yes."

"It will take everything you have left to mend it."

"I know."

"You will not survive."

Damilola looked at the tear. At the silver threads on her arms. At her hands, which had woven time itself.

"Then I will make it count."

She began to weave.

The tear resisted. It had been open too long. The threads around it had forgotten how to hold together. Every pass of the shuttle was a battle.

She felt the cost draining her. Years of her life, flowing into the weave. Decades. She felt herself growing thinner, lighter, becoming thread.

She did not stop.

---

Chapter 47: The Voice of Oshun

Oshun appeared in the weave.

Not as a vision. Not as a voice. The goddess stepped into the space between threads, wearing her true form — a woman made of river, crowned with flowing water, her eyes the color of the deepest sea. Her presence alone was a force, like standing at the edge of a waterfall. The light around her was not light but the memory of light, and her presence made the Great Loom tremble.

"You are dying," Oshun said.

"I know." Damilola's voice was thin. She could barely feel her hands anymore. The silver had climbed past her elbows.

"You do not have to. You can stop. The fabric will hold, even with the tear. I have seen the pattern. It will hold long enough for you to grow old."

"It will fray. The tear will spread. In a generation, it will consume everything you have woven. Every thread I repaired will come loose."

"That is a generation from now. You could live that generation. You could see your mother again. You could grow old. You could teach others what you know." Oshun's voice was not pleading, but it was close. For a goddess, Damilola realized, that was the same thing.

Damilola looked at the tear. At the work still to be done.

"I would rather die whole than live torn."

Oshun was silent for a long time. The river that was her body slowed. The light dimmed.

"Your mother said the same thing," Oshun said. "Before she wove her way into the threads. Before she left you."

"I know."

"She was brave. Foolish, but brave. I have watched a thousand weavers die. It does not get easier."

"Then do not watch. Weave with me."

Oshun looked at Damilola. At the silver threads consuming her. At the life draining from her with every pass of the shuttle.

"Then I will weave with you."

The goddess placed her hands beside Damilola's. Her fingers were made of water. They were cold and warm at once. Together, they worked the final threads, the river and the weaver, the immortal and the dying. The Orisha's Voice became a song that spanned the entire fabric of time.

The tear closed.

---

Chapter 48: The Thread of Eternity

The tapestry was whole.

Damilola stood back and looked at the fabric of time. Every thread was in place. Every pattern was correct. The tears were gone, the frayed edges sealed, the colors bright and true. The river of silver that ran through the center flowed without interruption from the beginning to the end.

She had done it.

But she could not feel her hands.

She looked down. The silver threads had consumed her. They covered her entire body now, a cocoon of light that pulsed with its own heartbeat. She was no longer fully human — she was becoming thread. Her flesh was translucent. Her bones were visible beneath the silver, glowing like filaments.

Oshun touched her face. The goddess's fingers were cool water against Damilola's fading warmth.

"You saved the tapestry," Oshun said. "But you gave everything to do it."

"It was worth it." Damilola's voice was distant, as if coming from somewhere else. The words took effort now. Her body was forgetting how to speak.

Oshun's eyes were sad. "You cannot return to your body. The threads have taken too much of you. But you do not have to fade. I offer you a place."

"What place?" The words were barely a thread themselves.

"In the weave. You will become part of the Great Loom — a pattern that lives forever, that guides future weavers, that holds the fabric steady. You will be what the ancestors are. A voice in the thread. A hand that helps from across time."

"A guardian of time."

"Yes."

Damilola looked at the tapestry one last time. She traced her fading vision across its surface. She saw Kofi, waiting at Belede, his indigo hands folded in his lap, his eyes on the horizon. She saw Amina in the Marqet, standing at the edge of the platform, looking out at the water. She saw Mama Bisi weaving indigo, her hands steady and strong, her face creased with age and love. She saw the baobab tree, the floating platforms, the sun setting over the lagoon.

"I accept," she said.

---

Chapter 49: The Weaver's Return

Damilola did not return to Belede.

Not in body, at least. But the weavers of the Gulf felt her presence in their looms. When a weaver sat down at an iroko loom, she would sometimes feel a warmth in the wood, a hum in the frame, a guiding pressure on the shuttle. The silver threads on their arms would pulse with warmth when they wove — not the cold hunger of the old cost, but a gentle warmth, like a hand placed over theirs. The patterns would align more easily. The cost of weaving would lessen. The cracks that had once threatened the guild's future began to close.

She became the ancestor who guided them.

Kofi felt her most strongly. He had aged — his hair was gray now, his back curved from decades at the loom — but his hands were still the same: blue-stained, precise, strong. The silver threads in his own palms had grown over the years, not from his own weaves but from the proxy bond that had never fully broken. They branched across his hands like the roots of the baobab tree, a permanent reminder of the girl who had changed everything. Every time he sat at a loom, he sensed her hands beside his, her rhythm intersecting with his own, the warmth of her presence guiding the shuttle. The proxy connection had not severed when she became part of the weave — it had transformed. It had become something deeper, a bond that transcended death itself. He wove with a ghost, and the ghost wove with him. When the guild asked how he knew which patterns were correct, he said, "I do not know what to tell you. But my hands know."

"She is still here," he told the guild. "She will always be here."

He became the new High Weaver of Belede, the first man to hold the title in the guild's history. He taught the proxy weave to every weaver who came to learn, his blue-stained hands guiding theirs across the threads. He spread the true Weave of Oshun across the gulf — to the Marqet, where Amina wove it into the curriculum of the market's weaving school; to the villages along the coast, where old women remembered the old songs and matched them to the new patterns.

Damilola's pattern became the standard. The cost of weaving was no longer death — it was service. Every weaver who used the true weave gave a thread of their life, yes, but the river gave back. The weave sustained.

The guild flourished.

---

Chapter 50: The Pattern Continues

In the Marqet, on a floating platform shaded by a baobab tree, a girl sat at an iroko loom. The tree had grown thicker over the years, its roots plunging deep through the platform into the water below. The carvings on the loom — Oshun, Ogun, Eshu — had been freshly oiled. The shuttle was new. The threads were white, waiting for color.

She was twelve years old. Her name was Taiwo. And she had been born with silver threads in her hands.

"Grandmother," she said, looking up at the old woman beside her. "The threads are moving on their own. I did not touch them."

Amina smiled. She was old now, her hair white as egret feathers, her hands lined with years of weaving. The silver threads under her own skin had long since faded to faint scars, reminders of a life lived between moments. But her eyes were still young — the same eyes Damilola had seen in the weave, the same eyes that had watched her daughter disappear into the loom and return. "They are responding to you," she said. "You have the Sight."

"What do I do?"

Amina placed her hand over her granddaughter's. Her palm was warm. The silver threads in Taiwo's hands reached up toward her grandmother's skin, recognizing kin, a greeting across generations.

"You weave."

Taiwo touched the loom. The threads came alive under her fingers, rising to meet her touch as if they had been waiting for this moment her entire life. The warp threads seemed to sing. The weft threads moved into place before she guided them. The Orisha's Voice hummed in her chest — a vibration she had never felt before, but recognized instantly, as if it had been waiting for her all along, dormant in her blood, patient across the years. She began a simple pattern, a straight weave, and the cloth grew beneath her hands. It came easily, naturally, like breathing, like the heartbeat that had been pulsing in her chest since before she was born.

Behind her, woven into the very fabric of the market, a pattern shimmered. It was silver, barely visible, shaped like a woman at a loom, her hands moving in time with Taiwo's. A guardian. An ancestor. A mother.

Damilola watched over her.
And the weave continued.

THE END

---

Epilogue: A Note on the Weave

The Weavers of Oshun still exist.

They are scattered now — across the gulf, along the coast, in cities that have not yet been built, in villages that have stood for centuries. Some know their heritage explicitly, trained from childhood in the true Weave. Others do not discover the silver threads in their hands until they sit at an iroko loom for the first time and feel the warmth rising — a sudden knowing that they are not alone, that someone is guiding their fingers.

Each generation, a child is born with silver threads in their hands somewhere in the world. Each generation, a weaver learns to hear the Orisha's Voice. The Sight does not die. It cannot die. It is woven into the fabric of the world, waiting for the moment it is needed most.

The true pattern survives. It is taught in Belede, passed down through the guild from High Weaver to apprentice. It is remembered in the Marqet, where Amina wove it into the floorboards of her stall so that anyone who sat there would feel the echo. It is whispered in villages along the coast, where grandmothers tell their granddaughters about the girl who wove time and became a thread in the Great Loom.

The cost is shared. No weaver carries the burden alone. The river flows both ways — taking, yes, but also giving back its grace. The weave sustains those who serve it.

Damilola Akin is still in the weave. If you sit at a loom made of iroko wood, the wood that remembers the old forests and the old ways, and if you feel warmth rising from the threads for no reason, and if you hear a hum in your chest that is not sound but knowing —

That is her. She is the hand that steadies yours. She is the pattern that forms beneath your fingers. She is the thread that holds.

And the weave continues. It always continues. Because time is a river, and rivers never stop flowing. They only change shape. They only find new paths. They only carry the stories forward.

This is one of those stories.

The End.
