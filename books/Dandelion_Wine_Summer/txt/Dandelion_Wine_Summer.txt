Chapter 1: The Last Day of School

The clock above the blackboard moved slower than any clock had ever moved. Benny watched the second hand crawl from one number to the next. Miss Dawes was reading aloud from a book about faraway places. Her voice droned like a bee trapped against a windowpane.

Benny's desk had a gouge in the corner where someone had carved a letter years ago. He traced it with his thumb. The room smelled of floor wax and chalk dust and thirty bodies that had been sitting too long in the heat.

Paul Henderson passed a note. Benny unfolded it under his desk. Meet at the creek after.

He folded it back and nodded. Paul turned around. Miss Dawes looked up from her book but did not say anything.

Through the window, Benny could see the elm tree at the edge of the playground. Its leaves were full and green. Beyond it, the road led out of town toward the farms.

The clock clicked forward one minute.

Benny counted the seconds until the bell. He reached one hundred and forty-seven before it rang.

The sound ripped through the room like a shot. Desks scraped. Chairs banged. Miss Dawes shouted something about next fall, but nobody listened. Benny grabbed his lunch pail and pushed through the door.

The hallway was chaos. Kids shouting. Locker doors slamming. The principal stood at the front doors with a bell in his hand, ringing it for no reason.

Benny burst outside and the heat hit him like a wall. The sun was high and white. He squinted and kept walking.

The school building shrank behind him. The gravel road stretched ahead. He did not look back.

Chapter 2: The Walk Home

Benny walked along the shoulder where the gravel met the grass. Cars passed every few minutes. Farm trucks mostly. People he knew. They waved. He waved back.

The heat shimmered off the road in waves. Cicadas buzzed in the ditches. The sound was so constant he barely heard it anymore.

He passed the Wilson place. Mrs. Wilson was hanging laundry on the line. She wore a wide straw hat and her arms were wet. She raised a hand. He raised his back.

Paul had pedaled ahead on his bike. Benny had walked because he liked the walk. It took forty minutes from the schoolhouse to his front gate. Forty minutes of nothing but road and sky and whatever he felt like thinking about.

Today he thought about summer. Two months. Maybe nine weeks if you counted the fringes. Time enough to do everything and nothing.

He passed the old water tower. It stood on four spindly legs at the edge of town. Someone had painted a smiley face on it years ago. The paint was faded now. The smile was barely there.

Benny stopped and looked up. The tower rose against the blue like a metal spider. He had never climbed it. He wondered if this would be the summer.

He kept walking.

The gravel turned to dirt at the county line. His house was the third one past the bridge. White clapboard. Blue shutters. A porch that sagged on the left side.

He smelled it before he saw it. Cinnamon. And something else. Yeast.

Grandma was baking. And Grandpa was making wine.

Chapter 3: Grandma's Kitchen

The screen door banged behind Benny. The kitchen was hot, hotter than outside, but the heat came from the oven instead of the sun. Grandma Helen stood at the counter with flour up to her elbows.

"There he is," she said. She did not turn around. She kept rolling the pin across the dough. "You get taller over the winter?"

"No, ma'am."

"Then you're due." She pointed her chin toward the porch door. "Your grandpa's out back. He's been waiting for you since breakfast."

Benny dropped his lunch pail by the stairs and went through the kitchen. Grandma's apron strings were tied in a perfect bow at her back. The same bow she had tied every day he could remember.

The porch was shaded by the roof overhang. Grandpa Tom sat in his rocking chair with a glass jar on the table beside him. The jar was full of yellow liquid. Cloudy. Swirling when he moved it.

"There you are," Grandpa said. He had a voice like gravel in a tin can. "School let out at noon. It's nearly one."

"I walked."

"Walking's good. Walking gives a man time to think." Grandpa held up the jar. "You know what this is?"

Benny sat on the top step. "Dandelion wine."

"First racking. From last week's batch." Grandpa swirled it. "See the sediment. That's the sugar settling. Means it's working."

Benny watched the yellow liquid spin. "When can we drink it?"

"Patience." Grandpa set the jar down. "Wine decides when it's ready. Not the other way around."

Chapter 4: The Dandelion Patch

Grandpa Tom led Benny past the chicken coop and the tool shed to the field behind the barn. Benny had seen it a thousand times, but he had never really looked.

The dandelions stretched from the barn wall to the creek bed. A solid sheet of yellow. The flowers stood tall on their stems, swaying in the breeze. Above them, the sky was a pale blue that went on forever.

"Look at that," Grandpa said. He stopped at the edge of the field. "You see a weed patch. I see money in a jar."

"How many does it take?"

"To make a gallon? About a thousand heads. Maybe more." Grandpa stepped into the field. The yellow parted around his legs. "Come on. I'll show you which ones to pick."

The flowers were warm from the sun. Grandpa bent and plucked one, holding it up by the stem. "Not too young. Not too old. The petals should be full out, but not starting to brown. See?"

Benny nodded. He picked one. The stem was slightly fuzzy. The petals were bright yellow, almost orange at the center.

"That's a good one," Grandpa said. "Now do that nine hundred ninety-nine more times."

They worked in silence. The sun moved. The field gave up its flowers. Benny's fingers turned green at the tips.

After a while, his basket was half full. Grandpa's was overflowing.

"That's enough for today," Grandpa said. "The rest will still be here tomorrow."

Chapter 5: The First Batch

They carried the baskets to the back porch. Grandpa spread newspapers across the table and dumped the flowers out. The yellow heap smelled of grass and earth and something sharp.

"First thing," Grandpa said. "You gotta wash them. Dirt carries bitterness."

Benny filled the basin from the pump. Grandpa dumped the flowers in and stirred them with his hands. The water turned brown. He drained it and filled it again.

"You strip the petals off the green parts. The green is bitter. Only the yellow parts go in."

They sat across from each other, pulling petals from stems. The pile of yellow grew. The pile of green stems grew beside it. Benny's fingers cramped. Grandpa's kept moving, steady as a machine.

"When your mother was little, she used to help me do this," Grandpa said. "She got tired after ten minutes. Ran off to play in the creek."

"What about my uncles?"

"They quit after one basket. Said it was women's work." Grandpa shook his head. "Work is work. Does not matter who does it."

Benny kept stripping petals. His thumbs were sore. The pile of yellow was growing.

"Now we boil the water," Grandpa said, standing. He took the kettle inside. "And we add sugar. Lots of sugar. Wine is just flowers and sugar and time."

Benny followed him into the kitchen. Grandma had moved her baking to the counter. The stove was free.

"We use your grandma's kitchen because she says yes." Grandpa winked. "Never make wine in a kitchen where the cook says no."

Chapter 6: The Recipe

Grandpa Tom reached into the cupboard above the refrigerator and pulled out a notebook. The cover was stained and warped. The pages were yellow and soft at the edges.

"This is the recipe," he said. He set it on the table. "It belonged to my father. And his father before that."

Benny opened the cover. The handwriting inside was not Grandpa's. It was older. Loops and flourishes from a different century. The ink had faded to brown.

"Can I read it?"

"Read it out loud. I'll tell you what it means."

Benny squinted at the first page. "Take one gallon of dandelion blooms, picked at the full of the sun."

"Means pick them at noon," Grandpa said. "When the flowers are open widest."

"Add one gallon of boiling water. Let steep for three days."

"That's the tea step. You let the flowers sit in the water until it tastes like nothing but sunshine."

Benny turned the page. The ink was lighter here. Harder to read. "Strain and add three pounds of sugar. One orange. One lemon."

"Citric acid," Grandpa said. "Helps it ferment. And sugar is what turns to alcohol. The yeast eats it."

"Yeast?"

"Comes later." Grandpa tapped the notebook. "Keep reading."

Benny read the rest silently. The recipe went on for two more pages. Steps about racking and bottling and waiting. The last line was in different handwriting, bigger, darker.

_Wine is memory in a bottle. Share it with people you love._

"That was my father's addition," Grandpa said. "He added that line the year before he died."

Chapter 7: The New Neighbors

Benny was in the yard throwing rocks at a fence post when the truck came. It was a big moving van, yellow with red lettering on the side. It turned up the gravel lane that led to the old Henderson place.

The Henderson house had been empty for two years. Since Mr. Henderson died and Mrs. Henderson moved to Kansas City to live with her sister. The grass was knee-high. The porch steps were rotted. Nobody had fixed anything.

But now there was a truck.

Benny walked to the fence line and watched. The truck backed up to the porch. Two men got out and lowered the ramp. A woman climbed down from the passenger side. She was thin and wore sunglasses. She stood in the yard with her hands on her hips, looking at the house.

Then a girl got out.

She was about his age. Maybe older. She had dark hair pulled back in a ponytail. She looked at the house the same way her mother did. Hands on hips. Taking it in.

Then she turned and saw Benny.

She did not wave. She just looked at him for a long moment. Then she turned back to the house.

Benny stayed at the fence until the men started carrying furniture inside. A sofa. A mattress. A cardboard box marked KITCHEN.

He wanted to go help. He wanted to introduce himself. But his feet would not move.

He went back to throwing rocks at the fence post.

Chapter 8: Clara

She came to him the next morning. Benny was sitting on his front steps, tying his shoes, when she appeared at the end of the walk.

"My name's Clara," she said. No hello. No introduction. Just her name.

"I'm Benny."

"I know. I asked my mom who lived here." She walked closer. She wore shorts and a t-shirt with a faded cartoon on it. Her feet were bare. "You got a creek around here?"

"Behind the Johnson farm. Quarter mile that way."

"Good." She sat on the step below him. "I hate being the new kid. Everyone stares at you for the first week, then they decide who you are without asking."

Benny did not know what to say to that. He finished tying his shoe.

"You got a bike?" she asked.

"Yeah."

"We could ride to the creek. If you want."

Benny looked at her. She was looking at the road, not at him. Like it did not matter whether he said yes or no. But he could tell it mattered.

"Okay," he said.

They rode their bikes through the dust. Clara's was a hand-me-down, painted blue, a little too big for her. She wobbled on the loose gravel but did not fall. She did not slow down.

At the creek, she dropped her bike in the grass and walked to the water's edge. She stood there looking at it.

"It's not very deep," she said.

"It's deeper in the middle. Past the rocks."

She nodded. Then she sat down on the bank and put her feet in the water.

"This is a good spot," she said.

Chapter 9: The Dare

They went to the water tower on the third day. Clara brought it up. They were sitting on Benny's porch, drinking lemonade, when she pointed at it.

"Ever climbed it?"

"No."

"Scared?"

"No. Just never did."

She set her glass down. "Let's climb it."

Benny looked at the tower. It was a hundred yards out past the railroad tracks. Four metal legs bolted into concrete. A ladder ran up one leg to a platform at the top. The platform had a railing that had rusted through in places.

"That's not safe."

"Since when do you care about safe?"

He did not have an answer for that. She was already walking.

The gravel was hot under his shoes. Clara walked ahead, not looking back. She reached the base of the tower and grabbed the ladder without hesitating.

"Coming?"

Benny looked up. The ladder disappeared into the sky. The platform was a small square against the blue.

He grabbed the rung above his head. The metal was warm. He pulled himself up.

The rungs creaked but held. He climbed one hand after the other. He did not look down. He could hear Clara's feet above him, steady and sure.

The wind picked up halfway. It pushed against him, trying to peel him off the ladder. He held tighter.

Clara reached the platform first. She pulled herself over the railing and stood up. Her hair was blowing across her face.

"It's amazing," she said. "Get up here."

Benny pulled himself over the railing. His hands were shaking. He stood up.

The whole valley spread out below them. The farms. The roads. The creek like a silver ribbon. His house was a white dot. His whole world, small enough to see at once.

Chapter 10: At the Top

They sat on the platform with their legs through the railing, feet dangling in the air. The wind was stronger up here. It whipped Clara's hair across her face and she did not push it away.

"I climbed one in St. Louis," she said. "Different one. Taller."

"When did you live in St. Louis?"

"Last year. Before my dad lost his job." She said it flat. Like she was reading it from a book. "We moved in with my aunt in Springfield. Then we came here."

Benny did not know what to say. Sorry felt wrong. He stayed quiet.

"Your grandpa makes wine?" she asked.

"Where'd you hear that?"

"My mom. She talked to your grandma at the grocery store." Clara looked at him. "She said your grandpa makes dandelion wine. I've never had wine."

"You're twelve."

"So? Have you had it?"

"Yeah. Well, a taste. From the bottle he keeps in the cellar."

"What's it taste like?"

Benny thought about it. "Like summer. But also like something else. Something you can't name."

Clara nodded like that made sense. She turned back to the view.

"I'm glad we moved here," she said. "I was scared we would. But I'm glad."

The sun was dropping toward the treeline. The shadows stretched long across the fields. Benny did not want to go down. He wanted to stay up here, where the world was small and he could see all of it.

Chapter 11: The Fermentation

Grandpa Tom was in the cellar when Benny got back. The cellar was cool and dark. Shelves lined the walls, crowded with jars. The air smelled like yeast and wet stone.

Grandpa stood over a row of glass jugs. Each one was fitted with a rubber stopper and a thin tube that ran into a smaller jar of water. Bubbles rose through the tubes, slow and steady.

"The yeast is eating the sugar," Grandpa said. He pointed at the bubbles. "That's the carbon dioxide coming out. When the bubbles stop, the fermentation is done."

"How long does it take?"

"Three weeks. Maybe four. Depends on the temperature. Depends on the yeast. Depends on a hundred things you cannot control."

Benny watched the bubbles. They rose in a steady rhythm. One after another. Never stopping.

"Can I do anything to speed it up?"

"No." Grandpa put his hand on Benny's shoulder. "That is the hardest lesson of winemaking. You do the work. Then you wait. The waiting is part of the recipe."

Benny thought about Clara. About the water tower. About the way the summer stretched ahead, endless and full.

He did not want to wait.

But the bubbles kept rising, one at a time, and there was nothing he could do to make them faster.

Chapter 12: Swimming Hole

The swimming hole was behind Johnson's farm, where the creek widened into a pool. The water was brown and cool and deep enough to swim in. The bottom was soft mud that squished between your toes.

Benny and Clara rode their bikes out on Friday afternoon. The sun was high. The air was thick with humidity. Even the cicadas sounded tired.

Clara dropped her towel on a rock and waded in without checking the temperature. She went straight to the middle, where the water came up to her chest.

"It's perfect," she said.

Benny went in slower. The water was colder than the air, but not by much. He ducked under and came up shaking his head.

They floated on their backs, looking at the sky. A hawk circled above. The clouds were thin and white and moving fast.

"Paul Henderson said there's a rope swing somewhere," Clara said.

"Upstream. But the rope broke last year."

"We could fix it."

"Maybe."

Clara splashed him. He splashed back. They wrestled in the water until they were both out of breath.

On the bank, drying off, Clara pointed at a scar on Benny's knee. "Where'd you get that?"

"Fell off the barn roof. Two years ago."

"What were you doing on the barn roof?"

"Trying to see if I could jump to the hayloft."

"Could you?"

"No." Benny touched the scar. "Hit the edge of the loft on the way down. Grandpa carried me to the house. I got five stitches."

Clara smiled. "I would have done the same thing."

Chapter 13: The Catfish

Frank Miller showed up at the swimming hole with his fishing pole and a can of worms. Frank was seventeen. He drove a rusted pickup and thought he knew everything.

He set up on the bank downstream from where Benny and Clara were swimming. He baited his hook and cast it into the deep part.

"Watch this," he said. "I'm gonna catch something big."

Benny ignored him. Clara watched for a minute, then turned away.

Twenty minutes later, Frank's line went tight. The rod bent double. Frank yelled and grabbed it with both hands.

"Get over here! I need help!"

Benny waded over. Frank was leaning back, his arms shaking. The line cut through the water in a wide arc.

"It's a monster," Frank said. "Grab the net."

The net was on the bank. Benny grabbed it and waded into the water. The fish was close now, a dark shape in the brown water. It was big. Bigger than anything Benny had ever seen come out of this creek.

Frank pulled the fish onto the mud bank. It was a catfish. Three feet long. Whiskers as thick as pencils. Its mouth opened and closed, gasping.

"Holy cow," Benny said.

Clara came over and looked at it. Her face did not change.

"You gonna eat that?" she asked Frank.

"Gonna mount it," Frank said. "Put it on my wall."

Clara looked at the fish. Then she looked at Frank. "You killed it for the wall."

She walked back to her towel, picked it up, and started walking home.

Benny watched her go. Then he looked at the fish. Frank was already measuring it with his hands, talking about how big it was going to look on the wall.

Chapter 14: Barn Loft

The storm came fast. One minute the sky was blue. The next, the clouds rolled in from the west, dark and low. The wind picked up. The first drops of rain hit the dust like pebbles.

Benny and Clara were in the backyard, trying to get Clara's kite untangled from the fence. By the time they got it free, the rain was coming down hard.

"Barn," Benny shouted.

They ran. The rain soaked through Benny's shirt before he made it ten steps. The barn door was heavy. He pulled it open and they stumbled inside.

The barn was dark. Dust motes floated in the light from the high windows. The air smelled of hay and horses, though the horses had been gone for years.

Clara was breathing hard. Water dripped from her hair down her face. She pushed it back.

"That came out of nowhere," she said.

Benny opened the ladder to the loft. "We can wait it out up there. More air."

They climbed. The loft was half full of hay bales, stacked against the walls. A small window faced east, toward the house. The rain hammered against it.

They sat on a hay bale, listening to the storm. Thunder rolled across the sky, long and low. Lightning lit up the cracks in the barn walls.

"This is nice," Clara said. "The sound."

Benny nodded. The rain on the roof was loud. A steady drumming that filled the whole barn.

"I used to be scared of storms," Clara said. "My dad would sit with me until I fell asleep."

"Where is he now?"

"Working. He got a job at the hardware store. He comes home late."

The rain kept falling. The light through the window turned gray. Clara leaned back on the hay and closed her eyes.

Benny stayed awake, watching the storm through the window.

Chapter 15: The Old Photograph

Grandpa Tom was sorting through a box in the living room when Benny came in. The box was full of old things. Photographs. Letters. A medal of some kind.

"What's all this?" Benny asked.

"Cleaning out the closet." Grandpa held up a photograph. "Look at this."

It was a picture of a young man in a soldier's uniform. The man was skinny. His hat was too big. He stood in front of a barren field, squinting at the sun.

"That's me," Grandpa said. "1944. Before I shipped out."

Benny took the photograph. The man in the picture looked nothing like the Grandpa he knew. This man was young. His face was smooth. He looked scared.

"Were you in the war?"

"Europe. I was a rifleman. I saw things I don't talk about."

Benny looked at the picture. "Did you ever think you wouldn't come back?"

"Every day." Grandpa took the photograph back. "But I did. I came back and I married your grandmother and I made wine. That's the point, I think. You come back and you do something with the time you got."

Benny did not know what to say. He watched Grandpa put the photograph back in the box.

"Can I see the medal?"

Grandpa handed it to him. It was a bronze star, tarnished at the edges. The ribbon was faded.

"You earned this?"

"I was in the right place. That's all."

Benny held the medal. It was lighter than he expected. He handed it back.

Grandpa closed the box. "Some things you keep. Some things you let go."

Chapter 16: Grandma's Story

Grandma Helen found Benny in the kitchen, staring out the window. She set a glass of milk in front of him and sat down.

"Your grandpa showed you his war things?"

"Yes, ma'am."

She nodded. She wiped a spot off the table with her thumb. "He does that when summer starts. Gets thinking about time passing."

"Grandma, how did you meet him?"

She smiled. It was a small smile, private, like she was remembering something she had not thought about in a while.

"Barn dance. 1938. I was seventeen. He was nineteen. He was home from the farm for the weekend, working in town. He showed up in a shirt that was too tight and boots that were too big."

"Was he handsome?"

"He thought he was." She laughed. "But he danced with me all night. Three hours. He stepped on my feet twice, but he kept asking. At the end of the night, he walked me home. He said he was going to marry me."

"And you believed him?"

"I told him he was crazy." She smiled again. "But I believed him."

Benny drank his milk. "Do you think I'll find someone like that?"

"You will. Or they will find you. That's how it works." She stood and took his empty glass to the sink. "But you got time. Don't rush it."

Chapter 17: Fireflies

The fireflies came out at dusk. Benny and Clara sat on the back steps with Mason jars, watching the first lights blink on in the grass.

"Ready?" Clara asked.

They ran into the yard. The fireflies rose around them, a cloud of yellow light. Benny swung his jar and caught two at once. He could see them inside, their lights flashing against the glass.

Clara was faster. She had already caught five. She held the jar up and watched them crawl up the sides.

"They're so weird looking up close," she said. "Not like lights at all. Just bugs with butts that glow."

"Don't ruin it."

"I'm not ruining it. It's more interesting. They're just bugs. But they make light. That's incredible, if you think about it."

Benny caught another one. His jar had four now. Their lights blinked on and off, each on its own schedule.

"Some people say they blink together when there's enough of them," Clara said. "Like they synchronize."

"Do you believe it?"

"I've seen it. In Springfield. There was a field behind my aunt's house and the fireflies would blink all at the same time. Thousands of them. Like a wave."

Benny looked at his jar. The lights were random.

"Maybe they need to be free to do it," he said.

He opened the lid. One by one, the fireflies crawled out and flew away. Their lights joined the others in the yard.

Clara opened her jar too. The fireflies rose and scattered.

They sat on the steps and watched them until it was too dark to see.

Chapter 18: The County Fair

The county fair came the second week of July. Benny woke to the sound of Grandpa Tom loading crates into the truck. Grandma's pies. Grandpa's wine. A whole summer of work packed into the bed of the Ford.

"Up and at 'em," Grandpa called through the window. "The pigs don't judge themselves."

The fairgrounds were on the north edge of town. A big field with tents and booths and a Ferris wheel that had been painted so many times the layers were cracking. The air smelled of frying dough and animal hay and dust.

Benny found Clara by the ticket booth. She was wearing a yellow dress he had not seen before. Her hair was down.

"You look different," he said.

"So do you." She handed him a ticket. "My mom bought extra. First ride's on her."

They walked through the midway. The games were rigged. Everyone knew it. But people kept throwing baseballs at milk bottles and darts at balloons.

Benny won a small stuffed bear at the ring toss. He gave it to Clara without thinking. She took it and held it for a long moment.

"Thanks," she said. She tucked it under her arm. "Nobody's ever won me anything before."

They walked to the Ferris wheel. The line was long. They waited in the sun. When they got to the front, the operator lifted the bar and they climbed into the swaying basket.

The wheel rose. The fairgrounds shrank. Benny could see everything. The tents. The fields. The road home.

He looked at Clara. She was looking at the sky.

Chapter 19: The Pie Contest

Grandma's apple pie sat on the judging table between two other pies. One was cherry, with a lattice crust. The other was peach, with crumb topping. Grandma's was simple. A solid crust. Rows of crimped edges. Steam still rising from a small cut in the center.

Mrs. Patterson from church stood beside the cherry pie. She had won the blue ribbon four years running. She was already smiling, like she knew how this would end.

The judge was Mr. Hollis from the bank. He wore a white suit and a straw hat. He approached each pie with ceremony, cutting a small slice, chewing slowly, writing notes on a pad.

When he got to Grandma's pie, he took his time. He cut. He chewed. He closed his eyes.

Benny watched from the front row. He was holding his breath.

Mr. Hollis wrote something on his pad. Then he stepped back and looked at all three pies.

"The winner," he said, "is Helen Kettering's apple pie."

Grandma did not cheer. She just smiled her small smile and stepped forward to accept the ribbon. Mrs. Patterson's smile cracked. She collected her pie and left without a word.

Benny ran to the table. "You won!"

"Of course I won," Grandma said. "I've been making that pie for forty years. Mrs. Patterson has been making that pie for four. Time matters."

She pinned the ribbon to her apron. Blue against the white flour dust.

Chapter 20: The Tilt-a-Whirl

The Tilt-a-Whirl sat at the edge of the midway, painted red and white. The cars were shaped like teacups. The motor groaned when it started.

"You sure?" Benny asked.

"Scared?" Clara said.

"No."

"Then get in."

They sat across from each other in the teacup. The operator pulled the bar down. The ride lurched and started spinning.

At first it was slow. Benny could see the fairgrounds rotating past. The tents. The people. The Ferris wheel.

Then it picked up speed. The teacup spun on its own axis while the platform spun underneath it. The world became a blur of color and motion.

Clara laughed. She leaned back and let the ride throw her around. Her hair flew across her face. She did not try to push it away.

Benny held on. The spinning pressed him into the seat. His stomach floated.

Then it stopped.

The world kept spinning for a moment. Benny stood up and stumbled. Clara caught his arm.

"Good ride," she said.

"Good ride."

They walked away from the Tilt-a-Whirl. Benny's legs were unsteady. Clara walked straight.

"You've done that before," he said.

"I've done everything before." She smiled. "But I haven't done any of it with you."

Chapter 21: Midnight in the Cornfield

Benny snuck out after midnight. The house was dark. His parents' room was quiet. He slid his window open and dropped onto the grass.

Clara was waiting at the fence. She wore a dark jacket over her pajamas. Her feet were in sneakers, untied.

"Took you long enough," she whispered.

"I had to wait until the snoring started."

They walked through the backyard to the cornfield behind the Johnson farm. The corn was high now, taller than both of them. The stalks rustled in the breeze.

Clara led the way. She knew where she was going. She had found it earlier that day, she said. A clearing in the middle of the field where the corn did not grow.

They pushed through the last row and stopped.

The clearing was round. Maybe twenty feet across. The sky opened above it, full of stars. More stars than Benny had ever seen. The Milky Way was a white river across the dark.

They lay on their backs in the dirt. The earth was still warm from the day. The corn rustled around them.

"That's Jupiter," Clara said, pointing. "And that's Mars. The red one."

"How do you know?"

"I read a book." She turned her head to look at him. "My dad gave it to me. About the stars. Said if I learned the sky, I'd never be lost."

Benny looked up. The stars did not move. They just burned, steady and far away.

Chapter 22: The Confession

They lay in the clearing for a long time. The stars wheeled overhead. Benny lost track of how long they had been there.

"We might move," Clara said.

The words came out flat. Like she had been holding them in and they just slipped out.

Benny sat up. "What?"

"My dad. He's been looking for work in St. Louis. There's nothing here. Not for him."

"But you just got here."

"I know." She sat up too. She pulled her knees to her chest. "He said it's not for sure. But he's been making calls. Sending letters."

"When would you go?"

"If it happens. August. Before school starts."

Benny looked at the corn. The stalks were dark shapes in the dark. "That's three weeks."

"I know."

"Did you want to tell me?"

"I didn't want to. But you should know." She looked at him. "I don't want to go."

"Then don't."

"I don't get to decide."

They sat in silence. The crickets were loud. The stars kept burning. Benny felt something press against his chest. A weight he could not name.

"We should go back," Clara said.

They walked through the corn without talking. The house was still dark. Clara climbed over the fence and crossed her yard.

She did not look back.

Chapter 23: Grandpa's Cough

Grandpa Tom was coughing. Benny noticed it at breakfast. Grandpa sat at the table with a cup of coffee, and every few minutes he coughed into his handkerchief. A dry cough. Deep. It came from somewhere low in his chest.

"You okay?" Benny asked.

"Fine." Grandpa wiped his mouth. "Just a tickle."

Grandma looked at him. She did not say anything. But her eyes stayed on him longer than usual.

Through the morning, the cough got worse. Grandpa was supposed to work in the garden, but he sat on the porch instead. His breathing was heavier than it should have been.

Benny brought him a glass of water. "You want me to get Grandma?"

"No. I'll be fine." Grandpa took the water. "Old bodies do old things. Nothing to worry about."

But Benny was worried. He had seen Grandpa cough before. Everyone coughed sometimes. This was different. This cough took something out of him. Each time it happened, Grandpa looked smaller afterward.

Dr. Miller came by in the afternoon. He was not wearing his doctor coat. He was wearing regular clothes, which meant he had not been called. He had come on purpose.

They sat in the living room with the door closed. Benny pressed his ear to the wood but could not make out the words.

When the door opened, Dr. Miller was putting his bag away. He nodded at Benny and left.

Grandpa went to bed early.

Chapter 24: The Doctor's Visit

Grandma found Benny in the hallway after Dr. Miller left. He was sitting on the floor with his back against the wall.

"You heard something?" she asked.

"No, ma'am. Couldn't make it out."

She sat down beside him. The floorboards creaked under her weight. She folded her hands in her lap.

"Your grandpa's lungs are sick," she said. "The doctor thinks it's been going on a while."

"What kind of sick?"

"Lung sickness. The kind that doesn't get better."

Benny looked at his hands. "Is he going to die?"

Grandma took a long time to answer. "We are all going to die, Benny. The question is when."

"That's not an answer."

"Sometimes not answering is the only kindness there is." She put her hand on his head. "Stay close to him. He wants you close."

She stood up and went to the kitchen. Benny heard her open the cupboard. Heard the clink of dishes. He heard the tap run and then stop. She was making tea. It was what she did when she did not know what else to do.

He stayed on the floor a long time, counting the nails in the floorboards. One row of twelve. Then another. He got to sixty before he stopped. Grandpa was dying. The words would not fit in his head.

Chapter 25: Secret Worry

Benny started listening. He did not mean to. It happened because he was always in the wrong place at the wrong time.

That night, he was on the stairs when his parents talked in the kitchen.

"Margaret, I don't know how much longer we can do this." His father's voice. Tired. "The doctor bills are going to pile up."

"What else can we do?" His mother's voice. "He's my father."

"I know. I know. But we have to think about Benny. About school supplies. About winter clothes."

"We'll manage. We always manage."

"And if we don't this time?"

Benny sat on the stairs. He did not breathe. The kitchen was silent for a long moment.

"We will," his mother said. "Because we have to."

Benny crept back to his room. He lay on his bed and looked at the ceiling. The cracks in the plaster made a map of somewhere far away. A place with rivers and roads and no sickness.

He thought about Grandpa's cough. About the doctor. About the word cancer that no one had said out loud but everyone was thinking. He thought about his mother's voice and how it had cracked at the end.

He pulled the pillow over his face and stayed there until he fell asleep.

Chapter 26: The Wine Cellar

The next morning, Grandpa was up. He was moving slow, but he was moving. He found Benny in the barn and handed him a flashlight.

"Come on," he said. "I got something to show you."

Grandpa lifted a trapdoor in the barn floor that Benny had never noticed. It was hidden under a pile of old feed sacks. The door opened to a set of wooden stairs leading down into the dark.

The cellar was small. Maybe ten feet by ten. Shelves lined every wall. Bottles covered every shelf. Hundreds of them. Dusty. Dark. Labeled in Grandpa's handwriting.

"This is the good stuff," Grandpa said. He ran his hand along a shelf. "Some of these bottles are older than your mother."

Benny counted the rows. "How many are there?"

"Last count, two hundred and sixteen." Grandpa pulled a bottle at random. The label said 1948. "This was the year I built the addition on the house. Good year. The dandelions were thick that spring."

He put the bottle back. "When I'm gone, this is yours. Every bottle."

"Grandpa, don't talk like that."

"It's just talking. Talking doesn't make it true." He turned to face Benny. "But I need you to know. This cellar. This wine. It's not about drinking. It's about remembering. Every bottle is a year. A season. A memory."

He put his hand on Benny's shoulder. "You remember that."

Chapter 27: A Taste

Grandpa took a bottle from the back of the cellar. The label was handwritten, faded. 1952.

"This is the year your mother was born," he said. "I made it the week she came home from the hospital."

He pulled the cork. The smell rose out of the bottle. Yeast. Honey. Something floral that filled the small space.

He poured a finger's worth into a small glass and held it up to the light. The wine was golden. Almost clear. Tiny bubbles clung to the sides.

"Your first taste should be a good one," Grandpa said. He handed Benny the glass.

Benny held it. The glass was cool in his hand. The wine swirled when he moved it.

"Just a sip. Let it sit on your tongue."

Benny brought the glass to his lips. The wine touched his tongue. It was sweet at first. Then sharp. Then something else. Something he could not name. It tasted like sun on his skin. Like grass. Like Grandpa's hands working the petals.

He swallowed. The warmth spread down his chest.

"Well?" Grandpa asked.

"It tastes like dandelions."

Grandpa laughed. "That's the point."

He took the glass and finished the rest himself. He closed his eyes while he swallowed. When he opened them, they were wet.

"Your mother was a beautiful baby," he said. "She cried for three days straight. The loudest cry I ever heard. And I made this wine."

He put the cork back in and returned the bottle to the shelf.

Chapter 28: Clara's News

Clara found Benny at the creek. She sat beside him on the bank and threw a rock into the water.

"It's happening," she said. "Dad got the job. We're moving August fifteenth."

The date hung in the air. Benny counted in his head. Eighteen days. Less than three weeks. The summer was already more than half gone.

"Where in St. Louis?"

"South side. Near my aunt." She threw another rock. "It's a good job. Manager at a warehouse. Better pay than anything he could get here."

"You don't have to explain."

"I want to." She looked at him. "I want you to understand why I'm leaving. It's not because I want to."

"I know."

They sat without talking. The creek flowed past. A blue jay called from the trees. The water made its constant sound over the rocks.

"Eighteen days," Clara said.

"Eighteen days."

"We should do something with them."

"What?"

"Everything." She stood up. "We should do everything we haven't done."

"What haven't we done?"

She thought about it. "I don't know yet. But we'll find it."

She looked at the water. Then she looked at him. "We still have eighteen days."

"Eighteen days is a lot."

"It's not enough." She picked up another rock and threw it. "But it's what we have."

Chapter 29: The Promise

Benny stood at the fence between their yards. Clara stood on the other side. The sun was setting behind her, turning her hair into a dark silhouette.

"I promise I'll write," Benny said.

"Every week?"

"Every week. You too."

"I already said I would." She hooked her fingers through the chain link. "But writing isn't the same."

"I know."

"Promise me something else."

"Anything."

"Don't forget this summer." Her voice was quieter now. "When I'm gone and school starts and everything goes back to normal. Don't forget."

Benny looked at the fence between them. At her fingers hooked through the metal. At the way the light caught the edge of her face.

"I won't forget," he said.

She nodded. Then she turned and walked back to her house. Her silhouette got smaller against the white of the house. The door opened and closed.

Benny stayed at the fence until the lights came on in her window. Then he walked back to his own house and sat on the porch steps. The fence was still there. The window was lit. He counted the seconds until the light went out, but it stayed on. He counted to a hundred. The light did not go out. She was probably looking at her window too, wondering the same thing.

Chapter 30: The Fourth of July

The sky over the fairgrounds exploded with color. Red. White. Blue. Green. The rockets shot up with a whistle and burst into streaming tails of light.

Benny stood between his parents. His father had his arm around his mother. His mother was watching the sky with her mouth slightly open. For a moment, she was not worried. She was just watching.

Clara was somewhere in the crowd. Benny had seen her earlier. Now she was lost in the dark among the families and the picnic blankets.

Another rocket went up. Green. It scattered into a hundred points of light that faded as they fell.

Benny thought about Grandpa, who was too tired to come. He was at home, sitting on the porch, probably listening to the booms from a distance.

The finale came fast. Rockets firing one after another. The sky turned white with light. The ground shook. Children covered their ears.

Then it was over. The smoke drifted across the fairgrounds. The smell of gunpowder hung in the air.

People packed their blankets and headed for their cars. Benny looked for Clara in the crowd. He did not find her.

On the way home, he sat in the back of the truck and watched the stars. They were brighter than the fireworks. They did not fade.

Chapter 31: Sparklers

Benny found Clara at the edge of the parking lot. She was holding a handful of sparklers she had bought from a vendor.

"Here," she said, handing him one. "Light it."

He struck a match. The sparkler caught and hissed. The fire ran up the wire and burst into white light.

Clara lit hers. She held it out and moved it through the air, leaving trails of light that faded behind her.

"I'm going to write my name," she said.

She moved the sparkler in loops and lines. The light traced the letters C-L-A-R-A. They hung in the air for a second, then disappeared.

Benny held his sparkler up. He did not write anything. He just watched it burn.

"You want another one?" Clara asked.

"Yeah."

She handed him a fresh one. He lit it. The white fire bloomed again, hot and bright.

They stood together at the edge of the parking lot, writing nothing in the dark. The sparklers burned down to the wire. Clara dropped hers in the dirt and stepped on it. Benny did the same.

"One more?" she asked.

"One more."

They lit the last two at the same time. The light caught their faces, made them look older. Clara held hers up like a torch.

Chapter 32: Grandpa's Secret

Grandpa Tom called Benny into his room. The curtains were drawn. The room smelled of sickness and old blankets. Grandpa was propped up on pillows, smaller than Benny remembered.

"Sit down," Grandpa said. His voice was a whisper now.

Benny sat in the chair beside the bed.

"I'm going to tell you something," Grandpa said. "And I need you to hear it without falling apart."

Benny nodded.

"I'm dying. Not maybe. Not someday. Soon. The doctor says weeks."

Benny felt the words hit his chest. They sat there, heavy and cold.

"There's things I wanted to do that I didn't do," Grandpa went on. "But there's more that I did. I raised a family. I made wine. I watched the sun come up over this valley more mornings than I can count."

He reached for Benny's hand. His fingers were thin. The skin hung loose on the bone.

"I wanted to see you grow up. I wanted to teach you more things. But this is what we got."

Benny could not speak. His throat was closed.

"I need you to be strong," Grandpa said. "For your mother. For yourself. Can you do that?"

Benny nodded.

"Good." Grandpa closed his eyes. "Now let me rest."

Benny sat in the chair until Grandpa's breathing evened out. Then he left the room and closed the door behind him.

Chapter 33: Learning to Fish

The next morning, Grandpa was in the kitchen. He had put on his work shirt. He was holding his fishing pole.

"Let's go," he said. Before Benny could argue, Grandpa was out the door.

They walked to the creek. Grandpa moved slow. He stopped twice to catch his breath. But he did not turn back.

They sat on the bank where the water was deepest. Grandpa baited both hooks and cast them in. The lines drifted with the current.

"It used to be that I could sit here all day and not catch a thing and still call it a good day," Grandpa said. "You know why?"

"Why?"

"Because fishing is just an excuse to sit still. To watch the water. To think."

Benny watched his bobber. It floated on the surface, barely moving.

"The hard part," Grandpa said, "is knowing when to let things go. When to cut the line and start over."

"Are you talking about fishing?"

"I'm talking about everything."

A fish tugged Benny's line. He jerked too fast. The hook came up empty.

"You'll get the next one," Grandpa said.

They did not catch anything. But they stayed until the sun was high and Grandpa was too tired to sit up.

Chapter 34: The Photograph Album

Grandpa brought out the album after lunch. He set it on the kitchen table and opened it to the first page.

The photographs were black and white. The edges were curled. Some were stuck to the paper and peeling.

"That's your great-grandfather," Grandpa said, pointing at a man in a stiff collar. "He built this house. Two rooms and a roof. Took him a year."

Benny looked at the man. He had Grandpa's eyes. The same steady look.

"What was he like?"

"Hard. Quiet. He worked from sunup to sundown and never complained about it." Grandpa turned the page. "And that's me. Age seven."

The boy in the photograph stood in front of the same house. Same porch. Different paint. He held a fish in one hand and a stick in the other.

"You look like me," Grandpa said. "At the same age. Standing in the same place."

Benny looked at the photograph. Then at Grandpa. The same nose. The same way of standing. The same house behind them both, separated by years.

"This is yours," Grandpa said, pushing the album toward him. "Take it. Keep it. Add to it. When you're old, there will be a boy who looks like you and he will want to see where he came from."

Benny took the album. It was heavier than it looked.

Chapter 35: Everything Changes

The days started running together. Benny woke up and the sun was already high. He went to bed and the sun was still in his eyes.

He spent the mornings with Grandpa. They did not talk much. They sat on the porch or in the garden. Grandpa's cough was worse now. He used a handkerchief every few minutes.

The afternoons belonged to Clara. They went to the swimming hole. They climbed the water tower. They lay in the cornfield and watched the clouds. Everything they did felt like a last time, even if they did not say it.

But everything was different now. The end was coming. Benny could feel it in the way his mother looked at Grandpa. In the way Grandma kept the kitchen curtains open so Grandpa could see the garden from his bed.

Clara's house had boxes stacked in the front room. He could see them through the window. Her mother was packing dishes in newspaper. The stack grew taller every day.

Grandpa's bed was in the living room now. He could not climb the stairs anymore. Grandma had set up a cot beside him.

Benny moved through the days like a swimmer against a strong current. He was trying to hold on. But the current was stronger.

Chapter 36: The Garden

The garden needed weeding. Benny did it alone. Grandpa sat on the porch and watched.

The sun was hot. The weeds were stubborn. Benny pulled them from the roots and tossed them into a pile. The soil was dark and crumbly. He worked his way down the row, pulling and tossing.

"The tomatoes are coming in," Grandpa said from the porch. "Look at them."

Benny looked. The tomato plants were heavy with green fruit. Some were starting to turn red. The vines were thick and dark.

"We should make sauce this year," Grandpa said. "Your grandmother makes good sauce. She uses basil from the herb bed."

"Grandpa, can I ask you something?"

"Ask."

"Are you scared?"

The pause stretched. Grandpa did not answer for a long time. The chair creaked as he shifted. Then he said, "Yes. But being scared doesn't change anything. I was scared in the war. I was scared when your mother was born. Being scared just means you know what's at stake."

Benny pulled another weed. His hands were dirty. His back ached.

"The secret," Grandpa said, "is to keep moving. Even when you're scared. Especially then."

Benny nodded. He kept weeding. The pile of weeds grew. The row got cleaner. He kept moving.

Chapter 37: Last Swim

The swimming hole was empty when they got there. The water was warm and still. A dragonfly hovered over the surface, its wings catching the light.

Clara waded in without speaking. She went to the middle and floated on her back. Her hair spread out around her like dark threads on the water.

Benny went in after her. The water closed around him. He floated beside her, looking at the same sky.

"Tomorrow's the last day," Clara said.

"I know."

"I don't want to say goodbye."

"Then don't. We'll just stay here."

She laughed. It was a small laugh. It did not last long.

A cloud passed over the sun. The water turned dark for a moment, then bright again.

"Remember the first time we came here?" Clara asked.

"You dropped your bike in the grass."

"You fell going in. Slipped on a rock."

"I did not."

"You did. I saw it." She turned her head to look at him. "I knew then you were going to be my friend."

They floated a while longer. The dragonfly came back and hovered above them. The clouds moved slow.

Then the sun started to drop and they got out and put their clothes on over their wet suits.

Clara rode home without looking back. Benny watched her go.

Chapter 38: Goodbye to Clara

The moving truck came at eight in the morning. Benny watched from his window. The same truck that had brought them, now taking them away.

He went outside. Clara was standing by the car. Her mother was locking the front door of the empty house.

Benny walked to the fence. Clara saw him and came over.

"I brought you something," he said.

He handed her a Mason jar full of dandelion seeds. The white heads were packed tight. Hundreds of seeds. Ready to fly.

"Dandelion seeds," she said.

"Plant them wherever you go. They'll grow anywhere."

She held the jar. Her fingers wrapped around the glass. "I'll write you."

"Me too."

She put her hand through the fence. He took it. Her fingers were small and warm.

"I'm going to miss you," she said.

"Me too."

The truck honked. Clara let go of his hand. She walked to the car and got in without looking back.

The truck pulled away. The car followed. Benny watched until they disappeared around the bend. The dust from the road hung in the air for a while, then settled.

He stood at the fence for a long time. The grass was wet with morning dew. His feet got cold. He did not move until the dust was gone and the road was empty.

Chapter 39: The Empty House

The house next door was dark. No lights in the windows. No movement behind the glass. The curtains were gone. The rooms were bare.

Benny stood at the fence and looked at it. He had done this every evening since Clara left. The house did not change. But he kept looking.

Grandpa was too weak to come to the porch now. Benny sat alone. The rocking chair next to him was empty. It creaked when he pushed it, but nothing else moved.

The sun set. The light drained from the sky. The house next door became a dark shape against the dark.

Benny thought about Clara. He wondered if she was looking at the sky too. He wondered if she was thinking about him. He tried to imagine her room in St. Louis, the pot of dandelion seeds on the windowsill.

He wanted to send her a signal. A light. A fire. Something that would let her know he was here, thinking of her.

But there was no way to send it. She was gone.

He went inside. The house was quiet. Grandma was reading in the living room. Grandpa was asleep.

Benny went to his room and lay on his bed. The ceiling was a map of a place he had never been.

Chapter 40: Grandpa Fades

Grandpa stopped getting out of bed. The effort was too much. His breathing was shallow and fast. His skin had gone gray.

Benny sat with him in the afternoons. He read aloud from the newspaper. Grandpa did not always respond, but his eyes were open. He was listening.

The room smelled of sickness. Grandma kept the window cracked, but it did not help much. The curtains did not move. The air was still.

"Read me the recipe," Grandpa said one afternoon. His voice was barely there.

Benny got the notebook from the kitchen. He opened it to the first page.

"Take one gallon of dandelion blooms, picked at the full of the sun."

Grandpa's eyes closed. His breathing slowed.

"Add one gallon of boiling water. Let steep for three days."

Benny kept reading. He read the whole recipe. The words felt like prayer in the quiet room. When he finished, Grandpa's eyes were still closed. But his hand moved. He reached for Benny.

Benny took his hand. It was light as a bird. The bones were close to the surface.

"Good," Grandpa whispered. "You know it now."

He fell asleep. Benny stayed holding his hand until Grandma came in and told him it was time for dinner.

Chapter 41: The Final Batch

Benny made the wine alone. Grandpa told him the steps from his bed. Benny wrote them down and followed them.

He picked the dandelions at noon. The petals were wide open. The sun was high and hot. He filled a basket and brought them to the kitchen.

He washed them in the basin, the way Grandpa had shown him. He stripped the petals from the stems. His fingers were sore by the time he finished. He boiled the water. He added the sugar.

Grandma helped him lift the pot onto the stove. She did not say much. She just helped. Her hands were steady on the handles.

"I wish your grandpa could see this," she said.

"He knows."

"He does."

Benny poured the hot water over the petals. The yellow color bled into the liquid. The kitchen smelled like summer and sugar and something that was almost like hope.

He covered the pot with a cloth and set it on the counter.

"Three days," he said.

"Then what?"

"Then I strain it. Add the yeast. Start the fermentation."

Grandma nodded. "He taught you well."

Benny looked at the pot. The cloth was white, the liquid yellow. It was his batch now. His first one. He did not know if it would be any good. But he had followed the steps.

He wiped his hands on his pants and went to check on Grandpa.

Chapter 42: Waiting

The days were all the same now. Benny woke up. He checked the wine. He sat with Grandpa. He ate. He went to bed.

The house was quiet. Nobody laughed. Nobody raised their voice. The silence was thick as blankets.

Benny sat in the chair beside Grandpa's bed. He read. He watched. He waited. He counted Grandpa's breaths. Twenty per minute when he was resting. Faster when he was awake.

Grandpa's breathing changed sometimes. It got faster, then slower. Sometimes it stopped for a few seconds. Then started again.

Benny stopped being scared by it. It was just breathing. Grandpa was still there.

One afternoon, the mail came. A letter from Clara. Benny took it to his room.

_St. Louis is loud. The house is small. I planted the seeds in a pot on my windowsill. They are growing. I think about you. Write back._

Benny read the letter three times. He folded it and put it under his pillow.

He went back to Grandpa's room and sat down.

"Who was it?" Grandpa asked. His eyes were closed.

"Clara."

"Good." Grandpa's lips moved into something like a smile. "She's a good girl."

He fell asleep. Benny stayed in the chair.

Chapter 43: The Storm

The storm came at night. The wind woke Benny first. It rattled his window. Then came the rain. Then the thunder.

He got out of bed and went to the living room. Grandpa was awake. His eyes were open. The lightning lit up the room every few seconds.

"Big one," Grandpa said.

Benny sat on the floor beside the cot. "Can't sleep?"

"Never could during storms. Your grandmother used to say I was part dog."

The thunder rolled. The house shook. The rain was loud on the roof.

"Clara and I got caught in a storm," Benny said. "We hid in the barn."

"Good place to hide."

"It was. We sat in the loft and listened. The rain was so loud we could not hear ourselves talk."

Grandpa's hand found Benny's head. His fingers were cold against Benny's scalp.

"I'm glad she was here this summer," Grandpa said. "For you."

"Me too."

"Not everyone gets a summer like this. Most people go their whole lives without someone who sees them the way she sees you."

The thunder rolled again. The rain hammered the roof. Benny did not know what to say.

The storm moved on. The thunder got quieter. The rain softened. By midnight, it was over.

Benny stayed on the floor until Grandpa fell asleep.

Chapter 44: After the Storm

The morning after the storm was bright and clean. The sky was washed blue. The air was cool and smelled of wet earth.

Benny went outside. The garden was flattened. The tomato plants lay on their sides. The cornfield was a mess of broken stalks and mud.

He started fixing what he could. He propped up the tomatoes with stakes, driving them into the soft ground. He pulled the broken corn and made a pile for compost. The soil was dark and wet.

Grandma came out with a cup of coffee. She looked at the garden. Her face did not change.

"Your grandpa planted those tomatoes," she said. "April. Right after the last frost."

"I know."

"He'd want them to stand up."

Benny kept working. The sun got higher. The sweat ran down his back. He straightened the stakes. He tied the vines. He pulled the last of the broken corn.

When he went inside, Grandpa was worse. His breathing was ragged. His eyes were closed and did not open when Benny said his name.

Grandma was sitting beside him. She looked at Benny and shook her head. A small shake. Barely a movement.

Benny sat on the floor. He did not say anything. He just sat.

Chapter 45: The Last Lesson

Grandpa opened his eyes in the late afternoon. The light was golden through the curtains. It fell across the bed in a long rectangle. He turned his head and saw Benny.

"Still here," he said.

"Where would I go?"

Grandpa smiled. It was a small smile. It took effort.

"I want to tell you something," he said. "And I want you to remember it."

"I'm listening."

"Life is short. Shorter than you think. You look up one day and the summer is over and you are old." He paused to breathe. The breath rattled in his chest. "So you have to pay attention. To the small things. The dandelions. The storms. The girl next door."

Benny nodded. His eyes were wet.

"Pay attention," Grandpa said again. "That's all. Just pay attention."

"I will."

"Good. Because the days go fast. They will go faster as you get older. Faster than you can believe." He closed his eyes. His breathing slowed. "Now let me rest."

Benny sat in the chair. The light moved across the floor. The shadows grew longer. He watched the dust float in the golden air. He heard the clock ticking in the kitchen. He paid attention. He did not look away.

Chapter 46: Goodbye, Grandpa

Benny woke to his mother's crying. The sound came from the living room. It was a sound he had never heard her make. High and broken.

He knew before he got out of bed.

The living room was full of people. His mother. His father. Grandma. Dr. Miller. They stood around the cot.

Grandpa was not moving. His chest was still.

His mother saw Benny and opened her arms. He went to her. She held him. She was shaking.

Grandma sat in the chair. Her hands were folded in her lap. She was not crying. Her face was still. She looked smaller than she had yesterday.

"He went in his sleep," Dr. Miller said. "He did not suffer."

Benny pulled away from his mother. He walked to the cot. Grandpa's face was peaceful. The lines were gone. He looked young somehow. Like the photograph from 1944.

Benny touched his hand. It was cold.

"Goodbye, Grandpa," he said.

He stood there until Grandma took his hand and led him to the kitchen. She poured him a glass of milk.

"Drink," she said.

He drank. The milk was cold going down. It tasted like nothing. He drank it anyway, because Grandma had poured it.

Chapter 47: The Funeral

The funeral was at the church in town. The pews were half full. People Benny had never seen before. Old friends. Distant cousins. Men Grandpa had served with.

The minister spoke about heaven. About rest. About a life well lived. Benny did not listen. He watched the coffin. It was plain wood. Grandpa had built it himself, years ago. Benny remembered him working on it in the barn, planing the boards, checking the joints.

His mother cried through the whole service. His father held her hand. Grandma sat straight-backed, not crying, not speaking. Her hands were folded in her lap.

When it was over, they carried the coffin to the cemetery behind the church. The hole was already dug. The dirt was piled beside it.

The minister said more words. Benny did not hear them.

They lowered the coffin into the ground. The ropes creaked. The coffin settled at the bottom with a soft thud.

Someone handed Benny a handful of dirt. He looked at it. It was dark and crumbly. Like the soil in the garden. He threw it in. It hit the wood with a sound like rain.

He did not cry until they started filling the hole. Then he could not stop.

Chapter 48: The Wine is Ready

Three weeks after the funeral, Benny remembered the wine. He walked to the kitchen. The pot was still on the counter, covered with the cloth.

He lifted the cloth. The liquid was clear now. Golden. It smelled like Grandpa. Like the cellar. Like the bottle from 1952.

He found a funnel and a bottle. He siphoned the wine from the pot into the bottle. It was not hard. He had watched Grandpa do it a hundred times. The liquid moved through the tube, clear and bright.

The bottle filled. The wine was beautiful. Clear and gold. It caught the light from the window.

He fitted a cork and set the bottle on the counter.

"That's yours," Grandma said from the doorway. She had been watching.

"Yes, ma'am."

"What are you going to do with it?"

Benny looked at the bottle. The gold liquid caught the light.

"I'm going to save it," he said. "For a special occasion."

"Every day is a special occasion."

He looked at her. She smiled her small smile. The same smile she had worn through the funeral. Through everything.

"You want to try it?" he asked.

"Not yet. It needs to age a bit. Your grandpa would say two weeks minimum."

"Should I wait?"

"What do you think?"

Benny looked at the bottle. "I think I'll wait."

Grandma nodded. "Good boy. You were paying attention."

Chapter 49: Toasting

Two weeks passed. The bottle sat on the counter. Benny looked at it every morning.

On the first day of school, he opened it.

He poured two glasses. One for him. One for Grandpa.

He carried them to the porch. The sun was setting. The light was golden. The dandelion field was brown now. The flowers had gone to seed. The white heads bobbed in the breeze.

He sat in Grandpa's rocking chair. He set the second glass on the table beside him. The chair creaked under his weight.

He raised his glass. "To Grandpa."

He drank.

The wine was smooth. Sweeter than the drop he had tasted in the cellar. It went down warm. He swallowed and felt it spread through his chest.

He sat in the rocking chair and looked at the field. The seeds were waiting for next spring.

He finished the wine. He set the glass down.

Then he picked up Grandpa's glass and poured it back into the bottle. He was not ready to let it go. He corked the bottle and held it. He would save it for another day. Maybe Christmas. Maybe next summer. He did not know. He only knew he was not done with it yet.

Chapter 50: Dandelions

The next morning, Benny walked to the dandelion field. The plants were dry and brown. The seed heads were white puffballs, ready to scatter.

He picked one. He held it up to his mouth.

He thought about Grandpa. About Clara. About the summer.

He blew.

The seeds rose into the air. Hundreds of them. They caught the wind and drifted across the field. Some landed. Some kept going.

He picked another and blew again. Then another.

He watched the seeds float away. They were going everywhere. To the creek. To the road. To places he had never been. Some would land in good soil. Some would not. That was how it worked.

He thought about what Grandpa had said. Wine is memory in a bottle.

He had a basement full of memories. Two hundred and sixteen bottles of years. He had a letter from Clara under his pillow. He had a batch of his own wine on the counter. He had a summer inside him that would never leave.

He picked one more dandelion. He held it for a moment. Then he let it go.

The seeds rose. They caught the light. They disappeared into the blue.

Benny turned and walked back to the house. The screen door banged behind him. He heard Grandma in the kitchen. He smelled coffee.

He went inside. The summer was over. But he was still here.
