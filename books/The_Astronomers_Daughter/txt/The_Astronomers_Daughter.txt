Chapter 1: The Final Vigil

The candle had burned to a stub. Catherine Whitfield did not notice. She sat in the straight-backed chair beside her father's bed, watching his chest rise and fall in uneven rhythms. The house was silent except for the ticking of the longcase clock in the hall and the shallow rasp of Richard Whitfield's breathing.

He had been a large man once. The illness had reduced him to bone and shadow. His hands, which had guided telescopes and filled page after page with precise figures, lay still on the counterpane. The fingers were yellowed with ink stains that would never wash clean.

He opened his eyes. The grey-blue irises were clouded but still sharp.

"Catherine."

"I am here, Father."

He tried to lift his hand. She took it. His grip was faint.

"The calculations." His voice was a dry whisper. "In my study. The comet."

"Do not tire yourself."

"You must finish them." He coughed, a wet sound that shook his frame. "I made an error. I could not find it in time. The comet — it is not where they think it is."

She had heard about the comet. Her father had spoken of little else for months. Comet 1889b, discovered by a French priest, was expected to pass at a safe distance. Richard Whitfield had been one of the few to question the published orbit.

"The society does not agree," she said.

"The society is wrong." His eyes closed. "You must show them. You are the only one who can."

She did not argue. She had never argued with him. She had learned astronomy at his knee, had memorized the constellations before she could read, had calculated ephemerides for him since she was sixteen. But she was a woman. The Royal Society would not listen to her.

"I promise," she said.

He seemed to hear her. His breathing slowed. The grip on her hand loosened. The clock in the hall struck four.

By the time the candlestub guttered and died, Richard Whitfield was gone.

Chapter 2: The Study

Dawn came grey through the study windows. Catherine had not slept. She had remained in her father's room until the doctor came, until the women from the village arrived to lay him out, until there was nothing more for her to do.

Now she stood in the doorway of his study. She had not entered it since his illness confined him to his bed. The room smelled of tobacco, old paper, and brass polish.

The telescope stood by the window, its tube pointed at a shuttered sky. A mahogany case held the chronometer, its glass face reflecting the grey light. Books lined every wall — star catalogues, mathematical treatises, bound volumes of the Philosophical Transactions. On the desk, papers lay in careful stacks, weighted down with a brass hand that had belonged to her grandfather.

She touched the back of his chair. It was still warm from his body. This was absurd, she knew. He had been in bed for three weeks. But the room felt inhabited. His presence clung to the objects.

She sat in the chair. It was too large for her. She pulled the nearest stack of papers toward her. Her father's handwriting covered every page — dense, precise, the numbers formed with the care of a man who knew that a single digit could mean the difference between truth and error.

She found a half-finished letter on Royal Society letterhead. The handwriting grew looser toward the end.

"To the Secretary of the Royal Society — It is my considered opinion that the ephemeris published for Comet 1889b contains a systematic error in the perturbation terms. I have undertaken a full recalculation and have found — "

The sentence stopped. The pen had scratched through the paper.

Catherine set the letter aside. She opened the top drawer of the desk. It was crammed with notebooks. She took out the most recent one and opened it to the last entry.

The date was three weeks before his death. The entries after that were blank.

She began to read.

Chapter 3: The Undertakers

They arrived on the third day. A carriage from the Royal Society pulled up outside the Hampstead house at half past ten. Three men climbed out.

Professor Ambrose Huxley led them. He was tall and carried himself with the authority of a man accustomed to being listened to. His beard was silver, his suit impeccable. Behind him came two younger men — clerks from the Society, carrying empty crates.

Margaret Heston, Catherine's cousin, met them at the door. She was small and fierce, with red hair that she wore pinned tight.

"Miss Whitfield is not receiving visitors."

Huxley smiled without warmth. "I am not a visitor, Miss Heston. I am here on Society business. The instruments and papers of the late Richard Whitfield are the property of the Royal Society. I have come to collect them."

Catherine appeared at the top of the stairs. She had not slept. Her dress was the same one she had worn for three days.

"Professor Huxley."

"Catherine." He did not offer condolences. "I am sorry to intrude at such a time. But the Society's records must be complete. Your father's work — his observations, his calculations — they belong to science now."

"They belong to me."

Huxley's expression did not change. "My dear girl. You cannot possibly understand the material. It is highly technical. Celestial mechanics. Perturbation theory. These are not subjects for — "

"I understand them."

"With respect, you are a woman. Whatever assistance you provided your father — filing, copying — it does not qualify you to judge the content. The Society has a duty to preserve his legacy."

Catherine descended the stairs. She stopped two steps above Huxley, so that she looked down at him.

"You may see the study," she said. "You may take nothing from it."

Huxley's jaw tightened. The clerks shifted their weight.

"This is not a request," he said.

"The will has not been read. Until it is, everything in this house remains mine. You may call the constable if you wish. I am sure he will be interested to hear that the Royal Society is attempting to seize property before probate."

Huxley stared at her. For a moment, something flickered in his eyes — not anger, but surprise. He had not expected resistance.

"Very well," he said. "I will return when matters are properly settled."

He turned and walked out. The clerks followed.

Catherine stood on the stairs, trembling.

Chapter 4: The Will

The solicitor arrived the following afternoon. Mr. Finch was a small, precise man who had handled the Whitfield family's affairs for thirty years. He sat at the dining table with the will spread before him, his spectacles perched on his nose.

Catherine and Margaret sat across from him. Professor Huxley had sent word that he intended to attend. Catherine had written back: "The reading of my father's will is a private matter." Huxley had not come.

Mr. Finch cleared his throat.

"The last will and testament of Richard Stephen Whitfield, Fellow of the Royal Society, of Hampstead, London." He paused. "It is straightforward. The house and all its contents pass to his daughter, Catherine Anne Whitfield, absolutely."

Catherine closed her eyes. She had expected this, but hearing it made it real.

"There is a codicil," Mr. Finch said. He turned the page. "I, Richard Whitfield, being of sound mind, do direct my daughter to a certain safe concealed beneath the loose floorboard in my study. The combination is known only to her. The contents of the safe are for her eyes alone, and I instruct my executors to respect her privacy in this matter."

Margaret looked at Catherine. "A safe?"

"I did not know," Catherine said.

Mr. Finch folded the will. "There is one more matter. Professor Huxley has written to me. He intends to challenge the disposition of your father's scientific papers, claiming they belong to the Society by prior agreement. I have advised him that there is no such agreement on record. However, he may pursue the matter in court."

"Let him try," Catherine said.

She excused herself and went to the study. She knelt on the floor and pressed the boards. One gave slightly. She worked her fingernails into the crack and lifted.

Beneath was a small iron safe.

Chapter 5: The Safe

The combination was her birthday. Catherine spun the dial, felt the tumblers click, pulled the handle. The door swung open.

Inside, the safe contained three items.

The first was a leather-bound journal, thicker than the ones on her father's desk. She opened it. The first page was dated sixteen years earlier. It was his private research notebook — observations, speculations, calculations that had never been published.

The second item was a set of handwritten position tables for Comet 1889b. They covered a period of eighteen months, from the comet's first detection in France through its predicted return. At the bottom of the last page, her father had written: "These data do not match the published ephemeris. The discrepancy is real and increasing."

The third item was a letter, addressed to her in her father's hand.

She broke the seal.

"My Dearest Catherine —

If you are reading this, I am gone. I have kept this safe for you because I did not trust the post, nor my colleagues at the Society.

For six months I have been tracking Comet 1889b. The published orbit is wrong. I have checked and rechecked my observations. The comet is not following the predicted path. The perturbations have been miscalculated by someone — possibly by me, in my younger days when I first computed the elements.

I have attempted to bring this to the attention of the Society. Professor Huxley has dismissed my concerns.

I cannot finish this work. My strength fails. But you can. You have the mind for it. You have always had the mind for it.

Trust your calculations, Catherine. They will not lie to you.

Your loving father,
Richard Whitfield"

Catherine read the letter three times. Then she took the journal, the tables, and the letter, and she locked herself in the study.

Chapter 6: The Numbers

The days passed in a blur of figures. Catherine barely ate. She slept in the armchair in the study, waking to check a calculation, to turn a page, to stare at the numbers until her eyes burned.

Margaret brought trays of food that went untouched. She stood in the doorway and watched her cousin cover page after page in her precise hand.

"You will kill yourself," Margaret said.

"I will finish this."

The work was immense. Her father had tracked the comet through two hundred observations, each requiring correction for atmospheric refraction, for the Earth's motion, for the slight wobble of the telescope mount. The perturbation calculations filled pages of dense algebra.

Catherine checked every digit. She found errors — small ones, transcription mistakes that her father would never have made in health. She corrected them. The numbers began to sing.

On the sixth day, she found it.

A single transposition. In the perturbation calculation for Jupiter's gravitational influence, her father had written 0.8473 instead of 0.8437. The difference was 0.0036 arcseconds per day — negligible in a single measurement, catastrophic over eighteen months.

She recalculated the entire orbit with the corrected value. The numbers fell into place with a terrible elegance.

The comet was not going to pass safely. It was going to hit.

She worked through the night to determine when. Her answer came at dawn.

Eighty-four days. She had eighty-four days.

Chapter 7: The Library

Catherine needed reference tables. Her father's library was comprehensive, but some volumes had been borrowed and never returned. She needed the Berliner Astronomisches Jahrbuch for 1887 and 1888.

The British Museum Reading Room was the only place she could find them.

She took the omnibus to Bloomsbury. The great domed reading room swallowed her as she entered. She approached the counter and asked for the scientific catalogue.

The librarian was a thin man with a pince-nez. He looked at her without interest.

"The scientific stacks are restricted to fellows of learned societies and their accredited representatives."

"I am the daughter of Richard Whitfield. Fellow of the Royal Society."

"My condolences. The policy remains."

She felt her face grow hot. "I need only the Jahrbuch for 1887. It is a reference volume. It cannot be borrowed."

"The reading room provides general reference. For specialized materials, you must be accredited. Perhaps your father's executor could — "

"Miss Whitfield."

She turned. A young man stood behind her. He was perhaps thirty, with sandy hair and a kind face. He wore the badge of a Fellow of the Royal Society.

"Thomas Greaves," he said. "I was a colleague of your father's. May I be of assistance?"

She explained what she needed. He spoke to the librarian in low tones. The librarian nodded and fetched the volumes.

Thomas carried them to a table. "Your father spoke of you often. He said you had the best mathematical mind he had ever encountered."

"He was biased."

"He was not given to flattery." Thomas hesitated. "I heard that you are continuing his work on the comet."

"Who told you that?"

"No one. But I saw your father's notes before he died. He believed the ephemeris was wrong. I believed him. I just did not have the skill to prove it."

Catherine looked at him. "Do you want to see the proof?"

"I would like that very much."

Chapter 8: The Error

They met in the British Museum each day for a week. Thomas Greaves brought coffee from a stall outside. Catherine brought her father's notebooks and her own recalculations.

She spread the pages across the table in the reading room's quiet corner.

"The error is here," she said, tapping the number. "He wrote 0.8473. It should be 0.8437."

Thomas examined the figures. He took out his own slide rule and recalculated. His lips moved silently. He set the rule down.

"Good Lord."

"The true value of the perturbation changes the entire trajectory. The comet's perihelion is closer than calculated. Much closer."

"How close?"

Catherine turned to a fresh page. She had drawn the orbits — the published path in dashed lines, the corrected path in solid ink. The two curves diverged like a branching river.

"The comet will pass within forty thousand miles of Earth's surface. The published ephemeris predicts a miss of six hundred thousand miles."

"And the time of closest approach?"

"August. Late August." She did not mention the date of impact. She wanted him to reach the conclusion himself.

Thomas traced the orbit with his finger. "Wait." He looked up. "This brings it through the Earth's orbital plane here. At this point, if the timing is — "

"It is."

"The comet will strike."

"Yes."

Thomas sat back. The colour had drained from his face. "Have you told anyone?"

"I wrote to Professor Huxley. He did not respond."

"I am not surprised. Huxley has invested his reputation in the published ephemeris. To admit an error would be — " He stopped.

"It would be what?"

"It would undermine his authority. He is Astronomer Royal. He cannot afford to be wrong."

Catherine gathered her papers. "Then he will be wrong. And the city will pay for his pride."

Chapter 9: The Trajectory

Catherine worked through the night at home. The numbers were relentless.

She calculated the comet's mass from its brightness and distance. She estimated the impact energy based on velocity and angle of entry. She mapped the zones of destruction.

A direct hit on London would level the city. The fireball would be visible as far as Edinburgh. The shockwave would flatten buildings for miles. The tidal wave — if it struck the estuary — would flood the lowlands as far as Cambridge.

But the trajectory was not fixed. Small changes in the perturbation parameters shifted the impact point by degrees. The comet might hit the North Sea. It might hit France. It might, with a favorable deflection, miss entirely.

She had to know the exact orbit.

She gathered all the observation data she could find. Her father's records. The published positions from the French discoverer. A series of measurements from the Cape of Good Hope observatory that had never been correlated.

She plotted each position on a celestial chart. The points formed a curve — the comet's path against the fixed stars. She connected them, then compared the curve to the published ephemeris.

The divergence was clear. The comet was ahead of schedule. It was brighter than predicted. It was coming faster.

She calculated again. The new figures confirmed the old.

Impact. North Atlantic, between 48 and 52 degrees north. Date: August 23 to August 28, 1889. Probability: above ninety percent.

She closed the book and sat in the dark.

Outside, the first birds were singing. Dawn was coming. She had eight weeks.

Chapter 10: The Letter

Catherine wrote the letter three times.

The first was too emotional. She had wept as she wrote it, and the ink was smudged. She burned it.

The second was too technical. It assumed Huxley would engage with the mathematics. She knew he would not.

The third was precise and formal. She signed it "C. Whitfield" — an omission she justified as practical, not deceptive.

"Professor Huxley,

I write to inform you of a significant correction to the orbital elements of Comet 1889b. My father, Richard Whitfield, identified a discrepancy in the perturbation calculation before his death. I have completed his work and confirmed the error.

The comet's true trajectory brings it within 40,000 miles of Earth on August 26, 1889. The probability of impact with the North Atlantic seaboard exceeds ninety percent.

I have enclosed the full recalculation. I request that the Society verify my figures and take appropriate action.

I am available to present my work before the Society at your convenience.

Yours faithfully,
C. Whitfield"

She sealed the envelope and walked to the post office herself. The clerk weighed it, stamped it, dropped it into the sack.

Catherine stood on the street for a long moment. The letter was gone. There was no taking it back.

She walked home through the grey streets of London. Carriages clattered past. Street vendors called their wares. No one looked at her. No one knew that the world was about to end.

She had done what she could. Now she waited.

Chapter 11: The Response

The reply came in five days.

Catherine recognized the seal of the Royal Society. She broke it with shaking hands. The letter was brief.

"Dear Miss Whitfield,

The Royal Society acknowledges receipt of your communication regarding Comet 1889b.

The calculations of the late Richard Whitfield FRS were made during a period of ill health. The Society's published ephemeris has been verified independently and requires no correction.

Your concern is noted. Your continued interest in astronomical matters, while unconventional for a person of your sex, reflects creditably on your filial devotion. However, I must advise you that celestial mechanics is a specialized field requiring years of formal training. It would be best for all concerned if you were to leave these matters to those qualified to address them.

I trust this settles the matter.

Yours,
Prof. Ambrose Huxley
Astronomer Royal"

Catherine read the letter twice. The condescension was deliberate. He had not even looked at her calculations.

She folded the letter and placed it in the drawer of her father's desk. Then she sat down and began to plan her next move.

She would not go quietly. She had made a promise to a dying man. She had discovered a truth that might save thousands of lives.

If Huxley would not listen, she would find someone who would.

Chapter 12: The Ally

Thomas Greaves arrived at the Hampstead house on a Sunday afternoon. He was out of breath, as if he had run from the station.

"I read the letter," he said.

"Which letter?"

"The one Huxley returned. He left it on the common table in the Society's reading room. For the amusement of the fellows, I imagine."

Catherine felt her face grow hot. "He made a spectacle of me."

"He made a spectacle of himself." Thomas pulled a folded paper from his pocket. "I copied your calculations before he discarded them. I have been checking them."

"And?"

Thomas smiled. "They are correct. Every figure. I have recalculated the perturbations three times. The comet will strike."

Catherine led him into the study. She showed him her charts, her timeline, her impact estimates. He examined each one in silence.

"What do you need?" he asked.

"Access to a proper telescope. The photographic equipment at Greenwich. Someone to present my work to the Society, since they will not hear it from a woman."

"I can arrange the first two. The third — I will present it myself. I am a fellow. I have the right to speak at meetings."

"They will destroy your career."

"They will try." Thomas looked at her. "Your father was my mentor. He taught me that truth is not a matter of convenience. If I abandon that now, I am no scientist at all."

Catherine did not know what to say. She had been alone in this fight for so long. The presence of an ally was almost more than she could bear.

"Thank you," she said.

Thomas nodded. "We begin tomorrow. Be ready at midnight. I will take you to Greenwich."

Chapter 13: The Observatory

Midnight. Catherine stood at her bedroom window, watching for Thomas's signal. A lantern blinked twice in the darkness below.

She put on the clothes she had prepared — her father's trousers, shirt, and coat, altered to fit her. She pinned her hair under a cap. In the dim light of her room, she looked like a young man.

Margaret had helped her dress. "If anyone asks, you are my brother's son, visiting from the country. A student of natural philosophy."

"Will they believe it?"

"Men believe what they expect to see."

They went out through the kitchen door. Thomas waited by the garden gate. He looked at her disguise and nodded.

"Good enough. Keep your head down. Do not speak unless you must."

They walked through the empty streets toward Greenwich. The stars were bright overhead. Catherine knew their names and distances.

The observatory stood on the hill, its dark silhouette against the sky. The great dome of the Meridian Telescope was closed against the night air. Thomas produced a key and unlocked a side door.

Inside, the observatory was cold and smelled of machinery. Thomas lit a gas lamp. The telescope rose in the centre of the room, a brass and steel giant, its tube as long as a carriage.

"The porter is paid to look the other way," Thomas said. "We have until four."

Catherine climbed the steps to the observing platform. She touched the telescope's brass fittings. Her father had used this instrument. He had shown her how to align it, how to read the circles, how to track a moving object across the sky.

"Where do I point it?" Thomas asked.

Catherine gave him the coordinates. The telescope swung silently. She put her eye to the eyepiece.

The stars swam into focus. She adjusted the fine adjustment. And there it was.

Comet 1889b. A bright nucleus, a diffuse coma, a tail beginning to form. Exactly where her calculations had placed it. Exactly where the ephemeris said it could not be.

She had proof.

Chapter 14: The Photograph

The astrographic camera was a new addition to the observatory — a gift from a wealthy patron who believed in photography's future in astronomy. Thomas had learned to use it under the previous Astronomer Royal.

He set up the instrument while Catherine kept watch at the telescope. The camera attached to the telescope's eyepiece like a metal box full of glass plates and chemicals.

"The exposure needs to be long," Thomas said. "Twenty minutes at least. The comet is faint."

"Then we need twenty minutes."

Thomas loaded a plate into the camera. He checked the focus, the tracking mechanism, the clock drive. He pulled the shutter slide.

"Beginning exposure. Mark the time."

Catherine noted the time in her father's journal. They stood in silence as the minutes passed.

The observatory creaked. The gas lamp hissed. Somewhere in the building, a door banged. Catherine held her breath.

Twenty minutes. Thomas closed the shutter.

"Done. Now we develop it."

He carried the plate to the darkroom — a closet off the main chamber, fitted with red glass over the lamp. Catherine followed. Thomas worked in the dim light, sliding the plate into the developing bath. The chemicals hissed.

The image appeared slowly.

First the background — a field of stars, sharp and clear. Then the brighter objects, points of light that marked the constellations. And then, in the centre of the plate, a smudge. A blur. A patch of light that was not a star.

The comet.

Thomas lifted the plate from the bath. Water dripped from its surface. He held it up to the red light.

"There it is."

Catherine stared at the image. The comet was real. It was exactly where her calculations said it would be.

She had proof. Incontrovertible, photographic proof.

Chapter 15: The Darkroom

Catherine measured the plate herself.

The darkroom was cramped and hot. The smell of chemicals clung to her clothes and hair. She worked by the red light, using a micrometer to measure the positions of the comet relative to the surrounding stars.

She identified reference stars from the catalogue. She noted their coordinates. She measured the distance from each star to the comet's nucleus. She calculated the comet's position by triangulation.

The numbers matched her predictions exactly.

She checked again. Same result.

Outside, Thomas paced. "How long?"

"Patience."

She made a third measurement. It agreed with the first two. The comet's position was where her father's corrected orbit said it should be, and nowhere near the published ephemeris.

She emerged from the darkroom, carrying the wet plate carefully in both hands.

"It is done."

Thomas looked at her. "You look like you have seen a ghost."

"I have seen the end of the world."

They washed the plate in fresh water and set it to dry. Catherine copied her measurements into the journal. She drew a small diagram of the starfield, marking the comet's position.

By the time they finished, the sky was turning grey. Dawn was coming. They had to leave before the morning staff arrived.

Thomas cleaned the equipment. Catherine wrapped the photographic plate in a cloth and tucked it under her coat.

"We will need more observations," she said. "Multiple nights. To confirm the motion."

"I will arrange it."

They slipped out of the observatory as the sun rose. The streets were empty. Catherine walked home in her father's clothes, carrying the proof of her discovery.

No one stopped her. No one looked twice. She was just a young man walking home at dawn.

Chapter 16: The Return

A constable stopped her at the corner of Heath Street.

It was half past five. The streets were empty. The constable was a large man with a florid face and a moustache that drooped over his lip.

"You there. What is your business at this hour?"

Catherine's heart seized. She kept her head down. "I am a laboratory assistant at the college, sir. I have been working through the night."

"The college, is it?" The constable looked her up and down. "Which college?"

"King's. In the Strand."

"You are out early. Or late, depending on how you look at it."

"I am expected home before my mother wakes. She worries."

The constable grunted. He was not convinced. He was about to ask another question when Thomas appeared around the corner, walking quickly.

"Ah, there you are." Thomas sounded cheerful, unconcerned. "I thought you would have reached home by now. Good morning, constable. Is there a problem?"

The constable relaxed slightly. "No problem, sir. Just checking."

"Good man. Always vigilant. This is my assistant — we have been at the observatory all night. A research project. Very important."

The constable nodded and moved on. Thomas took Catherine's arm and walked her to the gate.

"That was close."

"Too close."

"You handled it well. The quick thinking about your mother."

Catherine did not answer. She was trembling. The encounter had rattled her more than she wanted to admit. She was a woman alone, dressed as a man, carrying evidence of a catastrophe that no one would believe.

She had to be more careful.

She went inside and locked the door behind her. Margaret was waiting in the kitchen. Catherine told her nothing about the constable. She did not want to hear the fear in her cousin's voice.

She went to the study and placed the photographic plate in the safe.

Chapter 17: The Paper

Catherine wrote the paper in three days.

She did not sleep. She did not eat properly. Margaret brought her tea and bread and took away the cold, untouched plates.

The paper was titled "On the Perturbed Orbit of Comet 1889b and a Predicted Terrestrial Encounter." She wrote it in the style of the Philosophical Transactions — formal, impersonal, every claim supported by mathematics.

She described her father's original observations. She identified the error in the perturbation calculation. She presented her corrected orbit and the evidence for it. She showed the photographic plate measurement. She stated her conclusion: the comet would strike the Earth in late August.

She did not mention her gender. She signed it "Catherine Whitfield" — the first time she had ever put her name to scientific work.

Thomas read it and made small corrections. "The mathematics is sound. The argument is clear. It is a good paper."

"It is a terrifying paper."

"Yes." He folded it and put it in his pocket. "I will present it at the next meeting of the Royal Society. Thursday next."

"They will ask who wrote it."

"I will tell them the truth. That the work was done by Catherine Whitfield, daughter of Richard Whitfield, and that her calculations have been verified by independent observation."

"They will laugh."

"They might. But they will have to read it first." Thomas smiled, but the smile did not reach his eyes. "And once they read it, they will have to answer it. You cannot laugh away mathematics."

Catherine watched him leave. She stood at the window and watched his figure disappear down the street.

Thursday next. Four days. She had four days before her work was made public.

She went back to the study. There was still work to do. The comet did not stop moving while London slept.

Chapter 18: The Presentation

The Royal Society met on Thursday evening in their rooms at Burlington House. Catherine was not permitted to attend. She waited at home with Margaret, watching the clock.

Thomas had promised to come directly after the meeting. She had asked him to bring news, whatever it was.

The hours passed slowly. Margaret tried to read. Catherine paced. The study clock ticked.

At half past ten, a cab pulled up outside. Catherine ran to the door.

Thomas stood on the step. His coat was wet with rain. His face was grey.

"Tell me."

He came inside. Margaret took his coat. He sat down heavily in the hall chair.

"I read your paper to them," he said. "I explained the calculations. I showed the photographic evidence."

"What did they say?"

"At first, silence. Then Huxley stood up. He said the paper was — he used the word 'imaginative.' He said that a woman's sensibilities, combined with grief, could produce such a conclusion. That it was not science but hysteria."

"The mathematics — "

"They did not engage with the mathematics. They do not know how. Huxley has the authority. The rest follow him."

Catherine sat down slowly. She had expected this. She had prepared for it. But the reality of the dismissal was a physical weight.

"Then it is over."

"No." Thomas looked at her. "Something happened at the end. A young fellow — Langham, from Cambridge — asked to see the photographic plate. I showed him. He examined it with a magnifier for a long time. Then he said: 'The position is exactly where Miss Whitfield predicts it. How do you account for that, Professor Huxley?'"

Thomas smiled. "Huxley did not have an answer. The meeting ended in disorder. Langham asked for a copy of your paper. He is going to examine the figures himself."

One voice. One scientist willing to look.

It was not enough. But it was a beginning.

Chapter 19: The Refusal

Huxley came to Hampstead the following morning.

Catherine was in the study when Margaret announced him. She considered refusing. But she knew that would look like fear. She told Margaret to show him in.

Huxley stood in the doorway, hat in hand. His face was composed, but she saw the tightness around his eyes.

"Miss Whitfield. I have come to speak with you directly."

"About my paper?"

"About your welfare." He stepped into the room. "I understand that the past weeks have been difficult. Your father's death. The confusion of his affairs. The natural desire to continue his work."

"I am not confused."

"Allow me to finish. I have known you since you were a child. I watched you grow up in this house, surrounded by books and instruments. It is natural that you should imagine yourself capable of — "

"I am capable."

"You are a woman." Huxley's voice hardened. "Whatever assistance you provided your father, it did not prepare you for the work of celestial mechanics. The equations you have presented contain fundamental errors that any trained mathematician would recognize."

"Name one."

He hesitated. "I have not examined them in detail."

"Then you do not know that they contain errors."

Huxley's jaw tightened. "Miss Whitfield, I am trying to be kind. The alternative is to have your work formally rejected by the Society, published as a cautionary example of amateur delusion. I am offering you a quiet retreat. Take it."

Catherine stood. She was shorter than him, but she did not feel small.

"I will not retreat. I will not be silent. And I will prove that I am right."

Huxley stared at her. For a moment, she saw something in his eyes — not anger, but something like fear.

"Very well," he said. "You leave me no choice."

He turned and left. The front door closed behind him.

Catherine stood in the study, her hands shaking. She had just declared war on the most powerful scientist in England.

Chapter 20: The Society of Amateurs

Margaret brought the letter at breakfast. It was from the British Astronomical Association.

"I wrote to them," Margaret said. "After the hearing. I thought they might help."

Catherine opened the letter. It was signed by the association's secretary, a Mrs. Elizabeth Brown.

"Dear Miss Whitfield,

I have followed your work with great interest. Several of our members have independently noted anomalies in the behavior of Comet 1889b. Your calculations seem to explain them.

We would be honored if you would share your findings with our society. Our next meeting is Tuesday. I have taken the liberty of reserving a place for you on the agenda.

We are not the Royal Society. We are amateurs, hobbyists, and — in many cases — women. But we have telescopes and we know how to use them.

Yours in science,
Elizabeth Brown"

Catherine read the letter three times. Amateurs. Hobbyists. Women. The terms that the Royal Society used as insults.

"Will you go?" Margaret asked.

"Yes. I will go."

The BAA met in a small hall near the British Museum. Catherine was nervous as she entered. She found herself among people who looked at her not with suspicion but with interest.

Mrs. Brown was a stout woman in her fifties, with grey hair pinned under a hat. She shook Catherine's hand firmly.

"Your father was a member, you know. He spoke at our meeting in '86. He said that the best science often comes from outside the establishment."

Catherine felt a lump in her throat. "He never told me."

"He would have." Mrs. Brown smiled. "Now. Let us see your comet."

Catherine presented her work. The amateurs listened. They asked questions. They understood.

When she finished, there was applause. Genuine, warm applause.

She was not alone after all.

Chapter 21: The Correspondence

The letters arrived in a flood.

Within a week of her BAA presentation, Catherine received responses from amateur astronomers across England and Europe.

A clergyman in York had been measuring Comet 1889b's position for three months. His figures showed the same anomaly. "I thought my eyes were failing," he wrote. "Or my telescope. I am relieved it is neither."

A schoolmaster in Edinburgh had independently calculated a collision course. "I did not publish because I could not believe my own results. Your work confirms them."

A woman in Paris — Madame Lefevre, a wealthy widow with a private observatory — had reached the same conclusion. "The comet will strike," she wrote. "I have told the Paris Observatory. They dismissed me. They always do."

Catherine answered every letter. She compared data. She shared calculations. She built a network of observers who trusted each other, even if the establishment did not.

The comet grew brighter in the dawn sky. It was now visible to the naked eye — a pale smudge in the east before sunrise.

People were beginning to notice. Newspaper articles appeared, asking questions. The Royal Society issued reassurances. The comet was harmless. The experts were certain.

But the experts were not watching the sky. Catherine and her amateurs were.

And what they saw made them afraid.

Chapter 22: The Press

Lord Balfour's carriage arrived on a Tuesday morning. The footman delivered a handwritten note.

"Miss Whitfield — I have heard of your work. I would like to publish your account in the Morning Chronicle. Please call on me at your earliest convenience. — Balfour."

Margaret read the note over Catherine's shoulder. "He is the proprietor. The man who owns half the newspapers in London."

"I know who he is."

"He is also dangerous. He will use you."

"He might also help me." Catherine folded the note. "No one else will."

The Balfour townhouse was in Mayfair. Catherine was shown into a study lined with books and hunting prints. Lord Balfour was a small man with a large presence. He rose when she entered.

"Miss Whitfield. Thank you for coming."

"I have not agreed to anything yet."

"Of course not." He gestured to a chair. "Sit, please. I will be frank. I have sources in the Royal Society. I know what you have discovered. I know that Huxley has suppressed it."

"Then you know more than most."

"I also know that you are right. I have had your calculations checked by a mathematician I employ. He tells me they are sound." Balfour sat across from her. "The comet is going to strike. And no one in authority will say so."

Catherine studied him. "What do you want?"

"A story. The truth. A warning that might save lives." He leaned forward. "I also want to embarrass the Royal Society. They have been obstructing my commercial interests for years. But that is secondary. The primary goal is the warning."

"If I agree, I will not be controlled."

"You will have full editorial control of any article bearing your name."

Catherine considered. She did not trust him. But she needed a voice. The newspapers were the only channel left.

"Very well," she said. "But I write the article. And I approve every word."

Balfour smiled. "I would not have it any other way."

Chapter 23: The Article

The article appeared on Saturday morning.

"A Woman's Warning: Comet to Strike England?" The headline spanned three columns. Beneath it, Catherine's name. Beneath her name, the full text of her findings.

She had written it for a general audience. No equations. No jargon. Just the story of a comet that was not where it was supposed to be, and the woman who had discovered the truth.

"On the night of June 3, 1889," the article began, "I stood at the Greenwich Observatory and photographed a comet. The image I captured shows that comet in a position where, according to the Royal Society, it cannot possibly be.

But it is there.

I have calculated its orbit. I have checked my figures. I have had them verified by independent observers. The comet will strike the Earth in late August. The impact will be catastrophic.

I have tried to warn the Royal Society. They have refused to listen. I am now warning the public, because the public has a right to know."

The response was immediate.

By midday, the Chronicle's offices were flooded with letters. By evening, the article was being reprinted in provincial papers. By Monday, it had reached Paris and Berlin.

Catherine's name was on every lip. She was a hero to some, a hysteric to others. But she was no longer invisible.

The comet was coming. And now, everyone knew it.

Chapter 24: The Backlash

Huxley's response appeared in The Times on Monday.

"Science is not conducted in the popular press," he wrote. "The claims made by Miss Whitfield are the product of grief, inexperience, and a tragic misunderstanding of complex mathematics. The Royal Society's published ephemeris for Comet 1889b has been verified by the leading astronomers of Europe. It is correct.

Miss Whitfield is to be pitied, not heeded. Her father's death has unhinged her judgment. She does not know what she is saying.

I urge the public to remain calm. The comet will pass at a safe distance. There is no danger."

Other papers joined the attack. The Daily Telegraph called Catherine "an hysterical female trading on her father's reputation." The Standard questioned her moral character. A cartoon in Punch showed a woman in a nightgown pointing a telescope at a teapot.

Catherine read them all. She cut them out and filed them in a drawer.

Margaret tried to take the papers away. "You should not read this poison."

"I need to know what they are saying. It tells me how afraid they are."

"Afraid?"

"Huxley would not respond if he were confident. He would ignore me. The fact that he is attacking means my work frightens him."

Margaret was not convinced. But she stopped trying to hide the papers.

Catherine continued her observations. The comet was growing brighter every night. Its tail was lengthening. It was coming.

And no amount of angry letters could change that.

Chapter 25: The Invitation

Lord Balfour's second invitation came in a gilt-edged envelope. "Dinner. Eight o'clock. I have something to show you."

Catherine went. She was curious despite herself.

The dining room was magnificent. Crystal and silver gleamed in the candlelight. Balfour sat at the head of the table, a single guest beside him — a woman in her fifties, grey-haired and sharp-eyed.

"Miss Whitfield, may I present Madame Lefevre."

The French astronomer. Catherine stared. "You are in London?"

"I arrived this morning." Madame Lefevre spoke English with a heavy accent. "I came to see you. Lord Balfour was kind enough to arrange it."

Over dinner, they talked of the comet. Madame Lefevre had brought her own observations — months of data from her private observatory in Normandy. She spread the charts across the table.

"My figures match yours exactly. The comet will strike. I have written to the Paris Observatory three times. They do not answer."

"The Royal Society does not answer me either."

"Then we must make them answer." Madame Lefevre looked at Balfour. "Your newspapers can apply pressure. You can make it impossible for them to remain silent."

Balfour nodded. "I have already begun. Tomorrow's editorial will call for a parliamentary inquiry."

Catherine felt a strange sensation — something she had not felt in weeks. Hope.

For the first time, she was not fighting alone.

Chapter 26: The Observatory

Balfour's money bought an old stable in Highgate and turned it into an observatory.

It was not grand. The telescope was a six-inch refractor, second-hand but serviceable. The dome was a wooden frame covered in canvas. The floor was dirt.

But it was hers.

Alice Marpole, her new assistant, was a young woman from the BAA. She was quiet and competent, with a gift for precise measurement. She had been working as a governess until Mrs. Brown recommended her for this position.

"Have you ever operated a telescope?" Catherine asked.

"A six-inch? No. But I learn quickly."

They set up the instrument together. They aligned the finder scope. They calibrated the setting circles. They tested the clock drive.

On the first clear night, Catherine turned the telescope toward the comet. It was now a spectacular object — a bright nucleus surrounded by a glowing coma, with a tail that stretched across several degrees of sky.

Alice gasped when she saw it. "It is beautiful."

"It is terrible. Every night, it grows brighter. It is warming as it approaches the sun. The closer it gets, the faster it moves."

"Will we see the impact?"

"From here? We will see the flash. We will feel the shockwave. If we are close enough, we will not see anything at all."

Alice was silent for a long moment. Then she said: "Then we should record as much as we can. For those who will come after."

Catherine looked at her young assistant with new respect.

"Yes. That is exactly what we should do."

Chapter 27: The Tail

The comet's spectrum told a story.

Catherine attached the pocket spectroscope to the telescope eyepiece. She adjusted the slit. She looked.

The spectrum was crossed by bright lines — emission lines, the signature of hot gas. Sodium. Ionized calcium. The band of carbon molecules. The comet was shedding material as the sun's heat vaporized its surface.

She made careful drawings of the spectrum. She estimated the relative intensities of the lines. She calculated the rate of mass loss.

The comet was larger than she had thought. And it was losing mass faster than her original model predicted.

That changed everything.

She recalculated the orbit, incorporating the new data. The comet's mass loss affected its trajectory — each outburst of gas acted like a tiny rocket, pushing it slightly off course.

The change was small. But over weeks, it accumulated.

She worked through the night. By dawn, she had the new numbers.

The impact was still certain. But the timing had shifted. The comet would arrive three days earlier than her original prediction.

August 23, not August 26.

She sent word to Thomas at the Royal Society. She wrote to Madame Lefevre. She telegraphed the clergyman in York and the schoolmaster in Edinburgh.

The warning had to be updated. The clock was moving faster.

Chapter 28: The Expedition

The letter from Algeria arrived on a Tuesday.

It was from Father Moreau, a Jesuit astronomer who had observed Comet 1889b from the mission observatory in Algiers. He had heard of Catherine's work through the BAA network.

"I observed the comet in January of this year," he wrote. "My measurements showed it was ahead of the published ephemeris by a significant margin. I wrote to the Royal Society. I received no reply.

I am now seventy-three years old and not long for this world. I do not care about my reputation. I care about the truth.

My observation books are enclosed. Use them as you see fit."

Catherine opened the parcel. Inside were three notebooks, filled with Father Moreau's careful observations. He had measured the comet's position on thirty-seven nights over three months.

She compared his data to her own. The two sets were separated by five months and thousands of miles. They should have shown a consistent trajectory.

They did. His positions connected to hers with a smooth curve — the comet's true path through the solar system.

But the published ephemeris showed a different curve. The two paths diverged like roads leading to different cities.

One led to a safe passage. The other led to destruction.

Father Moreau's data confirmed what Catherine already knew. But it was more than confirmation. It was proof that the error was not local, not recent, not a fluke.

The comet had been on a collision course since before anyone noticed.

Chapter 29: The Committee

The Royal Society announced the formation of a special committee to investigate Catherine's claims.

The press called it a victory for her cause. Catherine knew better. Huxley chaired the committee. He had chosen every member.

Thomas Greaves was included as the most junior member. "A token," he said when he came to tell her. "They want to be able to say that my views were represented."

"Can you convince them?"

"I can try. But Huxley has stacked the committee with his allies. Men who owe their positions to him. Men who have built their careers on the same methods he used."

"Then it is a farce."

"It is a formality. They will conduct their investigation, they will produce a report, and they will conclude that your work is unsound. The public will be reassured. The matter will be closed."

Catherine sat down. She had known this was coming. But hearing it spoken aloud made it real.

"How long will they take?"

"Three weeks. They have asked for all your data to be submitted to the committee secretary. They will examine it at their leisure."

"Three weeks. The comet arrives in six."

"Then we must find another way." Thomas looked at her. "Is there anyone else? Anyone with enough authority to challenge Huxley?"

Catherine thought. There was one person. A man her father had respected above all others. A German astronomer who had retired to a village in the Alps.

Professor Karl Weber. The greatest living celestial mechanic.

If he could be convinced — if he would look at her work — his word would carry weight that even Huxley could not dismiss.

She wrote the letter that night.

Chapter 30: The Hearing

The committee hearing was held in the Royal Society's grand meeting room. Catherine was allowed to attend but not to speak. She sat in the visitors' gallery, looking down at the men who would judge her work.

Thomas presented her evidence. The photographic plate. The corrected calculations. The Algerian data. The amateur observations. The spectrum analysis.

He spoke for three hours. He did not falter.

When he finished, Huxley rose.

"Mr. Greaves has presented a compelling narrative. But narrative is not science. Let us examine the facts."

Huxley produced a stack of papers. He had prepared his own calculations — an analysis that purported to show errors in Catherine's work.

"The perturbation correction that Miss Whitfield claims to have discovered is based on a misreading of her father's notes. The true value is 0.8473, as originally published. The corrected value of 0.8437 produces an orbit that does not match the observed positions of the comet."

"That is false," Thomas said. "I have shown you the photographic evidence."

"You have shown me a blur on a plate. It could be anything. A defect in the emulsion. A reflection in the optics. A deliberate — "

He stopped. He had been about to say "fabrication."

The room was silent.

Catherine stood up. From the gallery, she spoke: "Professor Huxley. You have not checked my figures. If you had, you would know they are correct."

The committee members turned to look at her. A woman speaking from the gallery was a breach of protocol.

Huxley's face reddened. "Miss Whitfield. You will be silent or you will be removed."

"I will be silent when the truth is told. Not before."

The committee chairman called for order. Catherine sat down.

But she had made her point. Huxley had hesitated. He had almost accused her of fraud. The room had seen it.

The hearing adjourned in confusion.

Chapter 31: The Verdict

The committee's report was published on a Monday.

"After careful examination of the evidence presented by Miss Catherine Whitfield, the committee finds that the calculations contain errors inconsistent with the established methods of celestial mechanics. Her predicted orbit for Comet 1889b is not supported by reliable observation.

The committee concludes that the published ephemeris remains the best available representation of the comet's trajectory. No danger to Earth exists.

The committee recommends that no further official resources be devoted to this matter."

Thomas Greaves had dissented. His dissenting opinion was appended to the report.

"I find that Miss Whitfield's calculations are sound, her observations are accurate, and her conclusions are justified by the evidence. The committee's refusal to engage seriously with her work is a failure of scientific method and a dereliction of duty."

His name was the only one on the dissent.

Catherine read the report in her study. She had expected it. She had known it was coming. But the official rejection was a blow she had not fully prepared for.

Margaret found her sitting in the dark.

"What do we do now?"

"We keep working." Catherine's voice was steady. "The comet does not care what the committee thinks."

She went back to her telescope. The comet was bright enough now to see in broad daylight, if you knew where to look. A pale ghost in the blue sky.

The public was starting to panic. The official reassurances rang hollow when the evidence was visible overhead.

Catherine kept measuring. She kept calculating. Whatever the committee said, the truth was in the numbers.

Chapter 32: The Eclipse

The partial solar eclipse of June 17 was a gift.

London gathered in parks and squares, looking up through smoked glass. Catherine had set up her telescope on the roof of the Highgate observatory. Alice stood beside her, ready with the photographic plates.

The moon began to cross the sun. Light faded to a strange twilight. Birds stopped singing. The air grew cold.

And there, beside the black disc of the moon, was the comet.

It was visible in the darkened sky — a bright star with a faint tail, hanging next to the eclipsed sun. People in the streets cried out. They had been told the comet was harmless. They had been told it was far away.

But there it was, visible in daylight, close enough to touch.

"Photograph it," Catherine said. "Quickly."

Alice worked the camera. The exposure was short — the comet was bright enough now that seconds sufficed. Plate after plate captured the image.

Below, in the streets, a wave of fear was spreading. Catherine heard the sound of shouting, of running feet. Someone was screaming.

The eclipse passed. The sun reappeared. The comet faded from view.

But everyone had seen it. The secret was out.

By evening, the newspapers had the story. "Comet Visible During Eclipse — Public Panic." The Royal Society issued another statement. "The comet's visibility during the eclipse is consistent with our predictions. There is no cause for alarm."

No one believed them anymore.

Chapter 33: The Mayor

The Lord Mayor's summons came by special messenger.

Catherine dressed carefully. She took her papers, her charts, her photographic plates. She took the letter from her father and the letter from Father Moreau.

The Mansion House was grand and intimidating. She was shown into a private chamber where the Lord Mayor sat with two aldermen. They looked grave.

"Miss Whitfield. Thank you for coming."

"Thank you for seeing me, my lord."

The Lord Mayor was a practical man, a merchant who had risen through the city's guilds. He did not understand astronomy. He understood risk.

"I have read your work," he said. "I have also read the Royal Society's report. I am not a scientist. I do not know whom to believe."

"The numbers do not lie, my lord."

"So I am told." He leaned forward. "Tell me plainly. What will happen?"

Catherine laid out her charts. She showed him the trajectory, the impact zones, the estimated damage. She did not soften the truth.

"London will not be destroyed directly. The impact will be in the North Sea, east of the estuary. But the shockwave will shatter every window in the city. The tidal surge will flood the lowlands. Fires will break out. There will be thousands dead."

"Thousands?"

"Hundreds of thousands, if no one prepares."

The Lord Mayor was silent for a long moment. The aldermen exchanged glances.

"What do you advise?"

"Move people away from the coast. Stockpile food and water. Prepare hospitals. Have fire brigades on standby. It is not too late to reduce the damage."

The Lord Mayor nodded slowly. "I will consider your advice."

Catherine left the Mansion House knowing that he would do nothing. There was no protocol for a woman's warning. No procedure for acting on the word of an amateur.

She walked home through streets where she was now recognized. People stared. Some whispered. One woman crossed herself.

Catherine kept walking. There was still work to do.

Chapter 34: The Riot

The crowd gathered outside the Royal Society on Friday evening.

Catherine heard about it from Thomas, who arrived at her door with dust on his coat and a cut on his cheek.

"They broke the windows," he said. "Someone threw a stone through Huxley's office."

"Were you inside?"

"I was leaving when it started. The crowd wanted Huxley. They wanted answers. He appeared on the steps and tried to speak. They shouted him down."

"What did he say?"

"That the comet was harmless. That the panic was manufactured. That — " Thomas paused. "That you were a fraud."

Catherine felt the words like a blow. "The crowd believed him?"

"They did not believe anyone. They were beyond reason. The police had to disperse them with truncheons. There are injuries."

Catherine sat down. The world was coming apart. The comet was approaching. The establishment was lying. The public was panicking.

And she was at the centre of it all, a woman alone, carrying a truth that no one wanted to hear.

"Did Huxley say anything else?"

Thomas hesitated. "He said that your father's work was always second-rate. That he only achieved his position through connections, not ability. That your supposed correction is a delusion inherited from a deluded man."

Catherine closed her eyes. She had known Huxley would attack her father eventually. It was the logical next step.

"He is afraid," she said. "That is why he attacks."

"Fear does not excuse it."

"No. But it explains it." She opened her eyes. "We have less than two months. Huxley can say whatever he likes. The comet will have the final word."

Chapter 35: The Confession

Thomas came to the Highgate observatory late one night. He found Catherine at the telescope, measuring the comet's position for the fifth time that week.

"I need to tell you something," he said. "Something I should have told you weeks ago."

Catherine stepped back from the eyepiece. "What is it?"

"Huxley and your father. They were rivals. Did you know that?"

"I knew they were not friends."

"It was more than that." Thomas sat down on a crate. "When the position of Astronomer Royal became vacant, your father was the obvious candidate. He was the better astronomer. Everyone knew it."

"Then why did Huxley get the position?"

"Because your father made an error. A small one, in a paper he published on the orbit of Neptune. Huxley found the error and used it to undermine your father's candidacy."

Catherine was silent. She had never heard this story.

"But here is the part I did not know until today." Thomas looked at her. "Huxley knew about the comet before your father died. Your father wrote to him, warning him of the danger. Huxley received the letter four weeks before your father's death."

"How do you know this?"

"Because I found the letter. It was in Huxley's private files, in a drawer he thought was locked. I should not have looked, but I did."

Catherine felt cold. "He knew. All this time, he knew."

"He knew. And he suppressed it. Because admitting your father was right would mean admitting that he stole the Astronomer's position from a better man."

The truth settled over Catherine like a shroud. Huxley had not been ignorant. He had been deliberate. He had chosen pride over lives.

She looked back through the telescope. The comet hung in the eyepiece, patient and inexorable.

"Then we will prove it," she said. "We will prove everything."

Chapter 36: The Gambit

Catherine emptied her bank account.

She sent telegrams to every observatory in Europe. To Paris, Berlin, Vienna, Rome, Stockholm, Copenhagen. To the Harvard College Observatory in America. To the observatory in Cape Town. To Madras.

She sent her data. Her calculations. Her photographic evidence. Her prediction.

She sent a simple message: "The Royal Society has suppressed this warning. Verify it yourselves."

The cost was enormous. Eighteen pounds for the transatlantic cables. Seven pounds for the European network. Three pounds for the Indian line. Margaret watched the money go with a look of quiet despair.

"That is your inheritance," she said.

"There will be no inheritance if the comet hits. And no one to inherit it."

The replies began to arrive within days.

Paris Observatory: "Your calculations are being examined. Preliminary assessment suggests they are correct."

Harvard College Observatory: "We confirm the perturbation error. The comet's trajectory is dangerous. We are issuing our own warning."

Berlin Observatory: "Professor Weber has reviewed your work. He endorses your conclusions fully. His statement follows."

Professor Karl Weber's statement was brief and devastating.

"I have examined Miss Whitfield's calculations. They are correct beyond any reasonable doubt. Comet 1889b will strike the Earth in August. The Royal Society's ephemeris is wrong. The Royal Society has chosen institutional pride over scientific truth. This is a betrayal of everything science stands for."

Catherine read the statement three times. Then she wept.

Chapter 37: The Response

The international response broke the dam.

Paris formally endorsed Catherine's findings. Berlin followed. Harvard's director issued a public statement calling for preparations. The Vatican Observatory confirmed the calculations independently.

The Royal Society was isolated. The scientific consensus had shifted. The weight of international opinion pressed down on Huxley and his committee.

The Morning Chronicle published a summary of the international responses under the headline "World's Scientists Back Miss Whitfield."

Thomas came to Highgate with news of the Royal Society's emergency meeting.

"They are in chaos. Huxley tried to defend the position, but no one supports him anymore. The international endorsements are too strong. He has been asked to step down as committee chair."

"Will he?"

"He refused. But he has lost control. The younger fellows are demanding a full review. They want to bring in outside experts."

Catherine felt a strange sense of calm. The battle was not over, but the tide had turned. Huxley could not hold back the truth forever.

"What happens next?"

"A new committee. Independent. They will review all the evidence — your data, the international responses, the Algerian observations. They will issue a new report within two weeks."

"And if they confirm my findings?"

"Then the government will act. Evacuations. Preparations. And Huxley will be finished."

Catherine looked out the window. The sky was clear. The comet was visible even now, before sunset, a pale star in the blue.

"The comet will not wait for their report," she said.

"No. But at least the people will be warned."

Chapter 38: The Debate

The Royal Society convened a special meeting to address the crisis.

Every seat was filled. Fellows crowded the galleries. The press occupied the back rows. Catherine was not permitted to attend, but Thomas reported everything.

Huxley spoke first. He defended his position with passion and desperation. The international endorsements, he claimed, were based on incomplete data. The European observatories had been rushed. Professor Weber was old and easily deceived.

"If we act on this woman's calculations, we will cause a national panic. We will empty London for nothing. We will be a laughingstock."

Thomas replied. He read the endorsements aloud. He presented the accumulating evidence.

"The question is not whether Miss Whitfield is right. The question is whether we are brave enough to admit it."

The debate went on for hours. Fellows shouted. The chairman threatened to clear the room.

At midnight, a vote was called.

The motion: "That the Royal Society accept the corrected orbital elements for Comet 1889b as presented by Miss Catherine Whitfield, and that the government be advised accordingly."

The result: 47 in favor, 43 against.

The Society had reversed its position. Huxley had lost.

Thomas walked home through the empty streets. The comet hung overhead, brighter than any star. He looked up at it and felt a strange emotion.

He had been on the losing side of science. He was now on the winning side.

The truth had won. But the comet was still coming.

Chapter 39: The Letter

The sealed statement arrived by hand on the morning after the vote.

It was from her father's solicitor, Mr. Finch. He had kept it in his safe for eight months, with strict instructions: deliver it to Catherine Whitfield only if her work was publicly suppressed by the Royal Society.

"The conditions have been met," the accompanying note said. "Your father foresaw this."

Catherine broke the seal.

The letter was in her father's handwriting, dated six weeks before his death.

"My Dearest Catherine —

If you are reading this, the Royal Society has rejected your work. I am sorry. I hoped they would prove wiser.

I write now to tell you something I should have told you long ago.

I knew about the comet's danger before I fell ill. I calculated the orbit and saw what was coming. I wrote to Huxley. I begged him to verify my figures and prepare the country.

He wrote back. He told me that my calculations were mistaken. That I was overwrought. That I should rest.

I did not rest. I checked my figures again. They were correct.

I wrote to Huxley a second time. I told him that if he did not act, I would go public. I would take my evidence to the newspapers.

He came to see me. He sat in this very study. He told me that if I published my findings, he would destroy my reputation. He had kept a record of every small error I had ever made — every misstep, every miscalculation. He would use them to prove that I was incompetent.

I did not publish. I was a coward. I chose my reputation over my duty.

I have regretted that choice every day since.

You must not make the same mistake. You must find a way. You must be braver than I was.

Your loving father,
Richard Whitfield"

Catherine folded the letter. She understood now. Her father had known. He had tried. And Huxley had silenced him.

She would not be silenced.

Chapter 40: The Confrontation

Catherine went to the Royal Society that afternoon.

She did not ask for an appointment. She walked past the porter and climbed the stairs to Huxley's office. She opened the door without knocking.

Huxley was at his desk. He looked up, startled, then angry.

"Miss Whitfield. This is a private office."

"I have read my father's letter."

Huxley went still. "What letter?"

"The one you received six weeks before he died. The one in which he warned you of the comet. The one you ignored."

"I do not know what you are talking about."

Catherine placed her father's sealed statement on the desk. "This letter. Written to me, to be delivered if the Society suppressed my work. He explains everything. Your threat. His cowardice. Your blackmail."

Huxley stared at the letter. His face had gone pale.

"You cannot prove — "

"I do not need to prove it. I only need to publish it." Catherine picked up the letter. "Which I will do, unless you tell the truth. Publicly. Before the government acts."

"You are blackmailing me."

"I am giving you a choice. The same choice you gave my father. You can tell the truth and save lives. Or you can protect yourself and let people die."

Huxley was silent for a long time. He looked old. Defeated.

"If I confess — "

"You will resign. Quietly. The Society will not prosecute. Your pension will be preserved. But you will admit what you did."

Huxley closed his eyes. "And if I refuse?"

"Then I will publish the letter. And the world will know that the Astronomer Royal chose his pride over the lives of his countrymen."

He sat for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"I will write the statement."

Catherine left the office without another word. She walked down the stairs and out into the street. The sun was shining. The comet was visible overhead.

She had won. But the victory tasted like ash.

Chapter 41: The Calculation

The Royal Society's new investigation took three days.

A team of five fellows, led by the newly appointed interim secretary, re-examined Catherine's work from first principles. They recalculated every perturbation. They verified every observation. They checked every digit.

Thomas brought the results to Highgate.

"Your figures are confirmed. Every one. The comet will strike on August 23. The impact zone is the North Sea, east of the Thames Estuary. The estimated energy is — " He stopped. "It is immense. Thousands of times more powerful than any explosion in human history."

"How much time do we have?"

Thomas looked at his notes. "Six days. The government has already begun evacuating the coastal areas. The army is moving people inland. It is the largest civilian operation in British history."

"And London?"

"Partial evacuation. The Lord Mayor has ordered the closure of all schools and businesses. Hospitals are preparing for casualties. The fire brigades are on standby."

Catherine nodded. It was not enough. But it was something.

"What about Huxley?"

"His resignation will be announced tomorrow. He has written a full confession. The Society will publish it alongside the new report."

"And Thomas Greaves?"

He looked surprised. "What about me?"

"Congratulations, Mr. Astronomer Royal."

Thomas blushed. "The appointment is temporary. Until the crisis is over."

"Then you had better start planning. A comet is about to hit England."

Thomas smiled, but his eyes were serious. "I will. And I will need your help."

Catherine looked through the telescope. The comet was a brilliant object now, visible even in full daylight. Its tail stretched across a third of the sky.

Six days. She had six days.

Chapter 42: The Plan

Catherine sat in the war council at Whitehall.

She was the only woman in the room. She was also the only person who understood what was coming. The generals, the ministers, the civil servants — they looked to her for answers.

"The impact will be at approximately six o'clock in the morning," she said. "Local time. The comet will approach from the east at an angle of thirty degrees."

"Can we destroy it?" A general asked. "Naval guns? Artillery?"

"No. It is moving at forty miles per second. It is the size of a mountain. Nothing we have can touch it."

"What about the tidal surge?"

"Twenty to thirty feet, depending on the exact impact point. The low-lying areas of Essex and Kent will be flooded. The Thames will reverse its flow. The water will reach central London within two hours."

The ministers exchanged glances. One of them — the Home Secretary — leaned forward.

"Can we evacuate in time?"

Catherine had calculated this. "We can move people away from the immediate impact zone. We cannot evacuate the entire southeast. But we can reduce the casualties."

"By how much?"

"If we move a million people inland, we might save half of them. If we do nothing, the death toll will be in the hundreds of thousands."

The room was silent.

"Then we move a million people," the Home Secretary said. "Miss Whitfield, you will coordinate with the military on the predicted impact zones."

Catherine nodded. She was not asked if she wanted the responsibility. She was simply given it.

She did not refuse.

Chapter 43: The Exodus

The trains began running at dawn.

Special timetables had been drawn up. Every available carriage was pressed into service. The stations were crowded with people carrying what they could — suitcases, children, pets, heirlooms.

Catherine stood on a bridge overlooking a railway yard. Below her, a train was loading families from the East End. The children were crying. The mothers were pale. The fathers were trying to look brave.

"Will they be safe?" Alice asked.

"The inland towns are beyond the flood zone. They should be safe from the surge. The shockwave will reach them, but it will be weakened by distance."

"And London?"

"The city will survive. The buildings will be damaged. Thousands will be homeless. But the city will rebuild."

They watched another train pull out. The faces in the windows were turned toward London, looking back at their homes.

Catherine felt the weight of what she had done. She had set this in motion. Her calculations had emptied the city. Her warning had turned millions into refugees.

She had done the right thing. She knew that. But the right thing had a terrible cost.

The comet hung in the sky, indifferent to the chaos below. It had been traveling toward this moment for thousands of years. It did not care about trains or families or the city of London.

It only followed the mathematics.

And the mathematics had brought it here.

Chapter 44: The Vigil

The night before impact, Catherine did not sleep.

She sat in the Highgate observatory with Alice and Thomas. The telescope was pointed at the comet, which now filled the eyepiece with its glow. The nucleus was a brilliant point of light, surrounded by layers of gas and dust.

"It is beautiful," Alice said.

"It is terrible," Thomas replied.

"It is both," Catherine said. "That is the nature of the universe."

She had been calculating all night. The impact point had shifted slightly — a last-minute perturbation from an asteroid fragment that had passed near the comet the previous week. The effect was small but meaningful.

The comet would now strike at 5:47 AM, eleven miles east of the previous prediction.

She sent a messenger to the military command. The evacuation zones were adjusted.

At three in the morning, she went outside. The sky was clear. The stars were bright. The comet dominated the eastern horizon, its tail spreading across the sky like a luminous fan.

She thought of her father. He had seen this comet. He had known what it meant. And he had tried to warn the world.

"I finished it, Father," she whispered. "I finished your work."

The wind picked up. The air grew cold. The first light of dawn began to show on the horizon.

The comet was coming.

Chapter 45: The Collision

Dawn, August 23, 1889.

Catherine stood on the roof of the observatory. Thomas and Alice stood beside her. The sky was clear, the stars still visible in the grey light.

The comet was enormous now. Its nucleus was brighter than Venus. Its tail stretched from the horizon to the zenith.

"Five minutes," Thomas said.

They watched in silence.

The comet seemed to grow brighter. Then brighter still. The light intensified until it hurt to look. Catherine raised her hand to shield her eyes.

And then the world went white.

The impact was not a sound. It was a physical force. The roof shuddered beneath them. The air itself seemed to compress. The white light faded to orange, then red, then a column of fire that rose from the eastern horizon to the top of the atmosphere.

The shockwave arrived seconds later. It was not a bang but a sustained roar, like thunder that never stopped. It went on and on, shaking the ground, rattling the windows.

Fires appeared along the coast. A wall of water surged across the estuary.

Catherine watched it all. She had calculated this. She had predicted this. But the reality was beyond calculation.

The comet had struck. England was changed forever.

And she was still standing.

Chapter 46: The Aftermath

The sky was strange for days after the impact.

Dust hung in the atmosphere, turning the sunsets red and the noons grey. Ash fell like snow.

Catherine traveled to the coast with a medical team. She had to see. She had to witness what her calculations had foretold.

The estuary was unrecognizable. The towns along the shore were gone — not destroyed, but erased. Where houses had stood, there was only mud and debris. Ships had been carried inland by the surge and left in fields.

The survivors were gathered in makeshift camps. They were silent. They had lost everything.

Catherine walked among them. No one knew who she was. She was just a woman in a grey dress, helping where she could.

She bandaged wounds. She carried water. She held the hand of a woman who had lost her children.

"Do you know when we can go home?" the woman asked.

"The water will recede. But it will take time. Months, perhaps."

The woman nodded. She did not cry. She was beyond tears.

Catherine stayed until dark. Then she returned to the cart that had brought her.

She looked back at the devastation. A city had been destroyed. Thousands had died. But hundreds of thousands had been saved.

Her father's work had been completed. The warning had been delivered. The truth had been told.

But the cost was more than she could measure.

Chapter 47: The Reckoning

Parliament convened a committee of inquiry.

Catherine was called to testify. She sat in the same chamber where she had been dismissed, where Huxley had mocked her. The faces were different now. Respectful. Attentive.

She told them everything. The calculations. The suppression. The letter from her father. Huxley's confession.

The committee asked her what should be done.

"Huxley should be removed from his positions. He knowingly suppressed a warning that could have saved lives. That is not a scientific failure. It is a moral one."

"And what of the Royal Society?"

"It must reform. It cannot continue as a club for gentlemen when the stakes are life and death. It must open its doors to women. To amateurs. To anyone with a telescope and a mind for the truth."

The committee took notes. They asked more questions. Catherine answered them all.

When she left the chamber, reporters surrounded her. She answered their questions too.

"Yes, I will continue my work. No, I will not accept a position at the Royal Society. They had their chance. They chose to protect their pride instead of the truth."

"What will you do now?"

Catherine looked up at the sky. The dust was clearing. The stars were beginning to reappear.

"I will build my own observatory. I will train my own students. I will do the work that needs to be done."

She walked away. The reporters let her go.

Chapter 48: The Public

Catherine's face appeared everywhere.

Newspapers. Magazines. Posters. She was the Woman Who Saved London. The Astronomer's Daughter. The Girl Who Defied the Royal Society.

She hated the attention.

"Can we not simply go back to work?" she asked Alice, who had become her assistant and friend.

"You are famous. People want to know about you."

"I do not want to be known. I want to be left alone."

But she could not be left alone. Letters arrived by the sackful. Proposals of marriage. Requests for autographs. Invitations to speak. Crank letters accusing her of witchcraft.

The letter that mattered came from a girl in Manchester.

"Dear Miss Whitfield,

I am twelve years old. I have always loved the stars but I did not think I could study them because I am a girl.

My mother showed me your picture in the paper. She said you saved London. She said you are a woman.

I have started reading my father's astronomy books. I am going to be like you.

Yours sincerely,
Emily Cartwright"

Catherine wrote back that night.

"Dear Emily,

Study the stars. Learn mathematics. Trust your calculations.

You can be anything you want to be.

Yours,
Catherine Whitfield"

She sent the letter and returned to her telescope. The sky was full of questions.

Chapter 49: The Observatory

The Government funded a new observatory.

It was built on a hill in Surrey, far from the smoke of London. The telescope was a twelve-inch refractor — the finest in England. The dome was copper and steel. The library was stocked with every catalogue and journal in existence.

Catherine was offered the directorship.

The title was "Chief Calculator" — a deliberate choice that sidestepped the question of her gender. She would be paid the same as a male fellow. She would have authority over the budget and the staff. She would answer only to the Board of Visitors.

She accepted on one condition.

"The observatory must be open to women. As students, as assistants, as astronomers. I will not preside over a closed institution."

The Board hesitated. They consulted lawyers. They worried about precedent.

But the public was watching. And Catherine Whitfield was too famous to refuse.

The charter was amended.

When the observatory opened, its first class of students included six women. Among them was Alice Marpole. Among them was a young woman from Manchester named Emily Cartwright.

Catherine stood at the door and welcomed them in.

"Science is not a matter of who you are," she said. "It is a matter of what is true. And the truth belongs to everyone."

Chapter 50: The Next Star

The observatory was quiet at midnight.

Catherine stood on the balcony, looking up at the sky. The dust had settled. The stars were clear.

She thought of her father. She thought of the comet. She thought of all the nights she had spent alone, fighting for a truth no one wanted to hear.

She had won. But the fight had changed her.

She went inside and sat at her desk. A new set of observations had arrived from the Paris Observatory — a faint object in the constellation Lyra, detected on photographic plates. It might be a comet. It might be an asteroid. It might be something new.

She spread the plates across the table. She took out her father's slide rule. She began to calculate.

The numbers emerged slowly. The object was moving. Its orbit was unusual — highly elliptical, tilted at a steep angle to the plane of the solar system.

It was a comet. A new comet. One that no one had seen before.

Catherine looked at the numbers. She traced the orbit with her finger. The comet was coming toward the inner solar system. It would pass near Earth in eighteen months.

She picked up her pen. She wrote the date in her journal.

This time, she would not have to fight alone. This time, she had an observatory. She had students. She had the truth.

And she had a promise to keep.

She opened the window and looked up at the stars. Somewhere out there, the new comet was approaching. It was small and faint and far away. But it was coming.

Catherine Whitfield smiled.

She was ready.
