# The Astronomer's Daughter — Outline

## Premise
In 1889 London, Catherine Whitfield secretly completes her dead father's astronomical calculations and discovers a comet that will destroy the city. She must navigate the rigid hierarchies of Victorian science to deliver her warning before time runs out.

## Core Cast
- **Catherine Whitfield** — 24, self-taught astronomer, daughter of the late Richard Whitfield FRS
- **Professor Ambrose Huxley** — 58, Astronomer Royal, her father's old colleague and rival
- **Sir Edgar Pemberton** — Secretary of the Royal Society, a gatekeeper of scientific orthodoxy
- **Thomas Greaves** — 30, junior fellow of the Royal Society, Catherine's cautious ally
- **Margaret Heston** — Catherine's cousin, pragmatic and loyal
- **Lord Balfour** — A newspaper magnate who senses the story

## Structure
Act I: The Inheritance (Chapters 1–12)
Act II: The Discovery (Chapters 13–30)
Act III: The Warning (Chapters 31–42)
Act IV: The Reckoning (Chapters 43–50)

## Chapter Plan

### Act I — The Inheritance

**Chapter 1: The Final Vigil**
Catherine Whitfield sits at her father's bedside in their Hampstead home. Richard Whitfield, renowned astronomer, is dying. He murmurs about unfinished work — calculations in his study. She promises to complete them. He dies at dawn.

**Chapter 2: The Study**
Catherine enters her father's study for the first time since his death. She touches his instruments — the brass telescope, the chronometer, the star charts. She finds his journals, dense with figures, and a half-finished letter to the Royal Society.

**Chapter 3: The Undertakers**
Men from the Royal Society arrive to collect her father's instruments and papers. Professor Huxley leads them. He is polite but firm — these belong to the Society now. Catherine refuses. Huxley warns her that a woman cannot possibly understand the work.

**Chapter 4: The Will**
The solicitor reads Richard Whitfield's will. The house and its contents pass to Catherine. There is a codicil: a safe hidden behind a loose floorboard in the study, combination known only to Catherine. Huxley watches from the doorway.

**Chapter 5: The Safe**
Catherine finds the safe. Inside: a sealed journal, a set of position tables for a comet designated 1889b, and a letter addressed to her. The letter explains that Richard has been tracking a comet whose orbital elements do not match the published ephemeris.

**Chapter 6: The Numbers**
Catherine begins to work through her father's calculations. Gregorian comet observations, perturbation equations, logarithms. She discovers an anomaly her father noted but never resolved — a discrepancy of 0.04 arcseconds in the comet's declination.

**Chapter 7: The Library**
Catherine visits the British Museum Reading Room to consult astronomical tables. The librarian refuses her entry to the scientific stacks. She must use the general reading room. Thomas Greaves, a young fellow, notices her and offers help.

**Chapter 8: The Error**
At home, Catherine finds the error. Her father, in his exhaustion, transposed two digits in the perturbation calculation for Jupiter's gravitational influence. The comet is not on the published course. It has been deflected toward Earth.

**Chapter 9: The Trajectory**
Catherine recalculates the entire orbit. The comet — she names it Whitfield's Comet, though she dare not say it aloud — will pass within 40,000 miles of Earth. It will strike the North Atlantic near the English Channel. The impact will be catastrophic. She has eight weeks.

**Chapter 10: The Letter**
Catherine writes to Professor Huxley at the Royal Society, presenting her corrections and her warning. She signs the letter "C. Whitfield" — an ambiguity that might get it read. She posts it herself.

**Chapter 11: The Response**
A terse reply from Huxley: "The calculations of the late Richard Whitfield FRS require no correction. His daughter's interference is noted and regretted." He has not read her work, only dismissed it.

**Chapter 12: The Ally**
Thomas Greaves arrives at Catherine's door. He read her letter before Huxley discarded it. He saw the math. He believes her. But he is a junior fellow with little influence. He offers to help her prepare a presentation for the Royal Society.

### Act II — The Discovery

**Chapter 13: The Observatory**
Thomas takes Catherine to the Greenwich Observatory at night. He has arranged access to the Meridian Telescope. Catherine must wear men's clothing to pass the porter. She trains the telescope on the comet's predicted position. It is there, exactly where her calculations placed it — and not where the ephemeris says it should be.

**Chapter 14: The Photograph**
Using the observatory's astrographic camera, Catherine captures a photographic plate of the comet. The plate will show its position relative to background stars. Hard evidence. But the plate needs development and measurement.

**Chapter 15: The Darkroom**
Catherine develops the plate herself in the observatory's cramped darkroom. The image emerges: a bright nucleus, a developing tail, and starfield. She measures the positions with a micrometer. The coordinates match her predictions. She has proof.

**Chapter 16: The Return**
Walking home at dawn, Catherine is stopped by a constable. A woman in men's clothing, out at night — he questions her. She cannot explain her errand. Thomas intervenes, vouches for her. The episode rattles her.

**Chapter 17: The Paper**
Catherine writes her paper: "On the Perturbed Orbit of Comet 1889b and a Predicted Terrestrial Encounter." She uses her full name, Catherine Whitfield. It is the first scientific paper she has ever written. Thomas agrees to present it before the Royal Society.

**Chapter 18: The Presentation**
Thomas reads Catherine's paper at a Royal Society meeting. The room is skeptical. Fellows mutter. Huxley sits stone-faced. When Thomas names the author — Miss Catherine Whitfield — there is laughter. A woman's calculations. The dismissal is casual and complete.

**Chapter 19: The Refusal**
Huxley calls on Catherine. He is not angry but condescending. He explains that her grief has led her to imagine patterns in the heavens. The comet is well understood. She must leave astronomy to those trained for it. Catherine asks him to simply check her math. He refuses.

**Chapter 20: The Society of Amateurs**
Margaret suggests Catherine contact the British Astronomical Association — a group that includes amateur observers, many of them women. Catherine writes to them. Within a week she receives replies from three members who have independently noticed something unusual about 1889b.

**Chapter 21: The Correspondence**
Letters fly between Catherine and amateur astronomers across England and Europe. A clergyman in York has been measuring the comet's position. A schoolmaster in Edinburgh noticed the same declination anomaly. A woman in Paris has computed a similar collision course. Catherine is not alone.

**Chapter 22: The Press**
Lord Balfour, a newspaper magnate with a taste for scientific scandal, learns of Catherine's claims through a servant in the Royal Society. He sends a reporter to Hampstead. Catherine refuses to speak. Margaret, seeing an opportunity, convinces her otherwise.

**Chapter 23: The Article**
The Morning Chronicle publishes "A Woman's Warning: Comet to Strike England?" The article is sensational but accurate. It includes Catherine's name, her father's legacy, and her prediction. London begins to take notice.

**Chapter 24: The Backlash**
Huxley denounces the article in a letter to The Times. "Science is not conducted in the popular press." The Royal Society issues a statement affirming the official ephemeris. Catherine is called a fraud, a hysteric, a woman seeking attention.

**Chapter 25: The Invitation**
Lord Balfour invites Catherine to his townhouse. He is impressed by her composure. He offers her resources: an observatory of her own, assistants, publication in his newspaper's science pages. She accepts — with conditions. She will not be his puppet.

**Chapter 26: The Observatory**
Balfour funds a small observatory in Highgate. Catherine moves her equipment there. She hires an assistant — a young woman named Alice from the BAA. Together they begin nightly observation of the comet, now visible to the naked eye as a smudge in the dawn sky.

**Chapter 27: The Tail**
The comet's tail has begun to develop. Catherine measures its spectrum with a pocket spectroscope. The emission lines show sodium and ionized gas. It is warming as it approaches the sun. Her collision model predicts a fireball of unimaginable scale.

**Chapter 28: The Expedition**
Through the BAA network, Catherine learns of a French astronomer who observed 1889b six months earlier from Algeria. She writes to him. His position data, which he has never published, confirms her trajectory. The collision probability rises to 94 percent.

**Chapter 29: The Committee**
The Royal Society forms a committee to investigate Catherine's claims. Huxley chairs it. Thomas Greaves is the most junior member. The committee's mandate: determine whether Miss Whitfield's calculations warrant further study.

**Chapter 30: The Hearing**
Catherine appears before the committee in the Royal Society's grand meeting room. She presents her evidence. The photographic plate. The Algerian data. The amateur observations. The corrected perturbation equations. Huxley asks no questions. The committee adjourns to deliberate.

### Act III — The Warning

**Chapter 31: The Verdict**
The committee's report: "Miss Whitfield's calculations contain errors inconsistent with the established methods of celestial mechanics. Her conclusions are not supported." Thomas Greaves dissents — the only dissenting voice. His career is effectively over.

**Chapter 32: The Eclipse**
A partial solar eclipse darkens London. Catherine watches from Highgate. The comet is now visible in daylight, a pale ghost near the eclipsed sun. People in the streets see it. Panic begins to spread. Huxley is asked at a press conference and dismisses the comet as "a harmless visitor."

**Chapter 33: The Mayor**
The Lord Mayor of London sends for Catherine. He has read her paper and the conflicting opinions. He asks her, privately, what she truly believes. She tells him: London must evacuate or be destroyed. The Mayor thanks her and does nothing. There is no protocol for a woman's warning.

**Chapter 34: The Riot**
A crowd gathers outside the Royal Society, demanding answers. Huxley appears and tells them the comet will pass safely. A stone is thrown. Windows break. The police disperse the crowd. Catherine watches from a cab. She realizes that Huxley would rather let the city burn than admit a woman was right.

**Chapter 35: The Confession**
Thomas comes to Catherine. He confesses that he knows why Huxley resists. Richard Whitfield and Huxley were rivals for the position of Astronomer Royal. Huxley won — but only because Richard Whitfield's early work contained a small error that Huxley exploited. To admit Catherine is right would be to admit that Huxley's entire career rests on a political victory, not merit.

**Chapter 36: The Gambit**
Catherine decides to bypass the scientific establishment entirely. She writes to every observatory in Europe and America, sending her data and her predictions. She pays for the telegrams herself. The cost depletes her inheritance. Margaret warns her she is risking everything.

**Chapter 37: The Response**
Responses arrive. The Paris Observatory finds her work compelling. The Harvard College Observatory requests more data. An astronomer in Berlin confirms the perturbation error independently. Catherine's findings begin to circulate in the international scientific community, even as London's establishment rejects them.

**Chapter 38: The Debate**
The Royal Society convenes a special meeting to address the growing international pressure. Huxley defends the official position. Thomas presents the accumulating evidence. The meeting ends in deadlock. Huxley announces he will stake his reputation on it: the comet will not strike.

**Chapter 39: The Letter**
Catherine receives a letter from her father's solicitor. Richard Whitfield, anticipating his death, had prepared a sealed statement to be opened only if his daughter's work was suppressed. The statement reveals that Huxley knew of the comet's danger before Richard's death — Richard had written to him. Huxley suppressed the warning to protect his position.

**Chapter 40: The Confrontation**
Catherine goes to the Royal Society and demands to see Huxley. She shows him her father's letter. Huxley is silent. Then he admits it: he received Richard's warning. He checked the math. He knew it was correct. But he had just negotiated a new royal charter for the Society. A comet warning would have derailed it. He chose the institution over the truth.

**Chapter 41: The Calculation**
With Huxley's tacit admission, the Royal Society reopens the investigation. A team of fellows recalculates the orbit. Catherine's figures are confirmed. The comet will strike in six days. Huxley resigns. Thomas is appointed interim secretary.

**Chapter 42: The Plan**
There is no time for full evacuation. But there is time to move people away from the coast and low-lying areas. The government, now convinced, begins the largest civilian relocation in British history. Catherine is consulted on every decision. She has become, overnight, the most important astronomer in England.

### Act IV — The Reckoning

**Chapter 43: The Exodus**
Trains, wagons, and ships carry people inland. London is half-empty. The streets are strange and quiet. Catherine stands on Greenwich Hill with Thomas and watches the comet hang in the twilight sky. Its tail now stretches across a third of the heavens. It is beautiful and terrible.

**Chapter 44: The Vigil**
Catherine does not sleep. She continues to refine her calculations. The comet is accelerating. The impact point shifts slightly — not London itself, but the estuary of the Thames. A direct hit on the city was always unlikely, Catherine realizes. Her father's error was in the magnitude, not the fact. The devastation will still be immense.

**Chapter 45: The Collision**
Dawn, June 21, 1889. The comet strikes the North Sea, forty miles east of the Thames Estuary. The impact is visible from the coast as a column of light reaching to the edge of space. The shockwave arrives minutes later — a sustained, rolling thunder that shatters windows across London. The sea rises. But the city stands.

**Chapter 46: The Aftermath**
Fires burn along the coast. The surge has flooded towns. Catherine travels to the estuary with a medical team. She sees the destruction — not as an astronomer but as a witness. The sea is strange, full of debris. The sky is hazy with dust. The comet's nucleus is gone, vaporized by impact. She feels no triumph. Only the weight of what was prevented.

**Chapter 47: The Reckoning**
Parliament convenes. A committee of inquiry is formed. Huxley testifies and admits his suppression of Richard Whitfield's warning. He is stripped of his honors. Thomas is confirmed as Astronomer Royal. Catherine is offered a fellowship at the Royal Society. She declines.

**Chapter 48: The Public**
Catherine is famous. Her face appears in newspapers. She receives letters from women across England who have been inspired by her. A girl in Manchester writes that she has begun studying the stars. A school in Bristol names a telescope after Catherine. She is not sure how to be a symbol. She is still, in her own mind, just a woman who did the math.

**Chapter 49: The Observatory**
The government funds a new observatory. Catherine is offered the directorship with the title "Chief Calculator" — a deliberately chosen term that sidesteps the question of her gender. She accepts on one condition: the observatory will be open to women as well as men. Thomas supports her. The charter is amended.

**Chapter 50: The Next Star**
Catherine stands on the balcony of her new observatory. The sky is clear. She turns the telescope on a new object — a faint smudge in the constellation Lyra. Another comet, perhaps. Or an asteroid. Something unseen. Something waiting. She begins her calculations.
