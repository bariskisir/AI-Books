Chapter 1: The Signal

The radio crackled at 0300 hours.

Captain Elias Shaw sat alone in the darkened bridge of the Sea Serpent. The coffee in his mug had gone cold two hours ago. He had been staring at the same patch of ocean for eleven days.

The Mayday repeater sounded again. Not a distress call. A salvage alert. A vessel adrift in the North Atlantic, coordinates eighty nautical miles east of their position.

Shaw pulled the headset over his ears and listened. The automated beacon gave the vessel's name. The Mermaid. A luxury yacht, two hundred feet, registered out of Monaco. Last known position logged three years ago.

Three years.

He reached for the satellite phone and dialed the salvage office. The line rang six times before a sleepy voice answered.

"I've got a tow," Shaw said.

"Where?"

"Eighty miles east. Derelict. A private yacht."

"How long has it been drifting?"

"Three years."

Silence on the line. Then: "You sure she's still floating?"

"She's broadcasting. I can see her on radar. She's intact."

The voice on the other end laughed. "Must be one hell of a boat. Bring her in. Half the salvage fee if she's empty. Full fee if there's cargo."

Shaw hung up and stared at the radar screen. The blip was steady. The Mermaid was waiting for them.

He woke the crew at 0330. They had a long tow ahead.

"Rig the cable," he told Voss. "We're going fishing."

The sea was flat and black. The stars were out. On the horizon, a shape emerged. Darker than the water around it.

The Mermaid was coming into view.

Chapter 2: The Tow

Dawn broke gray and cold over the North Atlantic.

The Sea Serpent approached the Mermaid at reduced speed. Jess Corrigan stood on the bow, hand on the rail, squinting through the spray.

The yacht was beautiful. White hull, sleek lines, three decks. The windows were dark. No lights. No movement. She sat low in the water, listing slightly to port.

"Looks clean," Voss said at her elbow. He was already running the math. This was a good catch.

Jess said nothing. She was watching the deck. There was something on the railing. Cloth. A piece of fabric caught in the rigging.

The Sea Serpent came alongside. Harlow killed the engines. The only sound was the slap of water against both hulls.

"Ready the line," Shaw called from the bridge.

Jess and Voss prepared the tow cable. The yacht was close enough now that Jess could see details. The name Mermaid was painted in gold script across the stern. The letters were faded but legible.

The lifeboats were still in their davits.

That was wrong. In a proper abandonment, the lifeboats would be gone. If the yacht had gone down, they would have been deployed. If there had been a rescue, they would have been recovered.

But the lifeboats hung in place. Neat. Untouched.

"Maybe they were picked up by helicopter," Voss said.

Jess nodded. But she did not believe it.

She fired the line gun. The rope arced across the gap and landed on the Mermaid's deck. No one retrieved it.

She would have to board.

Chapter 3: First Look

Jess boarded the Mermaid through a shattered hatch on the starboard side.

The glass was broken inward. Someone or something had come through from the outside. She stepped carefully over the shards and into a narrow corridor.

The interior was dark. Her flashlight cut a narrow beam through the blackness. The walls were paneled in mahogany. The carpet was thick and blue. The air was still and heavy.

She moved forward. The corridor opened into a lounge. Leather sofas. A grand piano. A bar stocked with bottles. Crystal glasses gleamed in the light of her torch.

Everything was clean. No dust. No water damage. The yacht was three years adrift, but it looked like the owners had stepped out an hour ago.

She found the galley. Pots hung on hooks. Knives sat in a magnetic strip. A coffee cup rested on the counter. She touched it.

It was warm.

She pulled her hand back. That was impossible. The yacht had no power. No generator. No heat. The cup should have been cold.

She backed out of the galley and radioed the bridge.

"Captain. There's coffee here. Hot coffee."

Shaw's voice crackled back. "The yacht's been dead for three years. There's no power."

"I know."

"Then how is the coffee hot?"

Jess had no answer. She moved on, deeper into the yacht.

The staterooms were arranged along a central passage. Each door was closed. She tried the first one. It opened smoothly.

Inside, a bed was made. Clothes hung in the wardrobe. A book lay open on the nightstand. She read the page. Someone had been reading about the sea.

Chapter 4: The Galley

Ana Reyes followed Jess's path into the Mermaid thirty minutes later.

She carried a satchel with supplies. Batteries. A portable radio. A camera to document the salvage. She was the youngest on the crew and she knew they expected less of her. She intended to give them more.

The galley stopped her cold.

The counters were clean. The sinks were dry. On the stove, a pot of soup sat at a low simmer. Steam rose from the surface.

She checked the refrigerator. It was cold. Food was inside. Fresh vegetables. A cut of beef. Dairy.

"This is impossible," she whispered.

She opened the freezer. Ice cream. Frozen fish. The temperature gauge read zero degrees.

The yacht had been adrift for three years. No crew. No power. But the food was fresh. The stove was on.

She touched the burner. It was warm.

She radioed Shaw. "Captain, the galley is operational. I have fresh food here. The stove is lit."

Silence on the line.

"I need you to confirm what you're seeing," Shaw said.

"I'm seeing a working kitchen on a dead ship."

"Check the generator."

Ana found the engine room ladder and descended. The generator room was clean. The diesel generator sat silent. Cold. It had not run in years.

But the lights were on now.

She looked up. The bulbs in the ceiling were glowing. A soft, warm light.

She had not turned them on.

Chapter 5: The Staterooms

Jess checked each cabin methodically. Twelve staterooms on the lower deck. All identical. All immaculate.

The beds were made with hospital corners. Pillows fluffed. Comforters smooth. In each room, a single personal item stood out.

A hairbrush on a vanity. A pair of reading glasses on a desk. A child's stuffed bear on a pillow.

No signs of struggle. No mess. No chaos.

She opened the wardrobe in the third stateroom. Evening gowns hung in a row. Men's suits beside them. Shoes arranged on the floor.

These people packed for a formal event. They expected to arrive somewhere.

She checked the bathroom. Towels were folded. Soap was dry. Toothbrushes stood in a cup.

"Jess." Voss's voice crackled over the radio. "I'm in the main lounge. You need to see this."

She found him standing before a wall of photographs. The passengers. Group shots. Couples. Families. All smiling. All dressed for the voyage.

They looked happy.

"Heading to New York," Voss said. "There's a brochure in the bar. The Mermaid was making a transatlantic crossing. A celebration. Some kind of anniversary."

"When did they lose power?"

"The brochure says the voyage was scheduled for fourteen days. They left Monaco on the tenth of May."

Jess did the math. "Three years ago this month."

Voss nodded. "They never arrived."

Chapter 6: The Ballroom

Voss forced the ballroom doors open with his shoulder.

The handles were cold. Locked from the inside. He had to hit them twice before they gave.

The doors swung inward. Music did not play. The chandeliers did not light. But the room was not dark.

Pale light filtered through salt-crusted windows. It caught the dust hanging in the air. It caught the figures standing in the center of the floor.

They were frozen. Dozens of them. Men and women in formal wear. Gowns and tuxedos. Jewelry catching the dim light.

They stood in pairs. Hands on shoulders. Hands on waists. Caught mid-step in a dance that had stopped years ago.

Voss did not move. He stood in the doorway and stared.

"They're preserved," he said. His voice was barely a whisper.

Jess pushed past him. She stepped into the ballroom. The air was cold. Dry. The smell of perfume and old fabric hung heavy.

She approached the nearest figure. A woman in a blue dress. Her hair was pinned up. Her skin was pale. Waxy. Her eyes were closed.

Jess touched her hand. It was cold. Firm. Not decayed. Not mummified. Preserved.

"They look like they're sleeping," Jess said.

"They're not sleeping," Voss said.

He was right. Their chests did not rise. Their eyes did not move. They stood on the ballroom floor in perfect stillness.

Jess counted them. Twenty-eight in the ballroom. Six more unaccounted for.

Chapter 7: The Captain's Table

Shaw boarded the Mermaid at noon.

He did not like the yacht. He did not like the way the air felt inside. He did not like the way his crew spoke in hushed voices. He did not like the coffee that stayed hot in a dead ship's galley.

But he was the captain. He needed to see the bridge.

The helm was on the upper deck. The door was open. Inside, the instruments were dark. The GPS was dead. The radio was silent. The ship's wheel stood unmanned.

He found the ship's manifest in a leather binder on the chart table.

Thirty-four souls. Twelve crew. Twenty-two passengers. All listed by name. Nationalities. Passport numbers.

He recognized some of the names. A tech billionaire. A film producer. A fashion designer. The Mermaid had been carrying money and fame across the Atlantic.

He found the captain's log on a tablet computer. The screen was dark. He pressed the power button. Nothing.

He shook it. The screen flickered. Then it lit up.

The battery was at seventy percent. After three years.

The last entry was dated May 24th. Three years ago. The text was short.

"All passengers assembled in the ballroom. We are proceeding at reduced speed. Electrical issues reported but under control. Spirits remain high. Eta New York, May 28th."

That was the last thing the captain wrote.

Shaw scrolled backward. The entries were routine. Weather. Navigation. Meals. Entertainment. The voyage of a luxury yacht crossing the Atlantic.

Nothing about disaster. Nothing about death.

Until the last line.

Chapter 8: The Log Begins

Shaw took the tablet back to the Sea Serpent.

He sat in his cabin with the door locked and read the log from the beginning.

Day One. The Mermaid departed Monaco at 1400 hours. The weather was clear. The passengers gathered on the deck for champagne. The captain noted that morale was high.

Day Two. The yacht passed through the Strait of Gibraltar. The passengers held a dinner party. The food was excellent. The wine flowed freely.

Day Three. The yacht entered the open Atlantic. The captain recorded a minor electrical fluctuation in the navigation systems. It was corrected within the hour.

Day Four. The passengers organized a dance in the ballroom. The captain reported that the electrical issues returned briefly. The lights flickered. Then they stabilized.

Day Five. The electrical problems worsened. The backup generator activated automatically. The captain radioed the manufacturer. They said it was a software glitch.

Day Six. The passengers complained of a smell. Something organic. The crew checked the vents. They found nothing. The smell disappeared.

Day Seven. The smell returned. Stronger this time. The passengers began gathering in the ballroom. They said it felt safer there.

Shaw put the tablet down. He stared at the wall of his cabin. The yacht's log told a story of a ship gradually coming apart. But the ship had not come apart. The ship was intact.

The people were not.

He picked up the tablet and kept reading.

Chapter 9: The Line Tightens

The Sea Serpent began the tow at 1400 hours.

Harlow engaged the winch. The cable rose from the water, dripping and cold. It pulled taut between the two vessels. The Mermaid groaned as the slack was taken up.

Shaw stood on the bridge, watching. The cable held. The yacht began to move.

"Speed at three knots," Voss reported.

"Bring it to five," Shaw said.

The Sea Serpent increased power. The cable tightened further. The Mermaid followed. Her bow cut through the water. She looked almost alive.

Jess watched from the stern. The yacht trailed behind them like a patient animal. The windows were dark. The decks were empty.

She thought about the woman in the blue dress. The one standing in the ballroom. The one whose hand she had touched.

She thought about the warmth in the galley. The lights that turned on by themselves.

"We're towing a tomb," she said.

Harlow was beside her. He had come up from the engine room to check the cable tension.

"She's heavy," he said. "Heavier than she looks."

"She's full of people."

"They're dead. They don't weigh anything."

Jess looked at him. "They're still there."

Harlow nodded. He did not argue.

The line held. The sea was calm. The sun was setting. Behind them, the Mermaid followed in silence.

Shaw set a course for port. Eight days at five knots.

Eight days.

Chapter 10: Voices Below

Ana could not sleep.

The crew quarters on the Sea Serpent were cramped and warm. She lay in her bunk, listening to the hum of the engines. The rhythm of the sea. The creak of the tow line.

And something else.

Music.

She sat up. The sound was faint. Distant. But it was there. A piano. Playing a melody she almost recognized.

She climbed out of her bunk and pulled on her boots. She walked to the stern.

The Mermaid was dark. But the music was coming from it. From the ballroom. She could see the windows. They glowed faintly. Like candlelight inside.

She stood at the rail and listened. The melody was slow. A waltz. Three-quarter time. It repeated. Over and over.

Voss found her there.

"You hear it too," he said.

"Yes."

He stood beside her. He did not look at the yacht. He looked at the water.

"Three years," he said. "They've been playing that song for three years."

"How do you know?"

"Because I heard it last night. And the night before. Every night since we found her."

Ana looked at him. Voss was a hard man. Superstitious, but practical. He did not frighten easily.

"Why didn't you tell the captain?"

"Because I don't know what it is. And I don't want to know."

The music continued. The yacht glowed. The tow line held.

Ana went back to her bunk.

She did not sleep.

Chapter 11: The First Night

The convoy moved slowly through the dark.

The Sea Serpent pulled the Mermaid at five knots. The sea was black and calm. The stars were hidden behind clouds. The only light was the tug's running lamps and the faint glow from the yacht.

Jess took the first watch. She sat on the stern with a blanket around her shoulders and a cup of coffee in her hands. The coffee was from the Sea Serpent's galley. It was cold.

She watched the Mermaid through binoculars.

The deck was empty. The windows were dark. The yacht looked like a ghost on the water. Silent. Patient.

She scanned the deck slowly. Stern to bow. Bridge to hull.

Nothing.

Then she saw it. A flicker of movement. On the upper deck. Near the railing.

She adjusted the focus. The binoculars sharpened the image.

A figure stood at the rail. A woman in a long dress. The same blue dress Jess had seen in the ballroom.

The woman was facing the Sea Serpent. Her arms were at her sides. Her head was tilted.

She was watching them.

Jess lowered the binoculars. Her hands were shaking.

She looked again. The figure was gone.

She sat on the stern for the rest of her watch. She did not look away from the yacht. She did not blink.

She did not see the woman again.

But she felt her watching.

Chapter 12: Lights

At midnight, the Mermaid began to glow.

It started in a single porthole on the lower deck. A soft light. Faint at first. Then brighter. Then a second porthole lit up. Then a third.

The crew gathered on the deck. They watched as the yacht came alive. Window by window. Deck by deck. Until the entire vessel glowed from within.

"Looks like a party," Harlow said.

Nobody laughed.

Shaw came out of the bridge. He stood with his crew and watched the lights spread.

"Are the generators running?" he asked.

"No," Harlow said. "The yacht's systems are still dead. I checked the instruments. No power draw. Nothing."

"Then what's making the light?"

Harlow had no answer.

The light was warm. Golden. Like candlelight. It spilled from the windows and reflected on the water. The Mermaid looked almost beautiful.

Almost alive.

Jess walked to the railing. She could see into the ballroom now. The chandeliers were lit. The room was bright.

And the dancers were moving.

She saw them through the windows. Shapes passing back and forth. Slow. Rhythmic. They were dancing.

"They're dancing," she said.

The crew fell silent. They watched the shapes move behind the glass.

The music was faint. But it was there. A waltz. The same one Ana had heard.

The Mermaid was having a party. And no one was invited.

Chapter 13: The Watch

Voss took the second watch at 0200.

He sat in the wheelhouse, alone. The autopilot held the course. The radar showed nothing but the Mermaid's blip behind them.

He did not look at the yacht. He kept his eyes on the instruments. The compass. The depth gauge. The engine telemetry.

But he could not ignore the glow. It filled the rear windows of the wheelhouse. Warm light pressing against the glass.

He turned in his chair. He forced himself to look.

The Mermaid was lit from within. Every window glowed. The deck was empty. But the shadows moved inside.

He saw a shape on the upper deck. A man in a dark suit. Standing at the railing. Facing the Sea Serpent.

Voss grabbed the radio.

"Bridge to captain. We have a person on deck. Starboard side. Upper deck."

Shaw's voice: "Understood. I'm on my way."

Voss kept his eyes on the figure. It did not move. It just stood there. Watching.

Shaw arrived in the wheelhouse. He took the binoculars and looked.

"There's no one there," Shaw said.

"What?"

"Look for yourself."

Voss took the binoculars. He looked. The deck was empty. The man in the suit was gone.

But the ballroom lights still glowed. And the shadows still moved behind the glass.

Voss handed the binoculars back.

"I saw him," he said.

Shaw nodded. "I believe you."

He sat down in the navigator's chair.

"We have eight more days of this."

Chapter 14: The Journal Entry

Shaw returned to the log.

He sat in his cabin with the tablet and read the entries for Day Eight through Day Twelve.

Day Eight. The smell had returned. The passengers complained of headaches. The crew checked the ventilation system. They found nothing. The captain noted that morale was declining.

Day Nine. Passengers began gathering in the ballroom unprompted. They said the room felt safe. The captain ordered the crew to open the windows. The air was stale. Stagnant.

Day Ten. The electrical failures increased. The navigation system went down for two hours. The backup generator engaged. The captain radioed for technical support. The message was never acknowledged.

Day Eleven. A passenger reported seeing something in the water. A shape beneath the hull. The captain checked the sonar. It was clear. But the crew grew uneasy.

Day Twelve. The smell was gone. The passengers reported feeling better. Much better. They gathered in the ballroom again. This time, they began to dance.

Shaw stopped reading. He looked at the tablet. The entries were becoming stranger. The captain's tone shifted. Formal. Controlled. As if he was writing under duress.

He scrolled to Day Thirteen.

The entry was brief.

"The passengers will not leave the ballroom. They have been dancing for twelve hours. They say they cannot stop."

Chapter 15: Strange Course

Morning came gray and wet.

The Sea Serpent churned through a rising swell. The tow line held. But the Mermaid was not following correctly.

Jess noticed it first. The yacht was drifting to starboard. Pulling against the cable. Straining.

"Captain, she's fighting the tow."

Shaw checked the instruments. The Mermaid's heading was off by fifteen degrees. He adjusted course to compensate. The tug turned. The yacht resisted.

"Bring her back to port," Shaw ordered.

Harlow adjusted the winch tension. The cable tightened. The Mermaid's bow came around. But the moment the cable relaxed, the yacht began to drift again.

"She wants to go east," Voss said.

"East is open water."

"Maybe she doesn't want to go home."

Shaw ignored him. He held the course. The engines strained. The cable groaned.

The Mermaid fought every inch.

By noon, Shaw had corrected course six times. The yacht resisted each adjustment. It pulled eastward like an animal tugging at a leash.

"Should we cut her loose?" Voss asked.

"No."

"She's going to damage the cable. Maybe the winch."

"Then we'll repair it."

Shaw stared at the yacht through the bridge window. She was beautiful and terrible. A white ghost on a gray sea.

"You're coming with me," he said. "Whether you like it or not."

Chapter 16: The Second Entry

Shaw found a second source of information.

He had been reviewing the tablet when he noticed a file labeled "Captain's Personal Notes." It was password protected. He tried the obvious ones. Mermaid. Password. 1234.

None worked.

He tried the yacht's registry number. The access code opened.

The notes were different from the log. More personal. More raw. The captain had been writing to himself.

Day Six: "Something is wrong with the air. The filters are clean. The system checks out. But the smell won't go away. It's sweet. Organic. Like flowers left in a warm room."

Day Eight: "The passengers are changing. They talk less. They spend more time in the ballroom. They say the music helps. But the music is not playing."

Day Ten: "I checked the ballroom last night. The room was warm. Unnaturally warm. The passengers were standing in the dark. Not dancing. Just standing. When I turned on the lights, they blinked. All of them. At the same time."

Day Twelve: "I think they are not alone in there. Something is with them. Something that came through the vents. Or through the hull. I do not know. But I feel it watching me when I write this."

Shaw closed the file.

He needed air.

Chapter 17: Music

Ana could not escape the music.

It followed her through the day. Faint, but present. A piano playing a waltz she could not name. Three-quarter time. Melancholy. Beautiful.

She heard it on the deck. She heard it in the mess. She heard it in her bunk, muffled through the hull.

She was not the only one.

"I hear it," Jess said. "It never stops."

"Does the captain hear it?"

"He says it's the wind."

"It's not the wind."

They stood at the stern together. The Mermaid was close. Close enough that Ana could see details in the windows. The chandeliers. The shadows moving behind the glass.

"The dancers," Ana said. "They never stop either."

Jess nodded. "I went back. This morning. I boarded the yacht again."

"Alone?"

"I needed to see."

Ana waited. Jess did not speak for a long time. She stared at the yacht.

"The ballroom door was open," Jess said finally. "They were still there. All of them. Standing exactly where they were before."

"Standing?"

"Yes. Not dancing. They were frozen. In the middle of the dance."

"But you saw them moving."

"I saw shadows. Shapes. Maybe it was the light."

The music changed. A new song. Slower. The piano dropped into a minor key.

Ana felt something in her chest. Something heavy and cold.

"They're playing for us," she said.

Jess did not answer.

Chapter 18: Harlow's Discovery

Harlow found the damage during a routine inspection.

He had gone down to check the tow cable connection. The bracket where the cable attached to the Sea Serpent's hull. He climbed down the ladder to the lower deck and examined the metal.

The cable was intact. The bracket was solid. But the cable itself was scored. Deep grooves in the steel braid. As if something had been cutting at it.

He ran his finger along the groove. It was smooth. Friction from an abrasive material. Not rust. Not corrosion.

Something had been trying to cut the line.

He checked the entire length visible above the water. The scoring was consistent. Regular. The marks ran parallel to the cable's twist. Like teeth marks. Or claw marks.

He climbed back up and found Shaw.

"The cable's been damaged."

"The strain?"

"No. Something cut it. Multiple passes. Deep enough to weaken the structure."

Shaw looked at the cable. The sun was setting. The grooves caught the light.

"From our side or theirs?"

Harlow hesitated. "The marks are deeper on the underside. Closer to the water."

"Something from the water."

"Or something from the yacht. Dangling below."

Shaw considered this. A loose wire. A piece of debris caught in the current. A fishing net.

"Can it hold?"

"For now. But whoever cut it knew what they were doing."

Shaw looked back at the Mermaid. The yacht was dark. Quiet. Patient.

"Double the watch on the cable. I want someone watching it at all times."

Chapter 19: The Third Entry

Shaw read deeper into the captain's personal notes.

Day Fourteen. The captain had stopped writing in the official log. The personal notes continued. They were fragmented. Handwritten. Shaw had found a paper journal in the captain's cabin.

The handwriting deteriorated as the entries progressed.

"I have not slept in three days. The passengers do not sleep either. They stay in the ballroom. They do not eat. They do not drink. They stand and they sway."

"I tried to order them to their cabins. They looked at me. All of them. At the same time. Their eyes were black."

"I checked the engine room. The generator is running. But it is not producing power. The lights are on. The systems are active. But the generator is disconnected. I do not know where the power is coming from."

"The radio is dead. The satellite phone is dead. We are alone."

"The passengers have stopped blinking."

Shaw turned the page. The next entry was shorter.

"Something is in the ballroom with them. Something that moves between their bodies. Something that speaks through their mouths. I heard it. It said my name."

The entry after that was one line.

"It does not want us to leave."

The next page was blank.

Then:

"The crew is in the ballroom now. All of them. They went willingly. They said the music was too beautiful to resist."

The final entry was scratched into the page. The pen had dug deep into the paper.

"I am the only one left. The lights are on. The music is playing. The door to the ballroom is open. They are waiting for me."

The journal ended.

Chapter 20: The Figure Returns

Jess saw the woman again at sunset.

She was standing on the upper deck of the Mermaid. The same blue dress. The same still posture. Facing the Sea Serpent.

Jess did not reach for the binoculars. She did not need them. The woman was close enough to see clearly.

Her skin was pale. Waxy. Her hair was dark and pinned up. Her eyes were open.

She was looking directly at Jess.

Jess stood frozen. The woman did not move. She did not blink. She stood at the railing with her hands at her sides.

Jess raised her hand. A small wave. She did not know why.

The woman raised her hand in response. The same gesture. The same angle.

Jess lowered her hand. The woman lowered hers.

"Captain," Jess said into the radio. "She's back."

"Who?"

"The woman in the blue dress. On the upper deck."

Silence. Then: "I see her."

Shaw was on the bridge. He had the binoculars. He was watching.

"That's not possible," he said.

"And yet."

The woman remained at the railing. The sun sank behind her. The sky turned orange and red. She stood silhouetted against the light.

"Maintain course," Shaw said. "Hold the line."

The woman watched them until the sun was gone and the sky was dark and the Mermaid was nothing but a shape and a glow on the black water.

Then she was gone.

Chapter 21: No Power

Harlow ran a full diagnostic on the Mermaid's systems.

He boarded the yacht with a portable generator and a multimeter. He worked methodically. The engines were cold. The fuel tanks were full. The batteries were at one hundred percent charge.

"The batteries are fully charged," he told Shaw. "After three years. No trickle charger. No solar panels. Nothing."

"How is that possible?"

"Lithium batteries lose charge over time. Even disconnected. After three years, they should be at fifty percent. Minimum."

"Unless someone has been charging them."

"There's no power source on this ship. The generator is disconnected. The alternator is offline. There's nothing to charge the batteries."

Shaw considered this. "Could the batteries have been replaced?"

"The date codes are from the year the yacht was built. These are the original batteries."

"Then explain it."

Harlow could not.

He checked the wiring. It was intact. No corrosion. No damage. The electrical system was clean and ready to operate. As if someone had been maintaining it.

He found one anomaly. A circuit that did not appear on any schematic. A cable running from the battery bank to the lower decks. To a compartment that was not on the ship's plans.

The cable was warm.

Harlow traced it through the bilge. The cable disappeared into a sealed compartment. Welded shut. No door. No access panel.

"Captain, there's a room on this ship that I cannot access."

"A storage locker?"

"Welded shut. On the inside."

Shaw was quiet. Then: "Come back to the tug. We'll deal with it when we reach port."

Harlow did not argue.

Chapter 22: They Chose Not To

Shaw checked the records from the Mermaid's final days.

He had the official log. He had the captain's journal. He had the navigation data. He had everything except an explanation.

The navigation data told the clearest story.

On May 10th, the Mermaid departed Monaco. On May 12th, she passed Gibraltar. On May 14th, she entered the Atlantic. On May 16th, she began to drift.

But she did not drift randomly.

The navigation records showed that after the electrical failures, the yacht maintained her heading. The autopilot was engaged. The engines were running. She was proceeding toward New York at reduced speed.

Then on May 18th, the engines stopped. The yacht went dead in the water.

The distress signal was never activated.

"No Mayday," Shaw said. "No EPIRB. No emergency beacon. They had a fully equipped vessel and they never called for help."

Voss stood beside him. "Maybe the systems failed."

"The EPIRB is independent. It has its own battery. Its own transmitter. It would have worked even if the ship was dead."

"Unless someone disabled it."

"I checked the log. No one disabled it."

Shaw looked at the data again. The yacht's final position. The drift pattern. The wind and current charts.

"It was a clear night," he said. "The sea was calm. They had working radios, working navigation, working engines. They had lifeboats. They had flares."

"And they did nothing."

"They gathered in the ballroom and they danced."

Shaw closed the file.

"They knew they were dying. And they chose not to leave."

Chapter 23: The Fourth Entry

The yacht's captain had recorded a change in the passengers.

Day Fifteen. The official log was still being updated. Brief entries. Clinical. The captain had retreated into professionalism.

"Passengers remain in the ballroom. They have not eaten in three days. They do not appear distressed. Vital signs are stable. I have ordered the crew to monitor them at all times."

Day Sixteen. "Passengers report feeling calm. Euphoric. They say the music is soothing. They ask to remain in the ballroom. I have agreed."

Day Seventeen. "The passengers are no longer speaking. They communicate through gestures. They move in unison. When one turns, all turn. When one smiles, all smile."

Day Eighteen. "I attempted to remove a passenger from the ballroom. A woman. French. Mid-forties. She resisted. When I touched her arm, her skin was cold. Not cool. Cold. Like refrigerated meat."

Day Nineteen. "The woman's eyes have changed. The iris is no longer visible. The pupil has expanded to fill the entire eye. It is not dilation. The structure has changed."

Day Twenty. "They are not my passengers anymore. They are something else. They wear their bodies like clothing. I do not know who is inside them now."

Day Twenty-One. "I have locked the ballroom door. They do not seem to mind."

Day Twenty-Two. "They unlocked the door. I do not know how."

The log entries stopped after Day Twenty-Two.

Chapter 24: The Dance

Ana watched the ballroom through a porthole.

She had boarded the Mermaid alone. Shaw did not know. She had taken the small skiff and crossed the gap between the two vessels. The sea was calm. The climb was easy.

She found a porthole on the upper deck that looked into the ballroom.

The chandeliers were lit. The floor was polished. The dancers moved in slow circles.

She counted them. Twenty-eight in the ballroom. Twelve crew. Sixteen passengers. The others were elsewhere on the ship.

They danced in pairs. Men with women. Women with women. Men with men. They moved through the steps of a waltz she did not recognize.

Their eyes were open. Black. Unblinking.

They did not speak. They did not sing. The music came from nowhere. The piano played itself.

Ana watched for ten minutes. The dancers did not acknowledge her.

Then one of them turned.

A man in a white tuxedo. He looked directly at the porthole. Directly at her.

He smiled.

His teeth were not his own.

Ana dropped from the porthole. She landed hard on the deck. She scrambled to the skiff and cast off.

She rowed back to the Sea Serpent with shaking arms.

She did not tell anyone what she had seen.

Chapter 25: Voss Breaks

Voss refused to take the watch.

He stood in the mess, arms crossed, face pale. Shaw faced him.

"I'm not going back on deck," Voss said. "Not while that thing is behind us."

"Nothing is behind us but a derelict yacht."

"You know that's not true."

Shaw did not argue. He had seen the woman in the blue dress. He had read the captain's journal. He knew what Voss meant.

"I need you on watch," Shaw said.

"I need you to cut the line."

"We have a contract."

"To hell with the contract."

Voss's voice was rising. Ana and Jess watched from the doorway. Harlow stood by the stove.

"The passengers are dead," Voss said. "They died three years ago. But they're still there. They're still moving. They're still dancing. And they're trying to get to us."

"Cutting the line is abandoning salvage. We lose everything."

"We lose more if we don't."

Shaw looked at his first mate. Voss was a good sailor. Reliable. Steady. He had never refused an order.

"You're relieved of watch duty," Shaw said. "Report to Harlow in the engine room. Stay below decks."

Voss nodded. He did not thank Shaw.

He walked past him and down the ladder to the engine room.

He did not look at the Mermaid.

No one blamed him.

Chapter 26: The Fifth Entry

Shaw searched the captain's cabin again.

He had read the journal. He had read the log. But there was something missing. Something the captain had not written down.

He searched the desk drawers. The wardrobe. The safe behind the captain's bunk.

The safe was unlocked. Inside, a single item. A voice recorder.

Shaw pressed play.

The captain's voice filled the cabin. Hoarse. Tired.

"Day Twenty-Three. I am the only one left on the bridge. The crew has joined the passengers in the ballroom. I tried to stop them. They did not resist. They walked past me like I was not there."

A pause. The sound of breathing.

"The thing that came aboard — it is not hostile. It does not want to hurt us. It wants to keep us here. It wants the ship. It wants the people on it. It wants to travel."

Another pause. Longer.

"I have been sitting here for two days. The food is running out. The water is running out. But I do not feel hungry. I do not feel thirsty. I feel..." A pause. "Peaceful."

The recording ended.

Shaw played it again.

The captain's final words echoed in the dark cabin.

"I feel peaceful."

Shaw pocketed the recorder. He left the captain's cabin.

He needed to get his crew home.

Chapter 27: The Engine Room

Harlow heard the knocking.

He was in the engine room of the Sea Serpent, checking the bearings on the main drive shaft. The work was routine. The noise of the engines was constant.

But beneath the engines, he heard something else.

A rhythm. A pattern. Three knocks. Pause. Three knocks.

It came from the hull. From the water. From somewhere below the waterline.

He turned off the engine. The sudden silence was heavy.

The knocking continued.

He grabbed a flashlight and opened the access panel to the lower bilge. The space was tight. Dark. The waterline was visible through a crack in the hull plate.

He shone the light into the crack.

Something moved.

He jerked back. The light caught a shape. Pale. Human. Pressed against the hull from the outside.

A hand.

Fingers. Pressing against the steel. Knocking.

He scrambled out of the bilge. His heart was pounding. His hands were shaking.

He found Shaw on the bridge.

"Someone's in the water. Under the hull. Knocking."

Shaw looked at him. "How long?"

"I don't know. I just heard it."

Shaw walked to the stern. He looked down at the water. The sea was dark. The Mermaid's lights reflected on the surface.

He waited.

Three knocks. From below.

They were not alone in the water.

Chapter 28: Jess Goes Alone

Jess boarded the Mermaid for the third time.

She did not tell anyone. She took a hand torch and a knife. She climbed the ladder and crossed to the yacht's deck.

The warmth hit her first. The deck was warm. The metal radiated heat like it had been sitting in the sun.

The air was thick. Heavy. The smell of perfume was overwhelming. Roses. Jasmine. Something underneath. Something organic and sweet.

She moved through the corridor. The lights were on now. Soft. Warm.

She passed the galley. The stove was lit. The pot of soup was simmering.

She passed the staterooms. The doors were open. The beds were made.

She reached the ballroom door.

It was closed.

She put her hand on the wood. It was warm. Vibrating. Like the room was alive.

She pushed.

The door did not open.

She pushed harder. Nothing.

She pressed her ear to the wood.

Inside, the music played. The dancers moved. She could hear the rustle of fabric. The shuffle of feet.

And breathing.

Many breaths. All at once. A chorus of lungs.

She pulled back.

The door handle turned.

She stepped back. The door opened.

No one was there.

The ballroom was empty.

Chapter 29: The Ballroom Door

Jess stood in the doorway of the empty ballroom.

The chandeliers were dark. The floor was bare. The room was cold.

The dancers were gone.

She stepped inside. Her footsteps echoed on the polished wood.

The room smelled of old perfume and dust. The windows were dark. The air was still.

She walked to the center of the floor. She turned in a circle.

Nothing.

Then she heard it. A whisper. Behind her.

She spun.

The woman in the blue dress stood in the doorway.

She was close. Close enough that Jess could see the details of her face. The pale skin. The dark eyes. The small smile.

She was beautiful. And she was not alive.

Jess did not run. She could not.

The woman raised her hand. Her fingers were long. White. The nails were painted red.

She gestured. Come.

Jess shook her head.

The woman's smile widened. Her mouth opened. A sound came out. Not a word. A note. A single clear note held for a long time.

The other dancers appeared behind her. Filing through the doorway. Arranging themselves around the room. Men in tuxedos. Women in gowns.

They formed pairs. They raised their arms.

The music began.

The woman in blue held out her hand to Jess.

"Come," she said. "Dance with us."

Jess ran.

Chapter 30: Escape

Jess ran through the corridor.

The lights flickered. The walls seemed to close in. The warmth pressed against her like a physical weight.

She reached the shattered hatch where she had first entered. The opening was there. The night air was visible. She lunged for it.

Something caught her ankle.

She fell. Her chin hit the deck. Blood filled her mouth.

She looked back. A hand gripped her ankle. Pale. Cold. The nails were red.

She kicked. The grip held.

She kicked again. Harder. She connected with something. A wrist. A face. She did not look.

The grip released.

She scrambled to her feet. She dove through the hatch.

She landed hard on the deck. The stars were above her. The cold air filled her lungs.

She crawled to the railing. The tow line was there. The cable that connected the yacht to the tug.

She grabbed it. She pulled herself up.

Behind her, the hatch was filled with faces. Pale. Watching.

She climbed onto the cable. She crawled across the gap. The water below her was black. The cable swayed.

She did not look back.

She reached the Sea Serpent's deck. Harlow was there. He grabbed her arm. He pulled her aboard.

"Jesus, Jess. What happened?"

She lay on the deck, gasping.

"They're not dead," she said.

She looked at the Mermaid. The yacht was dark again. The windows were black.

But in the ballroom, the lights flickered. And the shadows danced.

Chapter 31: Aftermath

They gathered in the mess.

Jess sat with a blanket around her shoulders. A cup of tea in her hands. She had stopped shaking, but she could not stop staring at the wall.

Ana sat beside her. Harlow stood by the door. Voss paced.

Shaw stood at the head of the table.

"Tell me what you saw," he said.

Jess took a long breath.

"They were gone. The ballroom was empty. I thought they had disappeared. I thought I had imagined them."

"But they hadn't."

"No."

She looked up at him.

"They came back. All of them. The woman in the blue dress. She spoke to me. She asked me to dance."

"And?"

"I ran."

Shaw nodded. He looked at his crew. Four people. Tired. Scared.

"I want to cut the line," Voss said. "I've been saying it for days."

"I hear you."

"Then do it."

Shaw was quiet. He looked at his hands on the table.

"The salvage fee is three hundred thousand dollars," he said.

"To hell with the money."

"It's not just the money. If we abandon salvage, we lose our license. We lose our reputation. We lose everything we've built."

"We lose more if we stay."

Jess spoke. "She knew me."

Everyone looked at her.

"The woman in blue. She knew my name."

"She said your name?"

"No. But she knew me. She looked at me like she was expecting me."

Shaw absorbed this.

"Secure the yacht," he said. "No more boarding parties. We maintain course and speed. We reach port in six days."

"And the dead?"

"We leave them where they are."

Chapter 32: The Decision

Shaw could not sleep.

He sat in his cabin with the captain's journal and the voice recorder. He had read them both multiple times. He had memorized the details.

The captain of the Mermaid had faced the same decision. Cut the line or stay. He stayed.

And he became one of them.

Shaw thought about the woman in blue. About what she wanted. About the hand reaching from the water. About the warmth of the ship.

The Mermaid was not just a derelict. She was a trap. A beautiful trap that had been waiting for anyone to find her.

He checked the navigation. They were three hundred miles from port. Six days at current speed.

Six days.

He opened the satellite phone. It had been quiet. No incoming calls. No messages.

The phone was dead.

He checked the radio. Static. He tried the emergency frequency. Nothing.

"Voss," he called. "Check communications."

A pause. Then: "They're down, Captain. All of them."

"When did this happen?"

"I don't know. I just checked. They were working an hour ago."

Shaw stepped onto the bridge. The night was clear. The stars were out.

The Mermaid glowed behind them.

She had cut their communications.

She was isolating them.

Shaw made his decision.

"Cut the line," he said.

Chapter 33: Cut

Voss grabbed the axe.

He had been waiting for this order. He climbed over the railing and dropped to the lower deck where the tow cable connected.

The cable was taut. Under immense pressure. Cutting it would be dangerous. The whip could kill him.

He did not care.

He raised the axe.

The blade came down. Sparks flew. The cable frayed.

He raised the axe again.

A sound stopped him.

Music. From the Mermaid. Loud. Clear. The piano playing the waltz.

He turned. The yacht was close. Closer than it had been. The gap between the vessels had narrowed.

The dancers were on the deck.

All of them. Thirty-four figures standing at the railing. Facing him. Their eyes black. Their mouths moving.

They were singing.

A wordless melody that rose above the wind.

Voss lowered the axe.

He walked to the railing. He stared at them. They stared back.

The woman in blue stepped forward.

She raised her hand.

Voss raised his hand in response.

"Voss!" Shaw's voice. "Cut the line!"

Voss did not move.

"Voss!"

The woman in blue smiled.

Voss dropped the axe.

He climbed over the railing. He stepped onto the cable. He began to walk toward the yacht.

The cable swayed. He kept his balance.

He reached the Mermaid's deck. The dancers parted. They let him through.

He walked into the ballroom.

The door closed behind him.

Chapter 34: The Sixth Entry

Shaw found another recording in Voss's cabin.

It was not from the yacht. It was Voss's own voice. He had been recording his thoughts.

Shaw pressed play.

"This is Day Three. I cannot sleep. I see them when I close my eyes. The dancers. The woman in blue. She speaks to me in my dreams."

Day Four: "I checked the history of the Mermaid. She was built in Italy. Commissioned by a shipping magnate. He died on board. Heart attack. They found him in the ballroom."

Day Five: "The man who died was the first. I think he is still there. I think he is the one who opened the door. I think he is the one who invited them in."

Day Six: "I saw the woman in blue today. Not on the yacht. On our deck. She was standing behind Jess. She put her hand on Jess's shoulder. Jess did not feel it."

Day Seven: "The captain wants to cut the line. But we cannot cut the line. The Mermaid does not want to be cut. She wants to come with us."

Day Eight: "I am afraid. But I am also curious. What is on the other side? What happens when you step through that door?"

The recording ended.

Shaw sat in the dark.

He had lost his first mate to the yacht. He had lost his communications. He was towing a ship full of dead people who were not staying dead.

And he could feel the Mermaid pulling. Not on the cable. On something deeper.

On his mind.

Chapter 35: The Serenade

The music played all night.

A requiem. Slow and mournful. The piano notes drifted across the water. The melody was unfamiliar. Ancient. It felt older than the sea.

Ana sat in the mess with her hands over her ears. The music passed through her fingers. It passed through the hull. It passed through her bones.

"It's in my head," she said.

Jess was beside her. She did not cover her ears. She listened.

"Don't fight it," Jess said. "It makes it worse."

"The woman," Ana said. "She's singing."

"No. They all are."

They sat in silence. The music swelled. The yacht glowed. The cable held.

"I saw Voss go," Ana said.

"I know."

"He just walked across the cable. Like it was nothing. Like he was walking home."

"He's not coming back."

Ana looked at her. "How do you know?"

"Because he doesn't want to."

The music changed. A new song. Faster. Brighter. A waltz.

"They're celebrating," Jess said.

"What are they celebrating?"

Jess did not answer.

But she knew. The Mermaid had her first new passenger in three years.

The party was just beginning.

Chapter 36: Ana's Vigil

Ana took the watch.

She sat in the wheelhouse with the radio headset on. The communications were dead, but she kept listening anyway. Static. Constant static.

She watched the Mermaid through the window. The lights were bright. The dancers moved behind the glass.

She picked up the microphone. She pressed the transmit button.

"Can you hear me?"

No answer. The static continued.

"I know you can hear me. I know you understand."

She waited. The static crackled.

"I'm not afraid of you."

The lights on the Mermaid flickered. Once. Twice. A pattern.

Ana pressed the transmit button again. "Was that you?"

The lights flickered again. Three times. A clear response.

Ana's hands trembled. But she kept going.

"This is Ana. I'm the crew member who boarded you. I watched you dance. I heard your music."

The lights flickered in a rhythm. Like a reply.

"What do you want?"

The lights stayed steady. Then a pattern. Long. Short. Long. Long. Morse code.

Ana grabbed a pen. She wrote the sequence.

Long. Short. Long. Long. R.

She waited. The next sequence came.

Short. Long. O.

Short. Long. O.

Long. Short. M.

Room.

They wanted to go to the room.

The welded compartment.

Ana dropped the microphone. Her blood ran cold.

They knew she knew.

Chapter 37: The Storm

The weather turned at 0200.

The wind came from the north. Cold. Sharp. The sea rose in dark swells. The Sea Serpent began to pitch.

Harlow reduced speed. The engines labored. The tow line strained.

Shaw stood on the bridge, bracing against the helm. The Mermaid was behind them, a white shape in the darkness. She rode the swells like a living thing.

"She's surfing," Shaw said.

Jess looked. The Mermaid was riding the waves. Not fighting them. Using them. She was accelerating.

"She's pushing us," Jess said.

The cable tightened. The Sea Serpent surged forward. The engines whined.

"Cut the line," Shaw said.

Harlow looked at him. "Now?"

"Now. Before she drags us under."

Harlow grabbed the emergency release. He pulled the lever.

Nothing happened.

"The release is jammed."

"Jam it harder."

Harlow pulled again. The lever did not move.

"The cable is too tight. The pressure is holding it."

The Mermaid surged again. The Sea Serpent heeled. Water crashed over the deck.

"She's going to flip us," Jess said.

Shaw grabbed the radio.

"All hands. Brace for impact."

The Mermaid pulled them faster. The wind screamed. The waves built. The cable groaned.

Ana grabbed the wheelhouse door frame. Harlow held the winch. Jess clung to the railing.

The Mermaid pulled them into the storm.

She was not following anymore.

She was leading.

Chapter 38: Cracks

The hull of the Sea Serpent cracked at 0300.

The sound was loud. A deep metallic groan. The ship shuddered.

Harlow was in the engine room when he heard it. He checked the bilge. Water was coming in. A thin stream through a fissure in the hull plate.

"The crack is below the waterline," he reported. "We're taking on water."

Shaw's voice: "How fast?"

"Slow. But it will get worse. We need to stop the tow."

"The release is jammed."

"Then we cut the cable manually."

Shaw looked at the Mermaid. She was close now. Too close. The gap was less than fifty feet. He could see the dancers through the ballroom windows.

He could hear the music. Even through the storm.

"Jess," he said. "Get the torch. We're cutting her loose."

Jess grabbed the cutting torch. She climbed down to the lower deck. The cable was there. Tight. Vibrating.

She lit the torch. The flame was blue. Hot.

She touched it to the cable.

The metal glowed. It began to melt.

The Mermaid shuddered. A sound came from the yacht. A scream. High-pitched. Human.

Then the crack in the Sea Serpent's hull widened. The water poured in faster.

The Mermaid was fighting back.

Chapter 39: Shaw's Reckoning

Shaw read the full log.

The final entry. The one he had been avoiding.

It was a video file. He had not seen it before. The tablet had not shown it. He found it on the voice recorder. A hidden file.

He pressed play.

The captain of the Mermaid appeared on the small screen. He was in his cabin. The lights were dim.

"Whoever finds this. My name is Captain Marco Rossi. I am the master of the Mermaid. I am writing this because I will not survive the night."

He paused. His eyes were tired. His face was gray.

"The thing that took my passengers came from the deep. It has been here for a long time. It sleeps in the trenches. It wakes when ships pass overhead. It reaches up."

He stopped. He listened. Music played in the background.

"It does not kill. It keeps. It preserves. It collects. The Mermaid is its collection. We are its guests."

He leaned closer to the camera.

"It will offer you a place. It will make you feel welcome. It will make you want to stay."

"Heard music." He touched his temple. "It's in here now.

"If you find this ship, do not tow it. Do not board it. Do not approach it. Cut every line and burn every trace. Let her drift. Let her sink."

He looked off-screen. Something had caught his attention.

"I have to go now. The music is beautiful. I want to see who is playing."

The video ended.

Shaw closed the screen.

The captain had known. And he had gone anyway.

Chapter 40: The Bargain

Shaw made his decision.

He walked to the stern of the Sea Serpent. The Mermaid was close. Thirty feet. He could see the faces in the windows.

The woman in blue stood on the deck. Waiting.

"Captain." Jess was beside him. "What are you doing?"

"Negotiating."

He faced the woman. She was real. Solid. Her dress moved in the wind.

"You took my first mate," Shaw said.

The woman did not speak.

"I want him back."

The woman's head tilted. A small gesture. Almost curious.

"You cannot have him."

It was not her voice. It came from everywhere. The wind. The water. The ship.

"Then you will not have my crew."

The woman smiled.

"Your crew is already mine, Captain. They just do not know it yet."

Shaw felt it then. A warmth in his chest. A pull. The music was beautiful. He wanted to hear it more clearly. He wanted to step onto the yacht and see the ballroom and —

He shook his head.

"I will make you a bargain."

The woman waited.

"We will deliver you to port. You and your passengers. We will complete the tow. We will not cut the line."

"And in return?"

"You leave my crew alone. They walk off this job alive."

The woman considered.

"You will stay with us, Captain. You will be our guest."

Shaw looked at his crew. Ana. Jess. Harlow. They were watching him.

"Done."

The woman nodded. She turned and walked back into the ballroom.

The door closed.

Shaw took a long breath.

"Set course for port. Maximum speed."

Chapter 41: The Ritual

The Mermaid began to list at dawn.

Not from damage. The passengers had gathered on the port side. All of them. Standing at the railing. Watching the Sea Serpent.

The yacht tilted. The water rose on the port deck.

"They're going to capsize her," Jess said.

"No," Shaw said. "They're bowing."

The passengers were leaning. Not falling. Leaning. All at once. Their bodies angled toward the tug. Their arms at their sides. Their faces blank.

A gesture of respect. Or of invitation.

Jess felt the pull again. The warmth in her chest. The desire to cross the gap.

She held the railing.

"Don't look at them," she said.

But she could not look away.

The passengers held their positions for an hour. Then they straightened. They turned. They walked back inside.

The Mermaid righted herself.

"She's getting ready," Ana said.

"Ready for what?"

"For the arrival."

Shaw looked at the horizon. Land was not visible. Not for days.

But the Mermaid knew something they did not.

The yacht was preparing to arrive.

Chapter 42: Voices

Shaw heard them now.

Not through the air. In his mind. A chorus of whispers. The passengers. They spoke to him while he worked. While he ate. While he tried to sleep.

"Captain."

"Come."

"The music is playing."

He shook his head. He pressed his palms to his ears. The voices did not stop.

"You made a bargain," they said. "You belong to us now."

He did not tell the crew. He kept it to himself. He sat on the bridge and stared at the sea and listened to the dead.

Jess knew. She saw it in his eyes.

"Captain. How long have you been hearing them?"

He looked at her. His eyes were tired.

"Since last night."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"What would be the point?"

The Mermaid glowed brighter now. The music was louder. The crew moved through their duties in silence. They did not speak unless necessary.

They were all hearing it. A constant undertone. A melody that never stopped.

"We need to get to port fast," Jess said.

"We are."

"Then we need to be ready. For whatever happens when we arrive."

Shaw nodded. He did not ask what she meant.

He knew.

Chapter 43: The Crossing Guard

Coast Guard hailed them at 0800.

The voice came through the static. Weak. Distant. But clear.

"Vessel towing at bearing two-seven-zero. Identify yourself."

Shaw grabbed the microphone. His hands were shaking.

"Coast Guard, this is salvage tug Sea Serpent. We are towing a derelict vessel. The Mermaid. Registry Monaco. Destination Norfolk."

A pause.

"Sea Serpent, we have no record of a vessel named Mermaid in our system. Repeat, no record. Confirm vessel name."

"The Mermaid. M-E-R-M-A-I-D."

Another pause. Longer.

"Sea Serpent, we have searched all registries. Domestic and international. No vessel named Mermaid has been registered in the last ten years."

Shaw stared at the microphone.

"That's impossible. She's right behind me."

"Sea Serpent, we show only one vessel on our radar at your position."

Shaw turned. He looked out the window.

The Mermaid was still there. White hull. Glowing windows. Visible to the naked eye.

"Coast Guard. I have a visual on the vessel. She is here."

"Sea Serpent, our radar shows nothing at your tow point. There is no vessel behind you."

The radio went silent.

Shaw looked at Jess. She had heard everything.

"She doesn't exist," Jess said.

"She did. Once."

"Not anymore."

Shaw felt the voices grow louder. Triumphant.

The Mermaid had erased herself from the world.

Chapter 44: The Fever

Harlow fell ill at noon.

He was in the engine room when his legs gave out. He collapsed beside the main drive shaft. Ana found him there.

His skin was cold. His eyes were dark. The pupils had expanded to fill the iris.

"Help me get him up," Ana called.

They carried him to the mess. They laid him on the bench. His limbs were rigid. His breathing was shallow.

"He's cold," Jess said. "Like the passengers."

"Don't say that."

"It's true."

Ana checked his pulse. It was slow. Forty beats per minute. Too slow.

"Harlow. Can you hear me?"

His eyes moved. They focused on her face. His mouth opened.

"The music," he said.

Not his voice. A different voice. Lower. Older.

"She wants me to dance."

"Who?"

"The woman in blue. She is waiting for me."

He closed his eyes. His body went slack.

"Is he dead?" Jess asked.

"No. But he's not here anymore."

Ana covered him with a blanket. She sat beside him.

"We need to get him to a hospital."

"We need to get all of us to a hospital."

Harlow began to hum. A melody. The waltz.

He had joined the party.

Chapter 45: Containment

They locked Harlow in the engine room.

It was the only space on the tug that could be secured from the outside. They moved him down the ladder. They laid him on a mattress. They bolted the door.

He did not resist. He lay still. He hummed the waltz.

Ana brought him water. He did not drink it.

"He needs a doctor," she said.

"I know," Shaw said.

"When will we reach port?"

"Three days."

"He does not have three days."

Shaw did not answer.

They stood outside the engine room. The humming came through the door. Steady. Constant.

"It's the same song," Jess said. "The one from the ballroom."

"He learned it from them."

"Or they learned it from him."

Ana pressed her hand to the door. The metal vibrated. The humming traveled through the steel into her palm.

She felt it in her chest.

"Don't listen," Jess said.

"I can't stop."

None of them could.

The music was everywhere. In the air. In the water. In the steel of the ship. In their bones.

The Mermaid was calling them home.

Chapter 46: The Last Dance

Ana saw them all on deck.

Thirty-four figures. Standing in rows. Facing the Sea Serpent. Their mouths moved in silent song.

The sun was setting. The sky was orange and red. The figures were silhouetted against the light.

"Captain. They're all on deck."

Shaw came to the window. He looked.

"They're saying goodbye."

"To what?"

"To the world."

The figures stood motionless. Their mouths moved. Their hands were at their sides.

Ana felt a pull. A desire to join them. To stand in that row. To sing that silent song.

The woman in blue stepped forward. She stood at the railing. She looked directly at Ana.

She raised her hand.

Ana raised hers.

"No," Jess said. She grabbed Ana's wrist. "Don't."

"I can't help it."

"Yes you can. You're still here. You're still you."

Ana looked at her hand. It was raised. She lowered it.

The woman in blue did not lower hers.

"Stay with me," Jess said.

Ana nodded. She held Jess's hand.

The figures stood on the deck. The sun set. The sky went dark. The stars came out.

The Mermaid glowed.

The dance had ended.

The last voyage had begun.

Chapter 47: Shaw's Confession

Shaw called them to the bridge.

The Mermaid followed behind them. The cable was tight. The course was set.

"I need to tell you something."

Jess and Ana stood before him. Harlow was locked below. Voss was gone.

"I knew about the Mermaid."

Jess stared at him. "What?"

"Before we took the contract. I heard stories. Whisper network. Salvage captains talk."

"You knew she was haunted?"

"I knew she was cursed. Every salvage captain who took her contract ended up dead. Or disappeared. Or worse."

"Then why did you take it?"

Shaw looked out the window.

"Because I am one of them."

The words hung in the air.

"I was on the Mermaid. Three years ago. I was her first mate."

Jess stepped back.

"I left the ship before the final voyage. I had a family emergency. I lost my berth. The captain who replaced me — he was the one who went down with her."

"You left them."

"I left them to die."

Shaw's voice broke.

"And I have been running from that decision ever since. I took the salvage contract because I thought I could save them. Or join them. I did not know which."

He looked at his crew.

"I am sorry."

The Mermaid's lights flickered. The music swelled.

The woman in blue stood on the deck. She was waiting for him.

Chapter 48: The Line Breaks

The tow cable snapped at midnight.

No strain. No weather. No warning.

The cable simply parted. A clean break. As if it had been cut from within.

The Sea Serpent surged forward. The sudden release threw them off balance. Shaw grabbed the wheel.

The Mermaid drifted astern. Her lights flickered. She began to turn.

"She's free," Jess said.

"No," Shaw said. "We are."

The Mermaid swung around. Her bow pointed east. Toward open water.

The woman in blue stood on the upper deck. She raised her hand. A wave. A farewell.

"She's leaving."

"She's going home."

Shaw watched the yacht begin to move. No engines. No sails. She moved through the water like she was being pulled from below.

"Her destination is not on any chart," Shaw said.

The Mermaid picked up speed. The gap between the vessels widened. Her lights grew smaller.

The music faded.

Ana felt the warmth leave her chest. The voices went silent. She could breathe again.

"Captain. What do we do?"

Shaw watched the Mermaid disappear into the dark.

"Set course for port. We're going home."

Chapter 49: The Choice

Jess stood at the stern.

The Mermaid was a glimmer on the horizon. Fading. The music was almost gone.

She felt the pull. Weak. But present. The woman in blue was still calling.

"Jess." Ana was behind her. "What are you doing?"

"Thinking."

"About what?"

Jess did not answer. She looked at the distant light. The Mermaid was going somewhere. Somewhere that was not on any chart.

"She offered me a place," Jess said.

"She offered all of us a place."

"Ana took her hand. "You are not going."

"You don't know that."

"I know that you are standing here with me. Not on that ship."

Jess looked at her. Ana's face was tired. Scared. But her grip was firm.

"I have to finish this."

"Finish what?"

Jess did not know. But she knew she was not done with the Mermaid. Not yet.

"When we reach port," she said. "I am going to find out where that ship came from. And what it wants."

Ana nodded.

"Then I am coming with you."

They stood together on the stern. The Mermaid was gone. The sea was empty.

But Jess felt the woman's eyes on her. Even from the horizon.

The Mermaid was waiting.

Chapter 50: Homecoming

The Sea Serpent limped into Norfolk three days later.

Harlow was taken to the hospital. He recovered. He never spoke of what he heard.

Voss was never found. The Coast Guard searched. There was no trace.

The Mermaid was not on any registry. The salvage company denied the contract existed. The log files were corrupted. The video was blank.

Jess stood on the pier and watched the sun rise over the harbor.

The sea was calm. The sky was clear. It was a beautiful morning.

She reached into her pocket. She pulled out a piece of fabric. Blue silk. A scrap from the dress of the woman in blue.

She had taken it without thinking. A keepsake. A proof.

She held it up to the light. The silk was cold. It did not reflect the sun.

She folded it. She put it back in her pocket.

Behind her, the harbor was quiet. The fishing boats were waking. The gulls were circling.

Somewhere out on the water, the Mermaid sailed on.

East. Always east.

Toward a destination that did not exist.

And in the ballroom, the dancers kept dancing.

The woman in blue was waiting.

Jess felt her smile.

She smiled back.

The end.
