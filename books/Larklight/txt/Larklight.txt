Chapter 1: The Road to Larklight

The road to Larklight Point was not a road in the sense that most roads were roads. It was a gravel spine that branched off the coastal highway at a place where no sign announced it and no mailbox marked its beginning, a concession to the fact that people who needed to find Larklight already knew where it was and people who did not had no business being there. Lena March had been away long enough to forget which unmarked turn was the unmarked turn, and she passed it twice before the shape of the headland resolved into something her body remembered.

She pulled onto the shoulder of the highway and sat with the engine idling. The car was a rental, a compact thing with Connecticut plates and a faint smell of someone else's coffee. She had been driving for seven hours from Portland, which was itself a three-hour flight from Boston, which was where she had been living for twelve years as if living in Boston meant she had left Maine far enough behind. The odometer said otherwise. Maine had a way of keeping its roads attached to you, even when you chose not to drive them.

The afternoon light was the particular gray of coastal June, a light that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere at once, diffused through a sky that could not decide between fog and cloud. The ocean was out there, beyond the fringe of spruce and granite. Lena could hear it — the long exhale of surf on ledges, the shingle dragging at the tide — but she could not see it. Larklight had always been like that, visible only when you had already arrived.

She made the turn on the third attempt, guided by a break in the roadside scrub that her hands recognized before her eyes did. The gravel started immediately, a washboard surface that vibrated through the chassis and made the rearview mirror tremble. The road narrowed. Spruce and fir closed in on both sides, their lower branches dead and silver, their upper crowns knitting into a tunnel that swallowed the gray light. Salt air came through the vents, carrying the smell of rockweed and cold water and something else, something metallic and old, that Lena associated with no other place.

She had not been to Larklight in twelve years. She had not spoken to her grandmother in eleven. The last conversation had been a phone call on Margaret's birthday, a strained exchange in which Iris had asked when Lena was coming home and Lena had said she was busy and Iris had said nothing and the silence had stretched until Lena made an excuse and hung up. She had meant to call back. She had meant to visit. She had meant a great many things that intention could not carry across the distance between a granddaughter and a grandmother who had stopped knowing how to speak to each other.

The lawyer's call had come on a Tuesday. Iris March had died in her sleep, the lawyer said, at ninety-four, in the house at Larklight Point, where she had lived alone for forty-eight years after the lighthouse was decommissioned. The house and the surrounding land had been left to Lena. There was no mention of Margaret. There was no mention of anyone else. Lena had stood in her Boston apartment with the phone in her hand and the afternoon traffic rising from the street and had felt something give way inside her, something she had not known was holding.

The trees fell away as the road crested the ridge. Lena saw the house first, because the house was what the eye expected: a two-story clapboard structure, white once, now weathered to the color of old bone, with dark green shutters and a roof that had been patched in several shades of asphalt. It sat in a shallow depression of the granite headland, sheltered from the worst of the wind by a ridge to the north and open to the sea on the south. Behind it, connected by a stone path that had been laid in 1884 and never entirely relaid, the lighthouse tower rose from the rock.

Lena stopped the car at the top of the drive and did not get out.

The lighthouse was shorter than she remembered. Or perhaps she had been smaller when memory was made. It was a cylindrical tower of dressed granite, tapering slightly as it rose, painted white with a black lantern room at the top. The glass was dark. The light had not burned since 1978, the year before Lena was born, but the tower still stood as if it expected to be called back to duty at any moment. A chain-link fence surrounded its base, installed by the Coast Guard before they handed the property over to the town. The gate was locked.

Beyond the lighthouse, the sea stretched to a horizon that was barely distinguishable from the sky. The ledges broke the surface in dark ridges, their tops white with gull guano, their bases churned by surf that had been working the same rock since before anyone thought to build a warning here.

Lena got out of the car.

The wind hit her immediately, salt and cold, carrying the full voice of the ocean. It was colder than Boston. It was colder than she had remembered, or she had forgotten how to stand in a wind that came all the way from the Grand Banks without anything to break it. She stood beside the car with her jacket collar turned up and let the wind do what it would.

The house keys were in her pocket. The lawyer had mailed them in an envelope with no return address, as if he expected her to refuse delivery. They were on a plain brass ring: three keys, none labeled. The house. The shed. The padlock on the garden gate. She had been carrying them for four days without putting them in a drawer.

She walked to the front door. The path was flagstone, uneven, moss growing in the joints. The garden beds on either side were overgrown — iris and daylily and something with gray leaves that had gone wild in the absence of anyone to restrain it. The door was painted the same dark green as the shutters, the paint blistered and faded on the south-facing panels.

Lena fitted the first key into the lock. It turned with resistance, the mechanism unused to visitors. She pushed the door open.

The smell of the house was the first thing to reach her, and it undid something in her chest that she had been holding closed. Old wood. Candle wax. The faint sweetness of dried lavender. Coffee grounds left too long in the percolator. Her grandmother's smell. The smell of Larklight as it had been every summer of her childhood, every Thanksgiving and Easter and the single Christmas she remembered, before the distance began to accumulate.

She stepped inside.

The front door opened directly into the kitchen, which was the heart of the house and had always been. The room was large, low-ceilinged, with a wood stove in the far corner and a sink beneath a window that looked out over the sea. The cabinets were original, built of pine that had darkened to amber over a century of use. The table was still in its place by the window, covered in oilcloth printed with blue flowers that had faded to ghosts of themselves. Iris's coffee cup sat on the counter beside the percolator, as if she had just stepped away.

Lena stood in the middle of the kitchen and did not move.

The lawyer had said there was no one to clean the house before Lena arrived. No one had touched anything since the morning Iris died. The cup. The newspaper open to the crossword puzzle, filled in in Iris's neat, upright hand. A pair of reading glasses on the windowsill. A tea towel folded over the edge of the sink.

The clock on the wall had stopped. It was a wind-up clock, and it had run down.

Lena did not know whether to sit, or touch something, or leave. She did not know the protocol for entering a dead woman's house when that woman had been your grandmother and you had not come to see her for twelve years and now you were here because there was no one else to come.

The wind pressed against the windows. The sea continued its long labor against the ledges. The house settled around her, making the small sounds that empty houses make when they are learning to be empty.

She put her bag on the kitchen table. She filled the percolator with water. She lit the stove. She made coffee because making coffee was something her body knew how to do, and she needed something her body knew.

The coffee was bitter and strong and tasted exactly like every morning she had ever spent in this kitchen. She sat at the table with the cup in both hands and watched the light change over the sea and felt the house begin, slowly, reluctantly, to tell her what it had been holding.

Chapter 2: The House

The house at Larklight had been built in 1884, the same year as the lighthouse, and it showed the evidence of a structure designed by engineers rather than architects. It was square and sturdy, built to withstand winter storms that could shake the windows in their frames and drive salt spray through cracks that no amount of caulking could seal. The walls were twelve inches thick, fieldstone below and plank above, insulated by nothing more substantial than the density of the wood itself. It had been built for function, not beauty, but a hundred and forty-two years of habitation had given it a beauty no architect could have designed: the worn hollow of the stairs, the glaze of the window glass, the particular way the morning light fell across the kitchen floor.

Lena spent her first day walking through the rooms, opening curtains, touching objects, learning the shape of the absence. The kitchen led to a narrow pantry, which led to a parlor that Iris had called the front room, though no one had ever used the front door. The parlor contained a sofa upholstered in faded rose, a rocking chair with a cushion that had been darned in three places, a bookshelf filled with books whose spines had been bleached by decades of sun, and a brass telescope on a tripod aimed at the window. The telescope had been there as long as Lena could remember. Iris had never explained it.

The stairs rose from the front room, steep and narrow, with a landing halfway up where a window looked out over the sea. Lena climbed them slowly, her hand on the rail, feeling each step as a measurement of years. There were three bedrooms upstairs. The largest had been Iris's. The smallest had been the guest room, though there had never been guests. The middle room, the one facing the sea, had been Lena's when she visited as a child.

She opened the door to Iris's bedroom and stood on the threshold.

The room was neat in the way of someone who had lived alone for a long time and had learned exactly where everything belonged. The bed was made, the quilt tucked tightly, the pillow in its place. A glass of water on the nightstand. A book facedown on the bedside table, open to a page Iris had been reading. Wuthering Heights. The page was marked by a strip of rag paper, torn from something else.

Lena picked up the book. The paper was a receipt from the Stillwater Pharmacy, dated October 2025. Eight months ago. Iris had been reading this book eight months ago, and she had put it down to sleep, and she had not woken up.

She put the book back exactly as she had found it.

The closet contained clothes arranged by season: winter coats at the back, summer dresses at the front, a single wool cardigan hanging on the hook behind the door. Shoes in a row: walking shoes, gardening boots, one pair of black flats that looked as if they had not been worn in years. On the top shelf, hatboxes, and in the hatboxes, photographs.

Lena brought the hatboxes downstairs and spread the photographs across the kitchen table. There were hundreds of them, spanning generations, stored without order or label. A woman in a high-collared dress standing beside a man in a keeper's uniform: the 1880s. A group of children in sailor suits on the granite steps: 1900. A woman in a cloche hat holding an infant: 1920. A young couple in front of the lighthouse, the woman laughing, the man looking at her with an expression Lena recognized as love: 1952. Her grandparents. Iris and William.

She had not seen a photograph of William March in years. He had died before she was born, drowned in the fishing accident that the family mentioned only in footnotes. She had grown up knowing her grandmother was a widow but never asking what that had meant. Iris had not offered.

Lena sorted the photographs by approximate decade, creating piles that spread across the table and onto the floor. Kitchen. 1880s. 1900s. 1920s. 1950. She could not find anything from between 1955 and 1965, which was strange, because those were the years when Iris was raising Margaret, when the house should have been full of photographs.

She was still sorting when the light began to fail. The kitchen window faced south, and the June evening took its time, but eventually the gray deepened to charcoal and the photographs became shadows.

Lena turned on the overhead light. It was a bare bulb in a ceramic socket, and its light was yellow and inadequate, but it was enough.

She made a sandwich from supplies she had bought in town: bread, cheese, an apple. She ate standing at the counter, watching the last of the light drain from the sea. The lighthouse stood in silhouette, a dark finger against a darkening sky.

After dinner, she went upstairs to the attic.

The attic was accessed by a pull-down ladder in the ceiling of the landing. Lena had to stand on a chair to reach the cord. The ladder descended with a groan of old wood, and she climbed into a space that smelled of dust and cedar and the particular cold of air that had been still for a very long time.

The attic ran the full length of the house, the roof beams visible above, the floor planked in wide pine. A single bare bulb hung from the ridge beam, operated by a pull chain. Lena pulled it. The light revealed boxes, trunks, furniture draped in sheets, a child's rocking horse, a basket of clothes, a stack of framed paintings leaning against the wall.

And in the corner, beneath a canvas tarp, a trunk she had never seen.

It was made of dark wood, bound in brass, the lid domed. It was old — older than the house, perhaps, or at least as old. The brass was tarnished to green in the crevices. The lock was a simple hasp, not secured.

Lena knelt beside it. She lifted the hasp. She opened the lid.

The smell that rose was not dust. It was paper, old and dry, and lavender, and something else, something resinous and faintly sweet. The trunk was filled with journals.

They were arranged in stacks, tied with string or ribbon or nothing at all. Leather, cloth, marbled paper, plain cardboard. Some were thick, some were thin. The oldest of them, at the bottom, was bound in cracked leather with no label. The newest, at the top, was a composition notebook with a mottled black-and-white cover of the kind sold in drugstores.

Lena lifted the top journal. It was heavy in her hands. She opened it to the first page.

The handwriting was familiar: Iris March's neat, upright hand, the same hand that had filled the crossword puzzle on the kitchen table.

*August 15, 1952. I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for. William met me at the landing with a handcart for my luggage. He said, It is not Boston, and I said, No, it is not. I do not know yet whether that is a lament or a relief.*

Lena turned the page. The journal continued, day after day, the entries growing longer as Iris's hand grew more confident. She read by the yellow light of the attic bulb, sitting on the dusty floor with the journal in her lap.

She was still reading when the bulb began to flicker and the wind rose and the house made its deep, settling sound around her. She did not notice any of it.

She was in 1952, arriving at Larklight for the first time, and she could not stop.

Chapter 3: Sarah's First Entry

The journal at the bottom of the trunk was the smallest, its leather cover cracked and powdery at the edges, its spine held together by thread that had been wound around it more than once. The paper inside was loose-leaf, unlined, the writing in iron-gall ink that had faded to a brown so pale it was nearly invisible in certain lights. The handwriting was elegant and controlled, the letters formed in the manner of someone who had been taught that penmanship was a moral discipline.

Lena brought it downstairs to the kitchen, where the light was better. She made fresh coffee. She sat at the table with the journal before her, afraid to open it too quickly, afraid of what fragility might mean.

The first entry was dated April 14, 1884.

*April 14, 1884*

*I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for.*

*This is a failure of imagination that I will try not to repeat. I had seen the ocean before, of course — the Atlantic from the heights of Nahant, the harbor at Boston, the wide mouth of the Kennebec from the deck of a steamer. But I had not stood at the edge of it in a place where nothing intervened between me and the next shore but water, wind, and the long curve of the earth. Larklight Point is not a place that prepares you gently.*

*The journey took four days from Boston: train to Portland, steamer down the coast, and finally a fishing sloop that carried me and my luggage the last twelve miles through fog so thick that the captain navigated by the sound of the surf on the ledges. I sat on the deck with my hands in my lap and told myself that I was not afraid. I was not entirely successful.*

*Elias met me at the landing. He was taller than I remembered from the photograph, broader in the shoulder, his face weathered by weather I had only begun to imagine. He wore the keeper's uniform, dark blue with brass buttons, and he stood at the end of the stone pier as if he had grown there. He did not smile when he saw me. He took my valise and said, You are smaller than I remembered, and I did not know whether that was a criticism or an observation, so I said nothing.*

*We walked up the path from the landing to the house, and I saw the lighthouse for the first time. It rose from the granite at the southern tip of the point, white and straight and severe, the lantern room glowing like an eye in the fog. I had known I was marrying a lighthouse keeper. I had not known what that meant until I saw the tower in its true element, surrounded by water and sky and the long, empty horizon.*

*The house is square and solid and smells of wood smoke and salt. The woman who kept house for Elias before my arrival had left it clean but not welcoming. The rooms are bare, the windows uncovered, the furniture functional. I will curtain the windows. I will soften the edges. I will make this house into a place that can hold a life.*

*Elias showed me the tower this evening. We climbed the eighty-seven steps to the lantern room, and I stood beneath the great glass prism while he explained the mechanism. The lens turns on a bed of mercury, he told me. It floats. I thought about that word — floats — and what it meant to build something so heavy that it could still float. He said, The light must turn. If it stops, the ships will not know where the ledges are. I said, I understand. I did not, entirely, but I understood that understanding was not yet required of me.*

*The first night in a new place has a particular quality. The sounds are not the sounds you know. The darkness is not the darkness you are accustomed to. I lie in the bed that is now mine and listen to the sea and try to learn its rhythms. The lamp on the nightstand is small and its flame is steady. I have decided that the flame will be my compass. As long as it burns, I am here. When it goes out, I will have become someone else.*

Lena read the entry twice. Then she read it a third time, because the voice in it was so present, so alive, that she could not believe a hundred and forty-two years had passed between the writing and the reading.

She looked around the kitchen. The same room. The same windows. The same view of the sea. Sarah March had sat somewhere in this room — perhaps at this very table — and written those words, and the house had held them ever since.

Lena turned the page.

The next entry was dated April 17, 1884. Three days after the first.

*The loneliness of this place is not what I expected. I expected silence. What I have is a constant noise — the sea, the wind, the bell on the buoy, the creak of the house settling, the distant lowing of the fog signal — and within that noise, a silence that is absolute. Elias speaks little. He rises before dawn, tends the light, returns for meals, speaks of practical matters, returns to the tower. He is not unkind. He is simply occupied by a duty that leaves no room for the kind of conversation I had imagined marriage would contain.

I have begun to talk to myself. I do it in the kitchen while I cook, in the garden while I weed, in the front room while I sew curtains that no one but me will see. I tell the house what I am doing. I describe the weather. I narrate my own life because if I do not, it will pass without anyone knowing it happened.

I have also begun this journal. I tell it what I cannot tell Elias, what I cannot tell the walls. I tell it that I am afraid of the winter. I tell it that I miss my sister. I tell it that I do not yet know whether I love my husband, and that this ignorance feels like a failure I will have to live with until the answer comes, one way or another.

Cora wrote before I left Boston. She said she will visit in the summer. I do not know whether to hope for this or dread it. To see her will be to remember what I have left. But to see her will also be to have a witness to this life I am building, and that, I think, is what I need most: someone to tell me that it is real.*

Lena closed the journal. Her hands were shaking slightly, though she could not have said why. The voice of Sarah March — her great-great-grandmother, a woman she had never known, a woman whose name she had heard only in passing — was as clear as if she were sitting in the room.

She looked out the window. The lighthouse stood against the fading sky. The same lighthouse Sarah had climbed on her first night. The same lens, the same mercury bath, the same eighty-seven steps.

Lena put the journal carefully on the table. She made another pot of coffee. She sat down and opened the next journal, the brown leather one, the one from the middle of the stack.

The handwriting was different — looser, less schooled, the letters pressed into the paper with more force.

*September 15, 1918*

*Edward left for Portland today. They are calling it influenza. I told him the light does not care for names.*

*We stood at the landing in the rain. The supply boat came early because the captain wanted to make Portland before the weather closed the bay. Edward carried his bag, a sea bag, the same one he had carried when he came home from the army in 1915. He said, I will write. I said, The mail boat comes when it comes. He said, I will come back. I said, The light will be burning.*

*Ruth cried. She is six and she does not understand why her father is leaving on a boat when the weather is bad and the sea is gray. I told her he is going to help the sick. She said, Are there sick people in Portland? I said, Yes, many. She said, Are there sick people here? I said, Not yet.*

*The boat disappeared into the fog. Ruth and I walked back up the path. The lighthouse was still burning from the night, the lens turning in its slow, implacable arc. I banked the flame and went to make breakfast. The house was very quiet.*

*I am the keeper now. For as long as Edward is gone, the light is mine.*

Lena stopped reading and looked at the date again. September 15, 1918. The fall of the Spanish influenza. The pandemic that had killed more people than the war.

She thought of Ada March, alone at Larklight with a six-year-old, responsible for a lighthouse and a child and a life that could not stop for sickness.

She turned the page and kept reading.

Chapter 4: The Keeper's Log

The journals were not the only records Lena found. In Iris's study, a small room off the parlor that Iris had used for paying bills and writing letters, a set of shelves held the official keeper's logs, bound in black cloth, their spines stamped with dates. They spanned from 1884 to 1978, a century of daily entries recording the weather, the condition of the light, the passing of ships, the small events of a life lived at the edge of the sea.

Lena pulled the first volume from the shelf. It was heavy, the cloth cover worn smooth at the corners, the spine cracked. She opened it to the first page.

*Larklight Point Light Station*
*First Light: October 15, 1884*
*Keeper: Elias March, Principal*

The entries were written in a hand that was firm and regular, the letters formed with military precision. Each entry followed the same format: date, time, wind, visibility, condition of the light, remarks.

*October 15, 1884. Light established. Wind NW, force 4. Visibility 8 miles. Lens aligned and tested. All satisfactory.*

*October 16, 1884. Wind variable. Light burning steady. No vessels sighted.*

*October 17, 1884. Fog in the morning. Light burning. Fog signal activated 0600–0900.*

Lena scanned the pages, watching the seasons pass in increments of wind and visibility. The entries were spare, factual, uninflected by anything personal. Elias March recorded the weather and the state of the light and nothing more. There was no mention of his wife, no mention of the child who appeared in later entries, no mention of anything that could not be measured.

She flipped forward through the years. 1885. 1886. 1887. The handwriting remained consistent, the entries identical in form, the only variation in the numbers.

Then, in 1892, the handwriting changed.

She found the page: May 3, 1892.

*May 3, 1892. Keeper Elias March relieved of duty due to illness. Assistant Keeper Thomas Wainwright assumes responsibility for the light.*

The entry was written in a hand that was not Elias's. It was smaller, more careful, as if the writer was conscious of performing a duty that was not yet his by right.

The next entry was dated May 4, 1892, in the same hand:

*May 4, 1892. Wind SW, force 3. Visibility unlimited. Light burning steady. Assisted Mrs. March with arrangements.*

*Arrangements.* The word sat in the margin of the log, unrevealing. What arrangements? Lena read the entry again, and then the entries that followed, searching for a clue. May 5, May 6, May 7. The logs returned to their routine, as if the change of keeper was a detail of no lasting significance.

She turned the pages of the log through the rest of 1892, then 1893, then 1894. Thomas Wainwright's hand continued, neat and conscientious. There was no mention of Elias March after the entry of May 3. It was as if Elias had been written out of the record, his tenure reduced to a single line of administrative notation.

Lena returned to the journals. She found Sarah's volume and opened it to the entries surrounding May 1892. But the journal in her hands ended in 1891.

She searched the trunk. She searched the shelves in Iris's study. She searched the boxes in the attic. There was no journal from Sarah March for 1892.

She went back to the keeper's log and read the entries for the months after Elias's removal, looking for something, anything, that would tell her what had happened. The logs remained stubbornly factual. Fog. Wind. Visibility. The condition of the light. The passing of ships.

But in the remarks column for June 15, 1892, she found a single line that stopped her:

*June 15, 1892. Mrs. March visited the tower this evening. Sat in the lantern room until midnight. Did not speak. I did not disturb her.*

Thomas Wainwright had written that.

Lena read it three times. She tried to imagine Sarah March climbing the tower stairs alone, sitting in the lantern room where her husband had kept his vigil, staying until the night had fully settled around her. She tried to imagine what Sarah had been thinking, what she had been mourning, what she had been deciding.

She closed the log and put it carefully back on the shelf.

The lighthouse stood in the dark beyond the window. The lens was still, the light extinguished, the tower empty. But the records remained — the logs, the journals, the accumulated evidence of a century of keeping.

Lena went back to the kitchen and opened the next journal. Ada's voice. She needed Ada's voice.

Chapter 5: The Dark Room

There was a room at the end of the hall that Lena had never been allowed to enter as a child.

It was not a rule that had been stated. Iris had simply never opened that door in Lena's presence, and Lena had learned, in the way that children learn the invisible boundaries of a house, that the room was not for her. She had stood outside it many times, pressing her ear to the wood, listening for whatever lived on the other side. She had tried the knob once, when she was eight and braver than she would ever be again. The door had been locked.

Now she stood before it again, thirty years later, and the knob turned under her hand.

The room was small, square, with a single window that faced the sea. It had been Iris's sewing room, or perhaps her study, or perhaps something else entirely. The walls were covered in pressed flowers and botanical drawings — iris, wild rose, Queen Anne's lace, goldenrod — arranged in patterns that had once been orderly and had drifted with time into something more organic. A sewing machine stood against the far wall, a Singer from the 1950s, its black metal case open, a half-finished dress draped over the arm. The dress was made of pale blue cotton, printed with small white flowers, and it was sized for a child.

Lena touched the fabric. It was soft with age, the cotton worn at the seams where the needle had not yet finished its work. The stitching was even and careful, the work of someone who had learned to sew before sewing machines were common. Iris had made this dress by hand, or nearly by hand, and she had never finished it.

Beneath the window, a small writing desk held a stack of letters tied with a faded blue ribbon. Lena sat in the chair — a wooden chair with a cushion that had been re-covered several times — and untied the ribbon.

The letters were addressed to Iris March at Larklight Point. The return address was a post office box in Camden, Maine. The handwriting was male, bold and sloping, the letters pressed into the paper with confidence.

Lena did not open the first letter. She held it in her hands, feeling the weight of it, the texture of the paper, the press of the ink. She had found the locket in the garden. She had found the photograph of Iris with a man who was not her grandfather. She knew, with the certainty of someone assembling a story from fragments, what these letters contained.

She opened the first one.

*Dear Iris,*

*I have been home for three weeks and I have not stopped thinking about the way the light looked on the last evening, from the point, when you were standing in the garden and the sunset was behind you and the grass was yellow in the long light. I am trying to paint it. I cannot. The colors do not exist in any tube I own. I think I will have to invent them.*

*I am staying in my brother's house in Camden. It is quieter than I remembered cities being. I think I have become accustomed to the sound of the sea, and everything else seems thin by comparison.*

*I have started a painting of the lighthouse. Not the tower — the light itself, the beam, the way it sweeps the water at night. I am trying to capture something I cannot name. I think you will understand what I mean when I say that some things can only be approached by metaphor.*

*I do not know if you will write back. I do not know if you can. But I will send this letter anyway, because not sending it feels like a lie, and I have told enough lies this summer.*

*Yours,*
*Julian*

Lena set the letter down. The date at the top was September 28, 1965. Julian had written it the season after the summer he had spent at Larklight.

She untied the rest of the bundle. There were more than fifty letters, spanning ten years. She did not count them. She arranged them by date, reading the postmarks: 1965, 1966, 1967, 1968. The intervals between them grew longer as the years passed. The last one was postmarked 1975.

She did not read them all. Not yet. She tied them back with the ribbon and put them in her bag.

Beneath the letters, at the bottom of the desk's single drawer, she found a photograph. It was small, black-and-white, the edges curled. It showed Iris — young, beautiful, her hair dark and loose, her face turned toward the camera with an expression of undisguised happiness — standing beside a man in a paint-stained jacket. He was tall, thin, with dark eyes and a face that looked like it had been drawn by someone who cared more about character than beauty. He had his arm around her shoulder. She was laughing.

On the back of the photograph, in Iris's handwriting:

*Julian and me. August 1965. The last day.*

Lena put the photograph in her pocket.

She left the sewing room and closed the door. The dress remained unfinished on the machine. The pressed flowers continued their slow dissolution on the walls. The letters were in her bag, and the story they contained was Iris's, and now Lena's, and she was not yet ready to know all of it.

She went downstairs and made dinner. She ate at the kitchen table with Ada's journal open beside her plate. The sea was dark beyond the window. The lighthouse was dark beyond the sea.

Somewhere in the house, a floorboard settled. Somewhere in the past, a woman was laughing at a man who had painted her in the garden.

Lena turned the page and kept reading.

Chapter 6: The Light Itself

The next morning, Lena walked to the lighthouse.

The path from the house to the tower was worn into the granite, a shallow depression that generations of feet had polished to a smooth, gray surface. It was not a long walk — perhaps fifty yards — but it crossed open ground where the wind had full force, and by the time Lena reached the base of the tower, her eyes were watering and her hands had gone numb in her jacket pockets.

The chain-link fence that surrounded the lighthouse was rusted at the joints, the gate secured by a padlock that had been there long enough for the keyhole to fill with salt corrosion. Lena tried the key from Iris's ring. It did not fit. She tried the other two keys. Neither worked.

She stood at the fence, gripping the cold chain-link, and looked up at the tower. From this close, it was more massive than she had remembered. The granite blocks had been cut and fitted by hand, their surfaces rough with lichen, their joints marked by generations of mortar repair. The tower rose eighty feet above the point—not tall by lighthouse standards, but tall enough to dominate the landscape, to claim the sky above it. The lantern room at the top was a cage of iron and glass, the glass dark, the iron painted black. The lens was visible as a shadow within.

She had climbed this tower once. She was seven, visiting for the summer, and Iris had taken her up on a clear afternoon. She remembered the narrow spiral staircase, the iron handrail cold in her grip, the light that changed as they climbed—from the gray of the stairwell to the white of the lantern room, where the sun came through the glass and made everything seem to glow. Iris had lifted her up to see the lens, a great glass prism that turned on a bed of mercury, and Lena had watched it rotate in its slow, silent arc, throwing light that was not yet lit across the empty sea.

She did not remember why she had never climbed it again.

The gate would not open. But the fence was old, and the ground beneath it had shifted over decades of freeze and thaw, leaving a gap at the bottom where a person might fit if she were willing to lie flat and push herself through. Lena looked around. No one was watching. There was no one within miles.

She got down on her knees in the gravel and worked herself under the fence, feeling the rusted wire scrape across her back. She came through on the other side, brushing gravel from her clothes, and stood at the base of the tower.

The door was iron, painted black, with a heavy lock that looked newer than the fence. This one, too, was locked. Lena tried the keys again, knowing they would not fit, and they did not.

She put her palm against the iron. It was cold, even through the June morning, cold with the accumulated chill of a hundred and forty-two winters. The tower had been standing here since before her great-great-grandmother had arrived as a bride. It had weathered storms that had sunk ships, wars that had emptied towns, winters that had frozen the sea solid beyond the point. And now it stood locked and dark, accessible only to the memory of what it had been.

Lena walked around the base of the tower, her hand trailing along the granite. On the seaward side, where the wind and salt had been hardest, the stone was worn into hollows, the mortar eroded to powder in places. Lichen grew in patches of orange and gray. A spray of sea grass had taken root in a crack near the foundation.

She found what she was looking for on the north side: a small door, set into the base of the tower, half hidden by a growth of wild rose. This was not the main entrance. This was the service door, used by the keepers to access the oil storage and the mechanism room without climbing the full height of the tower. It was smaller than the main door, barely four feet high, and its lock was simpler — a latch, not a deadbolt.

Lena knelt in the wild rose, ignoring the thorns that caught at her sleeves, and tried the latch. It moved. The door swung inward, scraping against stone.

The smell that came out was oil and rust and dry stone and the particular cold of a place that the sun never reached. Lena took the flashlight from her bag and shone it into the darkness.

The service room was small, circular, its walls the same granite as the tower above. A spiral staircase rose from the center, the iron steps worn into curves by a century of feet. Against the wall, old oil cans stood in a row, their labels faded to illegibility. A workbench held tools: wrenches, a coil of wire, a glass cutter, a box of wicks wrapped in oiled paper. Everything was arranged with the precision of someone who had expected inspection at any moment.

Lena stepped inside. The door swung closed behind her, but she did not notice.

She climbed the stairs.

The spiral was tight, the steps shallow, the iron handrail cold and rough with rust. The light from her flashlight bounced off the curved walls, illuminating patches of stone and then losing them again. She climbed past the service room, past the first landing, past the second, counting the steps as she went. Eighty-seven steps. Sarah March had counted them. Ada March had counted them. Iris March had counted them, on the last night of the manned light, when she had climbed alone to say goodbye.

Eighty-seven steps.

The lantern room door was not locked. It was a simple wooden door with a brass handle, and it opened when she turned it.

The lantern room was smaller than she remembered. It was a circular chamber, perhaps twelve feet across, surrounded by glass panes set in iron frames. The lens dominated the space — a first-order Fresnel lens, six feet tall, its glass prisms arranged in concentric rings around a central drum. It stood on a pedestal of iron and brass, its mechanism still intact, the mercury trough still in place beneath it.

Lena stood in the center of the room and turned slowly, watching the landscape pass beyond the glass. To the south, the open Atlantic, gray and white-capped, stretching to a horizon that was barely visible in the haze. To the east, the curve of the coast, the dark line of the spruce, the white fringe of surf on the ledges. To the north, the house, small and square, its windows dark, the garden wall visible behind it. To the west, the road, the gravel track that led back to the highway and the world beyond.

She had seen this view from the ground a thousand times. She had never seen it from here, from the height of the light, where the world arranged itself into patterns that distance made legible.

She sat down on the floor of the lantern room, her back against the lens pedestal, and looked out at the sea. The wind pressed against the glass. The lens above her was dark and still. But the room held something — an echo, a residue — that she had not expected.

It felt like a chapel.

She sat there for a long time, until the light began to change and the gray of the afternoon deepened toward evening, and then she climbed back down the eighty-seven steps, locked the service door behind her, and walked back to the house to read another journal.

Chapter 7: Ada's Journal Continues

Ada March had been twenty-four years old in the autumn of 1918, with a six-year-old daughter and a husband who had gone to a city to help the dying and might not come back. She was the keeper of Larklight Light for the duration of the influenza pandemic, and her journal was the record of a woman holding a world together with her hands.

Lena had been reading Ada's journal in fragments, a few pages at a time, as if too much at once would be more than she could hold. But the evening after she climbed the tower, she sat down at the kitchen table with the brown leather volume and read it straight through.

*October 8, 1918*

*Ruth is ill. The fever came on in the night. I woke to find her burning, her small body hot to the touch, her eyes not focusing when I spoke her name. I have the medicine cabinet: quinine, whiskey, mustard plaster. Dr. Parrish gave me instructions before he left, but he did not tell me what to do when my daughter looked at me without recognition and I could not find her behind her eyes.*

*I have lit the light. I have banked the flame for the night. The lens is turning. The sea is calm. The fog is holding off. These are the things I can manage.*

*I cannot manage the fever. I can only sit beside her, and wet the cloth, and wait.*

*October 10, 1918*

*The fever has not broken. Ruth speaks in her sleep, words I cannot understand, names I do not know. I have not slept in two days. I climb the tower every four hours, tend the light, check the mechanism, climb back down. The stairs are the only thing I can count on. They are always there. They do not change.*

*The supply boat did not come today. I do not know if it will come tomorrow. I do not know if the sickness has reached the captain, or if he is afraid of what he might carry, or if the weather has simply kept him at the pier. I have enough oil for a week. I have enough food for two weeks, if I ration carefully. I have enough hope for much less than that.*

*October 12, 1918*

*A boat came today. Not the supply boat. Dr. Parrish, rowing himself, his coat collar turned up against the spray, his hands red with cold. He had come to check on us. He had come because he knew we were alone.*

*He examined Ruth. He listened to her chest. He took her pulse, her temperature, looked at her throat by the light of the lamp. He said, You have done everything right. I said, That is not helping. He said, I know.*

*He gave her something. A tincture. I do not know what it was. He said it was the best he had, and that was good enough for me.*

*He stayed. He said he would stay the night, to give me time to sleep. I protested. He said, I have seen a hundred cases this week. I will see a hundred more. One night will not make the difference to the town, and it will make a difference to you.*

*I did not argue. I slept for five hours, the deepest sleep of my life, and when I woke, Ruth's fever had broken.*

*October 15, 1918*

*Ruth is sitting up. She asked for bread and jam. I wept. She did not understand why I was crying, and I could not explain it to her in words that would make sense.*

*Dr. Parrish left at dawn. He said he would come back in a week if the weather held. He said the town was losing people every day. He named them for me: the Harbach twins, old Mrs. Pell, the miller's apprentice, the telegraph operator's wife. I knew them all. I had not known they were sick.*

*I asked him why he had come to us when so many needed him in town. He said, Because you are alone, and the light must burn. If the light goes out, ships will be lost, and I cannot save the people on ships I do not know exist, but I can save the keeper who keeps the light.*

*I think he is a good man. I think he is a man who will carry the weight of this autumn for the rest of his life, whether he knows it or not.*

*October 20, 1918*

*Ruth is well. She is running again. She asked today whether her father would recognize her when he came home, and I told her yes, of course he would, and then I realized I did not know if that was true.*

*I have not heard from Edward in three weeks. Letters are not reaching the coast. The mail boat has been suspended. I do not know if he is alive.*

*I have stopped asking myself whether I love him. The question seems irrelevant now. He is my husband. He is the father of my child. He is a man who left to help others because he could not stay idle while people died. Whatever I felt when I married him, I feel something different now. Something quieter. Something that does not need a name.*

*November 11, 1918*

*The armistice. I heard it on the wireless, the signal carrying through the static from a station I could not identify. The war is over. The influenza is not. But the war is over.*

*I lit an extra lamp in the window. I do not know why. Perhaps to signal to the town that someone was watching, that someone was still awake. Perhaps to signal to Edward, wherever he is, that the light is still burning and the house is still here.*

*Ruth asked me what armistice means. I said it means the fighting has stopped. She said, Does that mean Papa will come home? I said, Yes, I think it does. She said, Good, because I am tired of the light being yours instead of his.*

*I did not tell her that the light is mine now, and I am not sure I want to give it back.*

Lena closed the journal.

She sat in the quiet of the kitchen, the sea dark beyond the window, the journal warm in her hands. Ada's voice was still in her head: *I am tired of the light being yours instead of his.* She had been six years old, and she had understood something her mother had barely admitted to herself.

Lena looked at the lighthouse through the window. She could see the lantern room, dark against the stars. She thought of Ada climbing the tower in the dark, tending the light, checking the mechanism, climbing back down to a sick child. She thought of the eighty-seven steps, and the weight of each one.

She opened the next journal. Iris's 1952 volume. She needed to hear Iris's voice now, to understand how the story continued.

Chapter 8: Iris Arrives

*August 15, 1952*

*I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for.*

*William met me at the landing with a handcart for my luggage. He said, It is not Boston, and I said, No, it is not. I do not know yet whether that is a lament or a relief.*

*The fog was thick, the kind of fog that muffles every sound and makes the world feel wrapped in wool. I could hear the bell on the buoy, a single, slow note repeated at intervals, but I could not see the buoy. I could not see the shore until we were at the pier. I could not see the lighthouse until I was standing at the base of it, looking up into whiteness.*

*William carried my bags up the path. I followed, my shoes wet with grass, my coat damp with fog. The house appeared as we crested the rise: white clapboard, green shutters, a chimney that was smoking. Someone had lit a fire for my arrival. That someone was William, who had lit it before he walked down to the pier, who had prepared the house for a wife he had met three times before the wedding.*

*I wondered whether he was kind or whether he was dutiful. I decided it did not matter, at least not yet. Kindness and duty can look the same in a man who does not speak much.*

*The kitchen was warm. The kettle was on the stove. A vase of wildflowers stood on the table — daisies, black-eyed Susans, something with blue petals I did not recognize. William had picked them. I knew this because the stems were uneven and some of the petals were bruised. He had picked them himself.*

*I sat at the table and he made tea. He is not a man who talks while he works, but he is a man who works while he thinks, and I watched him move around the kitchen — finding the cups, the sugar, the milk — and I thought, I can learn this. I can learn the shape of his silence.*

*Later, he showed me the lighthouse.*

*The tower is eighty-seven steps. He counted them as we climbed. I did not need him to count them — I am capable of counting my own steps — but I understood that he was sharing something, that the number mattered to him, that he wanted me to know it.*

*The lens is beautiful. It is a first-order Fresnel, built in France, shipped across the Atlantic in 1883, installed in this tower a year later. It stands six feet tall, a hive of glass and brass, and when William lit it for me — just a demonstration, not the full night light — the prisms caught the flame and threw it into a pattern of rings and beams that made me catch my breath.*

*I said, It must be wonderful to be the one who lights it. He said, It is. That was the longest conversation we had all day.*

*I write this now in the kitchen, after dinner, while William is in the tower for the evening check. The fog has not lifted. The bell on the buoy continues its slow, patient note. I am a wife. I am a keeper's wife. I am living in a lighthouse at the edge of the sea, and I do not know what I am supposed to feel.*

*But I will write it down. I will keep this journal as the other women have kept theirs. I will leave a record that I was here, that I saw this, that I tried.*

*Perhaps that is enough.*

*Perhaps that is everything.*

Chapter 9: The Garden Wall

Lena spent the next morning in the garden.

The walled garden behind the house had been overgrown for years. The stone wall that enclosed it was still intact, though sections had collapsed where frost had pushed the granite apart, and the gate hung at an angle, its hinges rusted through. Inside, the garden was a wilderness of volunteer flowers, escaped perennials, and weeds that had taken advantage of neglect to establish a kingdom.

Lena had found gardening gloves in the shed, along with a pair of shears and a hand trowel. She had no plan for the garden — she was not a gardener, had never been a gardener — but the journals were overwhelming, and the house was beginning to press in on her with the weight of a century of accumulated memory. She needed to do something with her hands.

She started at the gate, cutting back the brambles that had grown through the bars, clearing the path so the gate could swing. The work was physical and uncomplicated, and it gave her mind space to sort through what she had read.

Sarah March had arrived in 1884, a bride of twenty-two, and had spent eight years learning to live with a man who spoke in the language of wind and visibility. She had kept a journal because she needed someone to speak to, and the journal had listened. Sarah had watched her husband lose his mind to the light, had watched an assistant keeper take his place, had watched Cora bear a child out of wedlock and stay. She had chosen to remain at Larklight after Elias's death — I will stay. Where else would I go? The light is the only thing that has not lied to me.

Ada March had kept the light alone during a pandemic, had nursed her daughter through a fever that could have killed her, had received a doctor who rowed through a gale to check on her, had waited for a husband who came home changed. She had learned that the light was hers, not his, and she had written that truth down for her daughter to find.

Iris March had arrived in 1952, young and romantic and unprepared for the sea. She had learned to be a keeper's wife, had raised a daughter at Larklight, had fallen in love with a painter who came for a summer and stayed in her memory for the rest of her life. She had kept a son she could not raise and a secret she could not tell.

And Lena's own mother, Margaret, had grown up in this house and left at eighteen and never come back.

Lena stopped weeding and sat back on her heels. The garden was taking shape around her. She had cleared the path to the center, where a stone bench sat beneath an apple tree that was still alive, its branches twisted but productive. Small green apples hung among the leaves, hard and sour, not yet ready for harvest.

She sat on the bench. The stone was cold even through her jeans. The apple tree cast a dappled shadow over the wall.

Beneath the bench, half hidden by a clump of iris, something glinted.

Lena knelt and pushed the leaves aside. A small iron box, rusted, its lid held shut by a clasp that had corroded to the body of the box. She worked the clasp loose with the tip of the shears and opened the lid.

Inside, wrapped in oilcloth, was a silver locket on a broken chain. The locket was tarnished, the silver gone dark in the crevices, but the engraving on the front was still legible: a curling script that read I.M.

Iris March.

Lena pressed the latch. The locket opened. Inside, on one side, a photograph: Iris at twenty-three, hair dark and loose, standing beside a man in a paint-stained jacket. Julian. The same man from the photograph in the sewing room. On the other side, a lock of dark hair, coiled in tissue paper.

The locket had been hidden beneath the bench in the garden, placed there by someone who wanted it found, or placed there by someone who wanted it lost.

Lena closed the locket and put it in her pocket. She had found it under the apple tree, in the garden where Julian had painted Iris, in the place where something had begun that the rest of the house had never been allowed to know.

She sat on the bench for a long time, the locket warm in her pocket, the garden slowly coming back to order around her. The apple tree. The stone wall. The wild iris that had seeded itself in every corner. The sea, audible beyond the wall, breathing its long rhythm.

Her grandmother had sat here. Her great-grandmother. Her great-great-grandmother. All of them, in this same walled space, looking at the same sea, carrying the same weight of things unsaid.

Lena picked up the shears and went back to work.

Chapter 10: What the House Asks

By the end of her first week at Larklight, Lena had read all the journals.

She had read them in order: Sarah, Ada, Iris. She had arranged them on the kitchen table in chronological order, a hundred and forty-two years of writing spread across the oilcloth. She had read them in the mornings with coffee, in the afternoons with lunch, in the evenings with wine, late into the night by the light of the single bulb over the stove.

She had not gone into town. She had not called her mother. She had not answered the emails from her office asking when she would be back. She had barely slept.

The journals had taken her over.

Sarah March had lived at Larklight for forty-three years after Elias's death. She had raised three children — Lena's great-grandfather among them — and had watched Thomas Wainwright become the keeper and then retire and then die. She had lived through the end of one century and the beginning of another. Her last journal entry, written in 1925 when she was sixty-three, was two lines:

*I am too old to tend the light, but I am not too old to watch it. I will sit in the garden and watch it until I cannot see.*

Ada March had kept the journal through the influenza and through the years that followed. Edward had come home from Portland in December 1918, thin and silent, his hands shaking. He had never fully recovered. Ada had kept the light, had raised Ruth, had written in her journal when she could. The entries had grown sparser in the 1920s, then stopped in 1931. The last entry was about Ruth's wedding.

Iris March's journals began in 1952 and ended in 1978. They were the longest, the most detailed, the most carefully written. Lena had read them in order, watching her grandmother transform from a young woman arriving at a lighthouse she had never seen into a wife, a mother, a woman who had loved a man who was not her husband and had kept the evidence of that love in a sewing room at the end of the hall.

And now Lena sat at the kitchen table with the journals around her and did not know what to do next.

The house was quiet. The sea was gray. The lighthouse stood in its silence.

She had been at Larklight for seven days, and she had not decided anything. She had not decided whether to stay. She had not decided whether to sell. She had not decided whether to call her mother. She had not decided whether to look for the brother she had discovered in Iris's letters.

She had only read the journals, and reading the journals had changed something in her that she could not yet name.

She stood up from the table. She walked to the window. The lighthouse was dark against the afternoon sky. The lens was still.

She thought about her grandmother Iris, sitting in the garden with Julian Cross, letting him paint her portrait, knowing that what she was doing would cost her something she could not afford to pay. She thought about Iris climbing the tower alone on the last night of the manned light, saying goodbye to something that had defined her life. She thought about Iris keeping the letters in the sewing room, never mailing them, never throwing them away, keeping them as evidence that she had been alive.

She thought about Sarah, who had come to Larklight as a bride and had stayed as a widow. She thought about Ada, who had kept the light through a pandemic and had learned that she was capable of more than she had been taught to believe.

She thought about the trunk in the attic, and the journals she had read, and the story they told of women who had lived at the edge of the world and had left a record of that living.

Lena took out her phone. She scrolled to her mother's number. She had not called it in a year.

She pressed dial.

The phone rang four times. Then Margaret's voice, cautious and familiar: "Lena?"

"Mom," Lena said. "I'm at Larklight. I need to ask you something."

The silence on the other end of the line was long. Then Margaret said, "I wondered when you would call."

And Lena began to tell her what she had found.

Chapter 11: The Bride

Sarah March closed the journal of her first day at Larklight and set it aside. The rest of her story — the years between 1884 and 1925 — remained in the trunk, waiting.

But Lena was not the only one reading.

In the quiet of the keeper's house, in the evenings when the light was burning and the sea was calm, Sarah's voice rose from the pages and told the story of a woman who had come to Larklight as a stranger and had become, over decades, its truest inhabitant.

The story began in April 1884, when Sarah Bell of Boston married Elias March of Larklight Point, Maine, in a ceremony so small that the church had room for more empty pews than full ones. She was twenty-two. He was thirty-seven. They had met once, four months earlier, when Elias had come to Boston to purchase supplies and a mutual acquaintance had introduced them at a dinner party. Sarah had worn a blue dress. Elias had worn his uniform. They had exchanged seven sentences, and four weeks later he had written to her father requesting permission to court her, and six weeks after that he had proposed, and she had accepted, and now she was at Larklight, standing at the edge of a life she had not imagined.

She unpacked her valise in the bedroom that faced the sea. The room was small and spare: a bed, a dresser, a chair, a washstand, a window with curtains so thin they did nothing to dim the light. She hung her dresses in the wardrobe, placing them beside Elias's uniforms, the cloth of her sleeves brushing against the wool of his coats. She arranged her brush and comb on the dresser. She set her Bible on the nightstand.

The whole of her former life fit into a single valise.

She stood at the window and looked out at the sea. The fog had cleared in the afternoon, revealing a stretch of water that was almost blue, the whitecaps breaking over the ledges, the horizon sharp and distinct. She had never seen a horizon so wide. In Boston, the horizon was interrupted by buildings, by masts, by the green of the harbor islands. Here, there was nothing between her and the edge of the world but water.

Elias had gone to the tower. He spent most of his time in the tower. The light required attention — cleaning, adjusting, fueling, watching — and Elias gave it all the attention he had. Sarah had understood this before she came. She had been told that the keeper's duty was constant, that the light never slept, that the tower was as much a part of the marriage as the wife. She had understood it in theory. In practice, she discovered that the tower was a rival she could not defeat because it did not compete.

She made the house hers.

She washed the windows until they were clear of salt residue. She scrubbed the kitchen floor until the pine boards were pale with age. She sewed curtains for every window — light cotton for summer, heavy flannel for winter. She planted a garden in the walled enclosure behind the house: vegetables in rows, flowers along the walls, a lavender bush that had survived from some earlier keeper's wife and that she tended with particular care.

She learned to cook on the wood stove, which had moods and opinions and refused to cooperate with recipes written for gas ranges in Boston kitchens. She learned to manage the kerosene lamps, to trim their wicks and clean their chimneys and keep them burning through the long winter nights. She learned to read the weather from the look of the sea, the feel of the wind, the way the light changed before a storm.

And she learned to live with solitude.

It was not that Elias was unkind. He was not. He was attentive in his way — he asked if she had enough wood for the fire, if the supply boat had brought everything she needed, if she was warm enough at night. But his attention was divided, and the larger share of it belonged to the light. He rose before dawn, checked the mechanism, trimmed the wick, lit the flame, extinguished the morning light, cleaned the lens, made repairs, recorded the weather, and did it all again at dusk. In between, he slept and ate and spoke in sentences that were efficient but not expansive.

Sarah learned to fill the hours between his brief appearances.

She read. She sewed. She walked the point in any weather that did not threaten to blow her into the sea. She learned the names of the birds that nested on the ledges — gulls, cormorants, guillemots, puffins. She watched the seals that hauled out on the outer rocks at low tide. She learned the rhythms of the tide, the phases of the moon, the constellations that appeared in the winter sky and the different ones that took their place in summer.

She wrote in her journal.

The journal became her companion, her confessor, the witness to a life that no one else saw. She wrote about the weather, the garden, the small triumphs of a successful loaf of bread or a well-tended lamp. She wrote about her loneliness, which she did not name as loneliness but described as a feeling of being translated into a language no one else spoke. She wrote about Elias: his silences, his absences, the occasional moments when he looked at her as if she were a stranger who had wandered into his house and he was still deciding whether to ask her to leave.

And she wrote about the lighthouse.

The lighthouse, she discovered, was not a building. It was a presence. It stood at the edge of the point, visible from every room in the house, its light sweeping the sea in a rhythm that became, over weeks and months, the pulse of her life. She learned to sleep through the beam passing across the window. She learned to wake when the rhythm changed — when the light faltered, when the fog set in and the horn began its lowing. She learned that the lighthouse had a voice, and that voice was the sound of safety for every ship within its range.

She had not expected to love it. She had not expected to understand it. But by the end of her first winter at Larklight, Sarah March knew the lighthouse better than she knew her own husband, and she had begun to suspect that this was not a failure of marriage but the nature of life at the edge of the world.

Chapter 12: The First Winter

The winter of 1884 was the coldest in twenty years.

That was what Elias told her, and Sarah believed him, because she had nothing else to measure it against. She had lived through Boston winters — winters of slush and gray snow and wind that came off the harbor — but she had never lived through a winter like this. The cold was absolute. It entered the house through walls that had stood for decades and found every crack, every gap, every weakness in the insulation. It frosted the windows from the inside. It turned the water in the pitcher beside the bed to ice overnight. It made the wood stove work constantly, consuming fuel faster than the supply boat could deliver it.

Elias spent more time in the tower than ever. The cold affected the mechanism — the oil thickened, the lens fogged, the brass contracted in ways that required adjustment. He climbed the stairs a dozen times a day, checking, adjusting, recording. Sarah watched him from the kitchen window, a dark figure moving against the white of the tower, and felt a loneliness so deep it was physical.

She wrote in her journal every day.

*January 3, 1885*

*The cold has not broken. Elias says the bay is freezing outward from the shore. He has seen ice forming on the ledges at low tide, a thing he says he has witnessed only three times in his years at Larklight. The spray freezes on the tower glass. He must chip it away each morning.*

*I have not left the house in four days. The path to the tower is impassable for anyone but Elias, who knows the granite well enough to find his footing in the dark. I stand at the window and watch him cross the open ground with his head down and his shoulders braced against the wind, and I think about the distance between us. It is not the distance across the yard. It is the distance between a woman who needs to speak and a man who needs to tend.*

*I do not blame him. I do not think he is wrong to prioritize the light. The light is the reason we are here. The light is the difference between a ship finding harbor and a ship breaking on the ledges. I understand this. I accept it. But understanding and acceptance do not keep a woman warm when her husband is in the tower and the wind is shaking the windows and the house feels like a ship that has lost its mooring.*

*I have begun talking to the journal as if it were a person. I call it my reader, though I know no one will read these pages. Perhaps someday someone will. Perhaps someday a woman will sit in this kitchen and read my words and know that I was here, that I lived, that I was lonely and cold and uncertain and still I stayed.*

*If you are that woman, know this: I am trying. I am trying to be a wife, a keeper's wife, a woman who can live at the edge of the sea without being swallowed by it. I do not know if I am succeeding. But I am trying.*

February was worse. The storms came one after another, each one piling more snow against the house, more ice on the tower. The supply boat could not get through. The food stores ran low. Sarah rationed flour, sugar, coffee, salt pork, the jars of preserved vegetables from her summer garden. She baked bread with the last of the yeast, stretching the dough with water and hope.

Elias fell ill in late February. A cough that started as a dry irritation became a chest-rattling congestion that kept him in bed for three days. Sarah tended the light.

She had watched him do it enough times. She knew the steps: trim the wick, fill the oil reservoir, clean the lens, wind the clockwork mechanism, check the alignment. She knew the eighty-seven steps, the temperature of the lantern room, the way the light changed when the glass was clean. She climbed the tower at dusk, lit the flame, watched it catch and steady, then climbed back down to check on her husband.

Elias did not thank her. He was too ill to speak much. But when she brought him broth and bread, he looked at her with something new in his eyes — not gratitude, exactly, but recognition. He saw her. For the first time since she had arrived, he saw her.

The cough passed. Elias returned to the tower. Sarah returned to the house. The spring came slowly, the ice retreating from the ledges, the first green appearing in the garden.

Something had changed between them, though neither of them spoke of it. A door had opened, just a crack, and the cold that had filled the space between them had begun to warm.

Sarah wrote in her journal:

*April 12, 1885*

*The first daffodil has bloomed in the garden. It is not much — a single yellow flower against the gray stone — but I knelt beside it and wept. I do not know why. Perhaps because it is proof that winter ends. Perhaps because it is beautiful, and beauty is the thing I have missed most.*

*Elias saw me weeping. He stood in the garden doorway, uncertain, and then he crossed the grass and knelt beside me and looked at the daffodil as if he were seeing it for the first time. He said, You planted those. I said, Yes. He said, I did not know they would come back.*

*I think he was not talking about the flowers.*

Chapter 13: Thomas Arrives

The supply boat came on the first of May, bringing the spring provisions and a passenger.

Sarah was in the garden when she saw the boat rounding the point. It was a day of clear light and fresh wind, the sea a deep blue, the sky the particular pale of early spring. She had been waiting for the boat — the stores were nearly depleted, and she had been making do with dried beans and the last of the salt fish — but she had not been expecting a passenger.

She watched the boat tie up at the landing. A figure in a dark coat stood on the deck, a single bag at his feet. He was young, lean, with a face that was handsome in a way that was not quite conventional. He looked up at the lighthouse, then at the house, and Sarah saw him take a breath as if steadying himself.

Elias came down from the tower. He had seen the boat. He walked to the landing with the measured stride of a man who did not hurry for anyone.

Sarah followed at a distance, curiosity pulling her across the yard.

The young man stepped onto the pier and extended his hand to Elias. "Thomas Wainwright," he said. "Assistant Keeper. The Lighthouse Service sent me."

Elias shook his hand without warmth. "I did not ask for an assistant."

"The Service thought you might need one. The winters here are long."

Elias said nothing. His jaw was tight. Sarah saw the stiffness in his shoulders, the way his hand stayed at his side instead of reaching out to take the newcomer's bag.

She stepped forward. "I am Sarah March. Welcome to Larklight."

Thomas Wainwright turned to her, and his face changed. The careful formality softened into something genuine. "Mrs. March. Thank you. I hope I will not be a burden."

"Every extra pair of hands is a help in summer," Sarah said, which was not entirely true — she had managed the house alone for a year and did not know what to do with another person in it — but it was the polite thing to say.

She led him to the house while Elias stayed at the landing, staring at the boat as if he could make it disappear through force of will.

The assistant keeper's cottage had been built at the same time as the main house, a smaller structure set behind the keeper's quarters, but it had been used for storage for years. Sarah had not even known it was meant to be a residence. She opened the door and found a single room with a bed frame, a table, a chair, and a layer of dust thick enough to write in.

"I am sorry," she said. "I did not know you were coming."

Thomas set his bag on the table. "I am used to making do. This will be fine."

"It will be fine after I clean it. Give me an hour."

He smiled. It was a warm smile, unexpected, and Sarah found herself smiling back.

"I will explore the point," he said. "I have heard Larklight is one of the most beautiful stations on the coast. I would like to see it."

He left her with the cottage and walked toward the sea. Sarah watched him go — a young man in a dark coat, climbing the granite slope with the ease of someone who was comfortable in his own body — and then she turned to the task of making the cottage livable.

She scrubbed the floor, swept the cobwebs from the corners, aired the mattress, found clean linens in the house. She put a vase of daffodils on the table because it was what she would have wanted if she had arrived at a strange place to find nothing prepared.

By evening, Thomas Wainwright had moved in.

He was quiet at dinner. The three of them sat at the kitchen table — Elias at the head, Sarah at his right, Thomas at his left — and the meal passed with the kind of conversation that was careful not to touch anything important. Thomas asked about the light. Elias answered in monosyllables. Thomas asked about the local waters. Elias gave the names of the ledges and the channels between them. Thomas asked about the keeper's duties. Elias said, "You will learn them."

After dinner, Thomas helped Sarah clear the dishes. Elias went to the tower for the evening check.

"I am sorry," Thomas said quietly, when Elias was out of earshot. "I did not mean to arrive as an imposition. The Service told me the keeper had requested assistance."

"He did not mention it to me."

"Men do not always mention things to their wives."

Sarah looked at him. There was no judgment in his voice, only observation. "No," she said. "They do not."

"He does not want me here."

"I know."

"Will that cause difficulty for you?"

It was a surprising question. A stranger, asking about her marriage. But his tone was not intrusive. It was concerned.

"I do not know," Sarah said honestly. "I hope not."

Thomas nodded. "I will try not to make things harder than they already are."

He took the dish towel from her hand and began drying the plates. Sarah watched him for a moment, then turned back to the sink.

Something had arrived at Larklight with Thomas Wainwright. She did not yet know what it was, but she felt it — a shift in the air, a new element in the chemistry of the house. She did not know whether to welcome it or fear it.

She decided, for the moment, to do neither. She would watch. She would wait. She would write it all down in her journal, and perhaps, in the reading, she would understand.

Chapter 14: Cora's Visit

Cora Bell arrived at Larklight in the summer of 1887, three years after Sarah's wedding, and the house woke up.

Sarah had not realized how quiet the house had become until her younger sister stepped through the door and filled it with noise. Cora talked constantly — about Boston, about their parents, about the opera, about the new styles in hats, about the young men who had called on her and the ones she had refused. She laughed at everything, including things that were not funny. She sang while she unpacked. She asked questions that Sarah could not answer and did not wait for the answers before asking more.

But beneath the chatter, Sarah saw something else. Cora was running from something. She had arrived unannounced, with a single bag, and she had not said when she planned to leave. Her eyes, when Sarah caught them unguarded, were the eyes of someone who had been looking over her shoulder for too long.

They sat in the garden on Cora's second evening, drinking tea that Sarah had made from the mint that grew wild along the wall. The sun was low, the sea was calm, and the lighthouse had just begun to turn its beam across the darkening water. Cora watched the beam as it swept, her face unreadable.

"So this is your life," she said. It was not a question.

"This is my life."

"It is very quiet."

"Yes."

"Do you like it?"

Sarah considered the question. No one had asked her that before. Elias had not asked. Her mother had not asked. She had not asked herself.

"Yes," she said. "I think I do."

Cora looked at her sharply. "You think?"

"I have learned to like it. It was not immediate. The first winter was hard. I thought I would go mad from the silence. But I have learned that silence is not emptiness. It is its own kind of language."

"And the keeper?"

"His name is Elias."

"And Elias. Do you like him?"

Sarah picked a leaf of mint and crushed it between her fingers, releasing the scent. "He is a good man. He tends the light. He provides for the house. He does not raise his voice or strike me or drink to excess."

"That is not what I asked."

"I know."

Cora was quiet for a moment. The beam swept across them again. "When I was a child," she said, "I thought marriage was about conversation. Two people talking through a lifetime. Now I know it is about something else. Proximity. Endurance. The ability to stand beside someone without needing them to speak."

Sarah looked at her sister. "That is a sad thing to know at twenty."

"It is a true thing at any age."

They drank their tea in silence. The mint was cool on Sarah's tongue. She watched the lighthouse beam continue its patient arc.

Then Cora said, "I am with child."

Sarah's hand stopped moving. The crushed mint fell from her fingers.

"The father is a married man," Cora continued, her voice flat, as if she had rehearsed the words. "He will not acknowledge it. My condition will become visible in two months. I could not stay in Boston."

"Who knows?"

"No one but you. And now I have told you, and you can decide whether to turn me out or keep me."

Sarah took her sister's hand. It was cold, despite the summer evening. "You will stay here."

"Mama and Papa do not know."

"They will not learn it from me."

"I cannot go back."

"Then you will not go back. You will stay here, and we will manage. The house is large. The garden needs work. There is always something to do at Larklight."

Cora's composure cracked. Her face crumpled, and she began to cry — not the dramatic tears Sarah had expected, but quiet, exhausted weeping, as if she had been holding the grief for weeks and had only now found a place safe enough to release it.

Sarah held her. She did not offer comfort in words. She simply sat beside her sister on the stone bench, her arm around her shoulders, letting the night air and the sound of the sea do what words could not.

The lighthouse beam swept across the garden, a rhythm of light and dark, light and dark.

That night, Sarah wrote in her journal by the light of the kerosene lamp, her pen moving slowly across the page:

*Cora is here. She is carrying a child that will have no father. She is afraid. I am afraid for her. But she is here, and that is something. The house is large enough for two women's secrets.*

*I have not told Elias. I will tell him when she is ready. For now, I will let her rest. For now, I will let the house hold us both.*

*I think this is what sisters are for. Not to solve each other's problems, but to sit beside each other in the dark and wait for the light to return.*

Chapter 15: The Obsession

The change in Elias began gradually, the way a crack in a stone wall begins — invisible for years, then suddenly wide enough to see through.

Sarah noticed it first in his sleep. Elias had always been a light sleeper, waking at any change in the wind, any variation in the rhythm of the light. But now he barely slept at all. He lay awake, staring at the ceiling, his body tense, his breathing shallow. When she asked if he was ill, he said he was fine. When she pressed, he turned away.

She noticed it next in his conversation. He had never been a talkative man, but now he was almost silent. He answered her questions with single words. He did not ask about her day, about the garden, about Cora. He ate his meals without tasting them, his eyes fixed on some middle distance that Sarah could not enter.

And she noticed it in his relationship with the light.

He spent more and more time in the tower. He would go up in the morning and stay until midday, then return after dinner and stay until the early hours. He was not just tending the light — he was sitting beside it, watching it, speaking to it in a low murmur that Sarah could not quite hear.

She mentioned it to Thomas.

They stood at the garden wall on an evening in late July, watching the sunset stain the western clouds. Cora was inside, resting. Elias was in the tower, as usual.

"He is not well," Thomas said. It was not a question.

"I know."

"I think it is the isolation. Some men cannot bear it. They begin to... dissolve."

Sarah looked at him. "Dissolve?"

"Lose the boundaries. Between themselves and the light, themselves and the sea, themselves and the dark. I have seen it before, in older keepers. They start to believe the light is speaking to them."

"And what does one do about it?"

Thomas was quiet for a long time. The sunset faded from orange to purple to gray. The lighthouse beam began its swing across the darkening water.

"I do not know," he said finally. "I do not think the Service has a protocol for it."

Sarah folded her arms across her chest. The wind was cold, even in July. "He will not leave. The light is his life."

"The light will kill him, if he does not leave it."

It was the first time anyone had said the truth aloud, and the words hung in the air between them, heavy and undeniable.

Sarah went to bed that night alone. Elias did not come down from the tower until dawn, and when he did, he did not come to the bedroom. She found him in the kitchen, drinking coffee, his eyes red, his hands trembling.

"I am fine," he said, before she could speak.

"You are not fine."

"I am the keeper of Larklight Light. I have been the keeper for fifteen years. I know this tower better than any man alive. I know its sounds, its rhythms, its needs. The light needs me."

"The light needs oil and a clean wick. It does not need you to sit beside it all night."

"You do not understand."

"No. I do not. Explain it to me."

Elias looked at her, and for a moment she saw something in his eyes that she had never seen before: fear. Not fear of the sea, or the storm, or the cold. Fear of himself.

"The light speaks," he said. "It speaks to me. It tells me things."

"What things?"

"I cannot explain. You would not understand."

"Try."

He shook his head. He stood up, left his coffee untouched on the table, and walked back to the tower.

Sarah sat at the kitchen table, alone, the coffee growing cold in front of her. Outside, the beam of Larklight swept its patient arc across the sea.

She picked up her journal. She wrote:

*He is not well. He believes the light speaks to him. I do not know what to do. I do not know who to tell. The Service would remove him. The town would whisper. He would have nothing left.*

*But if I do nothing, I am afraid of what he will become.*

*I am keeping a secret that may destroy us. I do not know how to stop.*

Chapter 16: What Thomas Knows

Thomas Wainwright had been at Larklight for three years by the summer of 1888, and in that time he had learned to read Elias March the way a sailor reads the sky: by the signs he did not want to see.

He had watched the keeper's slide into obsession with a growing sense of dread. It was not dramatic — Elias did not rave or rage or wander. He simply withdrew, hour by hour, day by day, into a private world whose center was the lantern room. The lens was his altar. The flame was his god.

Thomas had reported his concerns to the Lighthouse Service in his quarterly letters, phrasing them carefully so as not to sound alarmist. *Keeper March appears fatigued. The winter was severe. I have assumed additional watch duties to allow him rest.* The Service had not replied. They did not reply to such letters. Keepers were expected to manage their own stations.

But Thomas had also noticed something else, something he had not put in any letter. Elias was falsifying the log.

The keeper's log was a legal document. Every entry was supposed to be an accurate record of conditions at the station. But Thomas had begun to find discrepancies: wind speeds that did not match his own observations, visibility ratings that were optimistic to the point of fantasy, remarks that described weather that had not occurred.

He confronted Elias in the spring of 1889.

They stood in the lantern room, the lens turning slowly behind them, the sea spread out in all directions. Thomas had brought the log book. He had marked the entries that did not match his own records.

"These are wrong," he said.

Elias did not look at the book. He was looking at the lens, watching the prisms catch the afternoon light and throw it in fragments across the walls.

"The log must be accurate," Thomas continued. "If the Service audits—"

"Let them audit."

"If they find discrepancies, they will question your fitness."

Elias turned. His eyes were clear, focused, and Thomas realized with a start that Elias was not confused or delusional. He knew exactly what he was doing.

"The log is a record of what the light sees," Elias said. "The light sees more than you do. The light sees the weather that is coming, not the weather that is here. I record what the light tells me."

Thomas held his ground. "The light is a machine. It does not tell you anything."

"The light is not a machine. It is a living thing. It has been burning at this point for five years. It knows this coast better than any man. I am its keeper. I listen to it."

"Elias—"

"You are my assistant. You assist. You do not question the keeper's judgment."

Thomas closed the log book. He had known, before he came to Larklight, that remote stations could change a man. He had heard stories of keepers who had gone mad in their towers, who had been found talking to the lens, who had had to be removed by force. He had not believed the stories. He believed them now.

"I will keep my own record," he said quietly. "For the Service."

Elias shrugged. "Keep whatever records you like. The light and I know the truth."

Thomas descended the tower in silence. He found Sarah in the garden, weeding the lavender bed. She looked up when he approached, and she must have seen something in his face, because she set down her trowel and stood.

"What is it?"

"Your husband is falsifying the log. He believes the light speaks to him. I think he is losing his grip on reality."

Sarah did not look surprised. She looked like a woman who had been waiting for someone else to say the words out loud.

"I know," she said.

"You know?"

"I have been watching him for years. I did not know how to describe it. I did not want to believe it."

"We need to report him."

"And then what? They will remove him. They will pension him. He will have nothing. He will become a man who lost his light, and it will kill him faster than the madness."

"Sarah." Thomas used her first name for the first time, and the sound of it stopped her. "He is dangerous. Not to us — not yet — but to himself. If he does something to the light, if he lets it go out, or if he does something to himself in the tower—"

"He would never let the light go out."

"Are you sure?"

Sarah did not answer. She looked past him, at the tower, where Elias was visible through the lantern room glass, a dark figure against the sky.

"I will talk to him," she said. "One more time."

"And if it does not help?"

She did not answer. Thomas waited, but she did not answer, because they both knew that there was no answer that would make anything right.

Chapter 17: Cora Stays

Cora Bell did not go back to Boston.

She stayed at Larklight through the summer of 1887, and then through the autumn, and then through the winter. The child was born in February 1888, a girl with dark hair and her mother's wide-set eyes. Sarah delivered her, with only the lighthouse beam for light and a book on midwifery that Cora had brought from Boston.

The birth was hard. Cora labored for eighteen hours, her cries swallowed by the wind that pressed against the windows. Sarah held her sister's hand, wiped her forehead, read aloud from the midwifery book by candlelight, and prayed to a God she was not sure she believed in. Thomas, hearing the sounds from the cottage, built a fire in the kitchen stove and kept water hot all night, ready to send for a doctor if needed.

But the baby came on her own, as babies do, at dawn when the lighthouse beam was at its palest and the sea was the color of pearl. She was small, perfect, and alive.

They named her Eleanor. Cora had chosen the name weeks before, writing it on a scrap of paper and folding it into her pocket. "It means light," she told Sarah. "I want her to have that, at least."

Cora wrote to their parents with the news: a birth, a healthy child, a decision to remain in Maine. She did not mention the absence of a father. She did not mention that she had no plan beyond the next day, and the day after that, and the day after that. She wrote her letter with a steady hand and a heart that had learned to lie for the sake of survival.

She stayed because there was nowhere else to go. She stayed because Sarah needed her — needed someone to talk to, someone who understood the weight of keeping secrets. She stayed because the lighthouse, for all its isolation, was safer than the world she had left.

Eleanor grew. She learned to walk on the granite slopes, her small hands gripping the rock, her feet finding purchase on surfaces that would have sent a less determined child tumbling. She learned to speak in the rhythm of the fog signal, to sleep through the sweep of the beam across the window. She called Sarah "Aunt" and Cora "Mama" and Elias "the keeper," which was what everyone called him. She did not ask why she had no father. She was too young to know that families had rules.

The house that had been built for a keeper and his wife became a house of women. Sarah and Cora managed the domestic life together, dividing the work, sharing the silences. They read aloud in the evenings — novels from the small library that had accumulated on the parlor shelves, poetry, the Bible when the weather was bad. They took turns cooking, discovering that two women could make a meal taste better than one. They sat in the garden when the weather allowed, watching Eleanor chase butterflies, listening to the sea.

They did not talk about the future, because the future was a luxury neither of them could afford. They lived in the present, in the daily rhythm of tending a house and a child and a garden. They made each day hold what it could.

But Cora watched Elias. She saw what Sarah saw — the slow dissolution, the growing distance, the way he looked at the lighthouse as if it were the only real thing in the world. She said nothing. She did not need to. The truth was written in the space between the keeper and his wife, in the meals he did not finish, the conversations he did not start, the way he touched the doorframe of the house as if he were leaving it even when he was coming in.

One evening in the spring of 1889, when Eleanor was a year old and the garden was beginning to bloom, Cora found Sarah in the kitchen, staring at the wall.

"You cannot save him," Cora said.

Sarah did not turn. "I know."

"You have to decide what you will save instead."

"I know that too."

"Is it yourself?"

Sarah turned. Her face was pale, her eyes red-rimmed, her hands gripping the edge of the counter as if she were holding herself upright. "I do not know. I do not know what I am saving anymore. I have been here for five years. I have kept this house, this garden, this marriage. I have kept his secrets. I do not know who I would be if I stopped."

"You would be Sarah," Cora said. "You would be my sister. You would be the woman who planted daffodils in a place where nothing was supposed to grow, and they grew anyway. You would be the woman who delivered my daughter and held my hand and did not ask me to explain. You would still be all the things you have always been."

Sarah's eyes filled with tears. "That does not feel like enough."

"It is more than most people ever become."

They stood together in the kitchen, the lighthouse beam passing across the window, the sea breathing in its long rhythm. The house held them, and they held each other, and neither of them knew what the morning would bring, but for that moment, they were not alone.

Chapter 18: The Storm

The nor'easter of December 1891 arrived without warning.

The barometer had been falling all day, but Elias had said nothing about it. He had been in the tower, as always, and Sarah had been in the house, and Thomas had been in the cottage, and none of them had seen the sky build its fury.

The first wave of wind hit at dusk.

Sarah was lighting the kitchen lamps when the house shuddered — a deep, structural groan that came up through the floor and resonated in the walls. She had heard storms before, had weathered them, but this was different. This was the sea rising against the land with intent.

She went to the window. The sea was white. Not white with foam — white with the sheer force of the wind tearing the tops from the waves and turning them into a mist that blotted out the horizon. The lighthouse beam was already burning, swinging through the chaos, but it seemed small against the scale of the weather.

Elias was in the tower. He had not come down for dinner. He had not come down for anything.

Thomas appeared at the kitchen door, rain streaming from his oilskin coat. "The keeper is in the tower?"

"Yes."

"He should not be alone."

"He will not leave the light."

"I know." Thomas looked at the window, at the sea, at the tower. "I am going up."

"Be careful."

He nodded and was gone, swallowed by the dark and the wind.

Sarah waited. The storm built. The wind rose to a shriek, and the house shook with each gust, and the rain hit the windows like gravel. She lit every lamp in the kitchen, not because she needed the light but because she needed the warmth.

Hours passed.

At midnight, the light went out.

Sarah felt it before she saw it — a change in the rhythm, an absence where the beam should have been. She ran to the window. The lantern room was dark. The tower was dark. The sea was invisible, the horizon erased, the world reduced to the small circle of the kitchen windows and the storm pressing against them.

The light had gone out.

She grabbed her coat and ran.

The path to the tower was slick with ice, the wind pushing her sideways, the rain turning to sleet that stung her face. She had never run this path in the dark. She had never needed to. She stumbled, caught herself, stumbled again. The tower rose ahead, dark and blind.

The service door was open. She went in.

The staircase was lit only by the lantern she had brought, a small oil lamp that threw shadows that leaped and twisted with her movement. She climbed. Eighty-seven steps. She counted them because counting was all she could do.

At the top, the lantern room door was open.

Thomas was on his knees beside the lens. The flame was out. The wick was smoking. And Elias was lying on the floor.

He was breathing. That was the first thing Sarah saw. His chest rose and fell. But his eyes were open, staring at the ceiling, and his lips were moving — forming words she could not hear.

"He collapsed," Thomas said. "I found him here. The light—"

"I will relight it."

She had never done it alone. She had watched, had assisted, but she had never been the one to trim the wick and strike the match and set the flame burning in its safe glass enclosure. She did it now.

The light caught. The lens began its slow rotation. The beam swept across the window, and the dark outside was pushed back, if only a little.

Sarah knelt beside her husband.

"Elias."

He looked at her. His eyes were clear, but they were not the eyes of a man who was seeing her.

"The light told me to rest," he said. "It said I had done enough."

"You have done enough," Sarah said. "You can rest now."

She did not know if she meant it for the night or for longer. She did not know if she meant it for his sake or for hers.

Thomas helped her get Elias down the stairs. They half-carried, half-guided him across the path to the house, through the wind and the sleet, and laid him in his bed. Cora had lit the stove and put water on to heat. Eleanor was sleeping through it all, innocent of the night.

Sarah sat beside Elias until dawn. He slept, or seemed to sleep. His breathing was steady. His hand, when she held it, was warm.

The storm passed. The light burned. The sea returned to its ordinary rhythms.

But something had changed. Something had broken in Elias that could not be repaired, and Sarah knew, as she sat in the gray dawn light, that the life she had known at Larklight was coming to an end.

Chapter 19: The Aftermath

The Lighthouse Service sent a physician in January, a man named Dr. Alden who had visited remote stations from the Grand Banks to the Florida Keys. He had seen everything, he told Sarah, and nothing surprised him anymore. He examined Elias, spoke with him in the kitchen for two hours, observed him in the tower as he sat beside the cold lens, and filed a report whose official language could not quite conceal its conclusion: the keeper was no longer fit for duty. The words he used were "nervous exhaustion" and "moral despondency," which Sarah recognized as the medical profession's way of saying something they did not fully understand.

Elias March was relieved of his position on May 3, 1892. The log entry was one line, written in Thomas's careful hand: *Keeper Elias March relieved of duty due to illness.*

Elias did not resist. He did not argue. He sat in the kitchen while Thomas read him the order, his hands folded on the table, his eyes focused on some middle distance that the rest of them could not see. He nodded as if he had been expecting it, as if the light had already told him.

"What will you do?" Sarah asked him that night.

He was sitting in the rocking chair by the stove, a blanket over his knees. He had aged ten years in the six months since the storm. His hands, once steady enough to trim a wick by feel, trembled constantly. His hair had gone gray at the temples, then everywhere.

"I will stay here," he said. "If I may."

"Of course you may."

"I do not need to be the keeper. I only need to be near the light."

Sarah looked at him. The man she had married eight years ago was still there, somewhere, beneath the layers of exhaustion and obsession. She could see him in the shape of his shoulders, the line of his jaw, the way his eyes still found the lighthouse through the kitchen window.

"You will always be near the light," she said. "This is your home."

He nodded. He did not speak again that night.

Thomas Wainwright was appointed acting keeper. He moved his things from the assistant's cottage to the main house, because the cottage was needed for the new assistant, a young man named Silas Bell who was no relation to Sarah's family despite the name. Thomas took the room at the end of the hall, the one that had been used for storage, and he filled it with his few possessions — his books, his charts, his spare uniform, the small box of letters from his mother that he kept under the bed.

Sarah watched the transition with a strange detachment. She had been the keeper's wife for eight years. Now she was the former keeper's wife, living in the keeper's house with her husband, who was no longer the keeper, and her sister, and her niece, and the new keeper, who was not her husband. The geography of the house had shifted, and she was still learning where the new boundaries lay.

The house rearranged itself around the change. Thomas took the watch duties, climbing the tower at dusk and dawn with the same precision Elias had once shown. Sarah continued to manage the domestic life — cooking, cleaning, gardening, mending. Cora helped with Eleanor, who was now four years old and full of questions that no one knew how to answer. And Elias sat in the rocking chair by the window, watching the lighthouse, speaking only when spoken to.

It was not the life Sarah had imagined. It was not a life she had ever imagined. But it was a life, and she was living it, and she was learning that survival was its own kind of victory. She was learning that a woman could lose everything — her husband, her marriage, the future she had planned — and still find herself standing at the kitchen stove, making soup, watching the light come through the window.

The spring came slowly that year. The ice retreated from the ledges. The daffodils bloomed again, the same bulbs Sarah had planted in her first spring at Larklight, returning as they always did, indifferent to the sorrows of the people who tended them.

Sarah knelt beside them one morning, her hands in the cold earth, and she felt something she had not expected: the smallest thread of hope.

Chapter 20: Sarah's Choice

Elias March died on a Tuesday in April 1892, three weeks after his removal from duty.

He died quietly, in his sleep, in the bed where he had slept beside his wife for eight years. Sarah woke to find him still beside her, his hand on the pillow where her head had been, his face peaceful. She did not know whether he had reached for her in the night or whether he had always slept with his hand there, a gesture of tenderness he had never been able to make in waking.

She sat beside the bed for a long time. The room was quiet. The lighthouse beam had stopped its sweep at dawn, and the morning light was gray and steady through the window. She heard the gulls crying on the ledges, the sound of Thomas building the fire in the kitchen stove, the distant rumble of the sea.

She did not cry. She had done all her crying in the years before, when Elias had been alive but already gone. What she felt now was not grief. It was something quieter — a release, a door closing, the end of a vigil she had not known she was keeping.

Sarah buried her husband in the small cemetery on the hill behind the lighthouse, where a previous keeper had been laid to rest and where the view of the sea was unobstructed. The gravedigger came from town, a man named Pelletier who had buried half the families on the coast. He asked if she wanted a stone. She said yes. She chose granite, the same stone as the tower, and she had it carved with Elias's name and the dates of his birth and death, and below them, the word *Keeper*.

She stood at the grave with Cora and Thomas and Eleanor, and she did not cry, because she had done all her crying in the years before.

She stayed.

The question presented itself: would she remain at Larklight? The house belonged to the Lighthouse Service, not to her. Elias's position had been his, not hers. She had no claim to the keeper's house, no right to the tower, no reason to stay.

But Thomas Wainwright, now the official keeper, said, "The house is too large for one man. The Service will not object to you staying. And Eleanor should not lose her home twice."

Sarah looked at him. He had been a good assistant, a good friend, a witness to her marriage and its slow dissolution. She did not love him. She was not sure she would ever love anyone again. But she trusted him, and that was something.

"I will stay," she said.

She wrote in her journal that night, sitting at the kitchen table by the light of the kerosene lamp, her pen moving slowly across the page:

*Elias is gone. The light still burns. I did not think both things could be true, but they are.*

*I will stay at Larklight. Where else would I go? The light is the only thing that has not lied to me.*

*I am no longer a wife. I am no longer a keeper's wife. But I am still Sarah March, and I am still here, and I will keep this house as I have kept it for eight years. I will watch the light. I will plant the garden. I will raise Eleanor as my own, because Cora cannot do it alone, and because a child should grow up knowing the sound of the sea.*

*This is my life now. I did not choose it, but I will live it.*

The journal entry was the last for many months. When Sarah picked up her pen again, it was 1893, and the world had moved on. Eleanor was learning to read. Cora had taken over the garden. Thomas had become a steady, quiet presence in the house.

But Sarah had stayed. Sarah March had stayed at Larklight Point, and she would stay for forty-three more years, until she was too old to climb the path to the tower, and then she would sit in the garden and watch the light until she could not see.

She was the first, but she would not be the last.

Chapter 21: The Second Journal Opens

Ada March did not begin her journal intending to continue a tradition. She began it because she was alone at Larklight with a six-year-old child and a husband who had gone to war and was now going to a different kind of war, one fought against an enemy that could not be seen but was killing faster than any bullet.

The brown leather journal had been in the house when she arrived as a bride in 1915. It was on a shelf in the parlor, between a copy of *The Wide, Wide World* and a guide to coastal navigation. There was no inscription, no explanation. It was simply there, waiting, as if the house knew she would need it.

She began writing in September 1918, the day Edward left for Portland.

The journal had already contained pages: Sarah's entries from the 1880s, the last of which ended in 1892. Ada read them first, sitting at the kitchen table while Ruth played in the garden, and she recognized in Sarah's voice something familiar — the loneliness of a woman who had come to Larklight unprepared, who had learned to live with silence, who had found her own way through.

Ada wrote her first entry on September 15:

*I do not know if I am writing for myself or for whoever comes after. Sarah March wrote for forty years in this house, and I read her words and felt less alone. Perhaps someday someone will read mine and feel the same.*

*Today, Edward left for Portland. Ruth asked when he would come back. I said, When the sickness is over. She said, When will that be? I said, I do not know.*

*The light is mine now. I have watched Edward tend it for three years. I know how to trim the wick, how to clean the lens, how to wind the mechanism, how to read the weather by the way the flame behaves. I know the eighty-seven steps. I know the sound the tower makes when the wind is from the east, and the different sound it makes when the wind is from the south.*

*I did not know I knew these things until he was gone.*

*Ruth cried at dinner. She said she wanted her father. I told her I wanted him too. We sat at the table and cried together, and then we washed the dishes and I read her a story and she fell asleep with her head on my arm.*

*Tomorrow I will light the light. Tonight I will sit in the kitchen and learn to be alone.*

Chapter 22: The Town Quarantines

The quarantine came quickly.

One day there was a town — Stillwater-on-Sea, twelve miles down the coast, reachable by boat in good weather and by foot over the headland road in any weather. There was a store, a post office, a church, a school, a physician's office, a wharf where the supply boat docked twice a week. There were people: the Harbachs, the Pells, the Stantons, the Crosses.

The next day, there was no town.

It was not a legal quarantine. There was no official order, no sign at the town line. But the supply boat stopped coming, and the mail stopped arriving, and the road became empty of traffic, and Ada understood that the town had closed itself off from the world, and Larklight was closed off with it.

She learned the news from Dr. Nathaniel Parrish, who rowed out to the lighthouse on the third day of the quarantine. He stood at the landing, his boat drawn up on the pebbles, his face gray with exhaustion.

"The influenza has reached Stillwater," he said. "I am advising everyone to isolate. No unnecessary contact. The boat will not come until we know more."

Ada stood on the pier. She had been expecting this, but the words still landed like a blow.

"What do we need to do?"

"Stay home. Limit contact. Boil your water. If anyone develops a fever—" He stopped. "Send word. I will come."

"You cannot come every time someone has a fever. There are only so many of you."

"Then I will come when I can."

He gave her a package: a small bottle of quinine, rolls of clean bandages, a jar of something that smelled like camphor. "For emergencies."

"How bad is it in town?"

"Bad. The mill is closed. The school is closed. The church is being used as a hospital." He paused. "The Harbach twins died yesterday."

Ada had known the Harbach twins. They were seven years old, freckled and loud, and they had come to Larklight once for a picnic on the point. She had given them gingerbread.

"How many others?"

"I do not have an accurate count. People are dying faster than I can record them."

He left. Ada watched his boat disappear into the fog, and then she walked back to the house and washed her hands with soap and boiled water.

That night, she wrote in her journal:

*Dr. Parrish came today. The town is sick. The twins are dead. I am afraid.*

*I have never been afraid like this. I have been afraid of storms, afraid of the sea, afraid of Edward not coming home. But this is different. This is fear without a shape, without a direction. The sickness is in the air, in the water, in the mail. It could be anywhere. It could be here already.*

*Ruth is sleeping. I checked her forehead. It is cool. I will check again in an hour.*

*The light is burning. That is all I can do tonight.*

Chapter 23: Ruth Falls Ill

The fever came on the night of October 8.

Ada had been checking Ruth's forehead every hour, waking from shallow sleep to touch her daughter's skin, finding it cool, finding it normal, breathing again. But at three in the morning, when she climbed down from the tower after the midnight check, she found Ruth burning.

The child's skin was dry and hot, her eyes glassy, her breath coming in short, shallow gasps. She was awake but not present — her eyes moved, but they did not focus on Ada's face.

Ada carried her to the kitchen, wrapped her in a cool, damp sheet, and sat with her through the night. She did not climb the tower for the four o'clock check. The light could manage for an hour. Ruth could not.

At dawn, the fever had not broken.

Ada opened Dr. Parrish's package. She measured a dose of quinine. She held Ruth's head and made her drink it. Ruth coughed, sputtered, cried. Ada held her.

She did not know what else to do.

She had tended the light through storms, through fog, through the long winter nights when the mechanism froze and needed to be coaxed back to life. She had learned to read the weather, to splice a rope, to repair a broken pane of glass, to manage a household on stores that had to last through months of isolation. She had learned everything a keeper's wife needed to know.

She had not learned how to save her daughter.

The journal entry for October 8 was short. It was written in pencil, the letters uneven, the lines crossing each other:

*Ruth is sick. Fever high. I have given her quinine. I have wrapped her in wet cloths. I have done everything Dr. Parrish told me to do. She is not better.*

*The light is burning. I do not know how. I climbed the tower at dusk and lit it, and I climbed down again, and I do not remember the steps. I do not remember anything but her face.*

*I have never been this afraid.*

Chapter 24: The Light Alone

Ada entered a period of days that she would later not be able to count. She existed in a rhythm of fever checks and tower climbs, of four-hour intervals marked by the turning of the lens, of waking and sleeping and not knowing which was which.

She climbed the tower at dusk. She lit the light. She checked the mechanism. She climbed down.

She checked Ruth. She changed the cloth on her forehead. She tried to make her drink. She sat beside her.

She climbed the tower at midnight. She checked the oil. She trimmed the wick. She stood in the lantern room, watching the beam sweep across the empty sea, and she felt nothing.

She climbed down. She checked Ruth. The fever had not broken. The child was thinner, her cheeks hollow, her eyes too bright.

She climbed the tower at four in the morning. The wind was rising. The fog was coming in. The light seemed dimmer than it should have been, and she spent an hour cleaning the lens, polishing it with a cloth that she had to hold with both hands because her fingers were shaking.

She climbed down. She made coffee. She sat at the kitchen table and stared at the wall until the gray light of dawn began to show through the window.

She climbed the tower at dawn to extinguish the light.

The days passed. No boat came. No word from Edward. No word from Dr. Parrish.

Ada stopped writing in the journal. She did not have the strength to hold the pen.

She spoke to Ruth even when Ruth could not hear her. She described the weather. She described the sea. She described the lighthouse, the beam, the way the lens turned. She told Ruth about the seals on the ledges, the gulls, the cormorants, the puffins that nested on the outer island. She told her about the spring flowers that would come, the daffodils, the iris, the wild rose.

"You have to see them," she said. "You have to see them bloom."

She was not sure if she was talking to Ruth or to herself.

Chapter 25: Dr. Parrish Returns

He came on the twelfth day.

Ada heard the oars before she saw the boat — the rhythm of them, the splash and pull, the sound of a man rowing against the wind. She left Ruth's bedside and walked to the landing, and there was Dr. Parrish, beaching his skiff on the pebbles, his coat collar turned up against the spray, his hands red with cold.

He did not ask permission. He walked past her into the house, found Ruth in her bed, and began his examination.

Ada stood in the doorway, watching. His hands were gentle. He spoke to Ruth in a low voice, telling her what he was doing, even though she was not fully conscious. He listened to her chest, felt her pulse, looked at her throat by the light of the lamp.

When he finished, he turned to Ada.

"You have done everything right."

"That is not helping."

"I know." He sat down heavily in the chair beside Ruth's bed. "She is dehydrated. She needs fluids. Have you been able to get her to drink?"

"A little."

"That is not enough. We need to get more into her."

He produced a bottle from his bag — a clear liquid that smelled of alcohol and something else. "This is a tincture. Quinine and a few other things. It will not cure her, but it will help her fight."

He measured a dose. Ada held Ruth's head. They gave it to her together.

"I will stay the night," Dr. Parrish said.

"You cannot. The town—"

"The town will survive one night without me. You will not."

It was not a negotiation. It was not a kindness. It was a fact, stated without emotion, and Ada accepted it because she was too tired to argue.

He sent her to sleep. She lay down on the parlor sofa, still in her clothes, and slept for five hours without moving.

When she woke, the light was fading and Dr. Parrish was sitting in the kitchen, drinking coffee. Ruth was awake. She was sitting up in bed, asking for bread and jam.

Ada wept.

Dr. Parrish left at dawn. Before he went, he sat at the table and wrote instructions on a sheet of paper: continued doses, bed rest, boiled water, watch for a return of the fever. He gave Ada a packet of letters that had accumulated at the post office.

Among them was a letter from Edward.

Ada did not open it until after Dr. Parrish had gone. She sat at the kitchen table, the letter in her hands, and read it twice.

He was alive. He was in Portland. He was helping at a hospital that had been set up in the armory. He had seen things he could not describe. He would come home when the sickness was over. He loved her.

Ada folded the letter and put it in her pocket. She had not cried when Dr. Parrish left. She had not cried when Ruth asked for bread. She did not cry now.

She was too tired for tears.

But she wrote in her journal that night, the first entry in twelve days:

*Dr. Parrish came. Ruth is better. Edward is alive.*

*I am still here. The light is still burning.*

*I think that is enough.*

Chapter 26: Nell's Story

The nurse arrived on the same boat that brought Dr. Parrish's next visit. She was a small woman in her forties, with graying hair pulled tight under a cap, and a face that looked like it had seen everything and was no longer surprised by any of it.

Her name was Nell O'Toole.

She had come to Larklight because Dr. Parrish had asked her to check on Ada, because he was worried about the keeper's wife who had nearly lost her daughter and was running the light alone and had not had a proper conversation with another adult in weeks.

Nell stayed for two days.

She did not fuss over Ada. She inspected the house, checked Ruth's recovery, pronounced both of them adequately cared for, and then sat down at the kitchen table and told Ada about her life.

She had been a nurse in Boston before the war. She had married a fisherman and had a daughter, and both of them had died — the child in the influenza outbreak of 1915, the husband at sea the following year. She had come to Maine because there was nothing left for her in Boston, and she had offered her services to Dr. Parrish because he was the only physician in the region who did not treat nurses as servants.

"I have buried everyone I loved," Nell said. "I have decided to stop loving people who are likely to die. This is a small population in a pandemic, but I am doing my best."

Ada laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in weeks, and the sound surprised her.

"Grief is a country with no maps," Nell continued. "You find your way by walking into things."

"Who told you that?"

"No one. I discovered it." She looked at Ada with clear, steady eyes. "You are in that country now. You have been there since Edward left, since Ruth fell ill. You will leave it eventually, but not by finding a path. You leave by learning to see in the dark."

Ada wrote the words in her journal that night:

*Nell O'Toole said: Grief is a country with no maps. You find your way by walking into things.*

*I will remember this.*

Chapter 27: Edward Writes

The letters from Edward came irregularly, in batches, whenever the mail could get through. Ada saved them all, reading them in order, building a picture of a world she could not see.

In October, he wrote:

*The hospital is full. We have converted the armory, the school, the church. Cots in every aisle. The sick lie beside the dying because there is nowhere else to put them. I have learned to identify the smell of the influenza. It is sweet and sour, like something fermenting. I will never forget it.*

In November, he wrote:

*The numbers are falling. The worst may be over. I am still healthy. Do not worry. I will come home when I can. Tell Ruth I love her.*

In December, he wrote:

*I have seen too much. I do not know how to carry it. I do not know how to come back to you and be the man I was.*

Ada read the last letter three times.

She understood. She had seen too much as well — the light burning alone, the child burning with fever, the sea stretching empty in all directions. She had become someone else in the months since Edward left, and she did not know if that someone else could be married to the man who would come home.

She wrote in her journal:

*Edward writes that he has seen too much. I have seen too much. We have both become strangers to ourselves. I do not know if strangers can recognize each other.*

*But I will try. He is my husband. He is the father of my child. He left to help others, and I stayed to keep the light. We have both done what we could.*

*I will try.*

Chapter 28: The Quarantine Lifts

November 11, 1918. The armistice.

Ada heard the news on the wireless, the signal carrying through the static from a station she could not identify. The war was over. The fighting had stopped. Men would come home.

She lit an extra lamp in the window. She did not know why. Perhaps to signal that someone was watching. Perhaps to mark the moment. Perhaps because she did not know how else to mark the end of a war and the continuation of a pandemic.

The quarantine lifted in stages. The supply boat returned. The mail began to flow again. Dr. Parrish wrote to say that the number of new cases was declining. The worst was over.

But the town was not the same.

Half the families had lost someone. The Harbach twins. Old Mrs. Pell. The miller's apprentice, who had been seventeen. The telegraph operator's wife, who had left three children. Dr. Parrish had aged ten years. The church was still being cleaned.

Ada rowed into town for the first time in three months. She walked the streets with Ruth, and she saw the faces of people who had survived, and she understood that survival was not the same as recovery.

She wrote in her journal:

*I went to town today. It is smaller than I remembered. The people are thinner. The store is open, but there is nothing to buy. The church is being repainted, as if paint could cover what happened there.*

*I saw the Harbach house. The shades were drawn. I do not know who will open them.*

*I came home to Larklight and climbed the tower and stood in the lantern room and looked at the sea. The light was burning. It had burned through everything. I touched the lens and felt it warm against my palm, and I thought: This is what endurance looks like. It does not stop. It does not falter. It burns.*

Chapter 29: Edward Returns

Edward came home in December, on a supply boat that arrived in a snow squall.

Ada saw him from the kitchen window. He was standing on the deck, thinner than when he had left, his coat too large for his frame, his face pale. He did not wave. He stood with his hands in his pockets and watched the lighthouse as the boat approached the landing.

Ada walked down to meet him. The snow was falling, small dry flakes that accumulated on the granite and made the path treacherous. She reached the landing as he stepped off the boat.

He did not embrace her. He stood on the pier, his bag at his feet, and looked at her as if he were trying to remember who she was.

"Edward."

"Ada."

"Ruth is at the house. She has been waiting for you."

He nodded. He picked up his bag. They walked up the path together, not touching, the snow falling between them.

Ruth ran to him at the door. He picked her up and held her, and for a moment, Ada saw the man she had married — the warmth, the tenderness, the capacity for love. Then he set Ruth down, and the mask returned.

That night, they lay in bed without touching. The lighthouse beam swept across the window. Edward stared at the ceiling.

"Are you going to tell me?" Ada asked.

"I cannot."

"Then who can you tell?"

No answer. The beam swept again.

"I do not need to know what you saw," Ada said. "I need to know that you are here."

"I am here."

"Then that is enough for now."

She reached for his hand under the covers. He did not pull away. He did not squeeze back. But he did not pull away.

Ada wrote in her journal:

*Edward is home. He is not the same. I am not the same. We are two people who have lived through different wars, and we do not know how to talk about either of them.*

*But we are here. The light is burning. Ruth is asleep in her bed.*

*That will have to be enough, for now.*

Chapter 30: Larklight Continues

Edward did not return to the tower.

He tried. He climbed the stairs once, the day after his return, and Ada found him standing in the lantern room, his hand on the lens, his face unreadable. He stayed there for an hour. When he came down, he said, "The light is yours now."

Ada did not argue.

She had become the keeper in truth, not just in practice. She trimmed the wick, cleaned the lens, wound the mechanism, kept the log. She knew the eighty-seven steps in the dark, in the fog, in the rain, in the snow. She knew the sound of the beam as it rotated, the whisper of the lens on its mercury bed, the creak of the tower in the wind.

She was good at it.

Edward found his place in the house. He repaired the roof, mended the fence, split wood, cleared the path. He spoke little. He slept poorly. But he stayed.

Ruth grew. She was seven, then eight, then nine. She learned to read from the books on the parlor shelf, to write from the letters her mother wrote to no one. She played on the granite slopes, watched the seals, collected shells, asked questions that Ada could not answer.

Ada wrote in her journal less frequently. The entries became sparser as the years passed, not because she had less to say but because the need to say it had diminished. She had found a way to live at Larklight. She no longer needed the journal to witness her life.

Her last entry was written in 1931, when Ruth was nineteen and had announced she was going to Boston to study nursing.

*Ruth is leaving. I knew she would. I have been preparing for it since she was old enough to walk down the path and look at the road that leads away from Larklight.*

*I will miss her. I will miss her voice in the house, her footsteps on the stairs, her laughter in the garden. But she is not meant for this life. She is meant for something larger, and I will not hold her back.*

*Edward died two years ago. I do not write about it because I do not know how. He was never the same after Portland, and I was never the same after the fever, and we lived beside each other for twelve years without finding a way back to what we had been. I do not regret the marriage. I regret the silence between us, which grew wider every year and which I did not know how to cross.*

*I am alone now. Ruth is leaving. Edward is gone. The light is still here.*

*I will keep it burning until I cannot.*

*Then I will give it to the next woman who needs it.*

Chapter 31: Iris Arrives

Iris Vance had never seen the ocean before she came to Larklight.

She had grown up inland, in the mill town of Lowell, Massachusetts, where the water was the Merrimack River, brown and industrial, carrying the waste of a hundred factories. The ocean, when she first saw it from the deck of the mail boat, was so vast that it did not look like water at all. It looked like the sky had fallen and spread itself across the world.

She was twenty years old. She had been married for three days. She was traveling to a lighthouse she had only seen in a photograph, to live with a man she had met three times before the wedding, and she was terrified.

William met her at the landing. He was taller than she remembered, broader in the shoulder, with a face that had been weathered by wind and salt into something that looked older than his thirty-two years. He took her luggage — two suitcases, a hatbox, and a crate of books — and loaded them onto a handcart.

"It is not Boston," he said.

"I am not from Boston."

"No. But you will miss it anyway."

She did not know whether he was joking. She decided to assume he was. "I am from Lowell," she said. "The only thing I will miss is the trolley."

He almost smiled. It was not quite a smile, but it was the closest thing she had seen from him since their wedding, and she counted it as progress.

They walked up the path from the landing. The fog was thick, the kind of fog that muffled sound and made the world feel wrapped in wool. She could hear the bell on the buoy, a single, slow note repeated at intervals, but she could not see the buoy. She could not see the shore until they were at the pier. She could not see the lighthouse until she was standing at the base of it, looking up into whiteness.

The house appeared as they crested the rise. White clapboard, green shutters, a chimney that was smoking. Someone had lit a fire for her arrival. That someone was William, who had lit it before he walked down to the pier, who had prepared the house for a wife he had met three times.

The kitchen was warm. A vase of wildflowers stood on the table — daisies, black-eyed Susans, something with blue petals she did not recognize. William had picked them. She knew this because the stems were uneven and some of the petals were bruised.

Iris sat at the table, and William made tea. She watched him move around the kitchen — finding the cups, the sugar, the milk — and she thought, *I can learn this. I can learn the shape of his silence.*

Later, he showed her the lighthouse.

They climbed the eighty-seven steps. William counted them as they climbed. Iris did not need him to count them, but she understood that he was sharing something, that the number mattered to him.

The lens was beautiful. It was a first-order Fresnel, built in France, shipped across the Atlantic in 1883. It stood six feet tall, a hive of glass and brass, and when William lit it for her — just a demonstration, not the full night light — the prisms caught the flame and threw it into a pattern of rings and beams that made her catch her breath.

That night, she wrote her first journal entry, sitting at the kitchen table while William was in the tower for the evening check. She did not know about the other journals — the trunk in the attic, Sarah's leather volume, Ada's brown journal. She had not found them yet.

*I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for.*

She did not know she was echoing Sarah March. She did not know that the same words had been written in this house, at this table, by a woman who had arrived at Larklight as a bride sixty-eight years before.

She was starting her own story, and she did not know yet that it was part of a longer one.

Chapter 32: The Coast Guard Era

The lighthouse had changed since Sarah's time. The Lighthouse Service had been absorbed by the Coast Guard in 1939, and with the new administration came new technology: electric light, radio communication, a telephone line that ran along the road from town.

William loved the new technology. He had been a keeper for eight years before Iris arrived, and he remembered the days of oil lamps and hand-wound mechanisms. The electric light was a revelation. No more trimming wicks or cleaning smoke residue from the lens. No more worrying about the oil supply in winter. The light turned on at dusk and off at dawn, controlled by a timer that was more reliable than any human keeper.

"The old keepers would not recognize this place," he said one evening, standing in the lantern room with the new electric beam sweeping its arc. "They did everything by hand. Every night, a man climbed these stairs and lit the flame. Every morning, he climbed again to extinguish it. For a hundred years."

"Does it feel like a loss?" Iris asked.

William considered the question. "No. It feels like progress. The light is more reliable now. Ships are safer. That is what matters."

Iris did not argue. But privately, she mourned the romance of the old way. She had read Sarah's journal by then — she had found the trunk in the attic during her first month, had spent hours reading the faded ink of the entries — and she had felt a kinship with Sarah that she did not feel with the electric light.

Sarah had kept the light with her hands. Iris watched it burn on its own.

"I miss the old light," she wrote in her journal, "though I never knew it. There is something in the way Sarah wrote about the flame that makes me think it was alive in a way the electric beam cannot be. Perhaps I am romanticizing hardship. Perhaps every generation romanticizes the hardship of the one before. But I wish I could have seen the flame."

Chapter 33: Margaret Is Born

Margaret March was born at Larklight on a December night in 1955, in the middle of a storm that kept the Coast Guard cutter at its dock and the midwife from reaching the point.

The midwife did not come. There was only William, and Iris's body, and the instructions Dr. Parrish had given her before she left the town clinic. Dr. Parrish was Nathaniel's son, trained in the same tradition of pragmatic medicine, and he had said, "The baby will come whether I am there or not. Your job is to stay calm and let it happen."

Iris had not stayed calm. She had screamed. She had wept. She had told William she would never forgive him for bringing her to this isolated rock where there was no doctor and no hospital and no help.

William had held her hand, and she had held his, and together they had brought Margaret into the world.

The midwife arrived the next morning, after the storm had passed and the sea had calmed. She found Iris in bed, Margaret in her arms, William making coffee in the kitchen. She examined mother and child and pronounced both healthy.

"You did not need me," she said.

"I needed you," Iris said. "I just did not get you."

The midwife laughed. She was a woman of sixty, with gray hair and hands that had delivered half the babies on the coast. "That is the story of motherhood," she said. "Needing help and not getting it. Learning to do it yourself. You will find it is a pattern."

Iris wrote in her journal:

*Margaret is here. She is perfect — ten fingers, ten toes, a voice that can fill the whole house. She looks at me with eyes that do not know the world, and I am terrified of what the world will show her.*

*She is the first March child born here since Ruth. The house feels full for the first time. Sarah and Ada must have felt this too — that moment when a new voice enters the old silence and changes everything.*

*I am a mother now. I do not know how to be one. But I will learn, as the others learned, as women have always learned: by doing it poorly and then doing it again.*

Chapter 34: The Quiet Years

The 1950s passed at Larklight in a rhythm that Iris came to love.

Mornings were for the house — cleaning, cooking, laundry, the thousand small tasks that kept a home running. Afternoons were for Margaret — walks along the point, reading in the garden, lessons at the kitchen table. Evenings were for the light — Iris would walk to the tower with William, or she would watch from the kitchen window as the beam began its sweep across the darkening sea.

It was a quiet life. There were no neighbors, no social calls, no trips to town except for supplies. The telephone rang rarely. The mail came twice a week. The radio brought the news of the world, distant and muffled, like a conversation in another room.

Iris found that she did not miss the world. She had not been happy in Lowell — the mill dust, the crowded streets, the noise of machinery and traffic. She had not known she was looking for silence until she found it.

But silence, she discovered, could become its own kind of noise.

William was a good man. He was steady, reliable, kind in his quiet way. But he was not a conversationalist. He did not share his thoughts, his fears, his dreams. He came in from the tower, ate his meals, read the newspaper, and went to bed. He loved Iris. She knew he did. But he loved her in silence, and silence, over time, began to feel like absence.

She wrote in her journal:

*I have been married for ten years. I have a daughter, a home, a life that many women would envy. I am safe. I am fed. I am loved.*

*Why do I feel like I am disappearing?*

She did not answer the question. She closed the journal and put it in the trunk with the others.

She did not write again for five years.

Chapter 35: The Painter

Julian Cross arrived at Larklight in June of 1965.

He came on the supply boat, a city man in a linen jacket, with a trunk of art supplies and a letter of introduction from the Coast Guard office in Portland. He was a painter, he explained. He had received a grant to document the lighthouses of the Maine coast. He needed a place to stay for the summer.

William was skeptical. "We do not run a hotel," he said.

"I will stay in the assistant keeper's cottage. I will not be any trouble. I will be out of your way."

"The cottage is not furnished."

"I do not need furniture. I need a roof and a view."

Iris watched the exchange from the kitchen window. The man was tall, thin, with dark hair that fell across his forehead and a face that seemed to be always on the verge of a smile. He spoke with his hands. He gestured at the lighthouse, the sea, the sky, as if he were already composing a painting.

William looked at Iris. She shrugged. The cottage had been empty for years. No one was using it.

"One summer," William said.

"One summer," Julian agreed.

He moved in that afternoon. Iris watched him carry his trunk across the yard, his linen jacket swapped for a paint-stained shirt, his city shoes replaced by boots that were clearly new. He stood outside the cottage for a long moment, looking at the lighthouse, and Iris saw him take a breath as if he were taking in the view through his lungs.

She went back to her work.

She did not think about him again until the evening, when she found him on the point, sitting on the granite with a sketchbook in his lap. He was drawing the lighthouse as the evening light caught it, the white tower glowing against the darkening sky.

He looked up when she approached. "The light is wrong now," he said. "I will have to come back tomorrow."

"The light is always changing."

"Yes. That is the frustration and the gift."

He closed his sketchbook. He did not seem to want to talk, and Iris did not push. She walked past him to the edge of the point and stood there, watching the waves break on the ledges, feeling the spray on her face.

When she turned to go back, he was watching her.

"I am sorry," he said. "I did not mean to stare. The light was behind you."

She did not know what he meant. She walked back to the house and made dinner.

That night, she took out her journal for the first time in five years.

*A painter has come to Larklight. He is staying in the assistant's cottage. He says he wants to paint the lighthouse. I think he wants to paint something else, but he does not know what it is yet.*

*I do not know why I wrote that. I will leave it.*

Chapter 36: What Begins

The summer of 1965 was the most beautiful summer Iris had ever known.

The weather was clear and warm, day after day, the sea a deep blue that she had not known the Atlantic could be. The garden bloomed extravagantly — the iris she had planted in her first spring, the lavender, the wild rose, the peonies that had come from Sarah's original plantings and had spread into a thicket of pink and white.

And Julian Cross painted.

He set up his easel on the point, on the rocks, in the garden, on the path to the tower. He painted the lighthouse at dawn, at noon, at sunset, in the fog and the rain and the clear light of high summer. He painted the sea in its different moods. He painted the house, the garden, the granite, the sky.

He painted Iris.

She did not pose for him. She was not a model. She worked in the garden, hung laundry on the line, walked to the tower, and Julian painted her as she moved, as if she were part of the landscape, as natural as the sea or the stone.

"You do not look at me," she said one afternoon, when she had stopped weeding to find him watching her, his brush held still.

"I look at you all the time," he said. "You just do not see me doing it."

"Why do you paint me?"

He considered the question. "Because you belong here."

"In what way?"

"In the way that the lighthouse belongs here. In the way the granite belongs here. You are part of this place. I have never seen a person so fully part of a place."

She did not know what to say to that. She went back to weeding.

But she felt his eyes on her, and she felt something shift in her chest, something that had been locked for so long she had forgotten it existed.

She did not write about it in her journal. She was afraid to put words to what was happening, because words would make it real, and she was not ready for it to be real.

But it was real. By the end of July, it was undeniable.

They did not touch for a long time. They circled each other, speaking in the language of glances and silences. Julian continued to paint. Iris continued her work. But the air between them had changed, charged with something that neither of them named.

The first kiss happened in the garden, on an evening in August, when the light was golden and the bees were drowsy and the sea was calm. Julian was packing up his paints. Iris was deadheading the roses. She looked up, and he was standing beside her, close enough that she could smell the turpentine and oil paint on his hands.

"Iris," he said.

That was all. He said her name, and she turned, and he kissed her.

She did not pull away.

The kiss was soft and hesitant, the kiss of people who knew they were crossing a line they could not uncross. When it ended, Iris stepped back.

"William—"

"Does not have to know."

That was the first lie. The second lie was that it did not matter. The third lie was that it would end when the summer ended.

The leaves began to turn. The goldenrod bloomed. Julian painted the autumn light. Iris stopped writing in her journal entirely, because she could not bear to record what she was doing and she could not bear to lie.

Chapter 37: The Summer Ends

Julian left on the first of September.

He did not say goodbye in the way Iris had imagined. He simply appeared at her door one morning, his trunk packed, his canvases wrapped in cloth, his face composed into an expression of careful neutrality.

"The boat is coming," he said. "I need to go."

"Today?"

"The grant requires me to document the southern stations before the weather turns. I have stayed longer than I planned."

She stood in the doorway. The house was behind her, the lighthouse beyond. She could hear William in the tower, checking the mechanism.

"Will you write?"

"I will write."

"Will you come back?"

He did not answer. He looked at her for a long moment, his face unreadable, and then he picked up his trunk and walked toward the landing.

Iris watched him go. She stood in the doorway until the boat disappeared around the point, and then she went inside and closed the door.

She did not cry. She sat at the kitchen table and stared at the wall, and she felt something close inside her, something that had opened over the summer and was now, with Julian's departure, sealing itself shut.

She wrote in her journal that night, the first entry in weeks:

*He is gone. I do not know if he will come back. I do not know if I want him to.*

*I do not know what I want. I only know that I am standing in this kitchen, in this house, in this life that I chose, and I cannot feel my own hands.*

*I will stay. Where else would I go?*

*The light is still here.*

Chapter 38: Margaret Leaves

Margaret March left Larklight in September 1973.

She was eighteen, and she had been accepted to the University of Massachusetts Boston, and she had been waiting for this day since she was old enough to understand that the lighthouse was not the world.

Iris helped her pack. They did not speak much. The air between them was charged with things unsaid, the accumulated silence of a mother and daughter who had learned to communicate through domestic routine rather than conversation.

"Will you visit?" Iris asked.

"Will you want me to?"

The question was sharp, and Iris felt the sting of it. "Of course I will."

"Then I will visit."

But they both knew it was a lie. Margaret would not come back. She would build a life away from Larklight, away from the isolation, away from the weight of a family history she had never asked to inherit.

Iris watched her daughter walk down the path to the landing, the same path Sarah had walked, the same path Ada had walked, the same path Iris herself had walked a thousand times. Margaret did not look back.

Iris wrote in her journal that night:

*Margaret is gone. She left without looking back. I do not blame her. I would have done the same at her age.*

*I am proud of her. I am broken by her leaving. Both things are true.*

*I have been waiting for this day since she was born. I knew she would go. I knew Larklight could not hold her. And still, now that she is gone, I do not know what to do with my hands.*

*She is doing what I should have done. This is pride, not pain. I think.*

*I am not sure.*

Chapter 39: The Decommissioning

The Coast Guard announced the automation of Larklight Light in June 1977.

William brought the letter from the post office. He set it on the kitchen table and watched Iris read it. The words were formal and impersonal: the light would be automated by the end of the year. The keeper's position was eliminated. William could accept early retirement or apply for a transfer to another station.

"I am retiring," William said.

"Where will we go?"

"We will stay here. The house will be deeded to the town. We can remain as caretakers."

"Caretakers."

"It is what we are, Iris. We have been caretakers all along."

She did not argue. She could not argue with the truth.

The last night of the manned light was December 31, 1977.

Iris climbed the tower alone. William had offered to come with her, but she had said no. This was something she needed to do by herself.

She climbed the eighty-seven steps, counting them as she went, as she had counted them a thousand times before. The tower was cold, the iron handrail rough with rust, the stone walls damp with condensation. The light was burning — the automated light, the electric beam that had replaced the old flame. It was steady and reliable and did not need her.

But she climbed anyway. She stood in the lantern room, the lens turning beside her, the beam sweeping across the dark water. She put her hand on the glass, feeling the cold.

She thought about Sarah March, who had come to Larklight as a bride and had stayed as a widow, who had written *The light is the only thing that has not lied to me.*

She thought about Ada March, who had kept the light through a pandemic, who had lit it with her own hands every night for months, who had written *The light is mine now.*

She thought about all the nights she had watched this beam from the kitchen window, from the garden, from the bedroom where she had lain beside William and listened to the sea.

The light did not need her. It had never needed her. But she had needed it.

She stood in the lantern room until the automated mechanism clicked and the dawn timer began the shutdown sequence. The beam slowed, stopped, went dark.

Iris descended the stairs.

She did not look back.

That night, she wrote her last journal entry:

*The light is automated now. It burns without us. It always did.*

*I will stay in the house. The light does not need me, but I am not ready to be needed by anything else.*

*I do not know what comes next. I have been a keeper's wife for twenty-five years. I have raised a daughter. I have loved a man who was not my husband. I have kept a secret that has shaped my entire life.*

*I will close this journal now. I do not know if I will open another.*

*Perhaps the silence is enough.*

Chapter 40: Iris's Last Entry

Iris March did not open another journal.

The trunk went to the attic, and the journals — Sarah's leather volume, Ada's brown journal, Iris's black composition books — were stacked and tied and put away. They remained there for forty-eight years, waiting.

Iris stayed at Larklight. She lived alone in the keeper's house, the lighthouse dark beside her, the garden growing wild. She did not leave. She did not visit Margaret in Boston, did not attend Margaret's wedding, did not meet her granddaughter Lena until the child was four years old.

She did not explain herself. She did not apologize. She lived in the house, kept the garden, watched the seasons pass, and waited for something she could not name.

She was still waiting when she died in her sleep, at ninety-four, on a Tuesday in October 2025.

She left the house to Lena. She left the trunk in the attic. She left the letters in the sewing room, the locket in the garden, the photograph in the false bottom of the trunk.

She left the story for someone else to finish.

Iris's last journal entry was brief. It was written on a loose sheet of paper, tucked into the back of the final composition book. It was not dated, but the handwriting was late — the hand of an old woman, careful and fragile.

*If you are reading this, you have found the journals. You have read the story of Larklight as I read it, as Sarah wrote it, as Ada kept it.*

*I do not know who you are. Perhaps you are Margaret. Perhaps you are the granddaughter I never knew. Perhaps you are a stranger who found this house by accident and opened the trunk out of curiosity.*

*Whoever you are, know this: I was happy here. It does not always look like happiness in the writing, because happiness is not the thing most worth recording. Pain is recorded. Love is recorded. Grief is recorded. But happiness — the ordinary, daily happiness of a life lived in a place you love — that is the thing that leaves no trace because it is the thing you do not need to remember.*

*I was happy at Larklight. I was lonely, and I was afraid, and I made choices I regret, and I loved a man I could not keep. But I was happy here.*

*I hope you find your own Larklight.*

*I hope you stay long enough to be happy.*

Chapter 41: The Last Journal

Lena read Iris's final entry and sat in the silence of the kitchen, the loose sheet of paper trembling in her hands.

She had read the entire story now. Sarah's arrival and her long widowhood. Ada's pandemic winter and her quiet endurance. Iris's marriage, her affair, her secret. The letters in the sewing room. The locket in the garden. The brother Lena had never known existed.

The sun had set while she was reading. The kitchen was dark except for the lamp on the table. Outside, the lighthouse was a black silhouette against a sky that still held a trace of violet.

She had not called Margaret back.

She had dialed her mother's number at the end of Chapter 10, but the call had gone to voicemail, and she had left a message — "Mom, I'm at Larklight. I need to ask you something" — and then she had set the phone aside and kept reading, and she had not picked it up again.

She looked at the phone now. The screen was dark. She did not pick it up.

Instead, she went to the sewing room.

The letters were still there, tied with the blue ribbon. Lena had read the first one — Julian's first letter, written after he left — but she had not read the rest. She had been saving them, perhaps. Or she had been afraid of them.

She sat down at the desk. She untied the ribbon. She arranged the letters in chronological order, the dates spread across ten years, from 1965 to 1975.

She read them all.

They were love letters, but they were not the love letters of young people. They were the letters of a man who had known, from the beginning, that the woman he loved belonged to another life. He wrote about his work, his travels, the other lighthouses he had painted. He wrote about the quality of light in different seasons, different latitudes. He wrote about the memory of Larklight, which he carried with him like a lens he could not stop looking through.

The last letter was dated June 1975.

*Dear Iris,*

*I have stopped expecting an answer. I do not know if my letters reach you. I do not know if you read them. I send them anyway, because not sending them would be a lie, and I have told enough lies in my life.*

*I am in Nova Scotia now. There is a lighthouse here that reminds me of Larklight, though it is smaller and the light is green, not white. I have painted it a dozen times. It is never quite right.*

*I have met someone. Her name is Catherine. She is kind and patient and she does not ask me why I am always looking at the horizon.*

*I will not write again. Not because I have stopped loving you — I will never stop loving you — but because I have learned that loving someone can also mean leaving them in peace.*

*You gave me a summer that I will carry for the rest of my life. I gave you a son that I will never know. I hope you have found a way to tell him who he is.*

*If you ever leave Larklight, come find me. If you never leave, I will understand.*

*Yours always,*
*Julian*

Lena read the letter twice. Then she folded it carefully and put it back in its envelope.

A son. Julian Cross March, born 1967. Her uncle. Her mother's half-brother.

Iris had given birth to a child she could not keep, and she had given him up for adoption, and she had never told anyone — not William, not Margaret, not even Julian, who had written *I gave you a son* as if he had known but had never been certain.

Lena sat in the dark sewing room, the letters in her lap, and tried to imagine what that had cost her grandmother. To carry a child in secret. To give birth at Larklight, attended by the midwife from town who had delivered Margaret twelve years before. To place the child in someone else's arms and never speak of it again.

She looked at the lighthouse through the window. The lens was dark, the beam stilled. But the tower stood, as it had stood through all of it — through Sarah's loneliness and Ada's fear and Iris's love and loss.

Lena gathered the letters, tied them with the ribbon, and went downstairs.

She had one more call to make.

Chapter 42: The Photograph

In the false bottom of the trunk, beneath the layers of journals and letters and the oilcloth-wrapped bundles, Lena found a photograph she had not seen before.

It was small, black-and-white, the edges curled with age. The image showed three people standing at the Larklight landing: Iris, Julian, and a child.

The child was a boy of about five, dressed in a sailor suit, his hair light — lighter than Iris's, lighter than Julian's. He was looking up at the lighthouse, his hand shading his eyes, his face full of the particular concentration of a child who was trying to understand something important.

On the back of the photograph, in Iris's handwriting:

*Julian Cross March. August 1972. His last visit.*

Lena turned the photograph over and looked at the boy again. Her uncle. Her mother's brother. A child who had been born at Larklight and given away and had never known where he came from.

She had the name: Julian Cross March. She had the date: August 1972. She had the place: Nova Scotia, where Julian had settled, where Catherine had been, where the son had been raised by a father who painted lighthouses and a mother who was kind and patient.

She did not know his last name. She did not know if he was still alive. She did not know if he knew about Iris, about Larklight, about the March women who had kept a light burning at the edge of the world.

But she had the photograph. She had the letters. She had the journal entries that described a summer of love and a lifetime of silence.

She had enough to find him.

Chapter 43: The Name

Lena searched for Julian Cross March.

It was not easy. He had been adopted. His name had been changed, or kept, or altered. He had grown up in Nova Scotia, the son of Julian Cross and a woman named Catherine. He might have married. He might have moved. He might have changed his name entirely.

She started with the adoption records.

Nova Scotia had open adoption records for adult adoptees, but the process required a formal request, and Lena was not the child — she was the niece. She did not have standing to request anything.

She tried a different approach. She searched online for "Julian Cross" "lighthouse painter" Nova Scotia. The search returned an obituary. Julian Cross, died 2008, survived by his wife Catherine and his son, Thomas March Cross.

Lena stared at the name.

*Thomas March Cross.*

The adoption had kept the name March. The boy had grown up as Thomas March Cross, son of Julian and Catherine, and he lived — according to an online directory — in a town called Lockeport, Nova Scotia. He was a fisherman. He had a wife and two daughters.

Lena found his phone number.

She sat at the kitchen table, the number written on a scrap of paper, her phone in her hand. The lighthouse was visible through the window, dark and still. The journals were stacked beside her. The letters were in her pocket.

She had not called Margaret back. She had not told anyone what she had found. She had been at Larklight for two weeks, and she had uncovered a century and a half of secrets, and now she was about to call a stranger who was her uncle.

She dialed.

The phone rang four times. A woman answered. "Hello?"

"I am looking for Thomas March Cross."

"This is his wife. Who is calling?"

Lena hesitated. "My name is Lena March. I believe Thomas is my uncle. I am calling from Larklight, in Maine. His mother — his birth mother — was my grandmother. Iris March."

The silence on the other end was long. Then the woman said, "I think you had better tell my husband that yourself."

There was a muffled sound, voices, footsteps. Then a man's voice, cautious: "Hello?"

"Thomas?"

"Yes."

"My name is Lena March. I think I am your niece."

There was a long pause. Then Thomas said, "I know who Iris March is. I found letters in my father's things after he died. I have been trying to decide whether to go to Larklight."

"You do not have to decide," Lena said. "I am here. The house is open. And I have a trunk full of journals that tell the whole story."

Thomas was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was rough. "I have been waiting for this call my whole life. I did not know it until now."

Chapter 44: The Coast Guard Records

Before she could go to Nova Scotia, Lena had one more thing to check.

She drove to Portland, to the Coast Guard archives, where the records of decommissioned lighthouse stations were stored. She had called ahead, and a young archivist had pulled the Larklight file for her.


And, clipped to the back, a report that had been filed separately.

*Mental Health Evaluation — March, Iris M.*

Lena read the report standing at the counter in the archive room.

The evaluation was dated September 1978, nine months after the decommissioning. It described an incident: Iris March had climbed the decommissioned tower during a storm and had refused to come down for twelve hours. The Coast Guard had been called. They had found her in the lantern room, sitting on the floor, her hands on the dark lens.

The report noted: *Subject appears disoriented and fixated on the former light. She stated that the lens should not be dark. She stated that she was the keeper. She stated that the light had called to her.*

The recommendation was observation, not commitment. Iris March was not a danger to herself or others. But the incident was recorded, filed, forgotten.

Lena stood in the archive reading room, the report in her hands, and thought about her grandmother climbing the dark tower, sitting beside the cold lens, waiting for a light that would never burn again.

She had known. Elias had believed the light spoke to him. Sarah had watched him dissolve into that belief. Ada had heard the sea speaking in the voices of the dead. And Iris — Iris had felt the absence of the light as a physical loss, had climbed the tower in a storm because she could not bear the dark.

The line ran through the generations. The isolation of Larklight did not pass without cost. Every woman who had kept that house had paid something — her sanity, her heart, her ability to live in the world beyond the point.

Lena copied the report. She thanked the archivist. She drove back to Larklight through the June afternoon, the sea on her left, the sky gray and uncertain above her.

She had the whole story now.

And she knew what she had to do with it.

Chapter 45: Calling Margaret

This time, when Lena called her mother, Margaret answered on the first ring.

"Lena. You left a message."

"I know. I did not know what to say."

"Say it now."

Lena sat at the kitchen table, the journals spread around her, the letters in a neat stack, the photograph of Iris and Julian and the boy propped against the sugar bowl.

"I found the journals, Mom. Sarah's, Ada's, Grandma Iris's. I found the letters from the painter. I found the photograph. I found my uncle."

The silence on the other end was long. Then Margaret said, "How did you find him?"

"I searched. It was not hard."

"Did you call him?"

"Yes."

"And?"

"And he is coming to Larklight. Next week."

Another long silence. Then Margaret's voice, softer than Lena had ever heard it: "I knew about the letters."

"You did?"

"After the decommissioning, when Grandma Iris was... unwell. I came home from college. I found the letters in the sewing room. I did not confront her. I did not tell anyone. I just put them back and left."

"Why did you leave?"

"Because I could not stay. Because the house was full of things I did not know how to carry. Because I was eighteen, and I was afraid of becoming her."

"Becoming her how?"

"Becoming a woman who stays in a place that is slowly erasing her. Who loves a man she cannot have and keeps the evidence in a sewing room. Who watches the light go out and does not know what to do with her hands afterward."

Lena closed her eyes. She had not expected her mother to say any of this. She had expected deflection, silence, the same walls that had always existed between them.

"Mom."

"Yes?"

"I found the mental health report. From 1978. I understand now. I understand why you left."

"Then you understand more than I did at your age." Margaret's voice cracked. "She never told me about the boy. My brother. I did not know until I found the letters, and then I did not know what to do with that knowledge. I have been carrying it for forty-seven years."

"You do not have to carry it alone anymore."

"No," Margaret said. "Neither do you."

They talked for an hour. They talked about Larklight, about Iris, about the house, about the journals, about the brother Margaret had never met and the niece he had and the strange, unexpected shape of a family that was forming across distances.

When Lena hung up, she went to the window. The lighthouse stood against the evening sky, dark and patient.

She had one more week until Thomas arrived. One more week to read the journals again, to walk the point, to sit in the garden, to learn what it meant to be a March woman in a place that had held so many of them.

She began to look forward to it.

Chapter 46: The Journey

Lena drove to Nova Scotia on a Tuesday, the photograph of Iris and Julian and the boy on the passenger seat beside her.

The drive was seven hours, most of it along the coast, the sea appearing and disappearing between the trees. She crossed the border at Calais, drove through Saint John, followed the coastal road through towns whose names she did not know. The radio picked up Canadian stations, voices with different accents, different weather reports, different frequencies.

She arrived in Lockeport in the late afternoon. The town was small and white, clustered around a harbor where fishing boats rocked at their moorings. The lighthouse was visible from the harbor — a squat tower painted red and white, its light not yet burning.

Thomas March Cross lived in a house on a hill overlooking the harbor. It was a wooden house, painted yellow, with a porch that faced the sea. A truck was parked in the driveway. A dog barked from behind a fence.

Lena parked the rental car on the street and sat for a moment, her hands on the wheel.

She had called ahead. Thomas knew she was coming. But knowing and being were different things, and she was about to meet a man who shared her blood and her grandmother's secret, a man she had never known existed until two weeks ago.

She got out of the car.

The front door opened before she reached the porch. A man stood in the doorway — tall, broad-shouldered, his face weathered by wind and salt, his hair gray at the temples. He was in his late fifties, his hands thick and calloused, his eyes a pale blue that Lena recognized.

He had Iris's eyes.

"You found it," he said.

"Your directions were good."

He stepped out onto the porch. They stood looking at each other, the distance between them filled with a lifetime of unknowing.

"I have the photograph," Lena said. "Iris and Julian, at the Larklight landing. You were there. You were five years old."

"I do not remember it."

"I have their letters. I have the journals. I have the whole story."

Thomas nodded. His jaw was tight. "My father never told me. Not directly. He left me the letters after he died, and I read them, and I understood."

"Did you try to find her?"

"I found her. Iris. I found her address. I wrote a letter. I did not send it."

"Why not?"

"Because she had been a secret for so long. I did not know if she wanted to be found."

Lena reached into her bag and pulled out the envelope. She held it out to him. "She kept every letter Julian ever sent. She hid them in the sewing room. She never told anyone about you. But she kept the evidence that you existed."

Thomas took the envelope. He did not open it. He held it in his hands, looking at it as if it were made of something fragile.

"I am sorry," he said.

"For what?"

"For not knowing what to feel. I have been imagining this moment since I read my father's letters. I thought I would know what to say."

"You do not have to say anything."

They stood in silence. The dog had stopped barking. The harbor was quiet. The lighthouse beam began its evening sweep.

"She was going to tell you," Lena said. "I think. At the end. She left the letters where I could find them. She left the photograph. She left the whole story for me to assemble. I think she wanted someone to finish what she could not."

"And you are finishing it."

"I am trying."

Thomas looked at the harbor, at the lighthouse, at the sky that was darkening toward evening. "Come inside," he said. "My wife has made dinner. We have a lot to talk about."

Lena followed him into the house.

Chapter 47: What They Shared

Dinner at Thomas's house was a strange and wonderful thing.

His wife, Alice, had prepared a meal of baked fish and potatoes and vegetables from the garden. Their daughters — Rachel, sixteen, and Emily, fourteen — sat at the table with the careful politeness of teenagers who did not understand why a stranger from Maine had driven seven hours to meet their father.

But they warmed to Lena when she showed them the photograph.

"That is Dad?" Rachel said, pointing at the boy in the sailor suit.

"That is your father, at Larklight, in 1972."

"I did not know he was ever in Maine."

"He did not know either. Not consciously."

Thomas looked at the photograph. "I have a memory," he said slowly. "I have never been sure if it was real. A white tower, with a light at the top. A woman with dark hair. The sound of a bell on the water."

"That was Larklight. That was Iris."

"I have been carrying that memory my whole life, and I did not know whose it was."

They sat at the table after dinner, and Lena told them the story. She told them about Sarah, arriving as a bride in 1884 and staying for forty-three years after her husband died. She told them about Ada, keeping the light alone during the influenza pandemic, nursing her daughter through a fever that could have killed her. She told them about Iris, arriving in 1952, falling in love with a painter who came for a summer, keeping the secret of a son she could not raise.

She told them about the decommissioning. About the journals. About the trunk in the attic. About the mental health report. About the locket in the garden.

She told them about the silence that had run through four generations of March women, and about the words they had left behind.

When she finished, Emily was crying. Rachel was staring at the table. Alice was holding Thomas's hand.

"My mother," Thomas said. "She gave me up."

"Yes."

"She did it because she could not keep me at Larklight. Not without everyone knowing."

"Yes."

"She did it because she loved me."

"I believe so. Yes."

Thomas nodded. He did not cry. He was a man who had spent his life on the sea, and he had learned to hold his emotions the way a boat holds a course — steady, no matter the weather.

"I would like to see it," he said. "Larklight. I would like to see where I was born."

"It is ready for you."

Chapter 48: The Return

Thomas drove back to Maine with Lena.

They left at dawn, the harbor of Lockeport behind them, the coast road ahead. The radio played Canadian stations until they crossed the border, then American ones. They did not talk much at first. They were still learning how to be uncle and niece.

But as the morning wore on, the conversation came. Thomas asked about Lena's work, her life in Boston, the furniture she restored. Lena asked about fishing, about Nova Scotia, about his daughters. They found the small rhythms of conversation that strangers use to build a bridge.

"They have never asked me about my parents," Thomas said, somewhere south of Saint John. "My daughters. They know they were adopted. They know my father was Julian Cross. They have never asked about my mother."

"They will now."

"Yes. And I will have an answer."

When they reached the coastal highway, the sea appeared on their left, gray and blue in patches, the sky clearing toward afternoon. Thomas recognized the landscape without having seen it before — the granite, the spruce, the particular quality of the light.

"It is different from Nova Scotia," he said. "Similar. But different."

"Larklight is different from anywhere."

They turned onto the gravel road. The trees closed in. The road wound through the tunnel of spruce. Thomas sat forward in his seat, his hands on his knees.

And then the trees fell away, and the house appeared, and the lighthouse rose against the sky.

Thomas did not speak. He got out of the car and stood on the gravel, looking at the tower.

"It is taller than I remembered," he said.

"You remembered it?"

"The memory I told you about. The white tower. It was real."

He walked toward the lighthouse. He did not go up to it — he stopped at the garden wall, the gate still hanging at its angle, the wild iris blooming in the beds. He stood there, looking at the tower, the house, the sea beyond.

"I was born here," he said.

"You were born in that house. In the room that faces the sea."

Thomas nodded. He did not say anything else for a long time.

Lena left him there. She went into the house, made coffee, and watched through the kitchen window as her uncle walked the path to the lighthouse, put his hand on the chain-link fence, and looked up at the dark glass of the lantern room.

He stayed there until the sun began to set. Then he came inside, and they sat at the kitchen table, the same table where Sarah had written her first entry, where Ada had nursed her daughter, where Iris had waited for a painter to come back.

Thomas picked up Sarah's journal. "May I?"

"It belongs to you as much as it belongs to me."

He opened it to the first page. *I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for.*

He began to read.

Chapter 49: The House Full

The house at Larklight filled with voices.

Thomas stayed for a week. He read the journals, one by one, in order. He walked the point at all hours. He climbed the tower — the service door was still unlocked — and stood in the lantern room where his mother had stood on the last night of the manned light.

Alice and the girls came on the weekend. Rachel and Emily explored the house, the garden, the ledges. They asked questions that Lena could not always answer. They climbed the tower and took photographs of the view. They stood at the edge of the point, their hair whipped by the wind, and laughed into the spray.

And Margaret came.

Lena had called her after Thomas arrived, and Margaret had said she would come, and on the third day, Lena watched her mother's car pull up the gravel drive. Margaret got out slowly. She looked at the house, the lighthouse, the garden. She looked at Lena.

"You opened the windows," she said.

"I aired it out."

Margaret walked to the front door. She stopped before entering, her hand on the frame. "I have not been here since she died."

"I know."

"I did not think I would ever come back."

"And now?"

Margaret looked at the lighthouse. The lens was dark, the beam still. But the tower stood, white and straight and patient.

"Now I think I was wrong to stay away."

She went inside.

The meeting between Margaret and Thomas was not dramatic. They stood in the kitchen, two strangers connected by blood and silence, and they looked at each other.

"You are my brother," Margaret said.

"I believe I am."

"I did not know you existed until a few weeks ago."

"Neither did I. Not really. Not until I found my father's letters."

Margaret sat down at the table. Thomas sat across from her. They did not touch. They did not embrace. But they sat together at the same table where their mother had written her secrets, and that was enough.

The house, for the first time in decades, was full.

Chapter 50: The Light Goes On

On the last day of Thomas's visit, Lena climbed the tower.

She climbed alone. She did not tell anyone where she was going. She walked the path in the afternoon sunlight, unlocked the service door, and began the eighty-seven steps.

The tower was warm, the stone holding the heat of the summer day. The spiral stairs curved upward through the silence. She counted the steps as she climbed, as Sarah had counted them, as Ada had counted them, as Iris had counted them on the last night of the manned light.

The lantern room was the same as she had left it: the lens standing in its iron frame, the glass clean, the floor swept. The view from the windows was the same view Sarah had seen, the same view Ada had seen, the same view Iris had seen — the sea stretching to a hazy horizon, the ledges breaking the surface in dark ridges, the house small and white below.

She stood in the center of the room, her hand on the lens. The glass was cool, even in the summer heat, the prisms catching the light and throwing it in fragments across the walls.

She had the key to the automated mechanism. Iris had left it in her desk, wrapped in a note that said *For whoever needs it.*

Lena unlocked the panel. She found the switch. She hesitated.

The light had not burned since 1978. Forty-eight years. Two generations of March women had lived and died beside a dark tower.

She thought about Sarah, who had written *The light is the only thing that has not lied to me.*

She thought about Ada, who had kept the flame alive through a pandemic, who had written *The light is mine now.*

She thought about Iris, who had climbed this tower on the last night and had never come back up until the storm that brought the Coast Guard. Who had kept the letters. Who had kept the secret. Who had kept the house, the garden, the silence.

She thought about Thomas, reading the journals at the kitchen table. About Margaret, walking the point for the first time in decades. About Rachel and Emily, who had discovered a family they had not known existed. About herself, who had come to Larklight to clear a house and had found a story instead.

She flipped the switch.

The mechanism hummed. The lens began to turn. The beam — not the old flame, not the oil lamp that Sarah had tended, but the modern electric beam — swept across the glass and out over the sea.

It was the middle of the afternoon. The sun was high. The beam was barely visible, a pale sweep against the bright sky.

But it was burning.

Lena stood in the lantern room, watching the lens turn, watching the light sweep its patient arc. The tower hummed around her, alive again after forty-eight years.

She did not know how long she stood there. Long enough for the light to change, for the afternoon to deepen toward evening. Long enough for the beam to become visible as the sky darkened, a white blade cutting across the gray.

Below, in the garden, Thomas and Margaret stood side by side, looking up.

The light swept over them, over the house, over the point. It reached the horizon and kept going, as it had done every night for a hundred years, as it would do every night from now on.

Lena descended the stairs.

She did not turn off the light.

The light would burn until the timer shut it down at dawn. And tomorrow, she would climb the tower and turn it on again. And the day after that, and the day after that.

She did not know how long she would stay at Larklight. She did not know if she would keep the house or sell it, return to Boston or find a new life on the coast. She did not know what the next chapter held.

But the light was burning.

And for now, that was enough.
