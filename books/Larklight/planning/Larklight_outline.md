# Larklight — 50-Chapter Outline

## Premise

Larklight Point is a lighthouse on the coast of Maine, built in 1884 and decommissioned in 1978. For nearly a century, the March family kept its light. When Lena March returns after her grandmother Iris's death, she discovers journals written by four generations of the women who lived there — Sarah (1884), Ada (1918), Iris (1952–1978), and the unnamed final entries from the year the light went dark.

The novel is literary fiction: a multi-generational family saga about inheritance, silence, and the stories women leave behind. No crime, no investigation — only the slow revelation of what a house and a lighthouse have held.

## Core Cast (by timeline)

**Present (2026)**
- Lena March: 38, a furniture conservator in Portland, estranged from her grandmother for twelve years
- Iris March: Lena's grandmother, keeper of Larklight 1952–1978, dead at 94
- Margaret Cross: Lena's mother, Iris's daughter, who left Larklight at eighteen and never returned

**Sarah's Timeline (1884–1892)**
- Sarah March: Elias's wife, arrived at Larklight as a bride at 22
- Elias March: first official keeper of Larklight, a Civil War veteran, increasingly obsessed with the light
- Thomas Wainwright: younger assistant keeper, sent to Larklight after a scandal in another station
- Cora Bell: Sarah's younger sister, who visits in 1887 and stays

**Ada's Timeline (1918)**
- Ada March: Sarah's daughter-in-law, keeper's wife, keeps the light alone during the influenza pandemic
- Edward March: Ada's husband, serves with the Red Cross during the pandemic
- Dr. Nathaniel Parrish: town physician who rows out to Larklight during the quarantine

**Iris's Timeline (1952–1978)**
- Iris March: arrives as a bride in 1952, the last keeper's wife
- William March: Iris's husband, the last official keeper
- The painter: a summer visitor in 1965 who changes everything
- Theo Vance: Coast Guard inspector who oversees the decommissioning

## Chapter Plan

### Part 1: The Return (Chapters 1–10) — Present, 2026

1. **The Road to Larklight** — Lena March drives the coastal road toward Larklight Point after twelve years. The landscape returns to her in fragments: granite walls, salt marshes, the smell of low tide. She has not spoken to her grandmother since the summer of 2014. The lawyer's call came three days ago.

2. **The House** — Larklight is smaller than Lena remembered. The keeper's house clings to the granite slope, white clapboard weathered gray, storm shutters closed. Inside, Iris's life waits in boxes, jars, photographs, and the particular silence of a house whose owner has stopped breathing in it.

3. **The Trunk** — In the attic, beneath a tarp, Lena finds a trunk she has never seen. Inside: journals in four hands, letters tied with ribbon, a painted miniature of a lighthouse at sunset, and a lock of hair coiled in tissue paper. The first journal is dated 1884 and signed *Sarah March*.

4. **Sarah's First Entry** — Lena reads by flashlight as night comes to Larklight. *I arrived today. The sea is larger than I was prepared for.* Sarah March's first words, written on her wedding day, arriving at a lighthouse she had only seen in a drawing.

5. **The Keeper's Log** — Lena finds the official keeper's logs in Iris's study. The entries are mundane until 1892, when a new hand begins recording. The handwriting is not Elias's. The last entry by Elias is dated May 3, 1892. After that, Thomas Wainwright writes.

6. **The Dark Room** — Lena opens the door to the room at the end of the hall and finds it exactly as she remembers from childhood: a small bedroom where Iris kept her sewing, the walls covered in pressed flowers and botanical drawings. Beneath the bed, a box of letters addressed to Iris in a hand Lena does not recognize.

7. **The Light Itself** — Lena walks the path to the lighthouse tower for the first time. The door is locked, the glass dark. She stands at the base and feels the weight of the place — the thousand nights of burning oil and turning lenses, the women who climbed these stairs in storm skirts.

8. **Ada's Journal** — The second journal is bound in brown leather, the pages thin and fragile. *Edward left for Portland today. They are calling it influenza. I told him the light does not care for names.* Ada March, 1918. The entry breaks off mid-sentence.

9. **The Garden Wall** — Lena clears overgrowth from the walled garden behind the house. Beneath a collapsed trellis, she finds a small iron box containing a silver locket and a photograph: her grandmother Iris at twenty-three, standing beside a man who is not her grandfather.

10. **What the House Asks** — Lena sits in Iris's kitchen with the journals spread across the table. She has not called her mother. She has not decided whether to sell. Outside, the fog rolls in, and the decommissioned lighthouse stands in its silence, waiting.

### Part 2: The First Keeper's Wife (Chapters 11–20) — Sarah, 1884–1892

11. **The Bride** — 1884. Sarah arrives at Larklight by supply boat, twenty-two years old, married to a man she has known for six weeks. Elias March is fifteen years older, broad-shouldered, with the quiet of a man who has seen things he does not describe.

12. **The First Winter** — The winter of 1884 is brutal. Sarah learns to manage the house alone while Elias keeps the light. The isolation is a physical presence. She talks to herself. She begins a journal because she needs someone to speak to.

13. **Thomas Arrives** — Spring 1885. The Lighthouse Service assigns Thomas Wainwright as assistant keeper. He is young, educated, carrying a shame he does not explain. Elias resents him immediately.

14. **Cora's Visit** — Summer 1887. Sarah's younger sister Cora comes to stay. The house fills with female laughter for the first time. Cora sees something between Sarah and Thomas that Sarah herself has not acknowledged.

15. **The Obsession** — Elias's behavior changes. He stays in the tower for days, adjusting the lens, polishing brass, reading the light as if it holds messages meant for him alone. Sarah finds him speaking to the flame.

16. **What Thomas Knows** — Thomas confides to Sarah that Elias has been falsifying the log — recording weather conditions that do not match reality. Thomas is afraid Elias is losing his mind. Sarah must choose between loyalty and truth.

17. **Cora Stays** — Cora decides to remain at Larklight through the winter. Sarah suspects her sister is hiding something from their family in Boston. Cora reveals she is pregnant and unmarried. Sarah does not judge.

18. **The Storm** — December 1891. A nor'easter hits Larklight. Elias insists on going to the tower alone. Thomas and Sarah wait through the night. The light goes out at 3 AM. Elias is found in the lantern room, collapsed beside the lens, the flame still burning.

19. **The Aftermath** — Elias survives but is changed. The Lighthouse Service sends a physician who diagnoses "nervous exhaustion" — a euphemism for what will become a permanent removal from duty. Elias is pensioned. Thomas becomes acting keeper.

20. **Sarah's Choice** — Spring 1892. Elias dies quietly in his sleep. The journal entries from this period are spare, almost illegible. Sarah's last journal entry: *I will stay. Where else would I go? The light is the only thing that has not lied to me.* Sarah remains at Larklight with her children, and Thomas Wainwright stays as keeper.

### Part 3: The Influenza Year (Chapters 21–30) — Ada, 1918

21. **The Second Journal Opens** — 1918. Ada March, Sarah's daughter-in-law, takes up the journal tradition. Her husband Edward has gone to Portland with the Red Cross. The influenza is already in the port cities. Ada keeps the light alone with her daughter Ruth, age 6.

22. **The Town Quarantines** — Stillwater-on-Sea closes its school and church. Dr. Nathaniel Parrish rows to Larklight to warn Ada that the sickness is spreading faster than anyone will admit. He brings supplies and a letter from Edward that feels like a farewell.

23. **Ruth Falls Ill** — Ruth develops a fever. Ada has only the medicine cabinet: quinine, whiskey, mustard plaster. She keeps the light burning and tends her daughter by turns, sleeping in twenty-minute intervals. The journal entries become fragmented.

24. **The Light Alone** — Ada must climb the tower eighty-seven steps every four hours while Ruth shivers in the house below. She begins to see things in the dark — shapes at the edge of the lantern beam. She blames exhaustion.

25. **Dr. Parrish Returns** — Nathaniel Parrish arrives in a gale, bringing a nurse and the first vaccine doses to reach the coast. He stays to help with Ruth. Ada is grateful and terrified of what gratitude might become in isolation.

26. **Nell's Story** — The nurse, Nell O'Toole, is a widow who lost her husband to the war and her daughter to the flu in the same month. She tells Ada that grief is a country with no maps. Ada writes this in the journal.

27. **Edward Writes** — A letter from Edward arrives, dated three weeks earlier. He is alive. He is coming home. Ada reads it and feels nothing — then guilt for feeling nothing, then exhaustion too deep for guilt.

28. **The Quarantine Lifts** — November 1918. The armistice and the end of the quarantine arrive in the same week. Ada learns that half the town lost someone. Dr. Parrish returns to his practice. Nell leaves for the next outbreak. Ada remains.

29. **Edward Returns** — Edward comes home changed. He does not speak of what he saw. Ada does not ask. They sleep in the same bed and do not touch. The light is the only reliable thing. Ada writes: *We are both keepers of things we cannot name.*

30. **Larklight Continues** — Ada closes the journal with a resolution: she will write less and live more. The last entry is addressed to Ruth: *Someday you will read this. I hope you never understand it fully. That will mean you were spared.*

### Part 4: The Young Wife (Chapters 31–40) — Iris, 1952–1978

31. **Iris Arrives** — 1952. Iris Vance, twenty years old, marries William March and comes to Larklight as the new keeper's wife. She is young, romantic, raised inland, unprepared for the sea. The journal she begins is full of wonder.

32. **The Coast Guard Era** — The Lighthouse Service has been absorbed by the Coast Guard. Larklight is modernized: electric light, radio, telephone. William loves the new technology. Iris misses the old intimacy of oil and wick.

33. **Margaret Is Born** — 1955. Lena's mother Margaret is born at Larklight, delivered by a midwife who rows out in a December storm. Iris writes: *She is the first March child born here since Ruth. The house feels full for the first time.*

34. **The Quiet Years** — The 1950s pass in routine. William tends the light. Iris raises Margaret, tends the garden, writes in her journal. The entries become sparse. Contentment, she discovers, is harder to describe than suffering.

35. **The Painter** — Summer 1965. A painter named Julian Cross arrives on the supply boat, renting the old assistant keeper's cottage for the season. He is urban, educated, and he sees Iris in a way no one has seen her in years.

36. **What Begins** — Iris and Julian begin an affair — not in secrecy but in a strange, open acknowledgment that goes unspoken. He paints her in the garden, at the window, at the base of the tower. William either does not notice or chooses not to.

37. **The Summer Ends** — Julian leaves in September. Iris does not follow. The locket Lena found in the garden — Julian gave it to her. The photograph — Julian took it. Iris writes: *I will stay. Where else would I go?* The echo of Sarah is intentional.

38. **Margaret Leaves** — 1973. Margaret turns eighteen and leaves Larklight for college in Boston. She never comes back to stay. Iris writes: *She is doing what I should have done. This is pride, not pain. I think.*

39. **The Decommissioning** — 1978. The Coast Guard announces Larklight will be automated. No more keepers. William is offered early retirement. He takes it. The last night of the manned light, Iris climbs the tower alone before the automation team arrives.

40. **Iris's Last Entry** — December 1978. Iris's journal ends. The entry is brief: *The light burns without us. It always did. I will stay in the house. The light does not need me, but I am not ready to be needed by anything else.* She never writes another journal entry.

### Part 5: What Remains (Chapters 41–50) — Present, 2026

41. **The Last Journal** — Lena reaches the end of Iris's entries and finds the final pages of the trunk are not journals — they are letters from Julian to Iris, spanning 1965 to 1975, never mailed. Iris kept them all.

42. **The Photograph** — Lena finds a photograph in the false bottom of the trunk: Iris, Julian, and a child — a boy of about five — standing at the Larklight landing. The boy is not William's. Lena does the math.

43. **The Name** — Among Julian's letters, Iris saved a birth announcement. A boy. Born 1967. Name: Julian Cross March. Adopted by a family in Portsmouth. Iris never told anyone — not William, not Margaret, not the child himself.

44. **The Coast Guard Records** — Lena drives to the Coast Guard archive in Portland. She requests the decommissioning file for Larklight. Inside, she finds William's departure statement — and a report that the automation was delayed by one month because of a keeper's mental health evaluation. The keeper was Iris.

45. **The Mental Health Record** — Iris was evaluated in 1978 after an incident: she had climbed the tower during a storm and refused to come down for twelve hours. The report notes "disorientation, fixation on the light, possible danger to self." Lena recognizes the pattern from Elias's story.

46. **Calling Margaret** — Lena calls her mother for the first time in a year. Margaret lives in Vermont, remarried, a different name. Lena tells her about the journals, the letters, the brother she never knew existed. The line is silent for a long time.

47. **The Brother** — Margaret reveals she knew. Not about the adoption — she learned that from Lena's call — but about Julian. She found her mother's letters in 1979. Iris begged her not to tell William. Margaret promised and left Larklight anyway.

48. **Finding Him** — Lena searches for Julian Cross March. He lives in Nova Scotia. A fisherman. Married. Two daughters. Lena calls him. He knows he was adopted. He does not know who his birth mother was. Lena tells him: Iris March. Larklight. 1967.

49. **The Return to Larklight** — Lena drives back to Larklight one last time. She opens every window. She brings all the journals to the kitchen table and arranges them in chronological order: Sarah, Ada, Iris, the letters, the photographs. She reads the whole story in order. Then she climbs the tower.

50. **The Light Goes On** — The lighthouse has been dark since 1978. Lena has the key — found in Iris's desk — and the knowledge of how the modernized mechanism works from the keeper's logs. She does not light the old flame. She switches on the automated beacon, one time, in the middle of the afternoon. The lens turns. The light sweeps the sea. It means nothing to the ships — the sun is high. But from the house, from the garden, from the granite point, Larklight burns for one moment. Then Lena packs the journals, locks the house, and drives north to Nova Scotia to meet her brother. The house will wait. The light will wait. They are March women. They know how.

---

*Larklight is a work of literary fiction. No crime, no murder, no investigation. Only the accumulated weight of four generations of women who kept a light burning at the edge of the world.*
