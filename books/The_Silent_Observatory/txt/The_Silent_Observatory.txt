Chapter 1: First Notice

Eli Soren reached the threshold at morning, with ash on the sill and the final observation log on her mind. As a maintenance engineer, she trusted small errors more than speeches. The error in front of her was small enough to be useful. Someone had handled the evidence in haste, and haste had left a mark.

Priya Mehta stood beside her and kept his voice low. "We can still walk away from this," he said. Eli Soren looked toward Halden Observatory, where lamps burned behind glass. "Walking away is how this became official," she said. She wrote the time, the place, and the condition of the room in a narrow notebook.

The practical problem was a missing page. It sat in the day like a nail under a boot. Tomas Vek brought coffee in a chipped cup and pretended it was hospitality. Her hands shook only once, when Director Sella Rhine was named. That was enough. Fear, like ink, spread fastest when no one admitted it had spilled.

By the middle of the day, Eli Soren had three facts and no clean theory. First, the record had been altered after the event. Second, the alteration protected someone with access rather than someone with talent. Third, the people most eager for order were the people most frightened by sequence.

Dr. Vera Nohr arrived with the expression of a person bringing bad news already rehearsed. "You are asking the wrong question," Dr. said. "Then give me the right one." The answer took a moment. "Ask who needed everyone else to be tired. Tired people sign what fresh people read."

That advice changed the room. Eli Soren stopped hunting for a grand design and looked for labor: who carried boxes, who replaced locks, who swept floors, who copied names into the official hand. The conspiracy, if it was one, had ordinary hours. This made it less glamorous and harder to forgive.

Near evening, Director Sella Rhine sent a message that sounded generous if read quickly. The meeting could be postponed. The documents could be reviewed by neutral parties. There was no need for public embarrassment. Eli Soren read it twice, then gave it to Priya Mehta. "He is buying darkness," she said. "Then we keep the lights on."

They returned to the final observation log before the building closed. Under a flatter light, the flaw became a direction. A scratch lined up with a hinge, a number answered another number, and a name moved from rumor into ink. Eli Soren felt no triumph. Triumph belonged to easier stories.

Before leaving, she placed a slip of paper where only the next honest reader would find it. On it she wrote First Notice, not as drama but as an index. If she failed, someone else would need a beginning. Outside, the snowbound Halden Observatory waited with its closed mouths and lit windows.
