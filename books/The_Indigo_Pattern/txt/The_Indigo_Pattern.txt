Chapter 1: Blue Dust

Mina Tor arrived at Maison Arel through the service alley, not the glass front where cameras had been set earlier in the evening. The alley was narrow, wet from a recent rinse, and crowded with rolling racks shrouded in muslin. Two smokers in black production clothes stopped talking when they saw the evidence case in her hand. One of them crushed his cigarette too quickly and left a streak of ash on his shoe.

Inside, the building held three odors at once: perfume from departed guests, steam from garment care, and the metallic fear of a show interrupted. The foyer lights were still theatrical, too flattering for panic. Beyond them, staff moved with the briskness of people who had no useful work left but could not bear to stand still.

The model had collapsed forty minutes earlier. Ambulance gone. Guests gone. Rumor everywhere.

Oren Vale, production manager, met Mina at the backstage door. He was lean, gray at the temples, and carrying three clipboards as if paper could keep the walls upright. A headset hung around his neck. The cord had knotted itself in the lapel of his jacket.

"Anouk Saye," he said, without greeting. "Conscious now. Hospital says exposure, maybe allergy, maybe poison. She was wearing the indigo archive gown."

"Who called me?"

"Celia. Then me. Then counsel, but counsel mostly called to say fewer words."

"Where is the gown?"

"Sealed in dressing room three. No one touched it after I called you."

That was rarely true. Mina had worked enough museums, houses, auction rooms, and panicked private collections to know that no one touched it often meant no one admits touching it. Still, she appreciated the aspiration.

They passed a table covered with abandoned champagne flutes. One held lipstick on the rim, another a drowned black hairpin. Programs lay across chairs in silver stacks. AREL RETURNS, the covers announced, as if type could command history to behave.

"Tell me the room," Mina said.

"Single door. Mirror wall. Dressing table. Steamer station. Anouk changed there with Maren, one of the dressers. Archive supervision from Talia. Brigitte hovered. Celia came in once."

"Luc Renard?"

Oren's mouth tightened. "Investor. He was everywhere and nowhere."

Dressing room three was small, bright, and too warm. The taped door opened with a complaint from the hinges. Mina stopped in the threshold. Powder dusted the vanity. A half-drunk water bottle stood beside a cracked compact. False lashes lay curled like black commas. The steamer sat unplugged, its hose draped with unnatural care over the stand.

The gown hung inside a clear garment bag on a padded hanger. Dark blue as midnight cloth, narrow-waisted, severe, beautiful enough to make people stupid. The crescent neckline cut inward with a precision that turned the body into architecture. Mina did not touch it yet.

"Who bagged it?" she asked.

"Maren says Talia did before the preview. After Anouk collapsed, I zipped it closed and taped the room."

"You zipped it?"

Oren looked pained. "Yes."

"With gloves?"

"No."

"Then we will have your prints and your regret."

"Both available in abundance."

Mina set down the case and took photographs before crossing the room. Wide shots first, then the table, the floor, the steamer, the garment bag, the latch. She worked slowly. Panic liked to edit scenes. The camera resisted.

On the brass latch, beneath the tape Oren had placed, she saw a smear of blue dust. Not lint. Too granular. It sat in the groove where a thumb would press, a small crescent of powder caught in tarnish.

"Is that from the gown?" Oren asked.

"Maybe."

She opened the case, pulled on nitrile gloves, and lifted the dust with sterile tape. Under the portable light the particles glittered faintly, brighter than old indigo usually did. She labeled the strip, then collected a control from the untouched lower hinge.

"Who opened this room after Anouk went on?" she asked.

Oren looked at the latch, then at her. His face gave her the answer before his mouth did.

"Everyone will say no one."

"Good," Mina said. "We can start with everyone."

In the mirror, behind their reflections, the gown hung like a witness that had learned silence from rich rooms.

Before Mina left the room, she asked Oren to stand exactly where he had stood when he zipped the bag. He did so, embarrassed by the pantomime, and the mirror multiplied him into three versions of the same regret. His reach explained one faint print near the zipper but not the latch dust under the tape. Mina marked that distinction in her notebook. Small mercies mattered to frightened witnesses; small exclusions mattered to cases. In the hall, someone laughed too loudly and then stopped, as if the building had corrected them. Mina sealed the room again, signed the tape, and handed Oren the pen. His signature came out jagged. "Good," she said. "Now the room has a before and after." He looked through the narrow glass at the gown. "And Anouk?" "She already had one," Mina said. "We are trying to make sure the after belongs to her."

Chapter 2: Sequence

Oren reconstructed backstage with the speed of a man who had survived fashion by mistrusting glamour. He brought Mina to a cutting table and covered it with call sheets, staff assignments, press schedules, and a hand-drawn plan of the route from archive lift to runway entrance. His handwriting was impatient but legible. That placed him above most witnesses.

"The indigo gown came from the archive at noon," he said. "Supervised by Talia Voss. Conservation check at two. Fitting at four. Press walkthrough at six. Anouk dressed at seven-thirty. Collapse at eight-oh-six, halfway down the runway."

"Who handled it directly?"

"Talia. Two dressers. Me. Anouk. Celia looked but did not touch. Brigitte touched the box, not the gown. Luc's people filmed everything until they were told not to."

"Luc's people."

"Investor documentation. Behind-the-scenes content. Proof of revival. Pick the phrase that makes your teeth hurt least."

Mina marked times in a notebook. "Names."

"Elise Varo is Luc's assistant. She coordinated camera crews, guests, social clips, and apparently the moral decline of my evening."

"Did she handle the gown?"

"She will say no."

"I asked you."

Oren looked toward the hall. Machines rattled faintly in the atelier, though no one should have been working. "I saw her near the garment bag before dressing. I did not see her touch it."

"Near is a distance people use when they are afraid of nearer."

"Then near."

They walked the route. The archive lift opened into a service corridor, then a turn past the steamer station, then the dressing rooms, then a black-curtained passage to the runway. Mina moved with a flashlight angled low to the floor. Blue particles appeared at three points: the dressing-room latch, the rubber mat near the steamer, and a pale trace on the runway carpet where Oren said Anouk had first stumbled.

Mina collected each with tape lifts and clean vials. She also collected nearby controls, because a single blue speck in a fashion house was evidence only if compared to all the other blue specks a fashion house could provide.

"Does indigo shed like that?" Oren asked.

"Old dyed cloth can shed. This looks handled."

"Handled how?"

"With purpose or incompetence. I try not to choose early."

He almost smiled. It faded when they reached the runway entrance.

The house lights were up now, unkind after the spectacle. The blue carpet ran down the room like a deliberate river. Empty chairs faced it in clean rows. A program lay open on one seat to the collection notes: heritage, rebirth, the crescent line, the return of Arel blue. Someone had stepped on the corner and left a damp heel mark.

"She came through here," Oren said. "Stopped there. One hand at her throat. Then she tried to keep walking."

"Who reached her first?"

"Stage manager. Then me. Then Celia. Brigitte stood up but did not come onto the carpet. Luc was talking to a guest near the aisle."

"Did anyone remove anything from her?"

"Maren loosened the gown at the neck before paramedics arrived. Talia cried about cutting fabric. Anouk told us to cut it if we had to. We did not."

"Why not?"

Oren's jaw worked. "Because paramedics said she was breathing. Because everyone in this building has been trained to fear damaging the archive. Because in the first thirty seconds people are idiots."

It was a useful answer because it was ugly.

Mina crouched where Anouk had stumbled. The trace on the carpet was faint, caught in the nap. She sealed it. A photographer at the far end lifted his camera, then lowered it when Oren looked at him.

"If this was poison," Oren said, "the show is dead."

Mina stood. "If this was poison, that is not the first thing dead people worried about."

He nodded once, chastened without resentment. "What do you need?"

"The gown undisturbed. Every steamer. Every spray bottle. Every glove. Access logs. Staff names. Hospital contact. And no statement blaming vintage fabric until vintage fabric earns it."

"You think someone will try?"

Mina looked back toward dressing room three. "People often prefer guilty objects to guilty people."

They returned by the same route once more, this time with Oren silent and Mina counting steps. Eleven from lift to steamer mat. Seven from steamer to dressing room three. Fourteen from dressing room to curtain. The distances were short enough for a person to move quickly and long enough for a rumor to change shape. At the steamer station, a shallow puddle had dried into a mineral ring. Mina collected it though Oren said the mop crew used treated water everywhere. "Everywhere is useful if everywhere differs," she said. A dresser passing with a bundle of black hangers slowed at the sound of her voice, then hurried on. Oren watched the retreat. "They think being near this makes them suspect." Mina labeled the vial. "They are right. Nearness is not guilt. It is where truth usually starts."

Chapter 3: Safe Before Sunday

Celia Arel received Mina in the creative director's office, a glass room overlooking the atelier floor. Below them, seamstresses and cutters sat at machines they were pretending to use. Fear made bad stitches. Everyone knew it, but nobody wanted to be the first to stop working.

Celia was in her thirties, dressed in black trousers and an unfinished ivory jacket pinned at one shoulder. One sleeve had not been set. White basting threads hung from it like loose nerves. Her face held exhaustion and inheritance in equal measure.

"I need to know whether the gown is safe," she said.

Mina set her evidence case beside a low chair and did not sit. "Safe for evidence or safe for another model?"

Celia looked away through the glass. "Sunday is the revival show. Tonight was press preview. If the archive gown is contaminated by age, we can withdraw it. If someone poisoned Anouk, everything becomes criminal."

"Everything is already potentially criminal."

"Potentially is a large word."

"So is poison."

The answer struck harder than Mina intended. Celia sat, then took a pin from her sleeve and pressed its point lightly against her thumb. She did not break skin. She seemed to need the threat of a small pain.

"Maison Arel has been closed for nine years," she said. "We kept the atelier for alterations, private clients, archive loans, but not a house collection. This revival is the only reason Luc Renard invested. The indigo silhouette is the house. My grandmother built her name on it."

"Your grandmother or your archive?"

Celia's eyes sharpened. "You have been here one hour."

"Cloth ages. Stories do too."

For a moment, Celia looked less offended than relieved someone had said it plainly. Then the director returned, smooth and careful.

"There are letters," she said. "Claims from an old cutter's apprentice. Sabine Holt. She says the crescent line was not Arel's original work."

"Is she credible?"

"Brigitte says no."

"That was not my question."

Celia looked down at her pinned sleeve. "I do not know. That is worse, in practice, than no."

Mina heard movement below. A worker carried a bolt of indigo wool to a table, then turned back when she saw Celia watching. Everyone in the room below knew decisions made in glass rooms could fall on their wages.

"Who is Brigitte to the show?" Mina asked.

"My aunt. Archive chair. Keeper of every story we have told about ourselves. She was my grandmother's daughter, though she would say disciple if it did not sound theatrical." Celia's mouth tightened. "She will make your work difficult. She believes the archive is a shrine."

"Shrines collect dust."

"This one collects lawyers."

"And Luc?"

"Investor. He bought debt, paid suppliers, promised retail expansion, and now holds several clauses I regret reading too late."

"Does he benefit if the archive gown is declared unsafe?"

Celia stared at her. "That is a very fast question."

"Money is often faster than grief."

"If the archive garment fails, we can use a new copy. Insurance covers delay, not cancellation, depending on cause. Luc gains control over remediation and licensing if heritage assets become unstable. That was framed as protection."

"For whom?"

The silence answered before Celia did. "For Luc."

Mina sat at last. The chair was low and expensive and designed for people with no work to do in it. "Anouk is conscious. We will speak to her. Toxicology is pending. Until then, no one uses the gown, no one cleans the room, and no one releases language about a tragic vintage reaction."

"Brigitte has already drafted something."

"Undraft it."

Celia almost smiled. "You do not know Brigitte."

"No. But I know evidence. It resents being managed."

The door opened without a knock. A woman in navy wool and pearls looked in, saw Mina, and decided not to apologize.

"The board is asking for Sunday assurance," Brigitte Arel said.

Celia rose. In the half-made jacket, one shoulder looked armored, the other vulnerable. "They can have the truth when we have it."

Brigitte's gaze moved to Mina's case. "Truth is seldom ready on schedule."

Mina stood too. "Then Sunday is in trouble."

After Brigitte left, Celia remained standing with one hand on the back of the chair. The atelier below had resumed its false industry. Needles rose and fell, but Mina saw two workers unpick the same seam twice. "When I was twenty," Celia said, "Brigitte made me identify ten black silks by touch before she let me sit in an archive meeting. I thought rigor and cruelty were the same education." Mina closed her case. "They often share classrooms." Celia looked at her sharply, then nodded as if filing the sentence somewhere private. "If Anouk dies, the house ends." "If Anouk lives, the question remains." "Which question?" "What the house is willing to do to remain a house." Below, the machines stopped together for a break, and the sudden quiet made the glass office feel exposed.

Chapter 4: Wrong Indigo

Dr. Elias Morn ran the toxicology lab in a hospital basement and had the serene manner of a man who trusted machines more than committees. He wore a sweater under his lab coat, kept his reading glasses on a chain, and labeled sample trays in block capitals so precise they looked printed.

"You brought me fashion," he said when Mina arrived.

"I brought you exposure."

"Fashion with consequences, then."

The samples had been rushed from Maison Arel and from Anouk's medical team: swabs from her neckline, a gauze pad from her throat, residue from the gown lining, tape lifts from the latch and runway carpet, a control from the hinge, and a vial of water taken from the steamer reservoir. Elias logged each item twice, because his distrust of human optimism extended to his own hands.

Mina stood behind the yellow line while he opened the first packet under the hood. The blue dust on the tape looked harmless in the bright lab light, almost decorative. That was one reason she disliked beautiful evidence. People wanted it to remain metaphor.

"Hospital notes?" he asked.

"Hoarseness, dizziness, skin irritation at the neckline, respiratory distress but no lasting airway compromise so far. Anouk reported a bitter almond smell and heat against the throat."

Elias lifted his eyes. "Bitter almond?"

"She said that to paramedics. Oren heard it. I have not interviewed her yet."

"People hear that phrase and think murder mystery. Usually it is cleaner to think chemistry."

"That is why I came here."

He worked without theatrics. Solvent extraction. Microscopy. Quick screens first, then chromatographic runs. Mina waited with vending-machine coffee and read the labels from Maison Arel's seized supply list. Indigo pigment. Conservation tissue. Cedar blocks. Starch. Steam water additive. BlueArc Finish 7, display use only.

After the first results printed, Elias made a small dissatisfied sound.

"Not historical indigo," he said.

Mina joined him at the glass. "What is it?"

"Indigo pigment is present, yes. Plant-derived or synthetic, I will separate later. But the toxic response points to a cyanogenic compound carried in a modern finishing spray. Bitter almond odor fits. Skin irritation fits. Respiratory symptoms fit."

"Could a vintage dye degrade into that?"

"Not politely."

"Impolitely?"

"Still no, unless someone added modern chemistry and invited moisture."

He handed her preliminary charts. Peaks rose sharp and bright across the paper. The compound was strongest near Anouk's neckline and on residue associated with the steamer, weaker on the hem, faint on the latch. The runway sample held enough to show transfer, not source. The hinge control was clean.

"Delivery method?" Mina asked.

"Aerosol or steam. The compound needs moisture and warmth to release efficiently. Dry on a dark garment, it might be nearly invisible. Heated near skin, it becomes rude."

"A steamer."

"Possible. Do not quote me as certain until I have ruined more samples."

Mina appreciated Elias for the same reason she liked good fabric: he held shape under pressure.

She called Oren from the corridor. He answered on the first ring.

"Tell me something that lets me breathe," he said.

"Secure every steamer, spray bottle, glove, and garment bag. Especially anything from dressing room three."

"Already doing it."

"Do it again. Lock them in one room and log every hand that touches the key."

There was a pause. She heard voices behind him, someone asking about journalists.

"How bad?" Oren asked.

"Bad enough that the gown may be innocent."

"People are going to hate that."

"People are fond of blaming cloth. Cloth rarely hires lawyers."

When she returned to the lab, Elias had isolated a clearer trace from the neckline swab. He looked less serene now and more offended.

"If someone used this on a garment to be worn, they ignored labels. If someone used it in a steamer, they ignored labels with enthusiasm."

"Could a dresser make that mistake?"

"A dresser who cannot read, cannot smell, and cannot understand the phrase do not aerosolize. Do you have one?"

"I have a fashion house full of people who read care labels like scripture."

"Then find the person who wanted scripture revised."

Mina looked at the blue peaks on the screen. They were too modern, too clean, too useful. "I intend to."

Elias walked her to the elevator, still holding the chart as if letting it out of his sight might make it less offensive. "The old-dye explanation will be attractive," he said. "It sounds tragic and nobody goes to jail." Mina pressed the call button. "That is why it needs killing cleanly." He nodded. "I will run confirmatory tests overnight. If the carrier is what I think, it will leave a polymer signature. The body metabolizes quickly. Objects remember longer." Mina smiled faintly. "That is my line." "Chemistry had it first." The elevator opened on a tired nurse pushing an empty wheelchair. For a second, the whole case narrowed to that chair, its vinyl seat shining under hospital light. Anouk had not needed it when she entered Maison Arel. Mina stepped inside and called Oren again before the doors closed.

Chapter 5: Opened Seam

The indigo gown lay on padded supports under cold light, no longer hanging like theater but flattened into evidence. Mina had asked for the sample room because it had a locking door, clean surfaces, and no portraits of dead Arel women watching from gilt frames. Oren stood behind the tape line. Talia Voss, archive assistant, stood beside him with a ledger hugged to her chest.

Mina worked with tweezers, magnification, and the slow respect due to old construction. The dress had a severe crescent neckline, a bodice cut in diagonal tension, and side seams so clean they looked inevitable. It was not fragile in the ordinary sense. It had strength, but old cloth kept strength differently from new cloth. It could hold and still resent surprise.

"Do you need to cut?" Talia asked.

"Not yet."

"Brigitte asked me to record any invasive action."

"Record that I heard her through you."

Oren coughed once, possibly to hide a laugh.

Mina photographed the exterior, then the lining. At the inner bodice, beneath a conservation stitch, she found the interruption. The original seam had been opened and closed again. The thread matched in color but not twist. Under magnification, tiny blue crystals clung to the newer stitch holes.

"After conservation," Mina said.

Oren leaned forward despite the tape. "How can you tell?"

"Conservation thread is cotton-wrapped polyester. This repair is silk. Someone wanted old-looking work, not stable work."

Talia's face went still. "The final conservation report showed no opened seam."

"Who handled the gown after final conservation?"

"Archive staff. Brigitte. Celia for review. Production for fitting, with supervision."

"And Luc's documentation team?"

Talia looked down at the ledger. "They filmed the box opening."

"Did they touch it?"

"They should not have."

"Should is a decorative word."

Mina continued. At the neckline, where Anouk reported the scratch, the edge had been lifted slightly higher than shown in conservation photographs. A tiny burr of thread protruded inward. Not enough to tear skin badly, but enough to scratch if pressed and dampened. The toxic residue concentrated there.

"Why open the bodice?" Oren asked.

"To insert, remove, dust, inspect, or adjust. Cloth remembers disturbance."

"Could Brigitte have opened it for fit?"

Talia answered before Mina. "Brigitte does not sew anymore."

"That is not the same as cannot."

The archive assistant flushed. "No."

Inside the garment bag, Mina found a single broken thread, pale beige, not from the gown. It looked like muslin basting thread. She lifted it and sealed it.

"Pattern room," Oren said.

"Maybe. Or a muslin cover. Or someone's cuff. We let the fiber earn its address."

Talia stepped closer. "There was a copy made."

Mina looked up. "Of the gown?"

"A runway-safe version, for Sunday if Brigitte changed her mind about the archive original. Celia did not want Anouk wearing the old dress at the main show. The copy was fitted in muslin first."

"Who cut the copy?"

"Pattern team, under Celia. Based on archive drafts."

"Which drafts?"

Talia's grip tightened on the ledger. "That is complicated."

Oren looked at her. "Everything becomes complicated when Brigitte enters a room."

Mina returned to the seam. The opened area was not clumsy. Whoever had done it understood enough construction to hide from a hurried eye. That widened the suspect pool in a couture house and narrowed it everywhere else.

She drew a particle map on acetate: neckline, inner seam, stitch holes, bag interior, latch. Then she asked Talia for the conservation photographs.

"Brigitte keeps originals in the archive," Talia said.

"And copies?"

"Digital system."

"I want both."

"She may refuse."

Mina sealed the final swab and looked at the gown, not at Talia. "Then she can explain why preservation requires withholding before the woman who wore this has finished coughing."

Oren rubbed his forehead. "The archive is going to become a war."

Mina placed the beige thread into the case. "Archives are wars that learned filing."

Talia lingered after Oren went to fetch the conservation drives. She watched Mina close the evidence pouch as if the small beige thread might accuse her first. "It could be from a toile," she said. "The copy bodice was fitted twice. Muslin everywhere." Mina wrote toile? beside the item number. "Then the thread will find friends." "And if it does not?" "Then it was traveling alone for a reason." Talia nodded, though the answer did not comfort her. In the corridor, Brigitte's voice rose, clipped and cold, asking why the archive room had not been restored to order. The word restored carried through the door. Mina looked at the opened seam. Some people used restoration to mean repair. Others used it to mean no one could see what had been done.

Chapter 6: The Shrine

Brigitte Arel kept the archive key on a chain around her neck. She wore navy wool, pearls, and a displeasure so complete it seemed tailored. When Mina arrived with Oren, Brigitte stood in the corridor before the archive door as if guarding a chapel from tourists.

"No cutting, no solvent, no destructive sampling," she said.

"Then no conclusion."

"You already have samples."

"I have some. I need records, storage boxes, handling logs, and comparable indigo pieces."

"Maison Arel survived occupation, bankruptcy, vulgar licensing, and my niece's education in minimalism. It will survive your impatience."

Oren looked briefly at the ceiling, perhaps asking it for strength.

Mina stood at the archive threshold. Behind Brigitte, visible through the glass panel, gowns rested in drawers, pattern books filled shelves, and muslin forms hung from the ceiling in long white rows. The room smelled even from the hall of cedar, tissue, and old starch.

"A woman collapsed wearing your archive garment," Mina said.

"A model collapsed during a stressful event."

"She had toxic exposure at the neckline."

"From what, you do not yet know."

"Correct. That is why I need access."

Brigitte turned her gaze on Oren. "You let production people become police when you panic."

Oren's jaw tightened. "I let evidence people see evidence."

"Evidence people do not understand houses. They reduce legacy to lint."

"Lint can testify," Mina said.

For the first time, Brigitte looked at her with something like interest. Not liking. Interest was more useful.

"You are impertinent."

"Only when delayed."

After a long moment, Brigitte unlocked the door. She did not step aside fully. Mina had to pass close enough to see the fine powder caught in the setting of her pearls.

"Talia will supervise," Brigitte said.

The archive assistant appeared from between shelves, small, neat, and watchful. She carried a ledger in white-gloved hands. Her dark hair was pinned so tightly it looked painful. Mina wondered whether she had slept.

"The indigo gown's box is this way," Talia said.

The archive was colder than the rest of the house. Drawers were labeled by decade and material. Cotton tape hung from shelf handles. There were framed photographs of models in gowns and only a few photographs of workers. Mina noticed because absence had mass.

Talia drew out a long flat box. Its cream label read AREL 1963, CRESCENT EVENING, RESTORED 2018. The blue ink looked aged at first glance. At second glance, Mina did not trust it.

"Who wrote this label?"

"I do not know. It was present when I began here."

"When was that?"

"Four years ago."

Brigitte, from behind them, said, "Labels are replaced as necessary. The object matters, not the paper."

"Paper often knows who touched the object."

Mina photographed the label, the box lid, the tissue, the drawer, the shelf, and the ledger entry. She asked Talia to turn pages without prompting. The assistant obeyed too quickly, as if relieved to be given precise tasks.

The ledger showed the gown moving for conservation, photography, investor presentation, and fitting. Several entries had different ink. One box number had been scraped and rewritten. Mina ran gloved fingers near the line, not touching yet.

"Why was I-14 changed to I-41?"

"Consolidation of indigo pieces," Talia said.

Brigitte added, "For clarity."

"Clarity usually does not require abrasion."

Silence cooled the room further.

Mina asked for comparative indigo threads from documented Arel garments. Brigitte objected to sampling. Mina asked for loose dust from box corners instead. That objection died under its own absurdity.

As Talia collected, Mina noticed a narrow strip of discolored wood behind export cartons on the far wall, as if a cabinet had once been opened more often than it now was. Talia saw her notice and looked away.

Shrines, Mina knew, hid their dust in corners no worshipper admitted existed. This archive had corners enough for a congregation.

Before leaving, Mina asked to see the handling table where the gown had been reviewed for investors. Talia led her to a side alcove with a padded surface and a camera mount still clamped to the shelf. The mount was Luc's, not archive equipment; its rubber foot had left a black oval on the pale wood. Brigitte disliked the mark visibly. "Documentation requires tools," she said. Mina photographed it anyway. Beside the table stood a glove bin with blue nitrile boxes pushed behind a tray of cream cotton pairs. "Presentation order," Talia said, too quickly. Mina lifted one cream glove with tweezers. Clean, old cotton, no pearl button. Not the glove Anouk had described, but another sign of aesthetic preference outranking procedure. Brigitte's shrine had learned to arrange even its safeguards for appearance.

Chapter 7: Bitter Almond

Anouk Saye met Mina in a private hospital room with one wrist taped for an IV and a face scrubbed clean of runway paint. She was thirty-two, hoarse, angry, and not interested in being called lucky. The room smelled of antiseptic and lilies someone had sent without knowing her well.

"Everyone keeps saying lucky," Anouk said before Mina finished introducing herself. "Lucky is what they say when they want you grateful instead of furious."

Mina sat beside the bed. "Furious is more useful today."

"Good. I am very useful."

Her voice rasped at the edges. A red line marked the left side of her neck where the gown had scratched. Around it, the skin was irritated in a pattern too focused for a simple allergy. Mina did not stare long. Witnesses were not exhibits.

"Tell me what happened before the runway."

"Which part? Being powdered like furniture, being told not to breathe too deeply, or being zipped into a family religion?"

"Start with the dress."

Anouk looked toward the window. The blinds were half-closed, striping the bedcover. "It was colder than I expected. Heavy but not heavy everywhere. The bodice pulled diagonally. Beautiful construction, if you like being converted into a line drawing."

"Did it hurt?"

"The neckline scratched here." She touched near the mark but not on it. "I told the dresser. She said vintage gowns have teeth."

"Which dresser?"

"Maren. Nervous, sweet, smelled like mints. She did not want me hurt. She tried to pad the edge with tissue, but someone said no visible correction."

"Who?"

Anouk closed her eyes. "Not Talia. Not Celia. A woman with pale gloves. Expensive gloves, pearl button at the wrist. She said Brigitte wanted the crescent to sit higher."

"Elise Varo?"

Anouk opened her eyes. "Luc's assistant. Yes. I remember because she had the tone people use when their salary depends on sounding gentle."

"What did she do?"

"She stood behind me and lifted the left edge. Pinched it. Smoothed it. The fabric was damp. I thought from steaming. Something touched my skin right where it scratched."

"Did she use a spray?"

"I did not see one. Maren had steamed the gown earlier. Then people left. Elise came in. Brigitte stood in the doorway for a minute, looking at me like a mannequin had inconvenienced her."

"Did Brigitte touch you?"

"No. She touched the air near me. People like that think proximity is ownership."

Mina wrote that down because good witnesses sometimes phrased motive better than investigators.

"When did you smell bitter almond?"

"After Elise adjusted the neckline. Bitter almond and hot sugar. I thought it was the steamer, or someone's perfume turning bad under lights. I felt light-headed before I stepped out."

"Did you tell anyone?"

"I told Maren I felt strange. She said breathe through it. Not cruelly. Backstage people say that to everyone about everything."

"On the runway?"

"Heat. Lights. The scratch burned. Then my throat felt narrow. I remember seeing the front row blur. I put one hand up and thought, absurdly, do not fall in the archive gown. Then I thought, fall if you need to breathe, idiot."

Her mouth almost smiled and failed.

Mina let the room rest before asking, "Did you threaten not to wear it?"

Anouk studied her. "Threaten? I said if the scratch worsened I would not walk. I said it in fitting, in front of Talia, Celia, Elise, maybe Brigitte. I am not twenty. I do not bleed for silhouettes."

"How did they respond?"

"Celia said they would adjust. Talia looked relieved. Elise looked like I had knocked over a stack of money."

"Am I publicity now?" Anouk asked after a moment.

"People may try."

"I am not wearing another myth for them."

"Good."

Anouk looked at her. "You say that like it helps."

"It does. Objects get quiet when people refuse to be props."

This time the almost-smile arrived. "Find the pale gloves."

"I intend to."

As Mina left, Anouk called after her.

"And use my name. Not incident. Not model."

Mina turned. "Anouk Saye."

"Yes," Anouk said, and closed her eyes, not to sleep but to end the interview on her own terms.

In the hallway, Mina paused to write while the details were still warm. Hospitals flattened time; every room hummed the same, every door wore the same number plate, and witnesses began to doubt whether sequence mattered. She wrote it mattered. Pale gloves after steaming. Damp touch at scratch. Bitter almond before runway. Refusal voiced in fitting. Anouk's agent arrived with a phone in each hand and asked whether the house had offered a statement. "They offered language," Mina said. "That is not the same thing." The agent looked toward Anouk's door and lowered both phones. "She hates being managed." "Good." Mina capped her pen. "Managed people become useful to other people's stories." The agent studied her, decided not to argue, and went in quietly.

Chapter 8: Sabine's Claim

Sabine Holt lived above a dry cleaner on a street that smelled of starch, rain, and old wool. She opened the door with a tape measure around her neck and a thimble on her finger, as if the world might need correcting at any moment. Her hair was white, cut blunt at the jaw. Her eyes were not old in the forgiving way people liked to assign to women who had endured things. They were sharp and dissatisfied.

"Arel stole the crescent cut," she said before tea.

Mina, still on the landing, lowered the hand she had raised to introduce herself. "From whom?"

"My mentor. Ida Sayegh. Pattern cutter, not family, not photographed, not invited to dinners. She drafted the diagonal bodice in 1962. Brigitte's mother signed it into legend."

"May I come in?"

"If you do not say alleged before every noun."

The room was small and ordered. Pattern paper lay in rolls beneath the window. A worktable occupied more space than the sofa. On the wall hung framed scraps of muslin with notches marked in pencil. Sabine poured tea without asking whether Mina wanted any and set out two cups. The tea was strong enough to make politeness unnecessary.

Mina placed photographs of the indigo gown on the table. Sabine touched the air above them, not the paper.

"That notch," she said. "See? Double bite at the left waist. Ida cut it because the client had one shoulder higher. Madame Arel liked the correction. She called it instinct. Ida called it arithmetic."

"A correction became a signature."

"Many signatures begin as labor someone else did."

"Can you prove it?"

Sabine laughed. "If proof mattered in couture, half the saints would be tailors."

She went to a flat drawer and brought out yellowed muslin scraps wrapped in tissue, sketches on onion-skin paper, and a photograph of Ida beside a dress form. Ida in the photograph was compact, dark-haired, unsmiling, sleeves rolled to the elbow. On the form hung an unfinished bodice with the same crescent line that had made Maison Arel's revival possible.

"Why come forward now?" Mina asked.

"I came forward for thirty years. Letters, interviews, one lawyer who died before his invoice did. They ignored me when the house was strong, when it was weak, when it closed, and when Celia announced this revival. Now someone used cloth to harm a woman. That gives old theft a new odor."

Mina photographed each item. The muslin smelled faintly of paper dust and lavender. In one sketch margin, blue thread had been taped to the paper, brittle at the ends.

"Indigo?"

"Ida kept thread with patterns. To remember which cloth taught the cut. She said a line behaved differently in wool, silk, cotton, and pride."

"Did she ever work directly for Arel?"

"Contracted. Paid late, when paid. She could draft what designers imagined and what they only pretended to imagine. That made her valuable and unsafe."

"Unsafe how?"

"If a cutter can say no, a designer has to know something."

Sabine sat and pushed a folder across the table. "Here. Copies of letters. Jules Fane interviewed Ida in 1998. It never ran. He was young enough then to think truth waited politely for publication."

"Do you know where the original pattern book is?"

The question changed the room. Sabine's hand tightened around the teacup. "Arel took it after Ida died. Brigitte came herself. Said family archives must preserve house history. I was foolish enough to think preservation included the person who made the thing."

"Was there a coffee stain on the book?"

Sabine stared. "Upper right corner. Ida spilled it during a wage argument and refused to clean it. She said the stain was more honest than the receipt."

Mina wrote that down.

"If the book is in Arel's archive, it has not been cataloged honestly."

"Nothing stolen is cataloged honestly," Sabine said. "That is how it stays stolen."

When Mina stood to leave, Sabine followed her to the door.

"Do not let them turn Ida into inspiration," she said. "Inspiration is what rich people call unpaid work after the worker dies."

"What should they call her?"

"The cutter," Sabine said. "Start there."

On the stairs down, Mina heard the dry cleaner's machines thumping below like a slow industrial heart. She stopped under the landing window and looked again at the photograph of Ida beside the dress form. The unfinished bodice had no glamour, only decisions: where cloth yielded, where it resisted, where a working eye had corrected asymmetry without humiliating the body. That was the kind of intelligence fashion liked to display after someone else had hidden the labor. Sabine opened her door again before Mina reached the street. "One more thing," she called. "Ida marked everything twice when she distrusted a client." "Where would the second marks be?" "Inside seams. Backs of patterns. Places designers never looked because they mistook fronts for truth." Mina thanked her. The rain had eased, but the pavement still held the color of old ink.

Chapter 9: Forged Label

The indigo gown's storage box carried a cream label in blue ink: AREL 1963, CRESCENT EVENING, RESTORED 2018. At first glance it looked old enough to command obedience. Its corners were softened. The ink had feathered. A faint brown smudge crossed the lower edge as if years had handled it.

Mina did not obey labels.

She set the box on the archive table with Talia watching and Brigitte standing nearby, silent in the way of someone building objections for later. Oren had insisted on attending, not because he understood labels but because he understood witnesses. He stood by the door and said nothing.

Under magnification, the paper fibers showed modern optical brighteners. The adhesive fluoresced under ultraviolet light. The ink had been scuffed to imitate handling, but the corners beneath the scuff were sharp.

"Recent," Mina said.

Talia folded her gloved hands. "Labels are replaced when damaged."

"With forged aging?"

"I do not know who made this."

Brigitte said, "You use the word forged with theatrical confidence."

"I use it because artificial aging was applied to make a recent label look old. If you prefer staged, I can write staged."

"Do not be insolent in my archive."

"Then do not give me insolent paper."

Oren looked hard at a shelf.

Mina opened the physical ledger again. The gown's box number had been changed from I-14 to I-41. In the digital system, the change occurred six weeks earlier, just after Luc Renard's investment announcement. The entry was clean, too clean. It lacked the small notes archivists usually made when moving objects: shelf condition, tissue replaced, escort initials, reason beyond consolidation.

"Why renumber it?" Mina asked.

"Consolidation," Talia said.

"That is a storage word for a story problem."

The assistant's composure tightened. "Brigitte wanted the indigo pieces grouped for investors. The old numbering was not elegant."

"Evidence rarely is."

Brigitte moved forward. "Investors require coherence. Coherence is not a crime."

"No. But rewriting without a trace often accompanies one."

Mina lifted the box lid. Inside, beneath the top tissue, the corner of an older label remained half-removed. Only a strip survived, the paper thinner and warmer than the new label. It held a typed mark: S.H. loan study. The rest had been torn away.

Sabine Holt.

Talia inhaled softly.

Mina photographed the remnant before touching it. "This gown left the archive."

Brigitte's eyes went to Talia, not to the label. That was useful.

"Loan studies are common," Brigitte said. "Scholars, curators, old employees. They handle what they do not own and later imagine ownership through proximity."

"When did Sabine study it?"

"I do not recall."

"Convenient."

"Ordinary. I am old enough to have forgotten more than you have accused."

Mina looked at Talia. "Where is the loan record?"

Talia's lips parted, closed, then opened again. "It is not in the current digital file."

"Was it in an older system?"

Brigitte said, "Talia cannot answer what she has not checked."

Talia looked at the door before answering anyway. "Many things were removed from older records during archive consolidation."

There it was: not confession, but a crack.

Mina bagged a tiny fiber from the older label edge. "I will need all pre-consolidation exports."

"They may not exist," Brigitte said.

"Then I will need to know who destroyed them."

Oren pushed away from the door. "I can pull backups from production servers if archive systems touched scheduling."

Brigitte turned on him. "This is not production."

"Tonight made it production."

The archive seemed to hold its breath. Mina placed the box lid back on its support. A label had tried to tell one story. The torn corner had answered with another.

Later, Talia brought the older box photographs from a backup drive. She did it without Brigitte present and without meeting Mina's eyes. In the image, the original label was intact: S.H. loan study, crescent comparison, return pending. The date was twelve years earlier. A handwritten note at the bottom read unresolved attribution. Mina asked who wrote the note. Talia knew without checking. "Brigitte." The assistant's shame changed texture then; it was no longer only fear of being caught, but anger at having helped conceal something already named by the person ordering concealment. Mina printed the photograph and pinned it to the board. The old label did not solve Anouk's poisoning. It gave motive a root system, and root systems mattered when people insisted the rot was sudden.

Chapter 10: Rewritten Log

Talia agreed to speak in the pattern room, away from Brigitte's office and the archive cameras. She chose the place herself, which told Mina she knew exactly where Brigitte listened and where she only believed she listened. Rolls of muslin leaned against the wall. Paper patterns hung from hooks, each one a ghost of a body. The room smelled of chalk, cotton, and machine oil drifting from the atelier beyond.

Oren waited outside after Mina asked him to. Talia watched the door close and still did not sit.

"The loan log was rewritten," she said.

"By you?"

"Entered by me. Ordered by Brigitte."

"For what purpose?"

"Investors do not like uncertainty. Luc's team wanted clean provenance. Celia wanted to show continuity from founder to revival. Brigitte wanted the house protected from Sabine's accusations."

"So the gown's study loan disappeared."

"And several pattern books were relabeled as family drafts."

Mina watched Talia's hands. They trembled only when she said family.

"Did you poison Anouk?"

Talia looked up, startled into honesty. "No."

"Did you handle the gown after conservation?"

"Only to move it, inventory it, and prepare it for fitting. I wore blue nitrile gloves. Archive standard. Not pale."

"Who wore pale gloves?"

Talia hesitated.

"This is not the moment to protect etiquette," Mina said.

"Brigitte, sometimes. Cotton gloves, cream or white. She says blue makes the archive look like a hospital. Elise Varo wore pale cosmetic gloves during filming. She said blue gloves looked ugly on camera."

Mina wrote the name. "Did Elise have archive access?"

"Not alone. Officially. But Luc's people were granted escorted access for due diligence and promotional recording. They asked for clean shots of labels, boxes, the indigo drawer."

"Who escorted her?"

"Me, once. Brigitte, once. Oren's staff during preview."

"Did she ask unusual questions?"

"She asked which garments would be worn and which would be copied. She asked whether the archive gown was fragile. She asked if Anouk had complained."

"Had Anouk?"

"About the neckline. It scratched her in fitting. Celia said we could lower it. Brigitte objected. Elise was in the room."

Mina wrote that down with a line beneath it.

"Why tell me?"

Talia looked at the hanging patterns. "Because logs can be corrected. People who collapse cannot be revised."

That was a sentence from someone who had revised too much.

"Where are the original exports?"

Talia pressed her palms together. "I kept duplicates."

"Where?"

"Personal drive. Encrypted. I told myself it was professional caution."

"And actually?"

"Cowardice with a backup."

Mina did not correct her. People named their own compromises more accurately than investigators did.

"I need them."

"If Brigitte knows, I lose my position."

"If Luc's side knows, they may make you the position everything falls on."

Talia sat then, as if her knees had received the argument before her pride did. "They can, can't they?"

"You altered logs. That is true. If the rest is hidden, the true part becomes useful to liars."

She took a long breath. Outside, someone laughed too loudly and stopped.

"I will give you the exports," Talia said. "But I want counsel present for any formal statement."

"Good. Use counsel. Do not use silence."

When Mina opened the door, Oren was leaning against the opposite wall with two coffees. He handed one to Talia without comment.

"I do not drink coffee," she said.

"Today you do," he answered, and for once she accepted production's authority.

Mina watched Talia hold the paper cup with both hands. The archive assistant had the careful posture of someone trained never to lean on tables, never to smudge a page, never to leave breath on glass. That discipline had made her useful to Brigitte and now made her useful to the truth. "When you bring the exports," Mina said, "bring the folder paths, not only the files. Where a lie is stored tells us who expected to retrieve it." Talia looked up. "You think like an archivist." "No. Archivists think like evidence when no one frightens them out of it." Oren glanced between them and wisely said nothing. From the pattern room hooks, paper bodies shifted faintly in the ventilation, each one waiting to be cut into something someone could wear or steal.

Chapter 11: Particle Map

Mina pinned a floor plan to the atelier corkboard and marked each blue particle lift with thread. Latch. Steamer. Neckline. Runway carpet. Glove cabinet. Archive box. The pattern was not a cloud. It was a route.

The atelier had become her temporary incident room because it was the only space large enough and because every person who passed the board had to see that the case was not gossip. Brown paper covered a cutting table. Evidence photographs lined one edge. Time stamps marched down another. Oren supplied colored pins, then complained about Mina's color logic until she gave him the red ones and let him mark production events.

"That looks like someone carried it," he said, arms folded.

"Or transferred it through objects."

"Objects do not sneak."

"No, but people blame them."

The highest concentration sat at the steamer station and the gown neckline. The latch showed secondary contact. The runway carpet showed fallout after Anouk moved. Archive dust from the box was older, duller, mixed with cedar and tissue fibers. The toxic particles were brighter, carrying a modern polymer.

Elias confirmed by phone while Mina put him on speaker. His voice sounded thin over the atelier noise.

"Same carrier as the finishing spray. Not raw pigment. The latch sample is weaker but chemically consistent with transfer from a contaminated glove or hand. The archive box dust is unrelated old indigo and storage debris."

"Can the spray be invisible?" Mina asked.

"On dark fabric, nearly. On a pale glove, not if you know what you are looking for."

Oren looked toward the glove cabinet. "We have blue nitrile, black heat gloves, cotton archive gloves, and those ridiculous cream gloves Luc's people used."

"Collect them all."

"Already locked."

"Inventory them with Talia and someone from counsel."

"You enjoy giving me three-person tasks."

"Evidence enjoys witnesses."

He went.

Mina marked the steamer station in red. "Who prepares steamers?"

Oren returned with a binder and a worse mood. "Dressers set them. Production checks water. Supplier sent a replacement unit yesterday because one head cracked."

"Where is the cracked head?"

He checked the equipment shelf. Labels had been taped beneath each hook: dressing rooms one through six, runway backup, alterations, archive-safe. Dressing room three's hook held a clean replacement head, its white plastic almost new. The cracked head should have been in the repair bin. The bin contained two cords, a lint brush, and somebody's abandoned lunch receipt.

"It should be here," Oren said.

It was not.

Mina added a blank red circle beside the route. Missing objects had shape. Sometimes more than present ones.

Talia arrived with the glove inventory. Blue nitrile boxes were full. Cotton archive gloves were counted and initialed. Cream cosmetic gloves were listed as "presentation accessories" and stored in Luc's filming kit. One pair was missing.

"Who signed them out?" Mina asked.

Talia read the sheet. "Elise Varo. For archive presentation. Yesterday morning."

"Returned?"

"No return mark."

Oren swore softly.

Mina pinned a photograph of the glove kit beneath Elise's name. The board began to change under their eyes. What had seemed like a scattering of blue became a sequence of decisions: product delivered, steamer replaced, glove missing, neckline adjusted, woman collapsed.

Celia entered quietly and stood behind them. The atelier workers pretended not to watch her watching the board.

"Does this tell you who?" she asked.

"It tells me how someone could."

"Is that comfort?"

"No."

"Good," Celia said. "I am tired of counterfeit comfort."

Mina handed her a red pin. "Then help me mark who knew Anouk might refuse."

Celia took the pin and, after a moment, placed it beside her own name.

The act changed the room more than Mina expected. A junior cutter at the nearest machine looked over, saw Celia mark herself, and stopped pretending not to listen. Accountability from the top was not absolution, but it gave permission for facts to travel upward. Oren added a small note beside the red circle for the missing steamer head: find before story does. "Stories move faster," he said. "Then evidence needs better shoes." Mina stepped back from the board. Threads crossed and recrossed until the route resembled a pattern draft with no garment yet cut from it. That was fitting. They were still in muslin, testing line, finding tension. The final shape would come later, after enough wrong shapes had been marked and rejected.

Chapter 12: Missing Head

The steamer head was found in a trash bag behind the loading dock, wrapped in damp muslin and two coffee cups. Oren found it because he had an opinion about how trash was sorted during shows and yelled until someone checked. By then the rain had started again, tapping the metal awning and turning cardboard soft at the edges.

"Do not touch the bag," Mina said when he called.

"I am standing over it like an unpaid statue."

"Keep doing that."

She reached the loading dock with a camera, gloves, and a security guard who looked offended that trash had become important. The bag sat half-hidden behind a bin of discarded foam-core signage. Its knot was loose. A dark damp patch showed through the plastic.

Mina photographed the bag in place, then the surrounding floor, the bin, the door, the camera above the dock, and Oren's shoes because he had stood too close.

"My shoes are innocent," he said.

"Most shoes are until questioned."

In the sample room, she opened the bag under controlled light. The muslin wrapper was wet but not soaked. Blue residue ringed the nozzle of the steamer head. The handle smelled faintly bitter even through her mask.

"That is not archive dust," Oren said.

"No."

The replacement steamer on the dressing-room rack had a clean head and no residue. Its serial number did not match the equipment log. Someone had switched the contaminated head out after Anouk collapsed and before Oren sealed the room, or earlier and then removed the old one during the confusion. Both possibilities required access and nerve.

"Camera?" Mina asked.

Oren made a face. "Backstage corridor cameras were updating."

"During preview?"

"Wardrobe system update. Scheduled vendor maintenance. I know."

"Who approved it?"

"I am finding out, and I am pre-angry."

Inside the wrapped muslin, Mina found a tiny snag of pale glove fiber. Nylon, high-count, cosmetic handling glove. Not the blue nitrile used by archive staff, not cotton. It had caught on a small burr where the steamer head casing had cracked.

"Pale gloves," she said.

Oren took one slow breath. "Elise Varo wears those. Luc's assistant."

"Does she have access to dressing rooms?"

"If she says investor, doors open."

Mina sealed the fiber and swabbed the nozzle. The blue residue lifted in a narrow ring, densest where steam would exit. Elias would enjoy the clarity and resent the reason for it.

"Who carried trash from dressing room three after the collapse?"

"No scheduled removal. We halted cleaning. But the loading dock was chaos. Caterers, press crates, florals, rental chairs, Luc's camera cases."

"Did Elise leave through here?"

"I saw her near the service corridor after the ambulance. She was on the phone. I remember because she had no gloves then, and I thought, stupidly, finally someone stopped dressing for documentary footage."

"Who else knew the head cracked?"

Oren checked his tablet. "Maren reported steam spitting at rehearsal. I filed replacement request. Supplier confirmed. The replacement came through Luc's logistics because their crew was already bringing equipment."

"That is unusual."

"Everything is unusual when a house reopens with borrowed money."

Mina placed the steamer head in an evidence bag. It was heavier than it looked. Not by weight; by connection. She did not yet have the poisoner. She had means, movement, and a glove with an excellent sense of entitlement.

Outside the sample room, a staff member whispered, "Was it the dress?"

Oren answered before Mina could. "It was a person. Go back to work or go home, but do not start worshipping fabric."

The staff member fled. Mina looked at him.

"Too much?"

"Only if you wanted subtlety."

"I manage fittings," he said. "Subtlety has failed me."

When Elias received the steamer head, he asked whether Maison Arel understood that cleaning tools were not magical absolution devices. Mina told him no and promised to put it in smaller words if necessary. The cracked casing yielded more than residue: a crescent-shaped smear where a gloved finger had pressed while twisting the head into place or out of place. That did not identify the hand, but it made Maren's account testable. Her prints would be expected on the threaded collar. A stranger's glove fiber on the crack would not. Mina wrote expected contact beside Maren's name and unexplained fiber beside pale glove. The distinction was thin enough to be missed by anyone hungry for a convenient suspect. It was also the distinction on which an innocent dresser might stand.

Chapter 13: Finishing Spray

Elias placed the steamer head under a fume hood and behaved as though it had insulted him personally. He disliked contaminated tools more than contaminated samples. Tools, he said, were accomplices with handles.

"Modern finishing spray," he announced after the first run. "Water-activated release. Not sold for garments worn close to skin. Industrial textile finishing. Mild when dry, ugly when heated."

Mina stood beside the bench with Oren's invoice binder open. "Brand?"

"BlueArc Finish 7, or a copy using the same carrier."

She recognized the name from the invoices Talia had produced and from the supply list taped to the evidence board. BlueArc supplied display coatings for archive garments when they were mounted behind glass or prepared for static exhibitions. Not for runway. Not for skin. Not for breath.

"Dose?"

"Enough on the neckline to cause collapse in a susceptible adult. Enough in the steamer to make exposure predictable. The steamer head residue indicates repeated heating, not a single accidental mist."

"Could accidental overspray do this?"

Elias pushed his glasses up. "If someone accidentally filled or coated a steamer component with a compound labeled do not aerosolize, accidentally directed it at the throat of a garment wearer, and accidentally adjusted the treated neckline against broken skin, yes. In the same sense that one can accidentally build a small bridge."

"So no."

"So not credibly."

He handed her the preliminary report. Anouk's blood markers supported inhalation and skin contact. The compound degraded quickly, which explained why early hospital tests had been nonspecific. The cloth and steamer carried the story longer than the body did.

"She should recover," Elias said, "but that is not permission to call it minor."

Mina had not planned to.

On the lab bench, the blue residue looked almost beautiful when diluted: a clear wash, a small sky. Fashion, chemistry, and violence all knew how to use beauty as a disguise.

She called Oren. "Find every BlueArc invoice, delivery receipt, safety sheet, and internal note."

"I was hoping for lunch."

"Eat after the poison."

"That is not a slogan."

"Good."

"Do we know who ordered it?"

"Not yet. We know who had access to it."

"Luc's supply chain?"

"Possibly. Talia's archive notes say display use only."

Oren's voice changed. "Talia warned about it?"

"Archive records did. We need to know who read them."

"I can pull access logs."

"Do it. And get Celia to freeze Luc's filming kit."

"That will be popular."

"Popularity is not evidence."

"In fashion it is usually treated as both."

When Mina returned to Maison Arel, BlueArc had become a word people lowered their voices around. Celia met her in the corridor outside the sample room, sleeves rolled, no jacket now.

"Was it deliberate?" Celia asked.

"The delivery was deliberate enough that accident is no longer a responsible assumption."

Celia closed her eyes. The corridor light made shadows beneath them. "Anouk could have died."

"Yes."

"Because of a neckline."

"Because someone wanted a story."

Celia looked toward the atelier. "We make stories from necklines here."

"Then you know how dangerous a story can become when money needs it."

Inside, Oren had already stacked invoices by vendor. He had added a handwritten label to one pile: THINGS THAT SHOULD NOT TOUCH HUMANS. Mina did not correct the phrasing. It was legally imprecise and morally efficient.

The BlueArc records showed three deliveries in two weeks. Two to archive storage. One to "display prep / investor preview," signed by Elise Varo.

Mina underlined the signature once. Ink, like thread, could become a trail when it stopped pretending to be administrative.

The safety sheet printed badly at first, a pale gray page from a low toner cartridge. Oren cursed the printer as if it had joined Luc's fund. Mina made him print it again. Warnings mattered visually; a faint warning became easier to call overlooked. The second copy came out black and blunt. Not for wearable textiles. Avoid heated vapor. Use respiratory protection in enclosed areas. Celia read each line aloud, slower with every sentence. "No one can say they did not understand this." "They can say it," Mina replied. "We make it expensive to be believed." Talia found an archive note from herself attached to the product file: display mounts only, never runway. She stared at her own caution as if it belonged to a braver woman.

Chapter 14: Silhouette

Celia brought Mina to the design room after midnight. The rest of the building had dimmed into emergency light and machine shadows, but the design room glowed with white lamps. The revival collection hung on racks: twenty garments built around the crescent neckline, diagonal bodice, and indigo-to-black palette. Without the archive gown, the collection still spoke its language. Without the silhouette, it had no grammar.

"Luc invested because this shape sells memory," Celia said.

"Whose memory?"

"That is becoming the expensive question."

She pulled one jacket from the rack. It was beautifully cut, modern but obedient to the old line. The crescent had been translated into a severe collar. The diagonal tension moved through the waist and into a narrow hip. Mina could see why investors loved it. It looked like heritage without asking viewers to learn anything.

"I grew up believing my grandmother invented it," Celia said. "Brigitte told the story like scripture. My mother left the business before I was old enough to ask why. Brigitte stayed. The archive became a childhood room I was allowed into only if I used both hands."

"And Sabine?"

"Letters. Registered mail. Sometimes flowers sent back with notes tucked inside. Brigitte said old employees became romantic about their importance. I accepted that because it was easier than opening the drawer."

"Then you built a collection on the drawer."

Celia flinched, but she did not defend herself. Mina respected that.

"Yes."

Mina ran a gloved finger above, not on, the jacket seam. The line was clever: a correction turned into elegance.

"If Sabine is right, Sunday becomes theft in front of cameras," Celia said.

"And if someone poisoned Anouk to protect the show?"

Celia looked toward the dark atelier. "Then the theft has learned new habits."

She gave Mina access to the design archive, including investor presentations. The slides were polished, full of market language and photographs of the indigo gown. One page called the crescent silhouette "defensible heritage IP." Another projected licensing revenue from "Arel Blue" fragrance, accessories, and bridal.

"Cloth is not a patent," Mina said.

"Investors disagree."

"Investors disagree with weather."

Celia almost laughed, then did not. "Luc's lawyers asked for proof that the silhouette belonged to the house. Brigitte said the archive held proof. When Sabine's letters resurfaced, Luc did not walk away. He renegotiated."

"For more control?"

"For collateral access to disputed archive assets if the revival failed."

"Including pattern books?"

Celia turned from the rack. "How did you know?"

"Because rich men rarely collect old paper for romance."

She opened a drawer and removed a printed contract summary. "I did not understand this clause fully until now. If a heritage claim materially disrupts the revival, Luc's fund can step in to stabilize and license archival material during remediation."

"If the archive gown appears unsafe, the house needs remediation."

"Yes."

"If Sabine proves the line was stolen, the heritage claim disrupts revival."

"Yes."

"If both happen, Luc holds the solution to a problem he helped finance."

Celia pressed the contract to the table. "Find who made this dangerous. I will deal with who made it false."

"Those may be the same room, not the same hand."

"I know." Her voice was quiet. "That is what frightens me."

Outside the windows, rain streaked the glass. On the rack, the indigo garments hung in exact order, each one a variation on a cut born from an uneven shoulder and made famous by erasure. Mina photographed them not as fashion, but as motive made wearable.

Celia lifted the jacket again and turned it inside out. The interior was more revealing than the exterior: basting lines, balance marks, clipped seam allowances, the hidden arithmetic of grace. "This is the part clients never see," she said. "Maybe that was always the problem." Mina looked at the inner chalk marks. "People protect what they see valued." "Then we valued fronts." Celia rehung the jacket with more care than before. "If we survive, the workroom stays visible." "Glass can become theater too." "Then not just glass. Names. Records. Contracts. Boring things." Mina nodded. "Boring things hold better than remorse." Celia wrote the phrase on a scrap of pattern paper, then crossed it out, perhaps because she did not want to turn even that into branding.

Chapter 15: Interview Tapes

Jules Fane had covered fashion long enough to distrust adjectives and lighting. He met Mina in a cafe that was too bright for secrets and handed her a box of old digital tapes. He wore a brown coat with a missing button and had the particular sadness of a journalist who had been right before anyone paid for rightness.

"Ida Sayegh interview, 1998," he said. "Never ran. My editor said no one wanted to read about pattern cutters when Brigitte was buying ads."

"Why keep it?"

"Vanity. I was right and wanted proof for later."

"Later has arrived."

"Later is rude. It takes decades and then asks for receipts."

They chose a table near an outlet. Jules carried a battered recorder in a canvas bag along with adapters that looked scavenged from several extinct offices. The first tape opened with hiss, a chair scrape, and a younger Jules clearing his throat too often.

Ida's voice was low, amused, and tired. She spoke slowly, not because she lacked words but because she had spent a career making them pass through rooms that preferred her hands.

"The crescent was my correction," Ida said on tape. "Madame Arel called it discovery. I called it Tuesday."

Mina paused playback. "Did she name Brigitte?"

"Wait."

On the next tape, Ida described a young Brigitte removing drafts from the atelier after the founder's death. "She said family papers must be with family. I said patterns belong to hands."

Jules watched Mina across the small table. "Enough?"

"For story. Maybe not court."

"Story moves court sometimes."

"Court resents being moved without documentation."

"Then you will like the photograph."

He gave her a print from the same interview. Ida sat at a worktable, hand resting on a pattern book with a coffee stain on the upper right corner and blue thread looped through the spine. Behind her, a muslin bodice hung on a form. The crescent line was unmistakable.

"That book is missing," Mina said.

"Then perhaps the missing book will enjoy being famous."

Mina doubted missing evidence enjoyed anything. It waited.

"Did Ida say where it went?"

"Brigitte took it after Madame Arel died. Ida said she was too tired to fight a family that had turned her work into a crest. Sabine tried later. Doors closed."

"Why did your piece die?"

Jules looked toward the cafe window. "Advertising, access, fear of being sued, an editor who liked Brigitte's parties. Pick the thread. They all knot."

He played another segment. Ida laughed at one of his questions.

"People think design is a lightning strike," she said. "Usually it is a woman with a pencil fixing what another person did not understand."

Mina wrote that down. It was not evidence in the strict sense, but it explained the moral weather.

"Do you have all the tapes?" she asked.

"I thought so. The archive is messy. Mine, I mean. Not theirs. Though theirs appears messy in the profitable way."

"Look again."

"For?"

"Any mention of Brigitte, the book, Sabine, payment, or transfer. Also any visual material from the interview."

Jules nodded. "You think old theft and new poison are connected."

"I think people with motive prefer old theft to stay old."

"And if the story runs?"

"Make it careful. Do not make Talia the villain because she has the smallest office. Do not make Anouk a symbol unless she chooses it. Do not call Ida a muse."

Jules smiled without warmth. "I know that one. Muse is a word men use when they cannot admit payroll."

Mina gathered the tapes. "Make copies."

"Already did."

"Good."

"I am vain, not careless," Jules said, and for the first time she believed both traits might help.

When Jules walked her to the door, he stopped beside a framed magazine cover from years before. Brigitte Arel stood in the photograph beside the indigo gown, one hand hovering above the crescent neckline, the caption praising a family eye for line. "I wrote that caption," Jules said. "Or something close enough to be guilty." Mina studied the young Brigitte in the image. She looked severe, proud, already guarding a story she had not made. "Then write better now." Jules nodded. "Better is slower." "Good." Outside, traffic hissed over wet pavement. Mina carried the tapes under her coat as if they were fragile cloth. Voices could fray too, especially when kept too long in boxes built by regret.

Chapter 16: Notches

Sabine's muslin pieces came to Mina's temporary lab in a flat archival box. Celia authorized the comparison over Brigitte's objection, which arrived in writing and used the phrase reputational vandalism. Oren printed the objection and taped it to the side of the evidence board until Mina told him to remove it because mockery was not chain of custody.

The sample room had become crowded with old cloth and new tension. Talia brought Arel's archived pattern copies. Sabine brought her own box and refused to let anyone carry it for her. Celia arrived last, face pale, hair pinned badly.

Mina laid Sabine's muslin beside Arel's archived pattern copies. The paper sizes differed. The dates differed. The notches did not.

At the left waist, a double bite. At the shoulder, a shallow corrective clip. At the inner bodice, a handwritten balance mark later converted into decorative seam alignment. These were not broad inspirations. They were fingerprints of cutting.

"Explain for the record," Celia said.

Mina pointed with a clean probe. "A pattern is not just outline. It holds decisions. Notches tell workers how pieces meet. Balance marks solve body irregularities. This double notch appears in Sabine's muslin and in Arel's later archive copy, even where the fit problem no longer exists. That means the correction was copied as style."

Sabine stood silently for a long time. When she spoke, her voice was less sharp than before.

"Ida did not care about fame. She cared about being paid."

"Was she?" Mina asked.

Sabine smiled without warmth. "In compliments. The cheapest currency."

Talia watched the matched pieces as if they might accuse her personally.

"You knew," Mina said.

"I suspected. Then I found relabeled books."

"Where?"

Talia's eyes flicked toward the archive wall beyond the room. "Hidden shelf. Behind export cartons."

Brigitte was not present, but her absence felt like a locked drawer.

Celia stepped closer to the table. "Why did you not tell me?"

Talia looked stricken. "Because Brigitte told me the claims were old disputes settled informally. Because Luc's due diligence made everything urgent. Because I had a contract and a supervisor and a talent for cowardice."

"Do not be cruel to yourself because it is easier than being precise," Mina said.

Talia blinked.

"You altered records under pressure. That is bad. It is also not the whole case."

Celia touched the table edge, not the muslin. "Can we credit Ida now?"

"Credit will not undo exposure," Mina said.

"No. But it may stop us adding new lies to old ones."

Sabine looked at Celia for the first time without contempt. Not kindness. Something more cautious.

"Credit is not a ribbon," Sabine said. "It comes with accounts."

"Then we will open accounts," Celia replied.

Mina photographed the matches from multiple angles, then used transparent overlays to mark the notches. The evidence was quiet, almost modest. No blue poison, no dramatic collapse, no rich man's message. Just marks made by a working hand and copied by a house that had wanted genius without debt.

Oren came in halfway through and stopped at the door. "Brigitte is asking whether the comparison is complete."

"Tell her the comparison is still speaking," Mina said.

He considered. "I will translate that into production."

"What does production say?"

"No."

For once, everyone in the room smiled except Sabine. She was looking at the double notch. Her hand hovered above it, not touching, as she had done with the photographs. Grief, Mina thought, had its own conservation rules.

Sabine asked for five minutes alone with the muslin and received three, with Mina standing at the door. She did not speak to Ida as she had in the apartment. She simply aligned the double notch with the Arel copy again and again, as if making the room see repetition without calling it coincidence. When Celia returned, Sabine had composed herself. "Do not make this a romance of hidden genius," she said. "Ida was tired, practical, occasionally vain, and very good." Celia answered, "Then that is what we will say." Mina watched the two women measure one another. A settlement might come later, with lawyers and numbers. This was not settlement. This was the first fitting of truth against people who had different scars from the same garment.

Chapter 17: Another Model

Brigitte announced the show would continue through a statement written in the old house voice: regret, resilience, artistry, continuity. Anouk was wished a full recovery. Another model would wear a newly made version of the indigo gown. The archive original would be "rested."

Mina read the statement in Oren's office while rain beat against the high windows.

"Rested," Oren said. "As if the dress had a headache."

"Who approved this?"

"Brigitte released it to the board and press list. Celia is currently inventing new forms of anger."

"Where is the copy?"

"Fitting room five."

They found the copy on a dress form, unfinished, the crescent neckline pinned high. It was beautiful in the way an echo could be beautiful, cleaner than the original, safer in theory, cut from new indigo wool with a silk facing. No residue on the cloth. No poison in the steamer. But the garment bag bore a blue smudge on the zipper tab.

Mina photographed before touching. "They moved the route."

"Or someone with dirty gloves touched everything."

"That is also a route."

The smudge was small, almost invisible unless the zipper turned under light. It sat where a thumb and forefinger would close the bag. Mina lifted it and sealed the tape. Oren stood too still.

"If the copy is clean, why touch the bag?"

"To test access. To frame continuity. To prepare another delivery. To panic. We do not choose early."

"I am beginning to hate that sentence."

"It protects us from liking neat answers."

Celia arrived with the statement in her hand, furious enough to look calm. "Brigitte released this without me."

"Can you stop the show?" Mina asked.

"Contractually? Maybe. Politically? Not without proof that danger remains."

"Then this smudge helps."

"That is a bleak sentence."

"Evidence often has poor bedside manner."

They opened the fitting room log. Maren had prepared the copy. Talia had checked archive reference. Elise Varo had entered "for camera placement." Brigitte had viewed the silhouette. No one had signed the garment bag out after that.

"Camera placement in a fitting room?" Celia asked.

Oren tapped the log. "Luc wanted comparison shots. Old and new. Heritage and revival. The usual religion."

Celia gripped the paper. "No more filming."

"You can order that," Oren said. "Luc will object."

"Let him."

Mina examined the pinned neckline. It had been raised almost exactly where Anouk had complained. The gesture bothered her. If the archive original had been blamed, the copy could walk with the line preserved, the show saved, and Anouk's body converted into an inconvenience already solved.

Outside, machines resumed their nervous work. The atelier wanted to believe motion meant survival. Mina had seen that in factories after accidents: stitch faster, polish harder, move the eye away from the stain.

The blue smudge did not move.

"Who is the replacement model?" Mina asked.

"No one yet," Celia said. "Brigitte implied there were options."

"No one wears this until toxicology clears every object and I know where that smudge came from."

Celia nodded immediately. Oren looked relieved. A voice from the doorway said, "That will breach the investor calendar."

Luc Renard stood there, dry despite the rain, suit immaculate, expression mild. Elise Varo stood behind him, hands folded, no gloves visible.

"Then the investor calendar can consult a doctor," Mina said.

Luc smiled as if she had made a charming provincial error. "You must be Ms. Tor."

"And you must be why evidence keeps moving."

The smile held, but only because men like Luc trained smiles for stress.

Elise looked at the garment bag when Mina said evidence. It was a small glance, downward and right, but fear had direction. Mina asked her to remain while the smudge was lifted. Elise said she had calls. Celia said the calls could wait. Luc began to object, then chose elegance and silence. The zipper tab yielded a brighter blue than old fabric dust, and the tape caught a pale filament too short for visual identification. Mina sealed it while Elise watched her own face reflected in the fitting mirror. "Beautiful copy," Luc said, to no one in particular. Anouk's empty place in the room answered more loudly than any person. Beauty was beginning to fail as camouflage.

Chapter 18: Evidence, Not Publicity

Anouk left the hospital against advice and arrived at Maison Arel wearing sunglasses, a scarf, and the expression of a woman prepared to disappoint publicists. Oren tried to stop her at the service door. She kissed his cheek, told him he looked terrible, and kept walking.

"Where is the gown?" she asked Mina.

"Secured."

"Good. Keep it that way."

Her voice was still rough. She moved carefully, not weakly. There was a difference. Staff turned as she passed, then looked away because staring at a woman who had nearly collapsed in front of cameras required a degree of shamelessness most of them did not possess.

In Celia's office, Anouk refused a prepared wellness statement. The paper lay on the desk between them, full of words like grateful, recovery, heritage, sensitivity, and private medical matter.

"I will not say I had a reaction to vintage fabric," Anouk said. "I had a reaction to someone putting something on me."

Brigitte stood by the window. "We do not yet know that."

Anouk removed her scarf. The scratch at her neckline had darkened to a thin, angry line. "I know what I felt."

Mina asked whether Anouk recognized Elise Varo.

"Luc's assistant. Pale gloves. She adjusted the gown. She smelled like expensive soap and panic."

Elise, summoned by Celia, stood near the door with Luc behind her. She wore black trousers, a cream blouse, and bare hands. Her nails were short and polished clear.

"Investor staff do not dress models," Brigitte said.

Luc smiled. "They help where needed. We were all trying to save a difficult evening."

"Someone nearly ended one," Anouk said.

Mina watched Luc's face. Concern came quickly, indignation slower. Calculated emotions sometimes arrived in the wrong order.

"Ms. Saye," he said, "we are relieved you are recovering. No one here would intentionally endanger you."

"That is the kind of sentence people use when they want the passive voice to carry the body."

Celia looked down, almost smiling despite herself.

Mina placed photographs on the desk: blue latch dust, steamer head residue, glove fiber, neckline irritation. "This is not a publicity matter."

Luc glanced at the images. "It is also not a trial."

"No. Trials usually come after evidence."

Elise's eyes had fixed on the steamer head photograph. Mina noticed, then moved the photo slightly closer to her. Elise looked away too late.

"Did you adjust Anouk's neckline?" Mina asked.

"I may have smoothed it. With permission."

Anouk laughed once, which made her cough. "Not mine."

"Brigitte asked for the crescent to sit correctly," Elise said.

Brigitte's face remained composed. "I did not ask you to touch the model."

"You said it was too low."

"Observation is not instruction."

"In this house, it often becomes one," Celia said.

The room changed around that sentence. Celia had not shouted. She had done something more dangerous. She had refused inherited ambiguity.

Anouk turned to Mina. "Use my name. Not incident. Not model. Anouk Saye."

"I will."

"And the gown?"

"Evidence."

"Good. It has had enough of being myth."

Luc gathered the wellness statement from the desk and folded it once. "No one will ask you to sign today."

"No one will ask me tomorrow either," Anouk said.

After Luc and Elise left, Celia sank into her chair. "He will move against us."

"He already is," Mina said.

Anouk retied her scarf. "Then move faster."

It was not a plea. It was an order from the person most entitled to give one.

Mina stayed after the others left and read the room through the objects still warm from argument: Luc's untouched glass, Elise's careful chair angle, Celia's phone facedown like a small surrendered weapon. Anouk had taken the scarf with her but left a single loose thread on the table. Mina sealed it in a paper fold, not because it would solve anything, but because cases often punished arrogance in the form of ignored trifles. Outside the office, a machine started and stopped. Someone had tried to work and discovered the sound indecent. Good, Mina thought. Let the building hear itself.

Chapter 19: Supplier Fund

BlueArc Finish 7 was distributed through a textile services company owned by North Channel Holdings. North Channel, after three corporate shells, one aggressively bland partnership, and a Luxembourg address that looked like a mailbox pretending to be a building, led to Luc Renard's investment fund.

Mina did not love financial trails, but invoices had fibers of their own. Dates, amounts, routing codes, approval initials. They frayed when pulled.

Oren found BlueArc deliveries billed to "display preparation," signed by Elise Varo. Talia found archive notes warning that BlueArc should not be used on wearable garments. Elias confirmed the compound on the steamer head matched the product batch delivered two weeks before the press preview.

"Passive poison," Oren said. "Elegant."

"No adjectives on the board."

"Fine. Poison with holding companies."

"Better."

They worked at the cutting table while Celia took calls in the next room and Brigitte refused to take any call that did not begin with apology. Oren had bullied accounting into providing purchase orders. Talia printed safety sheets from the archive server. Mina created a timeline that began with Luc's investment announcement, then the archive renumbering, then BlueArc delivery, then camera update, then steamer replacement, then Anouk's collapse.

"Motive?" Oren asked.

Celia, coming in with her phone still in hand, answered before Mina could. "If the show fails because the silhouette is stolen, Luc loses leverage and money. If a model has an unexplained vintage reaction, the archive is blamed, the copy replaces it, and the show continues under his remediation clause."

"That assumes cruelty and planning," Oren said.

"Fashion has departments for both."

Mina looked at her.

Celia rubbed her eyes. "Sorry. That sounded clever. I am tired of clever."

"Good."

In the invoice margin, Elise had written: pale gloves only for camera-facing archive work. Mina underlined it once.

"What does North Channel say?" Celia asked.

"Luc's counsel says the fund holds passive interests and product use was a house matter."

"He will make it our negligence."

"He will try. Negligence still needs a path. We have his assistant signing the deliveries, requesting the replacement, entering the dressing room, and wearing gloves consistent with the fiber."

"And Brigitte entering too," Oren said.

Mina pinned Brigitte's access log under archive pressure, not chemical delivery. "Brigitte benefits if Ida stays erased. Luc benefits if he controls both the lie and the correction. Elise moves between them."

"Where does Talia fit?" Celia asked quietly.

Talia stood at the printer, holding fresh pages. "In the part where someone scared enough becomes useful."

No one contradicted her.

The safety sheet was blunt: display surface treatment only, avoid heated aerosolization, avoid prolonged skin contact, not for wearable textiles. Elise had downloaded it two days before the preview. Luc's office had received a summary through due diligence materials.

"Can we prove Luc read it?" Oren asked.

"Not yet."

"Can we prove Elise did?"

Mina held up the access log. "Yes."

Celia looked from the sheet to the collection racks beyond the glass. "All this to keep a neckline walking."

"And a pattern book hidden," Mina said.

"You think Luc has it."

"I think Luc made sure the disputed asset did not remain where a frightened archivist or guilty aunt might surrender it."

Oren tapped the board. "He has a temporary suite upstairs."

"Do not search it without warrant."

"I was going to say it has terrible wallpaper."

"Good. Keep your crimes decorative."

He gave her a tired salute and went to pull more access logs.

The board now had four columns: cloth, money, archive, pressure. Luc appeared in three. Elise appeared in three. Brigitte appeared in two. That did not solve the case, but it told Mina where the cloth had begun to tear.

Before she left the table, Mina asked Oren to print every delivery signature at full size. Initials had habits. People disguised names badly when tired, and Elise's E leaned forward as if arriving before the rest of her. Talia found the same slant on a request for "camera-ready handling accessories." Celia read the phrase and pressed her thumb into the bridge of her nose. "Accessories," she said. "Gloves, bags, clips, whatever made the lie look professional." Mina taped the page beneath movement. The word was bland. The timing was not.

Chapter 20: Hidden Shelf

Talia led Mina to the hidden shelf after Brigitte left for a board call. The archive was quiet in the way only controlled rooms are quiet, full of machines humming softly to keep air, humidity, and history within acceptable limits. Behind export cartons labeled Asia Retrospective lay a narrow cabinet built into the archive wall. Its door had no visible handle. Talia pressed a seam in the paneling, and the cabinet sighed open.

"This is where uncertainty goes," she said.

Inside were pattern books with replaced spines, loose labels, tissue packets of blue thread, loan slips, and file folders tied in cotton tape. Dust sat on some items, not on others. The difference mattered. People had been here recently.

Mina did not touch anything until she had photographed the cabinet, the floor, the carton positions, Talia's hands, and the hinge marks.

"Who knows this exists?"

"Brigitte. Me. Possibly Celia, though not what is inside. Luc saw photographs during due diligence. He asked for 'sensitive archive materials.'"

"Did you give him access?"

"Brigitte did."

Mina lifted the first book with a cradle beneath it. Its spine read AREL FAMILY DRAFTS 1964. Inside, several pages bore erased shop marks from Ida Sayegh's atelier. Another book contained loan slips signed S. Holt, never returned in the digital log. A packet of blue thread matched the kind Sabine had described.

"Where is the coffee-stained book?"

Talia swallowed. "Gone. It was here before Luc's due diligence."

"Who took it?"

"Brigitte had me prepare it. Luc wanted collateral for the investment. He said if the house myth was challenged, the book protected his position."

"Protected or controlled?"

Talia did not answer.

Mina photographed each book and label. In the back of the cabinet, she found a packet of old indigo thread. The dust inside was dull, natural, harmless by Elias's tests later. It was not the poison. It was the color of the original lie.

"Why not tell Celia?" Mina asked.

Talia's eyes filled. "Because archives teach you to preserve first and speak later. Later becomes a room you never enter."

"Archives do not teach that. People do."

The correction was gentle, but Talia flinched anyway.

Footsteps sounded in the corridor. Talia froze. Mina closed the cabinet halfway but did not hide what they were doing. Oren appeared with a key ring and a face like weather.

"Brigitte's board call ended early," he said. "She is coming."

"Good," Mina said.

"You and I use good differently."

Brigitte entered the archive thirty seconds later. She saw the open cabinet and stopped. For a moment, age left her face. Not youth. Fear.

"That cabinet contains sensitive family materials," she said.

"It contains relabeled work by Ida Sayegh and evidence of removed provenance," Mina said.

"You had no right to open it."

"Talia did. You gave her the key, and Celia gave me authority over evidence connected to Anouk's exposure."

"Old pattern books did not poison a model."

"No. But hiding them may explain why someone wanted the gown blamed."

Brigitte looked at Talia. "You have mistaken access for judgment."

Talia's voice shook but held. "I learned from the archive."

Mina closed the cabinet only after logging each visible item. "We are entering the room now."

No one asked which room she meant. They all knew.

After Brigitte left, the archive did not relax. Talia remained by the hidden shelf, one palm flat against the closed panel, as if feeling for a pulse in the wood. "I used to think secrets had weight," she said. "That if enough of them were in one place, the floor would complain." Mina photographed the cartons again from the corridor. "Floors are patient," she said. "People less so." Oren came back with security tape and a fresh seal. This time Talia signed first. Her name looked small, but it was upright.

Mina asked Talia who had dusted the cabinet last. Talia gave a small, embarrassed laugh. "No one dusts secrets." Under the portable light, the floor showed two recent shoe marks: Brigitte's narrow heel and a broader tread that did not match Oren. Mina photographed both and asked for visitor shoes from Luc's suite. Oren brightened at having a practical errand. "I can ask without committing burglary." "Try that first," Mina said. The archive seemed to grow edges around them as ordinary maintenance became evidence.

Chapter 21: Cloth Board

Mina built the evidence board on an unused cutting table because walls in Maison Arel were either glass or covered with flattering history. She pinned swatches, invoices, photographs, and timelines onto brown paper. Oren stood to her left with a marker and a roll of tape. Talia stood to her right with duplicate metadata exports. Celia watched from the end of the table, no longer pretending she was merely hosting an inquiry.

Cloth: indigo gown, opened seam, pale glove fiber, BlueArc residue, steamer head.

Money: Luc's fund, supplier ownership, show insurance, investment collateral.

Archive: forged label, rewritten loan log, hidden pattern books, Sabine's muslin, Ida's tape.

Pressure: Sunday show, revival debt, founder myth, investor clauses.

Oren capped the marker. "Where do we put Brigitte?"

"Archive and pressure."

"Luc?"

"Money and pressure."

"Elise?"

"Movement."

"Talia?"

"Archive."

He looked at the board. "No one gets to be in only one place."

"That is how cases become useful."

Celia joined them, silent for several minutes. The overhead lamps cast the pins into tiny shadows. "My whole collection sits in the overlap."

"Yes."

"If I cancel, the house may not survive."

"If you continue falsely, what survives?"

Celia traced the line between Ida's muslin and the revival jacket. "A logo."

No one needed to answer. In the atelier beyond, machines stitched on. The sound was steady, almost brave, and Mina knew how easily bravery became denial when payroll was due.

"I have eighty-seven people on payroll," Celia said. "Some came back after years of freelance repairs and bridal alterations because I told them the house would reopen honestly. That word seems larger now than when I used it."

"Honesty often grows after it is tested."

"Does it save jobs?"

"Sometimes. Sometimes lies save them briefly and cost more later."

Oren leaned on the table. "Staff know more than they are saying."

"Fear makes bad witnesses," Mina said.

"Fear makes people pay rent."

"Then we give them a way to speak without standing alone."

Celia nodded. "Closed staff review. Counsel present. No press. Anyone with information can come through Oren or Talia."

Talia looked surprised. "Me?"

"You know the archive," Celia said. "And you know what it feels like to be afraid of Brigitte."

Talia's eyes lowered. "Yes."

Mina added a new sheet to the board: QUESTIONS. Who ordered camera privacy mode? Who removed the cracked steamer head? Who had the coffee-stained pattern book? Who knew Anouk might refuse? Who benefits from archive degradation narrative? She did not write who poisoned Anouk. Too soon. Questions should invite facts, not vanity.

Brigitte passed the open door, stopped, and looked in. Her eyes moved across the board, pausing at Ida's photograph. She did not enter.

Oren muttered, "She hates brown paper."

"Why?"

"It does not flatter."

Celia's phone buzzed. She read the message and exhaled. "Luc wants a meeting. He says uncontrolled speculation threatens brand value."

Mina pinned the message under pressure. "Useful of him to provide a label."

The board had begun as a map. Now it behaved like a mirror. Everyone in the room could see their own complicity, whether criminal, managerial, or inherited. Mina preferred mirrors when they were placed where people could not adjust the light.

She made them stand with the board for five silent minutes before anyone returned to calls. It was not ritual. It was memory training. Oren noticed a missing half hour between the steamer delivery and the camera gap. Talia noticed that Brigitte's archive note used the phrase original posture, the same language Elise later repeated to Maren. Celia noticed her own initials on an access approval she did not remember signing. "I sign too much," she said. "Then sign less," Mina replied. The board did not accuse in a voice. It made handwriting visible.

Celia ordered coffee and then forgot to drink it. The cup cooled beside the evidence board while staff came and went in wary glances. Mina noticed who looked at the board and who looked away. Dressers studied movement. Cutters studied cloth. Executives studied liability. Talia studied empty places where names should have been. "We need categories for omission," she said. Mina handed her a blank card. "Then make one." Talia wrote OMITTED MAKERS in block letters and pinned it under archive. The board grew more accurate and less comfortable.

Chapter 22: Camera Gaps

Backstage cameras had gone offline for a wardrobe-system update between seven-twelve and seven-forty. The collapse occurred at eight-oh-six, but the gown had been steamed and bagged during the gap. Oren found the update request after two hours of phone calls, one threat to cancel a vendor contract, and a conversation with an IT manager who kept saying protocol until Oren asked whether protocol had a lawyer.

The request came from an external vendor. The vendor claimed Maison Arel requested it. Maison Arel claimed the vendor scheduled it. Both emails routed through Elise Varo.

"Investor assistant, wardrobe IT coordinator, emergency dresser," Oren said. "Busy woman."

Mina examined the update logs on a laptop in the production office. The system did not need to go dark. It had been placed in privacy mode, camera feed disabled but access cards still recorded. Privacy mode required credentials and a reason code. The reason entered was model change.

"Who entered dressing room three?" Mina asked.

Oren pulled the badge log. "Talia at seven-oh-five. Me at seven-ten. Maren at seven-twenty. Elise Varo at seven-twenty-six. Brigitte at seven-thirty-one. Anouk at seven-thirty-eight."

"Brigitte entered during the gap."

"She will say archive supervision."

"Elise entered before Anouk."

"She will say investor documentation."

"What did Maren do?"

"Dressed Anouk. She is terrified."

They found Maren in alterations, unpicking a sleeve with hands that shook so badly the seam ripper snagged the lining. She was in her late twenties, hair pinned with a pencil, eyes red from not crying professionally enough.

"I did not poison anyone," she said before being asked.

Mina sat across from her. Oren stayed by the door, arms folded but face soft. "Tell me who told you to leave the room."

Maren looked at the closed door. "Elise said Brigitte wanted the gown adjusted privately."

"Did that seem normal?"

"Nothing with Brigitte seems normal. It just seems mandatory."

"What had you done before leaving?"

"Steamed the gown. Helped Anouk into it. She complained about the neckline scratch. I tried to soften it with tissue. Talia said the tissue might show. Celia said lower the line. Then she was called away. Elise came in and said Brigitte wanted the original crescent height."

"Was the steamer working normally?"

"It spit a little. I had a replacement head from the new kit. Elise said production approved it."

Oren looked at Mina. He had not known that detail.

"Who changed the head?" Mina asked.

"I unscrewed the cracked one. Elise handed me the replacement from the box. I attached it, steamed, then left. I did not know."

"Where did you put the cracked head?"

"On the tray."

"Did you see it later?"

Maren shook her head.

"When did Elise wear pale gloves?"

"When she came back in. Cream gloves with a little button. I thought it was ridiculous. She said fingerprints showed on old silk."

"Did Brigitte touch Anouk?"

"No. She looked in. She said the line was still wrong. Elise said she would handle it."

Mina let the silence sit. Maren's hands had stopped shaking because they were clenched around the seam ripper.

"Did Elise give you anything after Anouk collapsed?"

Maren's face emptied.

"Maren," Oren said quietly.

"A tissue bundle," she whispered. "She said it was used gloves and thread scraps, and I should put it in my kit because press were everywhere. I did. I did not open it."

"Where is it?"

"My kit."

The kit was searched with Maren present. The bundle was gone. Someone had either retrieved it or Maren had moved it and forgotten under stress. Mina did not accuse. Not yet.

She added Maren's statement to the board. The camera gap had failed to erase movement. It had made movement more deliberate.

Maren asked whether she should call her mother. The question was so young that it startled the room, though Maren was not young. Fear made adults reach backward. Oren said he would sit with her while counsel came. Mina told Maren not to discuss facts by phone, not even with love. "Love repeats when frightened," she said. Maren nodded and wiped her face with the back of her wrist, careful not to touch the seam ripper still lying on the table like a small bright accusation.

Chapter 23: Coffee Stain

Sabine identified the missing pattern book from Jules's photograph without hesitation. She did not sit down first. She held the print under the workroom light, thumb near but not on the paper.

"Coffee stain upper right. Blue thread through spine. Ida spilled the coffee during a wage argument."

"What was inside?" Mina asked.

"Original crescent draft, fitting notes, client measurements, and Ida's account of the Arel transfer. She wrote everything. She trusted paper more than people."

"Where would Brigitte keep it?"

"If she feared it, locked. If she needed it, close. If Luc has it, he is using it."

Mina showed her photos from the hidden shelf. Sabine's face worked through anger into grief. The relabeled spines, the erased shop marks, the loan slips without returns: each image seemed to remove Ida a second time.

"They relabeled her hand."

"Yes."

"That is worse than theft."

"How?"

"Theft says you had something worth taking. Relabeling says you never existed."

Mina had no technical term better than that.

Sabine gave Mina a spool of old blue thread from Ida's estate. "Compare it to the packet."

The spool was kept in a small tin that once held pastilles. Inside, the thread had darkened unevenly, indigo fading at the exposed turns. A handwritten label read Ida blue, 1962. Sabine's handwriting, not Ida's.

"I kept it because I am sentimental in ways Ida would mock," Sabine said.

"Sentiment preserves evidence more often than institutions admit."

"Do not make me sound noble. I kept it because I was angry."

"Anger preserves too."

Elias later compared the fibers to the packet from the hidden shelf. Same indigo dye profile, same cotton twist, same uneven tension from hand-winding. Harmless, natural, old. Proof of pattern lineage, not poison.

Sabine stayed while Mina logged the tin. "If the book appears, do not let them call it inspiration."

"What should they call it?"

"Work."

Mina wrote the word on the board. Some evidence needed a single syllable.

While Sabine was there, Celia entered carrying a folder of contract pages. The two women looked at each other across the table. Their shared history was mostly made of other people's decisions.

"I am sorry," Celia said.

Sabine's mouth tightened. "For what precisely?"

Celia absorbed the question. "For believing Brigitte without asking to see the paper. For building a collection on a story I did not test. For not answering your letters myself."

"Good. Precise apologies are rarer than good buttonholes."

"I will correct the record."

"Records are not corrected by press phrases."

"Then tell me what you require."

Sabine laughed once, not kindly but not cruelly. "You have lawyers. Ask them what back pay becomes after sixty years."

"I will."

"And ask whether Ida's name fits in your famous crescent. It should. She cut it."

Celia nodded. "It will."

Mina watched without interrupting. Cases often turned when injured parties spoke directly, not because forgiveness bloomed, but because evasions lost oxygen. The air in the workroom was still difficult. It was also cleaner than it had been.

Sabine stayed long enough to teach Celia how to read one of Ida's notches. She drew no grand lesson from it. She simply placed the photograph beside the muslin and showed how a small diagonal cut corrected the shoulder without spoiling the crescent. "This is intelligence," she said. "Not flourish. Not magic." Celia bent close, her hair falling forward, and for once looked like a student rather than a director. Mina watched the gesture settle. Attribution began in courts and statements, but sometimes it began with one person learning how another person's hand had solved a practical problem.

Before Sabine left, she asked to see the gown from a distance. Mina allowed her to stand at the tape line. Sabine did not reach toward it. "Ida would have hated that raised line," she said. "It makes the body fight the cloth." Celia looked at the form, then at Anouk's medical report lying on the table. The phrase body fight the cloth seemed to pass from craft into evidence. Mina wrote it down under targeted scratch. Useful language sometimes came from people who had never meant to testify.

Chapter 24: After Conservation

The conservation records showed the indigo gown had been stabilized six months earlier by an outside specialist named Yvonne Kreel. No toxic residue. No opened bodice seam after final stitching. No BlueArc treatment. Photographs showed the neckline lying lower than it did on Anouk's body.

Yvonne arrived with her own copies, her own magnifier, and the restrained fury of a professional whose work had been blamed by strangers. She wore gray linen and spoke to the gown before speaking to anyone else.

"You poor thing," she said, then looked at Mina. "What did they do?"

"That is what we are measuring."

They compared before and after images on a light table. The final conservation photograph showed the inner bodice seam intact with cotton-wrapped polyester support thread. The current gown showed a later silk thread repair. The dust pattern sat on top of conservation tissue fibers, not beneath.

"So the poison was applied recently," Celia said.

"Yes."

"And after the gown left legitimate care."

"Yes."

Brigitte, standing in the doorway, said, "Legitimate care includes family stewardship."

Yvonne did not look up. "Family stewardship did not sign my treatment report."

Mina turned the gown lining under the light. "Family stewardship opened a seam."

"You do not know that," Brigitte said.

"I know the seam was opened after conservation. I know Brigitte Arel and Elise Varo entered during the camera gap. I know a pale glove fiber was on the steamer head. I know BlueArc came through Luc's supply chain."

Brigitte's expression did not change, but her hand went to the pearls at her throat.

"You put many facts together carelessly," she said.

"No. I put them near each other. Carelessness is what happens next if the show continues."

Yvonne pointed to the photograph. "The neckline has been raised and pressed after my treatment. See this? The fabric remembers the original support line. Someone forced a sharper crescent."

"Could that cause the scratch?"

"If the edge was damp and pinched upward, yes. The facing would turn slightly. Old silk can become a small knife when vanity insists."

Celia looked from Mina to Brigitte. For the first time, she did not seem younger than the house.

"No more archive garment movement without Mina present," she said.

Brigitte left without agreeing. She also left without objecting.

After she was gone, Yvonne asked, "Is she always like that?"

Oren, from the wall, said, "Sometimes she is less festive."

Mina logged the conservation comparison. It mattered because it removed an escape: old cloth had not spontaneously produced modern poison, had not opened its own seam, had not raised its own neckline. The gown had been acted upon.

Celia stayed after the others left. She stood beside the light table, looking at the old photograph where the gown looked calmer, lower, less weaponized.

"I thought preservation meant keeping things unchanged," she said.

"Often it means documenting every change honestly."

"Then we have not preserved. We have curated obedience."

Mina closed the file. "That can change."

"Can it? After Anouk?"

"Not before. Only after."

Celia nodded, not comforted. Mina did not try to comfort her. The work ahead required discomfort to remain awake.

Yvonne packed her photographs with deliberate care, sliding each print between sheets as if returning bones to order. "If anyone says conservation caused this, call me before you call counsel," she said. "I have opinions with footnotes." Oren promised to fear them. Mina asked for the raw image files, not compressed copies. Yvonne approved of the request with one sharp nod. In cases like this, clarity degraded quickly: a forwarded image, a cropped detail, a rumor dressed as expertise. Mina wanted the first image, the first stitch, the first false move.

Yvonne also identified a pressure shine along the inner crescent that had not appeared in her treatment photographs. Under magnification, the silk showed minute flattened areas where a heated edge had been forced upward. "This is recent," she said. "Old silk does not decide to become obedient in a week." Oren, who had been quiet, asked whether a person could do that by accident. Yvonne gave him a look that made the question regret itself. "Accident can nick a stitch. This is insistence." Mina added the word to the report margin.

Chapter 25: Family Myth

Celia confronted Brigitte in the founder's salon, a room arranged around a portrait of the first Madame Arel. The portrait was larger than necessary and hung above a marble mantel that had never known soot. Madame Arel looked out in black satin, one hand on a table, the other holding a pair of shears she probably had not used often. Mina attended because Celia requested evidence be present, which was not the same as requesting comfort.

"The crescent cut was Ida Sayegh's," Celia said.

Brigitte stood under the portrait. "Many hands shape a house."

"Then why erase one?"

"Because houses need founders, not committees."

"They also need truth."

Brigitte laughed softly. "Truth is a poor fabric. It wrinkles under lights."

Mina watched Celia absorb the sentence like a slap she had expected but not ceased to feel. The salon windows showed the city in wet fragments. Outside, traffic moved. Inside, a family myth came apart slowly, expensive thread by expensive thread.

"Did you take the pattern book?" Celia asked.

"I protected it."

"From whom?"

"From people who would use one cutter's notes to dismantle sixty years of work."

"Ida's work."

"Arel work."

The portrait looked past them all, serene and useless.

Mina asked, "Did you open the indigo gown's bodice?"

Brigitte's eyes moved to her. "I inspected a faulty line."

"With pale gloves?"

"With clean gloves."

"Did you apply BlueArc?"

"No."

The denial came quickly and cleanly. Mina believed it more than she wanted to. Brigitte had stolen history. Poison had a different tempo.

"Did you instruct Elise to adjust Anouk's neckline?"

"I said the line was wrong."

"To Elise?"

"To the room."

"Rooms do not pinch fabric."

Brigitte's mouth tightened. "Elise was eager to be useful. Luc's people always are when cameras are near."

"Did you know BlueArc was in the building?"

"Of course. It is used for display."

"Did you know it should not be steamed against skin?"

"I am not a chemist."

"You are an archive chair."

"Then ask Talia what labels say. She reads warnings like prayers."

Celia stepped closer. "Do not put this on Talia."

"I put nothing on her. I describe staff function."

"You asked her to rewrite logs."

Brigitte's face hardened. "I asked her to correct institutional confusion."

"You asked her to erase Sabine."

"Sabine has spent her life mistaking proximity for authorship."

Mina placed Ida's photograph on the mantel beneath the portrait. The room seemed to dislike the smaller image.

"Ida is holding the coffee-stained book," Mina said. "The one you protected."

Brigitte looked at it for a long moment. "Ida was difficult."

Sabine's words returned to Mina: Inspiration is what rich people call unpaid work after the worker dies.

"Difficult to underpay?" Mina asked.

Brigitte did not answer.

Celia picked up Ida's photograph and held it beside the portrait. "I grew up under that painting. You could have put this beside it."

"And what then? Every buyer, every editor, every licensee asking whether Arel made its own line? Whether the house was fraud?"

"Maybe we would have had to become honest earlier."

"Honesty does not pay cutters when orders stop."

"Neither does collapse," Celia said.

For the first time, Brigitte looked old rather than formidable. It lasted only a second. Then she turned away.

"I did not poison Anouk."

Mina believed her. But belief, like cloth, needed support.

Celia did not release the photograph. She held Ida beside Madame Arel until her wrist trembled. "I used to think this room made me responsible," she said. "Now I think it made me obedient." Brigitte looked toward the wet windows. "Responsibility is obedience to the right thing." "Then you chose the wrong thing for years." The sentence left Celia quietly, but it landed harder than shouting. Mina let the silence record itself. There were no cameras in the salon. There were still witnesses.

Before leaving the salon, Mina photographed the space between the two portraits: Madame Arel above, Ida's image temporarily below. The gap was the point. It showed hierarchy made visible by wall placement. Celia noticed. "We measure everything now?" "Only what people used to call natural," Mina said.

Chapter 26: Steamer Residue

Elias's final steamer report arrived with photographs sharp enough to make Oren mutter. The contaminated head contained BlueArc Finish 7 residue mixed with indigo pigment and a trace of cosmetic glove powder. Heating the residue released the compound that had sickened Anouk.

Mina read the report twice in the production office while rainwater clicked from umbrellas drying near the door. Oren paced. Celia stood still. Talia sat with her hands folded around a cup of coffee she still did not drink.

"Could someone contaminate the gown without contaminating the steamer?" Mina asked Elias by phone.

"Yes."

"Could this pattern occur if the steamer was used to deliver it?"

"Very much yes."

"Could Brigitte's old archive dust do it?"

"No. Old indigo thread is not trying to murder anyone."

Mina appreciated clarity when it deserved bluntness.

She returned to Maison Arel's equipment room and photographed every steamer station. Only dressing room three had a replacement unit. The service request for that replacement came from Elise Varo's account, approved by Luc's office under press preview readiness.

Oren found delivery footage from the loading dock. Elise signed for the box at four-fifty-two. Maren carried it upstairs at five-oh-eight. An unknown courier left with the old head later, but the timestamp came after the collapse.

"Unknown courier?" Mina asked.

"Hat, mask, no badge. Carrying a small black thermal bag. Not food delivery. Wrong entrance."

"Service corridor?"

"Yes."

They checked badge logs. Elise Varo's guest pass opened the service corridor three minutes before the courier appeared.

Oren leaned back in his chair. "Movement."

Mina added a red line to the board. "Movement with chemistry."

The service request itself had been written in falsely casual language: cracked head compromises visual readiness; replacement needed before press walk. Mina disliked phrases like visual readiness. They made danger sound like poor styling.

"Would Maren know the replacement was contaminated?" Celia asked.

"Not unless she prepared it."

"Did she?"

"No evidence yet. Her prints will be on it because she installed it. That makes her vulnerable."

Talia looked up. "Like me."

"Yes."

Oren's expression darkened. "Luc's people keep putting hands between themselves and objects."

"That is what staff are for in rooms like this," Mina said.

Celia did not defend the room.

The courier remained unidentified, but the thermal bag suggested someone wanted to remove or protect a damp object. The old cracked head had been found in trash, not carried out, which meant the courier may have moved something else: gloves, bottle, cloth, or documentation.

"Can we get external street footage?" Oren asked.

"Police can. We should not freelance surveillance."

"You say that as if I have not already considered three illegal options."

"Consider them silently."

At the board, Mina placed the steamer report beside Anouk's medical notes. The route was now physical: BlueArc delivered, replacement head installed, contaminated steam applied, neckline pinched, exposure occurred, old head vanished, evidence partially discarded.

Celia touched the edge of the report. "He will still call it accidental."

"Then we will make accidental too heavy for him to lift."

Downstairs, someone restarted a machine and stopped it again. The whole house seemed to be waiting for permission either to work or to fall. Mina could not give either. She could only keep placing facts where lies wanted to sit.

She returned to dressing room three once more before leaving. The steamer stand had left a damp ring on the floor finish, pale against black paint. Mina measured it and photographed the direction of the cord. It showed the machine had been dragged closer to the mirror than Maren first remembered. When Mina told her, Maren closed her eyes. "Elise moved it," she said. "She said the outlet was safer there." Another small correction. Another lie losing space.

Elias called back with one annoyance, which meant useful detail. The replacement head had been wiped after use, but wiping had pushed residue into the threaded collar. Whoever cleaned it knew surfaces mattered and forgot joints. Mina liked joints. Hinges, collars, seams, hems: places where careless confidence went to die. She asked Oren for photographs of the replacement box before Maren opened it. He found one in a backstage assistant's phone. The seal had already been broken when Maren received it.

Chapter 27: Accidental, Please

Luc Renard met Mina, Celia, and Oren in the investor suite, where renderings of future Arel boutiques stood on easels. The wallpaper was indeed terrible: pale gray vines climbing nowhere. Luc wore a charcoal suit and the patient disappointment of a man explaining weather to children.

"The contamination must be classified as accidental until facts settle," he said.

"Facts have settled enough to require police," Mina replied.

"Police kill brands."

"Poisoning people does worse."

Luc's eyes cooled. "No one has established poisoning."

"Elias has established toxic delivery through a steamer."

"Misuse of product, perhaps. Archive confusion. Staff panic. These things happen when old houses attempt modern production."

Celia said, "BlueArc came through your fund's supplier."

"Passive investment. I do not manage product labels."

Mina placed the service request in front of him. "Your assistant requested the steamer replacement."

"Elise manages logistics."

"She also entered dressing room three during the camera gap."

"As did Brigitte."

Luc smiled then, slightly. He had been waiting to place that fact on the table. It was a practiced move: offer a guilty person adjacent to a crime and invite everyone to stop walking.

Oren leaned forward. "Brigitte did not sign for the steamer."

"No. She merely controlled the archive object, rewrote provenance, and had motive to discredit anyone questioning the gown. You see the difficulty."

Celia's face tightened. "Do not use our lie as your shelter."

For the first time, Luc looked irritated rather than smooth. "Your house has enjoyed shelter for generations. Do not become moral because a consultant with tweezers rearranged your furniture."

"Careful," Mina said.

He turned to her. "With what?"

"Confusing contempt with strategy."

Silence crossed the room. Oren's expression suggested he wanted to applaud and also live.

Luc sat back. "Here is what will happen. Maison Arel will issue a statement describing an adverse reaction during handling of a historic garment. The show will be postponed, not cancelled. The archive original will be retired. The new collection will proceed after review. Insurance covers delay. Anouk receives private settlement. The house survives."

"And Ida Sayegh?" Celia asked.

"A separate matter of attribution can be handled after stabilization."

"After you control the archive."

"After professionals control risk."

Mina placed another page before him: the insurance clause. "You want the contamination called accidental because the show insurance pays if archive degradation interrupts the revival, not if investor staff commit a crime."

The smile left.

Celia turned to him. "Is that true?"

Luc said nothing. Silence, Mina had learned, had many fabrics. This one was expensive.

"Did you know BlueArc should not be steamed?" Mina asked.

"I do not read every safety sheet in a portfolio company."

"Elise downloaded it."

"Elise is thorough."

"And obedient?"

His jaw moved once. "Ms. Tor, you are building a melodrama from operational errors."

"No. I am building a case from objects people hoped were too small to matter."

Oren picked up the service request. "Police get copies."

Luc's lawyer, who had remained quiet near the window, finally spoke. "All further requests should come through counsel."

"Good," Mina said. "Counsel writes things down."

As they left, Celia paused at one of the boutique renderings. It showed a future store with a wall of indigo accessories and the crescent logo glowing above polished stone.

"It looked so clean on paper," she said.

Mina looked at the rendering, then at the evidence folder in her hand. "Most paper does until someone asks where it came from."

In the lift, Celia leaned against the brass rail and shut her eyes. "He made survival sound so reasonable." Mina watched the floor numbers descend. "That is how leverage works. It borrows the voice of necessity." Oren stared at the mirrored doors. "I hate that I heard him for a second." "Then remember the second," Mina said. "It will help you hear it sooner next time." The lift opened on the workroom, where the same renderings had been taped above half-finished garments. Oren took them down without asking.

Chapter 28: The Tape Names Brigitte

Jules found the missing part of Ida's interview on a mislabeled tape marked HATS, 1999. He called Mina and sounded ashamed of the delay, as if magnetic media obeyed morality.

"I stored it with accessory interviews," he said. "Ida must have been on the end of a tape after a milliner. I am professionally humiliated."

"Bring it."

They played it in Celia's office with Sabine present and counsel taking notes. The recorder clicked, hissed, and produced a younger Jules asking whether Ida had ever confronted the Arel family directly.

Ida's voice emerged through static. "Brigitte came after Madame died. She took the blue book, the crescent drafts, the fitting notes. Said the family would preserve them. I said preserve is not the same as own."

Jules asked, younger on the tape, "Did you object formally?"

Ida laughed. "To whom? The door?"

Then the important line:

"Brigitte knew. She always knew which hands she was hiding."

No one spoke after it. The tape hissed into room tone, then the milliner interview resumed abruptly with a discussion of pheasant feathers. Jules stopped playback.

Sabine sat with both hands flat on her knees. "She hated being sentimental. Good."

Celia looked ill but steady. "This proves theft."

"It supports it," Mina said. "The pattern book proves more."

"Luc has it."

"Or had it."

They sent the tape to counsel and made three copies. Jules kept one, Mina kept one, Celia kept one. Sabine refused.

"I have heard enough," she said. "Use it. Do not make me curate pain."

Mina understood. Evidence had weight. Not everyone needed to carry the original.

Brigitte learned of the tape within the hour. She entered Celia's office without knocking, as always. Mina had expected rage. Instead Brigitte looked almost calm.

"Ida enjoyed drama late in life," she said.

Sabine, who had not yet left, stood. "Ida enjoyed rent."

"You reduce a house to wages."

"No. You inflated theft into heritage."

Celia raised a hand. "Enough."

Brigitte looked at her niece. "Do you think public confession will save you? They will not reward you for tearing down your own name."

"It may not save us," Celia said. "But your version is already killing us."

Mina watched Brigitte register the plural. Us. Not me. Not the house as portrait and logo. The people now trapped in the cost of her preservation.

"Where is the book?" Mina asked.

Brigitte's gaze stayed on Celia. "I do not have it."

"Who does?"

"Ask the man who understood its value better than my niece did."

"Luc."

Brigitte did not confirm. She did not need to. Her omission had a direction.

After she left, Jules exhaled. "That woman could make a weather report feel hereditary."

Sabine picked up her coat. "Weather passes. Mold stays if you keep the windows shut."

Mina added the tape transcript to the board. The old crime had acquired a voice. It named Brigitte, but it also named a culture of rooms that treated hands as tools and family names as owners of every useful line.

Jules stayed to label the copy in his careful archival hand. He wrote date unknown, speaker Ida Sayegh, subject transfer of pattern book. Then he paused. "Transfer is too polite." Sabine, already at the door, said, "Write taking." He did. The word sat bluntly on the sleeve. Celia looked at it for a long time. Mina had seen entire lawsuits turn on whether someone could bear a plain verb. Maison Arel had avoided one for sixty years.

Celia asked Jules to play the line once more after Brigitte left. He hesitated until Sabine nodded. Ida's voice filled the office again: Brigitte knew. On the second listening, Mina noticed not accusation but fatigue. Ida had spoken like a woman tired of explaining the obvious to rooms that profited from not understanding. Sabine heard it too. "She was done asking them to be fair," she said. "She was making a record." Mina looked at the tape turning in the machine. "She succeeded."

Chapter 29: Ordered Logs

Talia gave her formal statement in the sample room with counsel present and a glass of water untouched before her. She admitted altering loan logs, replacing box labels, and moving pattern books under Brigitte's orders. Her voice shook at the beginning, steadied in the middle, and broke only once, when she described typing Ida's erased shop marks into a temporary field labeled legacy note.

"Did Brigitte order you to contaminate the gown?" Mina asked.

"No."

"Did Luc?"

"No."

"Did Elise?"

Talia looked down. "Elise asked which archive garments would be copied and which would be worn. I told her the indigo original would open the preview. She asked whether Anouk had complained in fitting."

"Had she?"

"About the neckline scratch. She said it sat too high."

"Who did you tell?"

"Brigitte. Celia. Elise was in the room. Luc was outside for part of it, taking a call. The door was open."

"Could he hear?"

"I do not know. He hears what benefits him."

Counsel glanced up but did not object. It was not a legal answer, but it was a human one.

Talia produced duplicate metadata exports showing the pattern book's relabeling before Luc's due diligence. A second export, hidden on her personal drive, showed the original Ida Sayegh marks. She had kept the files in folders named for preservation reports because Brigitte never opened folders that promised maintenance.

"Why keep duplicates?" Mina asked.

"Archivists make copies when asked to lie."

"Did you plan to sell the pattern book?"

Talia's head snapped up. "No."

"Luc may say you did."

"Then he will need a better lie. The book left with him."

Mina marked that sentence. It had the sound of a drawer opening.

"Tell us how."

"Two weeks before the preview, Brigitte told me to prepare sensitive provenance materials for investor escrow. She said Luc required proof the house could defend its heritage. I boxed the coffee-stained book, two folders of loan slips, and a packet of blue thread. Elise collected them with a courier. I logged the transfer in a temporary file. Brigitte told me to delete it after the agreement was signed."

"Did you?"

"From the shared system. Not from my export."

"Why not?"

Talia looked at her hands. "Because deleting felt different from relabeling. Relabeling was cowardice. Deleting was murder."

No one corrected the metaphor.

"Did you see the book again?"

"No. Brigitte asked Luc to return it after Sabine's latest letter. Luc said custody was safer with him until public risk passed."

"Which public risk?"

"The Sunday show."

Oren, standing in the corner, wrote something on his clipboard with unnecessary force.

"Did Elise know about the BlueArc warning?"

"Yes. She asked me whether it was safe for display shots. I told her display only, never wearable, never steam. She said she was not dressing anyone, only making old fabric read well on camera."

Talia finally drank the water. Her hands shook again afterward, as if the statement had been holding them still.

When the formal recording ended, Celia said, "You should have told me."

Talia nodded. "Yes."

"I should have asked better questions."

"Yes."

It was a hard exchange, and Mina trusted it. Forgiveness was not being performed. Accountability was beginning its dull work.

Talia asked to see the altered entries one more time before they were sealed. She did not ask to change them. She read them as if memorizing a route she should never take again. "I made the interface look clean," she said. "That was the pleasure of it. One click and the past behaved." Celia answered without softness. "And now?" Talia looked at the export drive in Mina's evidence bag. "Now it looks like what I did." Mina preferred that answer. Shame that named its object could become useful.

Oren later found the temporary transfer file in a backup queue, not deleted so much as orphaned. Talia looked embarrassed by the system's mercy. Mina did not. "Machines are bad at shame," she said. "That is why they sometimes preserve what people erase." The file joined the export drive.

Chapter 30: Targeted Scratch

Anouk returned to the dressing room with Mina because memory sometimes needed architecture. She came without sunglasses this time, wearing a gray coat and flat shoes, her scarf loose enough not to touch the healing mark. The room had been cleaned only where evidence collection allowed. Powder still dusted the vanity. The mirror still multiplied every movement too brightly.

She stood before it without the gown, touching the air beside her neck.

"Here," she said. "Elise adjusted from behind. She pulled the left side up. I said it scratched. She said pain was posture leaving the body."

Oren, listening from the doorway, closed his eyes. "That sounds like investor yoga."

Anouk pointed to the steamer position. "Maren steamed the gown first. Then Elise came in after everyone left. She had pale gloves. She pinched the neckline. Something damp touched me. I thought it was steam left in the fabric."

"Did Brigitte touch you?" Mina asked.

"No. She looked at the dress like I was inside her property."

Mina asked Anouk to show the movement. Anouk stood behind the dress form and lifted the neckline with two fingers, careful not to touch the evidence cloth draped there for reconstruction. The gesture matched the dust pattern: pressure at latch from entry, steamer residue, then neckline transfer.

"Why target you?" Oren asked.

Anouk's mouth tightened. "I asked about the scratch in front of Talia and Elise. I said I would not walk if the gown hurt. Then Celia came in, and everyone smiled."

"You became the person most likely to stop the gown," Mina said.

Anouk nodded. "So they stopped me first."

The statement hung in the dressing room with the harsh lights. Mina did not soften it. Softening belonged to people who wanted the crime to be more tasteful.

"What did Elise's glove feel like?" Mina asked.

"Smooth. Not cotton. A seam at the wrist. A little button. She pressed with two fingers, then used her thumb to flatten the edge. The glove caught once. I felt a tug."

"On your skin?"

"On the scratch or the fabric. Hard to tell. I remember thinking she was too close. Not in a dramatic way. Backstage people are always too close. But she was close without being useful."

Mina wrote that down.

"Did she say anything after you coughed?"

"She said, 'Stage is holding.' Like I was traffic."

Oren looked away.

Anouk turned to him. "You did not do it."

"I ran the room."

"Then run it better next time. But do not take what belongs to her."

"Her?"

"Elise. Luc. Whoever gave orders. I am generous, not stupid."

Mina admired the distinction.

They walked the route from dressing room to runway with Anouk leading. She stopped where the carpet began. The chairs were gone now, stacked in rows along the wall. Without guests, the runway looked thin and practical, just a strip of fabric under lights.

"Here the heat hit," Anouk said. "The scratch burned. I smelled almond again. Then my ears filled."

"Did you see Luc?"

"At the aisle. Watching me too closely for a man watching fashion."

"Elise?"

"Near the curtain. She had taken the gloves off."

Mina marked both positions. Anouk did not embellish. Her memory contained gaps and admitted them. That made the clear parts stronger.

Before leaving, Anouk looked back at dressing room three. "Replace the latch."

"It is evidence," Mina said.

"After evidence."

"Why?"

"Because rooms remember badly when people keep touching the same brass."

Mina had heard less precise forensic language from experts. She wrote replace latch after release in the room notes. Some repairs mattered because they were not evidence. They were permission to stop being a scene.

Before Anouk left, she asked to sit for one minute in the chair where Maren had tied her shoes. Mina expected anger. Instead Anouk looked at the mirror and practiced breathing, slow and square, until the room seemed less in command of her body. "I needed to know I could do that here," she said. Oren looked stricken. Anouk stood before he could apologize again. "Do not spend all your guilt on speeches," she told him. "Spend some on doors." He nodded once. Mina wrote improved room authority protocol in her notes.

Chapter 31: Collateral

Luc's safe was not in his office. It was in the temporary investor suite behind a panel painted the same white as the wall. Oren found the panel because the paint dried differently near hinges. Mina appreciated production managers more each hour. He did not open it. He photographed it, called Mina, and said, with visible pain, "I am waiting for the warrant like a lawful citizen."

Police opened the safe under warrant two hours later. Inside were contracts, insurance riders, a flash drive, and a cloth-wrapped book tied with blue thread. The upper right corner bore a coffee stain.

Sabine did not need to be present to identify it. The book identified itself.

Mina opened it on a padded support. Ida Sayegh's handwriting filled the first pages: measurements, corrections, wage notes, complaints about bad scissors, and the crescent bodice draft. Blue thread looped through the spine. Not myth. Work.

Celia stood very still.

"He held it as collateral," she said.

The contract beside it confirmed: archive dispute materials held in escrow pending brand stabilization. If Maison Arel failed to perform under the investment agreement, Luc gained rights to license archival assets, including disputed patterns.

"He profited either way," Mina said. "Show succeeds, he owns revival. Show fails under controlled terms, he owns the pieces."

Oren looked at the book. "And Anouk?"

"If her collapse became vintage-garment accident, the archive looked dangerous, the copy replaced it, the show continued, and the pattern dispute stayed locked."

The book lay open, patient and stained. Some evidence waited decades. Some waited in a rich man's wall.

The flash drive contained scans of the same book, investor notes, and a valuation memo. One internal phrase made Celia sit down: disputed origin increases leverage if public correction delayed until after relaunch. Mina printed it and placed it beside Ida's wage notes. Two documents, sixty years apart, used different language for the same appetite.

Sabine arrived after counsel called her. She entered the suite slowly. When she saw the book, her face lost all armor.

"Hello, Ida," she said.

No one interrupted.

She did not touch the pages. "This was not his to lock."

"It will be logged under police custody," Mina said. "And copied for estate counsel."

"Estate," Sabine repeated. "Ida would laugh. She left three chairs, two irons, and a grudge."

"Grudges can have heirs."

Sabine gave Mina a look. "Do not become poetic. It weakens you."

"Noted."

Luc, brought in by police after the safe opened, claimed the book was held legally under collateral terms. His lawyer said escrow six times. Celia said nothing until he finished.

"You put my family's lie in your wall," she said.

"Your family's risk," Luc corrected.

"Her work," Sabine said.

Luc glanced at her as if she were an unexpected invoice.

Mina watched his reaction to the book more than his words. He was not frightened by old theft. He was frightened by possession becoming visible. That told her the book had not merely protected investment; it had enabled command.

As police sealed the safe contents, Oren whispered, "He thought walls were loyal."

"Walls rarely are," Mina said. "They just wait for hinges."

The safe itself was photographed after emptying: velvet-lined, temperature uncontrolled, a luxury box pretending to be preservation. Talia looked inside and made a sound of professional pain. "He stored a working pattern beside contract binders." Sabine did not laugh this time. "Of course he did. To him they were the same." Mina bagged a loose blue fiber from the safe floor. It would likely prove old and harmless, but it placed the book in that box in a way a lawyer could not rephrase. Objects resisted euphemism when handled correctly.

The valuation memo included photographs cropped so tightly that Ida's handwriting appeared decorative rather than documentary. Mina printed one uncropped page beside it. With the margins restored, the notes became human again: sleeve too tight, client impatient, ask for wages. Cropping had been another form of theft.

Chapter 32: Protected Theft

Brigitte came to the archive after the book was recovered and asked to see it. Celia allowed it with Mina present, police custody maintained, and Sabine notified. The old woman did not touch the pages. Her hands remained at her sides. Without the book hidden, the archive looked less like a shrine and more like a room caught lying.

"Ida was brilliant," Brigitte said.

"You erased her," Celia replied.

"I protected the house."

"From its own debt."

Brigitte looked at the pattern book. "Your grandmother built a name that fed hundreds. Ida had a cut. A house made it world."

Mina said, "The book records wages unpaid."

"Everyone was underpaid then."

"That is not a defense."

"It is history."

Sabine, who had entered quietly, said, "No. It is what people with names call history."

Brigitte's face hardened. "You think one book entitles you to dismantle Arel?"

"No," Sabine said. "It entitles Ida to enter the room."

Celia closed the book. "Did you contaminate the gown?"

"No."

"Did you open the seam?"

"I inspected it after Anouk complained. I did not apply poison. I did not know Luc's assistant would touch the steamer."

Mina watched her carefully. Brigitte's theft was old, proud, nearly theological. The poisoning was practical, hurried, financial. Different hands. Same room.

"Why inspect the seam privately?" Mina asked.

"Because the line was wrong."

"Wrong for whom?"

"For Arel."

"Anouk said it scratched."

"Models complain. Bodies change. Cloth teaches posture."

Anouk, who had come despite being told the meeting was optional, said from the doorway, "Cloth does not teach breathing."

Brigitte turned. The two women looked at each other: archive chair and living body, myth keeper and injured wearer.

"I regret your harm," Brigitte said.

Anouk considered. "Do you regret causing the room where it happened?"

Brigitte did not answer quickly enough.

Celia said, "Aunt Brigitte, you will step back from archive authority during the investigation."

"You cannot remove me from my mother's house."

"I can remove your keys from my working archive."

The distinction landed. Brigitte's hand went to the chain at her neck.

"You will destroy what you do not understand."

"No," Celia said. "I will stop inheriting understanding from fear."

Mina did not intervene. Some custody transfers required family language before legal forms.

Brigitte removed the key slowly and placed it on the table beside the pattern book. The metal made a small sound. In another room, under other circumstances, it might have seemed ceremonial. Here it sounded practical.

Sabine looked at the key, then at Ida's book. "She should have had one of those."

Brigitte's face changed, just enough to show the sentence had entered. Not transformed her. Entered.

Afterward, Mina logged the key as temporarily surrendered to Celia and Talia under counsel oversight. The archive's door still locked. The difference was not mechanical. It was named.

Talia made a new access sheet in pen because the digital system no longer deserved immediate trust. Every entry required two names, a reason, and an exit time. Brigitte watched the page being taped to the archive desk and said nothing. Her silence had changed texture. It was no longer command. It was containment. Sabine signed out after viewing the book and added one note beneath her name: Ida Sayegh's estate reserves all rights. Oren read it and whispered, "Good handwriting for a threat." Sabine heard him. "All good threats are legible."

After Brigitte surrendered the key, Celia asked everyone to leave the archive for ten minutes except Talia. Mina waited in the corridor. Through the glass she saw Celia and Talia move drawer by drawer, checking locks, not speaking much. It looked like inventory and grief in the same motion. When they emerged, Celia had a list of objects needing immediate provenance review. "We start with the ones that made money," she said. Talia answered, "Then we start with half the room." Neither woman smiled.

Chapter 33: Swatch Reconstruction

Mina reconstructed the contamination path with swatches pinned to a dress form, a clean steamer, and colored powder that looked nothing like poison. Police filmed. Elias watched to correct chemistry. Oren watched to correct backstage reality. Anouk watched from the doorway, arms folded, because she had earned the right to see the shape of what had happened to her.

The sample room had been cleared except for the dress form, table, and marked floor positions. Mina used inert tracer dust and a steam-safe substitute Elias had approved. He made everyone repeat inert twice before allowing the demonstration.

"I dislike theater," he said.

"Then treat this as teaching."

"Teaching is theater with footnotes."

First, Mina demonstrated the contaminated steamer head delivering warmed residue to the neckline. On dark swatch cloth, the tracer nearly disappeared. Second, a pale-gloved hand lifted and pinched the crescent edge, concentrating residue where Anouk felt the scratch. Third, the glove touched the dressing-room latch, leaving secondary dust. Fourth, the form was moved along the route, shedding particles along carpet and runway.

"Could Brigitte's inspection create the same pattern?" a detective asked.

"Not without the contaminated steamer," Mina said.

"Could Talia's archive handling?"

"No. Archive dust lacks the carrier compound and does not concentrate at the neckline."

"Could Anouk apply it herself?"

Anouk said, "Ask better questions."

The detective had the grace to look embarrassed.

Mina repeated the reconstruction twice. The particle distribution matched the evidence within useful tolerance. Science, she knew, rarely gave perfect theater. This was close enough to be ugly.

Elias signed off on the delivery mechanism. Oren signed off on the backstage sequence. Mina signed off on fiber transfer.

Anouk looked at the swatch form. "That is how she touched me."

No one asked who she meant.

After the formal filming ended, Maren asked to see the demonstration. She stood behind the tape line, face blotched from crying she had done elsewhere.

"I installed the head," she said.

"Yes," Mina said.

"If I had smelled it?"

"You might not have. Steam, perfume, pressure, old cloth. The smell likely sharpened when heated close and again under lights."

"I left her alone with Elise."

Anouk turned. "Maren."

The dresser's eyes filled. "I should have stayed."

"You were told to leave by people paid more to sound certain."

"That is not an excuse."

"No. It is a fact. Use it correctly."

Maren nodded, though she looked unconvinced. Mina knew guilt often tried to take responsibility away from the person who had planned harm. It felt moral. It could become useful to the wrong side.

Elias packed his tracer compounds. "The report will state the sequence requires recent application, heat, and direct contact. That should make the vintage reaction narrative scientifically unattractive."

"How unattractive?" Oren asked.

"Like a polyester lining in August."

Everyone stared at him.

"I work with textile people now," Elias said. "I am adapting."

Mina bagged the demonstration gloves and swatches. Even inert reconstructions deserved order. On the board, she replaced a question mark with a line: delivery mechanism established.

The case had moved from possible to probable. Probable was not conviction, but it changed how lies had to behave. They could no longer lounge in the open. They had to run.

Mina kept one failed swatch as well as the successful demonstration. The first attempt had too much tracer at the shoulder and too little at the throat. Failure mattered. It showed the match had not been forced for the camera. Harrow asked why she bagged it. "Because defense will ask whether we tried until the evidence looked right." "Did we?" "We tried until the method matched known facts." He smiled faintly. "That sounds like a sentence I can use." "Use it accurately," Mina said.

Elias made the detective repeat the word inert on camera before the reconstruction began. It seemed excessive until one assistant whispered that people online would think they had poisoned another mannequin. Mina let Elias have his caution. Public misunderstanding could contaminate a case almost as efficiently as bad handling.

Chapter 34: Blue Gloves

Maren was caught trying to burn a pair of blue-stained pale gloves in the alley behind the loading dock. She had wrapped them in tissue and struck two matches before Oren took them from her with a gentleness that made her cry harder. Rain had soaked the cardboard beneath her feet. The matches hissed out almost at once.

"I was going to bring them back," she said in Mina's temporary lab. "Then I thought I could not. Then I thought if they were gone, it would be over. It was stupid."

"Stupid is not specific enough," Mina said. "Start with who gave them to you."

"Elise told me to get rid of them. She said they made me look involved."

"Were you?"

"I dressed Anouk. I left when Elise said Brigitte wanted privacy. I switched garment bags because Elise said the archive bag looked cloudy for camera."

"Did you switch the steamer head?"

"I installed the replacement she handed me. I did not know it was contaminated."

"Did you know the gloves had residue?"

Maren shook her head. "After Anouk collapsed, Elise shoved them into my kit. Later she said if police found them, everyone would say the dresser poisoned the model. She said I had touched the steamer, the gown, everything."

"Why burn them?"

"Because I believed her for ten minutes."

"That is often enough time to ruin evidence."

"I know."

Mina examined the gloves. BlueArc residue on fingertips. Indigo pigment. Cosmetic powder. A tiny snag at the right index finger matched the fiber from the discarded steamer head. The right thumb showed a darker concentration consistent with pinching the neckline. The wrist held a small pearl button, absurdly delicate.

Anouk, called for possible identification later, remained outside. Mina would not let her see the gloves informally and spoil procedure. Maren watched the bag as if it contained her future.

"You are a witness," Mina said. "Act like one from now on."

Maren nodded. It was not forgiveness. It was instruction.

Oren stood in the corner, arms crossed. "Why tell us now?"

"Because I saw the reconstruction," Maren said. "Because Anouk looked at me and did not hate me, which was worse. Because Elise texted again."

Mina held out her hand. "Phone."

The message read: gloves gone? do not let Talia touch your kit. L will handle. The sender was saved as E.V.

"L," Oren said.

"Luc or lawyer or logistics," Mina said. "We verify."

"Could be liar."

"That too."

Maren gave a formal statement with counsel. She admitted trying to destroy evidence before it burned, described Elise's instructions, and identified the tissue bundle. Her fear made her a damaged witness, but damaged did not mean useless. It meant the fracture had to be disclosed.

After she left, Oren sat down heavily. "I hired her."

"And?"

"And I keep thinking I should have made the room safer."

"You should have."

He looked up, surprised by the lack of comfort.

"So should Celia. So should Talia. So should every system that made Elise's order sound louder than Maren's judgment. None of that prepared the gloves."

Oren rubbed both hands over his face. "That is unpleasantly fair."

"Fair often is."

Mina added the glove evidence beneath Elise's name. The blue on pale fabric looked vulgar after all the house's careful indigo. It lacked romance. Mina preferred it that way.

Later, Maren returned with a list written on pattern paper: every instruction Elise had given her that week. Some were innocent. Some were not. Mina read them in order because sequence was a form of truth. Bring clear archive bag. Replace cracked head. Leave room when Brigitte arrives. Keep used scraps out of sight. Burn nothing appeared nowhere, which was useful too. The last line read: I was afraid of losing my job. Mina did not treat it as evidence of innocence. She treated it as evidence of pressure, and pressure had already become part of the crime.

Chapter 35: Elise's Payment

Elise Varo had left Maison Arel by the time police reached Luc's suite. Her apartment yielded garment bags, visitor passes, two phones, and a receipt for cash deposit just under the reporting threshold. The deposit came the morning after the preview. Her sister's flat, where police found her, was above a pharmacy and smelled of boiled coffee and fear.

Luc claimed assistant discretion. Elise, when interviewed, claimed Brigitte asked her to protect the gown from Anouk's complaints.

Mina listened through glass. Elise sat with perfect posture. Without the pale gloves, her hands looked younger than the rest of her presentation. She rubbed thumb against forefinger as if feeling residue that was no longer there.

"Protect how?" the detective asked.

"Adjustments. Handling. I did not know the spray was harmful."

"Who gave you the steamer head?"

"It was in the replacement box."

"Who told you to switch it?"

Elise looked toward her lawyer. "Luc said the original head was unreliable. He said the show could not survive archive panic."

"Did he mention insurance?"

Silence.

The detective placed the BlueArc safety sheet on the table. "You downloaded this two days before the preview."

"For display shots."

"It says do not use on wearable items."

"I was not using it on wearable items."

"The steamer head was."

Elise's composure cracked at the edge. "You do not understand how these rooms work."

Mina, behind glass, almost smiled. That phrase had carried many sins through many rooms.

The second phone contained messages from Luc:

No illness before runway. If she refuses, use copy.

Then, after the collapse:

Archive reaction is acceptable narrative.

Elias would not call that chemistry, but Mina saw the compound clearly: pressure, money, contempt.

Elise did not confess fully. She did not need to. Her phone had better discipline.

The cash deposit came from a withdrawal tied to a consulting entity used by Luc's fund. Elise said it was a performance bonus. The memo line said preview readiness. Mina placed the receipt beside the safety sheet. Money liked euphemisms as much as fashion did.

After the interview, Elise asked to speak to Mina. Her lawyer objected. Elise insisted. They met with counsel present.

"Brigitte made it possible," Elise said.

"Many things made it possible."

"She wanted the line higher. She wanted Anouk quiet."

"Wanting is not delivery."

Elise's eyes sharpened. "You believe her."

"I believe evidence."

"Then believe this. Luc said the dose was harmless. He said models faint, shows survive, the archive story would take blame. He said the house needed a controlled incident more than an uncontrolled refusal."

"Who prepared the steamer head?"

Elise looked at her lawyer. The lawyer said nothing, which was advice by absence.

"Luc gave me the bottle," Elise said. "I coated the head in the service room. I thought it would irritate, make her step aside, not send her to hospital."

"You applied it near her skin."

"I was told the copy would replace it."

"After she collapsed."

Elise began to cry then, not from remorse alone but from the collapse of usefulness. "He said archive reaction, not poison."

"Those words did not protect Anouk."

Elise covered her face. Mina left the room. Confessions often arrived dressed as complaints about being misled. The evidence did not care what costume truth wore if it finally stood still.

When Elise was led out, she looked through the glass and saw Maren in the hallway. Neither woman spoke. Maren did not move aside. Elise had to pass close enough for both to understand proximity without authority. It was a small, ugly mercy. Mina watched Maren's hands. They shook, but they stayed empty. No matches. No tissue bundle. No borrowed guilt. Oren stood behind her, not touching her shoulder, finally learning that protection sometimes meant leaving a witness room to stand.

Mina asked for Elise's expense reports going back a month. The glove purchase sat between lunch receipts and courier fees, ordinary as a pin. That ordinariness mattered. Harm had not entered the room with a drumroll. It had entered through reimbursement, approval codes, and the confidence that no one read small lines.

Chapter 36: Closed Review

Celia cancelled the public show and replaced it with a closed evidence review for staff, counsel, police, and the few board members brave or trapped enough to attend. The runway remained lit, but no guests sat in the chairs. No music played. The blue carpet looked almost ordinary without applause waiting at the end.

"This is not a show," Brigitte said.

"No," Celia replied. "That is why it may be useful."

Mina presented cloth evidence. Elias presented toxicology. Oren presented backstage logs. Talia presented archive changes. Jules played Ida's tape. Sabine stood beside the pattern book and did not cry. Anouk sat in the front row, scarf loose at her neck, watching the reconstruction without looking away.

Luc arrived late with counsel. His face held annoyance, not fear. That changed when the safe photographs appeared on the screen.

"Collateral," Celia said. "My family's lie in your wall."

Luc's lawyer objected to characterization. Celia ignored him.

"The revival collection will not proceed under false attribution. The indigo gown remains evidence. The copy remains unworn."

Staff shifted. Some looked frightened. Some looked relieved. Machines had been silent all morning. Mina had not realized how much she appreciated the quiet until no one tried to fill it.

When Elias explained BlueArc, he did not dramatize. He showed the safety sheet, the residue maps, the heat release curve, and Anouk's medical markers. His calm made the facts harder to dismiss.

"The evidence is inconsistent with age-related archive degradation," he said. "It is consistent with recent application of a modern finishing compound, heat activation through a steamer, and direct contact at the neckline."

Anouk touched her scarf once, then dropped her hand.

Oren stood next. He was not polished. That helped. He showed the camera gap, badge logs, steamer replacement, service corridor pass, and Maren's statement. He did not spare himself.

"Production allowed investor staff into working rooms with unclear authority," he said. "I allowed that culture because the money was treated as oxygen. That changes now if there is a now."

The staff listened harder after that.

Talia's portion was the quietest and in some ways the most damaging. She showed original metadata, altered metadata, Brigitte's instructions, and the hidden shelf photographs. She did not say she had been forced. She said she had obeyed.

Brigitte sat motionless.

Jules played Ida's tape. The old voice filled the runway where music should have been.

"Preserve is not the same as own," Ida said.

Sabine closed her eyes.

At the end, Celia turned to her workers. "No one sews today."

That, more than anything, told Mina the house had heard. In a couture house, stopping machines was not symbolic. It was costly, specific, and impossible to hide.

Luc's lawyer requested private discussion. Celia refused in front of everyone.

"All discussions about the archive, the investigation, and staff responsibility will be documented," she said.

Brigitte stood and left before anyone could ask whether she endorsed that. Luc stayed, because leaving would have looked worse.

After the review, Anouk approached the evidence board. "It is strange to see harm become diagrams."

"Does it help?"

"It makes it less lonely," she said. "Not better. Less lonely."

Mina accepted the distinction. It was one of the few honest gifts evidence could offer.

After the staff dispersed, Celia walked the runway alone from entrance to end. No one applauded. At the midpoint, where Anouk had fallen, Celia stopped and looked back at the workroom. "We built a path and forgot it had to carry a person," she said. Mina stood at the edge of the carpet. "Then rebuild the path." "With what money?" "With the money that remains after you stop paying for silence." Celia gave a short, humorless laugh. "That may be a smaller budget." "Better accounting," Mina said.

At the end of the review, Maren placed her recovered kit on the table and asked police to inventory it completely. "I do not want to wonder what else someone put in my work," she said. Harrow accepted the kit without ceremony. Mina saw Oren swallow whatever apology rose in him and simply witness the handover.

Chapter 37: Rogue Archivist

Luc's public statement named no one directly and blamed everyone precisely. It referred to "a rogue archival employee," "historic confusion," and "unauthorized handling of disputed materials." Reporters found Talia's name within an hour. The internet, which loved a small villain, began building one from her job title and a photograph lifted from a conference panel on textile storage.

Talia sat in the archive office, white-faced, while her phone shook on the desk.

"I altered logs," she said. "So they can make the rest fit me."

"Not if the evidence does not fit."

"People like one small villain."

"Then we make the villain the wrong size."

Mina and Celia released Talia's duplicate metadata through counsel: original exports, altered exports, Brigitte's instructions, Luc's collateral agreement, Elise's access logs. Jules published a careful piece that named structures before people and people before rumors. He quoted no one without permission. Mina had not known journalists could behave so well when vanity was pointed correctly.

Brigitte did not defend Talia, but she did not accuse her either. That was the nearest she came to decency that day.

Oren posted staff shift records in the internal system with a note: Do not speak to press alone; do not let anyone make you singular.

Talia read it and finally cried.

Mina stood by the pattern shelves. The archive had always been full of names removed from work. For once, the danger was a name being added falsely. Both were forms of theft.

Celia came in carrying printed comments from the board. "They want resignation."

"Whose?"

"They are flexible. Talia if it calms press. Brigitte if it calms counsel. Mine if it calms investors."

"Flexible blame is rarely honest."

"I know."

Talia wiped her face. "If my resignation protects staff, I will offer it."

"No," Celia said.

Talia stared.

"You may face discipline for altering records. That will be handled openly. You will not be fed to Luc's statement."

Mina saw Talia struggle with relief because relief felt undeserved. That was common among witnesses who had done wrong but not the worst wrong. They believed proportionality was indulgence. It was not. It was accuracy.

Outside the archive, staff had begun taping paper over the glass walls where photographers from across the street might catch silhouettes. The paper was plain brown, the same as Mina's evidence board. It made the atelier look less glamorous and more like a place where work happened.

Luc's statement backfired by evening. Jules's article ran with images of Ida's pattern book, the BlueArc safety warning, and the investment clause. The headline did not mention rogue archivists. It mentioned leverage.

Oren read it aloud until Celia told him to stop enjoying himself.

"I am not enjoying," he said. "I am processing with emphasis."

Mina allowed herself one tired smile. The case was not solved, but a false narrative had been interrupted before it hardened. In her work, that counted as a significant event.

Talia turned off her phone and placed it in a drawer. The buzzing continued in her imagination; Mina could see it in the way she glanced at the wood. "Do I deserve protection?" she asked. Celia answered before Mina could. "You deserve process. Protection comes from process if we do it correctly." It was an imperfect answer, which made it useful. Outside, staff finished taping the last sheet of brown paper over the glass. The archive became, for one night, less visible and more honest.

At midnight, Mina found Talia asleep upright at the archive desk with a pencil still in her hand. The phone in the drawer buzzed again, faint and trapped. Mina woke her gently. "Go home." Talia looked at the taped paper on the glass. "What if they peel it off?" "Then Oren tapes it again." "What if I deserve the articles?" "You deserve the parts that are true. Not the parts Luc bought." Talia absorbed that like medicine with a bad taste. Then she took her coat and signed herself out properly.

Chapter 38: Duplicate Metadata

Talia's duplicate metadata did more than clear her. It showed Luc's due diligence team had received the original Ida Sayegh records before investment. They knew the silhouette's provenance was disputed. They knew the coffee-stained pattern book mattered. They valued it as leverage.

One internal note from Luc's analyst read: if Arel myth collapses, disputed pattern asset may be separately exploitable.

Celia read the line aloud without expression.

"Asset," Sabine said. "Ida would have hated that."

"Everyone should," Mina said.

The metadata also showed Elise accessed the digital gown file eight times in the week before the preview, focusing on conservation photographs and neckline notes. Brigitte accessed the same file twice. Talia accessed it once to print a handling form. Celia had opened the design photographs but not the conservation close-ups.

"Movement," Oren said again.

"And knowledge," Mina added.

Luc's counsel argued that due diligence was not operational instruction. Elias argued, in his own report, that the poison dose required a delivery decision after the due diligence period. The two truths did not cancel. They stacked.

Celia began drafting a public correction. Not a tribute. Not a campaign. A correction. The first version used too many adjectives. Sabine crossed them out with a pencil.

"Ida does not need luminous," Sabine said. "She needs paid."

Celia nodded and rewrote.

"Will it save the house?" Oren asked.

She looked at the data, the pattern book, the staff gathered in doorways. "I do not know."

For once, nobody punished her for the honest answer.

Mina worked with Talia to preserve the metadata properly. Original exports were hashed, copied, printed, and placed with counsel and police. Talia explained each field: object ID, prior title, related maker, lender notes, rights conflicts, condition history. The old system had not lacked places to tell the truth. People had chosen not to populate them.

"This field," Mina said, pointing to related maker.

"It was blanked during consolidation."

"By you."

"Yes."

"Who instructed wording?"

"Brigitte. Luc's team reviewed. They wanted fewer names in investor-facing files."

"Fewer names," Sabine said from across the room. "There is a polite little knife."

The duplicate metadata also contained a scan request from Elise for high-resolution neckline images. Her note read: need exact stress points for presentation styling. Elias, when shown the phrase, raised both eyebrows.

"Stress points. That is unexpectedly candid."

"Fashion uses technical words without morality," Mina said.

"Chemistry too."

They added it to the board.

By night, the evidence table looked less like a crime scene and more like an argument nobody could reduce. Old erasure, modern financial leverage, physical delivery, and a model's injury were distinct strands twisted into one cord. Pulling only one would not loosen the knot. Mina had to keep all of them under tension.

She asked Talia to print the blank related maker fields beside the restored ones. The empty boxes were almost more damning than the filled text. Absence had been formatted, approved, exported, and archived. Sabine ran one finger above the blank space without touching the page. "That is where Ida was made convenient," she said. Celia asked for a copy to keep on her desk. Mina allowed it only after marking it duplicate. Even remorse needed version control in a house that had confused feeling with record for too long.

Luc's analyst note also named potential merchandise streams: crescent scarf, crescent clasp, crescent perfume bottle. The stolen line had already been broken into products before its maker was restored. Oren read the list and looked physically offended. "A perfume bottle?" "Licensing likes shapes it can repeat," Celia said. "So did Brigitte," Sabine replied. Mina let them sit with that. Repetition could honor when chosen openly. Hidden repetition was extraction. The difference would matter when Maison Arel tried to sell anything blue again.

Talia also restored a deleted rights note linking Sabine's earlier letters to the object file. The letters had not been lost. They had been made inconvenient, then invisible. "Invisible is not gone," Sabine said. Mina thought of all the rooms that relied on that confusion. The archive was becoming less hospitable to it.

Chapter 39: Needle Case

Brigitte's old needle case was found in the founder's salon, inside the drawer of a sewing table no one used because it had become decorative. Mina opened it under witness. The table's surface held a vase of white flowers and no scratches, which told her it had served memory more than work for decades.

Inside the needle case were needles, chalk, a silver thimble, two buttons, and a folded square of tissue containing indigo dust. Not BlueArc. Old indigo thread dust. Harmless, but visually similar. The kind of dust that could confuse a person who wanted confusion.

"Why keep this?" Celia asked.

Brigitte was present, seated under the portrait as if awaiting judgment from an ancestor she had edited.

"It was Ida's," she said.

Sabine made a sound but did not speak.

The tissue also held a tiny paper label: Sayegh crescent, original. Brigitte had kept the truth in a needle case while telling the lie in public rooms.

"Did you use this dust on the gown?" Mina asked.

"No. I compared it. Years ago."

"To what?"

"To the thread in the pattern book."

"You knew the match."

Brigitte looked at the case. "Of course."

Celia closed her eyes. The admission hurt more because it was quiet.

Mina bagged the tissue. It did not prove poisoning. It proved knowledge, possession, and concealment. Sometimes the old crime had to be fully named before the new crime could stop hiding behind it.

"Why keep the label?" Sabine asked.

Brigitte's answer came slowly. "Because I did not want to forget."

"You made everyone else forget."

"That is different."

"No," Sabine said. "It is aristocratic."

The word landed hard in the room. Maison Arel was not legally aristocratic. It had merely practiced the habits.

Celia picked up the silver thimble with gloved fingers. "Was this Ida's too?"

"Yes."

"Why was it in our founder's salon?"

"Because your grandmother kept it after Ida left."

"As trophy?"

Brigitte looked offended. "As remembrance."

"Remembrance without name is theft with flowers," Celia said.

Mina saw Sabine glance at her, surprised. The sentence was sharper than Celia usually allowed herself to be. It did not fix anything. It showed she had stopped cushioning the room.

Elias tested the dust later and confirmed what the eye already suggested: old indigo thread, cotton fibers, no BlueArc carrier, no toxic compound. Luc could not use the needle case as alternate source for Anouk's exposure. Brigitte could not use harmlessness to erase concealment.

Mina added the needle case to the archive column. Its evidentiary value was moral as much as forensic, but moral evidence mattered when motive wore family names.

Before leaving the salon, Sabine stood before Madame Arel's portrait.

"She cut beautifully sometimes," Sabine said.

Celia looked at her. "Ida?"

"Both. That is the nuisance of truth. It does not let you hate cleanly."

Brigitte, for once, did not answer.

Mina sealed the needle case last. Brigitte watched the thimble disappear into evidence paper with a look that was almost grief, though grief did not excuse ownership. "You may get it back," Mina said. "No," Sabine replied. "It will not go back there." Brigitte did not challenge her. The portrait above the mantel remained unchanged, which suddenly seemed absurd. Celia noticed Mina looking. "We will move it," she said. "Where?" "Somewhere it has to share a wall." That was not evidence, but it was a plan.

Mina asked Brigitte whether anyone else had handled the needle case recently. "Celia, once, as a child," Brigitte said. "She liked the thimble." Celia remembered it then: a rainy afternoon, Brigitte letting her hold silver while saying the best tools stayed in the family. The memory made Celia sit down. "You were teaching me possession before I knew the word." Brigitte looked at her hands. "I was teaching you care." "No," Celia said. "Care would have included the name." The needle case lay between them, small enough to fit in a pocket, large enough to hold a doctrine.

Chapter 40: Sabine's Testimony

Sabine testified in a conference room because no court date could move fast enough for the house review. Counsel recorded. Mina documented. Celia listened. Brigitte did not attend. Sabine wore a black jacket with cuffs mended in visible blue thread. Mina suspected the choice was deliberate and did not ask.

"Ida Sayegh drafted the crescent bodice in 1962," Sabine said. "I was apprentice then. She cut it for a client with uneven shoulders. The correction pleased Madame Arel, who asked for variations. Ida requested pattern credit and wages. She received neither."

She identified the muslin, the notches, the blue thread, the coffee stain, the handwriting, the needle-case dust. Her voice did not tremble. Anger had kept it oiled for years.

"Did Ida authorize transfer to Maison Arel?"

"She authorized work. Not erasure."

"Did she ever call the silhouette Arel's?"

Sabine smiled. "In public, when she needed work. In private, never."

The lawyer asked whether memory could be mistaken after so many years.

Sabine looked at him as if he had brought dull shears. "Memory, yes. Notches, no."

Mina saw Celia write that down.

The lawyer tried again. "Could Madame Arel and Ida Sayegh have developed the line collaboratively?"

"Many garments are collaborative. Collaboration has names on both sides. This had one."

"Could Ida have been inspired by Arel's existing house style?"

"A woman with a pencil can be inspired and unpaid at the same time. That does not make unpaid disappear."

Mina kept her face still. Sabine was a dangerous witness in the best way: precise, irritated by vagueness, and uninterested in performing injury for sympathy.

After the formal questions, Celia asked one of her own. "Why did Ida keep working after the first dispute?"

Sabine looked at her. "Rent. Pride. Habit. Hope that the next beautiful thing would be treated better. People stay in rooms that harm them for many reasons. Do not romanticize any of them."

Celia nodded.

"Did she hate the house?"

"Some days. Other days she loved a sleeve too much to leave it wrong."

That answer seemed to hurt everyone present more than accusation would have.

Afterward, Sabine stood over the pattern book. "Hello, Ida," she said, too softly for the official recording.

Mina heard it. She did not write it into evidence. Some things entered the room without needing to become exhibits.

In the hallway, Anouk waited with a paper cup of tea for Sabine. They had barely spoken before. Sabine took the cup.

"Thank you," she said.

"You looked like you needed something to hold," Anouk replied.

"That obvious?"

"I model clothes for a living. Holding is half the job."

Sabine almost smiled. The two women stood together for a moment: one harmed by the old erasure, one by the new effort to preserve it. Mina left them there. Not every useful conversation needed an investigator in it.

Inside the conference room, Celia remained with the transcript. She underlined rent, pride, habit, hope. "Those are not legal categories," she said. "No," Mina answered. "But they explain why harm can last without chains." Celia closed the folder. "I used to think exploitation needed cruelty every day." "Sometimes it only needs the rent due every month." The sentence sat between them with the dull force of accounting. Maison Arel understood ledgers. It was beginning to understand what had been kept off them.

The lawyer asked Sabine whether she had any financial interest in her testimony. Sabine looked at him for a long second. "Yes. Wages." Counsel shifted in his chair. "Current financial interest." "Back wages are current when unpaid." Mina watched the court reporter's fingers hesitate, then continue. Celia looked down at the table, not hiding from the answer but taking it in. The room had expected bitterness. Sabine gave them arithmetic. It was harder to dismiss.

After Sabine left, the lawyer asked whether her testimony had been too emotional. Celia looked at him until he lowered his pen. "She identified a pattern draft, not a ghost," she said. Mina marked the exchange in her notes only because it showed Celia's reflex changing. She was beginning to defend fact before image.

Chapter 41: Brigitte's Line

Mina confronted Brigitte in the archive because that was the only room where the old woman seemed fully present. Between drawers of gowns and shelves of patterns, Brigitte looked less like a villain than a custodian who had mistaken custody for ownership. The surrendered key had been replaced by a temporary access card that required Talia's presence. Brigitte wore the absence around her neck like an insult.

"You stole Ida's work," Mina said.

"I inherited the theft and maintained the house."

"That is an admission wearing gloves."

Brigitte's mouth tightened. "You speak as if every institution is born clean except for people like me."

"No. I speak as if people like you keep them dirty and call it preservation."

For once, Brigitte seemed tired. "I did not poison Anouk."

"I believe you."

Surprise moved across her face before pride closed it.

"But you made the room where it was possible," Mina said. "You taught everyone that truth could be adjusted if the house required it."

Brigitte looked down the row of garment drawers. "Arel was all I had."

"It was never only yours."

She did not answer.

Mina asked one final question. "Who knew Anouk threatened not to walk?"

"Celia, Talia, Elise, myself. Luc was outside the fitting room."

"Could he hear?"

Brigitte closed her eyes briefly. "Luc hears money through walls."

That was the closest she came to helping.

"Did you tell him afterward?"

"I told him the preview was at risk because the model was difficult and the line was wrong."

"Difficult."

"Do not make me rehearse my cruelty. I know it."

That was new. Mina waited.

Brigitte opened a drawer and looked at an evening coat wrapped in tissue. "My mother believed houses were built by singular genius. She needed to believe it because buyers did. Press did. Men with money did. A house with too many names looked like a workshop, and workshops were replaceable."

"So you made Ida replaceable."

"We made many people replaceable."

"That is worse."

"Yes."

The word was small. It did not absolve. It did not perform. Mina accepted it as data.

"Will you state that publicly?"

Brigitte smiled without humor. "You overestimate repentance."

"I estimate pressure."

"Celia will do it. She has always enjoyed martyrdom."

"No. She has begun enjoying precision."

Brigitte looked at her then, and for the first time Mina saw fear for Celia not as property but as person. It vanished quickly.

"Luc will not stop," Brigitte said. "He did not invest in cloth. He invested in control."

"Then help us remove it."

"I have given you what I can."

"You have given what was forced."

"Often the same thing at my age."

Mina left with Brigitte's statement noted: Luc knew preview risk, heard or was told of Anouk's refusal, controlled collateral, and framed archive reaction. It was not enough. It was more than before.

Behind her, Brigitte remained among the drawers, a woman surrounded by evidence of hands she had refused to name.

Talia waited outside the archive with the access sheet. Brigitte signed out under supervision, the pen pausing before the time. "I used to enter whenever I pleased," she said. "Yes," Talia replied. Nothing more. The restraint cost her; Mina saw it in her jaw. Brigitte handed back the temporary card. For a second, the two women held the same piece of plastic, old authority and new procedure meeting in an object worth less than a button. Then Talia took it and logged the return.

Before leaving the archive, Brigitte paused at a drawer labeled EVENING, 1962-1964. "There are pieces here Ida touched," she said. Talia's pen hovered. "Then we note that." Brigitte turned as if the simplicity insulted her. "You cannot prove every hand." "Then we do not claim what we cannot prove," Talia said. "We record uncertainty instead of using it as cover." Mina saw Celia's influence there, and Sabine's, and Talia's own bruised pride becoming method. Brigitte gave no answer, but she did not open the drawer.

Chapter 42: Dose

Elias proved the dose with a mannequin, inert fabric, and a safe tracer compound that behaved like BlueArc without harming anyone. He insisted on using a lab training room rather than Maison Arel because, in his words, "I have seen that building's relationship with boundaries." The mannequin wore a mock bodice cut from dark fabric, its neckline marked where Anouk's scratch had been.

"Archive dust cannot do this," he said for the fourth time.

"Say it for court," Mina replied.

"I intend to say it until everyone is bored."

The tracer showed why Anouk collapsed on the runway rather than in the dressing room. Heat activated initial release. Movement, stage lights, and breathing completed exposure. The scratch at the neckline increased contact but did not create the poison. The amount delivered by the contaminated steamer head was enough to sicken an adult predictably, especially when concentrated by a gloved pinch along the throat.

"So the delivery required planning," Mina said.

"Planning or a chain of improbabilities so long it becomes planning with better tailoring."

Elias looked embarrassed by his own metaphor.

"Keep that," Mina said.

"Absolutely not."

The report stated: toxic dose required contaminated steamer head, recent application, direct neckline contact, and wearer movement under warm stage conditions. Accidental archive degradation was inconsistent with the evidence.

Mina sent the report to Celia, police, and counsel. Then she stood in the lab and let herself feel the relief of a false explanation dying. False explanations took effort to kill. They were bred for survival.

Detective Harrow, assigned after the case became formally criminal, read the report with a pencil in hand. He was a careful man with a tired face and no visible interest in fashion.

"This gives us mechanism," he said.

"Yes."

"Elise gives us action."

"Partly."

"Luc gives us motive."

"And messages suggesting command."

"But not a clean order to poison."

"People who write clean orders to poison do not usually own investment funds."

Harrow tapped the report. "We can charge Elise. Luc will be harder."

"Harder is not no."

"I know. I am setting expectations, not surrendering."

Mina appreciated the distinction. She had no patience for theatrical certainty from investigators. Cases were built from weight, not wishes.

Elias returned with a final chromatogram. "One more thing. The residue on the glove and steamer head includes a trace additive used to tint BlueArc for application visibility. Same lot as the bottle from Luc's supplier delivery. Not merely same product."

Harrow looked up. "Can you put that in words a jury can survive?"

"Same batch," Elias said.

"Good."

Mina added batch match to the board. One more line tightened.

That night, Celia called. "I read the dose report."

"And?"

"I keep thinking of Anouk walking because everyone expected her to."

"She stopped."

"Almost too late."

"But she stopped."

Celia was quiet. "Tomorrow I make the public statement."

"Use precise nouns."

"Sabine already cut the adjectives."

"Then you are prepared."

After hanging up, Mina looked at the lab's clean benches and sealed samples. It was easier here than at Maison Arel. Chemistry did not care about founders. It reacted, measured, and reported. People could learn from that if they were willing to lose some romance.

Elias sent one final note after midnight: the lot additive would fluoresce weakly under a specific light. Mina returned to the evidence room and checked the glove, the bottle neck, and the steamer head. The same faint line appeared, a cold green at the edge of blue. It was not dramatic. It was better than dramatic. It was repeatable. Mina photographed it with scale and exposure notes, then turned off the lamp. In darkness, the evidence did not glow. It waited for the right question.

Harrow asked Elias whether the tracer demonstration could be simplified for court. Elias said yes and then produced a diagram so complicated that Oren stared at it like a foreign menu. Mina took a pencil and drew four boxes: bottle, steamer, glove, throat. Elias sighed. "Crude." "Useful," Mina said.

Chapter 43: Service Corridor

Oren traced the service corridor badge to a temporary pass issued to Elise Varo and used twice: once before the contaminated steamer head appeared in dressing room three, once after Anouk collapsed when the old head vanished into the trash. He sent Mina the log with the subject line: DOORS HAVE MEMORIES. She forgave the capitalization because he was right.

The corridor camera had not gone offline. It was merely pointed at the floor because a rolling rack had been parked in front of it. Oren found the rack in storage and matched one wheel to a scuff in the corridor.

"Fashion sabotage by furniture," he said.

"Rack placement," Mina corrected.

"Less catchy."

The footage showed pale trouser hems, white gloves, and the edge of Luc's watch as he passed the corridor entrance. He did not carry the steamer head. He spoke to someone off-camera, then left. Elise entered two minutes later with a garment bag and exited without it.

"Enough?" Oren asked.

"For movement. Not for command."

They found command in Luc's calendar: E.V. corridor before preview, note attached: avoid archive issue before press.

The note might have meant many things in a kinder world. Maison Arel was not currently a kinder world.

Mina added the badge logs and footage to the board. The red lines no longer looked speculative. They looked like stitching pulled tight.

Detective Harrow obtained exterior footage from a delivery entrance across the street. It showed the unidentified courier leaving empty-handed after the collapse, not with the steamer head. The thermal bag had been folded under his arm. He was a BlueArc contractor used for rush deliveries, not a criminal courier. He had delivered the bottle earlier, then been called back by Elise to retrieve "unused display product." He left without it because, according to his statement, no one met him.

"So the bottle stayed," Oren said.

"Where?"

"Good question."

They found it in Luc's filming kit beneath lens cloths, half-used, cap wiped clean but label intact. The lot number matched Elias's additive. Elise's print was on the bottle neck. Luc's was on the outer kit handle, which proved little and irritated him greatly.

"Why keep it?" Oren asked.

"Because people cleaning in panic hide objects in familiar containers."

"That is disappointing. I wanted criminal genius."

"Most criminal genius is poor housekeeping with money."

Mina logged the bottle, the kit, and the footage. The service corridor now held three facts: access, concealment, and recovery. It also showed how easily a working building could be turned against itself. A rack placed six inches left, a pass issued without question, a kit granted investor immunity. No single gesture looked like violence until Anouk's throat closed.

Celia watched the footage once and refused to watch it again.

"I authorized those passes," she said.

"Yes."

"Because Luc said documentation increased investor confidence."

"Yes."

"And I heard confidence as rescue."

Mina closed the laptop. "Now you hear it differently."

"Now I hear a door opening."

That was accurate enough for the board.

Oren replaced the temporary pass policy before anyone asked him to. Investor access now required a staff escort with authority to refuse, not merely accompany. He brought the printed rule to Mina as if expecting a grade. "Too late," he said. "But written." Mina read it. "Add consequence." He did. Pass revoked; incident reported; room cleared. "People need to know what no costs," she said. "And what yes costs," Celia added from the doorway. Oren wrote that sentence at the bottom, not as policy but as reminder.

The service corridor became more legible after that. Oren marked the wall where the rack had blocked the camera, then refused to move the mark until police finished photographs. Staff detoured around it all day. "Looks ridiculous," a cutter said. "Good," Oren answered. "Remember ridiculous." Mina approved. Buildings trained people through inconvenience as much as policy. A blocked camera should leave a bruise in the routine, not merely a line in a report. By evening, someone had taped a handwritten sign beside the mark: no storage in sightline. Oren pretended not to be moved.

Chapter 44: Leverage

Luc admitted to holding the pattern book as leverage during his second interview. He used cleaner words: escrow, asset protection, brand stabilization. Counsel let him have the words until Mina placed Ida's book photograph beside Anouk's medical report.

"One is old theft," Luc said. "One is an unfortunate exposure incident. Do not confuse them."

"You connected them," Mina said.

"I protected investment against family instability."

"You protected your investment by controlling proof that the signature silhouette was stolen."

"I did not poison anyone."

"Did you instruct Elise to prevent Anouk from refusing the gown?"

Luc leaned back. "Models threaten refusal constantly."

"Did you instruct Elise to switch the steamer head?"

"No."

"Did you know BlueArc could be harmful when steamed?"

"I know many products have warnings."

"Did you read this one?"

Silence.

The detective showed him the message: Archive reaction is acceptable narrative.

Luc said, "That was after the fact."

"After you expected an incident?" Mina asked.

His lawyer stopped him there. The room did not need more. Luc had not given a confession. He had given the outline of one and trusted wealth to fill it with fog.

Mina had spent her career reading what people thought fog could hide.

Outside the interview room, Harrow rubbed his eyes. "He will say Elise exceeded instructions."

"Elise will say he minimized danger."

"Minimized is not ordered."

"No. But the messages, safety sheet, insurance clause, payment, bottle, and calendar note build pressure."

"Pressure is good. Juries understand pressure."

"So do employees."

Elise's counsel began negotiating cooperation that afternoon. She admitted coating the replacement head and adjusting the neckline. She insisted Luc told her the compound would cause "irritation sufficient to justify removing the archive gown." She said he described the desired outcome as controlled archive reaction. She said he handed her the bottle in the service corridor and told her Maren would install the head without questions if Elise sounded confident.

"Did he say Anouk might be seriously harmed?" Harrow asked.

"No."

"Did you read the warning?"

"Yes."

"Then you knew more than he said."

Elise cried. "He said warnings are written for lawsuits."

Mina, listening from behind glass, felt no sympathy for the excuse. She felt interest in how neatly it described Luc's world: risk as language to be managed until a body proved it real.

When Luc was told Elise was cooperating, he requested counsel and stopped speaking. That, too, had evidentiary shape. Not guilt by silence. Strategy by silence.

Celia asked Mina later whether Luc would be charged.

"Likely. Proving degree will be difficult."

"Degree."

"Intent, knowledge, recklessness. Law grades harm."

"Anouk does not get graded harm."

"No. She gets all of it."

Celia looked toward the workroom. "So does Ida, in another tense."

Mina did not answer. Some sentences were best left standing.

Harrow later asked Mina why the old theft mattered so much to the new charge. She answered with the board rather than theory. Without Ida's book, Luc had no collateral. Without collateral, no remediation clause mattered. Without the clause, a controlled archive failure lost value. Without value, Elise's order made less sense. "Motive is not decoration," she said. "It is architecture." Harrow copied the word architecture into his notebook and frowned at it. "Fashion case," he said. "I am learning dangerous vocabulary."

Elise's cooperation statement arrived with edits from her lawyer, each sentence trimmed of moral language. Mina read the original notes first. They were messier and more useful. Luc told me to make the archive look fragile. Luc said Celia needed a crisis she could manage. Luc said Anouk was being theatrical. In the formal version, theatrical became resistant. Mina circled both. Translation was evidence when power softened its own verbs. Harrow agreed to preserve drafts. "Lawyers hate drafts," he said. "So do liars," Mina answered.

Luc's interview transcript had another silence Mina liked: six seconds after the word warning. Six seconds was not proof, but it was space where calculation showed. Harrow marked it in the timing log. "Silence is not evidence," he said. "No," Mina replied. "But it tells us where to look for some."

Chapter 45: Insurance Clause

The show insurance clause sat in a rider negotiated by Luc's team. If the preview failed because of criminal interference by investor personnel, coverage was void and the investment agreement could unwind. If the preview failed because an archive garment proved unsafe from age-related degradation, the insurer covered postponement and Luc gained additional licensing control during remediation.

"So Anouk's collapse was supposed to be useful," Celia said.

"Not fatal. Not traceable. Useful."

Mina hated the word.

The BlueArc supplier emails showed Luc's fund had received safety sheets. The sheets warned against aerosolized heat exposure. Elise downloaded them two days before the preview. Luc was copied on a summary: do not use on wearable items.

Mina placed the summary beside Luc's message about acceptable narrative. The gap between warning and action was narrow enough to cut with.

Celia looked at the board. "He needed the gown to fail, not the copy."

"And he needed the failure to look like archive weakness."

"So he could control the archive."

"Yes."

Oren, who had been quiet, said, "He made Anouk into a business condition."

No one improved the sentence. It did not need polish.

Police took the rider, emails, supplier records, badge logs, glove evidence, and steamer report. The case had crossed from house scandal into charges. Mina felt no triumph. Only alignment.

An insurance investigator arrived in a navy suit and asked questions so dry they seemed designed to remove oxygen from fraud. Mina liked her immediately. Her name was Priya Desai, and she read the rider as if it had personally disappointed her.

"This clause is a loaded room," Priya said.

"Loaded how?"

"It incentivizes a non-criminal archive failure over cancellation from provenance dispute. If the archive object becomes unsafe, remediation rights expand. If the heritage claim collapses before show, investment exposure increases. Someone with knowledge of both could profit from a controlled incident."

"Can you write that plainly?"

"I just did."

Oren whispered, "I love insurance now."

"You do not," Mina said.

"I love this insurance."

Priya also found a draft claim notice prepared before the preview. It described possible archive handling interruption and referenced "model sensitivity" as a contingency. The file metadata showed creation three days before Anouk collapsed. Elise had edited it. Luc had viewed it.

Celia sat when she read that.

"They had language ready."

"Yes."

"For her body."

"For the narrative of it."

Anouk chose to read the draft herself. She did not flinch visibly, but she folded the paper with great care afterward.

"Model sensitivity," she said. "Tell them my sensitivity is excellent. I detected poison."

Mina wrote that down nowhere. She remembered it instead.

By evening, the insurer suspended coverage pending criminal investigation. Luc's financial leverage weakened. His lawyers became louder, which Mina took as measurement. In the workroom, staff began speaking in full sentences again. Not cheerful ones. Full ones.

Priya asked to walk the route herself, not for drama but for timing. She carried the draft claim notice in one hand and a stopwatch in the other. From service corridor to dressing room, from dressing room to runway, from runway to investor suite: every interval fit the prepared language too neatly. "Prewritten claims are not crimes," she said. "No," Mina replied. "But they show which accident someone was ready to monetize." Priya stopped at the curtain where Anouk had coughed. "Insurance dislikes imagination after the fact. It dislikes imagination before the fact even more."

The draft claim notice had one line Priya kept returning to: anticipated interruption due to sensitivity of archive presentation. "Anticipated by whom?" she asked. No one answered quickly. Celia read the phrase as if it were a foreign language she had once spoken fluently. "We made sensitivity sound like taste," she said. "Then they used it for harm." Priya folded the page into her file. "Insurers are dull for a reason. We prefer words that do not flirt." Oren whispered that he loved insurance again. This time Mina did not correct him.

Chapter 46: Removing the Book

Luc tried to remove the pattern book during the closed board review by the most boring method available: paperwork. His lawyer presented a custody claim under the investment agreement, arguing the book was collateral and should be held by neutral counsel. The phrase neutral counsel had been typed in bold, as if typography could sanctify possession.

Sabine laughed so sharply that everyone turned.

"Neutral counsel," she said. "The cemetery of living evidence."

Celia refused. Police had already logged the book. Mina had photographed every page. Talia had exported metadata. Jules had copies of Ida's tape. Still, Luc's attempt mattered. It showed he believed possession could still become power.

"The book is subject to criminal evidence hold and provenance dispute," Mina said. "It does not move under private contract."

Luc's lawyer objected. Detective Harrow enjoyed saying no more than Mina expected from a tired man. He said it three times, each better than the last.

During the argument, Elise Varo tried to leave through the side hall. Anouk saw her first.

"That's the glove," Anouk said.

Everyone looked.

Elise froze, one hand on the door, wearing no gloves now. On her right index finger, a healing nick crossed the nail bed. Maren's recovered glove had a tear at the same point.

"She scratched me there," Anouk said. "The glove caught."

Mina had not expected memory to arrive so cleanly. She photographed Elise's finger before anyone could object usefully. Harrow asked Elise to remain. Her lawyer appeared from nowhere with the speed of bad news.

Luc's lawyer stopped talking about neutral custody.

The book stayed on the table.

Afterward, Anouk sat in a small office while Mina took her supplemental statement.

"I did not remember the nick until I saw her hand," Anouk said. "Is that bad?"

"No. Memory often waits for the right object."

"I worried it sounds convenient."

"Convenient for truth is allowed. We document how it emerged."

Anouk nodded. "She was so careful with that stupid glove. Like the button mattered more than my throat."

"People reveal hierarchy in small gestures."

"Did I?"

"You told them to cut the gown if you stopped breathing."

Anouk looked away. "I do not remember that."

"Oren does."

"Good. I am glad one of us was sensible."

In the boardroom, Celia and Sabine signed an interim custody agreement for the pattern book with police hold acknowledged and estate representation pending. Brigitte refused to sign but did not contest. Her power had become narrower, and she knew it.

Luc left through the main hall under police advisement, not yet arrested in the dramatic way television preferred. Elise stayed. The book, photographed and copied, remained open to Ida's crescent draft. It had survived theft, concealment, collateralization, and one last paperwork abduction.

Mina closed the cover gently. Evidence could be handled with care without being worshipped. That, she thought, was the lesson Maison Arel kept failing and learning again.

Sabine stayed after the room emptied and asked to see the page with Ida's wage note. Mina opened it under gloves. The note was small, squeezed between measurements: ask again Friday, no credit without paper. Sabine read it twice. "She knew," she said. "Not all of it. Enough." Celia stood beside her and did not apologize this time. Apologies had been spent. Instead she said, "We will make paper now." Sabine nodded. "Make money too." "Yes." The practical answer pleased Mina more than sorrow would have.

After the custody argument failed, Luc stood by the window and looked down at the service alley where he had first entered like a man inspecting property. Mina saw him see the smokers, the racks, the staff door, all the unpolished infrastructure his renderings omitted. Perhaps he understood then that the building had more witnesses than investors. Perhaps not. Understanding was not required for evidence to work. Harrow asked him to remain available. Luc said, "I am always available through counsel." "Then counsel can find you," Harrow replied.

Chapter 47: The Glove

Anouk identified the glove in a formal procedure the next morning. She did not dramatize. She looked at six pairs laid under glass and pointed to the pale pair with blue residue and the torn right index finger.

"That one."

"How do you know?" the detective asked.

"The seam at the wrist. It had a pearl button. I remember thinking it was stupid for backstage work."

Mina checked. Tiny pearl button, cosmetic brand, style sold in limited sets. Elise had purchased two pairs on Luc's expense account under archive presentation. The receipt was dated four days before the preview. The second pair was found clean in Luc's filming kit.

The tear held a minute trace of Anouk's foundation makeup and skin protein. The neckline scratch held glove fiber. The steamer head held glove fiber. Movement, contact, delivery.

Elise's lawyer advised silence. Elise took it badly.

"Luc said it would make her step aside," she said. "He said the dose was harmless. He said archive reaction, not poison."

"Who prepared the steamer head?" the detective asked.

Elise cried then, not from remorse alone but from the collapse of usefulness. "He gave me the bottle. I did what he asked."

Mina watched through glass. Confessions often arrived dressed as complaints about being misled. The evidence did not care what costume truth wore if it finally stood still.

Harrow asked the necessary questions again and again, changing order, removing sympathy, testing consistency.

"Where did he give you the bottle?"

"Service corridor."

"When?"

"Before Maren changed the head. Around seven."

"What did he say?"

"Use enough that she cannot wear the original if she complains. Make it look like the garment reacted."

"Exact words?"

"He said, 'The archive can be fragile tonight. Let it be fragile.'"

Mina wrote the phrase. It had Luc's shape: deniable, elegant, rotten.

"Did you intend to harm Anouk?"

Elise covered her face. "I intended to make her stop."

"By harming her."

"By irritating her."

"With a product you knew should not be heated or worn."

Elise's answer dissolved. The recorder kept running.

The glove procedure completed the physical chain. Mina met Anouk afterward in the hall. The model looked tired but lighter.

"Is that enough?" Anouk asked.

"For Elise, very likely. For Luc, it helps."

"Helps is a terrible word."

"It is an honest one."

Anouk leaned against the wall. "I thought I would feel something when she said it. Relief, maybe. Instead I keep thinking she believed my refusal was a problem to solve."

"She did."

"And Luc believed her solution was business."

"Yes."

"Then do not let them call it ambition."

"I will not."

Downstairs, reporters waited beyond the front doors. Celia had not yet made the public correction. The house existed in a narrow hour between private proof and public account. Mina knew those hours were dangerous. People tried to bargain inside them. But the glove had named the touch. Bargaining would have to work harder now.

Mina asked Anouk whether she wanted the glove covered before passing the evidence table. Anouk said no. She looked once, long enough to take control of the sight, then turned away. "It is smaller than I made it in my head." "Most tools are," Mina said. "That does not make them harmless." Anouk gave a thin smile. "You should put that on Luc's office wall." "He would frame it badly." The joke was not light, but it let Anouk breathe without instruction.

The second clean pair of gloves mattered more than it first seemed. Elise had not bought a single tool for one careless moment. She had prepared a look: archival, delicate, deniable. Mina placed the clean pair beside the stained pair under glass. The contrast was obscene in its tidiness. Anouk looked at both and said, "Costume." "Yes," Mina said. "For whom?" "For the room," Anouk answered. "So everyone would see care instead of contact." Mina wrote that down. It was witness language, not forensic language, but it explained why the gloves had worked until they did not.

Chapter 48: Public Credit

Celia's public statement was held not on the runway but in the workroom. No models, no music, no dramatic lighting. Behind her stood cutters, stitchers, dressers, archivists, Sabine, Anouk, and the covered indigo gown on its support. The machines were visible but silent. The choice made the room honest in a way the runway could not be.

Reporters crowded behind a tape line. Jules stood among them with his recorder lowered, not wanting to become part of the event he had helped uncover. Brigitte stood in the back, face unreadable. Luc was absent on counsel's advice. Elise was in custody discussions. The space they had occupied felt cleaner without them and not yet repaired.

Celia unfolded one sheet of paper. Sabine had crossed out every decorative word. What remained looked severe.

"Maison Arel's crescent silhouette was created from the work of Ida Sayegh," Celia said. "The house failed to credit and compensate that work. We are correcting the record, placing the disputed pattern book under shared custody with Ida Sayegh's estate representatives, and withdrawing all false provenance claims."

Reporters wrote quickly. Cameras clicked.

Celia continued. "The preview injury to Anouk Saye was not caused by age-related archive degradation. It is under criminal investigation. We will not use archive myth to hide modern harm."

Anouk stood straight. Sabine looked at the floor.

Jules asked whether Maison Arel would survive the admission.

Celia looked around the workroom. "If it does, it will survive as a house where work is named."

It was not a triumphant answer. Mina trusted it more for that.

Another reporter asked whether Brigitte Arel had resigned. Celia's mouth tightened.

"Brigitte Arel has stepped back from archive authority pending governance review. The archive will be overseen by a committee including conservation professionals, staff representatives, and Ida Sayegh's estate counsel."

"Is this an admission of theft?"

"It is an admission of failure to credit and compensate work. Lawyers may use additional words. We are beginning with the true ones."

Sabine glanced up at that.

Afterward, reporters surged toward Anouk. She raised one hand.

"I am not a symbol for your opening paragraph," she said. "I was harmed because people with power treated a body as an obstacle and cloth as an excuse. Use my name correctly, then write about the evidence."

The reporters, for once, obeyed for several seconds.

Sabine touched the covered pattern book. "Ida would have asked about back pay."

"She still can," Celia said.

For the first time, Sabine smiled.

When the room cleared, Brigitte approached the worktable. She did not speak to Celia. She spoke to Sabine.

"I have Ida's thimble," she said.

"I know."

"It should go with the book."

Sabine studied her. "Yes. It should have gone with Ida."

Brigitte bowed her head once, not enough for apology but enough to mark impact. "I will arrange transfer."

"Do not arrange. Bring it."

"Very well."

Mina watched the exchange and thought of how little justice often looked like: not thunder, not repair, but an object returned by a person who had kept it too long.

When Brigitte left to fetch the thimble, the room did not follow her with its eyes the way it once had. Cutters returned to their tables. Oren answered a reporter's question about payroll. Talia corrected a caption before it went live, insisting the photograph showed a draft, not a sketch. These were small shifts, but Mina trusted small shifts when they altered who was allowed to name things. Sabine stood by the covered gown and said, almost to herself, "Now the work begins." Celia heard. "Yes," she said. "The unglamorous part." "The only part that counts."

After the statement, Celia sent the staff home early with pay. Oren argued about schedules until she looked at him. "With pay," she repeated. He stopped arguing. Mina saw several workers check the memo twice, as if generosity might be a typo. Repair, she thought, often began by making the obvious official.

Chapter 49: Evidence and Object

The indigo gown became two things at once: evidence in a criminal case and an archive object in need of care. Mina supervised the separation of samples, the documentation of damage, and the creation of a support mount that did not pretend the garment had not been harmed. Yvonne Kreel returned to advise. Talia worked beside her, slower now, naming every action aloud before making it.

"Sample point one: inner neckline residue area, already documented," Talia said.

"Proceed," Yvonne replied.

No hidden cuts. No erased damage. Future viewers, if permitted, would know what had been taken and why. Mina insisted on that language in the condition report. Damage from investigation was not vandalism if recorded. Concealed intervention was how rooms like this had learned to lie.

Anouk came to see the gown before it moved to secure storage.

"It looks smaller," she said.

"Most myths do when removed from lighting."

The gown's crescent neckline bore tiny sample points, visible only close. Anouk leaned in but did not touch. Her scratch had faded to a pale mark.

"Do you hate it?" Mina asked.

Anouk considered. "No. It did not choose."

That was fairer than most people would be.

Brigitte signed the custody transfer with a hand that shook once. Sabine signed as estate representative. Celia signed for Maison Arel. Talia signed as archivist, her name clear beneath the others. Mina packed the old indigo thread separately from the toxic residue. One belonged to history. One belonged to crime. They shared color, not meaning.

When the evidence van arrived, Oren insisted on carrying nothing. "I have touched enough," he said.

"You can hold the door," Mina said.

"That feels appropriately symbolic and low risk."

He held it.

The van driver checked seals, signatures, and container numbers. The gown went into a climate-controlled crate. The steamer head went into a separate evidence box. The gloves, bottle, latch tape, and residue samples traveled under criminal chain. The pattern book remained under police hold in a secure archive case, copied and witnessed.

After the van left, Oren stood beside Mina in the alley. The pavement smelled of rain and wool. The service entrance looked ordinary again, which annoyed her. Crime scenes often did. They reverted faster than people.

"Blue used to be my favorite," Oren said.

"It will recover."

"Blue?"

"You."

He accepted that without comment.

Inside, Celia had posted a staff memo with names of every worker who contributed to the revised collection records. Not glamorous. Not public yet. But visible where payroll, schedule, and pattern notes lived. Mina saw Maren reading it with one hand over her mouth.

"Do you think they can change?" Oren asked.

"Who?"

"The house."

"Houses do not change. People change procedures and then defend them from nostalgia."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is."

"Worth it?"

Mina looked at the alley where the evidence van had turned out of sight. "Ask Anouk. Ask Sabine. Ask Talia in a year."

Oren nodded. "You never give easy answers."

"You work in couture. You should distrust easy fit."

He smiled, tired but real. For the first time since Mina arrived, the building behind them seemed less like a myth in crisis and more like a workplace after harm: damaged, accountable, not yet safe, but capable of being made safer by unromantic labor.

Before Mina packed her case, Maren brought the old dressing-room checklist and a revised one. The new version required two staff in any archive fitting, toxic-product clearance, camera exceptions signed by production and counsel, and a model's refusal logged as safety information, not attitude. Anouk had added that last phrase in blue ink. Mina signed as reviewer. "This will annoy people," Maren said. "Good procedures often do." "Will it protect everyone?" "No. It gives everyone a tool before fear starts talking." Maren folded the checklist into a clean plastic sleeve. This time, she did not ask someone else to hold it.

Yvonne's final crate report included photographs of the gown on its new mount. She had refused to make it look untouched. The support showed the neckline without forcing it back into fantasy, and the condition notes described both the conservation history and the criminal sampling. Celia read the report and said, "It looks wounded." Yvonne replied, "It is." No one argued. Mina liked that the object had been allowed to keep its injury visible. Repair did not require pretending harm had failed.

Chapter 50: Named Dust

Maison Arel reopened three months later for a small appointment presentation, not a show. No runway. No archive gown. The workroom stayed visible behind glass, but names were printed beside garments: cutters, drapers, embroiderers, pattern researchers. Ida Sayegh's name appeared on the crescent pieces, not as inspiration but as origin.

Brigitte did not attend. Luc Renard awaited trial. Elise Varo cooperated. Talia rebuilt the archive logs under shared oversight and developed a habit of saying no in writing. Anouk returned to work elsewhere and refused every interview that began with trauma or muse. Sabine attended one legal meeting, corrected three lawyers, and sent Celia an invoice for Ida's estate research that made Oren whistle.

Mina came only to deliver the final conservation packet. Celia met her by the pattern shelves.

"The house is smaller now," Celia said.

"Smaller than the lie."

"Yes."

"That may be the correct size."

In the archive, the coffee-stained pattern book rested in a case beside a digital copy available to Sabine, Celia, and the estate lawyers. The label did not say discovered. It did not say attributed. It said Ida Sayegh, crescent bodice draft, working pattern.

Mina approved.

The needle case sat nearby with Ida's thimble, the blue thread tin, and Sabine's explanatory note written in plain language. Brigitte had returned the thimble herself, according to Talia. She had stayed ten minutes, read the new label, and left without correcting punctuation.

The label had been argued over for six drafts. The first said attributed to. Sabine crossed it out. The second said associated with. Talia crossed that out herself. The final card used made by where records supported it, origin where the hand could be proven through pattern, and unknown where vanity would once have invented certainty. It was less smooth than the old labels. It was more useful. Mina approved the unevenness. Truth often had bad line breaks at first.

Celia had also changed the staff contracts. Mina did not review them as evidence, but Sabine did, with a pencil and a face that made lawyers sit straighter. Credits, archive access, design derivation, wage adjustments, dispute procedure: the language was dry, which pleased Mina. Romance had done enough damage. Oren said the new forms would slow production. Celia answered, "Then production will know why it is moving." In the workroom, a cutter pinned a paper name tag to a muslin and did not ask permission first.

Before she left, Talia handed Mina a final archive export on a plain drive. "For redundancy," she said. "And for courage, if that is not too dramatic." Mina took it. "Redundancy is courage with better filing." Talia smiled, briefly and with effort. The archive door closed behind her with a clean mechanical click, not a secretive one.

"Progress," Oren said when he joined them.

"Low bar," Celia replied.

"Still cleared."

The appointment presentation began at noon. Clients walked through slowly, quieter than usual, perhaps because the workroom was visible and the labels left less room for fantasy. A jacket with the crescent line hung on a form. Its card read: Pattern origin Ida Sayegh; design adaptation Celia Arel; cutter Noura Bell; draper Elian Cho; stitch team listed in full. It was not elegant in the old sense. It was better.

Anouk sent a note rather than attend. Celia showed it to Mina. It read: Do not put me on the wall. Pay the people in the room. A.

"I framed a copy in my office," Celia said.

"That may violate the first sentence."

"It faces my desk, not the public."

"Acceptable."

On her way out, Mina passed the dressing rooms. The brass latch from room three had been replaced, but the old one sat bagged in evidence storage, blue dust named at last: old indigo, modern poison, and the residue of people who believed beauty could launder theft.

Outside, the service alley smelled of rain and wool. The smokers were gone. A rack rolled past carrying unfinished coats with paper name tags tied to each hanger. Names fluttered where silence used to hang.

Mina closed her evidence case and walked into the plain afternoon.
