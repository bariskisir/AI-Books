Chapter 1: The Monthly Challenge

The employee of the month plaque hung above the customer service desk, a row of brass name tags on dark wood. Bea Hartley had memorized every name on it over three years. Her own had never appeared there.

When Darius called her into the back office on Tuesday morning, she expected a complaint about the canned goods display. Instead, he slid a single sheet of paper across his cluttered desk. Monthly sales figures. Customer satisfaction scores. Her name and Miles Chen's sat side by side, both at 97.4 percent.

"Never happened before," Darius said, rubbing his temples. "Two people tied. In twelve years."

Bea studied the numbers. Miles Chen. The guy who straightened soup cans by label color. The guy who corrected her pricing in front of customers with the patience of a schoolteacher.

"He'll be insufferable," she said.

Darius shrugged. "Figure it out. Promotion discussions start in a few weeks."

She found Miles in Aisle Three, labeling a clearance section with a precision that bordered on obsessive. His apron was tied tight at the waist, black hair neatly tucked under his cap. He did not look up until she spoke.

"I heard," he said before she could say anything.

"Get over yourself."

"I didn't say anything."

"You were thinking it."

Miles pressed the label gun onto a box of pasta. The click echoed in the quiet aisle. "Ninety-seven point four," he said. "You must have worked hard for that."

"And you didn't?"

He smiled then. The first time she had seen him smile at anything other than a satisfied customer. "I always work hard, Bea. You just never noticed."

She noticed now. The way he held the label gun like a surgical instrument. The way every box sat perfectly aligned. The way he said her name without looking at her.

"I'll beat you next month," she said.

"I'm counting on it."

Bea walked away. Her hands were shaking. Not from anger. Something else, something she tucked away in the corner of her chest and refused to examine.

Chapter 2: The Aisle Six Incident

The woman in the blue coat planted herself at the customer service desk like a general claiming territory. She wanted a refund on a half-eaten bag of organic spinach, and she wanted it with a side of managerial authority.

"There was a slug," the woman announced, her voice carrying across the front of the store. "In the bag. A living creature."

Bea arrived first, smoothing her apron. "Ma'am, I can absolutely process that return for you."

"I don't want a return. I want to speak to a manager."

"I'm the senior floor associate. I can handle—"

"I said a manager."

Miles appeared beside Bea as if he had been there the whole time, waiting for the right moment. "I can take this," he said.

"I have it," Bea said.

"Clearly you don't."

The woman looked between them. "Are you both children?"

Miles stepped forward, his posture shifting into something calm and practiced. "Ma'am, I apologize for the delay. Let me get you a full refund and a gift card for your trouble. The slug situation — completely unacceptable. I will personally inspect our organic produce shipments going forward."

His voice was so steady that the woman deflated. Her shoulders dropped. She nodded and accepted the refund without further argument.

Bea watched him process the transaction, issue the gift card, and walk the woman to the exit. He did not gloat. He did not even look at her.

But in the break room later, he appeared in the doorway as she poured coffee.

"You hesitated," he said.

"I didn't."

"You paused before you said senior floor associate. You don't believe you deserve the title."

Bea's coffee cup stopped halfway to her mouth. "You don't know what I believe."

"I know you undersell yourself." His voice was quiet, not cruel. "It's the only reason we're tied."

He left before she could find a response. She stood alone, the coffee cooling in her hands, and realized he had paid more attention to her in thirty seconds than most people did in a year.

Chapter 3: Darius's Announcement

Darius gathered the entire day staff near the floral section on Thursday morning. The fluorescent lights made everyone look slightly ill, casting a pale green tint across the potted mums and the assembled employees.

"Corporate approved a new assistant manager position," Darius said. "One slot. Six weeks from now."

The staff murmured. Bea's pulse quickened. She gripped her notepad tighter.

"The position will be filled internally. I'll be evaluating candidates based on performance, leadership, and customer feedback over the next six weeks. Formal interviews at the end."

Bea scanned the crowd. Miles stood near the deli counter, arms crossed, his face unreadable. But when she looked at him, his eyes found hers and held.

"The two strongest candidates," Darius said, "are Bea Hartley and Miles Chen."

Mrs. Kowalski let out a low whistle from register four. "A fight, then."

Darius ignored her. "I expect professionalism. No sabotage. No games. May the best person win."

After the meeting, Bea found herself in the produce section, staring at a pyramid of Gala apples without seeing them. She did not hear Miles approach until he was beside her.

"Six weeks," he said.

"Six weeks."

"May the best person win."

"Your face when he said that — you looked scared."

Miles picked up an apple, turned it over in his palm. "I'm not scared of you, Bea."

"Then what are you scared of?"

He set the apple down, rotating it until it aligned perfectly with the others. "Losing to someone who wants this as much as I do. Because if I lose to you, I won't be able to pretend it didn't matter."

He walked away. Bea stayed among the apples, the refrigerated air raising goosebumps along her arms.

Chapter 4: The Scoreboard

Bea started keeping score on a notepad she kept in her locker. She drew a line down the center of each page and wrote the date at the top. Bea on the left. Miles on the right.

Day one: Miles helped an elderly woman load groceries into her car, carefully arranging bags so nothing would tip. Point to Miles. Bea caught a pricing error on the chicken display before any customer could complain, saving the store a potential violation. Point to Bea.

By day three, the tally was seven to six in her favor. She reviewed her entries each night, searching for patterns in his wins and her losses.

She did not know Miles kept his own score until she found a folded receipt on the break room floor. On the back, in his small, precise handwriting: Bea 4, Miles 6. Different metrics. Different judgment. He credited her for things she had not thought to count — a customer she had helped without being asked, a spill she had cleaned before anyone noticed. He tracked a different game entirely.

She slipped the receipt into her pocket. That night, she added a new category to her notepad: "Things Miles Notices."

She wrote: He corrects me because he thinks I can do better. He smiles at customers like he is performing a duty. He never eats lunch in the break room. He smells like ginger and soap.

She stared at the last line. Then she ripped the page out, crumpled it, and threw it in the trash.

The next morning, Miles had already restocked the dairy section before she arrived. Her section. A note was taped to the milk cooler: "You were busy. I had time. — M."

She wanted to be angry. She was not angry. That was the problem.

Chapter 5: Mrs. Kowalski's Bet

Mrs. Kowalski stood at register four with a clipboard, which was unusual because the store did not use clipboards. She was sixty-three, with silver hair pinned into a tight bun and reading glasses hanging from a chain around her neck. She had worked at Green Valley Grocery for twenty-two years and feared no one.

"Twenty-two people so far," she said when Bea walked past. "Mostly on you."

"What are you talking about?"

"The pool. Betting pool on the promotion. House takes ten percent. Winner gets six hundred dollars."

Bea grabbed the clipboard. Names filled the page. Dollar amounts in careful columns. Her name appeared on most lines. Miles appeared on the rest.

"You can't do this," Bea said.

"I can, and I have. It's not gambling if there's no money changing hands on store property. The money lives in my trunk."

"That's somehow worse."

Mrs. Kowalski shrugged. "You're the favorite. People like you better. But Miles has a quiet thing going. The older ladies trust him."

"They shouldn't."

"Why? Because he's tidy? Because he remembers every customer's name and asks about their grandchildren?" Mrs. Kowalski leaned across the conveyor belt. "Or because he got under your skin?"

"He hasn't gotten under my skin."

"Sure, honey. That's why you've been cutting your lunch break short to re-stock his section. Very professional behavior."

Bea walked away, but Mrs. Kowalski's laugh followed her all the way to the back room. She told herself the woman was just stirring trouble. She almost believed it.

Chapter 6: The Spoiled Shipment

The refrigerated truck arrived at 6 a.m., three hours late, with its cooling unit broken. Bea found the driver in the loading bay, sweating through his uniform, gesturing at pallets of dairy products that were warming by the minute.

"Two hours ago," the driver said. "Temp's been climbing since."

Bea did not wait for instructions. She called the floor staff, divided them into teams, and organized a triage system. Yogurt and cheese went to the store's backup coolers. Milk was moved directly to the dairy section. Anything borderline got marked down and pushed to the floor for immediate sale.

She worked for three hours without stopping, directing traffic, checking temperatures, making judgment calls on what could be saved. By the time Darius arrived, the salvage operation was finished. The only loss was one pallet of premium ice cream, already melted into sweet soup.

"Impressive," Darius said, surveying the organized chaos.

"Just doing my job."

"It's above your job description, Bea. That's the point."

She felt the victory warm in her chest. Then she saw Miles watching from the doorway of the back office. He did not approach. He just watched, taking in her process, her decisions, her leadership. His face gave nothing away.

He nodded once. Acknowledgment. Respect.

She nodded back.

That night, she updated her notepad. "Miles watched the whole thing. Did not offer to help. Did not need to. He was cataloging everything I did. Learning."

She underlined the last word twice.

Chapter 7: Miles Counters

Bea arrived the next morning to find the back room unrecognizable.

Shelves that had been chaotic for years were organized by category, then by expiration date. Labels faced forward in perfect rows. The floor had been mopped. Even the light seemed brighter, reflecting off clean surfaces.

Miles stood near the dry goods section, placing a box of canned tomatoes onto a shelf. He wore the same calm expression he always wore, as if he had not just transformed the entire workspace.

"You did this," Bea said.

"Someone had to."

"Last night. You stayed late."

"Midnight to six. Quiet time. No interruptions."

She walked through the transformed space, running her fingers along the newly organized shelves. It was better than her salvage operation. More permanent. More visible. Darius would see it the moment he walked in.

"The tomatoes go on the lower shelf," she said. "Heavier items lower. Safety code."

"I know."

"You did it anyway."

Miles set down the box and turned to face her. "I put them there on purpose. The lower shelf has a tilt. Tomatoes roll off. Then someone has to restock them."

"You created a problem."

"I created job security." He almost smiled. "Also, I wanted to see if you'd notice."

She noticed. She noticed everything about this room now. About him. About the way his mind worked three moves ahead while she only thought about the next one.

She went to her locker and updated her notepad. "Miles reorganizes the back room. He thinks in chess moves. I think in sprints. He will win unless I learn to think like him."

She closed the notepad, wondering when this had stopped being about the promotion.

Chapter 8: Sabotage or Coincidence

Bea's seasonal display collapsed at 10 a.m. on Saturday.

One moment it stood tall — a pyramid of pumpkin-spiced products arranged in a harvest theme she had spent three hours designing. The next moment, it was a heap of orange and brown on the floor. A customer stood nearby, holding a box of granola, her face apologetic.

"I barely touched it," the woman said.

Bea knelt among the ruins, her knees pressing into scattered boxes. The base had shifted. Someone had moved the support boxes. Not a customer — the display was in an interior aisle, away from high traffic.

She scanned the store. Miles stood at register two, processing a transaction. He did not look at her.

She rebuilt the display, stacking each box with deliberate care. She did not accuse anyone. But at the end of her shift, she found a receipt in her locker. On the back, in Miles's handwriting: "It wasn't me. But I know who it was. Carl from dairy. He has a crush on you and thought he was helping by rearranging things."

Bea stared at the note. Then she laughed. She laughed until her stomach hurt, sitting on the bench in the locker room, the crumpled receipt in her fist.

She wrote that night: "Miles cleared Carl. I believe him. This means I trust him. When did that happen?"

She did not have an answer. But the question stayed with her through the weekend, persistent and warm.

Chapter 9: The Night Shift Mandate

Darius called them both into his office on Monday afternoon. He looked exhausted — his tie loosened, coffee rings marking his desk in overlapping brown circles. The wastebasket was overflowing.

"I have a problem," he said. "Night crew is down two people. Flu season. I need coverage for two weeks."

Bea and Miles exchanged glances across the cramped office.

"You're putting us on graveyard together," Miles said.

"Ten at night to six in the morning. Two weeks. Together."

"Together," Bea repeated. The word felt strange in her mouth, heavy with implications she did not want to examine.

"The store needs stocking. Inventory needs updating. You're my best people. Figure out how to work together or don't. I don't care. The store gets done either way."

"This is a test," Miles said.

Darius leaned back in his chair. "Everything is a test. You wanted a fair competition? Here it is. Same shift. Same conditions. No audience."

Bea opened her mouth to argue. She closed it. Because Darius was right. And because some part of her — a part she refused to acknowledge — was not entirely unhappy about the arrangement.

"Fine," she said.

Miles nodded. "Fine."

"Great." Darius waved them out. "First night is tomorrow. Don't kill each other. The paperwork is terrible."

Chapter 10: First Night

The store at midnight was a different world.

The fluorescent lights hummed at a lower frequency, a deeper note that vibrated in the ribs. The registers sat silent, their screens dark. The air smelled of bleach and floor wax instead of coffee and produce. Everything felt quieter, slower, like the building itself had settled into sleep.

Bea arrived at five to ten. Miles was already there, standing in the middle of Aisle Five with a clipboard. The overhead lights caught the sharp angles of his face, the careful set of his jaw.

"I've divided the work," he said. "Dry goods and frozen for me. Produce and dairy for you. Customer-facing restocking in the first four hours. Inventory audits in the second half."

"You planned this."

"I made a schedule."

She took the clipboard. His handwriting was small and precise. The schedule accounted for every hour, every task, every break. It was annoyingly perfect.

"Fine," she said. "But I'm not doing your frozen section when you fall behind."

"I won't fall behind."

He did not. Neither did she. They worked in silence for three hours, the only sounds the squeak of cart wheels and the thud of boxes hitting shelves. The quiet felt like a blanket pressing down.

At 1 a.m., Bea caught herself watching him from across the store. He was stacking cans of soup, his movements economical and practiced. He did not waste motion. He did not waste words.

"Stop staring," she muttered, and forced herself back to work.

But the silence pressed against her, and she realized she had never been alone in a building with someone she disliked this much — or wanted to understand this badly.

Chapter 11: The Coffee Truce

At 3 a.m., Bea's body hit a wall. Her eyes burned. Her arms ached. Every muscle complained. The coffee from the break room was hours old and tasted like burned regret mixed with dish soap.

Miles appeared in the doorway holding two cups, steam rising from both. The sleeves of his shirt were rolled up, revealing forearms dusted with flour from the bakery section.

"I made fresh," he said.

"I don't need your charity."

"It's coffee, Bea. Not a blood pact."

She took the cup. He sat across from her at the small break room table, the fluorescent light buzzing overhead. They drank in silence for a full minute.

"This is terrible," she said.

"It's the only thing open. The good coffee is in the morning."

"So you admit there's good coffee and you hide it from the night shift."

"I admit I bought good coffee for myself and you just drank the last of it."

She stared at him. He stared back. Then she laughed — a short, exhausted sound that surprised her.

"You're a terrible person," she said.

"I know."

"How do you make it through these shifts without sleeping?"

Miles looked into his cup. "I don't sleep much anyway. Haven't for a while."

"Why?"

He did not answer. The silence stretched, comfortable now instead of hostile. Bea did not push. She had her own reasons for sleeplessness, and she understood when someone was not ready to share.

"Thank you," she said finally. "For the coffee."

He nodded. Something shifted between them — small, almost imperceptible. Like the first crack in a wall neither had realized they were building.

Chapter 12: The Quiet Hours

The man came in at 4 a.m. every night.

He wore a canvas coat and moved with the careful deliberation of someone who had all the time in the world and not nearly enough of it. He bought the same things every time: a single banana and a cup of coffee from the self-serve station.

Bea noticed him on night three. "Who is that?"

Miles did not look up from his inventory sheet. "Lawrence. Walks from the assisted living facility on Grove Street. Comes here because he can't sleep and the walls in his room are too thin."

"How do you know that?"

"I asked him. Night two of my first week."

Lawrence shuffled to the register, his hands trembling slightly. Bea rang him up. The banana was seventeen cents. The coffee was a dollar fifty-nine.

"My wife used to make coffee," Lawrence said as he counted out coins with shaking fingers. "Better than this. But she's gone now."

Bea did not know what to say. The silence felt inadequate. Miles appeared beside her.

"One seventy-six," he said gently. "You're all set, Lawrence."

Lawrence paid with exact change and carried his purchases to the small cafe table near the window. He sat facing the parking lot and ate his banana in tiny, careful bites. The lot was empty, the streetlights casting orange pools on the asphalt.

"He comes every night," Miles said quietly. "Stays until the sun comes up. Then he walks home."

"How do you know all this?"

"Because I was here. Because I talked to him." He looked at her. "There's a whole world that happens in these hours, Bea. People you never see during the day. Stories you never hear."

Bea watched Lawrence sip his coffee, staring out at the dark parking lot. She had worked at Green Valley Grocery for three years and had never seen him before this week. The thought bothered her more than she expected.

Chapter 13: Promotion Interview Date

Darius sent a store-wide email on Friday morning. Formal interviews for the assistant manager position would be held in three weeks. Each candidate would prepare a five-minute presentation on their vision for the store's future.

Bea read the email three times, standing at the customer service desk. Three weeks. Twenty-one days. Everything she had worked for came down to twenty-one days, and the countdown had started.

She found Miles in the break room, reading the same email on his phone. The coffee machine gurgled behind him, the only sound in the room.

"Three weeks," she said.

"I can count."

"You scared?"

He set down his phone. "Terrified." He said it flatly, without irony. "But I've wanted this for two years. I'm not stopping now."

"Neither am I."

"Good."

He held her gaze a moment longer than necessary. Bea felt something catch in her chest — a thread pulling tight where she had not known there was loose fabric. She turned away first.

That night, during their shift, they worked side by side in the cereal aisle without speaking. Their hands brushed once, reaching for the same box of granola. Both pulled back. Neither apologized.

The contact lasted less than a second. Bea replayed it for the rest of the night.

Chapter 14: Stocking in Silence

Bea heard it at 2 a.m. — a voice, low and melodic, drifting from the frozen foods section.

She rounded the corner slowly, her cart silent on the polished floor. Miles had his back to her, stacking boxes of frozen vegetables. He was singing. Softly, under his breath, a song she almost recognized. Something about rain and waiting and a train that never arrived.

She stood still and listened. His voice was rough but true, carrying the melody with an ease she had never seen in his waking movements. The freezer door fogged with each breath.

He stopped when he sensed her. His shoulders tensed.

"You sing," she said.

"It's a nervous habit."

"It's good."

He did not turn around. "It's nothing."

"It's not nothing."

Miles set down a box of peas. "My mother used to sing while she cooked. I picked it up. Didn't realize I was doing it."

"My mother never sang," Bea said. "She worked double shifts and came home too tired to hum."

He turned. Their eyes met over a frost-crusted freezer case. The cold air curled between them.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Don't be. She got me through school. That's her song, in a way."

They worked in silence after that. But when Bea passed the frozen section an hour later, she heard him singing again — the same melody, a little louder now. She smiled in the dark and did not let him see.

Chapter 15: The Broken Pallet Jack

The pallet jack died at 3:30 a.m. in the middle of the back room, loaded with two hundred pounds of rice bags. The lever jammed. The wheels locked. The load sat there, immovable, blocking the entire aisle like a fallen tree.

Bea tried everything. She kicked the lever. She checked the release valve. She sweated and cursed in the dim light of the loading bay.

Miles appeared beside her. "Let me see."

"I have it."

"You don't."

"I said I have it."

He crouched, ignoring her, and examined the undercarriage. His fingers traced the hydraulic line, the release mechanism. "The valve is jammed. I can fix it, but I need to get underneath."

"Absolutely not."

"Do you have a better idea?"

She did not. So she held the pallet steady while Miles lay flat on the concrete floor, reaching into the greased machinery. He cursed softly as his fingers found the jam. She held her breath, feeling the weight of the rice above him.

"If this falls," she said, "you'll lose your arm."

"Then don't drop it."

"You're insane."

"I'm a chef. We're all insane."

He worked for ten minutes, his arm buried in the mechanism. Finally, the jack released with a hiss of hydraulic fluid. He slid out, shirt covered in grease, a fresh scratch on his forearm.

"Fixed," he said.

Bea handed him a rag. "You were a chef?"

"I was. Am. Not anymore."

"Why not?"

He wiped his hands. The rag came away black with grease. "My father got sick. I came home. Some things you don't go back to."

She saw something in his face then — a door cracked open, just wide enough to glimpse the room beyond. She did not walk through it. Not yet. But she noted the crack and filed it away.

Chapter 16: Personal History

They sat in the break room at 4 a.m., eating stale pretzels from the vending machine. The clock ticked. The fluorescent light buzzed. The pretzels were soft from humidity.

"I dropped out of art school," Bea said.

Miles looked at her. "Why?"

"Money. My dad left when I was twelve. Mom worked two jobs. My brother was still in elementary school. Art school was a luxury we couldn't afford."

"Did you want to be an artist?"

She considered the question. The pretzel in her hand was limp. "I wanted to make things. Beautiful things. Instead, I make grocery displays."

"That's also making things."

"It's not the same."

"It's the same muscle." Miles leaned back in his chair. "I wanted to be a chef. Cooking was the only thing that quieted my head. Chopping vegetables. Timing sauces. It's meditation with knives."

"When did you stop?"

"When I realized my father was living alone, forgetting to eat, forgetting to pay the electric bill. I couldn't cook fancy food in a city three hours away while he survived on crackers and canned soup."

"So you came home."

"So I came home." He met her eyes. "And now I'm here, stacking cans, competing for a job I don't even want."

Bea blinked. "You don't want the promotion?"

"I want stability. I want to afford his medication. The promotion gives me that." He paused. "I never wanted to run a grocery store. I never did."

She sat with that for a moment. The pretzels tasted like cardboard in her mouth.

"Why are you telling me this?" she asked.

"Because you asked. And because you're the only person here who would understand."

Chapter 17: The Midnight Regular

The woman appeared at 2 a.m. on the second Friday. She was small and silver-haired, wearing a floral housecoat over her pajamas, her feet in worn slippers. She carried a woven market basket that looked older than Bea.

"Miles," she said, her voice carrying across the empty store.

Miles looked up from the dairy cooler. "Popo?"

His grandmother shuffled toward him, slippers scraping the linoleum. "I need lotus seed paste. And a good mooncake mold. You know where they keep them?"

"Popo, it's two in the morning."

"The moon festival is next week. You think the ingredients wait for reasonable hours?"

Bea watched from the end of the aisle. Miles's shoulders dropped. He looked younger suddenly, the tightness in his jaw dissolving into something soft.

"I'll help you find it," he said.

His grandmother noticed Bea. Her eyes narrowed with interest. "Who is this? She's pretty. Why haven't you brought her to dinner?"

"Popo, this is Bea. My coworker."

"Just coworker?" The old woman studied Bea with undisguised curiosity.

"Just coworker," Bea confirmed.

"Then why is he blushing, girl?"

Miles was not blushing. But his ears had gone red. He guided his grandmother toward the international foods aisle, muttering something in what Bea guessed was Cantonese.

She watched them go — the old woman's hand on his arm, the way he bent his head to listen to her, the way he laughed at something she said. She had never seen him laugh before. It transformed his face entirely.

She stood in the quiet aisle and realized she wanted to hear that sound again.

Chapter 18: Bea's Defense

Bea's brother called at 5 p.m., just before her shift started. She took the call in the break room, her back against the wall, her free hand pressed to her forehead.

"Tuition is due next week," Leo said. "I need the second half."

"I know. I have it."

"Bea, if you don't have it, just tell me. I can take a semester off. Work for a while."

"No. Absolutely not. You're finishing in four years. That was the deal."

"We didn't make a deal. You decided."

"Because I'm the one paying."

She hung up before he could argue. When she turned, Miles was standing in the doorway. She did not know how long he had been there, his expression unreadable in the dim light.

"Tuition," he said.

"It's nothing."

"Bea."

"My brother. He's at State. I told my mother I'd get him through. She worked double shifts for me. I owe her."

"You don't owe anyone."

"I owe everyone." She grabbed her apron from the hook. "That's why I need this promotion. That's why I can't lose to you."

Miles stepped aside to let her pass. She felt his eyes on her back as she walked into the bright store.

"Bea," he called.

She stopped. The store noise washed over her — beeps, chatter, the deli slicer.

"Your brother is lucky."

She did not turn around. She did not let him see her face. But the words followed her through the stacks of canned goods and into the fluorescent glare of the store floor.

Chapter 19: Miles's Admission

They were in the stockroom, counting paper goods by hand. The clock read 2:17 a.m. The only light came from the single bulb above the loading bay door, casting long shadows across the boxes.

"My father has early-onset Parkinson's," Miles said.

Bea's hand stopped mid-reach for a box of napkins. "I didn't know."

"Nobody does. He was diagnosed two years ago. He can still walk, mostly. But his hands shake. He drops things. He forgets words."

"How bad is it?"

"Bad enough that he can't work. Bad enough that the medication costs more than my rent." He set down his clipboard. "My mother died when I was nineteen. It's just us now."

"Why are you telling me?"

"Because you told me about your brother. And because I'm tired of pretending this is just a competition for a job." He looked at her, and for once, there was no barrier in his eyes. "It's not a job to either of us. It's survival."

Bea nodded slowly. "So what do we do?"

"Keep competing. Keep pretending. What else is there?"

She did not have an answer. They finished the inventory in silence, side by side, two people who understood each other perfectly and had no way to say it.

When they were done, Miles held the door for her. She walked past him into the darkened store, close enough to catch the scent of ginger and soap.

Chapter 20: The Fixed Pallet Jack

The pallet jack broke again on Wednesday. Same one. Same jammed release valve.

This time, Miles handed Bea the tools.

"Your turn," he said.

"I don't know how."

"I'll teach you."

He walked her through it — the release valve, the hydraulic seal, the angle to approach from underneath. She lay on the concrete floor, her arm deep in the grease, and felt his hand guide her wrist.

"Gently," he said. "It's not a fight."

"It feels like one."

"That's your problem, Bea. You fight everything. Even things that want to be fixed."

She turned the valve. The mechanism released with a soft hiss. She slid out from under the pallet, covered in grease, her hair a tangled mess. A smear of oil ran across her cheek.

"You did it," he said.

"We did it."

He smiled. Full. Real. It changed his entire face — the sharp lines softened, the careful composure fell away. He looked like someone she had never met.

Bea felt something crack in her chest. A door she had been holding shut. A lock she had not known the combination to.

"The display," she said quickly. "I need to finish the display."

She walked away. But his smile followed her for the rest of the night, burning at the edges of her thoughts.

Chapter 21: Darius Spies

Darius arrived at 4 a.m. without warning. No phone call. No footsteps on the approach. He simply appeared in the break room doorway.

He found them both asleep. Bea was slumped in a plastic chair, her head tilted back, her mouth slightly open. Miles was on the floor, his back against the wall, his chin dropped to his chest. The coffee pot sat half-full on the counter. An open bag of pretzels rested between them on the table.

Darius stood in the doorway for a long moment, taking in the scene. Then he pulled out his phone and took a picture.

Miles woke first, his eyes snapping open. "Darius."

"I don't know what I'm looking at," Darius said.

"We were resting."

"You were unconscious."

Bea stirred. She saw Darius and straightened so fast the chair nearly tipped. Her neck cracked as she turned.

"We were on break," she said.

"For how long?"

Miles checked his watch. "Twenty minutes."

"You're both lucky I came for coffee, not a report." Darius poured himself a cup from the pot. "But this is interesting."

"What is?" Bea asked.

"You. Together. Not killing each other."

Bea and Miles exchanged a glance. Darius drank his coffee, watching them over the rim of the cup. He smiled on his way out — a small, satisfied curve that said more than any speech.

"Sleep well," he called over his shoulder. "You're going to need it."

Chapter 22: Everyone Notices

Mrs. Kowalski cornered Bea at the start of her first day shift after the night rotation, blocking the break room door with her body and a shopping cart.

"You look terrible," Mrs. Kowalski said. "And also different."

"I'm tired."

"It's not tired. It's something else."

Bea tried to sidestep. Mrs. Kowalski shifted to block her.

"You two are getting along," Mrs. Kowalski said. "I have eyes. I have sources."

"Your sources are wrong."

"My sources are the security cameras. Carl showed me the footage. You smiled at each other three times."

"That's not—"

"And you fixed a pallet jack together. Carl says that's romantic."

"Fixing equipment is not romantic."

"It is when you're both covered in grease and making eye contact." Mrs. Kowalski crossed her arms. "The pool is changing. People are betting on different outcomes now."

"What outcomes?"

"The kind that don't involve a promotion."

Bea felt heat rise to her cheeks. She pushed past Mrs. Kowalski and walked into the store. Carl from dairy waved at her from the cheese section, a hopeful look on his face. She did not wave back.

But she could not stop thinking about what Mrs. Kowalski had said. Different outcomes. The phrase followed her through the morning like a radio playing in another room, persistent and impossible to ignore.

Chapter 23: The Team-Up

The health inspector arrived at 11 p.m. on a Tuesday. She was a short woman in a navy suit, carrying a clipboard and wearing glasses designed to intimidate. Her shoes clicked on the linoleum with authority.

Darius was not there. The night crew manager had called in sick. The store sat half-stocked, cardboard boxes stacked on the floor like a maze.

"I'll need to see temperature logs, sanitation records, and the back room handling procedures," the inspector said.

Bea and Miles looked at each other. No time to panic. No time to strategize.

"I can get the logs," Bea said.

"I'll handle the back room," Miles said.

They moved. Bea sprinted to the office and pulled temperature logs from the filing cabinet, organized by month, flagged for every variance. Miles cleared the back room in twelve minutes flat — boxes to proper storage, surfaces wiped, handwashing stations stocked.

They met at the customer service desk. Bea handed Miles the logs. He handed her a completed checklist.

"Walk-in cooler is at 38," he said. "Freezer at zero. Dairy is cold-chain compliant."

"Logs are consistent for the last three months. I highlighted the variance from last week — that was the broken truck, corrected within four hours."

The inspector reviewed the materials in silence. She tested the fish counter with a thermometer. She checked the sanitation logs. She walked the back room with a flashlight, peering into corners.

When she was done, she nodded once. "Everything looks good. I'll file the report."

After she left, Bea leaned against the customer service desk, her heart pounding.

"We passed," she said.

"She didn't even write a citation."

"We did that."

"We did that together."

They stood in the empty store, breathing hard. Miles held out his hand. She shook it. His palm was warm. Neither let go first.

Chapter 24: Inspector's Verdict

Darius received the inspection report by email the next morning. He printed it out, walked to the break room, and taped it to the bulletin board over the coffee-stained sign-up sheet.

"Perfect score," he announced to the room. "First time in three years."

The day staff gathered around. Mrs. Kowalski let out a low whistle. Carl from dairy peered over someone's shoulder.

"Who was on duty?" someone asked.

"Bea Hartley and Miles Chen," Darius said. "Night shift."

The room went quiet. Heads turned. Bea stood at the back, holding a cup of coffee. Miles was at the front, pretending to read the posted report.

"They worked together," Darius said. "Seamlessly. No supervision needed."

"Our displays were good," Bea offered.

"The health inspector doesn't care about displays. She cares about procedure. You two covered every gap."

Miles finally looked up. "We split the work. It was efficient."

"It was more than efficient." Darius studied them both. "It was coordinated. Like you'd been doing it for years."

Bea felt Miles's eyes on her. She did not look back.

But at the end of her shift, she found a note tucked into her locker. "We make a decent team. — M."

She folded the paper carefully and slid it into her wallet, next to the receipt she had saved from weeks ago.

Chapter 25: The End of Night Shift

Their last graveyard shift arrived on a Friday night. The store was quiet. The world outside the windows was dark and still. Even the streetlights seemed dimmer.

Bea stocked the soup aisle slowly, deliberately. She caught herself reading labels, straightening cans that were already straight, wiping dust that was barely visible. She was drawing it out, stretching every task to its limit.

Miles appeared at the end of the aisle. "Last night."

"I know."

"Back to days on Monday."

"I know."

They stood there, the fluorescent lights humming above them. The freezer cases kicked on and off in their endless cycle.

"I won't miss the coffee," Bea said.

"I won't miss your snoring."

"I don't snore."

"You do. Around 4 a.m. It's a small sound. Almost cute."

"Cute?"

He shrugged. His mouth twitched at the corner.

"Don't call me cute," she said.

"I won't. Not to your face."

They worked until dawn. When the first light crept through the front windows, they stood at the entrance together, watching the sun paint the parking lot in shades of pink and gold. The asphalt gleamed with morning dew.

"Goodnight, Miles," she said.

"Good morning," he said.

She walked to her car. She did not look back. But she wanted to — and that wanting felt like a secret too heavy to keep.

Chapter 26: Back to Day Shift

Bea's first day shift in two weeks felt like a foreign country.

The store was bright and loud and full of people. Registers beeped in constant rhythm. The deli slicer whined. Customers interrupted her with questions she had answered a thousand times. The noise pressed against her from all sides.

Miles was three aisles away, helping a woman find baking soda. He smiled at the customer. He did not look at Bea.

At 2 p.m., she caught herself walking toward the frozen section for no reason. She stopped mid-aisle, turned around, and forced herself back to her assigned area. The frozen section was just an aisle. It meant nothing.

During her break, she sat alone in the break room. The chair across from her was empty. She stared at it, remembering the nights she had sat across from Miles, sharing bad coffee and worse pretzels.

You're being ridiculous, she told herself. It was two weeks. It was nothing.

But when Miles walked in, saw her, and said nothing — when he grabbed his lunch from the fridge and walked back out — she felt the absence like a physical weight in her chest.

She pulled out her notepad. The scoreboard was still there, but she had stopped updating it days ago. She turned to a fresh page.

"Day shift feels wrong. Miles will not look at me. I do not know why it matters. But it does."

She read the words back. They sounded like a confession.

Chapter 27: First Silence

Three days passed.

They worked the same store. They passed each other in the same aisles. They did not speak.

Not because they were fighting. There was nothing to fight about anymore. The silence was worse — it was intentional, a wall they had both rebuilt, brick by brick, each of them carrying mortar.

On Wednesday, Bea restocked the coffee section and found the canister of good coffee Miles had mentioned during the night shift. She stared at it, her hand hovering. He had told her about it weeks ago, in the dark hours of the morning, when the world felt smaller and easier.

She bought it anyway. She made a cup in the break room. It was good. Rich and smooth, nothing like the burned sludge they usually drank.

She left a second cup on the break room table with a note: "You were right. It is better."

She did not stay to see if he drank it. She walked away before she could watch him ignore it.

But the next morning, the cup was gone. The note was gone. And a new note sat in its place, written in his small, precise hand: "Told you. — M."

She smiled before she could stop herself.

Chapter 28: The Interlude

Miles brought her coffee on Thursday. He set it on the customer service desk where she was working and walked away without a word. No explanation. No greeting. Just the cup and the steam rising from the lid.

Bea stared at it for a long moment. She opened the lid. Black, one sugar. Exactly how she took it.

She found him in Aisle Five, adjusting a display of canned beans. He did not look up when she approached.

"How did you know how I take my coffee?"

"I paid attention."

"During the night shift."

"Yes."

"You were paying attention to how I take my coffee?"

Miles straightened a can, aligning it with its neighbors. "I was paying attention to everything."

Bea did not know what to say. She held the warm cup in both hands, letting the heat seep into her fingers.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't mention it."

"I mean it."

He looked at her then. Really looked — his eyes meeting hers, holding. "I know."

She walked back to the customer service desk. The coffee was perfect. She drank every drop, and when she was done, she saved the cup on the shelf under the register.

Chapter 29: Store Gossip

Mrs. Kowalski had never been subtle about anything in her life. She treated discretion as a personal insult.

"He brought you coffee," she announced from register four.

Bea was at register three, three customers deep. The store was busy. Everyone within thirty feet could hear.

"It's not a story," Bea said.

"He brought you coffee and you didn't throw it at him. That's a story."

The customer at Bea's register laughed. Bea felt her face burn.

"Miles Chen," Mrs. Kowalski announced to the entire front end, "brings coffee to Bea Hartley. Black with one sugar. She drinks it. My betting pool is changing as we speak."

"Mrs. Kowalski—"

"I'm not saying anything. I'm observing. It's a cashier's right to observe."

Miles appeared from the stockroom, a box of cereal in his arms. His ears were red.

"Mrs. Kowalski," he said, "don't you have customers?"

"I have plenty. I also have a retirement fund riding on you two."

"Please stop."

"I'll stop when there's something worth stopping for."

She winked at them, broad and theatrical. The customer at her register laughed. Bea wanted the floor to open and swallow her whole.

Miles caught her eye. He looked mortified. But he was also fighting a smile.

She fought one too. She lost.

Chapter 30: The Challenge Resumes

Darius called them both into his office on Friday morning. The coffee rings on his desk had multiplied into a brown galaxy.

"Two weeks until the interview," he said. "I've added a practical component."

Bea's stomach dropped. Miles shifted in his chair, the wood creaking under him.

"Themed displays. One per candidate. Same budget, same time frame, same resources. I'll judge based on creativity, execution, and customer response."

"When?" Miles asked.

"Displays go up two days before interviews. One week to plan, one week to execute."

Bea nodded. Displays were her strength. She could win this if she stayed focused.

"What are the constraints?" Miles asked.

Darius smiled. "No constraints. That's the point. Show me what you can do when there are no rules."

They left the office together. The hallway felt narrow, the walls closer than they had been before. The fluorescent light flickered above them.

"Good luck," Miles said.

"You too."

They meant it. That was the strangest part — the sincerity in his voice, the absence of competition between the words. They were still rivals. But something had shifted underneath.

Chapter 31: Miles's Idea

Miles found Bea in the break room, sketching display concepts on a napkin. The napkin was damp from condensation, the pen bleeding into the paper.

"A World Foods display," he said.

She looked up. "What?"

"For my display. Not the usual international aisle — something curated. Dishes from different cultures, ingredients arranged by recipe. A cookbook display attached."

Bea studied his napkin. The sketch was crude but the concept was clear. Ambitious. Original. "That's actually good."

"Thank you."

"You'll need someone who knows sourcing. Unusual ingredients. Specialty distributors."

"I know."

"You're asking me to help you."

"I'm asking you to collaborate." He sat across from her. "Look. We both want the promotion. But we both want to do something good here. Something that matters. I cannot do this display alone — I do not know the suppliers the way you do."

"And if I help you, you'll win."

"Or I'll help you with yours. We can both win. Or neither. But the store wins either way."

Bea looked at the sketch again. It was better than her idea. More ambitious. More interesting. It was the kind of thing that made people remember a store.

"One week," she said. "I'll share my supplier contacts. You share your design."

"I can work with that."

They shook hands. The contact lasted a beat too long. Neither of them pulled away first.

Chapter 32: Bea's Compromise

Bea told herself she was being strategic.

Helping Miles with his display meant she could influence it, learn from it, maybe even subtly sabotage it if the opportunity arose.

She was not doing any of those things. She knew this by day three, when she spent two hours on the phone tracking down a distributor for Malaysian palm sugar. The conversation was detailed and specific, and she took notes without being asked.

"What do you need it for?" the distributor asked.

"A World Foods display. I'm building something."

"For the store?"

"For myself, actually. But the store benefits."

She hung up. She had secured the sugar. She had not thought about her own display in days.

That night, she sat in her apartment, staring at a blank page where her display concept should have been. Her own display was supposed to be her ticket. Instead, she was sourcing ingredients for Miles.

She called her brother.

"Am I making a mistake?" she asked.

"Depends," Leo said. "Does the mistake involve a guy?"

"It doesn't."

"Then it's probably not a mistake. You've never made a mistake that didn't involve a guy."

"I hate you."

"I love you too. Now go build your food display or whatever."

She hung up, smiling despite herself. Her brother was not wrong. But that did not make the feeling in her chest any easier to name.

Chapter 33: The Supply Run

The specialty distributor was in Lancaster, two hours away. The order had to be picked up in person.

"Road trip," Miles said.

"I hate road trips."

"You've never been on one with me."

They left at 6 a.m. in Bea's beat-up Honda. The radio played static. The air conditioning barely worked. The morning light slanted across the highway, casting long shadows across the farmland.

"I'll drive," Miles said.

"I don't trust you with my car."

"I don't trust you with my life."

"Good. We understand each other."

They swapped drivers. Miles drove with one hand on the wheel, the other resting on the center console. Bea watched the farmland roll past — cows, silos, barns with peeling paint, mailboxes leaning at odd angles.

"Tell me something I don't know," she said.

"I won a chili cook-off in 2019. Second place."

"I don't believe you."

"I have the ribbon. It's in my sock drawer."

"You keep a chili ribbon in your sock drawer."

"I keep all my ribbons there. I also have one from a spelling bee in the sixth grade."

"That explains so much about you."

He laughed. She laughed. The highway stretched ahead, flat and endless, and for an hour there was no rivalry, no promotion, no store. Just two people who liked each other's company.

Chapter 34: Almost a Kiss

The distributor operated out of an industrial park on the edge of Lancaster, a low concrete building with loading docks and faded signage. They loaded the supplies into Bea's trunk — palm sugar, dried chilies, tamarind paste, threads of saffron wrapped in paper.

Dusk settled over the parking lot as they finished. The sky was a bruise of purple and orange, the clouds streaked with pink. The air was warm and still.

Miles closed the trunk and turned. Bea was closer than she had realized. Close enough to see the flecks of gold in his brown eyes. Close enough to smell the ginger and soap on his skin.

"I should check the list again," she said.

"You already checked it."

"Maybe I missed something."

"You didn't miss anything."

They stood there, the parking lot empty around them. A truck hummed in the distance, fading into the evening.

Bea looked at him. He looked at her. She saw his hand move toward hers.

And then a car alarm went off — shrill, insistent, two rows away. Red lights flashed in the growing dark.

They jumped apart. The moment shattered like glass.

"Car alarm," Miles said.

"Car alarm," she repeated.

They got in the car. They drove home in silence. But Bea's hand hovered near the center console the whole way, close enough to touch, and she knew he noticed.

Chapter 35: The Retreat

They did not speak for two days.

Bea told herself it was for the best. They were competitors. A kiss would have ruined everything, tangled the competition with something neither of them was ready to name.

But she could not stop thinking about the parking lot. The way he had looked at her. The way she had wanted him to close the distance. The way her skin had tingled with anticipation.

On the second day, she found a note in her locker. "That was a mistake. Not the trip. The almost. We should focus on the competition. — M."

She read it three times. She wanted to feel relieved. She wanted to agree with him and move on.

She was not relieved.

She wrote back: "Agreed. Focus. — B."

She folded the note and put it in her pocket. She kept it there for days, touching the corner of the paper when no one was watching, tracing the letters of his handwriting.

Chapter 36: Darius Observes

Darius stopped Bea in the break room as she was pouring coffee. His presence filled the doorway.

"Everything okay?"

"Fine."

"You and Miles seem distant."

"We're competitors. We're supposed to be distant."

Darius studied her over the rim of his own cup. "I've been a manager for fifteen years. I've seen people compete. I've also seen people pretend to compete because they don't know what else to do with their feelings."

"I don't have feelings."

"Everyone has feelings, Bea. The question is whether you're honest about them."

She did not answer. The coffee pot gurgled behind her.

"Your display," Darius said. "How's it going?"

"Fine."

"His is supposedly excellent. World Foods concept. I hear he's been getting help from someone with great supplier connections."

Bea's stomach tightened. "That's good for him."

Darius nodded. "It's good for the store. But make sure you're not giving away more than you're getting back."

He left. Bea stood alone in the break room, the vending machine humming. She was giving away more than she was getting. She knew it. And she could not stop.

Chapter 37: The Pivot

Bea threw herself into her display. A harvest celebration theme. Autumn colors — oranges, deep reds, warm browns. Local produce stacked in wooden crates. A recipe card station tying every ingredient to a seasonal meal.

She worked late. She worked early. She stopped looking at Miles entirely.

On Thursday, she stood in front of her half-built display and saw it clearly for the first time. The proportions were wrong. The color scheme was muddy. The concept was generic — the kind of display anyone could have built.

"It's not working," she said aloud.

"Because you're forcing it."

Miles stood behind her. She had not heard him approach. His footsteps were silent on the linoleum.

"Stay out of this."

"I'm not in it. I'm observing."

"Observe somewhere else."

"Bea. You're good at this. But this isn't your best. You're playing it safe because you're scared."

"I'm not scared."

"You're scared of losing. And you're scared of winning. And you're scared of what happens after either."

She turned to face him. "What do you want me to say? That I'm distracted? That I cannot focus because I keep thinking about a parking lot in Lancaster?"

"Yes."

"Yes what?"

"Yes, that's exactly what I want you to say. Because then we can stop pretending."

A customer walked past with a shopping cart, oblivious to the tension between them.

"Your display is going to beat mine," Bea said quietly.

"Probably."

"And then you'll get the promotion."

"Probably."

"And then what?"

Miles looked at her, his eyes unreadable. "And then I don't know. That's the problem."

Chapter 38: Display Night

The displays went up on a Saturday evening. Darius walked through the store with a clipboard, his face revealing nothing. Customers parted around him like water around a stone.

Bea's harvest display was competent. Warm colors, local pumpkins, recipe cards printed on nice paper. It was good — solid, professional, safe. Everything she had aimed for.

Miles's World Foods display was spectacular.

He had built a curved structure that wound through the international aisle like a market street. Each section represented a different cuisine — Thai, Indian, Mexican, Italian. Ingredients were grouped by recipe, with hand-lettered signs explaining each dish. A QR code linked to cooking videos he had recorded himself.

Customers gathered around it. They took pictures. They bought things. The display hummed with life.

Bea stood at the end of the aisle, watching. She felt pride — and underneath it, the strange ache of defeat mixed with relief. It was good. It deserved to win.

"It's good," she said when Miles appeared beside her.

"It's our contacts. Your suppliers made this possible."

"It's your design. Your execution."

"We did this together."

Darius finished his walkthrough and stood in the middle of the store, looking from one display to the other. His pen hovered over the clipboard.

"I'll make my decision after the interviews," he said. "Both of you. My office Monday at 9 a.m."

He walked away. The store hummed around them, customers flowing past.

"I meant it," Miles said quietly. "We did this together."

Bea nodded. The store lights flickered. She did not move away from him.

Chapter 39: The Offer

Monday morning. Darius's office. The coffee smelled burnt. The air conditioning hummed overhead, rattling the blinds.

Bea sat in one chair. Miles sat in the other. The space between them felt enormous, filled with everything they had not said.

Darius closed the door. "I've made my decision."

Bea held her breath. Her hands were cold.

"The assistant manager position goes to Bea Hartley."

She blinked. The words reached her ears but took a moment to land. She had won. After all the weeks, the competition, the nights, the displays — she had won.

"What?" Miles said.

"Her display was more practical. Her customer feedback is slightly higher. Her tenure is longer." Darius stacked papers on his desk. "It was close. But the decision is made."

Bea looked at Miles. His face was calm. He was not angry. That made it worse.

"Congratulations," he said. The word was soft, genuine. It hurt more than hostility ever could.

"There's a condition," Darius said. "Two-week evaluation period. You pass, the job is yours. You fail, we reconsider."

"I understand," Bea said.

She did not understand anything. She had gotten what she had fought for. It felt nothing like she had expected.

Chapter 40: Miles's Congratulations

Miles found her in the break room after the meeting, standing in front of the vending machine without seeing it. The snacks glowed behind glass.

"You're the assistant manager," he said.

"Evaluation period."

"You're going to pass. You're the best person for the job."

"Am I?"

He sat across from her. "I meant what I said. I'm not angry. I'm not disappointed. You earned it."

"That's worse than if you yelled at me."

"Why?"

"Because now I cannot be angry at you. And I need to be angry at you."

"Why?"

She looked at him. "Because I don't know how to feel about you when I'm not competing."

Miles leaned forward, his elbows on his knees. "Then figure it out. I'll wait."

He left. She stayed. The vending machine hummed.

She pulled out her notepad — the scorebook, the tallies, the lists she had been keeping for weeks. She looked at the page where she had written "Things Miles Notices."

She tore it out. Folded it carefully. Put it in her pocket.

She had stopped keeping score days ago. She just had not admitted it to herself.

Chapter 41: The Evaluation

Darius watched her constantly.

Not in a hostile way. He simply appeared — when she processed a return, when she directed a stocker, when she helped a customer find an ingredient. His presence was a quiet weight, always at the edge of her vision.

"You're doing fine," he said on day three. "Stop being nervous."

"I'm not nervous."

"You're nervous. You keep looking over your shoulder."

She was looking for Miles. She did not say that. She was checking the aisles, watching for the familiar shape of his shoulders, the precise way he moved.

She found herself wandering toward Aisle Five during her breaks. The World Foods display was still up. Customers lingered around it, studying the ingredients, taking pictures with their phones.

She touched the label on a jar of tamarind paste. She had sourced that. She had driven two hours to get it. She had stood in a parking lot and almost kissed the man who built the shelf it sat on.

"Missing something?" Mrs. Kowalski asked, appearing beside her.

"No."

"The display, or the person who made it?"

"I don't know what you're talking about."

Mrs. Kowalski patted her arm. "That's the problem, honey. You should know."

She walked away. Bea stood in Aisle Five, alone, surrounded by the evidence of what she and Miles had built together.

Chapter 42: Mrs. Kowalski's Advice

Bea found Mrs. Kowalski in the break room, counting bills from the betting pool. The money was spread across the table in neat stacks — twenties, tens, fives.

"Who won?" Bea asked.

"Nobody yet. The pool is still open. People are hedging."

"On what?"

Mrs. Kowalski looked up. "On you. On him. On whether either of you is smart enough to see what's in front of you."

"I got the promotion."

"And you're miserable."

"I'm not miserable."

"You're ordering salad instead of your usual sandwich. You're wearing mismatched socks. You forgot the sale signs yesterday." Mrs. Kowalski set down her cash. "You're a mess, Bea."

Bea sat down across from her. The plastic chair creaked. "What am I supposed to do?"

"Ask yourself what you actually want."

"I want the promotion."

"Do you?"

"Yes."

Mrs. Kowalski studied her with the patience of someone who had watched twenty-two years of employees make the same mistake. "Winning and wanting are different things, Bea. You wanted the promotion. Now you won it. And you look like you lost something."

Bea did not answer. Because Mrs. Kowalski was right. The victory felt hollow, like a room with no furniture.

"Maybe," Mrs. Kowalski said, "you should figure out what you actually want before it is too late."

Chapter 43: Miles's Plan

Miles was in the back room on his phone when Bea walked in. The door was half-open, and he had not heard her approach.

She froze in the doorway. He was reading something on his screen, his face lit by the blue glow. She could hear every word.

"Department of Culinary Arts," he read aloud. "I confirm my acceptance of the spring cohort position."

Bea's blood went cold. The words hung in the air between them.

He looked up. Saw her. The phone dropped to his side.

"Bea."

"You applied to culinary school."

"Bea, let me explain."

"You're leaving."

"I don't know yet."

"Your father—"

"There's a program in Cleveland. My aunt can stay with him. I can commute on weekends." He stepped toward her. "I wasn't going to tell you until after the evaluation."

"Why?"

"Because I didn't want to distract you."

"Distract me from what? The job you helped me get?"

"The job you earned."

Bea shook her head. "You were going to leave without telling me."

"I was going to tell you. When it was real."

"When is it real? When you're on the plane?"

Miles's jaw tightened. "I didn't think you'd care."

"Of course I care."

"Why?"

She opened her mouth. Nothing came out.

"Exactly," he said. And he walked past her, out of the back room, into the store.

Chapter 44: The Discovery

Bea did not sleep that night. She lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, the fan blades turning in slow circles above her. The numbers on her clock glowed red, each hour marking another failed attempt at rest.

Miles was leaving. Maybe. He had a plan — a way out, a future that did not involve Green Valley Grocery. Culinary school. Cleveland. A life she was not part of.

She had the promotion. The evaluation period. The job she had wanted for years.

She should be happy.

She got up at 3 a.m. She drove to the store in her pajama bottoms and an old coat. The parking lot was empty. The lights were off. The building sat dark against the night sky, a familiar silhouette.

She sat in her car, the engine idling, looking at the front entrance. Somewhere inside, the World Foods display still stood — ingredients she had sourced, a vision he had built. They had built it together.

She thought about the night shift. The coffee at 3 a.m. The pallet jack. The highway at dusk. The way his hand had almost found hers in a parking lot in Lancaster.

She thought about almost kissing him.

She started the car. She drove home. She did not know what she was going to do. But she knew she was not going to do nothing.

Chapter 45: The Confrontation

Bea found Miles in Aisle Five the next morning.

He was adjusting the World Foods display, straightening labels, wiping dust off jars. His movements were methodical, almost mechanical, as if the repetition could keep his mind occupied.

"Miles."

He did not turn around. "I'm working."

"We need to talk."

"We passed that point."

"Miles."

He turned. His face was tired, the lines deeper than she remembered. Dark circles shadowed his eyes. "What do you want, Bea?"

"I want to know why you didn't tell me."

"Because it doesn't matter. You got the promotion. I got a plan. We both get what we wanted."

"Do we?"

"Don't."

"Don't what?"

"Don't stand there and pretend this is about the job. Or the school. Or any of it." His voice cracked on the last word, raw and unguarded. "This is about the fact that I fell in love with you during those two weeks, and I have no idea what to do with it."

The words hung in the air between them. A customer turned into the aisle and quickly turned back out.

Bea's hands were shaking. "I know."

"Know what?"

"I know. Because I fell in love with you too."

Chapter 46: The Confession

They stood in Aisle Five. The store continued around them — announcements over the intercom, the beep of registers, the rumble of shopping carts. None of it reached them.

"I don't know what to do," Bea said.

"Neither do I."

"Then what happens?"

Miles leaned against the shelf, his hand resting on a jar of tamarind paste. "I've been thinking about that since I got the acceptance letter. Every option feels wrong."

"What are the options?"

"I stay. I take a job I don't want, working next to someone I cannot have."

"Or?"

"Or I leave. I go to Cleveland. I learn to cook. I try to forget about you."

"Can you forget about me?"

"No." He met her eyes, steady and sure. "Can you?"

"No."

They stood there. The fluorescent lights hummed. The cooler in dairy kicked on, a distant rumble.

"I don't want to be the reason you don't follow your dream," Bea said.

"You're not the reason I would stay. You're the reason I would want to."

"Then stay."

"And resent you when I'm forty and still stacking cans?"

"Then go."

"And spend the rest of my life wondering?"

The question had no answer. They stood in silence, both of them trapped, both of them unwilling to be the one who walked away.

Chapter 47: Darius's Decision

Darius called Bea into his office on Friday. The coffee rings were still there, but now there was a framed photo of a golden retriever on his desk that she had never noticed before.

"I'm postponing the promotion," he said.

Bea stared at him. "What?"

"You're distracted. Miles is distracted. The store needs focused leadership."

"I can focus."

"Can you?"

Bea opened her mouth. Closed it.

"Talk to me," Darius said. "Whatever is going on between you two — deal with it. Then come back. The job will still be here."

"You're giving me time."

"I'm giving you permission to figure out what matters."

Bea sat back in her chair. The leather creaked. "What if I do not know?"

"Then figure it out. That's the job. Not the assistant manager job. The real one."

She left the office. She walked through the store — past the displays, past the registers, past the customers who had no idea they were witnessing a life falling apart. She stopped in Aisle Five.

The World Foods display was still there, perfect and beautiful. A monument to what they had built together.

She pulled out her phone. She called her brother.

"I think I'm in love with him," she said.

"I know," Leo said. "You've been in love with him for weeks. I was waiting for you to catch up."

Chapter 48: The Real Choice

Bea sat on her apartment floor that night with a notebook in her lap. The carpet was worn thin beneath her. The only light came from a single lamp.

She wrote two lists.

List one: Reasons to stay at Green Valley. Stable income. Health insurance. Proximity to family. The promotion she had worked three years to earn. Her brother's tuition. The familiar rhythm of the store.

List two: Reasons to leave. A feeling. A person. A future she could not see clearly but wanted to reach for anyway.

The first list was longer. More practical. More responsible. It had numbers and facts and security.

But when she closed her eyes, she saw the parking lot in Lancaster. The highway at dusk. The way Miles had looked at her when he said he loved her.

She thought about the night shift — the coffee, the singing, the pallet jack, the way he had taught her to fix something instead of fighting it. The way he had seen her clearly, without the armor she wore during the day.

She thought about the housecoat his grandmother had worn. The way Miles had laughed at her jokes. The way he had straightened the apples in Aisle Three.

She picked up her phone.

"Miles," she typed. "Can we talk? For real this time."

She hit send before she could change her mind.

Three dots appeared. Then: "Aisle Five. Midnight."

Chapter 49: Aisle Five

She found him in Aisle Five at midnight.

The store was closed. The lights were dimmed to their night setting, casting long shadows across the floor. He had a key. She had a key. They stood in the dark, surrounded by the World Foods display.

"I've been thinking," she said.

"Me too."

"About Cleveland."

"About staying."

"About us." She took a breath. "I want the promotion. I earned it. But I do not want it if it means losing you."

"You won't lose me. I'm not going anywhere you don't want me to."

"That's not fair."

"Life isn't fair."

"Miles."

He stepped closer. "I've been thinking too. About what we built here. Not just the display. The trust. The honesty. The way you looked at me when I fixed the pallet jack."

"I looked at you like you were showing off."

"You looked at me like you were proud."

She was. She had been. Proud of him then, proud of him now. Proud of what they had built together, piece by piece, in the dark hours of the night.

"I don't know what comes next," she said. "But I know I want you in it."

Miles reached for her hand. She let him take it. His palm was warm, his fingers threading through hers.

"Whatever comes next," he said. "Together."

Together. She tested the word. It felt right.

They stood in Aisle Five, hands intertwined, the store dark around them. The air smelled of tamarind and ginger and floor wax. And for the first time in months, Bea felt like she was exactly where she was supposed to be.

Chapter 50: New Inventory

Six months later, the food truck was parked at the corner of Grove and Main.

"Sun-Drenched" was painted on the side in yellow letters against a blue background, the design simple and bright. A line of customers stretched down the block, their breath fogging in the morning air.

Bea worked the window, taking orders with a marker in her hand. Miles worked the grill, his movements quick and precise, the spatula an extension of his arm. The smell of chilies and lemongrass drifted into the street, drawing people from the bus stop and the coffee shop.

"Two specials," Bea called over her shoulder.

"Two specials," Miles confirmed.

Mrs. Kowalski was first in line. She had driven twenty minutes to find them, her car parked illegally at the curb. She wore her best coat.

"Not bad," she said, studying the truck. "For two people who could not admit they were in love."

"We admitted it," Bea said.

"Eventually."

"Better late than never."

Mrs. Kowalski paid for her order. Bea tried to refuse the money. Mrs. Kowalski insisted, pressing the bills into her hand.

"Consider it a final bet payout," she said. "I won, by the way. The pool. I bet on both of you."

"Both of us?"

"I bet on neither of you getting the promotion and both of you ending up here."

Bea laughed. "That is not how betting works."

"When you've been running a pool as long as I have, it works however I want."

Mrs. Kowalski took her food and walked to a nearby bench. Miles came up beside Bea, sliding his arm around her waist. His apron was dusted with flour.

"She's not wrong," he said.

"About what?"

"About better late than never."

Bea leaned into him. The line of customers stretched on. The winter sun broke through the clouds, warming the metal siding of the truck.

"Ready for lunch rush?" she asked.

"With you?" Miles smiled. "Always."

She kissed him — quick, warm, a promise.

Then she turned back to the window. "Next customer."

The line moved. The day passed. Bea handed bowls of curry to waiting customers, and each transaction felt like building something new. She thought of the World Foods display, still standing in Aisle Five after all these months. She thought of Lawrence, who still walked to the store at 4 a.m. for his banana and coffee.

She thought of the promotion she had almost wanted, and the life she had found instead.

This was real. This was theirs.

And it was only the beginning.
