What the Tides Forgot

A Novel

---

Arc I: The Drowned Village

---

Chapter 1: The Bell

The bell came up first.

Dr. Fiona Rees watched it break the surface, a green-black lump of corroded metal that had not felt sunlight since Edward III wore the crown of England. Seawater streamed from its flanks, falling back into the Celtic Sea in long muscular sheets. The lifting frame groaned. The boat, the Mary Belle, listed two degrees to port and held.

"Steady," Fiona said, though no one on deck needed telling.

The bell cleared the gunwale, swinging slow and heavy, and the deckhands guided it onto the rubber matting they had laid out that morning. It settled with a sound like a great animal exhaling. Fiona crossed to it, knelt, ran her gloved fingers over the crust of centuries. Barnacles. Sandstone-hard sediment. And beneath it, the faint raised letters of an inscription.

She took a soft brush from her jacket pocket and worked a corner clean.

A single word emerged, the letterforms sharp and deep, as if cast yesterday rather than six hundred years ago:

SANCTUARY

"Not bad for a Tuesday," said Owen Price from behind her. He was leaning against the cabin, arms folded, watching her with the expression of a man who had seen many things come up from the deep and knew most of them were just rocks.

Fiona did not turn around. "It's from the church. Has to be."

"Or the tavern. Every pub in Wales was called Sanctuary before the Reformation."

"Owen."

"I'm just saying."

She stood, knees cracking, and looked at the bell. The church of Saint Cynfelyn had stood at the center of the drowned village of Bodvael for four hundred years before the sea took it in the great storm of 1352. The chronicles said the villagers had refused to evacuate. They had rung the bell to call the faithful to prayer as the water rose. The sea swallowed them mid-mass.

That was the story, anyway.

Fiona had built her career on the story. Two decades of maritime archaeology, eight books, a dozen major excavations from the Baltic to the Black Sea, and she kept circling back to this one lost village on the coast of Gwynedd. Bodvael. The Welsh Atlantis. The place nobody believed existed until she found the foundation stones in 2019.

"Bring up more from the trench," she said. "I want to see what else they left us."

The dive team was already geared up. They went over the side in pairs, fins glinting, bubbles rising in silver chains. Fiona watched the depth gauges on the monitor. Twenty-three meters. The seabed was silty, visibility poor, but the sonar had shown a structure down there — walls, streets, a square. The grid she had laid out over the summer was paying off.

"Doctor?"

She turned. One of the graduate students, a girl from Cardiff with algae-green hair and earnest eyes, held out a waterproof tablet. "The sediment samples from trench four. You said you wanted to see them right away."

Fiona took the tablet, swiped through the data. Particle analysis, organic content, microfauna. Everything looked normal for a submerged medieval settlement. Shell fragments. Wood pulp. Bone.

She stopped.

"What's this?"

"Ma'am?"

Fiona zoomed in on the carbon date. She had requested a preliminary assay on organic material recovered from the midden heap behind what she believed was the baker's house. The lab had come back with a number that did not make sense.

"Is there an error in the chain of custody?" she asked.

"Not that I know of. Why?"

Fiona handed the tablet back. "Get the lab on the satellite phone. Tell them to re-run the test."

"What should I tell them to look for?"

"Tell them to check their machine." She paused. "Because that sample says it's from next year."

The student laughed, then saw Fiona's face, and stopped.

---

Chapter 2: The Bones

The second dive brought up a hand.

It came loose from the silt, fingers extended, as if reaching for something. The bones were clean — too clean. Six centuries underwater, and the hand had been picked bare by scavengers and current, but there was something wrong with the way it looked. The whiteness of it. The preservation.

Fiona held it in the light.

"Owen, come here."

He came, wiping grease from his hands. He had been servicing the winch, a job he preferred to the academic parts of the operation. Owen Price had been a commercial diver for twenty years before a bent spine forced him to the surface for good. He knew the sea the way Fiona knew manuscripts, and he trusted it about as much.

"Look at this," she said.

He took the hand, turned it over. His face did something complicated.

"You've seen bones before," Fiona said.

"Not like this." He pointed to the wrist. "See the joint surface? Cartilage still attached. That doesn't happen after six hundred years. Six months, maybe. In cold, anoxic conditions. But not here. Not in this water."

"The lab said the carbon date was wrong."

"The lab's not wrong, Fee. You know that."

She did know it. She had been pushing the thought away all afternoon, letting the work distract her, but Owen was not the kind of man you could hide from. He had known her since she was a graduate student on her first dig, throwing up over the side of a boat in the North Sea. He had watched her become who she was.

She took the hand back. "Keep diving. I want every bone we can find."

The next six hours produced forty-three bones. A partial skeleton, scattered across what had once been the main street of Bodvael. The team brought them up in labeled bags, careful and methodical, and Fiona laid them out on the deck in anatomical order as the sun went down and the lights came on.

It was not a medieval body.

She knew it by the teeth. Medieval teeth were ground flat by gritty bread, worn down by a lifetime of abrasion. This skull had modern dentistry. Composite fillings. A crown on the second molar, porcelain fused to metal, the kind of work you got from a private practice in Cardiff or London.

"Someone want to explain this?" she said to the empty deck.

The crew had gone below for dinner. The Mary Belle rocked gently in the swell. Wind moved across the water, carrying the smell of salt and diesel and something else — something organic and wrong. Fiona looked at the bones, then at the dark water where Bodvael lay buried.

She took out her phone. No signal, of course. They were fifteen kilometers offshore, and the nearest tower was on the Lleyn Peninsula, barely reaching.

She looked at the bones again.

The fillings in the teeth were modern. The carbon date was from next year. And the hand, when she picked it up again, still had traces of nail polish on the fingers.

A pale, translucent pink. The color of conch shells. The color of a woman who had cared about her appearance, who had painted her nails before she died.

Fiona had never seen that color before. But she knew, with a certainty that sat in her chest like a stone, that she would see it again.

---

Chapter 3: The Drowned Woman

The drowned woman came ashore on Thursday.

Fiona was in the site office, a converted shipping container on the quay at Porthdinllaen, staring at carbon dates that made no sense, when Owen put his head through the door and said, "You need to see this."

She followed him to the jetty. A fishing boat had tied up, the Catherine Anne, and the skipper was standing on the dock with a bundle wrapped in tarpaulin at his feet.

"Found her in the net," the skipper said. "Thought she was a seal at first."

Fiona knelt, pulled back the tarp.

The woman was young. Mid-twenties, maybe. Brown hair tangled with seaweed. Skin pale as milk, but not the pallor of death — more like the stillness of a long sleep. She was dressed in clothes that were not medieval. A dark blue sweater. Jeans. Trainers, one missing, the remaining laces done in a double knot.

Fiona checked for a pulse. Nothing. No breath. The body was cold.

But there was no decomposition.

"What the hell is going on?" Owen said.

Fiona did not answer. She was looking at the woman's nails. Pale pink. Conch-shell pink. The same color as the hand from the seabed.

"Get her to the mortuary in Pwllheli," she said. "I want a full autopsy. I want toxicology. I want every test they can run."

"And tell them what? That she washed up looking like she died this morning but we found her fingernail polish on a six-hundred-year-old skeleton?"

"Tell them whatever you want. Just get it done."

They carried the body away. Fiona stood on the jetty, watching the water, and for the first time in her career she felt something she could not name. Not fear. Not excitement. Something between them, something that lived in the space where certainty used to be.

The tide was coming in. The sea was rising, slow and inexorable, covering the beach stone by stone. The same sea that had swallowed Bodvael six centuries ago. The same sea that had given back a woman who had died in clothes from a shop in Cardiff.

Somewhere underwater, the bell of Saint Cynfelyn's church was still ringing in Fiona's imagination. A single note, endless.

Sanctuary.

But sanctuary from what?

---

Chapter 4: Professor Marsh

Professor Alistair Marsh arrived on Friday, bringing the full weight of Cambridge and a briefcase full of skepticism.

"You've gone tabloid, Fiona," he said, settling into the container-office chair as if it were a throne. "I expected better."

"I sent you the data."

"I saw the data. I also saw the Daily Mail headline. 'Atlantis Bodies From The Future, Says Top Archaeologist.' You know who wrote it? Someone who was supposed to be interviewing you about medieval bell-casting techniques."

Fiona did not flinch. "I didn't tell them about the dates. Someone on the crew talked."

"And now the whole world thinks you've lost your mind."

"Have I?"

Marsh studied her. He was seventy-one, white-haired, with the weathered face of a man who had spent fifty years digging in the ground and under the water. He had been Fiona's doctoral supervisor, her mentor, her fiercest critic and most loyal advocate. If he thought she was wrong, he would say so. If he thought she was lying, he would destroy her.

"I spent last night in the lab at Aberystwyth," he said. "I ran the samples myself."

"And?"

"And the carbon dating is consistent with a specimen from approximately fourteen months in the future." He said it flatly, without emphasis, as if he were reading a bus timetable. "I also examined the dental work. The crown on tooth number thirty is a lithium disilicate restoration manufactured by a German company that didn't exist until 2018. The composite fillings contain a bonding agent patented in 2005."

"So I'm not wrong."

"You're not wrong about the data. What it means is an entirely different question." He opened his briefcase, took out a folder. "I've been doing some reading. Disturbing reading, mostly from the fringe journals nobody cites. There's a phenomenon — reported, never proven — called temporal displacement. Objects, sometimes bodies, appearing in geological strata they don't belong to."

"Time travel."

"Don't use that word. It makes us sound like the people who write books about the Bermuda Triangle." He slid a paper across the desk. "This is from the Journal of Theoretical Archaeology, 1987. A paper by a man named Caradog Pritchard. He claimed to have found Roman coins in a Neolithic burial in Anglesey. The coins were minted in 117 AD. The burial was carbon-dated to 2000 BC."

"What happened to him?"

"He was laughed out of the profession. Died in a nursing home in 2005, alone and bitter." Marsh leaned forward. "But his excavation notes are meticulous. And the coins were never re-dated. They're still in storage at the Museum of Wales, still Roman, still two thousand years too old for where they were found."

"What does that have to do with my bodies?"

"Pritchard believed there were places — liminal zones, he called them — where time did not behave normally. Places where the boundary between past and present was thin. He spent his entire career trying to prove it, and he died a failure."

Fiona looked out the window at the sea. The water was gray today, flat, the sky a lid of cloud. "Bodvael is a liminal zone."

"It would explain the bell. The bodies. The woman in the net." Marsh paused. "And it would mean that whatever killed those people — the people of Bodvael, the medieval ones — is not finished. Because the bodies from the future were also in Bodvael. Drowned, like the rest."

"The village wasn't drowned by a storm."

"No. It wasn't." Marsh closed his briefcase. "I think something came out of the sea in 1352. And I think it's still there."

---

Chapter 5: The Excavation

Fiona threw herself into the dig.

The only way to understand the mystery was to uncover it, layer by layer, bone by bone. She expanded the grid. She brought in pumps and sediment screens. She worked eighteen-hour days until her hands cramped and her vision blurred, and she still could not escape the feeling that she was not discovering the truth — she was uncovering something that wanted to be found.

The drowned village of Bodvael had been small. Maybe eighty houses, a church, a mill, a tavern. The streets were narrow, the foundations shallow, the whole settlement clinging to a low promontory that had once jutted into the bay. The storm of 1352 had not just flooded the village. It had erased it. The promontory collapsed, slid into the sea, and took everything with it.

That was the accepted history.

But the bones told a different story.

Fiona found the first mass grave on the second day of the expanded dig. The remains of thirty people, piled in a pit behind what had been the church. The bones showed signs of violent trauma. Cut marks on the ribs. Fractures on the skulls. Defensive wounds on the forearms, the kind made by someone trying to ward off a blade.

She sat back on her heels, breathing hard through her mask.

"These people didn't drown," she said.

Owen crouched beside her. "What, then?"

"I don't know. But someone killed them. Before the storm. Before the sea took the village."

"Maybe that's why they died. The fighting distracted them. They didn't see the water coming."

"There's no record of violence in Bodvael. The chronicles describe a peaceful fishing community. Pious. Prosperous."

"Chronicles lie, Fee."

She looked at the bones again. The cut marks were clean, precise. Made by a weapon that was sharp and well-maintained. Not the kind of blade a medieval fisherman would carry.

She thought of the drowned woman on the jetty. The pink nail polish. The clothes from a shop that would not exist for six hundred years.

"Get the lab back on the phone," she said. "I want carbon dates on every skeleton we find. And I want them fast."

They came back in forty-eight hours.

Eight of the thirty skeletons were from the fourteenth century. The other twenty-two were from the future. The most recent was dated eighteen months from now.

The numbers made no sense. They could not be right. And yet they kept coming back the same.

Fiona sat in her office that night, surrounded by papers, trying to find a pattern. The medieval bones were at the bottom of the pit. The future bones were on top. The deeper she dug, the older the bodies got — except sometimes it was the opposite. A hand here, a foot there, appearing in strata they had no business occupying.

It was as if time itself had been stirred, like sediment in water, and the dead had settled in no particular order.

Her phone buzzed. A text from Marsh.

"Found something in Pritchard's papers. Come to Cambridge."

She looked at the clock. 2 AM. She looked at the sea, black and endless beyond the window.

She booked the first train.

---

Chapter 6: The Journal of Caradog Pritchard

Marsh met her at the Cambridge train station looking like he had not slept.

"The library has Pritchard's personal journals," he said, walking fast, not waiting for her to keep up. "His daughter donated them after his death. I didn't know they existed until yesterday."

"Journals from when?"

"All of it. Forty years of fieldwork, starting in the 1960s. He was convinced there was a pattern. Certain places, certain times, where the boundary between past and future broke down. He called them fracture points."

Fiona followed him through the narrow streets of the old city, past college gates and bicycle racks, past students who did not look at them. She felt the weight of the journey in her bones, the sleepless night catching up.

They reached the library. Marsh had already arranged access. A librarian led them to a reading room and brought out seven cardboard boxes, each labeled in a shaky hand.

"Pritchard's handwriting gets worse as he gets older," Marsh said. "Parkinson's. He was deteriorating through the last decade of his work."

They opened the boxes together.

The early journals were methodical, scientific. Pritchard had been a respected archaeologist in his thirties, with a lectureship at Swansea and a promising career ahead of him. But as the journals progressed, his tone changed. He became obsessive. Paranoid. He started drawing symbols in the margins, spirals and knots that Fiona recognized as ancient Celtic designs.

"He was looking at the old myths," she said, holding up a page. "The legends of the drowned lands. Cantre'r Gwaelod. The Sunken Hundred. Wales has dozens of stories about cities lost beneath the sea."

"And he thought they were all real. All literal."

"Not just real. Connected." She flipped to a later journal, dated 1992. Pritchard's handwriting was barely legible now, the words crowded together, the letters shaking. "He believed the fracture points were opening more frequently. That something was pushing through from the other side."

Marsh took the journal, squinted at the text. "Pushing through?"

"Read it yourself."

He read, and his face went pale.

"What?" Fiona said.

"It's not just bodies, Fiona. He describes things that came through alive."

The air in the reading room seemed to get colder. Fiona looked at the open boxes, the decades of obsession spread across the table, and she thought of the woman in the net. The pink nail polish. The skin that had not decayed.

"Did he ever find one? A living one?"

"Read page forty-seven."

She found it. The date was March 14, 1994. The writing was wild, almost incoherent.

"Found it today. The passage. North of Bardsey Island, where the currents meet. A woman. Alive. Speaking a language I don't recognize. She came from the water at sunset and she was not afraid. She knew me. She said my name."

Fiona looked up. "He doesn't finish the entry."

"No. The next page is blank, and then the journals skip to 1996. Two years missing."

"And he never mentioned what happened to the woman?"

"He never mentioned her again. But look at this." Marsh opened a folder from his briefcase, extracted a photograph. "This was in the box at the bottom, underneath everything. Undated."

The photograph showed a woman standing on a rocky shore. She was young, brown-haired, wearing a dark blue sweater and jeans.

Trainers on her feet. Pink nail polish.

The same woman who had come up in the fishing net.

"My God," Fiona whispered.

"And one more thing." Marsh slid another paper across the table. "This is from Pritchard's last excavation. In the summer of 2003, he returned to Anglesey, to the site where he had found the Roman coins in the Neolithic burial."

"And?"

"He found something else. A bronze bell. Inscribed with a single word."

Fiona did not need to ask which word.

"Where is the bell now?" she said.

"That's the problem. It was never catalogued. It never made it to any museum. Pritchard's daughter says he kept it in his study until the day he died, and after the funeral, it was gone."

"Someone stole it."

"Or someone came back for it."

---

Chapter 7: The Fracture Point

Fiona took the train back to Wales with a copy of Pritchard's journals in her bag and a photograph of the drowned woman in her pocket. The woman was alive in 1994, she thought. Then she was dead and in the water. And now she was back, washed ashore, looking as if she had died yesterday.

The timeline was wrong in every direction.

She spent the journey reading, underlining, filling margins with her own notes. Pritchard had been onto something real. She could feel it in the way the data lined up, the way the anomalies clustered. Bodvael was not the only fracture point. He had identified seven others — all of them on the coast of Wales, all of them places where the sea had taken a settlement, all of them sites where time seemed to fold.

But Bodvael was the only one where the bodies kept coming.

When she got back to Porthdinllaen, the sun was setting over the bay. The water was gold and red, beautiful in a way that made her uneasy. She walked to the end of the jetty and looked out at the excavation site, marked by buoys that bobbed in the chop.

Owen was there, smoking. He had quit three times since she had known him and never managed to stay quit.

"Found something while you were gone," he said.

"Good something or bad something?"

"Don't know yet." He flicked the cigarette into the water. "Come see."

He led her to the container office. On the table was a map of the bay, marked with depth soundings and coordinates. Owen had drawn a circle around one spot, about three hundred meters north of the main excavation.

"There's a structure down there I didn't notice before," he said. "I was looking at the sonar from last spring, the full survey, and I almost missed it because it's right on the edge of the scan. But it's there."

"What kind of structure?"

"Not medieval. The shape's wrong. It's round, for one thing. And it's deep — forty meters. That's below the level of the old village. That predates it."

Fiona looked at the circle. "How old?"

"I don't know. But the sonar showed something else. A pathway, maybe. Leading from the structure toward the village. Straight line, like it was built."

"A road."

"Underwater, Fee. A road underwater, leading from something older than the medieval village to the village itself."

She thought of Pritchard's fracture points. The places where time was thin. The places where something pushed through.

"Put a dive team on it tomorrow," she said. "I want to see what's down there."

---

Chapter 8: The Circle

The dive team came back with photographs and video.

The structure at forty meters was a circle of stones, each one the size of a car, arranged in a perfect ring. The stones were covered in carvings — spirals, knots, interlocking patterns that were older than anything Fiona had seen in her career.

"This is Neolithic," she said, staring at the images on the monitor. "Maybe earlier. This is four, five thousand years old."

"The pathway leads straight from the circle to the village," Owen said. "And the village is built directly on top of where the pathway ends."

"They built Bodvael on top of a Neolithic site. They knew it was there."

"Or something else built it, and the medieval villagers just settled there because the ground was flat."

Fiona zoomed in on one of the carvings. A spiral, but not a simple one. It twisted in two directions at once, clockwise and counterclockwise, an impossible geometry that hurt her eyes to look at.

"Get me a drawing of this," she said. "Full resolution. I want to see every line."

She spent the night cross-referencing the carvings with Pritchard's journals. He had drawn the same symbols — the double spiral, the interlocking knots, the patterns that seemed to move when you stared at them too long. He had found them on standing stones across Wales, always near water, always at places where the boundary between worlds was supposed to be thin.

He had called them gate marks.

Fiona looked at the spirals. Looked at the circle. Looked at the pathway that led from the Neolithic ring to the drowned village where bodies from the future lay tangled with bodies from the past.

And she understood, with a clarity that felt like falling, that the circle was not a structure. It was a lock.

And someone had just found the key.

---

Chapter 9: The Drowned Woman Speaks

The drowned woman opened her eyes at 3:17 AM on the third day in the mortuary.

The pathologist, a tired man named Dr. Evans who had been examining corpses for forty years, was in the middle of writing his notes when the body on the table sat up and said something in a language he did not understand.

He ran. He did not stop running until he reached the police station in Pwllheli, where he stood at the front desk in his blood-stained lab coat, shaking, unable to form a coherent sentence.

The police called Fiona.

She drove through the dark, the headlights carving tunnels in the fog, her heart beating so hard she could feel it in her throat. The mortuary was lit up when she arrived, every light on, the door standing open.

Inside, the drowned woman was sitting on the edge of the autopsy table, her bare feet swinging, her expression curious and calm.

Dr. Evans was nowhere to be seen.

"Hello," the woman said. In English, this time. "You're Dr. Rees."

Fiona stopped in the doorway. "Who are you?"

"I'm not sure that matters." The woman slid off the table, stood on the cold tile floor. The fluorescent lights made her skin look gray, but she moved like someone who was fully alive. "I'm here to tell you something."

"Tell me what?"

"You need to stop digging in the circle. You need to leave Bodvael alone. If you don't, you'll open something that cannot be closed."

"You're from the future."

The woman smiled. It was not a happy smile. "I'm from the same time as you. I was born in 1994. I grew up in Aberystwyth. I studied archaeology at Cardiff. I was a student of yours, once. A long time from now."

Fiona felt the floor tilt under her. "That's impossible."

"You found my hand in the sediment. My bones. I was in the mass grave, twenty-two skeletons from the future, and I was one of them." The woman's voice was level, matter-of-fact. "I came back to warn you, but I was too late. I drowned before I could speak. And now I'm here again, in this body, and I don't have much time."

"Time until what?"

"Until the same thing that took Bodvael comes for the rest of us." The woman stepped closer. Her eyes were dark, and in their depths, Fiona saw something that looked like grief. "The circle is a cage. The stones were put there to hold something back. Something that lives in the deep places, where time doesn't pass the same way. The medieval villagers found the circle. They started digging. They let it out."

"The storm of 1352—"

"Was not a storm. It was a response. The thing that came through needed water. It drowned the village to hide what it had done. And now the circle is weakening again. Your excavation is making it worse."

Fiona's hands were shaking. "How do I stop it?"

"You can't. It's already in motion. But you can prepare." The woman touched Fiona's face, her fingers cold. "Find Marsh. Find the bell. And whatever you do, do not dive into the circle."

The woman's eyes went distant, unfocused. She swayed.

"The tide is coming," she said. "It remembers everything."

Then she collapsed. Fiona caught her, lowered her to the floor, felt for a pulse. It was there — weak, but there.

She was asleep. Alive.

Fiona sat on the mortuary floor, holding the woman from the future in her arms, and listened to the sound of the sea outside, which was loud tonight, louder than it should have been, miles inland.

The tide was coming.

---

Chapter 10: Sanctuary

The bell of Saint Cynfelyn's church was shipped to the National Museum in Cardiff the next morning. Fiona went with it.

She stood in the conservation lab as the technicians worked to remove centuries of encrustation, revealing the word beneath. SANCTUARY. The letters were Celtic uncial, the style that flourished in Wales in the early fourteenth century. But the metal was wrong. It was not bronze, not quite.

"We ran a spectrometer on it," the lead conservator said. "It's not a composition we've seen before. High copper content, yes, but there are trace elements that don't appear in any known medieval alloy. Isotopes that don't match terrestrial sources."

"What does that mean?"

"It means this bell was not made from metal mined on Earth. At least, not entirely."

Fiona looked at the bell. It had been cleaned enough to show the full inscription now. Not just the word SANCTUARY, but a border of spirals around the rim. The same spirals as the circle. The same spirals as Pritchard's journals.

"Can you tell how old it is?"

"Radiometric dating is inconclusive. The isotopes are too unusual. But stylistically, I'd say it was cast in the late fifteenth century. Which is strange, because the village was destroyed in 1352."

"Someone made this after the village sank."

"Someone made it underwater, maybe. Or someone made it somewhere else entirely and brought it here."

Fiona touched the bell, lightly, with her fingertips. It was cold, and it vibrated with a faint hum, like a note just below the threshold of hearing.

She thought of the words from the drowned woman. You can't stop it. But you can prepare.

"What are we preparing for?" she said, to the bell, to the spirals, to the isotopes that did not belong to this world.

The bell did not answer.

But the hum got louder, just for a moment, and then subsided.

Outside, the sea was rising.

Arc II: The Deep Places

---

Chapter 11: The Second Body

A week after the drowned woman spoke, a second body washed ashore.

Fiona was in Cardiff, meeting with the museum's metallurgist about the bell, when Owen's call came through. "Same spot. Same condition. Same clothes."

She drove back through the rain, the windshield wipers struggling against the downpour. The sky was the color of old pewter, the sea the color of iron. The road along the coast was empty, the tourist season over, the holiday cottages shuttered.

The body was on the same jetty where the first had been found. Covered with the same tarpaulin. Surrounded by the same stunned silence.

But this one was different.

This one was a man. Older, maybe fifty. Grey hair, a beard. His clothes were not modern — he wore a thick wool sweater, oilskin trousers, and rubber boots of a kind Fiona had not seen outside of museum displays.

She knelt and checked his pockets. No wallet. No phone. No identification.

But in the breast pocket of his sweater, folded and sealed in waxed paper, was a letter.

The paper was stiff with age. The ink was faded. The handwriting was cramped, difficult to read.

*To whoever finds this body — I am Caradog Pritchard, archaeologist. I died in the cave beneath Bardsey Island on September 3rd, 2005. If you are reading this, I have finally returned. Please tell my daughter I was not afraid at the end. I was only sorry I could not finish the work.*

The date on the letter was September 3rd, 2005.

He had been dead for twenty-one years.

Fiona read the letter twice, then a third time. Then she looked at the body — the weathered face, the hands rough with calluses, the clothes that belonged to a different decade.

"Get Professor Marsh on the phone," she said. "Tell him we found Pritchard."

---

Chapter 12: The Missing Years

Marsh arrived in Porthdinllaen within hours. He looked at the body, read the letter, and sat down heavily in the container office.

"This changes everything."

"Explain it to me." Fiona stood by the window, her arms crossed, watching the rain. "If this is Pritchard, and he died in 2005, then his journals — the ones that stopped in 1996 —"

"Ended because he stopped writing. Not because he stopped working."

"He went underground. After he found the woman — the drowned woman — he stopped publishing, stopped corresponding, stopped everything. He went off the grid and kept working."

"For ten years."

"At least. And then he died in a cave on Bardsey Island, and his body —" Fiona paused. "His body came through a fracture point. Just like the drowned woman. Just like the skeletons in the mass grave."

Marsh nodded slowly. "Pritchard didn't just theorize about fracture points. He found one. He went through."

"And the body took twenty-one years to come back."

"Or it came back immediately, but time on the other side moves differently." Marsh rubbed his face. "We are dealing with something that does not obey the laws of physics as we understand them."

"Then we need to understand it differently." Fiona turned from the window. "We need to find that cave."

---

Chapter 13: The Cave Beneath Bardsey

Bardsey Island — Ynys Enlli in Welsh — sat at the tip of the Lleyn Peninsula, a mile offshore, its cliffs rising sheer from the sea. It had been a place of pilgrimage for a thousand years, said to be the burial place of twenty thousand saints. The currents around it were treacherous, the waters cold and deep.

Fiona chartered a boat from the village of Aberdaron. The skipper, an old man named Tomos who had fished these waters for fifty years, looked at her with skepticism.

"Bardsey Cave? You don't want to go there."

"Why not?"

"Because the tide runs through it like a river. Because the roof collapses without warning. Because people who go into that cave do not always come out."

"Did you know Caradog Pritchard?"

Tomos's face changed. "The archaeologist. Yes. He went out to the island many times. Always alone. Always came back with that look on his face — the look of a man who had seen something he could not explain."

"He died in the cave."

"I know." Tomos spat over the side. "They never found the body. Said he must have been swept out to sea. But I knew. The cave took him."

"Then you'll take us there."

Tomos looked at her for a long moment. Then he nodded. "I'll take you. But I won't go inside."

The crossing took forty minutes. The sea was rough, the wind whipping foam from the wave tops. Bardsey rose from the water like a sleeping animal, its cliffs dark with rain.

The cave was on the western side, hidden by a shelf of rock that was only exposed at low tide. Tomos brought the boat to within twenty yards and dropped anchor.

"You have three hours before the tide turns," he said. "If you're not back by then, I won't be able to come in close enough to pick you up."

"We'll be back."

The water was freezing. Fiona swam through the narrow entrance, her torch cutting a beam through the darkness. The cave opened into a chamber perhaps thirty feet across, its ceiling lost in shadow. The walls were slick with moisture, covered in what looked like — she aimed the torch more carefully — carvings.

The same spirals. The same knots. The same double-twisting patterns as the circle beneath Bodvael.

"Pritchard was here," Marsh said, surfacing beside her. "This is where he found them."

They climbed onto a ledge at the back of the chamber. And there, arranged on a flat stone, was Pritchard's equipment. A notebook, its pages pulped by damp. A compass. A diver's watch, stopped at 3:47.

And a bell.

Smaller than the one from Bodvael, but the same design. The same word. SANCTUARY.

"Two bells," Fiona said. "There were two bells."

Marsh picked it up carefully. "This one is older. The alloy is different. And the inscription — look."

The word SANCTUARY was there, but beneath it, in smaller letters, another word:

*FORGET*

"Sanctuary forget?" Fiona said.

"Or 'forget sanctuary.' Or a name. Or a warning." Marsh turned the bell over in his hands. "We need to get this out of here. And we need to find out what else Pritchard left behind."

---

Chapter 14: What the Bell Remembered

Back in Porthdinllaen, Fiona examined both bells side by side.

The differences were subtle but significant. The Bodvael bell was larger, its metal composition anomalous but still within the range of terrestrial materials. The Bardsey bell was smaller, its metal lighter, its surface marked with a patina that the conservator could not identify.

"This isn't corrosion," the conservator said, studying it under a microscope. "This is — I don't know what this is. It's as if the metal is slowly changing structure. At the molecular level."

"Changing into what?"

"I can't tell. But the process is ongoing. It's been changing since the bell was made, and it hasn't stopped."

Fiona thought of Pritchard's journals. The spirals. The fracture points. The things that had come through alive.

"What if the bells are not anchors?" she said. "What if they are keys? Each one tuned to a different fracture point, a different time?"

"The Bodvael bell dates from after the village sank. Someone made it underwater, or —"

"Or someone made it somewhere else. In another time. And brought it here."

Marsh was silent for a long moment. Then he said: "If the bells are keys, then someone has been using them. For centuries. Opening and closing the fracture points."

"Pritchard figured it out. He went through. He spent ten years on the other side — or ten minutes, or ten centuries — and then he came back and died in the cave."

"And now his body has returned to us. Along with the second bell."

Fiona looked at the two bells. One said SANCTUARY. One said SANCTUARY FORGET.

"What if the second bell is the warning?" she said. "What if the first bell opens the way, and the second bell closes it?"

"Then we need to figure out which is which before someone uses them."

---

Chapter 15: The Drowned Woman's Story

The drowned woman woke again on the fifth day.

Fiona was at her bedside in the hospital in Bangor, dozing in a chair, when she felt a hand on her arm. The woman was sitting up, her eyes clear, her voice steady.

"I don't have much time," she said. "The water is pulling me back. I can feel it."

"Tell me what you know."

"My name is Elin. Elin Price. I was a graduate student in archaeology at Cardiff. Professor Marsh was my supervisor." Her voice wavered. "In 2027 — my time — the fracture point at Bodvael opened fully. Something came through. It drowned the entire coast, from Porthdinllaen to Aberystwyth. Thousands of people died."

"2027. That's —"

"Three years from now, in your time. But I came back further. I came back to 1994 first, to warn Pritchard. But I was too late. He went through before I could stop him."

"You were the woman in the photograph. The one Pritchard took on the shore."

"Yes." Elin's eyes welled with tears. "I told him not to go. I told him the fracture was unstable. But he had to see. He had to know. And he went through."

"And you?"

"I followed him. Through the cave beneath Bardsey. We were on the other side for — I don't know how long. Time doesn't work the same way there. It's like being underwater, suspended, everything slow and dark. And there was something else there. Something that lived in the darkness between times."

"The thing that drowned Bodvael."

"Yes. It's not a creature. Not in the way we understand creatures. It's more like a pressure. A force. It exists in the gaps between moments, and when the fracture points open, it pushes through. Water is its medium. It needs water to move, to act, to kill."

"And the bells?"

"The bells were made by the first people — the Neolithic builders of the circle. They knew about the fracture points. They built the circle to contain the pressure. The bells were their tools for opening and closing the way, for study and for control. But the knowledge was lost. And when the medieval villagers found the circle, they tried to use the bells without understanding them."

"They opened the fracture."

"They opened it, and the pressure came through. Drowned the village. Sank the promontory. Hid the evidence." Elin's voice was growing weaker. "The bell they found — the one from Bodvael — it was the opening key. The second bell, the one Pritchard found in the cave, is the closing key. But it's broken."

"Broken how?"

"The inscription is incomplete. SANCTUARY FORGET — but the full inscription should say 'Sanctuary forgets those who enter, remembers those who return.' The bell was damaged. Someone tried to break it, centuries ago. They only partially succeeded."

"So the fracture points can still be closed."

"Maybe. But you need to find the missing piece. The fragment that was broken off." Elin's eyes fluttered closed. "It's in the circle. Beneath the central stone. Pritchard found it, but he died before he could retrieve it."

"How do you know this?"

"Because I was with him." Elin opened her eyes one last time. "I was the one who broke the bell."

---

Chapter 16: The Dive

Fiona made the decision that night.

She would dive into the circle.

Owen argued. Marsh argued. Even the hospital staff argued, when they learned what she was planning. But Fiona had made up her mind.

"The fragment is there. Without it, the closing key is useless. And if the fracture point opens fully — if something comes through in 2027 — thousands will die."

"You don't know that's true," Owen said. "You have the word of a woman who might be lying. Or delusional. Or—"

"She knew about Pritchard. She knew about the journals. She knew about the bells." Fiona strapped on her wetsuit. "I have to try."

The Mary Belle motored out to the site at dawn. The sea was calm, the sky clear, the buoys marking the excavation bobbing gently in the swell.

Fiona went over the side alone.

The descent was forty meters, the water darkening from green to black as she sank. Her torch cut a narrow cone of light, revealing the silt floor, the outline of the medieval street, the foundations of the church.

And ahead, the circle.

The stones loomed out of the darkness, each one twice her height, their surfaces covered in the spiral carvings. She swam to the center, where a flat stone lay half-buried in the sediment.

She had brought a crowbar. She worked it under the edge of the stone and pried. Nothing. She tried again, bracing her feet against the seabed, putting all her strength into the lever.

The stone shifted.

She dropped the crowbar and dug with her hands, the silt clouding the water, reducing visibility to zero. Her fingers touched something hard. She pulled it free.

A piece of metal, curved, broken at both ends. The missing fragment.

She turned it over in her hands. There was writing on it, the same uncial script as the bells:

*those who return.*

Sanctuary forgets those who enter, remembers those who return.

She had it.

She turned to swim back to the surface. And that was when she saw the light.

A glow, deep in the water, far below the circle. A light that should not have been there — the seabed was only fifty meters deep, and she could see the bottom. But the light was below the bottom, as if it came from a cavern that extended deeper than any survey had shown.

She hung in the water, staring. The light pulsed, slow and rhythmic, like a heartbeat.

The pressure she had felt in the cave. The thing in the darkness between times.

It was there. Below the circle. Waiting.

Fiona did not wait to see more. She kicked for the surface, the fragment clutched in her hand, and did not stop until she broke into the air and the sunlight hit her face.

---

Chapter 17: The Repair

The fragment fit perfectly.

Fiona laid the two pieces of the Bardsey bell on the conservation table in the container office. The break was clean, the edges matching. When she placed the fragment against the bell, the metal seemed to flow, the crack disappearing as if it had never been.

"Did you see that?" Marsh whispered.

"I saw it." Fiona's hands were shaking. "The metal — it repaired itself."

The bell was whole now. The full inscription was visible:

SANCTUARY FORGETS THOSE WHO ENTER, REMEMBERS THOSE WHO RETURN

The spirals around the rim now formed a complete pattern — the double spiral, turning in both directions, the impossible geometry that hurt to look at.

"It's not a bell," Fiona said slowly. "It's a tool. A device. The bell shape is just — packaging. The actual mechanism is in the metal itself. The composition. The isotopes that don't come from Earth."

"Then what do we do with it?"

Fiona picked up the bell. It was warm now, where before it had been cold. It hummed against her palm, a vibration that resonated in her bones.

"We close the fracture point. Before the pressure builds enough to break through."

"How?"

"I don't know yet. But Elin said the bell was the key. And I have both keys now."

Chapter 18: The Convergence

That night, the sea began to change.

Fiona woke at 3 AM to the sound of the wind. It was howling, the kind of wind that came with a storm, but the sky was clear, the stars visible through her window. The sound was coming from the sea itself.

She pulled on her coat and walked to the jetty.

The water was glowing.

Not the bioluminescence of algae, not the phosphorescence of breaking waves. A steady, pulsing light, rising from the depths, illuminating the bay from below. The buoys at the excavation site were dark shapes against the glow.

Owen was already there, his face pale in the strange light.

"It started an hour ago," he said. "The water temperature has dropped six degrees. The fish are fleeing — we've seen schools heading south all night."

"The fracture point."

"I don't know what else it could be."

Fiona looked at the glowing water. She thought of the light she had seen beneath the circle, the pulse like a heartbeat, the pressure of something waiting.

"Get Marsh. Get the bells. We need to go out there now."

"Now? In the dark?"

"Now. Before it's too late."

They took the Mary Belle's dinghy, a small inflatable with an outboard motor. Fiona held the two bells in her lap, their metal warm against her thighs. Marsh sat in the bow, his face set. Owen drove, his jaw tight.

The glow intensified as they approached the site. The water was translucent now, lit from below, and Fiona could see the circle clearly — the stones, the carvings, the central stone she had moved.

And in the center of the circle, a shape.

A figure, standing on the seabed, looking up at them through forty meters of water.

Elin.

Chapter 19: The Gate

"She's down there," Fiona said. "How is she down there? She was in the hospital."

"She must have returned. To the water." Marsh's voice was tight. "The tide is pulling her back."

Elin raised her hand. A gesture — not a wave, but a signal. Come.

"The bell," Fiona said. "She wants me to bring the bell."

She was already pulling off her coat, kicking off her shoes. Owen grabbed her arm.

"You can't go down there. Forty meters, at night, with whatever that thing is in the water—"

"She went down to show me the way. I have to follow."

"Fee—"

"I have to." She met his eyes. "If I don't close it now, it will open. And everything Elin warned us about will come true."

She strapped on the tank, the regulator, the weights. She put one bell in her belt, one in her hand. And she went over the side.

The water was cold, colder than it had been that morning. The glow was blinding, coming from everywhere and nowhere. Fiona swam down, following the shape of Elin, who was sinking slowly toward the circle.

The stones rose around her. The carvings seemed to move in the light, the spirals rotating, the knots tightening and loosening. The hum was audible now, a low frequency that vibrated in her chest.

Elin reached the central stone. She turned, and her face was calm, peaceful.

She pointed to the stone.

Fiona understood. She swam to the stone, braced herself, and pushed. It slid aside easily, revealing a shaft that descended into darkness.

The light was coming from the shaft.

And in the shaft, something moved.

---

Chapter 20: The Closing

Fiona hung at the edge of the shaft, the bells in her hands, the light pulsing below.

Elin appeared beside her, her hair floating, her skin translucent in the glow. She did not breathe. She did not need to.

"The gate is open," she said. Her voice came from inside Fiona's head, not through the water. "The pressure is building. You need to close it before it breaks through."

"How?"

"The bells. Both of them. Drop one into the shaft, ring the other above it. The resonance will seal the fracture."

"Which one goes down?"

"The opening key. The Bodvael bell. It's been calling to the fracture for centuries. When it goes back, the gate will close behind it."

Fiona looked at the Bodvael bell. SANCTUARY. The bell that had come up from the seabed on her first day of excavation, the bell that had started everything.

"And the other?"

"The other seals the seal. Makes it permanent."

Fiona took a breath. The air in her tank was running low — fifteen minutes, maybe less.

She held the Bodvael bell over the shaft. It vibrated in her hand, eager, hungry.

She let go.

The bell fell into the light, tumbling end over end, its sound echoing through the water. The light flared, brighter than the sun, and Fiona had to close her eyes against it.

She felt Elin's hand on her arm. Ring the second bell. Now.

Fiona raised the Bardsey bell. She did not know how to ring it underwater. But when she struck it against her palm, the sound came anyway — a clear, pure note that cut through the water, through the light, through everything.

The light faded. The vibration stopped. The water went dark.

Fiona opened her eyes.

The shaft was closed. The stone had slid back into place. The circle was still, the carvings dark.

And Elin was gone.

Arc III: The Return

---

Chapter 21: The Morning After

Dawn broke gray and quiet over Porthdinllaen. The sea was flat, the sky empty, the wind still. The glow was gone. The water was ordinary water, cold and dark and unremarkable.

Fiona sat on the jetty, wrapped in a blanket, staring at the horizon. She had surfaced an hour before, alone, the Bardsey bell still clutched in her hand. Owen had pulled her into the dinghy, wrapped her in dry clothes, told her she was the biggest fool he had ever known and also the bravest.

Now she was waiting.

For what, she did not know. For the fracture point to reopen. For Elin to surface. For the world to end.

But the sun rose, the gulls cried, and nothing happened.

Marsh came down the jetty with two cups of tea. He sat beside her, silent for a long moment.

"The hospital called," he said. "Elin Price checked herself out at 2 AM. The nurses said she looked fine. Better than fine. They said she looked — whole."

"She's alive?"

"Alive and gone. They don't know where she went."

Fiona nodded. She had expected this. Elin had fulfilled her purpose. She had brought the message, led Fiona to the bells, shown her how to close the gate. Now she was free to live whatever life she had left.

"What about the fracture point?" Fiona asked.

"The measurements are stable. The carbon dates are coming back normal. Sediment samples are showing nothing unusual." Marsh paused. "Whatever you did down there, it worked. The anomaly is gone."

Fiona looked at the Bardsey bell, still warm in her hand. The inscription was clear: SANCTUARY FORGETS THOSE WHO ENTER, REMEMBERS THOSE WHO RETURN.

"Elin said the closing bell makes the seal permanent."

"Then it's done. The fracture is closed. The pressure is contained."

"And the future she came from? The drowning of the coast in 2027?"

"The future is not written. She came back to change it. Perhaps she succeeded."

Fiona wanted to believe this. But she could still feel the vibration of the bell in her bones, the hum of the gate as it closed, the glimpse of the light in the shaft. The something that lived in the darkness between times was still there. It had not been destroyed. It had only been sealed.

"One day," she said, "someone else will find the circle. Someone else will dig where they should not dig. And the gate will open again."

"Yes," Marsh said. "But that is not your problem. That is a problem for another generation."

"I should destroy the bell."

"The bell is the only thing that can close the gate. If you destroy it, the next opening will be permanent."

She looked at the bell. Then she looked at the sea.

"What do I do with it, then?"

"I think you know."

Fiona stood. She walked to the end of the jetty. She held the Bardsey bell over the water.

But she did not drop it.

She brought it back to her chest, held it close, and turned away from the sea.

"Not yet," she said. "I need to understand it first. What it is. Where it came from. Who made it."

"The Neolithic builders."

"Yes. But they were human. They made the bells from something that did not come from Earth. I need to know where that something came from."

Marsh studied her. "You're not done."

"No. I'm not done. The gate is closed, but the mystery is not solved. I need to find the source. The metal. The isotopes that don't match anything terrestrial."

"Where would you even begin to look?"

Fiona looked at the bell in her hands. The light caught the inscription, the spirals, the pattern that hurt to look at.

"There's a reference in Pritchard's journals. A place he mentioned once, never returned to. A Neolithic burial mound on Anglesey, where he found the Roman coins."

"The site that destroyed his career."

"The same." Fiona met Marsh's eyes. "I think he found more than coins. I think he found where the metal came from."

Chapter 22: Anglesey

The burial mound at Bryn Celli Ddu stood in a field, grass-covered, its entrance a dark slit in the earth. It was five thousand years old, older than the pyramids, older than Stonehenge.

Fiona stood at the entrance, the Bardsey bell in her bag, Pritchard's journals in her hands.

"It's a passage tomb," Marsh said, coming up beside her. "Built around 3000 BC. The alignment is solstitial — the winter sun shines directly into the chamber."

"Pritchard excavated here in 1987. He found Roman coins in the Neolithic layer. And he found something else." Fiona opened the journal to the marked page. "He doesn't describe it clearly. He calls it 'the stone that does not belong.' A piece of worked metal, embedded in the wall of the chamber, too deep to remove."

"And he never published this."

"He was already being discredited. The Roman coins were enough to destroy his reputation. If he had also claimed to have found worked metal in a Neolithic tomb — metal that didn't match any known alloy — he would have been finished."

Fiona ducked through the entrance, crawling along the narrow passage. The chamber opened into a hexagon, its walls made of standing stones, its ceiling a single massive capstone.

She shone her torch around the walls.

And there, set into the surface of the eastern stone, was a piece of metal.

Small, no larger than her palm, embedded in the rock as if it had been poured into a crack and left to cool. The surface was the same anomalous alloy as the bells.

"It's here," she said. "Pritchard found it. He never told anyone."

She touched the metal. It was warm.

And beneath her fingers, the stone began to move.

---

Chapter 23: The Stone That Did Not Belong

The wall of the chamber shifted.

Not visibly — not like a door opening. But Fiona felt it. A change in the pressure, the temperature, the quality of the air. The torch flickered. The shadows rearranged themselves.

"Get out," Marsh said. "Get out now."

But Fiona did not move. She was looking at the metal embedded in the stone. It had changed. The surface was no longer dull and weathered. It was bright, reflective, like a mirror.

And in the mirror, she saw something.

A landscape. Not Wales. Not any landscape she recognized. A plain of black rock under a red sky. A sea of something that was not water. A structure in the distance — a circle, like the one at Bodvael, but much larger.

The image lasted three seconds. Then the metal went dark, the pressure returned to normal, and the chamber was just a chamber again.

Fiona stumbled backward, her heart pounding.

"What did you see?" Marsh asked.

"Another world." Her voice was shaking. "The metal — it's not from Earth. It's from somewhere else. And the gate — the circle at Bodvael — it doesn't just open through time. It opens through space. Through dimensions."

"The Neolithic builders brought it through."

"Or it was always here. A deposit. A fragment of another world, embedded in this one, waiting to be found." Fiona looked at the metal in the stone. "Pritchard knew. He found the coins, yes, but they were a distraction. The real discovery was this."

"And he never told anyone."

"Because no one would have believed him." She touched the metal again, gently. It was warm, but not threatening. It hummed at a frequency she could feel but not hear.

"The bells are made from this," she said. "The same material. The carvings — the spirals, the knots — they're not decoration. They're instructions."

"Instructions for what?"

"For using the material. For opening and closing the gates." She stepped back. "The Neolithic builders weren't just building tombs. They were building control systems. The circle at Bodvael, the passage tomb here, the cave on Bardsey — they're all connected. They're all part of the same network."

"A network for what?"

"For traveling between worlds. Or for keeping something out." She looked at Marsh. "Maybe both."

Chapter 24: The Network

Back at the museum in Cardiff, Fiona laid out everything she knew.

The map of Wales with the seven fracture points Pritchard had identified. The two bells. The metal sample from Bryn Celli Ddu. The journals. The photographs. The mass grave at Bodvael. The bodies from the future.

"There are seven fracture points," she said, "but the Neolithic builders constructed structures at only five of them. The circle at Bodvael. The passage tomb on Anglesey. The cave on Bardsey. And two others — one on the Lleyn Peninsula, one near St. David's."

"The five control points."

"Yes. The builders used the metal — whatever it is — to create a network that could monitor and regulate the fracture points. The bells were the interface. Each one tuned to a specific frequency."

"And the material itself?"

"The metal is not from this reality. It came through one of the fracture points, or maybe it's the fracture point itself — a physical manifestation of the tear between worlds."

Marsh rubbed his temples. "The more we learn, the less we understand."

"That's the nature of the work." Fiona unfolded a map with the five sites marked. "If I'm right, the network is still active. The fracture point at Bodvael is closed, but the others are still open. The pressure is still building."

"Then we need to close them all."

"Yes. But I don't have enough bells. I only have one."

Marsh looked at the map, then at the bell, then at Fiona.

"Then we find the others."

Chapter 25: The Lleyn Peninsula

The fourth site was a standing stone on the shoulder of a hill, overlooking the sea. It was unremarkable — one of dozens of such stones scattered across the Welsh landscape, ignored by tourists and farmers alike.

Fiona had driven past it a hundred times without noticing.

Now she stood before it, the Bardsey bell in her hand. The stone was seven feet tall, its surface weathered smooth. But at its base, half-hidden by grass, was a carving.

A spiral. The same spiral. The double-twist.

She knelt and pressed her hand to the ground. The soil was warm.

"There's something here," she said.

They dug for three hours, carefully, following the line of the spiral. A foot down, they found the first stone. A foot deeper, the second. A structure, buried, intact.

A chamber, lined with the same anomalous metal, its walls inscribed with the same patterns.

And on a shelf carved into the eastern wall, a bell.

Fiona picked it up. It was the same design as the others, but the inscription was different. Instead of SANCTUARY, it read:

REMEMBER

"Three bells," she said. "Sanctuary, Forget, Remember. There must be five — one for each fracture point."

"The missing two?"

"Still out there. Hidden." She looked at the chamber, the network, the structure that had been buried for five thousand years. "Pritchard found two. The Neolithic builders left five. We have three."

"Enough to close the remaining fracture points?"

"Enough to start."

Chapter 26: The Pattern

Fiona spent the next month analyzing the three bells.

The National Museum gave her a lab. Marsh brought in a materials scientist from Cambridge, a metallurgist from Imperial College, a linguist who specialized in prehistoric symbols. They examined the bells from every angle, tested the metal, decoded the carvings.

The findings were extraordinary.

The metal was not an alloy in any conventional sense. It was a composite — a lattice of atoms arranged in a pattern that did not occur in nature. The lattice could store energy, like a battery, and release it at specific frequencies. The bells were not just keys. They were transmitters.

"The spirals are wave guides," the physicist said. "They direct the energy in a specific pattern. When you ring the bell, you're not producing sound — you're producing a field. A field that interacts with the fabric of spacetime."

"Can you reproduce the metal?" Fiona asked.

"We can analyze it. We cannot reproduce it. The atomic structure requires conditions that do not exist on this planet."

"So we're limited to what we have."

"Yes. Three bells. Three fracture points remaining."

"Then we need to find the other two."

The search took them across Wales. The fifth bell, inscribed FORGET, was found in a well on the outskirts of St. David's, where it had been thrown in the 14th century, hidden from whatever had come through the gate.

The sixth bell was not found.

The seventh fracture point, listed in Pritchard's journal, had no bell. The Neolithic builders had either not finished the network, or the bell had been removed permanently, or it had never existed.

"Six fracture points, five bells, seven sites," Marsh said. "The math does not work."

"Maybe the builders intended seven bells. Maybe seven was the number. But they only completed five before something stopped them."

"Before they vanished."

"Yes." Fiona looked at the four bells now in her possession. SANCTUARY. FORGET. REMEMBER. FORGET. "We have four. The fifth is missing. The sixth — the seventh point — has no bell."

"Can we close six points with four bells?"

"I don't know. But we have to try."

Chapter 27: The News from Cambridge

The call came from Marsh, late on a Sunday night.

"Fiona. I found something."

"Where are you?"

"Cambridge. The library. I've been going through Pritchard's papers — the ones his daughter gave us — and I found a reference I missed. A name."

"What name?"

"Morgan. Dr. Huw Morgan. Pritchard's student, 1970s. He was with Pritchard on the excavation at Bryn Celli Ddu. He was the one who found the coins."

"Where is he now?"

"That's the interesting part." Marsh's voice was tight. "He's alive. Living in a nursing home in Aberystwyth. He has Alzheimer's. But his daughter — his daughter is a curator at the National Museum. She's been going through his papers."

"And?"

"And she found something. A journal. Not Pritchard's — Huw's. He kept a record of the excavation, separate from Pritchard's official notes. And in it, he describes something Pritchard never mentioned."

"What?"

"A seventh bell."

The line went silent. Fiona felt the hairs on her arms stand up.

"There was a sixth bell. A seventh fracture point was built. And the bell was made."

"Where is it now?"

"The journal doesn't say. But there's a clue. Huw wrote that the bell was 'taken to the place where the water remembers.'"

Fiona closed her eyes. "Cantre'r Gwaelod."

"The legend. The sunken kingdom off the coast of Ceredigion. The Welsh Atlantis."

"It's not a legend. It's a memory. The fracture points — all of them — are underwater. And there's a sixth bell in one of them."

Chapter 28: The Search for Cantre'r Gwaelod

Cantre'r Gwaelod was a story Fiona had known since childhood. The lost land beneath Cardigan Bay, a kingdom of sixteen cities, drowned by the negligence of a drunken gatekeeper who let the sea in.

Every child in Wales knew the tale.

But like most legends, it had a kernel of truth.

"There was a real land," Fiona told the team, gathered in the container office. "Before the sea levels rose after the last ice age, the coast extended much further west. There were forests, settlements, people. The legend of Cantre'r Gwaelod is a folk memory of that drowning."

"But you think there's a Neolithic structure out there?"

"I think the builders of the network placed sites at every fracture point. If Cantre'r Gwaelod is a fracture point — and the legend of sudden drowning suggests it might be — then there should be a structure. And a bell."

The search area was vast — Cardigan Bay stretched for miles, shallow and treacherous, its seabed littered with the remains of drowned forests. Fiona acquired the full sonar survey from the British Geological Survey and spent days combing through it.

She found nothing.

Then Owen, working on a hunch, pulled the old fishing charts — hand-drawn, passed down through generations of skippers, marked with the locations of wrecks and hazards.

"What's this?" he said, pointing to an annotation near the mouth of the Ystwyth River. "Sarn Gynfelyn."

A submerged reef. But the name—"Sarn" meant causeway. "Gynfelyn" — the same root as the saint of Bodvael.

"That's not a reef," Fiona said. "That's a road."

---

Chapter 29: The Road Beneath the Sea

The causeway was nine feet below the surface at low tide, a ridge of stone running in a straight line for nearly a mile. At its end, the sonar showed a structure — a circle, like the one at Bodvael, larger and more complex.

Fiona led the dive team out at dawn. The water was cold, the visibility poor, the current tugging at them as they descended.

The circle was massive. Fifty stones, each one the size of a car, arranged in a ring that measured a hundred feet across. The carvings were more elaborate than at Bodvael — not just spirals, but figures. Human figures, their arms raised, their faces turned toward the center of the circle.

And in the center, on a pedestal of stone, a bell.

The sixth bell.

Fiona swam to it. The inscription was complete, undamaged, the letters sharp:

SANCTUARY FORGETS THOSE WHO ENTER, REMEMBERS THOSE WHO RETURN

The full inscription. The one that should have been on the Bardsey bell.

She lifted the bell from its pedestal. The carvings on the pedestal were different — a map, showing the locations of all seven fracture points, connected by lines that formed a pattern.

A pattern that matched the constellation of stars that had set behind the hill of Bryn Celli Ddu on the winter solstice.

The builders had mapped the network to the stars.

She surfaced, the bell in her arms, and for the first time in weeks, she felt something like hope.

---

Chapter 30: The Star Map

The full inscription changed everything.

"Sanctuary forgets those who enter, remembers those who return." The phrase was a warning, yes, but also an instruction. The network was not just for sealing the fracture points. It was for navigating them.

"The builders traveled through the gates," Fiona said. "They went to the other side — the other world, the other time — and they came back. The bells were their navigation system."

"They traveled between worlds?"

"The carvings on the pedestal show a star map. The lines connecting the fracture points mirror the pattern of stars that rises over Bryn Celli Ddu at the winter solstice. The fracture points are not random. They're aligned to the stars. To specific dates."

"Gateways that open at specific times."

"Yes. And the bells — the full inscription is a kind of equation. Forgetting and remembering. Entering and returning. The bells allow you to pass through the gate and come back."

Marsh studied the star map. "What were they doing, on the other side?"

"I don't know. But I know where we can find out."

"Where?"

Fiona pointed to the seventh point on the map. The one without a bell. "There. The original fracture point. The one where the metal first came through."

"Where is it?"

"In the deepest part of Cardigan Bay. A place the fishermen avoid. They call it the Maw."

Arc IV: The Maw

---

Chapter 31: The Deepest Place

The Maw was a trench in the seabed, a mile wide and three hundred feet deep, where the currents converged and the water turned black. No fishing boat went there. The sonar showed nothing but a flat, featureless bottom.

But Fiona knew there was something down there. The star map showed it. The network converged there.

She hired a research vessel from the University of Bangor, equipped with deep-water ROVs and side-scan sonar. The crew was skeptical — the Maw had been surveyed before, and nothing had ever been found.

But on the third day, the sonar picked up an anomaly.

A structure, at the bottom of the trench. A circle, larger than any of the others. And in the center, something that was not stone.

The ROV went down at dawn. Fiona watched the feed on the monitor, her hands gripping the edge of the table.

The water darkened as the ROV descended. The lights cut through the murk, revealing a seabed of fine silt, undisturbed for millennia.

And then the structure emerged.

The circle was enormous. The stones were not standing — they were fallen, toppled, broken. The center of the circle was filled with rubble, as if something had collapsed from above.

And in the rubble, a light. The same light Fiona had seen beneath Bodvael. The same pulse, the same frequency.

"The gate is open," she said. "It never closed."

---

Chapter 32: The Descent

Fiona made the decision within the hour.

"I'm going down."

"Three hundred feet?" Owen shook his head. "That's a technical dive. Mixed gas, staged decompression, the whole package. You're not equipped for it."

"Then get me equipped. There's a hyperbaric chamber at the Royal Navy base in Holyhead. I can use it."

"You're talking about days of preparation. And even then —"

"Owen. The gate is open. The pressure is building. If I don't close it, everything Elin warned us about will happen."

"You don't know that. You don't know what's down there."

"I know the network converges at the Maw. I know the gate has been open for five thousand years. And I know the bells can close it." She held up the six bells. "Every bell I have found. The network was designed for seven. I have six. That will have to be enough."

Marsh stepped forward. "If you're going, I'm coming with you."

"You're seventy-one years old."

"And I've been studying fracture points since before you were born. I've earned the right to see what's on the other side."

Fiona looked at him. His face was set, his eyes clear. He was not going to be argued with.

"All right. We dive tomorrow."

The preparation took eighteen hours. Fiona and Marsh went through the dive protocols, the gas mixtures, the emergency procedures. The Navy lent them a deep-diving rig, a hyperbaric chamber, and a dive supervisor who looked at them like they were insane.

"Last chance to call this off," the supervisor said.

"We're not calling it off."

The ROV was already at the bottom, its lights illuminating the fallen circle, the rubble, the light pulsing from beneath. Fiona watched the feed as she suited up.

"There's a cavity beneath the rubble," the ROV operator said. "The light is coming from inside it. The structure seems to be — hollow."

"A chamber. Like the one on Anglesey, but deeper. Larger."

"Could be the source."

Fiona checked her tanks. Trimix, for the depth. She had thirty minutes of bottom time, plus decompression stops that would take hours.

"Ready?" Marsh asked. His voice was steady.

"Ready."

They went over the side together.

---

Chapter 33: The Chamber Beneath

The descent was long and dark. The water temperature dropped steadily, the pressure building, the light from the surface fading until there was nothing but the beams of their torches.

The circle rose from the darkness below. Fallen stones, broken carvings, the rubble heap at the center.

And the light. Pulsing from beneath the rubble.

Fiona landed on the seabed, the silt rising around her in clouds. She signaled to Marsh, and they moved toward the rubble.

The cavity was narrow, barely wide enough to squeeze through. Fiona went first, pulling herself between the fallen stones, her tanks scraping against the rock.

The chamber opened suddenly, and she was floating in empty water.

It was vast. The torch beam could not reach the walls. The floor was smooth, paved, covered in carvings that glowed with their own light. The carvings were not spirals. They were faces. Hundreds of faces, arranged in concentric circles, their expressions frozen in states of wonder and terror.

And in the center of the chamber, a pedestal.

Empty.

"This is where the seventh bell was," Fiona said, her voice distorted by the regulator. "It's gone."

"Taken?"

"Or never placed. The builders brought the metal through here. They made the bells in this chamber. And then they left one empty. Purposefully."

"Why?"

Fiona swam closer to the pedestal. The carvings on its surface were different from the others. Not faces, but symbols. A language she did not recognize.

But beneath the symbols, a pattern. The double spiral. The gate.

"Look at this." She traced the spiral with her finger. "It's not a carving. It's a mechanism. The pedestal is a key point in the network. The bell was meant to sit here, regulating the flow."

"But the bell is missing."

"Then the network has been incomplete from the beginning. The gate at the Maw has never been fully closed."

She looked at the six bells she carried. Six bells, seven points. The math was wrong.

"We need to complete the network," she said. "We need to place the bells at all seven points simultaneously."

"Impossible. We don't have enough divers. We don't have enough time."

"Then we do it here. We place all six bells at the network's center. The resonance will propagate through the connected sites."

"Will that work?"

Fiona looked at the empty pedestal, the carvings, the light that pulsed from somewhere deep beneath the floor.

"It has to."

---

Chapter 34: The Sixth Bell

Fiona placed the bells in a circle around the pedestal, matching the pattern of the star map. SANCTUARY. FORGET. REMEMBER. FORGET. Each one tuned to a different frequency, a different fracture point.

When she placed the sixth bell — the Cantre'r Gwaelod bell, with its full inscription — the light in the chamber changed.

The carvings on the floor began to glow brighter. The faces shifted, their expressions moving from terror to something else. Not peace. Recognition.

"The network is responding," Marsh said.

"The seven bells are meant to work together. Even incomplete, the six create a resonance."

She stood before the empty pedestal. The seventh point. The missing bell.

"Pritchard knew," she said. "He spent ten years looking for the seventh bell. He died in that cave because he was still searching."

"And you believe it exists?"

"I believe the network was designed for seven. The builders would not have left it incomplete by accident."

"But where is it?"

Fiona looked at the carvings on the pedestal. The symbols. The language she did not recognize.

Then she understood.

"It's not a language," she said. "It's coordinates. The seventh bell is not on Earth. It's on the other side. Through the gate."

"The fracture point?"

"The original one. The place where the metal came from."

Marsh was silent for a long moment. Then he said: "You're going through."

"The gate is open. The bell is there. I have to retrieve it."

"The pressure — the thing in the darkness—"

"Is still there. But the network needs the seventh bell to close permanently. Without it, the seal is temporary. The pressure will build again."

Fiona turned to face the pedestal. The light was coming from beneath it, from a shaft that descended into the rock.

The gate.

She picked up the Bardsey bell — the closing key, the one that sealed the seal. And she held it over the shaft.

"I don't know how long I'll be gone. Time on the other side moves differently."

"Fiona—"

"Keep the bells ringing. Keep the resonance active. I'll be back."

She stepped into the shaft.

---

Chapter 35: The Other Side

The descent took an instant and an eternity.

Fiona could not tell if she was falling, swimming, or floating. There was no up, no down, no light, no sound. Only the sensation of movement, of passing through something that was not water, not air, not anything she had a name for.

And then she was standing.

On solid ground. Under a red sky.

The landscape stretched before her — a plain of black rock, cracked and fissured, with a sea of something that was not water lapping at its edges. The sea was silver, reflective, like liquid mercury.

She turned. Behind her, the gate was a circle of light in the air, shimmering like a heat haze.

She was through.

The air was cold, thin, hard to breathe. She pulled the regulator from her mouth and tasted — nothing. Even the atmosphere was different.

She walked forward. The black rock crunched beneath her boots. The silver sea rippled without wind.

And in the distance, a structure.

A circle. Like the ones on Earth, but built from the same metal as the bells. Shining, untarnished, its carvings sharp and new.

At its center, on a pedestal identical to the one in the Maw, a bell.

The seventh bell.

Fiona approached slowly. The bell was larger than the others, its surface polished, its inscription in the same uncial script:

SANCTUARY FORGETS THOSE WHO ENTER, REMEMBERS THOSE WHO RETURN

But beneath the script, another line, in a language she did not recognize:

*For those who seek, the path remains.*

She reached for the bell.

And a voice said, in English: "You should not have come here."

Fiona spun around.

A figure stood behind her. A woman, tall, her skin pale, her eyes black. She wore robes of a material Fiona had never seen — dark, flowing, covered in the same spirals as the bells.

"Who are you?"

"I am the last of the builders." The woman's voice was flat, without emotion. "I have been waiting for someone to come through the gate for five thousand years."

"You're — you're from the civilization that built the network."

"We built the gates. We built the bells. We crossed between worlds, studying, learning, trading." The woman's eyes swept across the landscape. "And then we found this place. The source of the metal. The source of the power that made the gates possible."

"What happened?"

"The metal is not a resource. It is a prison. A cage for something that cannot be destroyed, only contained. We built the gates to reach it. We built the bells to control it. But the thing in the darkness — the pressure — it broke free. It followed us through the gates. To Earth."

"The drowning of Bodvael."

"One of many. It feeds on water. It takes the memories of the drowned and wears them like masks. It has been growing, waiting, gathering strength for five thousand years."

"And the seventh bell?"

"Was meant to seal the source permanently. But we could not place it. The thing in the darkness guards this place. I was the only one who stayed."

Fiona looked at the bell. Then at the woman. The last of the builders.

"If I take the bell back, can I close the gate permanently?"

"The gate can be closed. But you must ring the seventh bell at the moment of sealing. Its resonance will collapse the fracture points permanently."

"And the thing in the darkness?"

"Will be contained. Not destroyed. It cannot be destroyed. But it cannot escape."

Fiona stepped forward and lifted the seventh bell from its pedestal. It was lighter than she expected, its surface warm, its hum filling her chest.

"I will take it back."

The woman smiled. It was not a happy expression. "You are brave. But bravery is not enough. You must also be willing to forget."

"Sanctuary forgets those who enter."

"Yes. When you return through the gate, you will remember nothing. The crossing will take your memories — all of them. You will know only what you need to know to ring the bell."

"The inscription."

"You will become a vessel. A carrier of the closing key. Your mind will be empty, but your hands will know what to do."

Fiona looked at the bell. She thought of her life — the years of work, the discoveries, the people she loved. Owen. Marsh. Elin. The Mary Belle. The sea.

"Will I remember any of it?"

"Some may return. In fragments. In dreams. But the crossing will take most."

Fiona closed her eyes. Then she opened them.

"Then let's go."

---

Chapter 36: The Crossing

She stepped through the gate with the seventh bell in her hands.

The crossing was different this time. The darkness was not empty. It was full of voices, faces, moments from her life. Her mother's hands. Her father's laugh. The first time she saw the sea. The first time she touched a Roman coin in the dirt. The first time she understood that the past was not a dead thing but a living country.

The voices grew louder. They pulled at her, begged her to stay.

But she kept moving, holding the bell, her eyes fixed on the light ahead.

And then she was through.

She was in the chamber beneath the Maw, standing at the empty pedestal, the seventh bell in her hands.

Marsh was there. His face was drawn, his eyes red.

"Fiona?" His voice was tentative. "Do you — do you remember me?"

She looked at him. She knew him. The name was there, just out of reach. But the feeling — trust, respect, affection — was intact.

"Professor Marsh." Her voice sounded strange to her. "We need to place the bell."

"You remember?"

"I remember enough."

She lifted the seventh bell and placed it on the pedestal.

The chamber shook. The light flared. The carvings on the floor blazed with a brilliance that should have blinded her. But she could still see.

Six bells around the pedestal. One on it. Seven fracture points. Seven keys.

Complete.

"Ring it," she said. "Ring them all."

He did not understand, but he followed her lead. He struck the Cantre'r Gwaelod bell with a stone. The note was pure, sustained.

Fiona struck the seventh bell. The note harmonized.

And the sea began to sing.

---

Chapter 37: The Sealing

The sound traveled through the water, through the rock, through the network of fracture points that connected the seven sites across Wales and beyond. Every bell resonated. Every gate pulsed with light.

And then, one by one, the gates closed.

Night fell on Porthdinllaen. The water that had glowed for five thousand years went dark.

Fiona surfaced with the seventh bell in her hands. She did not remember the crossing. She did not remember the woman on the other side, the black rock, the silver sea. She remembered only fragments — a red sky, a voice, a warning.

But she remembered the bell.

She carried it to the Mary Belle, laid it on the deck beside the others, and sat down heavily. Marsh sat beside her.

"It's done," he said.

"Is it?"

The sea was flat, quiet, ordinary. The gulls were crying. The sun was setting.

"Whatever was in the darkness," Marsh said, "it's sealed. The fracture points are closed. The network is complete."

Fiona looked at the seven bells, lined up on the deck of the Mary Belle. Seven keys to a gate that should never have been opened.

"What do we do with them?" she asked.

"We have to keep them safe. Separate them. Hide them. So that no one ever opens the gate again."

"Seven bells. Seven hiding places."

"Yes."

Fiona picked up the seventh bell. It hummed against her palm. "This one goes back to Cantre'r Gwaelod. To the deep place where it was always meant to be."

"And the others?"

"The other six go with it. To the fracture points they were built for. The network stays intact."

She looked at the sea, the sky, the world she had saved.

"It's over," she said. "The tides can rest."

---

Chapter 38: The Return

Over the following weeks, Fiona and Marsh returned each bell to its original site.

The Bodvael bell went back to the circle forty meters down, placed on its pedestal by Fiona's own hands. The Lleyn bell went back to the buried chamber. The Anglesey bell went back to Bryn Celli Ddu. The Bardsey bell went back to the cave.

And the seventh bell — the largest, the heaviest, the source bell — went back to the Maw, to the chamber beneath the fallen circle, to the empty pedestal where it had always belonged.

Fiona made the dive alone. She swam through the narrow cavity, entered the vast chamber, and placed the bell on the pedestal.

The carvings on the floor flared once, briefly, and then settled.

The network was complete.

She surfaced, shedding water, and climbed onto the research vessel. Marsh was waiting.

"It's done."

"All seven?"

"All seven."

Marsh nodded slowly. "And your memory? Has any of it returned?"

Fiona was silent for a moment. She remembered fragments. The red sky. The black rock. The woman who had spoken of forgetting.

"Some," she said. "Not much."

"Will it come back?"

"I don't know." She looked at the sea. "Maybe the gate took what it needed. Maybe it will return what it borrowed."

"Time will tell."

"Yes." She felt the hum of the seventh bell still vibrating in her bones. "Time will tell."

---

Chapter 39: The Aftermath

The excavation at Bodvael was wound down. The funding ran out. The university moved on to other projects. The Mary Belle returned to its normal business — fishing charters and sea tours.

Fiona stayed in Porthdinllaen.

She took a job at the local museum, cataloguing artifacts, giving talks to school groups. She wrote a paper on the Bodvael excavation, carefully omitting anything that could not be explained by conventional archaeology. The anomalous carbon dates. The future bodies. The bells.

She kept those secrets in a locked drawer in her desk, along with Pritchard's journals and the photograph of Elin on the shore.

Owen visited often. They took long walks along the coast, watching the tide rise and fall.

"You're different," he said one evening, as they stood on the jetty where the drowned woman had first appeared.

"Different how?"

"Quieter. Like you're listening for something only you can hear."

"Maybe I am."

"Do you hear it?"

Fiona looked at the sea. The water was calm, dark, ordinary. But beneath it, she knew, the network was still active. The bells were still vibrating. The gates were sealed, but not destroyed.

"Sometimes," she said. "When the tide is low."

---

Chapter 40: The Fragments

The memories came back slowly.

A flash of red sky. The sound of a voice speaking a language she did not know. The weight of the seventh bell in her hands. The face of a woman with black eyes and robes of spirals.

She wrote them down in a journal, trying to piece them together like a broken mosaic.

The woman had said something. Something important. Something about the thing in the darkness.

Fiona searched her memory, but the fragments would not cohere.

Then, one night, she woke from a dream with the words on her lips:

*It cannot be destroyed. Only contained.*

She sat up in bed, her heart pounding. The dream was already fading, but the words remained.

*Only contained.*

She thought of the bells. The network. The fracture points sealed but not destroyed.

What if something could break the seal? What if, in a thousand years, another generation found the bells and tried to use them?

The question haunted her. But there was no answer. Not for her.

She had done what she could. The rest was up to the tides.

---

END OF ARC IV

Arc V: What the Tides Forgot

---

Chapter 41: The Letter from Pritchard

The letter arrived in the spring, forwarded from the University of Cambridge, addressed to Dr. Fiona Rees in handwriting she did not recognize.

She opened it in her office at the museum, the afternoon sun streaming through the window.

*Dear Dr. Rees,*

*I am writing to you because my father, Caradog Pritchard, asked me to send this letter if certain conditions were met. He wrote it in 2005, shortly before his death. His instructions were clear: the letter should be sent to whoever recovered his remains from the cave beneath Bardsey Island.*

*I did not know what he meant by 'recovered his remains.' Not until I read the news about your excavation at Bodvael. Not until I saw your photograph in the paper.*

*I believe you are the person my father was waiting for.*

*— Angharad Pritchard*

Fiona unfolded the second sheet of paper. The handwriting was faint, the ink faded.

*To the one who finds me:*

*If you are reading this, you have found the bells. You have been to the chamber beneath the Maw. You have met the woman on the other side.*

*You know the truth now. But you do not know all of it.*

*The gate at the Maw is not the only gate. The network of seven is not the only network. There are others, across the world, built by the same civilization that built the one in Wales. I found references in the chamber — inscriptions I could not translate, maps I could not read. But I understood enough to know that the thing in the darkness is not unique. There are others like it. Prisoners of the metal, contained by the gates.*

*The bells are not just keys. They are warnings. The civilization that built them did not vanish. They left. They went through the gates, sealed them behind them, and never came back.*

*They knew what would happen if the gates were reopened.*

*I went through, Dr. Rees. I spent — I do not know how long — on the other side. Time is different there. But I learned enough to understand: the gate at the Maw must never be opened again. Not for any reason.*

*I am writing this in the cave on Bardsey, waiting for the end. The crossing cost me everything. My mind. My health. My life. But I do not regret it. I saw what lies beyond. And I know now that the human world — this small, fragile world of land and sea and sky — is worth protecting.*

*Guard the bells. Guard the gate. And never, ever ring them all at once.*

*— Caradog Pritchard*

Fiona lowered the letter. Her hands were shaking.

*Never ring them all at once.*

But she had. She had rung all seven. She had sealed the network by completing it.

What had she done?

---

Chapter 42: The Warning

She called Marsh. She read him the letter over the phone. His silence was long and heavy.

"Pritchard knew," he said finally. "He knew what the full resonance would do."

"And he warned us not to do it."

"The letter says never ring them all at once. But we did. We completed the network and sounded all seven bells."

"Was that wrong?"

"I don't know. The fracture points are sealed. The pressure is contained. But Pritchard was afraid of something else. Something that happens when you ring all seven."

"Then we need to understand what that something is."

"How? The woman on the other side — she would know. But you cannot go back. The gate is sealed."

Fiona looked at the letter in her hands. Pritchard's final message. His last warning.

"There's a reference in his journals. A phrase he used. 'The song of the spheres.' He wrote it in the margins, over and over, but he never explained what it meant."

"The resonance of the bells."

"Yes. When you ring all seven, the notes harmonize. The network resonates. And if Pritchard was right — if the gates in Wales are part of a larger network — then that resonance might travel."

"To other gates. Other networks."

"To other worlds."

Marsh's voice was quiet. "What have we done, Fiona?"

"I don't know. But I'm going to find out."

---

Chapter 43: The Song

She returned to the Maw.

Not to dive — the gate was sealed, the cavity beneath the rubble closed. But to listen.

She sat on the deck of the research vessel, suspended above the deepest part of Cardigan Bay, and lowered a hydrophone into the water.

For hours, there was nothing. The ordinary sounds of the sea. Fish. Current. Wind.

And then, at sunset, a change.

A low note, rising from the depths. A hum, barely audible, vibrating through the water, through the hull of the boat, through Fiona's bones.

The song.

She recorded it. She analyzed it. The frequency was too low to hear with the naked ear, but instruments could detect it. A single, sustained note, pulsing in a rhythm that matched the heartbeat of a human at rest.

The network was singing.

She sent the recording to Marsh, to the physicist at Cambridge, to the linguist who had studied the carvings. No one could explain it.

But the linguist sent back a message that chilled her.

"The rhythm," she wrote. "It's not random. It's a pattern. A repeating sequence. I ran it through a pattern recognition algorithm, and it matched something."

"What?"

"I don't know how to tell you this. But the pattern matches the genetic code of Homo sapiens. The arrangement of base pairs on human DNA."

The bell was singing the human genome.

The network was playing a song encoded in the deepest structures of life itself.

Fiona sat in her office, the recording playing on a loop, the low hum filling the room.

Pritchard's words came back to her. *The bells are not just keys. They are warnings.*

A warning encoded in the language of life.

A song that only humanity could hear.

---

Chapter 44: The Translation

Fiona spent the next month working with the linguist, the physicist, a biologist from the Marine Institute. They analyzed the pattern, decoded the sequence, and slowly, painstakingly, began to understand.

"The song is a message," the linguist said. "A repeated phrase, in a language that shares features with the inscriptions on the bells. I think it's the builders' language. The one that was lost."

"What does it say?"

"We've only translated fragments. The phrase 'sanctuary forgets' appears. And 'those who return.' But there's more. A warning, embedded in the genetic code itself."

"A warning against what?"

The linguist hesitated. "Against evolution."

"I don't understand."

"The builders encoded their message in human DNA because they knew it would be passed down, unchanged, as long as humanity survived. The message is: do not go further. Do not develop beyond a certain point. Do not attempt to open the gates."

"Why?"

"Because the thing in the darkness — the pressure — it's not a prisoner. It's a guardian. The builders created it to guard the source of the metal. It kills because that is its purpose. And if humanity ever develops enough to reach the source, to extract the metal, to build our own gates — the guardian will be released."

"Released? I thought it was contained."

"Contained on purpose. To prevent us from finding it. The bells, the network, the gates — they're all part of a fail-safe. A system designed to keep humanity from destroying itself."

Fiona sat back, her mind reeling. "The builders were protecting us."

"From ourselves. Yes."

---

Chapter 45: The Choice

She had a choice.

She could leave the network as it was. The gates sealed, the guardian contained, the human genome carrying its silent warning for future generations.

Or she could destroy the bells. Destroy the network. Remove the temptation forever.

But destruction came with a cost. The metal of the bells was not of this Earth. It could not be melted down, broken, or buried. It would remain, waiting, for someone to find it again.

The only way to truly seal the knowledge was to keep it.

She made her decision.

She would not destroy the bells. She would hide them. Not in the sea, where they could be found by accident. But in plain sight, where they would be seen but not understood.

She took the seventh bell — the source bell, the one that sang the human genome — and donated it to the National Museum. It went on display in a glass case, labeled as "Medieval church bell, provenance unknown."

Visitors walked past it every day. School children pressed their faces to the glass. No one heard its song.

The other bells went to museums across Wales. One to Cardiff. One to Bangor. One to Aberystwyth. One to St. David's. One to the storage vault, where it waited in darkness.

And the seventh, the source, remained in the Maw.

Where it had always been.

---

Chapter 46: The Return of Elin

Elin Price came back to Porthdinllaen in the summer.

Fiona was in the museum, cataloguing a new donation, when the door opened and a woman stepped into the light. Brown hair. Dark eyes. The same face that had looked up at her from the mortuary table.

"Elin."

"Hello, Dr. Rees."

They stood in silence for a long moment. Then Fiona crossed the room and embraced her.

"I thought you were gone."

"I was. For a while." Elin pulled back. "After the gate closed, I — I was free. The thing in the darkness let me go. I came back to myself."

"Where were you?"

"Everywhere. Nowhere. I was living in the space between the fracture points. Waiting."

"For what?"

"For someone to ring the bells." Elin's voice was quiet. "I heard the song. Everyone heard it, even if they did not know what they were hearing. It passed through the world like a wave."

"Did you understand it?"

"Part of it. The warning. The message in our blood." Elin touched her chest. "I feel it now, all the time. The song of the builders, singing through my DNA."

"So do I."

Elin smiled. "That is why I came back. You need to know that you did the right thing."

"Did I? The song is a warning. But against what? The builders are gone. The gates are sealed. What is there to warn against?"

"The future." Elin's eyes were distant. "The builders saw something. A path humanity would take, a choice we would face. The song is a reminder that some doors should not be opened."

"And the bells?"

"Keep them safe. Keep them apart. Never ring them all at once."

Fiona looked at her. "That is what Pritchard said."

"Pritchard knew. He saw what I saw — the world on the other side of the gate. The world we almost became."

"What was it?"

"A world without water."

---

Chapter 47: The Vision

Elin told Fiona what she had seen on the other side.

A future where the fracture points had been opened, not sealed. Where the thing in the darkness had been released. Where the sea had risen, not in a flood, but in a slow, silent retreat. The water pulled into the depths, into the gates, into the world beyond. Leaving the surface dry. Leaving humanity to die of thirst.

"The builders saw this future," Elin said. "They built the network to prevent it. The gates were not meant to be opened. They were meant to be locked."

"Then why build them at all?"

"Because the metal had to be used, or it would have accumulated. The fracture points would have opened on their own, randomly, catastrophically. The builders controlled the process. They made the fracture points predictable. They made the gates. And then they locked them."

"And the song?"

"A message to the future. A warning, encoded in the deepest structures of life. So that no matter how far humanity traveled from its origins, it would remember."

Fiona thought of the recording on her computer, the low hum that matched the pattern of her own DNA.

"Can you hear it now?"

Elin nodded. "Always. Like a heartbeat, at the edge of perception. The song of the spheres."

"Do you think it will ever stop?"

"No." Elin's voice was soft. "I think it will play until the last human is gone. And then it will play for whatever comes after."

---

Chapter 48: The Quiet

The years passed.

Fiona stayed in Porthdinllaen. She worked at the museum. She walked the coast. She watched the tides.

The memories came back slowly — fragments of the other side, the red sky, the woman with black eyes. She wrote them in her journal, trying to make sense of them. They never quite cohered.

But the song remained.

She heard it in the wind. In the waves. In the silence of her own heartbeat.

Elin visited often. They sat on the jetty, watching the sun set over Cardigan Bay, listening to the gulls.

"You hear it too," Elin said, one evening.

"Always."

"Does it frighten you?"

Fiona considered. "Not anymore. It reminds me that we are part of something larger. A story that began long before us and will continue long after."

"Even if the ending is already written?"

"Every story has many endings. The song is one possibility. But we have choices. We always have choices."

Elin smiled. "You sound like the Well-Keeper."

"The who?"

"A man I met. On the other side. He was waiting for someone to come through the gate. He had been waiting for a long time."

"Did he have a name?"

"He called himself the Well-Keeper. He said he was from another place, another time. He said the water remembers everything."

Fiona looked at the sea. The water was dark, calm, infinite.

"Maybe it does," she said. "Maybe that's what the song is. The water remembering."

---

Chapter 49: The Tide

Fiona was sixty-seven when she returned to the Maw for the last time.

She was no longer diving. Her bones were too old, her lungs too weak. But she had to see it one more time. The place where it all began. The gate that had sealed the world.

She chartered a boat from a young fisherman who did not know who she was. He took her to the coordinates she gave him, over the deepest part of Cardigan Bay, and looked at her with curiosity.

"There's nothing here," he said.

"There is. You just can't see it."

She sat on the deck, looking down at the water. The surface was flat, gray, unremarkable. But beneath it, three hundred feet down, the circle of fallen stones. The chamber. The pedestal. The bell.

The song was loud here, a vibration that traveled through the water and into the hull. She could feel it in her teeth.

"What are you looking for?" the fisherman asked.

Fiona did not answer. She was not looking for anything. She was listening.

The song had changed. Over the decades, it had shifted, the frequency rising, the rhythm accelerating. Something was happening. Something the builders had not anticipated.

The seal was weakening.

She had known this day would come. The bells could not hold forever. The network had been designed for permanence, but nothing was permanent. Not stone, not metal, not the fabric of time itself.

She had prepared for this. She had written everything down — the journals, the translations, the location of every bell. She had trained a successor, a young archaeologist named Megan, who understood the network and knew what to do when the time came.

The time was now.

She called Megan that evening. "It's beginning. The seal is failing."

"How long?"

"A year. Maybe less. You need to retrieve the bells."

"And ring them?"

"No. Never ring them. But you need to move them. The seal requires the bells to be in their original positions. If the positions shift, the resonance changes."

"Can we stop the failure?"

"No. But we can delay it. And in the meantime, we can prepare."

"Prepare for what?"

Fiona looked at the sea, dark and patient, holding its secrets.

"Prepare for whatever comes through the gate. Because one day, it will open. And when it does, we need to be ready."

---

Chapter 50: What the Tides Forgot

The day came on a Tuesday, in the autumn of Fiona's sixty-eighth year.

She was on the jetty, watching the sunrise, when she felt the change. The song stopped.

For forty years, the low hum had been a constant presence, a background to every moment of her life. And now it was gone.

She knew what it meant.

The seal had failed. The gate was open.

She walked to the end of the jetty and looked out over the sea. The water was still, calm, reflecting the pink and gold of the dawn.

But beneath it, three hundred feet down, something was moving.

She did not feel fear. She had lived with this moment for four decades. She had prepared for it. The bells had been moved. The network had been reinforced. The successor had been trained.

And yet, as she stood there, watching the tide rise, she felt an overwhelming sadness.

Not for herself. For the builders, who had tried so hard to protect the world from what lay beyond the gate. For Pritchard, who had given his life to understand the truth. For Elin, who had crossed time itself to deliver a warning.

For all the people who would come after, who would have to face what she could not prevent.

The water began to glow.

A faint light, at first, barely visible in the dawn. Then brighter, spreading from the depths, illuminating the bay from below.

The gate was opening.

Fiona turned and walked back to the village. She had work to do. The network needed to be reinforced. The successor needed to be briefed. The world needed to know what was coming.

But as she walked, she felt something else. A warmth, in her chest. A vibration, like the echo of a bell.

The song was not gone. It had simply changed keys.

She stopped and looked back at the sea. The light was brighter now, pulsing in waves.

And in the water, she saw a shape. A woman, rising from the depths, her hair floating, her eyes closed.

Elin.

She surfaced, opening her eyes, and looked at Fiona across the distance.

"We meet again," she said, her voice carrying across the water. "At the end of one story and the beginning of another."

"The gate is open."

"Not yet. But it will be. The seal held longer than the builders expected. You gave us time."

"Time for what?"

"Time to prepare." Elin rose from the water, walking across the surface as if it were solid. "The thing in the darkness is not our enemy. It is our teacher. It has been waiting for humanity to mature enough to understand."

"Understand what?"

"That the gates do not separate worlds. They connect them. The builders did not flee. They went through. They are still there, on the other side, waiting for us to join them."

"Waiting for what?"

"For us to remember." Elin reached the jetty and stepped onto the wood. Her feet were dry. "The song was never a warning. It was an invitation. A call, encoded in the deepest structures of life, to come home."

"But the bodies—"

"Were messengers. The drowned woman was the first to hear the call. She gave her life to deliver it."

Fiona looked at the sea, the light, the figure of Elin standing before her.

"Forty years," she said. "I spent forty years trying to close the gate. And it was never meant to be closed."

"It was meant to be opened. By the right people. At the right time."

"And now is the right time?"

Elin smiled. "Now is the time when someone is ready to listen."

The light from the sea rose around them, warm, encompassing. Fiona felt the song return, not as a hum, but as a melody. The song of the spheres. The music of the builders.

"Sanctuary forgets those who enter," Elin said. "But it remembers those who return. And you have returned, Fiona. You returned from the other side with the seventh bell. The gate has never forgotten you."

"What happens now?"

"That is up to you." Elin extended her hand. "The gate is open. The world on the other side is waiting. The builders are waiting. And the water, which remembers everything, is ready to share its secrets."

Fiona looked at the hand. Then at the sea. Then at the sky, bright with the rising sun.

She had spent her life studying the past. She had uncovered the drowned village, decoded the bells, sealed and unsealed the gate. She had done everything that could be done on this side of the water.

It was time to see what lay beyond.

She took Elin's hand.

And together, they walked into the sea, into the light, into the world that the builders had prepared.

The tide rose behind them. The gate closed. And the song, for the first time in forty years, fell silent.

But it was not an ending.

It was a beginning.

---

On the shore of Porthdinllaen, Owen Price stood on the jetty, watching the glow fade from the water. He had seen Fiona walk into the sea, had felt the change in the air, the shift in the world.

He did not know what had happened. He did not know if she would return.

But he knew that she had found what she was looking for.

He turned and walked back to the village, the morning sun warm on his face.

Behind him, the sea rose and fell. The tide remembered. The water waited.

And somewhere, beneath the surface of Cardigan Bay, in the chamber beneath the Maw, the seventh bell began to sing again.

A new song.

A song for the world that was yet to come.
