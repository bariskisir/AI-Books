Chapter 1: Detection

The SCADA logs scrolled in green text on Maya Chen's monitor. Two in the morning. The NCSC operations floor was empty except for the hum of servers and the occasional ping from a cooling fan.

She was reviewing routine traffic from the PJM interconnection. The grid handled millions of data points per hour. Most looked normal. Most were noise.

This packet was different.

It appeared at 02:14:37. A heartbeat signal from a relay station in Maryland. Nothing unusual on the surface. But the sequence number was wrong. The handshake protocol expected a four-byte header. This one had five.

Maya isolated the packet. She pulled it into a hex viewer. The extra byte was a fragment. Part of something larger.

She sat forward. Her coffee had gone cold hours ago.

The packet didn't match any signature in their threat library. It wasn't a known virus, worm, or trojan. It was custom-built. Someone had written this code by hand.

She flagged it for deep analysis. The system would need six hours to run the sandbox. Maya couldn't wait that long. She bypassed the queue and ran it herself.

The terminal returned a single warning: "Unrecognized binary structure. Recommendation: isolate immediately."

Maya reached for her phone. Director Okonkwo needed to see this.

Chapter 2: Verification

The sandbox environment ran on an air-gapped machine. No network access. No way for the code to phone home.

Maya watched the binary execute. It unpacked in three stages. First, a compression wrapper. Then an encryption layer. Finally, the payload.

It was a rootkit. A kernel-level rootkit designed for industrial control systems. It exploited a vulnerability in the Siemens S7 protocol — the communication standard for most of the nation's substations.

She checked the CVE database. No match. This wasn't a known exploit.

It was a zero-day.

Maya felt the air leave her lungs. A zero-day in SCADA meant the attacker had spent months developing this. Years, maybe. The code was clean. Elegant. No wasted instructions.

The rootkit established persistence. It wrote itself to the controller's firmware. Every reboot would reload it. Standard antivirus would never detect it. It lived below the operating system.

She documented everything. Screenshots. Memory dumps. Timestamps.

The exploit had one trigger mechanism: a timer. Maya couldn't decode the target time from the binary alone. She needed more processing power.

She transferred the binary to the main cluster. Thirty-two cores began the brute-force analysis.

Three hours later, the cluster returned a result. The timer was set for Saturday at 18:00:00. Seventy-two hours from now.

Chapter 3: Alert

Sarah Okonkwo arrived at 3:47 AM. She wore jeans and a sweatshirt — the director of the National Cyber Security Center dressed for an emergency, not a board meeting.

"Show me," she said.

Maya pulled up her analysis. The exploit's structure. The timer. The air-gapped sandbox results. Everything.

Okonkwo read in silence. She asked three questions. What systems are affected. How deep does it go. Can we stop it.

Maya answered each one. The exploit targeted PJM, NYISO, and ISO-New England. Three of the country's largest grid operators. The rootkit was embedded at the firmware level. She didn't know if they could stop it yet.

Okonkwo picked up the secure phone. She dialed a number Maya didn't recognize.

"This is Director Okonkwo," she said. "I need a line to General Reeves. Priority Alpha. Yes, I'll hold."

She waited. Thirty seconds. A minute.

"Marcus. We have a situation. A zero-day in the Eastern Interconnection. Seventy-two hours to trigger. I need you at my office within the hour."

She hung up.

"The general is coming," Okonkwo said. "Get your analysis ready for briefing. Every detail."

Maya nodded. Her hands were shaking. She put them in her pockets.

"Who else knows?" Okonkwo asked.

"Just me. And now you."

"Keep it that way until I say otherwise."

Chapter 4: The Briefing

General Marcus Reeves filled the room. Six feet four, crew cut, a jaw like a cinder block. He wore combat boots and a black polo shirt. No uniform. No insignia. He wanted to be clear this wasn't a military operation.

It was, though. They all knew it.

Reeves looked at Maya's charts. The exploit map. The cascade diagram. The timer.

"Talk to me like I'm not a programmer," he said.

Maya explained. Someone had broken into the grid control systems. They'd installed a rootkit that would activate in seventy hours. When it did, it would disable safety protocols and send conflicting signals to substations across the East Coast.

"How bad?" Reeves asked.

"New York, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Washington. No power. Possibly for weeks."

"Casualties?"

"Hospitals have backup generators. But they're not designed for extended operation. If the outage lasts more than forty-eight hours, people will die."

Reeves absorbed this. His face didn't change.

"Who did it?"

"We don't know yet. The code is professional. State-level capability."

"Or someone who learned from state-level training."

Maya hadn't considered that possibility. A lone actor with military-grade skills. That was worse. You couldn't predict a lone actor.

"We need to find the attacker before the timer hits zero," Reeves said. "That's your job."

He looked at Maya. Then at Okonkwo.

"And we need to do it quietly. No public announcement. Not yet."

Chapter 5: Countdown

The timer was precise. Saturday, June 6, 18:00:00 Eastern. Peak load time. The moment when the grid was most strained.

Maya calculated the impact zones. PJM served sixty-five million people. NYISO served twenty million. ISO-New England served fifteen million. One hundred million people would lose power simultaneously.

She ran a secondary simulation. The grid had redundancies. Automatic failovers. But the exploit was designed to disable failovers too. It would hit every backup pathway simultaneously.

The blackout would be total.

Maya thought about her mother in Queens. Her brother in Philadelphia. Both in the impact zone.

She pushed the thought away. Focus.

Reeves set up a command center in the NCSC's basement conference room. Secure lines. Encrypted video feeds. Representatives from DHS, FBI, and the energy department.

Everyone wanted to know the same thing. Can we stop it?

Maya didn't have an answer yet.

She studied the timer code. It was wrapped in a cryptographic container. She couldn't modify it without the decryption key. And she didn't have the decryption key.

The exploit was designed to be permanent. Once active, it would be nearly impossible to remove from the field. Every controller would need to be manually reflashed. Thousands of devices. Months of work.

Assuming they could even get the parts. Supply chains were still recovering from the pandemic.

Seventy-one hours now. The clock was ticking.

Chapter 6: The Signature

Maya examined the exploit's encryption layer. The algorithm was AES-256-GCM. Standard. But the key derivation function was custom.

She recognized the pattern. A modified version of Argon2 with unusual memory parameters. She had seen it before.

Three years ago. A breach at a defense contractor. Lockheed Martin. The attacker had used the same KDF variant.

That attacker was never caught. But the FBI had attributed the breach to a group they called IronCompromise.

Maya pulled up the IronCompromise file. The group had been active since 2018. Targets included energy companies, defense contractors, and government agencies. Their code had a consistent fingerprint.

She compared the KDF implementation line by line. Match.

She compared the error-handling routines. Match.

She compared the comment style. No comments. That was the signature. IronCompromise never commented their code. They considered it a badge of honor.

The group was linked to Russian intelligence. Not proven, but strongly suspected.

Maya reported her findings to Reeves.

"Russian?" he said.

"Linked. Not confirmed."

Reeves's jaw tightened. Attribution was never clean. But this was enough to escalate.

"Keep digging," he said. "I am calling the White House."

Maya watched him leave. The call would change everything. Russian involvement meant this was no longer a law enforcement matter. It was a national security crisis.
Chapter 7: Deep Dive

Maya spent the next four hours inside the exploit. Layer by layer, she peeled it apart.

The rootkit had three components. A dropper that installed the payload. A persistence module that hid in firmware. And the trigger mechanism.

The dropper was the entry point. It arrived as a legitimate-looking firmware update. Signed with a stolen certificate from Siemens. The certificate was still valid.

Maya traced the certificate. Issued to Siemens AG in 2019. Used for legitimate updates until six months ago. Then it went dark.

Someone had stolen the private key. Or bribed someone who had access.

The persistence module was elegant. It hooked the controller's bootloader. Before the operating system loaded, the rootkit loaded. Antivirus never saw it.

The trigger mechanism was the most impressive piece. It listened for a specific frequency signal on the grid itself. A pulse. When it detected the pulse, it activated.

But there was a secondary trigger. The internal timer. If the pulse never came, the timer would activate the exploit anyway.

Ghost wanted redundancy.

Maya closed her laptop. She needed sleep. Her brain was foggy.

She lay down on the couch in the break room. Closed her eyes.

The timer ticked. Sixty-eight hours.

Chapter 8: The First Clue

Maya woke to the sound of printers. Someone had sent a job to the network printer. She checked the time. Five hours of sleep. Better than nothing.

She grabbed coffee and returned to her terminal.

The code analysis had finished processing. The system had extracted every string, every function call, every imported library.

One string stood out. Deep in the binary, buried in what looked like a debug symbol: "192.168.4.88:8443".

An internal IP address. The attacker's development network.

Maya checked the range. 192.168.4.0/24 was registered to a company in Arlington, Virginia. A small IT consulting firm.

She called the company. No answer. Weekend.

She checked public records. The firm was a shell. Registered to a P.O. box. No employees listed on LinkedIn. No website. The address was a UPS store.

The IP address was a dead end during the breach. But it was a clue. The attacker had developed the exploit on a network connected to this shell company. Somewhere in Arlington, someone had touched this code.

She handed the information to Reeves.

"Arlington," Reeves said. "That is fifteen minutes from here."

"I know."

"Someone at this company might have seen something."

"Or someone at this company is our attacker."

Reeves nodded. "I will send a team." 
Chapter 9: Ghost's Call

Maya's terminal went dark. Not a crash. The screen turned black and a single line of white text appeared.

"Stop looking. You do not know what you have found."

She checked her network connection. The machine was still online. Nothing unusual in the traffic logs.

The text changed.

"I am watching. You found my IP. Good work. But you will not find me."

Maya's hands hovered over the keyboard. She should not respond. Standard protocol was to alert the security team.

She typed anyway.

"Who are you?"

The reply came instantly.

"Call me Ghost."

"Why are you doing this?"

"That is not the right question. The right question is: how can you stop it."

Maya waited. The cursor blinked.

"I will tell you," the message continued. "Fifty million dollars. Monero. For the kill code."

"Where do we send it?"

"I will provide the wallet. Do not try to trace it. Do not tell anyone about this conversation. Or the timer moves up."

The screen went black again. Then the login prompt appeared. The machine had been rebooted remotely.

Maya sat back. Ghost had access to her terminal. He was inside their network. She checked the system logs. Nothing. He had covered his tracks.

She stood up and walked to Okonkwo's office.

"We have a problem." 
Chapter 10: The Ransom

The message arrived forty minutes later. An encrypted email with Monero wallet instructions and a PGP signature.

Maya verified the signature. It matched the message on her terminal.

Fifty million dollars. Anonymous cryptocurrency. Non-negotiable.

Reeves convened an emergency meeting. The FBI's cyber squad joined via secure video.

"Can we trace the wallet?" Reeves asked.

The FBI agent shook his head. "Monero is private by design. We can see the transaction on the blockchain. We cannot see sender, receiver, or amount. No way to trace."

"Can we pay it?"

"Not without congressional approval. And we would need to buy Monero on an exchange. Ghost would see that coming."

"Then how do we get the kill code?"

Maya spoke. "He said he would give it to us after payment. But I do not believe him. Once he has the money, he has no incentive to help us."

"Agreed," Okonkwo said. "We need to find another way."

Reeves looked at Maya. "You have been in his code. Can you reverse the kill sequence?"

"I can try. But he designed it to be irreversible."

"Try harder."

Maya returned to her terminal. Sixty hours left.
She sat down and opened the disassembler. The kill sequence was buried deep. She would need to trace every instruction path.
She checked the timer. Fifty-nine hours now. Every minute mattered.

Chapter 11: The Debate

The command center was divided.

Reeves wanted to go public. Evacuate the affected areas. Activate emergency protocols. The public had a right to know.

Okonkwo argued against it. Mass evacuation would cause chaos. Hospitals could not move patients that far. The economic damage would be catastrophic.

"People will die either way," Reeves said. "At least this way they can prepare."

"If we evacuate, we confirm the attacker's power. We show them they can disrupt a hundred million lives."

"Sarah, we cannot hide a blackout of this scale. When the lights go out, people will know."

"They will know it is an attack. We want to avoid panic."

"Panic is better than death."

Maya listened. She understood both positions. Reeves was right that people deserved warning. Okonkwo was right that warning would cause its own damage.

But neither of them was asking the question that mattered. How do we stop the attack?

She raised her hand.

"There is a third option," she said. "We find Ghost before the timer goes off."

Both of them looked at her.

"Then do it," Okonkwo said.
The room fell silent. Maya had proposed a solution that satisfied neither side. But it was the only path forward.
She pulled up the threat map on the main display. Fifty-five hours. The clock was their real enemy.

Chapter 12: Two Paths

Maya set up parallel workstreams.

The first track was financial. She coordinated with the FBI's cryptocurrency task force. They monitored exchanges for large Monero purchases. They watched the wallet address Ghost had provided. Nothing yet.

The second track was forensic. She dove deeper into the exploit code. Ghost had left something behind. A mistake. A signature. There was always something.

She found it in the network stack. The exploit used a custom TCP implementation. Non-standard window sizing. Slightly off timing on the SYN-ACK handshake.

It was a fingerprint. Anyone who touched the exploit would leave traces of this same TCP stack. She could use it to trace Ghost's other activities.

She wrote a scanner. It searched internet-wide for the same TCP fingerprint. The scan would take hours.

She checked the timer. Fifty-eight hours.

The financial track came back empty. Ghost had not moved money. He had not contacted anyone. He was waiting.

Maya stared at her screen. Ghost was out there. Somewhere. Connected to the same internet she was.

She just had to find him before he pulled the trigger.

She started writing queries. Hunting.
She set the scanner running and watched the first results trickle in. Nothing yet. But the scan would grow more comprehensive over time.
Her phone buzzed. A message from Danny. She ignored it. Work first. Answers later.

Chapter 13: The Dead End

The IP address traced to a server at Ohio State University. A research server in the engineering department. Compromised six months ago.

Maya accessed the server's logs. The attacker had used it as a relay. SSH in, bounce, SSH out. Clean.

The university IT team confirmed the compromise. The server had been running a cryptominer. The attacker had used it for cover traffic. Mining operations disguised command-and-control.

"Can you preserve the logs?" Maya asked.

"Already wiped," the IT director said. "We reimaged the machine this morning."

Maya closed her eyes. Someone had tipped off the university. Or Ghost had triggered a remote wipe.

She checked the timing. The wipe happened two hours after she queried the IP. Coincidence? Unlikely.

Ghost was monitoring their queries. He knew what they were looking at.

She reported to Reeves. "The IP is a dead end. Ghost wiped the server."

"Can we trace the wipe?"

"It was a script. Automatic. Triggered by a keyword in the server logs. We triggered it when we queried."

Reeves slammed his hand on the table. "He's two steps ahead."

"Not for long."

Maya had an idea. She couldn't follow Ghost's trail forward. But she could follow it backward. Trace the origin of the code. Find where it was compiled.

"You've got something," Reeves said.

"Maybe."

She started working.

Chapter 14: A Second Look

Maya stepped back from the network analysis. She needed to understand the exploit at a deeper level.

She loaded the binary into a disassembler. The code was clean. Professional. But there was something odd about the function call patterns.

The exploit called several system functions that weren't necessary for its stated purpose. Functions for pipe pressure monitoring. Gas flow regulation.

She checked the exploit's manifest again. The target was the power grid. Not gas pipelines.

Unless the exploit was broader than she thought.

She isolated the additional functions. They were dormant. Triggered by a separate condition. A condition she hadn't found yet.

She dug deeper. The functions were designed to disable safety systems on natural gas pipelines. Specifically, they targeted the pressure relief valves on high-volume transmission lines.

If the power grid went down, pumps would stop. Pressure would build. The valves would be disabled by the exploit.

Pipelines would rupture.

Maya calculated. Three major pipelines ran through the impact zone. The Colonial Pipeline. The Transco Pipeline. The Texas Eastern Transmission.

If all three ruptured simultaneously, the resulting explosions would level city blocks.

The kill count wasn't from the blackout alone.

It was from the secondary attacks.

Chapter 15: The Cascade

Maya ran the full simulation.

Phase one: the grid goes dark. One hundred million people without power.

Phase two: pipeline pumps stop. Pressure builds in the lines.

Phase three: safety valves fail. The exploit has disabled them.

Phase four: pipelines rupture at their weakest points. Near population centers.

Phase five: fires. Explosions. Civilian casualties.

She calculated the casualties. Conservative estimate: three thousand dead in the first seventy-two hours. Ten thousand injured. The numbers grew from there. Secondary effects would add to the toll.

She presented the simulation to the command center. The room went silent.

"This is worse than a blackout," Reeves said. "This is a coordinated strike on critical infrastructure."

"The pipelines and the grid share control networks," Maya said. "Ghost compromised both."

"How did we miss this?"

"The pipeline functions were obfuscated. Buried under three layers of encryption. I only found them because I was looking for something hidden."

Okonkwo spoke. "Can we decouple the pipeline controls from the grid?"

"Not in fifty-five hours. We would need to physically disconnect the SCADA networks. That requires shutting everything down."

"Then we need to stop the exploit itself."

Maya nodded. "I know."

She went back to work.
Chapter 16: The Mole

The secure chat log appeared on Maya's monitor. It was a fragment. Recovered from a packet capture she'd set up on the internal network.

Two lines.

"they found the ohio server"
"i know. already handled."

The second line was from an internal IP address. The NCSC building.

Someone inside the building had communicated with Ghost.

Maya checked the timestamp. The conversation happened one hour after she found the Ohio server. Before the server was wiped.

Someone in the building had tipped him off.

She went to Okonkwo. Closed the door.

"We have a leak."

Okonkwo's face went still. "Who?"

"I don't know. But the communication came from inside this building. Someone with network access."

"How far does this go?"

"I don't know that either. But Ghost knew about the Ohio server before we could act on it. Someone told him."

Okonkwo sat down. She looked old for a moment. Tired.

"Who knows about the operations?"

"Everyone in the command center. Twelve people. Plus the FBI team."

"That's too many."

"I can narrow it down. But whoever it is, they're careful. The communication was encrypted."

"Keep this between us. No one else."

Maya nodded. She had a traitor in the building. And fifty-four hours on the clock.

Chapter 17: The Purge

Maya accessed the Ohio server. Nothing. The machine had been overwritten. Seven passes of random data. Military-grade wipe.

Ghost was thorough.

She checked the server logs. The wipe command originated from the server itself. A cron job that had been planted months ago. Ghost couldn't have triggered it manually. The timestamps didn't match.

He'd set a trigger phrase. When the server logs recorded the phrase "threat actor," the wipe initiated.

Maya had written that phrase in her query. She'd activated the trap herself.

She sat back. Smart. Ghost had anticipated her methodology. He knew how investigations worked. He'd built countermeasures.

This wasn't a random hacker. This was a professional who understood forensic procedure.

She thought about the leak. Whoever it was, they'd known about the Ohio query. They'd reported it to Ghost. Ghost had confirmed the wipe.

The leak and Ghost were connected. If she found one, she'd find the other.

She pulled up the building access logs. Who had entered the NCSC between her discovery and the wipe? Seven people. She listed them.

Okonkwo. Reeves. Three analysts. Two security guards.

One of them was the leak.

She started background checks on all seven. Fifty-three hours.

Chapter 18: Starting Over

The Ohio server was a loss. The university timeline was a loss. The IP address was a loss.

Maya had nothing.

She stared at her terminal. The exploit code was still there. She knew its structure. Its behavior. Its trigger mechanism.

But she did not know who wrote it. Where they were. How to stop it.

She needed a different approach.

She thought about Ghost's message. "Stop looking. You do not know what you have found."

He was scared. Not of her. Of being found. He was confident in his code. He knew she could not reverse it in time. But he was worried about identification.

That meant he had something to lose. A reputation. A network. A life outside this operation.

He was not a state actor. State actors did not care about identification. They had diplomatic immunity.

Ghost was a mercenary. He needed to keep working after this. A public face would end his career.

Maya pulled up the dark web forums. She knew a few. Places where hackers traded exploits and services.

She started looking for WinterGhost.
She navigated the forums carefully. No direct questions. Too risky. Ghost monitored the channels.
A post from six months ago mentioned a developer selling SCADA exploits. The handle was deleted. But the post remained.

Chapter 19: The Underground

The phone rang three times before Danny Hsu picked up.

"It is two in the morning, Maya."

"I know. I need help."

Danny had been a black hat. A good one. He had broken into three Fortune 500 companies before he turned twenty. Then the FBI caught him. Maya was the analyst on his case. She had recommended leniency. He got probation and a consulting gig.

He owed her.

"What kind of help?" Danny asked.

"I need information on a hacker. Alias WinterGhost. He is running a zero-day on the grid. Fifty-two hours to trigger."

Silence on the line.

"That is not a joke?"

"I am not joking."

"WinterGhost is a ghost. No one knows who he is. He works alone."

"You know the scene. Someone must know something."

Danny sighed. "I will ask around. But I am not promising anything."

"Be careful. He is watching."

"Everyone is watching, Maya. That is the point."

He hung up.

Maya waited. The command center hummed around her. Reeves was on the phone with the White House. Okonkwo was coordinating with the energy department.

She stared at her screen. Waiting for Danny to call back.

Forty-nine hours.
The command center noise faded behind her. Maya closed her eyes and thought. Danny was her best lead.
The timer showed forty-eight hours now. Half the time was gone. They were no closer to stopping the attack.

Chapter 20: The Whisper

Danny called back at dawn.

"WinterGhost," he said. "Real name unknown. Probably American. He started on the Russian forums about five years ago. High-end work. Custom exploits. No one knows his face."

"What else?"

"He's working for someone. Big money. The kind of money that buys silence."

"Russian intelligence?"

"Could be. But he doesn't work exclusively for them. He's a mercenary. Highest bidder."

"Where can I find him?"

"You can't. He finds you. That's his thing."

Maya thought. "There must be a way to reach him."

"You don't want to reach him. You want to understand him. There's a difference."

"What do you mean?"

"Ghost leaves a pattern. He uses the same encryption, same protocol, same methodology every time. It's elegant but predictable. If you know his pattern, you can anticipate his moves."

"I've been in his code. It's clean."

"That's the pattern. He's proud of his craft. Exploit developers usually are. They leave traces in their style."

Maya understood. Ghost's signature wasn't a mistake. It was a byproduct of pride.

"One more thing," Danny said. "Ghost monologue. He likes to talk. Make him angry, he'll make mistakes."

"Thanks, Danny."

"Stay safe, Maya."

He hung up.

Chapter 21: The Profile

Maya assembled the behavioral team. Two FBI profilers and a psychologist from DHS.

She gave them everything. Ghost's messages. His code. His timing. His methodology.

"He's a perfectionist," the lead profiler said. "The code is clean. The operation is precise. He didn't leave loose ends."

"Aggressive," the psychologist added. "He contacted you directly. He wanted you to know he was watching. That's a power play."

"He's also isolated," the profiler continued. "He works alone. No team. That means he trusts no one. Probably has trust issues from a past betrayal."

"Age?"

"Late twenties to early forties. Male. Above-average intelligence. Technical education, possibly military."

"Nationality?"

"American. The contract work suggests he's comfortable in the U.S. The infrastructure knowledge is domestic. He knows these systems."

Maya took notes. The profile narrowed the search. American. Male. Technical background. Working alone but for a state sponsor.

"One thing," the psychologist said. "He wants to be caught. Not badly enough to surrender. But he's leaving traces intentionally. The message to you. The timer. The spectacle."

"Why?"

"Because he wants recognition. Someone to witness his work. He's been invisible his whole life. This is his moment."

Maya thought about that. Ghost wanted an audience.

She could use that.

Chapter 22: Mobility

The traffic analysis team reported a pattern.

Ghost's internet connections came from different cities. New York. Chicago. Miami. Denver. Each connection lasted exactly four hours. Then he moved.

"He is using mobile hotspots," the lead analyst said. "Buys prepaid data plans. Uses each one for four hours, then disposes of the device."

"How does he move between cities?"

"Probably drives. No flight records match the timeline. No train tickets. He is using a personal vehicle."

Maya studied the map. The connection points formed a rough circle. Starting in New York, then west to Chicago, south to Miami, west to Denver, then back east.

"He is testing the network," she said. "Checking latency and routing paths from different locations."

"He is building a relay chain for the final command."

"Correct. When he triggers the exploit, he will use all these nodes simultaneously. We cannot block one source because there are dozens."

She looked at the pattern harder. One connection did not fit. A node in Washington D.C., logged only once. A short session. Less than an hour.

"Why D.C.?" she asked.

The analyst shrugged. "Maybe a stopover."

Maya made a note. She highlighted D.C. on the map.

Forty-five hours.
Chapter 23: The Trail

Danny called again. He had something.

"WinterGhost uses a specific VPN exit node. In Chicago. It is not random. He uses it every Tuesday."

"Every Tuesday for the past three months. Same node. Same time window. Like clockwork."

"Can we get the node provider to cooperate?"

"I know the node. And I know he has used it for twelve consecutive Tuesdays. That is not random behavior. It is routine."

Routine meant comfort. Ghost felt safe in Chicago. He had a base there. A place where he could work without fear.

Maybe he even lived there.

Maya briefed Reeves. "Chicago. Ghost connects from Chicago every Tuesday. We have a two-hour window."

Reeves did not hesitate. "I will have a field team in the air within the hour."

"I need to coordinate remotely."

"You will have full bandwidth. Take whoever you need."

They had ninety minutes before Ghost connected.
Chapter 24: The Team

Reeves's field team assembled at Andrews Air Force Base. Twelve operators. Two surveillance specialists. One technical intercept team.

They loaded onto a C-17 at 12:30. Flight time to Chicago was ninety minutes.

Maya watched the tracking screen. The aircraft was a blue dot moving west.

Chapter 25: The Safehouse

The field team arrived at 13:50. Ten minutes before Ghost's expected connection.

The building was a four-story brick structure. A laundromat on the ground floor. Apartments above.

The team set up surveillance positions. Two operators in a van across the street. Two on the roof of the adjacent building. The intercept team positioned themselves near the building's network junction box.

"How long to isolate?"

"Five minutes. Ten max."

Maya watched the timer. Fourteen minutes until ghost time.

The intercept team worked. They mapped each network. Identified the router. Traced the physical line.

"We've got it. Apartment 3B."

"Occupant?"

"No name on the lease. Cash payment. Landlord says he's a quiet guy. Keeps to himself."
