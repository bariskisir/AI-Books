Chapter 1: Cleaning Window

Selin Arda made the first cleaning window smaller than a postage stamp. A lacquer screen does not forgive enthusiasm. It rewards patience with half-truths and punishes hurry with permanent ghosts.

Under the cotton swab, the black surface changed from dull to deep. Then a pale mark appeared beneath the shine, not on top of it. A stroke. Then the hook of another. Older writing, buried under restoration lacquer.

She stopped breathing long enough to notice.

Mina Park leaned over the barrier. "Please tell me that is dirt."

"Dirt does not make grammar."

The screen stood in the museum's conservation room, six panels wide, black as standing water, gold cranes flying over a landscape that looked peaceful because someone had paid for peace to be the story. It was to be unveiled in twelve days at the Venn Foundation gala.

Selin photographed the window with scale, light, and no interpretation. Interpretation came later, when panic wanted names.

Mina read the tiny exposed mark on the monitor. "Could it be a workshop note?"

"Could be."

"You do not sound like could be is helping."

Selin saved the file twice. Beneath the black surface, the mark waited with the patience of buried things. The official label said Gift of the Venn Foundation, acquired from a Kyoto private collection, 1954.

The older writing seemed to disagree by surviving.

Work changed character when it had to answer for itself. Selin stayed close to the first exposed mark, letting detail slow the hour down: cotton fibers, solvent breath, black lacquer, the tick of the fume hood. Procedure gave the fear edges. A glove was changed, a note was dated, a file was named twice before it moved. That discipline did not soften the problem. It kept the problem from being handled by mood.

Mina came nearer only after the first pass was done. "Who benefits if we call this uncertainty?" They looked at the file as if it might apologize. They had learned to distrust the sentences that sounded ready for donors. The useful ones were rougher. They had dates in them, or measurements, or the honest shape of not knowing. A clean sentence could still be dirty work if it erased the trouble that made it clean.

The conservation room remained cool and bright, a place built for patience but now crowded by consequence. Around the first exposed mark, the story kept changing from ornament to evidence. A corner notation pointed to a missing enclosure. A missing enclosure pointed to a person who had decided it was not missing enough to matter. When they returned to the screen, its black surface gave nothing away for free. It required light, angle, patience, and the humility to stop before wanting became damage.

"There may still be an innocent explanation," Mina said. In another week the line might have sounded cautious. Now it sounded like a door that had been closed too often. Selin nodded anyway. "Then the record can hold that, too. It does not have to protect it." Fairness, Selin had always believed, was not the same as making every possibility equal after the evidence had started to lean.

The freight elevator groaned, rose, and settled like an old argument. Inside, the file thickened by small increments. Photographs, scans, typed notes, and witness names accumulated without drama. That was how pressure changed: not in a single speech, but in the loss of easy phrasing. People stopped calling the screen simply the Venn gift. They paused before saying acquired. They began to hear the gap inside private collection, Kyoto, 1954.

By late day, the evidence had made the discovery a fact that could not be unmade. The fact did not announce itself. It sat under the lamp with the rest of the evidence and waited for people to become decent enough to describe it. Selin cleaned the table, checked the cabinet, and wrote one more sentence in the log because sentences, when made carefully, could keep hands honest after courage had gone home.

Before leaving, Selin imagined the planned version of the evening: lights raised, speeches timed, the screen made to stand behind gratitude. The image no longer held. The cranes were still graceful; the river still dark; the gold still exact. But beauty had been recruited to make ownership feel settled, and the room now knew better than to help.

At the corridor, Mina walked beside Selin for a while. Neither of them offered comfort. Comfort would have been another kind of hurry. "Will the record be enough?" Mina asked at last. Selin looked back toward the closed door. "It is what we can leave for the next person who is asked not to see." The record, at least, had begun to resist them.

Chapter 3: Resin Sample

Dr. Kenji Sato preferred machines to committees because machines did not ask whether truth could wait until after dinner. He took a microscopic sample from an existing loss at the screen's lower edge, mounted it, and placed it under spectroscopy.

Selin watched the graph rise in colored peaks.

"Two campaigns," Kenji said.

"Original and repair?"

"Original urushi layer, mineral pigment, shell powder. Later synthetic-modified lacquer and fill. Mid-twentieth century, not nineteenth."

Mina stood behind them with her arms folded. "Could later repair be innocent?"

"Repair is innocent often. Concealment less so."

Kenji enlarged the cross-section. The later layer sat above scraped damage where material had been removed before filling. Not a crack. Not wear. A deliberate clearing.

Selin felt the case begin inside her, unwelcome and exact. Objects did not accuse. They presented conditions. People made accusations by wanting conditions ignored.

"I need a layer map," she said.

"You need a hold on treatment."

"I know."

Mina looked toward the closed door. "The gala walkthrough is in an hour."

Chapter 8: Old Crate

Ivo Marek had moved art for thirty years and trusted crates more than curators because crates at least admitted they were built to hide things. He listened to Selin describe the screen, then rubbed his thumb along an old scar on his wrist.

"When?"

"To offsite storage, six years before the gift announcement. Venn Foundation loan, temporary review. Crate said lacquer cabinet panels. Wrong weight. Wrong length."

Mina frowned. "Why would a screen arrive as furniture?"

"Customs categories. Insurance categories. People categories. Pick one."

He took them to the loading bay records. The incoming shipment photograph showed a long crate with stenciled wording: household decorative panels. No artwork declaration on the visible side. The handling notes mentioned unusual internal bracing.

"Do you have the flag?"

Chapter 49: Return Terms
