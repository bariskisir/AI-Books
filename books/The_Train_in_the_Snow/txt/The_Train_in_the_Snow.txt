Chapter 1: The Glacier Express

Inspector Felix Brandt boarded the train in Chur and immediately regretted it. The platform was thick with snow. The sky was the color of old iron. He found his compartment in the first-class carriage and settled into the corner seat.

The train was nearly full. Winter travelers bound for the Engadin valley. Skiers with expensive luggage. Couples holding hands. A few solitary figures like himself.

Brandt watched them with professional habit. He had spent twenty years reading people in train stations and hotel lobbies. It was automatic now. That man there — too much luggage, nervous hands, running from something. That woman — expensive coat but cheap shoes, a pretender. He catalogued them silently.

His own case had ended badly. A missing girl in Zurich. He had followed every lead into a dead wall. The family had demanded a new investigator. His superior had suggested time off with a gentle firmness that was not a suggestion.

St. Moritz. A week at the Palace Hotel. Do nothing. Rest.

The whistle blew. The train lurched forward. Snow swirled against the windows. Brandt watched the station recede and felt the familiar pull of the rails. He always thought better on trains.

A conductor passed in the corridor. Middle-aged, crisp uniform, worried eyes. He checked his watch and moved on.

A woman in black took the compartment across the aisle. Widow's clothes. She moved with precision, every gesture measured. She did not look at anyone.

Brandt noted the time. 3:17 PM. The journey would take four hours.

He did not know then that most of them would never reach St. Moritz.

Chapter 2: First Class Passengers

The dining car was warm and bright. Brandt took a table near the window and ordered coffee. The snow had not let up. It fell in sheets, erasing the landscape.

He counted the first-class passengers. Seven in total. Himself. The woman in black. A doctor with a medical bag he kept close. A loud businessman and his silent wife. A journalist Brandt recognized from Zurich.

The journalist was Stefan Gross. Brandt knew the name from the crime desk. Gross wrote exposés. He also wrote checks to unnamed sources. He was not popular among his colleagues.

Gross sat at the bar, drinking whiskey. He was in his element, holding court. The businessman laughed at his jokes too loudly. The businessman's wife stared at her hands.

The doctor ate alone, a book propped before him. His medical bag sat on the empty chair beside him. He touched it every few minutes.

The woman in black had not come to dinner. The stewardess carried a tray to her compartment.

Brandt sipped his coffee. Seven strangers in a metal tube climbing into a storm. The thought unsettled him more than he cared to admit.

The conductor appeared in the dining car doorway. He spoke quietly to the stewardess. She nodded and hurried away. The conductor's hands trembled as he adjusted his cap.

"Herr Meier," Brandt said as the man passed. "Is there trouble?"

The conductor stopped. His name was Hans Meier. Brandt had read it on his uniform.

"Nothing serious, sir. The weather is complicating our schedule. We may be delayed."

Brandt nodded. He had expected as much.

Meier moved on. His step was heavy, reluctant.

Chapter 3: The Widow's Silence

Brandt did not sleep that night. He sat in his compartment with the light off, watching the snow. The train moved slowly now. The engine strained against the grade.

At midnight he walked to the dining car. It was empty except for the woman in black. She sat alone with a glass of wine.

"May I?" Brandt asked, gesturing to the seat across from her.

She looked at him for a long moment. Her eyes were gray, steady, unreadable.

"It's a free train," she said.

Brandt sat. "Felix Brandt."

"Clara Voss."

"Traveling to St. Moritz?"

"Yes."

"For the skiing?"

She almost smiled. "No. I don't ski."

Brandt waited. The silence stretched. She was comfortable with it. That was unusual.

"My husband died six months ago," she said finally. "Everyone told me to get away. So I got away."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be. It was a kindness."

She said it flatly, without emotion. But her eyes drifted to the doorway where Stefan Gross had been standing earlier. She caught herself and looked away.

"You knew the journalist," Brandt said. Not a question.

She did not answer. She finished her wine and stood.

"Good night, Herr Brandt."

She left. Brandt watched her go. In the corridor, she passed Gross's compartment. She did not look at it. She did not look away. She simply walked as if it did not exist.

That was the thing, Brandt would think later. She was too careful not to look.

Chapter 4: The Doctor's Bag

Dr. Markus Engel came to breakfast looking as if he had not slept. His eyes were red. His hands shook as he lifted his coffee cup.

He had not let go of the medical bag. It sat on the seat beside him, gripped by his free hand.

"Rough night?" Brandt asked.

The doctor looked up sharply. "The motion of the train. I am not a good traveler."

"The storm has you worried."

"We are high in the mountains in a blizzard. Yes, I am worried."

Brandt nodded. He ordered eggs and toast. The doctor ate nothing.

"That bag," Brandt said. "You keep it close."

"It contains medical equipment. Expensive equipment."

"Of course."

The doctor's jaw tightened. He knew Brandt was probing. He did not like it.

"I am a physician," he said. "I was called to consult at the St. Moritz clinic. A difficult case. They requested me personally."

"Important work."

"Yes. Important."

The doctor finished his coffee in a single gulp. He stood, lifting the bag with both hands.

"If you will excuse me."

He walked away quickly. Too quickly. As if escaping.

Brandt finished his breakfast slowly. When he finally left the dining car, he passed the doctor's compartment. The door was slightly ajar. Inside, Dr. Engel was bent over his bag, checking its contents with frantic urgency.

He looked up. Their eyes met. The doctor slammed the bag shut.

The door closed firmly. The lock clicked.

Chapter 5: The Conductor's Routine

Hans Meier had worked the Glacier Express for twenty-three years. He knew every bend of the track, every tunnel, every village they passed. He knew the rhythm of the wheels on the rails.

But this journey was wrong.

Brandt found him in the service corridor, checking his pocket watch against the station clock for the tenth time in an hour.

"We have lost thirty minutes," Meier said. "The snow is too deep."

"Is it dangerous?"

Meier hesitated. "The tracks run through avalanche corridors above Filisur. If the snow continues at this rate—"

He did not finish. He did not need to.

Brandt looked out the window. The world had become white and formless. No sky, no ground, no horizon. Just snow in every direction.

"The passengers should be informed," Brandt said.

"Management says not to alarm them."

"Management is in Zurich. We are here."

Meier nodded slowly. He respected this passenger. Something about the way he spoke, the way he observed.

"After lunch," Meier said. "I will make an announcement if conditions worsen."

He moved on, checking doors, testing locks. Everything was in order. But his hands would not stop trembling.

At 2:30, he paused at the door to Stefan Gross's compartment. He stood there a moment too long. His hand went to his pocket where he kept his wallet.

He walked away without knocking.

Brandt saw it all from the end of the corridor.

Chapter 6: Snowfall Intensifies

The afternoon brought no relief. The snow fell harder. The train crept forward at walking pace. The heating system struggled. Passengers wrapped themselves in blankets.

The dining car became a gathering place. People needed company, needed warmth. They sat together in uneasy groups.

Herr Obermann, the businessman, drank brandy and complained loudly about the delay. "I have meetings. Important meetings. This is unacceptable."

His wife sat beside him, saying nothing. A bruise bloomed on her wrist where his hand had gripped her.

The doctor had retreated to his compartment. The widow had not emerged since breakfast.

Gross circulated like a shark. He moved from table to table, spreading unease. He stopped at Obermann's table and leaned close.

"Don't worry about your meetings," he said softly. "Everyone will understand. These things happen."

Obermann went pale. His brandy glass trembled.

"Leave me alone," he said.

Gross smiled. "Just being friendly."

He moved on. He passed Brandt's table and paused.

"You're the detective," he said. "Zurich police. I recognize you."

"Retired," Brandt said.

"Sure. Retired." Gross laughed. "Enjoy your vacation."

He walked away. Brandt watched him go and felt a cold certainty settle in his chest. This man was trouble. This train was trouble.

The lights flickered. The train stopped.

Chapter 7: Stefan Gross at Dinner

Dinner was served despite the circumstances. The dining car stewardess, Greta Lind, moved between tables with practiced efficiency. She was young, perhaps twenty-two, with the tired eyes of someone who worked too hard for too little.

Gross was in high spirits. He ordered wine for the table. He told stories. The other passengers listened with reluctant attention.

"I once followed a politician to a brothel in Vienna," he said. "Three weeks of surveillance. Cost me my marriage, but the story was worth it."

The widow had come to dinner. She sat at a corner table, eating slowly. Gross's voice carried.

"People think they can hide anything," he said. "They're wrong. Everyone has a secret. Everyone has something they'd kill to protect."

He looked directly at Clara Voss when he said it.

She did not flinch. She met his gaze and held it. Something passed between them, some current of recognition and hatred.

The doctor excused himself. He could not eat. His hands were shaking too badly.

The conductor appeared in the doorway. His face was gray.

"Ladies and gentlemen," he said. "I must inform you that we have encountered a significant obstruction. An avalanche has blocked the track ahead."

Silence.

"The line behind us is also blocked. We are waiting for rescue crews."

"How long?" Obermann demanded.

"Hours. Possibly tomorrow. The storm is severe."

The silence stretched. Then Gross laughed.

"Well," he said. "Looks like we're all stuck together."

He raised his glass.

"To new friends."

No one drank.

Chapter 8: The Avalanche

Even now, Brandt would remember the sound. A low rumble at first, like distant thunder. Then a deep groan, the protest of the mountain itself. The train shuddered. Lights flickered.

Passengers grabbed the tables. Wine glasses tipped and shattered. Greta cried out.

Then stillness. Complete, absolute stillness.

The train had stopped.

Outside, the world had changed. Snow pressed against the windows, white and absolute. They were buried.

Meier appeared in the dining car doorway. His face was wet. Tears or melted snow, Brandt could not tell.

"The avalanche came down fifty meters ahead," he said. "It has covered the tracks entirely. We cannot move forward."

"And behind?" Brandt asked.

Meier shook his head. "The signal from the rear station is lost. The lines are down. We are cut off."

The widow set down her fork. She wiped her mouth with a napkin. She looked almost relieved.

"How long will the heat last?" she asked.

"Several hours. Perhaps until morning. The emergency generators have fuel."

"Then we wait," she said.

She said it as if waiting was something she understood.

Gross stood up. He stretched ostentatiously.

"Well, I'm going to bed. Wake me when they dig us out."

He walked out of the dining car. No one watched him go.

But Brandt did. He watched every step.

It was 9:47 PM.

Chapter 9: Waiting for Rescue

The hours passed slowly. The dining car became a waiting room. Passengers sat in their seats, wrapped in coats and blankets. The heating grew weaker. The cold crept in.

Brandt walked the length of the train. He checked every compartment, every door. The conductor followed him.

"What are you doing?" Meier asked.

"Looking."

"For what?"

"I'll know when I see it."

The train was modern. The compartments were small but well-appointed. The windows sealed tight. The corridor narrow. A single door at each end.

At the rear of the train, the baggage car held skis and suitcases. And Dr. Engel's medical bag, now sitting alone on a shelf.

Brandt stopped. The doctor had left his bag here. The man who would not let it out of his sight had left it in an unlocked baggage car.

"Why would he do that?" Brandt murmured.

"Perhaps he felt safe now that we are stopped," Meier offered.

"Or perhaps he wanted it found."

Brandt did not touch the bag. He noted its position, its condition. Then he returned to the dining car.

Greta had made coffee. The passengers drank it in silence. The widow had returned to her compartment. The doctor had retired as well. Obermann snored in his chair. His wife stared at the wall.

At 11:30, Greta carried a cup of tea to the widow's compartment.

At 11:45, the dining car was empty except for Meier and Greta.

At 11:47, Stefan Gross was still alive.

At 11:50, someone killed him.

Chapter 10: Last Call

Greta Lind made her final round at 11:30. She carried a tray with tea for the widow and a nightcap for anyone still awake.

The dining car was empty except for the conductor. Meier sat at a table with a cup of cold coffee. He was writing in his logbook.

"Anyone else awake?" Greta asked.

"The doctor turned in early. The detective is in his compartment. Obermann is sleeping in the dining car. His wife went to bed."

"And Mr. Gross?"

Meier's face tightened. "His light is still on. He asked not to be disturbed."

Greta nodded. She carried the tea to compartment seven. The widow answered immediately. She was in her nightgown, reading. She took the tea with thanks.

"Will you be needing anything else, Frau Voss?"

"No. Thank you, Greta."

The door closed. Greta returned to the dining car.

She and Meier sat together. They talked about the weather, the delay, the passengers. Ordinary conversation. The kind that fills time in a crisis.

At 12:05, Greta went to check on the passengers one last time.

At 12:07, she reached compartment four. Stefan Gross's compartment.

The door was locked.

She knocked. No answer.

She knocked again. Harder.

"Mr. Gross? Are you all right?"

Silence.

She called for the conductor. Meier came running. He tried the door. Locked.

He slammed his shoulder against it. Once. Twice.

On the third try, the lock gave.

Inside, Stefan Gross lay on the floor. His eyes were open. A letter opener stood upright in his chest.

Blood soaked the carpet.

Greta screamed.

Chapter 11: The Body

Brandt heard the scream and was moving before his mind caught up. He found Meier in the doorway, frozen. Greta had her hands over her mouth.

He pushed past them.

Gross lay on his back. His arms were outstretched, palms up. The letter opener protruded from his chest at a slight angle, driven downward with force. Not much blood. The heart had stopped quickly.

Brandt knelt. He checked for a pulse he knew he would not find. The body was still warm.

"Don't touch anything," he said. "Don't let anyone in."

He examined the room. The window was shut, locked. Frost covered the glass. The door had been bolted from inside. The bolt was now broken from Meier's forced entry.

A locked room.

Gross's belongings were undisturbed. His wallet was in his pocket. His watch was on his wrist. His suitcase was open on the rack, clothes neatly folded.

But his briefcase was missing.

"Who was the last person to see him alive?" Brandt asked.

Greta's voice was a whisper. "He was in the dining car. He left around ten."

"Alone?"

"Yes."

"And then?"

"I brought the widow her tea at eleven-thirty. That's when I saw his light was still on."

"You didn't speak to him?"

"He asked not to be disturbed."

Brandt turned to Meier. "You were in the dining car the whole time?"

"Yes. From eleven-twenty until Greta screamed. I was doing the accounts."

"Both of you?"

"Yes."

Brandt looked at the body again. At the locked door. At the impossible window.

"Someone on this train killed him," he said. "And I intend to find out who."

Chapter 12: Locked Room

Brandt sealed the compartment. He used Meier's master key to lock it, then pocketed the key.

The passengers had gathered in the corridor. The widow stood at the door of her compartment in her nightgown. The doctor had emerged, his shirt untucked. Obermann was awake now, his wife clutching his arm.

"What happened?" the doctor demanded.

"Mr. Gross has been murdered," Brandt said.

Murmurs. Disbelief. The widow's face was unreadable.

"How?" Obermann asked. "The door was locked. I heard them break it down."

"Exactly," Brandt said. "The door was locked from inside. The window is sealed. No one entered or left through normal means."

"That's impossible," the doctor said.

"Is it?"

Brandt looked at each of them in turn.

"I am Inspector Felix Brandt of the Zurich Kriminalpolizei. I was traveling privately, but circumstances require me to act. No one leaves this train. No one enters the crime scene without my permission."

He paused.

"We have a killer among us. And I will find them."

The widow smiled slightly. It was a strange expression. Almost approving.

"Well, Inspector," she said. "It seems your vacation is over."

She turned and closed her compartment door.

The others dispersed slowly, reluctantly. Brandt watched them go. The doctor. The Obermanns. Greta. Meier.

One of them was lying.

All of them had secrets.

And somewhere, hidden among the train's silent compartments, was the truth.

Chapter 13: Inspector Revealed

Brandt commandeered the dining car as his investigation headquarters. He sat at a table with a notebook and the conductor's timetable. Greta brought him coffee.

"Tell me everything," he said.

She was scared. Her hands shook as she spoke. She had worked this route for eighteen months. She knew the regulars, the routines. Gross had traveled with them before, three times in the past year.

"He always asked for compartment four," she said. "The same one. He said he liked the view."

"Did he have visitors?"

"Sometimes. People from other compartments. I don't remember faces."

"Did he argue with anyone?"

She hesitated. "He argued with everyone. That was his way."

"Specifically. Anyone on this train."

She looked down. "He argued with Frau Voss. The first night. I heard voices from her compartment. He was angry. She was calm."

"What did he say?"

"I couldn't hear. Just the tone. He was threatening her."

Brandt wrote it down. "Anyone else?"

"He spoke to Herr Meier privately. Several times. Herr Meier seemed upset afterward."

"What about?"

She shook her head. "I don't know. He wouldn't tell me."

Brandt thanked her. He sat alone with his coffee and his notes.

Four passengers with motive. The widow, the doctor, the conductor, the businessman. Each had a reason to want Gross dead.

Each had an alibi.

Chapter 14: The Alibis Begin

Brandt interviewed them one by one in the dining car. Meier first.

"I was here," the conductor said. "From eleven-twenty until Greta screamed. I have the accounts to prove it."

"Did you leave at any point?"

"No."

"Not even to use the lavatory?"

Meier hesitated. "I stepped out briefly. Five minutes. Maybe ten."

"When?"

"I don't recall exactly. Around midnight."

Brandt noted it. "Where did you go?"

"To the service closet. I needed to check the heating logs."

"And then?"

"I came back."

"Did anyone see you?"

"No."

Brandt studied him. The man was sweating despite the cold.

"Thank you, Herr Meier. Please send in Fräulein Lind."

Greta confirmed his story. She had been in the dining car from eleven-twenty onward. Meier had stepped out briefly, but she was not sure when. He could have been gone twenty minutes. She was busy with the accounts.

"Did you see anyone else during that time?"

"No. The corridor was quiet."

"Did you hear anything?"

"Nothing. Just the wind."

Brandt dismissed her. He stared at his notes.

The widow was next. She sat across from him, composed. She wore black, as always.

"I was in my compartment," she said. "Greta brought me tea at eleven-thirty. I read until midnight, then slept."

"Did you hear anything from Gross's compartment?"

"Muffled voices. Earlier. Around ten."

"Could you identify them?"

"No. They were indistinct."

"Did you leave your compartment after eleven-thirty?"

"No."

"Can anyone confirm that?"

She smiled. "Only Greta. She saw me at eleven-thirty. After that, I was alone."

The perfect alibi. Alone, with no one to contradict her. But also no one to confirm her story.

Chapter 15: The Widow's Alibi

Brandt pressed harder.

"Greta brought you tea at 11:30," he said. "What time did she collect the cup?"

"At 12:15. She knocked, and I handed it to her through the door."

"She didn't enter?"

"No. I was in my nightgown. I asked her to leave it outside."

"So between 11:30 and 12:15, you were alone."

"Yes."

"And you heard nothing from compartment four?"

"Nothing."

"No voices? No struggle?"

"I told you. Nothing."

Brandt leaned back. He had a feeling about this woman. She was too calm. Too controlled.

"You knew Stefan Gross," he said.

"I knew of him."

"From before this journey."

She was silent for a moment. Then she nodded.

"He contacted me six weeks ago. He claimed to have information about my husband's death."

"What kind of information?"

"He said my husband did not die of natural causes. He said someone killed him."

"And you believed him?"

"I believed he had proof. He offered to sell it to me."

"For how much?"

"Fifty thousand francs."

Brandt raised an eyebrow. "Did you pay?"

"No."

"But you considered it."

She looked away. "I considered many things."

She stood up. "I have told you everything. If you have more questions, I will be in my compartment."

She left. Brandt watched her go.

The widow had motive. The widow had opportunity. The widow had no alibi.

But she could not have done it. The door was locked from inside.

Chapter 16: The Doctor's Alibi

Dr. Engel came to the dining car with his medical bag. He set it on the table and kept his hand on it.

"I was asleep," he said. "I told you."

"Can anyone confirm that?"

"My door was locked. I did not wish to be disturbed."

"The walls are thin. You heard nothing?"

"Only the storm."

"Gross's compartment adjoins yours. If there had been a struggle, you would have heard it."

The doctor's face tightened. "Perhaps I sleep deeply."

"You are a light sleeper. You told me yourself. The motion of the train keeps you awake."

"Then I was lying. People lie to strangers on trains."

"Why would you lie about that?"

Dr. Engel was silent. His hand tightened on the bag.

"What are you hiding, Doctor?"

"Nothing."

"Then open the bag."

"No."

Brandt stood. "I am investigating a murder. I can search this train compartment by compartment. But if you open the bag voluntarily, it looks better for you."

The doctor's face went pale. He looked at the bag. His hands trembled.

"I cannot," he whispered.

"Why?"

"Because there are things inside that do not belong to me."

"What things?"

The doctor closed his eyes. "Evidence. From a case years ago. I kept it. I should have destroyed it. But I kept it."

"Gross knew about this?"

"Yes. He found out. He was blackmailing me."

"And you killed him."

"No. I did not kill him. But I cannot open this bag because it implicates me in something else. Something I did not do."

"What did you not do?"

But the doctor would not say more.

Chapter 17: The Conductor's Alibi

Brandt summoned Meier again. This time, he was not gentle.

"Your alibi is weak," Brandt said. "You were alone for fifteen to twenty minutes. Long enough to kill a man."

"I did not kill him."

"Prove it."

Meier's face reddened. "I told you. I was checking the heating logs."

"Show me."

"I already filed them. They went to the company."

"So there is no record."

"The record exists. In the company office."

"Which we cannot access because we are trapped in snow."

Meier said nothing. He was trapped and he knew it.

"Why did Gross lend you money?"

The question hit like a physical blow. Meier stepped back.

"How did you—"

"I searched his belongings. I found the promissory note."

Meier's shoulders sagged. "I have a gambling problem. It started five years ago. Small bets at first. Then larger. I lost my savings. I borrowed from the company. When they found out, they would have fired me. Gross offered to cover the debt."

"At what interest?"

"Twenty percent. Compounded monthly."

"He was bleeding you dry."

"Yes."

"And you hated him for it."

"Yes."

"Enough to kill him?"

"No. I wanted to. But I did not."

"You were in the dining car with Greta."

"I was."

"Except for the fifteen minutes you were not."

Meier's face fell. "Except for the fifteen minutes I was not."

Brandt dismissed him. The conductor was lying. But about what?

Chapter 18: The Impossible Window

Brandt returned to compartment four. He needed to understand the impossible.

The window was a standard train window. Double-glazed. Locked with a latch that required a key. The key was still in the lock.

Outside, snow pressed against the glass. It was packed solid, three feet deep at least. No one had entered or left through this window. The snow would have been disturbed.

The door had been bolted from inside. The bolt was a simple sliding mechanism, well-maintained. When engaged, the door could not be opened from outside. Meier had broken it when he forced entry.

So how?

Brandt examined the walls. Solid. The ceiling. Solid. The floor. Solid.

The ventilation grille caught his eye. It was set into the wall near the ceiling. A standard train ventilation system, common in Swiss rolling stock. The grille was held in place by four screws.

He pulled a coin from his pocket and tested the screws. Loose. Very loose.

He removed the grille. The shaft beyond was dark and narrow. Perhaps thirty centimeters wide. A passage between compartments.

He shone his torch inside. The shaft ran horizontally. It connected to the adjacent compartment — Dr. Engel's.

A path. Impossible for a large man. But possible for someone small.

Brandt measured the opening with his hands. The shaft was tight. Slight build required. Flexible joints.

He looked at his notes. The widow and Greta were the smallest passengers. But the widow was in her compartment. Greta was with Meier.

Or were they?

Chapter 19: The Missing Bag

Brandt went to the baggage car. The doctor's medical bag was still on the shelf. He picked it up. It was lighter than before.

He opened it.

The contents were neatly arranged. Bandages, scissors, a stethoscope. A surgical kit with velvet-lined slots.

One slot was empty.

A scalpel was missing.

Brandt closed the bag. He carried it to the dining car and set it before Dr. Engel.

"Open it," Brandt said.

"I already told you—"

"Open it."

The doctor opened the bag. His hands shook as he revealed the contents.

"Where is the scalpel?" Brandt asked.

The doctor stared at the empty slot. "I don't know. It was there this morning."

"You moved the bag to the baggage car. Why?"

"I was afraid. I thought if someone found it, they would think I was the killer."

"Instead, someone took the missing scalpel and used it."

"Used it for what?"

"The murder weapon. The letter opener was planted. The real weapon is a scalpel from your surgical kit."

The doctor went pale. "That's not possible."

"It's possible. And I think you know who took it."

The doctor shook his head. "I don't know. I swear."

But Brandt saw something in his eyes. Fear. Recognition. The doctor knew who had taken his bag. He knew and he would not say.

"Why are you protecting them?"

The doctor did not answer.

Chapter 20: Motive Emerges

Brandt spent the night assembling his evidence. He spread the contents of Gross's briefcase across the dining car table.

Photographs. Letters. Bank records. The tools of a blackmailer.

Clara Voss in the arms of a man who was not her husband. Dates stamped on the back. Recent. Six months ago, while her husband was still alive.

A letter from Dr. Engel's former patient's family. "We will pursue this matter. Our son died because of your negligence."

A photograph of Herr Obermann meeting with a known insurance fraudster. Dated three weeks ago.

A promissory note from Hans Meier. Signed and witnessed.

Each passenger had a secret. Each secret was worth killing to protect.

But each passenger had an alibi.

Brandt sat back. The snow continued to fall. The train remained buried.

He thought about the locked room. The impossible alibis. The missing scalpel.

Someone was lying. Not about one thing. About many things.

He needed to find the crack. The one inconsistency that would break the whole story open.

He started with the timeline.

11:30 — Greta brings tea to the widow.
11:47 — Gross reportedly alive, his light on.
12:05 — Greta checks on passengers.
12:07 — Body discovered.

Between 11:30 and 12:05, the killer acted. The widow was in her compartment. The doctor was asleep. The conductor was with Greta.

Three alibis. Three walls. One of them was false.

Chapter 21: The Photographs

Brandt studied the photographs of Clara Voss. She was with a younger man. Dark hair. Strong jaw. They were in a restaurant, laughing. In another, they were kissing outside a hotel.

The dates were from February. Five months before her husband's death.

He summoned her to the dining car. He laid the photographs on the table.

She looked at them without expression.

"These are from February," Brandt said. "Your husband died in July."

"Yes."

"Who is the man?"

"His name is not important."

"He was your lover."

She was silent.

"Your husband found out. You argued. He died."

"He died of a heart attack."

"So the doctors said."

"And the police."

"The police who investigated. Not the police who knew about this." He tapped the photographs. "Gross discovered these. He threatened to expose you."

"Yes."

"He wanted money."

"Yes."

"How much?"

"Fifty thousand francs. I told you this."

"You told me he offered to sell you information about your husband's death. You did not tell me the information was about your affair."

"I am telling you now."

"Because you have no choice."

She smiled. "Because it no longer matters. The truth is the truth."

She had a point. But Brandt was not satisfied.

"Where were you at midnight?"

"I told you. In my compartment."

"Reading."

"Yes."

"What were you reading?"

She hesitated. "A novel. The Trial."

"By Kafka."

"Yes."

"When did you last turn a page?"

She met his eyes. "I don't remember. I was thinking."

"About what?"

"About how I ended up here. Surrounded by snow. Trapped with a dead blackmailer."

She stood. "I have answered your questions. I am tired. If you need me again, I will be reading."

She left. Brandt picked up the photographs. He looked at the laughing woman. The woman in love.

She was not the woman he had met on this train.

Chapter 22: The Doctor's Secret

Dr. Engel's secret was more complicated. Brandt found the letter in Gross's files. It was from a law firm, dated twelve years ago.

The Engel case. A thirteen-year-old boy had died during a routine tonsillectomy. The official finding was anesthetic complication. But the family claimed negligence. They sued. The case was settled out of court.

Gross had obtained the hospital records. They showed Dr. Engel had administered the wrong dosage. A mistake. A fatal mistake.

The doctor's license was never revoked. The settlement included a confidentiality agreement. But Gross had the records. He was threatening to release them.

Brandt interviewed Dr. Engel again.

"Twelve years ago," Brandt said. "A boy named Daniel Fischer."

The doctor's face crumpled. "That is not fair."

"Murder is not fair either."

"I did not kill Gross."

"Then tell me about the boy."

The doctor took a long breath. "It was a mistake. I was tired. I had been on call for thirty-six hours. I miscalculated the dosage. The boy died."

"And you covered it up."

"The hospital covered it. They did not want the scandal. I agreed to the settlement. I have lived with it every day since."

"Gross found out."

"Eight months ago. He contacted me. He wanted money. I paid."

"For how long?"

"As long as he asked."

"But you stopped."

The doctor looked away. "I could not afford it anymore. He raised the price. He said he would release the records if I did not pay."

"So you killed him."

"No. I did not."

"Your scalpel is missing."

"I know."

"Someone used it."

"I know."

"Who?"

The doctor shook his head. "I don't know. I was asleep. I heard nothing."

"You heard nothing because you were not asleep. You were in the dining car."

The doctor's eyes widened.

"Greta saw you," Brandt lied.

"She did not see me. She would have said."

"She did say. She told me you came to the dining car at 11:45. You asked for water."

The doctor was trapped. He knew it.

"I could not sleep," he said. "I went for water. That is not a crime."

"At 11:45, Gross was still alive. At 12:07, he was dead. You had the opportunity."

"But not the weapon. The scalpel was still in my bag."

"Unless you took it."

"I did not."

"Then who did?"

The doctor did not answer. But his eyes flickered to the door. To the direction of the widow's compartment.

Chapter 23: The Obermann Connection

Herr Obermann was easier to break. He was a man of bluster and noise. His secrets sat close to the surface.

"The insurance scheme," Brandt said. "You were defrauding your company."

"It was a minor exaggeration. Every businessman does it."

"Gross had proof. Photographs. Documents."

"He was extorting me."

"Yes. And you killed him for it."

"No! I was in my compartment. Asleep. My wife will confirm."

Frau Obermann was brought to the dining car. She sat with her hands folded. She did not look at her husband.

"Where was your husband at midnight?"

"With me. Asleep."

"Are you certain?"

She did not hesitate. "Yes. He was in bed. I woke several times during the night. He was always there."

Brandt noted it. A reliable witness. But the woman was afraid of her husband. Would she lie for him?

"Frau Obermann," he said gently. "You understand the seriousness of this. A man is dead."

"I understand."

"Your husband could be the killer."

"He is not."

"How can you be sure?"

She met his eyes. "Because he was afraid of Gross. He would never have had the courage. He is a coward."

The words hung in the air. Obermann's face went red.

"You—"

"It is true," she said quietly. "You are a coward. You beat me. You lie to your company. But you do not kill."

She looked at Brandt. "He did not do it."

Brandt believed her. Obermann was loud. He was cruel. He was dishonest. But he was not a killer.

Back to the widow. Back to the doctor. Back to the conductor.

Someone was lying.

Chapter 24: The Conductor's Debt

Meier's story had a hole. Brandt found it in Gross's records.

The promissory note was for ten thousand francs. But Gross's bank statements showed payments from Meier totaling fifteen thousand. Spread over eight months.

"You paid him more than you owed," Brandt said.

Meier's face was gray. "Interest."

"The interest was twenty percent. That would be twelve thousand. You paid fifteen."

"I made mistakes in the accounting."

"You do the accounts for this train. You do not make mistakes."

Meier was silent.

"You were paying Gross for something else."

"There was nothing else."

"Then explain the extra three thousand."

Meier's hands shook. He looked around the dining car, as if searching for escape.

"I was paying for silence," he said finally.

"About what?"

"About the accident."

Brandt waited.

"Two years ago. A maintenance error. A coupling failed during a routine inspection. A worker was injured. It was my fault. I should have reported it. I did not."

"Gross knew?"

"He found out. He said it would cost me my pension. He said he would tell the company."

"So you paid."

"Yes."

"Did you kill him?"

"No. I told you. I was in the dining car."

"Except for the fifteen minutes."

"I was checking the heating."

"You were checking the heating."

"Yes."

Brandt wanted to believe him. The man's terror was real. But the holes in his story were real too.

"Go," Brandt said. "I will have more questions."

Meier left. He walked slowly, like a man carrying a great weight.

Brandt watched him go. The conductor had motive. The conductor had opportunity. The conductor had no real alibi.

But neither did anyone else.

Chapter 25: Second Interrogation: The Widow

Brandt called Clara Voss to the dining car for the third time. He was tired. She was serene.

"You killed your husband," he said.

She did not flinch. "No."

"The photographs. The affair. He found out. You argued. He died."

"He died of a heart attack. The autopsy confirmed it."

"Autopsies can be wrong."

"This one was not."

"Gross believed it was murder."

"Gross believed what served him."

"He was blackmailing you."

"Yes."

"For murder."

"He said he had evidence. He did not."

"Then why did you pay?"

She was silent for a long moment. "Because I was afraid. Not of the police. Of the shame. My husband's family is prominent. They would have taken everything. The house. The money. My daughter."

"You have a daughter?"

"She is ten. She lives with my sister in Geneva. I am rebuilding my life. Gross threatened to destroy it."

"So you killed him."

"I was in my compartment. Greta saw me."

"Greta saw you at 11:30. The murder happened between 11:50 and 12:05."

"Then I could not have done it. I was alone. But that is not proof."

"No. It is not."

Brandt studied her. The gray eyes were steady. The hands were still. She was either innocent or the most composed killer he had ever met.

"What happened to the letter opener?" he asked.

"The letter opener?"

"It was taken from Gross's desk. Used as a murder weapon. But the real weapon was a scalpel. Why would the killer switch weapons?"

"I don't know."

"To confuse the investigation. To hide the true method."

"That seems elaborate."

"Murder often is."

She smiled. "I would not know, Inspector. I have never killed anyone."

She stood. "May I go now?"

"One more question. The ventilation grille in Gross's compartment. Did you know it was loose?"

Her face flickered. A fraction of a second. Then it was calm again.

"No," she said. "I did not."

She left. Brandt watched her go.

She knew about the grille. He was certain of it.

Chapter 26: Second Interrogation: The Doctor

Dr. Engel was deteriorating. His hands shook constantly. He had not eaten in hours. His eyes were bloodshot.

"I did not kill him," he said before Brandt could speak.

"Then tell me about the ventilation shaft."

"The what?"

"The shaft that connects your compartment to Gross's."

The doctor's face went blank. "I did not know it existed."

"You have been in that compartment for two days. You never noticed the grille?"

"I am a heavy sleeper. I do not examine the walls."

"The grille was loose. Loose enough to remove. Loose enough to crawl through."

"Crawl through? That shaft is tiny. No one could fit."

"Someone small could."

The doctor's eyes widened. He understood.

"You think the widow—"

"I think someone used that shaft to enter Gross's compartment. Someone small. Someone with a reason to kill."

"I am not small."

"No. You are not."

The doctor sagged with relief. Then a new thought occurred to him.

"But the widow was in her compartment. Greta saw her."

"Greta saw her at 11:30. The shaft leads from your compartment, not the widow's. To use it, someone would have to be in your compartment."

"I was asleep."

"Were you?"

The doctor's face fell. "I told you. I went for water."

"At 11:45. Before that, you were in your compartment."

"Yes."

"Alone?"

"Yes."

"Then the widow could have entered your compartment. You could have been asleep. Or not."

The doctor realized the implication. "I would have woken."

"Would you?"

"I am a light sleeper."

"You told me that. But the widow is small. She moves quietly."

The doctor said nothing. He understood now. He was the key to the alibi. If he was asleep, the widow could have used his compartment to access the shaft. If he was not asleep, he was an accomplice.

"Did you help her?"

"No."

"Did you know?"

"No."

"Then you were asleep."

The doctor nodded slowly. "I was asleep."

It was not convincing. But it was all Brandt had.

Chapter 27: Second Interrogation: The Conductor

Meier was the hardest to read. His fear was constant, but his story was consistent.

"The heating logs," Brandt said. "Show me where you wrote them."

"I told you. I filed them."

"You keep a personal log. Every conductor does."

Meier hesitated. Then he reached into his jacket and pulled out a small notebook.

Brandt took it. He flipped through the pages. Times. Temperatures. Observations. It was meticulous.

He found the entry for the night of the murder.

"12:03 AM," he read. "Checked heating system. All normal."

"That is when you left."

"Yes."

"You returned to the dining car at?"

"12:20. Greta was there."

"She confirms."

"Yes."

Brandt closed the notebook. "That gives you seventeen minutes. Gross's compartment is a two-minute walk. Two minutes to kill him. Two minutes to return. Eleven minutes unaccounted for."

"I was checking the system."

"Where?"

"The service closet. Behind the dining car."

"I want to see it."

Meier led him to the service closet. It was small, filled with tools and supplies. A heating panel on the wall. Brandt examined it. The panel was old. The readings were handwritten.

"You wrote these?"

"Yes."

"At 12:03?"

"Yes."

Brandt looked at the panel. The ink was the same. The handwriting matched. But the time could have been written at any point.

"This proves nothing."

Meier's face fell. "It proves I was here."

"It proves you were here at some point. Not that you were here at 12:03."

Brandt left the closet. He walked back to the dining car.

Meier followed. Desperate.

"I did not kill him."

"Then who did?"

Meier had no answer.

Chapter 28: The Stewardess

Greta Lind was the only person on the train without a motive. She barely knew Gross. She had nothing to hide.

But Brandt had learned long ago that the innocent often saw more than they realized.

"Think carefully," he said. "The night of the murder. Was there anything unusual?"

"Everything was unusual. The storm. The avalanche."

"Before that. During dinner. After dinner."

She thought. "Herr Gross was drinking. He was loud. He made Frau Voss uncomfortable."

"Did you see anyone enter or leave his compartment?"

"No."

"Did you hear anything?"

"The wind. That was all."

Brandt tried a different approach. "Tell me about the widow. Did she seem nervous?"

"No. She seemed... calm. Too calm, maybe."

"And the doctor?"

"He was scared of Herr Gross. Everyone could see it."

"The conductor?"

She hesitated. "He was not himself."

"In what way?"

"He was distracted. He kept checking his watch. He asked me to stay in the dining car with him."

"That night?"

"Yes. He said he did not want to be alone."

"Did he say why?"

"No. But I think he was afraid."

"Of Gross?"

"Yes."

Brandt nodded. "One more thing. The letter opener on Gross's desk. Did you notice it earlier that evening?"

"Yes. It was there. He used it to open his mail."

"Was it still there when you left the dining car?"

She thought. "I don't know. I didn't check."

"Then you don't know if it was missing before the murder."

"No. I don't."

Brandt thanked her. He sat alone with his notes.

The letter opener had been on Gross's desk. Then it was in his chest. Someone had taken it. Someone had used it.

But the weapon was a scalpel. Not a letter opener.

Why?

Chapter 29: A Second Search

Brandt searched the train again. He started at the front and worked his way back. Compartment by compartment. Millimeter by millimeter.

The widow's compartment was immaculate. Her clothes were hung with precision. Her books were stacked. Nothing was out of place.

The doctor's compartment was messy. Clothes on the floor. The bed unmade. A glass of water on the nightstand. A book face-down on the pillow.

The Obermanns' compartment was divided. His side was neat. Her side was neat. Two people living parallel lives.

Gross's compartment remained sealed. Brandt entered alone.

The body was gone, moved to the baggage car. But the blood remained. The carpet was stained. The air was cold and stale.

He searched again. The desk. The wardrobe. The bed. The window.

The ventilation grille. Still loose. He removed it again and shone his torch into the shaft.

A faint impression in the dust. A handprint.

He measured it. Small. A woman's hand.

He photographed it. He measured the shaft. Thirty centimeters wide. Tight. Possible.

He crawled into the shaft. It was dark and cold. The metal scraped his shoulders. He moved slowly, carefully.

The shaft ran straight for two meters. Then it opened into the doctor's compartment. The grille on that side was loose too.

He emerged into the doctor's compartment. Dust on his clothes. A theory confirmed.

The killer had used this path.

Chapter 30: The Missing Scalpel

Brandt returned to the doctor's compartment. He searched it thoroughly this time. Under the bed. In the wardrobe. Behind the curtains.

Nothing.

He checked the lavatory. The small sink. The cabinet.

In the trash bin, he found a handkerchief. It was stained brown. Dried blood.

He picked it up carefully. The initials embroidered in the corner: C.V.

Clara Voss.

Brandt stared at the handkerchief. The widow's bloodstained handkerchief in the doctor's trash. The missing scalpel. The ventilation shaft.

It was coming together.

He summoned the widow to the dining car. He laid the handkerchief on the table.

"Explain this."

She looked at it. Her face went pale. "It is mine."

"It was in the doctor's trash. Stained with blood."

"I cut myself. The doctor gave me a bandage."

"When?"

"This morning. I asked him for a bandage."

"You went to his compartment?"

"Yes."

"The doctor did not mention this."

"Perhaps he forgot. He has been under stress."

"Stress."

"Yes."

Brandt did not believe her. The blood was dried. It was not fresh. It had been there since the night of the murder.

"Who killed Stefan Gross?"

"I don't know."

"Then why is your handkerchief in the doctor's trash?"

She had no answer.

Brandt stood. "I will find the truth, Frau Voss. I always do."

He left her at the table. She sat alone, staring at the handkerchief.

For the first time, Clara Voss looked afraid.

Chapter 31: The Doctor's Explanation

Brandt confronted the doctor about the handkerchief.

"She came to my compartment," Dr. Engel said. "This morning. She asked for a bandage."

"At what time?"

"Early. Around seven."

"That was before I found the handkerchief."

"Yes."

"Why did you not mention it?"

"I did not think it was important."

"A widow visiting your compartment at seven in the morning. You did not think it was important?"

The doctor's face reddened. "She cut her finger. I bandaged it. That is all."

"And the handkerchief?"

"She gave it to me. She said to throw it away."

"And you did not think that was strange?"

"No. People throw away bloody handkerchiefs."

"With their initials on them?"

The doctor was silent.

"I think she came to your compartment the night of the murder," Brandt said. "I think she used the ventilation shaft to access Gross's compartment. I think she took the scalpel from your bag. I think she killed Gross."

"That is not possible."

"Why?"

"Because she was in her compartment. Greta saw her."

"Greta saw her at 11:30. The murder happened later."

"She would have had to pass through my compartment. I was there."

"Were you?"

The doctor's face went white.

"You were not asleep," Brandt said. "You were in the dining car. Getting water. You said so yourself."

"For five minutes."

"Five minutes is long enough."

"But I would have seen her."

"Would you?"

The doctor had no answer. He sat in silence, the weight of the accusation pressing down.

"I did not help her," he said finally. "I did not know."

"Then you were a tool. Unwitting. But a tool nonetheless."

The doctor said nothing. He had nothing left to say.

Chapter 32: The Time Puzzle

Brandt spread the timeline across the dining car table. He stared at it for hours.

11:30 — Greta brings tea to the widow.
11:45 — Greta returns to the dining car.
11:47 — Gross is alive (light on, Meier saw it).
11:50 — Estimated time of death (body temperature).
12:03 — Meier checks heating.
12:05 — Greta goes to check passengers.
12:07 — Body discovered.

The window of opportunity was fifteen minutes. Maybe less.

The widow: Alone in her compartment. No alibi for the window. But she would need to go to the doctor's compartment, use the shaft, kill Gross, return, clean up. Fifteen minutes was tight but possible.

The doctor: Claimed he was asleep. But he was not. He was in the dining car. He had no alibi.

The conductor: In the dining car with Greta. Except for seventeen minutes. He had the time. He had the motive. But the shaft was too small for him.

Greta: In the dining car with Meier. No motive. No opportunity.

The Obermanns: Alibi each other. Motive for Obermann, but no evidence.

Brandt circled the widow's name. Then the doctor's. Then the conductor's.

Three suspects. Three alibis that did not hold.

He needed the truth. He needed to break one of them.

He started with the weakest link.

The doctor.

Chapter 33: The Ventilation Grille

Brandt brought the doctor to compartment four. He pointed at the ventilation grille.

"Look at this."

The doctor looked. "It's a vent."

"It connects to your compartment."

He removed the grille. The dark shaft opened before them.

"You could fit through this."

"I am too large."

"Yes. But someone smaller could."

The doctor stared at the shaft. His face was unreadable.

"Frau Voss is small enough," Brandt said. "Greta is small enough."

"Greta? She barely knew him."

"She had access. She was in and out of compartments all night."

"You think Greta did it?"

"I think someone did it. And I think you know who."

The doctor shook his head. "I don't."

"You moved your bag to the baggage car. You tried to hide it. Why?"

"I was afraid."

"Of what?"

"Of being accused."

"You were already being accused. You made it worse."

The doctor said nothing.

"Who took the scalpel?"

"I don't know."

"You do know. You always knew."

The doctor's eyes welled with tears. "If I tell you, I will destroy her."

"The widow."

The doctor nodded slowly. "She came to my compartment that night. After I returned from the dining car. She was there, waiting."

"What did she want?"

"She wanted my key. She said she needed to retrieve something she had left in Gross's compartment."

"And you gave it to her?"

"She was crying. She said Gross had taken something from her. Something precious. She needed it back."

"The photographs."

"Yes."

"But she did not go through the door. She used the shaft."

The doctor nodded. "She said key would be too loud. She asked me not to watch."

"And you did not watch."

"No."

"So you don't know what she did."

"No."

"But you know she killed him."

The doctor put his head in his hands. "I know."

Chapter 34: The Impossible Route

Brandt reconstructed the murder in his mind.

Clara Voss had waited. She knew the doctor was restless. She knew he would leave his compartment eventually.

At 11:45, he did. He went to the dining car for water.

She slipped into his compartment. She took the scalpel from his bag. She removed the ventilation grille.

She crawled through the shaft. Dark. Tight. The cold metal pressing against her.

She emerged in Gross's compartment. He was at his desk. Writing. He turned.

She stabbed him. Once. Clean. The heart.

He fell. She found the letter opener on his desk. She planted it in the wound. To confuse. To hide the true weapon.

She took the photographs from his briefcase. She took the letters. She took everything that connected her to him.

She crawled back through the shaft. She replaced the grille.

She returned to her own compartment. She washed her hands. She waited.

Greta knocked at 12:15. She handed the empty cup through the door. Goodnight.

Perfect.

Except for the handkerchief. She had used it to clean her hands. She had left it in the doctor's trash.

The one mistake.

Brandt admired the plan. It was bold. It was clever. It was almost perfect.

But almost perfect was not perfect enough.

Chapter 35: A Break in the Weather

The morning brought change. The storm had passed. The sky was clear. The rescue crews were making progress.

Brandt had hours left. Perhaps less.

He assembled everyone in the dining car. The widow. The doctor. The Obermanns. Meier. Greta.

They sat in silence. The tension was thick enough to touch.

"I know who killed Stefan Gross," Brandt said.

The widow did not move. The doctor stared at his hands. Meier held his breath.

"The killer used the ventilation shaft. The shaft between compartment four and compartment five. The doctor's compartment."

The widow's face was stone.

"The killer took a scalpel from the doctor's bag. Used it to stab Gross. Then planted a letter opener to confuse the investigation."

He paused.

"The killer took Gross's briefcase. The photographs. The blackmail evidence. Everything that connected the killer to the victim."

He looked at Clara Voss.

"Then the killer returned to their compartment. Washed. Waited. And alibied by the doctor's silence."

The widow stood. Her face was pale.

"Do you have proof, Inspector?"

"I have your handkerchief. Bloodstained. In the doctor's trash."

"A cut finger. I explained."

"You have no cut."

She looked at her hands. Clean. Unmarked.

"I healed."

"In one day?"

"Some people heal quickly."

Brandt shook his head. "The blood on the handkerchief matches Gross's blood type. I checked the doctor's records."

It was a lie. He had not checked. But the widow did not know that.

Her composure cracked. Just slightly. A flicker in her eyes.

"It was his blood," she said quietly.

"Yes."

She sat down. The room was silent.

"Then I suppose you have your killer, Inspector."

The words were calm. But her hands were shaking.

Chapter 36: The Gathering

The dining car fell silent. The widow sat alone at a table. The others watched her.

"Why?" Meier asked. "Why kill him?"

The widow looked at him. Her eyes were cold.

"He was destroying me. Piece by piece. He would have taken everything. My daughter. My freedom. My life."

"So you killed him."

"Yes."

The word hung in the air. Simple. Final.

Brandt sat across from her. "Tell me how."

She took a breath. "I waited until the doctor left. I knew he would. He was restless. He went to the dining car."

"He told you?"

"I watched. I saw him leave."

"And then?"

"I went into his compartment. The scalpel was in his bag. I took it."

She paused. "The shaft was tight. I had to squeeze. But I am small. I fit."

"Gross was awake?"

"He was writing. He did not hear me. I came out of the shaft behind him. He turned. I stabbed him."

"One blow?"

"One. It was enough."

"What did you do then?"

"I took the letter opener from his desk. I pushed it into the wound. To hide the scalpel mark. Then I took the photographs. The letters. Everything."

"And you went back through the shaft?"

"Yes."

"Did the doctor see you?"

"No. He was still in the dining car."

"Did he know?"

She was silent. Then she said, "Not then. Not until the handkerchief."

"He knew after that."

"Yes."

"And he protected you."

She nodded. "He had his own secrets. He understood."

Brandt leaned back. The story was complete. The pieces fit.

But something bothered him. Something at the edge of his thoughts.

He looked at the doctor. The doctor would not meet his eyes.

Chapter 37: The Impossible Alibi

Brandt reviewed the timeline one more time.

11:45 — Doctor leaves for dining car.
11:48 — Clara enters doctor's compartment.
11:50 — Clara enters shaft.
11:52 — Clara stabs Gross.
11:55 — Clara returns through shaft.
11:57 — Clara exits doctor's compartment.
12:00 — Doctor returns from dining car.

It fit. Almost perfectly.

But one thing did not fit.

The doctor had been gone for five minutes. Five minutes. Clara had done all of that in five minutes.

Impossible.

Brandt looked at the timeline again.

The doctor had left his compartment at 11:45. He had returned at 11:50. He had not been gone for five minutes.

He had been gone for fifteen.

11:45 to 12:00.

Brandt looked at the doctor. The doctor was lying. He had been gone for much longer than he claimed.

"Why did you lie about the time?" Brandt asked.

The doctor's face went pale. "I did not lie."

"You said you were gone for five minutes. The dining car is two minutes away. You were gone for fifteen."

The doctor was silent.

"Where were you for those ten minutes?"

No answer.

"Were you in Gross's compartment?"

"No."

"Then where?"

The doctor looked at Clara. She stared back. Something passed between them.

"I was in the baggage car," the doctor said. "Hiding my bag."

"You moved it after the murder?"

"Before. I moved it before. I was afraid of what would happen."

"So you were not in the dining car."

"No. I was in the baggage car. I stayed there for ten minutes. Deciding."

"Deciding what?"

"Whether to report the murder. Whether to protect myself."

"And you decided to protect yourself."

"Yes."

Brandt wrote it down. He did not believe the doctor.

But he could not prove otherwise.

Chapter 38: The Conductor's Error

Brandt turned to Meier. There was a crack in his story too.

"You said you were in the dining car from 11:20 until Greta screamed."

"Yes."

"But you also said you stepped out for seventeen minutes."

"For the heating check."

"The heating check. At 12:03."

"Yes."

"Gross was killed between 11:50 and 12:05. You were alone during that window."

"I was checking the heating."

"You could have killed him."

"No. I told you—"

"You told me many things. You told me you stayed in the dining car. You told me you stepped out for five minutes. Now you say seventeen minutes. Which is it?"

Meier's face was red. "I was confused. I was scared."

"Seventeen minutes. Alone. Unaccounted for."

"I was checking the heating."

"Then explain this."

Brandt pulled out the promissory note.

"You owed Gross money. You paid him for years. He bled you dry."

"I already told you—"

"You told me you did not kill him. But you had reason. You had opportunity. You had no real alibi."

"I did not kill him."

"Then who did?"

Meier looked around the room. His eyes landed on Clara. Then on the doctor. Then on Greta.

"Greta," he said. "Greta was with me. She knows I did not leave."

"She said you left."

"For the heating check."

"She said she did not know how long you were gone."

The conductor's face fell. He had no answer.

Brandt watched him. The man was not a killer. He was a man in over his head, drowning in debt and fear.

But Brandt had learned long ago that desperate men did desperate things.

"I did not kill him," Meier repeated. "I was checking the heating."

The words were hollow. No one believed them.

Chapter 39: Greta's Confession

Greta was crying. The events of the night had broken her composure.

"I lied," she said.

The room went still.

"I lied about the timing."

Brandt leaned forward. "Explain."

"When I brought Frau Voss her tea, it was 11:30. But I did not go back to the dining car immediately. I stopped. I talked to Herr Gross."

"What did you talk about?"

"I told him I knew what he was doing. The blackmail. I told him to stop."

"Why would you do that?"

"Because I liked him. I thought I could reason with him."

"Could you?"

"No. He laughed at me. He said I was nothing. A servant. He said I should mind my own business."

"So you killed him?"

"No! I left. I went back to the dining car. It was 11:45."

"Not 11:30."

"No. I lied because I did not want anyone to know I had talked to him."

Brandt wrote it down. The timeline shifted.

If Greta returned at 11:45, then the window was smaller. 11:45 to 12:05. Twenty minutes.

But Clara had done it in five.

Unless Greta was still lying.

"Did you see the widow's compartment light when you returned?"

"Yes. It was on."

"Did you see the doctor?"

"No."

"The conductor?"

"He was in the dining car. Writing."

"Are you sure?"

"Yes. I am sure."

Brandt stared at her. She was young. She was scared. She was probably telling the truth.

But probably was not enough.

Chapter 40: The Conductor's Story

Meier tried again. His voice was steady now.

"I left the dining car at 11:55, not 12:03. I was wrong about the time."

"Why did you lie?"

"Because I was scared. I thought if my time was wrong, you would suspect me."

"Instead, you made yourself more suspicious."

"I know. I am not good at this."

Brandt almost smiled. The man was a terrible liar. That was in his favor.

"What did you do at 11:55?"

"I went to see Gross. I wanted to talk to him about the debt. I wanted to ask for more time."

"Did you talk to him?"

"I knocked. He did not answer. The door was locked."

"So you did not see him alive."

"No."

"Then what?"

"I went to the service closet. I stayed there until 12:12. I was trying to calm down."

"Then you returned."

"Yes. Greta was in the dining car. She said she had checked on the passengers. She said Gross was not answering."

"And you went with her?"

"Yes. We found him."

Brandt considered the story. It was plausible. It was consistent with the evidence.

But it left the conductor without an alibi for the critical window.

"You were alone," Brandt said. "From 11:55 to 12:12. You could have killed him."

"I could have. But I did not."

"Why should I believe you?"

"Because I am telling the truth."

Brandt shook his head. "That is not enough."

Chapter 41: The Missing Time

Brandt returned to the timeline. The missing seventeen minutes. The doctor's missing ten minutes. The widow's missing everything.

He laid it out chronologically.

11:30 — Greta brings tea.
11:45 — Greta returns to dining car (revised).
11:47 — Gross alive (Meier saw light).
11:55 — Meier leaves for Gross's compartment.
11:57 — Doctor leaves for baggage car (his claim).
12:00 — Doctor in baggage car.
12:05 — Greta checks passengers.
12:07 — Body discovered.

The window was shrinking. The killer had acted between 11:47 and 12:05. Eighteen minutes.

Clara could have done it if she had entered the doctor's compartment before he left. But the doctor had left at 11:45. He was gone for five minutes. She needed time to enter, access the shaft, kill, return.

Unless the doctor was in on it.

Brandt looked at the doctor. The doctor looked at Clara. Clara looked at the table.

A conspiracy.

The doctor had let her in. He had left his compartment unlocked. He had waited in the dining car to give her time.

He had moved his bag to the baggage car after she had taken the scalpel. To distance himself from the evidence.

He had lied about the time.

The doctor and the widow had planned it together.

Brandt stood. He had the answer.

But he needed proof.

Chapter 42: The Doctor's Bag Revisited

Brandt went to the baggage car. The medical bag was still on the shelf.

He opened it again. He examined every pocket. Every seam. Every lining.

In the inner pocket, he found something. A piece of paper. Folded small.

He unfolded it. A note.

"It is done. Keep silent. —C."

Brandt stared at the note. It was Clara's handwriting.

He brought the note to the dining car. He laid it on the table.

"Explain this."

The doctor's face went white. "I have never seen that before."

"It was in your bag."

"I did not put it there."

"She did."

Brandt looked at Clara. Her face was unreadable.

"He did not know," she said. "I put it there after."

"After the murder."

"Yes."

"Why?"

"Because if he was caught, the note would implicate him. I wanted insurance."

"So you framed him."

"I protected myself."

The doctor stared at her. "You used me."

"Yes."

"From the beginning?"

"No. Only when I needed to." She turned to Brandt. "He is innocent. He did not know anything. I took his scalpel. I used his compartment. I killed Gross. He is not guilty."

"But you made him guilty."

"If that is what you need to believe."

Brandt looked at the note. At the doctor's face. At the widow's calm.

He had his killer. The doctor was a victim of circumstance. The widow was the murderer.

But something still bothered him.

The handkerchief. Why had she left it?

Chapter 43: The Widow's Handkerchief

"The handkerchief," Brandt said. "Why did you leave it?"

Clara was silent.

"It was your one mistake. You were so careful. You planned everything. But you left a bloodstained handkerchief in the doctor's trash."

"I needed to clean my hands."

"You had a compartment. You had a sink. Why the doctor's trash?"

She did not answer.

"You wanted him to be implicated," Brandt said. "You wanted to leave evidence that would connect him to the crime."

"No."

"Yes. The handkerchief was not a mistake. It was deliberate."

She looked at him. Her eyes were cold.

"You are clever, Inspector."

"I am thorough."

"Yes. Thorough."

She sat back. "I left it because I wanted insurance. If the investigation pointed to me, the handkerchief would point to the doctor. It would confuse things. Give me time."

"And if the investigation pointed to the doctor?"

"I would be free."

"But the doctor was innocent."

"He knew what I did. He protected me. That is not innocence."

"He was complicit after the fact."

"Yes."

Brandt shook his head. "You planned this meticulously. The shaft. The scalpel. The letter opener. The note. The handkerchief. You thought of everything."

"Almost everything."

"What did you miss?"

"The conductor's watch. He looked at it constantly. He noted the time of everything. He was the only one who could place the exact moment of death."

"Meier."

"Yes. I did not account for him."

Brandt picked up his notebook. The timeline was complete. The mystery was solved.

But the story was not over.

Chapter 44: The Widow's Confession

Clara Voss told everything in a flat, steady voice.

Her husband had been cruel. He had beaten her for years. She had stayed for her daughter. When she met another man, she found the courage to leave.

But her husband found out. He threatened to take her daughter. He said he would destroy her.

So she killed him. Slowly. A poison in his food. It looked like a heart attack. No one suspected.

Except Stefan Gross. He had been at her husband's funeral. He had seen her face. He had investigated. He found the affair. He found the poison.

He came to her with proof. He demanded money. She paid.

But he wanted more. He always wanted more.

She agreed to meet him on the train. She would pay him one last time. Fifty thousand francs. Then she would be free.

But she knew he would never stop. So she planned his death.

"The shaft," Brandt said. "How did you know about it?"

"I checked the train schematic. It is posted in the service closet. I saw the ventilation system."

"When?"

"The first night. After dinner."

So it was premeditated. First degree.

"You brought the scalpel?"

"No. I took it from the doctor. I knew he carried surgical tools. I saw him with the bag at dinner."

"You planned to use his tools?"

"I planned to use whatever was available."

"And the letter opener?"

"I found it on Gross's desk. I used it to hide the scalpel wound."

"To misdirect."

"Yes."

Brandt took a breath. The story was complete. The truth was ugly.

Clara Voss was a killer. She had killed twice. Her husband. Stefan Gross.

But the husband had been a monster. And Gross had been a parasite.

Was justice ever clean?

Chapter 45: The Impossible Murder

Brandt addressed the assembled passengers. He spoke with the authority of a man who had solved the puzzle.

"The widow killed Stefan Gross. She used the doctor's scalpel. She used the ventilation shaft. She planted the letter opener. She left the handkerchief as insurance."

He paused.

"She had motive. She had opportunity. She had the intelligence to plan it."

Clara Voss sat still. She did not deny it.

"But here is the truth," Brandt said. "All of you had motive. Gross was blackmailing everyone in this room. He was a predator. He fed on fear."

He looked at each of them.

"The doctor lied about his past. The conductor lied about his debt. Herr Obermann lied about his business. Frau Obermann lied about her husband. Greta lied about her conversation with Gross."

He turned to Clara.

"And the widow lied about her grief. She was not grieving. She was planning."

Clara met his eyes. "Would you have done differently?"

"Perhaps not."

"Then do your duty, Inspector. Arrest me. I am ready."

Brandt stood. The train was silent. The snow had stopped falling.

"Clara Voss," he said. "I am arresting you for the murder of Stefan Gross."

She nodded. She held out her hands.

Brandt did not handcuff her. He simply led her out of the dining car.

The passengers watched in silence.

The case was closed.

But the questions remained.

Chapter 46: The Widow's Story

That night, Brandt sat with Clara Voss in the dining car. The other passengers had retired. The train was quiet.

"Why did you confess?" he asked.

"Because you knew."

"I had a handkerchief. A note. Circumstantial evidence."

"You had the truth. You would have found the rest."

"Perhaps."

She smiled. "You are a good detective, Inspector. You would not have stopped."

Brandt was silent.

"Her husband," he said. "Was it really poison?"

"Yes."

"Why did you kill him?"

"Because he was killing me. Slowly. Every day. He broke my bones. He broke my spirit. He would have broken my daughter."

"So you poisoned him."

"It took six months. I was careful."

"And you felt justified."

"I felt nothing. That was the worst part. By the end, I felt nothing at all."

Brandt looked at her. The calm exterior. The steady eyes.

He had arrested killers before. They were always broken in some way. This woman was no different.

"What will happen to your daughter?"

"My sister will raise her. She is a good woman. She will tell my daughter the truth. Or a version of it."

"Will she understand?"

Clara looked out the window. The snow was starting to melt.

"No," she said. "But one day, she will."

Chapter 47: The Doctor's Shame

Dr. Engel came to Brandt the next morning. He looked older than he had three days ago.

"I need to tell you everything," he said.

"About the boy?"

"Yes. And about Clara."

"I know about Clara."

"You know what she did. You do not know what I did."

Brandt waited.

"I saw her. The night of the murder. I was leaving my compartment. She was in the corridor. I asked her what she was doing. She said she could not sleep. I believed her."

"Did you see her enter your compartment?"

"No. But when I returned, my bag was open. The scalpel was gone."

"You knew she had taken it."

"Yes."

"And you did nothing."

"I was afraid. I thought if I reported it, the investigation would find the scalpel. It would lead to me. To the boy. To everything."

"So you hid the bag."

"Yes. I moved it to the baggage car. I thought if it was hidden, the scalpel could not be traced to me."

"But the bag was found."

"By you. You found everything."

Brandt studied him. The doctor was not a bad man. He was a coward. He had made mistakes. He had covered them up.

"Your career is over," Brandt said.

"Yes."

"Go home. Talk to your family. Tell the truth."

The doctor nodded. He walked away slowly.

Brandt watched him go. The doctor was free. But he would never be free.

Chapter 48: The Morning After

The rescue crews arrived at dawn. The snow had been cleared from the tracks. The train could move again.

The passengers gathered their belongings. They were quiet. The murder had changed them.

Meier made the final announcement. "We will arrive in St. Moritz in two hours. Please remain in your compartments until then."

Clara Voss was taken off the train by the local police. She did not resist. She did not say goodbye.

Greta served coffee. Her hands were steady now. She had survived.

The Obermanns sat together. They did not speak. The bruise on Frau Obermann's wrist was darker now. She did not look at her husband.

Dr. Engel sat alone. His medical bag was beside him. He had packed it carefully. He did not open it.

Brandt sat in the dining car. He watched the landscape change. The snow gave way to rock. The rock gave way to trees. The trees gave way to the town.

St. Moritz. The Palace Hotel. A week of rest.

He did not feel like resting.

The train pulled into the station. The doors opened. The passengers stepped out into the cold mountain air.

The journey was over.

But the story was not.

Chapter 49: Brandt's Reflection

Brandt sat in his hotel room. The Palace was luxurious. Warm. Comfortable. He noticed none of it.

He thought about Clara Voss. He thought about her husband. The dead husband. The man she had poisoned.

Had she been justified? The law said no. The law said murder was murder, regardless of the victim.

But Brandt was not the law. He was a man. A man who had seen too much cruelty. Too many victims.

He thought about the boy. Daniel Fischer. Dead because of a doctor's mistake. The doctor had hidden it. He had gone on to save other lives. Was that justice?

He thought about Meier. The gambling. The debt. The desperation. A good man destroyed by a weakness.

He thought about Greta. The lies she told to protect a man she barely knew. The guilt she carried.

He thought about Gross. The journalist who became a predator. The man who fed on secrets. The man who died by his own weapon — the weapon of hidden truth.

The train was a microcosm, Brandt realized. A small world of secrets and lies. A world where everyone had something to hide.

And now the snow had passed. The truth was exposed.

But was anyone better for it?

Brandt did not know.

He poured himself a drink. He looked out at the mountains. They were white. Clean. Indifferent.

Tomorrow, he would write his report.

Tonight, he would sit in the silence.

Chapter 50: The Train in the Snow

Brandt left St. Moritz three days later. He took the same train. The Glacier Express. The train in the snow.

The compartment was empty. He sat alone. He watched the landscape pass.

The snow was melting. Spring was coming. The mountains were shedding their white coats.

He thought about the case. The locked room. The impossible alibis. The widow with the steady hands.

He had solved it. But he did not feel victorious.

He had arrested a woman who had killed her abuser. He had exposed a doctor's old mistake. He had ruined a conductor's pension. He had broken a family.

Was that justice?

The train entered a tunnel. The world went dark. Then light returned.

Brandt looked out at the snow. It covered everything. The tracks. The trees. The graves.

The train moved on. It always moved on.

He thought of Clara Voss. She would spend years in prison. Her daughter would grow up without her. The lover in the photographs would disappear.

He thought of Gross. The predator who became prey. The blackmailer who pushed too far.

He thought of himself. The detective who found the truth. The man who delivered a woman to justice for killing a monster.

The snow fell again. Light flakes. The last storm of the season.

Brandt closed his eyes.

The train in the snow continued its journey.

And the snow would remember.
