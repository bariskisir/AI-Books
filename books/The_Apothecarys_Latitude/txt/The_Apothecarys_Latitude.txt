THE APOTHECARY'S LATITUDE

A Novel

---

ARC I: THE ILLNESS

---

Chapter 1: The Canals at Dawn

The fog came off the Amstel before sunrise, carrying the smell of tar and rotting vegetables. Saskia Veldman pulled her shawl tighter and walked faster, her wooden clogs clicking against the wet cobblestones. The canal boats had begun their morning journeys, their hulls cutting through water the color of old pewter.

She carried a leather satchel over one shoulder, bulging with jars and dried herbs. At twenty-six, Saskia was small—barely five feet—but she had learned that in Amsterdam, a woman alone needed more than height to survive. She needed skill. She needed knowledge. And she needed the stubbornness of a reef in a storm.

Her shop occupied the ground floor of a narrow house on the Herengracht, wedged between a spice merchant and a tailor who made coats for the burgomasters. The sign above her door showed a mortar and pestle carved in oak, painted green and gold. "Veldman's Apothecary," it read, the letters faded by salt air and time.

She unlocked the door and stepped inside. The smell of her shop always comforted her—camphor and lavender, dried foxglove and the bitter tang of wormwood. Shelves lined every wall, filled with ceramic jars labeled in her own careful hand: Valerian root for sleeplessness. Willow bark for fever. Digitalis for the failing heart.

Her younger sister Clara was already there, stirring a pot of honey over the small iron stove. Clara was seventeen, with lighter hair and a disposition that found joy in small things—a talent Saskia envied.

"He's been here since the bells," Clara said, nodding toward the front of the shop.

Saskia frowned. "Who?"

"A servant. From the house of Van der Meer." Clara lowered her voice. "The merchant. Ships to the Indies."

Saskia knew the name. Everyone in Amsterdam knew the name. Pieter van der Meer was one of the wealthiest men in the Dutch Republic, a director of the East India Company, a man whose ships sailed to Java and Ceylon and the Spice Islands. His house on the Keizersgracht was a palace of marble and Chinese silk.

She walked to the front of the shop. A young man in plain but well-made clothes stood waiting, twisting a hat in his hands.

"Mevrouw Veldman?" he said.

"Mevrouw," Saskia said. "What brings you so early?"

"My master sent me. Master van der Meer. He is ill, and he asks that you attend him."

Saskia felt a small thrill—the kind she always felt when a new case presented itself. But she kept her face still. "There are physicians on the Keizersgracht. Men with university degrees. Why does your master send for an apothecary?"

The servant glanced at the floor. "The physicians cannot help him. They do not know what is wrong."

Saskia considered this. She had heard such words before. Often they meant the patient had a disease the doctors had given up on—something that did not match the patterns in their Latin textbooks. And often, that was exactly the kind of case Saskia was best at solving.

"Tell your master I will come at midday."

The servant bowed and left. Saskia watched him go, her mind already turning through possibilities. A wealthy merchant, ill for some time, unresponsive to conventional treatment.

She did not know it yet, but this was the day her life would change forever.

---

Chapter 2: The House on the Keizersgracht

The Van der Meer house rose four stories above the canal, its brick facade perfectly proportioned, its windows tall and many-paned. Saskia stood before it at midday, feeling the weight of her satchel on her shoulder.

A maid led her through a marble entrance hall hung with paintings—ships at sea, maps of distant lands, a portrait of a stern-faced man in a black coat that Saskia assumed was Van der Meer himself. The floors gleamed. The air smelled of beeswax and expensive perfume.

Pieter van der Meer lay in a bed the size of a small ship, propped against pillows of white linen. He was perhaps fifty, with gray-streaked hair and a face that had been handsome before illness carved lines into it. His skin had a yellow pallor that Saskia recognized immediately—jaundice.

But there was something else. His hands trembled. His eyes had a glassy quality, and when he spoke, his voice came out in a whisper.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said. "Forgive me for not rising."

"Keep your strength, Heer van der Meer." Saskia set her satchel on a table and sat in the chair the maid pulled up for her. "Tell me everything. When did this begin?"

Van der Meer closed his eyes. "Three months ago. Perhaps four. I cannot remember exactly. It started with weakness in my legs. Then the shaking. Then the color of my skin changed."

"And the physicians? What did they say?"

He opened his eyes and laughed, a dry, bitter sound. "They said I had a humor imbalance. They bled me. They purged me. They applied leeches. They prescribed elixirs of gold and powdered pearl." He shook his head. "I have spent a fortune on their treatments, and I grow worse by the day."

Saskia nodded slowly. "May I examine you?"

She spent the next hour doing what the physicians had not: she asked questions. What did he eat? When did the tremors worsen? Had he traveled recently? Had he received any unusual gifts? Had he changed anything in his household—food, drink, servants?

Van der Meer answered patiently. He ate simply, he said. His household staff had been with him for years. He had not left Amsterdam in a decade. But yes, the tremors were worse after meals. Particularly after dinner.

"What do you eat for dinner?" Saskia asked.

"Roasted meat, usually. Bread. Wine. The same as any man."

"And after dinner?"

He paused. "I take a cordial. A special preparation. It helps me sleep."

Saskia's interest quickened. "Who prepares this cordial?"

"A man I employ. He calls himself the East India Man. He traveled to the Spice Islands for years. He knows medicines from the East." Van der Meer smiled weakly. "He says it is an elixir from Java. It gives peaceful dreams."

"I would like to see this cordial," Saskia said.

---

Chapter 3: The East India Man

The servant who brought the cordial was called Jan, but everyone in the household referred to him as the East India Man. He was tall and lean, with skin weathered by tropical sun and eyes that seemed to take in everything at once. He moved with the quiet economy of a man accustomed to ships and cramped quarters.

Saskia did not trust him.

She could not say why, exactly. There was nothing in his manner she could point to—he was polite, deferential, helpful. But when she took the bottle he offered, her fingers brushed his, and she felt a chill that had nothing to do with the temperature.

The bottle itself was beautiful. Dark blue glass, stoppered with silver, wrapped in a network of fine silver wire. Inside, a liquid the color of amber caught the light.

"What is in it?" Saskia asked.

"Rare herbs from the East," the East India Man said. "The secret was given to me by a Javanese healer. I cannot reveal it."

"I am treating your master," Saskia said. "I need to know what he consumes."

The East India Man smiled, and the smile did not reach his eyes. "With respect, Mevrouw Veldman, the cordial is what keeps Master van der Meer alive. Without it, he cannot sleep at all."

"And with it, he cannot stop shaking, and his skin turns yellow as a saffron field."

The smile vanished. "The physicians say the illness causes these things. Not the cordial."

"The physicians are fools," Saskia said. "They bled a man who is already weak."

She turned away, holding the bottle. "I will take this to my shop and analyze it. I will return tomorrow."

The East India Man watched her go. She felt his eyes on her back all the way to the door.

That night, alone in her workshop, Saskia performed the tests her father had taught her. She heated a small amount of the cordial over a candle flame. She added vinegar. She added lye. She held a silver spoon over the steam and watched for discoloration.

Nothing. The cordial seemed to be what it claimed: a mixture of herbs and honey, perhaps with a small amount of opium for sleep.

But the taste bothered her. She had placed a single drop on her tongue—a tiny amount, barely enough to register—and her mouth had gone numb for nearly an hour.

She sat back in her chair, staring at the bottle in the lamplight.

This was not a cure. This was a mask. Something was hiding beneath the surface of this amber liquid, something the crude tests of an apothecary could not detect.

She needed better methods. She needed to think like a poisoner.

---

Chapter 4: The Poisoner's Art

There was a man in Amsterdam who knew more about poisons than anyone else alive. His name was Dr. Hendrik van der Weyden, and he had been a friend of Saskia's father.

He had also been banished from the medical college for "unethical experiments."

Saskia found him in his laboratory on the Rozengracht, a cramped room filled with alembics and retorts, the walls stained with decades of chemical residue. Van der Weyden was old now, his beard white, his hands gnarled with arthritis, but his eyes were as sharp as they had ever been.

"Saskia Veldman," he said when she entered. "Your father's daughter. You have the look of someone who has found something strange."

"I need your help," she said. "I need to identify a poison that leaves no trace."

Van der Weyden laughed, a wheezing sound. "My dear girl, the old poisons—arsenic, mercury, lead—they always leave traces. That is why we catch the poisoners. If you want a poison that leaves no trace, you must think like nature itself."

He gestured to a shelf of books—heavy leather volumes with titles in Latin and Greek. "The ancients knew of plants that could kill without the crude markers of mineral poisons. Hemlock. Aconite. The death cap mushroom. But these, too, can be detected, if one knows what to look for."

"What if the poison is not from Europe?" Saskia asked. "What if it comes from the East Indies?"

Van der Weyden's eyes narrowed. "Now you interest me. Sit down. Tell me everything."

She told him about Van der Meer. About the tremors, the jaundice, the cordial. About the East India Man, and the bottle of blue glass.

When she finished, Van der Weyden was silent for a long time.

"There is a plant," he said finally. "I read about it in a Dutch translation of a Portuguese navigator's journal. It grows on a single island in the eastern seas. The natives call it the Silent Flower. It produces a poison that builds in the body over months. The symptoms match what you describe: weakness, trembling, jaundice, eventual death. And there is no known test for it—not in Europe, at least."

"How do I find this island?"

Van der Weyden shook his head. "You cannot. The Portuguese charts were destroyed. The Dutch captains who found it again kept the location secret—they wanted the island for themselves. But somewhere in Amsterdam, the coordinates may still exist. In a ship's log. In a merchant's private journal. In a map that was never surrendered to the Company."

Saskia felt the shape of the puzzle forming in her mind. "The East India Man. He knows. He brought the poison from somewhere."

"Then he knows the latitude," Van der Weyden said. "And he knows that if you find it, you find the proof of his crime."

---

Chapter 5: The Silent Flower

Saskia spent the next three days in the library of the East India Company.

It was a vast room, paneled in dark wood, filled with the accumulated knowledge of a century of exploration. Maps lined the walls—charts of the Indian Ocean, the Java Sea, the islands of the Spice Archipelago. Saskia had convinced a clerk to let her search the Company's botanical records, claiming she was writing a treatise on Eastern medicines.

She searched through dozens of journals, logs, and correspondence. She found descriptions of nutmeg trees and clove plantations, of cinnamon harvests and pepper vines. She found accounts of fevers and fluxes, of strange animals and stranger customs.

Of the Silent Flower, she found nothing.

On the third day, just as she was about to give up, she found a mention in the log of a ship called the Hoorn. The entry was brief, written in the cramped hand of a ship's surgeon:

"Made landfall at an uncharted island, 5 degrees south latitude, longitude uncertain. The island is small, perhaps two leagues across, covered in dense forest. The natives are hostile and would not let us land. We observed them gathering flowers from a plant that grows only on the cliffs—white blossoms with black centers. One of our men, who had tasted the nectar, fell ill that night with shaking and yellowing of the skin. He died before we weighed anchor."

Saskia copied the entry into her notebook. Five degrees south latitude. The longitude was uncertain—a common problem in that part of the world, where the magnetic variation made measurements unreliable.

But the latitude was enough. If she could find the specific island, if she could obtain a sample of the plant—she could compare its toxin to the cordial. She could prove the East India Man was poisoning Van der Meer.

She closed the log and looked up at the maps on the wall. Somewhere in that vast blue ocean, an island held the key to everything.

And somewhere in Amsterdam, the East India Man knew where it was.

---

Chapter 6: The Cordial's Secret

Back in her shop, Saskia subjected the cordial to every test she could devise.

She evaporated a sample over gentle heat and examined the residue under her father's old magnifying lens. She saw crystals, fine as dust, glittering with a faint golden color. They dissolved in alcohol but not in water.

She mixed the residue with oil of vitriol and watched the color change—first green, then a deep, angry red. She added a few drops of water, and the red faded to yellow.

She had never seen a reaction like this.

She compared it to known poisons. Arsenic turned oil of vitriol white. Mercury produced no color change at all. Lead turned it gray. The golden crystals were something else entirely.

She thought about the Silent Flower. A plant that grew only on one island, whose location had been erased from every chart. A poison that built in the body over months, mimicking disease. A cordial that supposedly helped Van der Meer sleep but was actually killing him slowly.

She needed proof. She needed the plant itself.

She went to see Van der Meer again. He was worse now, his skin a deeper yellow, his hands shaking so badly he could not hold a cup.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said, his voice barely a whisper. "Have you found a cure?"

"I have found the cause," she said. "But I need more information. The cordial you take—do you know what island the ingredients come from?"

Van der Meer frowned. "The East India Man speaks of a place called the Emerald Isle. He says the herbs grow nowhere else. He keeps the coordinates secret—claims it is a sacred location, known only to initiates."

"And you trust him?"

Van der Meer was silent for a long moment. "I knew him when he was a young sailor, before he made his fortune. He saved my life once, in Batavia. A fever. He found a native healer who cured me. I have trusted him ever since."

"Then he has been planning this for years," Saskia said.

She left the house with her mind racing. The Emerald Isle. Five degrees south latitude. An island that appeared on no map, whose location was known only to a few men—and the man who was slowly killing Pieter van der Meer.

As she walked home along the canal, she noticed a figure watching her from the bridge ahead. He was tall and lean, wearing a dark coat, his face shadowed by a wide-brimmed hat.

The East India Man.

He raised his hat in a mock salute, then turned and disappeared into the evening fog.

---

Chapter 7: The Map That Wasn't

Saskia's sister Clara found her that night still bent over her worktable, surrounded by books and papers.

"You haven't eaten," Clara said, setting a bowl of soup on the only clear space.

"I'm not hungry."

"You're never hungry when you're working on something. It worries me."

Saskia looked up at her sister. Clara's face was full of concern—the same expression their mother used to wear when their father stayed up too late in his laboratory.

"I think I've found something dangerous," Saskia said. "A plant that doesn't grow in Europe. A poison that leaves no trace. And a man who wants to kill one of the richest merchants in Amsterdam."

Clara's eyes widened. "Should we go to the authorities?"

"With what proof? I have a bottle of cordial with an unusual residue. I have a ship's log from thirty years ago describing an island. I have the word of an old alchemist who was expelled from the medical college." Saskia shook her head. "The East India Company directors are Van der Meer's partners. They won't act on rumors."

"Then what will you do?"

Saskia looked at the map of the Indies spread across her table—a chart she had borrowed from the Company library. It showed the known islands, the trade routes, the dangerous reefs. But one island was missing.

"The Emerald Isle isn't on any official chart," she said. "The coordinates were erased. But someone in Amsterdam knows where it is. The East India Man knows. And somewhere in this city, there's a record of the true location—a private map, a captain's log, a letter home."

"Or the East India Man himself," Clara said quietly.

Saskia met her sister's eyes. "Yes. He knows. And he's watching me."

They sat in silence as the candles burned lower and the fog pressed against the windows.

---

Chapter 8: The Leak in the Company

The next morning, Saskia returned to the Company library. She had a new strategy: she would look not for the island itself, but for the man who had erased it.

The East India Man's real name, she had learned from Van der Meer's servants, was Abraham van Ness. He had sailed for the Company for twenty years, rising from cabin boy to factor—a merchant agent who negotiated treaties with native rulers. He had retired five years ago with a considerable fortune and had come to live in Amsterdam, where he attached himself to Van der Meer's household.

She found his name in the Company's personnel records. Abraham van Ness, born in Middelburg, 1604. Sailed on the Hoorn, 1630-1635. Served in Batavia, 1636-1642. Factor in the Spice Islands, 1642-1650. Retired, 1655.

There was a gap in his service between 1642 and 1645. Three years unaccounted for.

She asked the clerk about it. The clerk shrugged. "Many factors stayed in the islands for years without regular correspondence. The Company only cared about cargo."

But Saskia noticed something else. Van Ness had been the factor responsible for a particular island group—a cluster of small volcanoes in the Banda Sea, known for their nutmeg plantations. And in 1644, the year he should have been there, the entire island group's coordinates were revised on the Company's master chart.

She pulled the old and new charts and compared them. The differences were small—a quarter-degree here, half a degree there—but they were systematic. Someone had deliberately shifted the entire island group on the map.

She looked more closely at the old chart. There was an erasure—a small island between two larger ones, scraped away and smoothed over. The ink on the new chart simply showed open water.

She had found it. The Emerald Isle had been there, marked on the old maps, part of the known world. And someone—someone with access to the Company's master charts—had erased it.

Abraham van Ness.

---

Chapter 9: The Alchemist's Laboratory

Saskia returned to Dr. van der Weyden with her findings.

"The island was on the old charts," she said. "Van Ness erased it. He's been hiding it for years, controlling access to the Silent Flower."

Van der Weyden examined the copies she had made of the charts. "He would need an accomplice in the Company's cartography office. Maps are not easy to change."

"Or he did it himself. He had access. He was a factor."

"But the Master Chart is kept locked in the director's vault. Only a director can authorize changes."

Saskia felt a cold weight settle in her stomach. "Then there's a director involved. Someone high in the Company."

Van der Weyden nodded slowly. "This is larger than a single poisoner, Saskia. You are dealing with men who control the spice trade. Men who have killed for nutmeg and cloves. If the Emerald Isle has a plant more valuable than nutmeg..."

"They don't want to share it," Saskia finished. "They're using the poison to eliminate anyone who knows about it. Van der Meer must have learned something. Or Van Ness decided he was a liability."

She looked at the old alchemist. "I need to find the island. I need to get a sample of the Silent Flower. It's the only way to prove the poison exists."

Van der Weyden was quiet for a long time. Then he went to a locked cabinet and took out a leather pouch.

"I have been saving this for thirty years," he said. "It belonged to a Portuguese sailor who died in my care. He claimed to have been to the Emerald Isle. He said the island's true coordinates were written in a Bible that belonged to the captain of the Hoorn."

He opened the pouch and pulled out a small object: a silver crucifix, worn smooth by handling.

"The sailor gave me this as payment for my treatment. He said it came from the island's inhabitants. Look at the back."

Saskia turned the crucifix over. Etched into the silver were numbers: 4°58'S, with a symbol she did not recognize beside them.

"The native script," Van der Weyden said. "The sailor said it was the name of the island in the local tongue."

Saskia stared at the coordinates. Four degrees, fifty-eight minutes south. Close to the Hoorn's log entry. Close enough to search.

"I need a ship," she said.

---

Chapter 10: The Voyage Begins

Pieter van der Meer, despite his illness, still had influence. When Saskia told him she needed to sail to the East Indies to find the source of his poison, he did not hesitate.

"I will give you a letter of credit," he said, his voice thin but determined. "Enough to charter a ship. Enough to hire men. Enough to buy whatever you need."

"But the Company—"

"Damn the Company. I am a director of the East India Company. My ships have sailed these waters for thirty years. One of them will take you."

He signed the papers with a shaking hand. The SS Merel, a fluyt of 300 tons, was being loaded at the Amsterdam docks for a voyage to Batavia. Her captain, Jan de Vries, was a man Van der Meer trusted.

"You will sail within the week," Van der Meer said. "Captain de Vries will follow any order I give. But Saskia—" He paused, his eyes meeting hers. "You must be careful. Van Ness knows what you are doing. You will not be safe once you leave Amsterdam."

"I am not safe now," Saskia said.

She returned home to pack. Clara insisted on coming with her.

"Absolutely not," Saskia said.

"Saskia, you cannot sail to the other side of the world alone. You are a woman. You need someone to watch your back."

"I need you here, running the shop."

"The shop can wait. You cannot."

They argued for an hour. In the end, Saskia agreed—not because she wanted Clara's company, but because she knew her sister was right. She would need someone she could trust completely.

Three days later, they stood on the deck of the Merel, watching Amsterdam disappear into the morning mist. The canals, the church spires, the red-tiled roofs—all of it grew smaller and smaller until it was only a memory on the horizon.

Saskia held the silver crucifix in her hand, running her thumb over the coordinates etched into the metal.

Four degrees, fifty-eight minutes south.

Somewhere in the vastness of the eastern seas, an island waited. A flower bloomed in silence. And a truth waited to be uncovered.

The wind filled the sails. The ship leaned into the North Sea.

The hunt had begun.

ARC II: THE VOYAGE SOUTH

---

Chapter 11: The Merel's Crew

Captain Jan de Vries was a man carved from sea salt and silence. He stood at the helm of the Merel with the stillness of a church column, his eyes fixed on the horizon, his hands resting lightly on the wheel. He spoke rarely and measured every word before releasing it.

He had sailed these waters for thirty years.

Saskia and Clara shared a cabin barely large enough for two hammocks and a sea chest. The space smelled of damp wood and tar, and the ship's motion took adjustment. Clara spent the first two days seasick, retching into a bucket while Saskia held her hair.

"You said you wanted to come," Saskia reminded her.

"I changed my mind," Clara groaned.

But by the third day, Clara found her sea legs. She began exploring the ship, making friends with the crew, learning the names of the sails and the ropes. She had a gift for conversation that Saskia lacked—she could draw stories from the most taciturn sailor.

The crew was a mix of Dutchmen, Scandinavians, and a few men from the East Indies who had signed on in Batavia years ago and never returned home. The most interesting among them was a Javanese man called Rudi, who served as the ship's cook and medic.

Rudi was small and precise, with hands that moved like birds. He knew the medicinal properties of every plant they carried, and he took an interest in Saskia's work.

"You are looking for a flower," he said to her one evening, as she sat grinding dried herbs in the galley.

She looked up sharply. "How do you know that?"

"The captain told me. He said you are searching for a plant that cures a disease." Rudi's dark eyes studied her. "But I think you are searching for something else."

Saskia hesitated. She did not know how much to trust this man. But something in his manner—the quiet certainty of someone who had seen much of the world—made her speak.

"I am searching for a plant that causes a disease," she said. "A poison. It comes from an island that isn't on any map."

Rudi's expression did not change. "I know of such an island."

Saskia's heart quickened. "You know where it is?"

"I know of it. I have never been there. But my grandmother told me stories when I was a child. An island of white cliffs and black flowers. She said the spirits of the island guard it, and any man who takes from the island without permission will die."

"The Silent Flower," Saskia whispered.

Rudi nodded slowly. "Yes. That is what the Javanese call it. Bunga Sunyi. The Silent Flower. It is known in the old stories, but no one speaks of it aloud. Because to speak of it is to invite the spirits' attention."

"Can you take me there?"

Rudi was silent for a long time. The ship creaked around them, the waves slapping against the hull.

"I can tell you what my grandmother told me," he said finally. "The island lies south of the Banda Sea, beyond the reach of the trade winds. The currents around it are treacherous, and the reefs hide beneath the water like crocodiles. But if you know the channel, you can enter the lagoon."

"I need the coordinates."

"The old people did not use coordinates. They used stars. My grandmother said you follow the Southern Cross until it touches the horizon, and then you turn toward the place where the sun sets in the month of the east monsoon."

Saskia's mind raced. She pulled out her notebook and sketched what she knew. The Hoorn's log gave five degrees south. The Portuguese sailor's crucifix gave four degrees, fifty-eight minutes. Rudi's star navigation would correspond to a latitude, if she could calculate it.

"We need to find the island before the east monsoon ends," she said. "Or we'll have to wait another year."

Rudi smiled. "Then we must sail quickly, Mevrouw Veldman. The east monsoon begins in three months."

---

Chapter 12: The Swimmer in the Night

They had been at sea for a week when the weather changed.

The sky turned gray, then green, then a bruised purple that made Saskia's skin prickle. The wind died, leaving the sails hanging limp. The sea grew flat and oily, and the air became thick and heavy.

"Storm coming," Captain de Vries said. He did not sound worried, but he began issuing orders in a voice that cut through the silence. The crew scrambled to secure the cargo, to reef the sails, to prepare for what was coming.

The storm hit at midnight.

Saskia had never experienced anything like it. The ship was lifted and thrown like a toy. Waves crashed over the deck, and the timbers groaned and screamed. Clara clung to Saskia in the darkness of their cabin, both of them praying to a God neither was sure was listening.

At some point in the night, Saskia heard a cry above the wind.

She crawled out of the cabin and onto the deck. Rain lashed her face. The world was a chaos of water and darkness. She saw men clinging to ropes, fighting to keep the ship from capsizing.

And she saw something else.

A figure in the water. A man, swimming alongside the ship.

For a moment, Saskia thought it was a sailor who had been thrown overboard. But the figure was moving too deliberately, too steadily. He was not trying to climb aboard. He was following the ship.

She watched as he reached into a pouch at his belt and pulled out something dark. He threw it—a small object that landed on the deck near Saskia's feet.

Then the waves swallowed him, and he was gone.

Saskia grabbed the object. It was a leather pouch, soaked through, tied with a cord. She stuffed it into her pocket and crawled back to the cabin.

When dawn came, the storm had passed. The sky was clear and blue, as if the night had never happened.

Saskia opened the pouch. Inside was a piece of parchment, the ink smeared but still legible:

"The East India Man is on your trail. He has hired a faster ship. Trust no one but the cook. — A Friend"

She read the note three times. Then she tucked it into her bodice and went to find Rudi.

---

Chapter 13: The Faster Ship

Captain de Vries studied the note with the same expression he studied everything—as if he were reading the sea itself.

"A faster ship," he said. "The Merel is a cargo vessel. Heavy. Slow. If Van Ness has a brigantine or a schooner, he could catch us before we reach the Cape."

"How long do we have?"

De Vries calculated. "If he left Amsterdam within a day of us—and he likely did—he could be three to four days behind. A fast ship could close that gap in two weeks."

"So we have two weeks to reach the island before he catches us."

"Assuming we know where the island is."

Saskia spread her notes on the captain's table. She showed him the Hoorn's log entry, the crucifix, the calculations she had made with Rudi's guidance.

De Vries studied everything in silence. When he looked up, there was something like respect in his eyes.

"You have done good work, Mevrouw Veldman. But the Banda Sea is vast. Without precise coordinates, we could search for months."

"Then we need someone who knows the precise coordinates," Saskia said. "Someone who has been there."

"Van Ness."

"Van Ness. He's following us. He has the location. And he has a faster ship."

De Vries understood immediately. "You want to let him catch us."

"No. I want to catch him. Or rather, I want to take what he knows."

She explained her plan. It was dangerous, reckless, and depended on timing and luck. De Vries listened without interrupting.

When she finished, he said, "You are not like other women I have met."

"I am not like other women in Amsterdam," Saskia agreed. "Will you help me?"

He nodded slowly. "I will. But if this goes wrong, we will all die at the bottom of the ocean."

"I know."

---

Chapter 14: The False Island

They changed course.

Instead of heading directly for the Banda Sea, they angled south, toward a cluster of small islands that appeared on the charts as the Guning Group. According to Van der Weyden's Portuguese sailor, the Emerald Isle was sometimes confused with one of these islands—a mistake that had cost several ships their lives.

De Vries ordered the crew to prepare a deception. They would sail openly toward the Guning Group, making no effort to hide their course. If Van Ness's ship was watching, he would see them heading for the wrong islands.

Meanwhile, Rudi and Saskia studied the wind patterns and currents. The real Emerald Isle, according to Rudi's grandmother's stories, lay further east, beyond the reach of the regular shipping lanes. It was deliberately hidden—not just on maps, but by nature itself.

"The currents around it are strange," Rudi said. "My grandmother said the sea there runs in circles, as if it is trapped by the reef. A ship that does not know the channel will be pulled onto the rocks."

"How do we find the channel?"

Rudi closed his eyes, reciting from memory. "Approach from the west at sunrise. Look for a break in the reef where the water turns from black to green. Follow the green water until you see the white cliffs. The channel is there, hidden beneath the surface."

They sailed for another week, making steady progress toward the Guning Group. On the seventh day, the lookout sighted another ship on the horizon.

"Fast brigantine," the lookout reported. "Dutch colors. Gaining on us."

Van Ness.

Saskia felt a cold thrill. Her plan was working. Van Ness thought they were heading for the wrong islands. He was following, waiting to see what they found.

But she knew he would not wait forever. At some point, he would realize the deception and act. And when he did, he would have the advantage of speed.

The question was: who would find the real Emerald Isle first?

---

Chapter 15: The Lagoon

The Guning Group appeared on the horizon like a row of teeth—jagged volcanic peaks rising from the turquoise sea. The largest island was surrounded by a coral reef, its surface dotted with palms and white sand beaches.

De Vries anchored the Merel in the lee of the island and sent a longboat to shore. Saskia and Clara went with the landing party, carrying collecting jars and notebooks.

The island was beautiful. The beach was powdered coral, soft underfoot. Palm trees leaned over the shore, their fronds rattling in the trade wind. Inland, the vegetation grew thick—ferns and vines and trees with trunks the size of barrels.

Saskia searched for three days. She climbed the volcanic slopes. She hacked through the jungle. She followed streams and animal trails. She found hundreds of plants—orchids, bromeliads, ferns of every size—but no Silent Flower.

On the third night, she sat on the beach with Clara, watching the sun set over the water.

"It's not here," she said. "We're wasting time."

"Maybe it's here, and we're not looking in the right place," Clara said.

"Rudi's grandmother said white cliffs. This island has no cliffs. It's volcanic, all slopes and craters."

"So the Emerald Isle is somewhere else."

"Somewhere Van Ness doesn't want us to find."

They sat in silence for a while. The stars came out, one by one, until the sky was thick with light.

"Look," Clara said, pointing at the southern horizon.

Saskia followed her gaze. There, low in the sky, was the Southern Cross, tilted and bright.

"Heading south," Saskia murmured. "Rudi said you follow the Southern Cross until it touches the horizon."

"And then turn west."

"West." Saskia stared at the constellation. "We've been going east. Toward the Guning Group. But what if the island is west? What if Van Ness's deception was not on the charts but in our expectations?"

"What do you mean?"

"What if the Emerald Isle is not in the Banda Sea at all? What if it's west of here? Somewhere along the route to Africa?"

Clara's eyes widened. "That would change everything."

Saskia stood up, her heart racing. "We need to tell Captain de Vries. We need to change course."

She looked out at the dark water, where the lights of the brigantine glimmered on the horizon.

And somewhere in the darkness, a faster ship was still watching.

---

Chapter 16: The Chart in the Dark

Saskia spent the night studying every map and journal she had brought.

The old charts. The Hoorn's log. The Portuguese sailor's scattered notes. Rudi's grandmother's stories. Piece by piece, she tried to assemble a coherent picture.

The Hoorn had been sailing from the Cape of Good Hope to Batavia when they discovered the island. That route took them across the Indian Ocean, passing south of Madagascar, past the islands of the Mascarene archipelago. It was a well-known route, used by Dutch ships for decades.

But a ship that strayed too far south, blown off course by a storm, could encounter islands that appeared on no chart. Islands that the Company had not surveyed. Islands that existed only in the logs of ships that had been lost at sea.

She studied the wind patterns. The prevailing winds in the Indian Ocean blew from east to west in the southern latitudes—the famous Roaring Forties. A ship blown south of the Cape could be carried toward islands that lay off the main shipping routes.

The Dutch had discovered many such islands: Mauritius, Réunion, Rodrigues. But there were others, smaller and more remote, that had been sighted and forgotten.

"Captain," she said, finding de Vries at the helm. "I need you to help me navigate."

She spread her chart on the deck and showed him her logic. De Vries listened, nodding slowly.

"It is possible," he said. "The Hoorn encountered a storm off the Cape. The log mentions it—they were blown south for three days. If they sighted an island during that time, it could be anywhere from thirty to forty degrees south."

"But the log says five degrees south. That's the latitude of the Banda Sea."

"Yes. Unless the log entry was made later, when the Hoorn had already returned to its proper course. The ship's surgeon might have recorded the island's location days after the actual sighting, writing from memory."

Saskia stared at the chart. "Then the latitude on the log is wrong. The true latitude is somewhere in the southern Indian Ocean."

De Vries nodded. "And Van Ness knows the truth. He knows we are looking in the wrong place."

"But he's following us. He thinks we're going to find the island."

"He is following you to make sure you don't. If you sail for the wrong island, he does nothing. But if you sail for the right one..."

"He will try to stop us."

Saskia looked at the chart, at the vast empty spaces of the southern Indian Ocean. Somewhere in that emptiness, an island waited.

"Then we need to stop him first."

---

Chapter 17: The Chase

The Merel weighed anchor at dawn and set a course west-southwest, away from the Guning Group and into the open ocean.

Saskia watched the brigantine on the horizon. For a long moment, it held its course, continuing toward the islands. Then, as if realizing the Merel had changed direction, it turned. The gap between the two ships began to close.

"Five days," de Vries estimated. "Maybe six. Then he'll be within cannon range."

"He has cannons?"

"He's a merchant ship, but he'll have at least four guns. Enough to damage us, if not sink us."

Saskia's plan had always carried this risk. Van Ness would not let her reach the island if he could prevent it.

"Is there any way to lose him?"

De Vries shook his head. "Not in open water. The Merel is slower. He will catch us."

"Then we need to reach the island before he does."

She turned to Rudi, who had joined them on the quarterdeck. "The channel into the lagoon. Can we find it without the precise coordinates?"

Rudi looked at the sky. "The stars will tell us, if we are close enough. But we need to be close. Very close."

"How close?"

"Within sight of the island. The lagoon channel is not visible from far away."

Saskia calculated. The brigantine was gaining. They had perhaps four days before Van Ness was close enough to fire on them.

"Rudi, you and I will work together. We'll use the stars, the currents, everything we know. We need to find that island in three days."

"And if we don't?"

Saskia looked at the brigantine on the horizon, growing larger by the hour.

"Then Van Ness wins."

Chapter 18: The First Shot

Two days later, the brigantine raised its colors.

They were close enough now that Saskia could see figures moving on the deck. She saw a tall man in a dark coat standing at the bow, watching them through a spyglass.

Van Ness.

A puff of smoke appeared from the brigantine's bow. A moment later, the sound reached them—a flat crack that echoed across the water. The cannonball splashed into the sea a hundred yards to starboard.

"A warning shot," de Vries said. "He wants us to stop."

"Can we return fire?"

"We have two small guns. They would not reach."

Saskia gripped the railing. "Then we keep going."

Another shot. This one landed closer—fifty yards astern. The crew was growing nervous. Saskia saw men crossing themselves, muttering prayers.

"Captain," she said. "How far to the island?"

"I cannot be certain. Rudi believes we are close—he has seen birds that nest only on islands. And the water has changed color. We may be entering shallower waters."

"Then we need to find the channel. Now."

Saskia climbed up to the crow's nest, ignoring the vertigo. From the top, she could see the ocean stretching in every direction. The brigantine was perhaps a mile behind them, its sails full and white.

She scanned the horizon. Nothing. Just water and sky.

But wait—there was something. A patch of water that was different from the rest. Not darker or lighter, but somehow... smoother. As if the surface had a different texture.

She called down to Rudi. "There's something northeast of us. About two miles. Can you see it?"

Rudi climbed up beside her. He stared at the patch of smooth water for a long time.

"The reef," he said. "That's the reef. The channel must be nearby."

"And the island?"

"Beyond the reef. Hidden by the curve of the earth."

A third cannonball whistled overhead, close enough that Saskia felt the wind of its passage.

"Hold on!" Rudi shouted.

They climbed down as the Merel changed course, heading toward the smooth patch of water. The brigantine was gaining, close enough now that Saskia could see the figures on its deck clearly.

Van Ness was at the bow, a smoking pistol in his hand. He raised it and fired.

The bullet struck the deck near Saskia's feet.

"You cannot escape!" he shouted across the water. "Turn back, and I will let you live!"

Saskia looked at the smooth water ahead. The channel, if it existed, was somewhere in that expanse of sea.

"We're not turning back," she said.

---

Chapter 19: The Reef

The Merel surged forward, heading straight for the reef.

De Vries stood at the helm, his face carved from stone. He had ordered the leadsman to take soundings, and the man called out the depths in a steady rhythm: twenty fathoms. Fifteen. Twelve.

"Reef coming up fast," the leadsman called. "Ten fathoms. Eight."

Saskia could see it now—the coral beneath the surface, a jagged wall of brown and green. The sea broke over it in white foam.

"The channel," Rudi said, pointing to a gap in the reef where the water turned from turquoise to deep green. "There. That's the entrance."

De Vries turned the wheel. The Merel heeled as it changed course, her hull groaning. The gap in the reef was narrow—barely wider than the ship itself.

"Seven fathoms," the leadsman called. "Six."

The brigantine was still behind them, but Saskia saw it slow as it reached the reef. It could not follow. The channel was too narrow for two ships.

Van Ness would have to wait.

The Merel slipped through the gap like a needle through cloth. Coral scraped the hull, a sound like grinding teeth. Then they were through, and the water opened into a lagoon of breathtaking beauty.

The island rose before them, green and lush, with cliffs of white limestone that caught the morning light.

The Emerald Isle.

"There," Saskia breathed. "It's real."

But even as she said it, she knew the danger was not over. Van Ness was still behind them. He would find a way through the reef, or he would wait for them to come out.

They had found the island. Now they had to survive it.

Chapter 20: The White Cliffs

The Merel anchored in the lagoon, and Saskia took a landing party to shore.

The beach was white sand, fine as sugar. Palms lined the shore, their trunks leaning toward the sea. Behind the beach, the cliffs rose sheer and white, their faces streaked with black volcanic rock.

Saskia, Clara, Rudi, and four sailors climbed the steep trail to the top of the cliffs. The vegetation changed as they ascended—from beach-side palms to dense scrub to a forest of strange trees with leaves like feathers.

At the top of the cliffs, Saskia stopped and stared.

The entire top of the island was a plateau, covered in a forest of white blossoms. Each flower was the size of a human hand, with petals like snow and centers like black velvet. They grew on low bushes, their leaves dark green and waxy.

The Silent Flower. Bunga Sunyi.

Saskia approached the nearest bush. The flowers gave off a faint, sweet scent, almost imperceptible. She knelt and examined one closely.

"Don't touch it," Rudi warned. "Even the pollen can be dangerous."

Saskia pulled out her collecting kit. With tweezers and a glass vial, she carefully took a sample—petals, leaves, a piece of root. She sealed it and labeled it.

"Now we need to get back to the ship," she said. "We have what we came for."

But as she turned, she saw something that made her blood run cold.

On the far side of the plateau, a figure stood watching them. Tall, lean, dressed in dark clothes.

Van Ness.

He was already on the island. He had found another way in.

And he was holding a pistol aimed directly at Saskia's heart.

"Hello, Mevrouw Veldman," he called across the clearing. "I was hoping you would find this place. It saved me the trouble of searching."

Chapter 21: The Gun in the Clearing

Saskia did not move. The pistol in Van Ness's hand was steady, aimed directly at her chest. He stood at the edge of the clearing, the white flowers at his feet, his dark coat blending with the shadows.

"You have been a persistent annoyance," he said. "The moment you arrived at Van der Meer's house, I knew you would be trouble. I should have dealt with you then."

"Dealing with a woman in Amsterdam is harder than dealing with one on a deserted island," Saskia said.

Van Ness smiled. "True. Here, there are no witnesses."

Clara stepped forward, her face pale but defiant. "If you hurt my sister, I will—"

"What? You will do nothing, child." Van Ness shifted the pistol slightly. "You are both going to have an accident. A tragic fall from the cliffs. The sea will claim your bodies, and no one will ever know what happened."

"The crew of the Merel saw us come ashore," Saskia said. "They know you are here."

"Your captain is a practical man. When he sees a faster ship with more guns blocking his exit, he will sail away. He will report that you disappeared on the island. A mystery, but not one worth dying for."

Saskia's mind raced. She needed time. She needed an opening.

"How did you get here before us?" she asked. "Your ship was behind us."

Van Ness laughed. "There is a second channel. A tunnel through the reef, known only to me. I have been coming to this island for ten years. I know every rock, every current, every path."

"You have been harvesting the Silent Flower."

"Of course. It is the most valuable plant in the world. More valuable than nutmeg. More valuable than gold. A poison that cannot be detected, that mimics natural death. Do you know how many wealthy men have died from this flower? How many inheritances I have claimed? How many rivals have been eliminated?"

He took a step forward. "Van der Meer was my crowning achievement. The longest, slowest poison I have ever administered. Months of suffering, perfectly mimicking a wasting disease. And then you came along."

"I will expose you."

"You will be dead."

He raised the pistol. Saskia saw his finger tighten on the trigger.

And then Rudi, who had been edging around the clearing during their conversation, threw a handful of white flower petals directly into Van Ness's face.

Van Ness fired, but the shot went wide, tearing through the leaves above Saskia's head. He staggered backward, clawing at his eyes, spitting pollen.

"Run!" Rudi shouted.

They ran.

Chapter 22: The Descent

The trail down the cliffs was treacherous, loose stones and crumbling edges. Saskia held Clara's hand, pulling her along, while Rudi covered their retreat.

Behind them, they heard Van Ness shouting curses, crashing through the undergrowth. He was faster than them, and he knew the island.

"The boat," Saskia gasped. "We need to reach the boat."

They burst out of the forest and onto the beach. The longboat was where they had left it, pulled up on the sand. The sailors who had stayed with it saw them running and shoved the boat into the water.

"Get in!" Saskia shouted.

They splashed through the surf, climbed into the boat. The sailors pushed off and began rowing. Van Ness appeared at the edge of the forest, his pistol reloaded. He fired, and a bullet tore through the air beside Saskia's ear.

"He'll follow us in his ship," Clara said.

"He can't," Saskia said. "The channel's too narrow for his brigantine. He'd have to take the long way around."

"Unless he uses the tunnel," Rudi said. "The second channel he mentioned."

They reached the Merel and scrambled aboard. De Vries was waiting, his face grim.

"You found what you needed?"

"I have samples. Enough to prove the poison."

"Then we need to leave. Now."

The Merel weighed anchor and began moving toward the channel. Saskia looked back at the island, at the white cliffs and the dark forest.

And she saw the brigantine moving into the lagoon through a different gap in the reef.

Van Ness had found his tunnel.

Chapter 23: The Escape

The Merel pushed through the narrow channel, coral scraping her hull. Behind them, the brigantine was emerging from the tunnel, faster and more maneuverable.

"He'll catch us before we clear the reef," de Vries said. "We cannot outrun him."

Saskia looked at the brigantine, at the cannons being prepared on its deck.

"Can we get close enough to board?" she asked.

De Vries stared at her. "Board? Mevrouw, they have more men and more guns."

"If we board, I can get to Van Ness. I can take his charts—the ones showing the true coordinates. Without them, he has no proof."

"Proof of what? That he owns an island?"

"Proof that he erased it from the Company's maps. Proof that he has been using the Silent Flower for years."

De Vries considered this. Then he smiled—a rare expression that transformed his stern face.

"You are the most dangerous woman I have ever met."

The Merel turned, presenting her broadside to the brigantine. De Vries ordered the grappling hooks prepared.

"On my command," he said. "We will pass close enough to throw the hooks. Then we board."

The brigantine fired. The cannonball tore through the Merel's rigging, sending ropes and canvas crashing to the deck.

"Now!" de Vries shouted.

The Merel swung toward the brigantine. Grappling hooks flew across the gap, biting into the enemy's rail. The two ships crashed together, timbers groaning.

Saskia grabbed her satchel and climbed over the rail, onto the deck of Van Ness's ship.

Chapter 24: The Captain's Cabin

The deck of the brigantine was chaos. Men were running, drawing swords, trying to repel the boarders. Saskia slipped through the confusion, heading for the captain's cabin at the stern.

She found the door locked. She pulled out a knife—small, sharp, her father's—and worked the blade into the gap. The lock gave way with a click.

The cabin was spacious, paneled in mahogany, with a large desk covered in charts. Maps of the Indian Ocean. The Banda Sea. And there, on top of the pile, a detailed chart of an island with a single annotation: "Emerald Isle, 4°58'S, 72°15'E."

She grabbed the chart. She also grabbed a leather journal, stuffed with notes in Van Ness's hand. Evidence of years of poisonings.

She turned to leave—and found Van Ness standing in the doorway.

"Put the chart down," he said.

"Or what?" Saskia said. "You'll shoot me? That would be loud. Your men would hear. They would know what you have been doing."

Van Ness's jaw tightened. "I have a faster ship. I have more men. Even if you escape now, I will find you. I will follow you to Amsterdam. I will kill you in your bed."

"Then I will make sure everyone knows the truth before I die."

She moved toward the window. Van Ness lunged.

He was fast, but Saskia was smaller. She ducked under his arm and threw herself through the window, shattering the glass. She landed on the deck below, rolled, and came up running.

Behind her, Van Ness shouted in fury.

She reached the rail, climbed over, and jumped back to the Merel just as the two ships began to drift apart.

"I have it!" she shouted, holding up the chart.

De Vries ordered the sails raised. The Merel turned and headed for open water.

Behind them, the brigantine was still tangled in the reef channel, unable to follow.

Chapter 25: The Journal

They sailed for three days before Saskia felt safe enough to open the journal.

Van Ness had kept meticulous records. Page after page of names, dates, doses. Merchants in Amsterdam. A rival factor in Batavia. A Company director who had discovered the island's secret. Two captains who had known the location. A Javanese healer who had shown him the flower's properties.

He had killed at least fifteen people over ten years.

And Pieter van der Meer was to have been his last—the richest, the most carefully executed. Van Ness had been planning this poisoning for two years, slowly administering the cordial, watching the symptoms develop. Van der Meer's death would have looked like a natural wasting disease. No one would have suspected.

But Saskia had ruined everything.

"You look troubled," Clara said, sitting beside her on the deck.

"I am reading about men who did not deserve to die," Saskia said. "Van Ness killed them all for money."

"And now we can stop him."

"Can we? This journal is evidence, yes. But it is also a confession. It could hang him. But only if we can prove the poison exists."

"Which is why we need the flower."

Saskia nodded. "And we have it. Or at least, I have samples. But I need to prove that the samples match what was in Van der Meer's cordial. I need to show that the Silent Flower produces the exact toxin that was killing him."

"Then we need to get back to Amsterdam," Clara said. "Before Van Ness does."

Saskia looked at the sky. They had a head start, but Van Ness had a faster ship. And he knew the shortcut—the tunnel through the reef had saved him time.

"We have three months before he can reach Amsterdam," de Vries said, joining them. "Maybe four, if the winds are against him."

"And we?"

"Six. The Merel is slow, and we need to reprovision at the Cape."

"Then we find a faster way home."

Chapter 26: The Decision at the Cape

They reached the Cape of Good Hope two months later, battered by storms and short on provisions.

Table Mountain rose before them, flat-topped and majestic, its slopes green from the winter rains. The Dutch settlement at the Cape was small—a fort, a few gardens, a hospital for sailors sick with scurvy.

Saskia found a Company clerk and sent a letter to Van der Meer, explaining what she had discovered and warning him about Van Ness. She also sent a letter to Dr. van der Weyden, asking him to prepare his laboratory for the analysis.

But the clerk had troubling news.

"A ship arrived last week," he said. "A brigantine, out of Batavia. The captain was looking for a woman—an apothecary traveling with her sister. He offered a reward for information."

Van Ness was ahead of them. He had reached the Cape before they did, and now he knew they were coming.

"He'll be waiting for us in Amsterdam," Clara said.

"Or he'll try to intercept us before we get there."

Saskia studied the map of the Atlantic. There were not many places a ship could take on provisions between the Cape and Europe. The Portuguese had settlements in Brazil, but the Dutch preferred to stop at the island of St. Helena, in the middle of the Atlantic.

"Captain," she said. "Can we avoid St. Helena? Sail directly for Amsterdam?"

De Vries shook his head. "Not without more provisions. We do not have enough food or water."

"Then we need to be careful. Van Ness will be watching the port."

They spent a week at the Cape, taking on fresh water, dried meat, and vegetables. Saskia used the time to examine her samples of the Silent Flower, pressing them, preserving them, making notes.

She also had an idea.

"If Van Ness is watching the ports, we need to confuse him," she said. "We need to make him think we are not going to Amsterdam."

"What do you propose?"

"We land elsewhere. A smaller port—Zeeland, perhaps. Or we sail to Hamburg and travel overland. Anything to get to Amsterdam before he expects us."

De Vries considered. "Hamburg is a good option. The winds are favorable, and the port is busy enough that we might slip through unnoticed."

"Then Hamburg it is."

Chapter 27: The Storm off the Coast

The Atlantic crossing was brutal.

Winter storms battered the Merel, driving her far off course. The crew grew sick and tired. The provisions ran low.

Saskia spent most of the voyage in her cabin, working on her analysis. She had brought a small portable distillation kit, and she used it to extract the active compound from the Silent Flower samples. She compared it to the residue from Van der Meer's cordial.

They matched.

The same golden crystals. The same reaction with oil of vitriol. The same numbness when placed on the tongue.

She had her proof.

But the storm that had driven them off course had also cost them time. By the time they sighted the coast of Europe, they had been at sea for five months. Van Ness had a three-month head start.

"Another day to Hamburg," de Vries said. "Maybe two."

Saskia looked at the gray coastline, the familiar shape of the European shore. She had been away for nearly a year. She had crossed two oceans. She had been shot at, chased, shipwrecked by storm.

And now, at the end of everything, she still had to face Van Ness one more time.

"Captain," she said. "When we reach Hamburg, I need you to find a fast messenger. Someone who can reach Amsterdam before we do. I need to warn Van der Meer that we are coming."

"And to tell him to be ready for Van Ness."

"Yes."

De Vries nodded. "I will find you the fastest rider in Hamburg."

Chapter 28: The Long Road

Hamburg was cold and gray, its streets muddy from autumn rains.

Saskia and Clara left the ship with their samples and journals, carrying everything they needed in two leather bags. They found a merchant willing to sell them horses—poor animals, but serviceable—and set out for Amsterdam.

The journey was two weeks on horseback, through the German states and the Dutch Republic. They traveled in heavy rain and cold wind, sleeping in inns that smelled of smoke and sour beer.

Clara complained constantly but kept riding. Saskia admired her sister's resilience. She had not wanted Clara to come, but now she was grateful for the company.

On the tenth day, they crossed into Dutch territory. The familiar landscape—canals, windmills, flat fields stretching to the horizon—made Saskia's heart ache with relief.

"Almost home," she said.

But home was not safe. Van Ness was there, somewhere in Amsterdam, waiting for them.

They arrived at the outskirts of the city on a gray afternoon. The church spires rose against the sky, the canals gleamed in the fading light. Amsterdam was beautiful and terrible, and somewhere in its streets, a murderer was walking free.

"Where do we go?" Clara asked.

"To Van der Meer's house. If he is still alive."

They rode through the city gates, their horses' hooves clattering on the cobblestones.

Chapter 29: The House of Silence

The Van der Meer house was dark.

No lights in the windows. No servants at the door. The brass knocker was cold and dusty.

Saskia's heart sank. She had been too late.

She knocked anyway. Nothing. She knocked again, harder.

The door opened a crack. An old face peered out—the butler, looking thinner and grayer than she remembered.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said. His voice was hollow.

"Is he dead?"

The butler shook his head. "Not dead, Mevrouw. But near to it. He stopped taking the cordial, as you instructed. But the poison had already done its work. He has not left his bed in months."

"Let me see him."

She followed the butler through the dark house. The paintings were still there—ships, maps, the stern-faced portrait of Van der Meer. But the house had lost its warmth.

Van der Meer lay in his bed, a skeleton wrapped in yellow skin. His eyes were closed, his breathing shallow.

Saskia sat beside him and took his hand. He opened his eyes, recognizing her after a long moment.

"Saskia," he whispered. "You came back."

"Of course I came back. I found the island. I found the proof."

"It does not matter. I am dying."

"No." Saskia's voice was firm. "You are not dying. I am going to save you, Heer van der Meer. And then I am going to see that Van Ness hangs for what he has done."

She opened her satchel and began preparing an antidote—a mixture of activated charcoal and other compounds she had developed on the voyage, based on Rudi's knowledge of the Silent Flower.

"They said I was going to die," Van der Meer said. "All of them. The physicians. The East India Man. They said there was no cure."

"They were wrong."

She fed him the antidote, spoonful by spoonful. She sat with him through the night, watching his breathing grow stronger, the color slowly returning to his cheeks.

By dawn, he was sleeping peacefully for the first time in months.

Chapter 30: The Accusation

The next morning, Saskia went to the headquarters of the East India Company.

She carried Van Ness's journal, the chart of the Emerald Isle, and her own analysis of the Silent Flower. She demanded to see the directors.

The clerk tried to turn her away. She refused to leave. She created such a scene that the directors eventually agreed to see her—if only to get her out of their lobby.

The boardroom was paneled in dark wood, lit by tall windows overlooking the canal. Twelve men sat around a long table, their faces stern and suspicious.

"Mevrouw Veldman," the chairman said. "This is highly irregular. A woman, demanding an audience with the directors of the East India Company—"

"I am here to accuse one of your factors of murder," Saskia said.

The room went silent.

She laid out her evidence. The cordial. The poison. The island that had been erased from the charts. The journal with fifteen names. The chart with the true coordinates.

"You are accusing Abraham van Ness of poisoning Heer van der Meer?" the chairman said.

"And fifteen others," Saskia said. "The evidence is here. The proof is in the bottle of cordial I have preserved. The toxin matches the samples I collected on the Emerald Isle."

"Van Ness is a respected factor. He has served the Company for twenty years."

"Then you know him well. Which is why you must know that he had access to the master charts. That he had the opportunity to erase the Emerald Isle from the maps. That he has been traveling to that island for a decade, harvesting the Silent Flower and using it to kill anyone who got in his way."

The directors exchanged glances. Saskia saw uncertainty in their faces.

"We will investigate these claims," the chairman said.

"Investigate them now," Saskia said. "Because Van Ness is in Amsterdam. I saw his ship at the docks. He knows I have returned. He will try to flee, or he will try to destroy the evidence."

She looked around the table, meeting the eyes of each man in turn.

"Or he will try to kill me again."

---

ARC IV: THE TRIAL

---

Chapter 31: The Arrest

The directors of the East India Company moved slowly, but they moved.

By evening, a squad of Company guards had been dispatched to the house where Van Ness was staying, on the Prinsengracht. Saskia insisted on accompanying them.

The house was a narrow building, three stories tall, with a warehouse attached. The guards hammered on the door. No answer. They broke it down.

The house was empty.

Van Ness had fled.

Saskia searched the rooms, looking for any trace of him. She found his clothes, his books, his papers. But no Van Ness. He had left in a hurry, taking only what he could carry.

"He's heading for the docks," she said. "He'll steal a ship. He'll go back to the island."

"The island is a day's sail from Batavia," one of the guards said. "He can't just—"

"He can do anything," Saskia interrupted. "He knows the island better than anyone. He has supplies hidden there. He could survive for years."

They ran to the docks. The harbor was crowded with ships, their masts rising like a forest of bare trees. The guards spread out, searching each vessel.

Saskia saw a figure moving along the quay, carrying a bag. Tall. Lean. Moving with the quiet economy of a man accustomed to ships.

Van Ness.

"Stop!" Saskia shouted.

He turned. His eyes met hers. For a moment, neither of them moved.

Then he threw his bag into the water and dove in after it.

"What is he doing?" a guard shouted.

Saskia realized in horror. "He's sinking his evidence. He's destroying everything."

But Van Ness did not surface. The bag was heavy—too heavy. It pulled him down into the dark water of the harbor.

The guards dove after him, but it was too late. Abraham van Ness, the East India Man, was gone, swallowed by the same waters that had carried his ships.

They found his body three days later, tangled in a fishing net.

Saskia stood on the quay, looking at the gray water, feeling nothing.

"He's dead," Clara said softly.

"Yes."

"Is that enough? Is it over?"

Saskia shook her head. "It's not over. His journal is safe. The evidence is safe. But there are men in the Company who helped him. Directors who knew what he was doing. The poison did not travel only one way."

Clara looked at her with concern. "What are you going to do?"

"I am going to find them all."

---

Chapter 32: The Conspiracy

Van Ness's death did not end the investigation. If anything, it deepened it.

Saskia spent the next weeks in the Company archives, cross-referencing the names in Van Ness's journal against Company records. She mapped out a web of connections: men who had received bonuses, promotions, land grants. Men who had died unexpectedly, leaving their positions open.

She found a pattern.

There were four directors who had benefited from every death associated with Van Ness. They had inherited shares, acquired ships, taken over trade routes. And they had all, at various points, signed off on changes to the Company's master charts—changes that conveniently removed small islands from official records.

One of those directors was the chairman himself.

Saskia went to Dr. van der Weyden with her findings.

"If I accuse them publicly, they will destroy me," she said. "They control the Company. They control the courts. They have more money than God."

"Then you do not accuse them publicly," van der Weyden said. "You accuse them privately. You give them a choice: resign quietly, or face a public trial that will destroy the Company's reputation."

"Blackmail."

"Justice."

Saskia considered this. It was not the clean, righteous outcome she had imagined. But it was the outcome that would work.

She composed a letter, carefully worded, laying out her evidence against each of the four directors. She did not accuse them of murder directly—she could not prove that—but she made it clear that they had known about Van Ness's activities and had chosen not to act.

She sent the letter to each director, with a copy to the full board.

Within a week, three of the four directors had submitted their resignations, citing "personal reasons." The fourth, the chairman, held out for a month. Then he, too, stepped down.

Saskia did not feel triumphant. She felt tired.

---

Chapter 33: The Return of the Sun

Pieter van der Meer recovered slowly.

The antidote Saskia had developed—a mixture of activated charcoal, milk thistle, and a compound derived from the root of the Silent Flower itself—gradually flushed the poison from his system. His skin lost its yellow tinge. His hands stopped shaking. He began to eat again, and then to walk.

By the spring of 1657, he was well enough to leave his house for the first time in over a year.

He stood on his doorstep, looking up at the sky, the sunlight falling on his face. Saskia stood beside him.

"I never thought I would see the sun again," he said.

"You are lucky to be alive."

"I am lucky to have found you." He turned to her. "Saskia, I owe you my life. I owe you everything. If there is anything I can do for you, anything at all—"

"I want to finish what I started," she said. "I want to study the Silent Flower. I want to understand its properties—not just as a poison, but as a medicine. Every poison can be a cure, if used correctly."

Van der Meer nodded slowly. "The island is yours. I will buy the rights from the Company. It will be your laboratory."

Saskia felt a weight lift from her shoulders. "Thank you."

"No. Thank you."

---

Chapter 34: The Apothecary's Return

Saskia's shop on the Herengracht had been closed for nearly a year, but Clara had kept it clean, dusting the shelves and airing the rooms. When Saskia finally unlocked the door and stepped inside, she felt a peace she had not known in months.

The smell of camphor and lavender. The familiar jars, labeled in her own hand. The mortar and pestle on the counter, waiting for her touch.

She was home.

The first customer came within an hour—a woman with a cough who had been waiting for Saskia's return. Then another, with a child suffering from fever. Then a third, an old man with joint pain.

Word spread quickly. Mevrouw Veldman was back.

Saskia worked through the afternoon and into the evening, seeing patients, filling prescriptions, grinding herbs. Clara helped, wrapping packages and taking payments.

When the last customer left, Saskia collapsed into a chair, exhausted but happy.

"I missed this," she said.

"You missed the smell of sick people?" Clara asked, grinning.

"I missed helping people. I missed solving puzzles. I missed being useful."

"You were useful on the island. You saved a man's life."

"I did." Saskia smiled. "But there are more puzzles to solve. The Silent Flower has secrets I have not yet uncovered. Its poison can kill, yes. But its root can cure. I am certain of it."

She looked around the shop, at the jars and bottles and Bunsen burners.

"I am going to find a use for that flower," she said. "Something that helps people, not kills them."

---

Chapter 35: The Letter from Batavia

Six months after Van Ness's death, Saskia received a letter from Batavia.

It was from Rudi, who had returned to the East Indies after the voyage. He wrote in careful, formal Dutch, his words carrying the weight of a man who had seen much and said little.

"Mevrouw Veldman," the letter began. "I write to you with news of the island."

Saskia read on, her heart quickening.

"Van Ness's death has left the Emerald Isle unguarded. The Company has not yet claimed it, and the native people who lived there—Van Ness drove them away years ago—have begun to return. They are rebuilding their village on the far side of the island, away from the cliffs where the Silent Flower grows."

"I have spoken with their elders. They remember the old stories, the ones my grandmother told me. The Silent Flower was not always a poison. It was used by the island's healers to treat fevers and wounds. They knew how to prepare it safely."

"But Van Ness learned only the poison. He never learned the cure."

Rudi ended the letter with an offer: if Saskia wanted to return to the island, he would arrange transportation. The elders would welcome her. They would teach her what they knew.

Saskia read the letter three times. Then she folded it carefully and tucked it into her pocket.

She was not ready to return to the island. Not yet. But the invitation was a door that might one day open.

---

Chapter 36: The Testimony

The trial of the East India Company's conspiracy never happened in a courtroom.

The directors who had resigned avoided public scandal by agreeing to a private inquiry. Saskia was called to testify before a panel of magistrates, in a closed session held in the Company's boardroom.

She sat at a long table, facing twelve of the most powerful men in Amsterdam. The four resigned directors were not present, but their lawyers were, watching her with cold, appraising eyes.

She told them everything. The cordial. The poison. The island. The journal. The years of murders.

The magistrates asked questions. They wanted to know how she had identified the poison. How she had found the island. How she had obtained the evidence.

She answered each question patiently, carefully, laying out her methods and her reasoning.

When it was over, the chief magistrate looked at her for a long time.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said, "you have done what no physician in Amsterdam could do. You have uncovered a conspiracy that spanned two continents. You have saved a man's life and exposed a murderer."

He paused.

"The East India Company owes you a debt. The city of Amsterdam owes you a debt. And I, personally, owe you an apology."

"An apology?"

"I was one of the physicians who treated Heer van der Meer. I bled him. I purged him. I prescribed gold elixirs." The magistrate shook his head. "I was wrong. Your methods—your careful observation, your chemical analysis, your willingness to look beyond the accepted wisdom—were right."

Saskia did not know what to say.

"From now on," the magistrate continued, "when there is a case that baffles the physicians, I will send them to you. The apothecary on the Herengracht."

It was the highest praise Saskia had ever received.

---

Chapter 37: The New Shop

The case of Pieter van der Meer made Saskia famous.

Within weeks, her shop was crowded with patients from across Amsterdam. The wealthy came in carriages, the poor on foot. They came with fevers and coughs, with broken bones and failing hearts, with mysterious ailments that no physician could diagnose.

Saskia treated them all.

She hired Clara as her full-time assistant, and she took on two apprentices—young women who wanted to learn the apothecary's art. She expanded her workshop, adding new equipment: a distillation column, a chemical furnace, a collection of glass retorts from Venice.

She did not forget the Silent Flower.

In her spare hours, she continued her research. She extracted the active compounds, tested them on small animals, recorded her observations. She learned that the poison worked by accumulating in the liver, slowly destroying its ability to process toxins. The antidote—the root extract—worked by stimulating the liver to regenerate.

She wrote her findings in a leather-bound journal, illustrated with careful drawings of the plant and its effects.

Some day, she knew, this knowledge would save lives.

---

Chapter 38: The Visitor from the Company

A year after the trial, Saskia received an unexpected visitor.

The man who arrived at her shop was young, perhaps thirty, dressed in the plain black coat of a Company clerk. He carried a leather case and wore an expression of nervous determination.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said, bowing. "My name is Pieter van der Meer's nephew. I am his heir."

Saskia looked at him with surprise. "How is your uncle?"

"He is well. Fully recovered. He sends his regards." The young man hesitated. "He also sends this."

He opened his case and pulled out a document, sealed with the Company's crest.

"What is it?"

"The deed to the Emerald Isle. My uncle purchased it from the Company. It is yours."

Saskia stared at the document. The island. The white cliffs. The Silent Flower. All of it, hers.

"I cannot accept this," she said.

"You can, and you must. My uncle is clear: without you, he would be dead. The island is nothing compared to the life you gave him."

Saskia took the deed. It felt heavy in her hands.

"What am I supposed to do with an island?"

"That is for you to decide, Mevrouw Veldman. But my uncle suggests that you might want to return to it. To study the plant. To learn from the people who lived there before Van Ness."

Saskia thought of Rudi's letter. The elders. The old knowledge.

"Perhaps I will," she said.

---

Chapter 39: The Letter from Clara

The next spring, Saskia received another letter.

This one was from Clara, who had returned to the island with Rudi and two of Saskia's apprentices. They had established a small settlement on the far side, working with the native elders to study the Silent Flower.

"Saskia," the letter read. "You would not recognize the island. The people have returned. They have rebuilt their village. They have taught us things that Van Ness never knew."

"The Silent Flower is not a poison. Not truly. The poison comes from the petals and the leaves. But the root—the root is a cure. It can heal the liver. It can reverse the damage that the petal poison causes."

"We have prepared several remedies from the root. They work. We have treated fevers, wounds, and a disease that the natives call 'the yellow death'—a wasting illness that matches Van der Meer's symptoms."

"I wish you were here. But I know you have your work in Amsterdam. So I send this letter to tell you: we are doing what you asked. We are learning. We are helping."

"And the island is waiting for you, when you are ready to return."

Saskia folded the letter and placed it beside the deed on her desk.

She looked around her shop. The jars, the scales, the mortar and pestle. The patients waiting in the front room. The work she had built over so many years.

One day, she would return to the island. But not yet.

---

Chapter 40: The Fifth Latitude

Years passed.

Saskia grew older, her hair streaked with gray, her hands steady as ever. She trained more apprentices. She published her research. She became known as the greatest apothecary in the Dutch Republic.

But she never forgot the island.

On the wall of her workshop, she hung a framed chart—the original map of the Emerald Isle, stolen from Van Ness's cabin. At the bottom, in her own hand, she had written the coordinates:

4°58'S — 72°15'E

The fifth latitude. The secret that a murderer had tried to erase.

She looked at it every day. It reminded her of what she had done. It reminded her of what she still had to do.

The Silent Flower was still out there, waiting to be understood. The island was still waiting for her return.

And one day, when the shop could spare her, she would go back.

She would stand on the white cliffs again. She would gather the flowers. She would listen to the wind in the palms.

She would finish the work she had begun.

---

ARC V: THE LEGACY

---

Chapter 41: The Fever Ship

In the summer of 1663, a ship arrived in Amsterdam carrying a cargo more terrible than any spice.

The crew of the Vrouw Maria had been struck down by a fever that burned through their bodies like fire through dry timber. By the time the ship reached the harbor, half the crew was dead, and the survivors were too weak to lower the sails.

The authorities quarantined the ship. They called for physicians. The physicians called for Saskia.

She stood on the dock, looking at the silent vessel. The air around it smelled of sickness and death.

"What is it?" Clara asked. She had returned from the island the previous year, driven by homesickness and a desire to see her sister.

"I don't know yet," Saskia said. "But I will find out."

She boarded the ship wearing a mask of herbs and vinegar, the same protection she used when treating plague patients. The deck was littered with bodies—men who had died where they fell, too weak to move.

She found the ship's surgeon in his cabin, barely conscious. He was a young man, perhaps twenty-five, with red hair and a face covered in sweat.

"The fever," he whispered. "It came on us three days out from Java. One by one. Burning up from the inside."

"What are the symptoms?"

"Fever. Vomiting. Yellow skin. Blood in the stool." The surgeon coughed, a wet, rattling sound. "I have never seen anything like it."

Saskia recognized the symptoms. She had seen them before—in Van der Meer, in the early stages of the Silent Flower poisoning.

But this was different. This was faster, more aggressive. The Silent Flower took months to kill. This fever killed in days.

She took samples of the sick men's blood. She examined them under her lens, looking for clues.

And she found something she had never seen before: tiny organisms, swimming in the blood, too small to be visible to the naked eye but clear under magnification.

The answer came to her in a flash of insight.

"The fever is carried by a parasite," she said. "It comes from the island."

"The Emerald Isle?"

"Somewhere in the same waters. A different island, perhaps. Or the same one. The Silent Flower is not the only secret those waters hold."

She spent the next week working around the clock, developing a treatment. She used the root extract from the Silent Flower, combined with quinine and a compound of her own invention. She treated the surviving sailors. She watched them slowly recover.

By the end of the second week, all of them were alive.

Amsterdam called her a miracle worker.

Saskia knew better. She was not a miracle worker. She was a scientist, and she had only just begun to understand the secrets of the eastern seas.

---

Chapter 42: The Expedition

The fever from the Vrouw Maria changed everything.

The East India Company, shaken by the loss of so many ships to mysterious illnesses, commissioned Saskia to lead an expedition. Their goal: chart the islands of the eastern seas, document the plants and their properties, and find cures for the diseases that plagued their sailors.

Saskia accepted without hesitation.

She assembled a team: Clara, Rudi, two of her apprentices, and a company of sailors under the command of Captain de Vries. They would sail on a new ship, the Onderzoeker—the Investigator—built specifically for exploration.

They left Amsterdam in the spring of 1664, carrying enough supplies for two years.

The voyage took them south, around the Cape of Good Hope, across the Indian Ocean. They stopped at the Emerald Isle first, where Saskia spent a month working with the native elders, learning their healing traditions.

She discovered that the Silent Flower had dozens of uses. The root treated fevers and liver ailments. The petals, when dried and ground, could be used to treat wounds. The sap, mixed with coconut oil, was a remedy for skin diseases.

And the poison—the deadly compound that Van Ness had used—could be neutralized by a simple preparation of lime juice and seawater.

The elders called the flower "Bunga Sunyi," the Silent Flower. But they also called it something else: "Bunga Dua Wajah." The Two-Faced Flower.

"Poison and cure," the head elder told Saskia. "They are the same thing, Mevrouw Veldman. It is only the dose that differs."

---

Chapter 43: The Archipelago

From the Emerald Isle, the Onderzoeker sailed east, into waters that no Dutch ship had ever charted.

They discovered islands that appeared on no map. Islands with volcanoes and white sand beaches, with birds that could not fly and trees that bore fruit the size of a man's head. Islands where the natives had never seen a European ship.

Saskia documented everything. She collected plants, pressed them, labeled them. She recorded the native names and the native uses. She filled journal after journal with observations.

On one island, she found a tree whose bark could cure the bloody flux. On another, a vine whose sap numbed pain as effectively as opium. On a third, a flower whose petals, when brewed as tea, could stop a fever in hours.

She sent samples back to Amsterdam with every passing ship. The physicians who had once dismissed her now wrote letters begging for more.

Clara, meanwhile, had found her own calling. She had learned to navigate by the stars, and she was charting the islands with a precision that impressed even Captain de Vries. She had a gift for languages, too—she learned to communicate with the native peoples they encountered, learning their words and their customs.

"Saskia," Clara said one evening, as they sat on the deck watching the sun set over an unknown sea, "I think I was meant for this life."

"The life of an explorer?"

"The life of someone who is always learning. I never knew, back in Amsterdam, how much there was to discover."

Saskia smiled. "Neither did I."

---

Chapter 44: The Lost Chart

On the island of Timor, Saskia found something unexpected.

She was visiting a Portuguese mission, looking for medicinal plants, when a priest named Father Antonio showed her his collection of old maps. Among them was a chart that made her breath catch.

It showed the Emerald Isle. Not at the coordinates she knew, but at a different location entirely—further south, further east. The chart was dated 1526, more than a century before Van Ness had erased the island.

"Where did you get this?" Saskia asked.

"It was given to me by a Javanese merchant," Father Antonio said. "He said it was a copy of a Portuguese chart from before the Dutch came. Before the Company."

Saskia examined the chart closely. The Emerald Isle was shown at 6°12'S, 74°48'E. Significantly different from the coordinates on Van Ness's chart.

"Could there be two islands?" she asked. "Two different Emerald Isles?"

Father Antonio shrugged. "The sea is full of islands, Mevrouw Veldman. The Portuguese found many that the Dutch never charted. They kept the locations secret, just as your Company does."

Saskia thought about this. If there were two islands, both with the Silent Flower, then the plant was more widespread than she had thought. And if the plant was more widespread, then the poison was more widespread, too.

"The old missionaries," she said. "Did they know about the Silent Flower? Did they use it?"

Father Antonio's face grew serious. "I have heard stories. The natives on some islands use a flower that can kill slowly, over months. They call it the Judgment Flower. They use it in their justice system—convicted criminals are given a small dose, and if they survive, they are forgiven."

"The same plant."

"It may be. But the natives do not share their knowledge with outsiders. They have learned that Europeans use such knowledge for harm."

Saskia nodded slowly. She understood. She had seen what Van Ness did with the Silent Flower. She could not blame the natives for their distrust.

"We need to find this other island," she said. "We need to know how widespread the flower really is."

---

Chapter 45: The Second Island

The search for the second Emerald Isle took six months.

They sailed east, past the Moluccas, past the islands of the Arafura Sea. They talked to traders and fishermen, showed them drawings of the Silent Flower. Some recognized it. Some did not.

And then, on a stormy night in December, they found it.

The island was smaller than the first, ringed by a coral reef that made approach treacherous. But the cliffs were the same—white limestone, rising sheer from the sea. And the flowers were the same: white blossoms with black centers, covering the plateau in a carpet of silent beauty.

Saskia landed with a small party. The island appeared uninhabited—no villages, no trails, no signs of human presence. But the flowers grew in abundance.

"This is different," Saskia said, examining a sample. "The leaves are larger. The petals have a slightly different shape."

"A different variety," Clara suggested.

"Or a different species entirely." Saskia collected specimens, careful not to touch the petals with her bare hands. "We need to test it. Compare it to the samples from the first island."

They spent three days on the island, documenting the plants, the birds, the geology. They found no evidence of human habitation, but they found ruins—stone foundations, overgrown with vines, that suggested a village had once stood there.

"Van Ness," Saskia said. "He must have found this island, too. He must have wiped it from the charts, just like the other one."

"But why hide two islands?"

"Because he wanted to control the supply. If only one island was known, someone else might find it. But if two islands were hidden, and only he knew about both..."

"He could harvest from one and keep the other secret, in case the first was discovered."

Saskia nodded. "He was planning for the long term. He was building an empire, one murder at a time."

---

Chapter 46: The Healer's Return

They returned to the Emerald Isle in the spring of 1665.

The village had grown. More families had returned, rebuilding the homes that Van Ness had destroyed. They had planted gardens, built a school, established a market. The island was coming back to life.

The head elder greeted Saskia with a warm embrace. "Welcome home, Mevrouw Veldman."

"It is good to be back," Saskia said.

She showed him the samples from the second island. The elder examined them carefully, his old hands gentle.

"This is the same flower," he said. "But different. Stronger. The poison from this one would be more powerful. The cure would be more powerful, too."

"What do you mean, stronger?"

"The Silent Flower adapts to its home. Each island makes it a little different. The flower on this island has a gentle poison—it takes months to kill. The flower on the other island... it would take weeks."

Saskia felt a chill. "Van Ness knew this?"

"Van Ness knew many things. But he did not know everything. He did not know the cure. He did not know the root medicine." The elder smiled. "You know more than Van Ness now, Mevrouw Veldman."

Saskia spent the next months on the island, learning everything the elders could teach her. She learned the old songs, the healing rituals, the methods of preparation. She learned to see the plant not as a poison or a cure, but as both—a two-faced flower that gave life and death in equal measure.

She also learned that the flower could be cultivated. The elders showed her how to plant the seeds, how to care for the young plants, how to harvest without damaging the root. She established a small plantation on the far side of the island, away from the wild growth.

"This is the future," she told Clara. "Not just the poison, but the cure. We can help so many people."

"But we have to be careful," Clara said. "The wrong person could use this knowledge for harm."

"Which is why we will guard it. The island, the flower, the knowledge—all of it. We will teach only those we trust."

---

Chapter 47: The Return to Amsterdam

Saskia returned to Amsterdam in the autumn of 1666.

She had been gone for two and a half years. The city had changed—new buildings, new faces, new fashions. But her shop on the Herengracht was the same, dusted and waiting, kept ready by the apprentices she had left behind.

She walked through the familiar rooms, touching the jars, the scales, the mortar and pestle. The smell of camphor and lavender welcomed her home.

"I missed this place," she said.

"Then stay," Clara said. "You have done enough traveling. Let the island be what it is. Stay here, where you belong."

Saskia considered this. She was no longer young. Her hair was more gray than brown, her hands marked with the aches of years. The cold Amsterdam winters were harder to bear than they used to be.

But the island was calling her.

"I cannot stay," she said. "There is too much work to do. The Silent Flower—the Two-Faced Flower—it has secrets I have not yet uncovered. And there may be other islands, other plants, other cures waiting to be found."

Clara sighed. "I knew you would say that."

"Come with me."

"I have already seen enough of the world. I want to stay in Amsterdam. I want to run this shop, to help the people who come here, to live a normal life."

Saskia embraced her sister. "Then you will be the best apothecary in Amsterdam."

"I learned from the best."

---

Chapter 48: The Governor's Request

A week after Saskia's return, she received a summons from the new Governor-General of the East India Company.

The Governor-General was a man named Johan van Hoorn, a veteran of the eastern trade who had risen through the ranks by intelligence and ruthlessness. He was younger than Saskia had expected, with sharp eyes and a quick smile.

"Mevrouw Veldman," he said, "I have read your reports. I have seen the samples you sent. I am impressed."

"Thank you, Governor-General."

"I am also concerned." He leaned forward. "The Silent Flower—the Two-Faced Flower—it is a threat. Not just to individuals, but to the Company. If this poison becomes known, if it falls into the wrong hands..."

"It already has. Van Ness used it for years."

"And you stopped him. But there may be others. Others who know about the flower. Others who would use it as Van Ness did."

Saskia nodded slowly. "There may be. The island system is vast. There could be more islands with the flower that I have not found."

"That is what I want to discuss." Van Hoorn opened a drawer and pulled out a map—a chart of the eastern seas, marked with notes in his own hand. "I am establishing a new division of the Company. The Medical Survey. Its purpose is to find and document the medicinal plants of the Indies."

"And you want me to lead it?"

"I want you to advise it. You are the only person in Europe who truly understands the Silent Flower. You know the islands. You know the people. You know the dangers."

Saskia looked at the map. The eastern seas spread before her, dotted with islands she had visited and islands she had not. The work was endless. The secrets were infinite.

"I will help," she said. "But on my terms. The knowledge of the Silent Flower must be controlled. The cure must be shared. The poison must be guarded."

Van Hoorn smiled. "I would not have it any other way."

---

Chapter 49: The Last Voyage

In the spring of 1667, Saskia sailed east one last time.

She was accompanied by a small team: two botanists, a cartographer, and a company of soldiers to protect them. The Governor-General had been generous—the Onderzoeker had been refitted and was ready for another voyage.

But Saskia knew this would be her last. Her body was tired. The sea was no longer a home.

They stopped first at the Emerald Isle, where Saskia spent a month with the elders, teaching them what she had learned, learning from them in return. She planted new gardens, built a dispensary, trained a new generation of healers.

From there, they sailed south, searching for the third island—the one that Father Antonio's map had suggested might exist.

They found it after three weeks of searching.

It was smaller than the other two, a crescent of white sand and green forest surrounded by turquoise water. The Silent Flower grew here, too, but in a different form—a bush rather than a tree, with smaller blossoms and darker leaves.

Saskia collected samples. She documented the differences. She established a small outpost, leaving two of her botanists to study the island further.

And then she sailed home.

---

Chapter 50: The Apothecary's Latitude

Saskia Veldman died on a spring morning in 1678, at the age of forty-seven.

The cause was not poison, or fever, or the sea. It was a seizure, sudden and violent, that took her while she was working in her shop on the Herengracht. She was grinding dried valerian root when her heart stopped, and she fell forward onto her worktable, scattering herbs across the floor.

Clara found her an hour later.

They buried Saskia in the Oude Kerk, the old church on the Oudezijds Voorburgwal. The funeral was attended by merchants and magistrates, by physicians and healers, by the poor who remembered Saskia's kindness.

Pieter van der Meer was there, old and frail but alive—a living testament to Saskia's skill. Dr. van der Weyden was there, leaning on a cane, his eyes wet with tears. The Governor-General was there, along with half the directors of the East India Company.

But Saskia's true monument was not in the church. It was in a cabinet in her workshop, locked and guarded: a leather journal containing everything she had learned about the Silent Flower. The islands. The poison. The cure. The coordinates.

In her will, Saskia left the journal to Clara, with instructions: "Guard this knowledge. Share it only with those who will use it for healing. Keep the latitude secret, but keep the cure alive."

Clara honored her sister's wishes for fifty years. She trained apprentices, established pharmacies, and corresponded with healers across the world. The knowledge of the Silent Flower spread slowly, carefully, responsibly.

And the island—the Emerald Isle, the Two-Faced Flower's home—remained where it had always been, at a latitude known only to a few.

4°58'S.

The apothecary's latitude.

The secret that a murderer had tried to erase, and a healer had preserved.

The flower still bloomed on the white cliffs, silent and patient, waiting for those who understood its nature.

Poison and cure. Death and life. The same thing, always, depending only on the dose.

And Saskia's work continued, long after she was gone.

THE END

Author's Note: The Silent Flower (Bunga Sunyi) is a fictional plant, but the Dutch East India Company's practice of erasing islands from charts to control valuable resources is well-documented. The medical knowledge described in this novel—including the principle that toxins at different doses become cures—reflects real advances in early modern pharmacology.
