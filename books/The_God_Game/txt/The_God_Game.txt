Chapter 1: The Amphora

The junior librarian's name was Domenico, and he had worked in the Vatican Apostolic Library for eleven months. His job was cataloguing — matching accession numbers to shelf locations in the Index Tower, a windowless corridor of steel shelving that held the least-requested holdings of the Church. The tower was a maze of identical aisles, each lined with cabinets full of documents no one had touched in living memory. That morning Domenico noticed a discrepancy in the catalogue. The map showed a room that did not exist on any floor plan. He traced the discrepancy to a wall at the far end of the third sub-basement. He found a door he had never seen. It was low, arched, sealed with wax impressed by a signet ring bearing the Chi-Rho monogram. The wax was dark red, brittle, flaking at the edges. Domenico stared at it for a full minute before fetching a crowbar from the maintenance closet. The wax cracked like old bone. Beyond the door, a stairwell descended into dry, breathless dark. The steps were stone, worn concave by centuries of use. At the bottom, on a stone shelf, sat an amphora sealed with lead. The clay was pale red, coated in dust so fine it seemed painted on. Domenico carried it upstairs with both hands, sweating despite the cold. He set it on his desk and called his supervisor. He did not know he had just ended his career and started something far older.

Chapter 2: The Summons

Father Matteo Lombardi was translating a sixth-century Syriac homily on the nature of the Trinity when his phone vibrated. The screen read: Prefect's Office. Immediate. He set down his magnifying loupe and pressed his palms flat against the manuscript table, feeling the cool wood against his skin. The Gregorian University library was quiet. A student slept over her laptop in the corner, her cheek pressed against a textbook. Matteo had been a Jesuit for twenty-two years, a professor of early Christian history for seventeen. He knew what immediate meant. It meant a visiting dignitary needed a tour. It meant a funding meeting. It meant someone had died. He walked the marble corridors at a measured pace — not fast enough to seem anxious, not slow enough to seem dismissive. His footsteps echoed in the high-ceilinged hallways. The Prefect's secretary, a nervous young priest from Milan with a persistent stammer, waved him past without eye contact. The door was open. Inside, the Prefect stood beside a table where a clay amphora rested on a felt mat. The window behind him framed the dome of St. Peter's. "Father Matteo," the Prefect said. His voice was controlled, careful. "I need you to tell me what we have found." He did not offer Matteo a seat.

Chapter 3: The Seal

Matteo did not touch the amphora immediately. He circled the table, hands clasped behind his back, studying the artifact from every angle as he had been trained to do. The clay was pale red, baked hard, coated in a fine layer of pale dust. The lead seal was intact — a disk the size of a coin, stamped with a Chi-Rho monogram and the letters CON. He leaned closer. The stamping was uneven, the work of a craftsman in a hurry. "Constantinus," Matteo said. "Constantine." The Prefect nodded. "The seal of the First Council of Nicaea, 325 AD. The same seal they used on documents deemed heretical." Matteo's throat tightened. He had seen drawings of such seals in textbooks, but never an actual example. The weight of history pressed against him. "You know what this means." "I know what it might mean," the Prefect replied. "That is why I called you." Matteo examined the amphora's base, turning it carefully with gloved hands. A single line of Greek had been scratched into the wet clay before firing: AGIOIS MARTYSIN — For the Holy Witnesses. He felt a pull in his chest, the same pull he had felt as a novice when he first touched a Dead Sea Scroll fragment. "I will need an epigrapher," Matteo said. "And a conservator. The best we can get." "Already arranged," the Prefect said. "Professor Hale arrives this evening. You will work together in the restoration lab. No one else is to know. Not the director. Not the cardinal. No one."

Chapter 4: Professor Hale

Evelyn Hale had not set foot in Vatican City in fourteen years. She remembered the last time: a conference on forgeries of the apostolic era, where she had called a cardinal's prized relic "a very skilled eighth-century fantasy." The room had gone silent. She had not been invited back. The flight from London had given her time to read the dossier — thin, deliberately vague, heavy on redaction. A sealed amphora. Possible first-century contents. Urgent authentication. She took a taxi from Fiumicino through the rain-slicked streets of Rome, watching the dome of St. Peter's grow against the orange sky. The Tiber was swollen, muddy, lapping at the embankment walls. At the Vatican gate, a Swiss Guard checked her passport against a list and waved her through without a word. His face was neutral, professional, giving nothing away. The restoration lab was in the basement of the library, a clean room of white tiles and adjustable lighting. Stainless steel counters. Dehumidifiers humming in the corners. Father Matteo was waiting. He was taller than she expected, with wire-rimmed glasses and hands stained with ink at the fingertips. He looked like he had not slept well in days. "Professor Hale," he said. "Thank you for coming." "The dossier was cryptic," she replied. "Show me what you have." She did not shake his hand.

Chapter 5: The Unrolling

The amphora was opened in a positive-pressure clean room designed to prevent contamination. The conservator, a woman named Silvia with steady hands and cropped gray hair, used a diamond saw to cut through the lead seal. The blade whined, then bit. The seal split with a clean crack. A thin gas escaped — the smell of dust and resin and something metallic, like old blood held in a copper bowl. Inside, wrapped in linen darkened by age to the color of tea, lay a scroll. Silvia lifted it with padded forceps and placed it on a sheet of acid-free paper. The linen was brittle, cracking at the folds. She worked the fibers apart with tweezers, millimeter by millimeter. The unrolling took four hours. Silvia worked steadily, humidifying the papyrus with a fine mist, coaxing the ancient fibers apart. Matteo watched through the glass partition, his breath fogging the window. Hale stood beside him, arms crossed, not speaking. When the last fold was open, the scroll lay flat — three feet of papyrus covered in dense Aramaic script, the letters dark against the pale fiber. Hale leaned forward, her hand pressing against the glass. "That is not a standard biblical hand," she said. "It is personal, cursive. Someone wrote this quickly, in private, perhaps in fear." Matteo felt the hairs on his neck rise. "Can you read it?" "I can read the first line," Hale said. She paused. "It says: 'I, Judas Iscariot, called the Twin, write this in the fifteenth year after the crucifixion of my teacher.'"

Chapter 6: Judas

Matteo did not sleep that night. He sat in the small office adjacent to the lab, a photocopy of the scroll's first column spread across the desk under a cone of yellow light. The letters were Aramaic, the dialect of first-century Judea. He had studied the language for thirty years, had memorized its grammar, its idioms, its quirks. He knew it the way a pianist knows the keyboard, without thinking. But the words did not make sense. The text claimed to be a firsthand account of the crucifixion — not from the perspective of a follower hiding in the shadows, but from the man who betrayed Jesus for thirty pieces of silver. The Judas gospel had been written about. Fragments existed, scraps of Coptic papyrus found in Egyptian caves. But a complete scroll, in Judas's own hand, matched against known examples of first-century Aramaic script — it would be the most significant biblical discovery since Nag Hammadi. Matteo read the opening lines again. The voice was raw, unpolished, the grammar sometimes loose. Judas did not defend himself. He did not apologize. He simply described: the arrest in the garden, the kiss, the trial before Caiaphas, the walk to Golgotha. Then a detail that made Matteo stop. The crucifixion did not kill Jesus. The Romans took him down alive. Matteo read the line five times, each time hoping he had mistranslated. Then he closed the folder and sat in the dark until dawn, staring at nothing.

Chapter 7: The Watcher

The Custodian sat in a room without windows. It was located in the Palazzo del Sant'Uffizio, the headquarters of the Dicastery for the Doctrine of the Faith, in a sub-basement that did not appear on any floor plan or security registry. The walls were concrete, painted gray. The air was recycled, cold, sterile. He was a thin man with silver hair combed flat against his skull, wearing the black cassock of a monsignor, a silver crucifix at his throat. On his desk, a tablet displayed a live feed from the restoration lab camera. The image was grainy, the angle fixed, but it showed everything. He watched Professor Hale lean over the scroll, her finger tracing the Aramaic script. He watched Father Matteo rub his eyes, exhaustion visible in every line of his body. He had known about the amphora for three years, ever since a routine structural survey of the Index Tower had revealed the sealed door. He had chosen not to open it. He had chosen to forget it, to let it remain buried, to hope that the concrete and the dark would keep the secret forever. But the junior librarian had found it, and now the chain of events was moving faster than he could control. He picked up a secure phone and dialed a number saved under no name. "We have an activation," he said. "Prepare the protocol." He hung up and watched the screen a moment longer. Domenico the librarian was being questioned in an office down the hall. He would be transferred to a parish in Sardinia by morning. Then the Custodian switched off the tablet and sat in the dark.

Chapter 8: The Dating

The carbon dating results arrived by encrypted email on the third day. Hale read them standing at the lab counter, a cup of cold coffee beside her, the screen glowing in the dim light. The papyrus dated to between 60 and 80 AD. The ink was carbon-based, consistent with first-century Roman manufacture. The scroll was authentic. She felt no triumph. She felt dread coiling in her stomach. An authentic first-century document claiming to be written by Judas Iscariot, describing a crucifixion that did not kill its victim — this was not a discovery. It was a bomb planted at the foundation of the Church. She carried the printout to the small office where Matteo was working. He looked up, saw her face, and set down his pen without a word. "Tell me," he said. "It is real," she said. "First-century. Genuine. The papyrus, the ink, the script. Everything checks out." Matteo closed his eyes, his lips moving silently. A prayer, perhaps. Or simply a breath. "God help us." "God had nothing to do with it," Hale said. "This is clay and ink and fiber. It is evidence. What we do with it is the only thing that matters now." She placed the report on his desk. He did not look at it. He was still staring at the translation in front of him, at the words of a dead man who had seen something the world was not meant to know.

Chapter 9: The Prefect

Matteo requested a private meeting with the Prefect. They met in the Prefect's office, a high-ceilinged room lined with portraits of popes stretching back four centuries. The gilt frames caught the afternoon light. The Prefect sat behind his desk, hands folded on the leather blotter, his face unreadable. Matteo placed the carbon dating report on the desk, smoothing the paper flat. "The scroll is authentic," he said. "First century. It claims to be written by Judas Iscariot. It describes an account of the crucifixion that contradicts the canonical gospels in material ways." The Prefect did not touch the report. He looked at it the way one looks at a snake that has entered the room. "I see." "You knew," Matteo said. "When you called me, you already knew what this was. You did not summon me to identify it. You summoned me to confirm your suspicion." The Prefect was silent for a long moment. "I suspected," he said finally. "The seal, the location, the condition of the amphora. It matched descriptions in certain archives I have access to." "What archives?" "Archives you do not have clearance to know about. Archives that do not officially exist." Matteo felt a cold anger rising in his chest. "I am a priest of this Church. I am a scholar. If there is something — " "There are things," the Prefect said quietly, "that the Church protects by not knowing them. By not having them known. This may be one of those things. And if it is, Father, you will wish you had never touched that amphora."

Chapter 10: The Chapel

The Custodian knelt in the private chapel of Cardinal Vincenzo Marchetti, the Camerlengo of the Holy Roman Church. The chapel was small, paneled in dark walnut, lit by a single candle on the marble altar. The flame threw shadows across the crucifix, making the figure of Christ seem to move. The cardinal sat in a chair behind the communion rail, his breviary open on his lap, a ribbon marking his place. "The scroll is authentic," the Custodian said, his voice barely above a whisper. "Carbon-dated. Aramaic. Written in a personal hand consistent with the period." The cardinal turned a page with deliberate slowness. "And the content?" "It claims Jesus survived the crucifixion. It describes a staged execution, a bribed centurion, a physician waiting in a house outside the city walls." "That is the tradition." "It claims he traveled to Rome with Mary Magdalene. That they married. That they had children." The cardinal stopped turning pages. His fingers rested on the vellum. "That is not the tradition." "No, Eminence." "And the genealogy?" "We have not located it yet. But the gospel contains geographical references that correspond to locations we have secured in the past." The cardinal closed his breviary with a soft thump. "You know what must be done." "I do." "Then do it. Cleanly. Without spectacle. Without names appearing in newspapers." The Custodian rose, bowed, and left the chapel. The candle guttered and went out, as if a draft had followed him through the door.

Chapter 11: The First Night

Matteo worked through the night, translating the gospel column by column. The script was dense, the grammar irregular — Judas wrote in a colloquial Aramaic, the language of fishermen and merchants, not of scholars or scribes. The sentences were short, declarative. He did not embellish. He did not theologize. The gospel opened with a prologue: I write this because the truth deserves a witness. Not the truth of the Sanhedrin. Not the truth of Rome. The truth of the garden, the kiss, the rope, and the tree. The narrative moved quickly, scene by scene, without commentary. Judas described the arrest in Gethsemane as a negotiation. The temple guards were paid. The Romans were compensated. The entire event had been stage-managed by a faction within the Sanhedrin who believed Jesus could be used as a political symbol if he survived. The crucifixion was real — the nails, the spear, the thirst, the long hours of exposure. But the soldiers had been instructed to take him down before death, to deliver him to a physician in a house outside the city walls. The physician was a woman. Her name was Salome. She had been trained in Alexandria. Matteo set down his pen. His hand was shaking. He looked at the clock on the wall: 3:47 AM. He had never felt more awake. He had never felt more afraid.

Chapter 12: The Garden

Judas described the garden where Jesus was taken after the crucifixion. It belonged to a Roman centurion named Lucius Gallio, a convert to the new faith, a man who had witnessed the execution and felt something shift in his chest. Gallio's villa stood on the Via Appia, south of Rome, and the text gave specific coordinates by reference to landmarks that still existed: a cypress grove, a stone well with a carved rim, a tomb cut into the hillside. Jesus was carried there on a litter, unconscious but alive, his breath shallow, his skin gray with blood loss. Salome, the physician, treated his wounds through the night. She removed the spear point from his side with forceps. She set his broken legs. She cleaned the gash across his scalp left by the crown of thorns. For three days he lay in a fever, drifting between consciousness and delirium. On the fourth day, he opened his eyes and spoke. The words Judas recorded were not divine pronouncements. They were practical instructions. Sell the property in Bethany. Move the community to Antioch. Never speak of what happened on the cross. Tell the story the people need to hear. Jesus was not resurrected on the third day. He was hidden. The resurrection was a narrative constructed by Peter and James to protect the movement from Roman persecution. Jesus had consented to the story. He understood that a living messiah was a legal vulnerability. A dead and risen messiah was a weapon that no empire could defeat.

Chapter 13: The Journey

After his recovery, Jesus did not remain in Judea. The political situation was too dangerous — Pilate would not tolerate a second spectacle, and the Sanhedrin had already spent their political capital on the first one. Judas recorded that Jesus traveled to Rome in the company of Mary Magdalene and a small retinue of trusted followers. They sailed from the port of Caesarea to Puteoli on a grain ship, a journey of twelve days. From Puteoli they walked the Via Appia north, a hundred and fifty miles through the Italian countryside. In Rome, they settled in the Trastevere district, among the Jewish community that had lived there since the time of Julius Caesar. Jesus took the name Yeshua ben Yosef, a common name shared with many others. He found work as a weaver. Mary Magdalene taught Greek to the children of Roman merchants. They married in a simple ceremony in a garden by the Tiber River, beneath a fig tree. The date: the twentieth day of Nisan, 37 AD. Judas was present. He witnessed the marriage. He kissed Jesus on the cheek and Mary on the forehead and left Rome the next morning, walking east, never to return. He wrote the gospel forty years later, in a cave near Damascus, knowing he was dying, knowing the Church was already becoming something Jesus had never intended. The gospel was his confession. His apology. His gift.

Chapter 14: The Fire

The fire alarm sounded at 2:14 AM. Matteo jerked awake at his desk, disoriented, the gospel translation still spread before him, his cheek numb from sleeping on his arm. The sprinklers did not activate — the fire was in the archival storage wing, where ancient documents were kept in low-oxygen environments designed to suppress flames. The system had not suppressed this flame. It had been bypassed. Smoke curled under the door, gray and acrid, carrying the smell of burning leather and paper. Matteo grabbed the scroll, still in its conservation frame, and stumbled into the corridor. The lights were out. Emergency strips glowed along the baseboards, casting faint blue light. He could see the fire at the far end of the hall — orange, hungry, eating through a row of manuscript cabinets. The heat hit his face like a wall. He ran the other way, the scroll pressed against his chest, his lungs burning. At the stairwell he collided with Hale. She was in her coat, a bag over her shoulder, her eyes wide. "Where is it?" she said. "I have it." "Give it to me." She took the scroll and wrapped it in her coat, holding it against her body. They descended the stairs together, past the second floor, past the first, into the basement parking garage. Hale had a rental car, a gray Fiat parked near the exit. She opened the trunk and placed the scroll inside, covering it with a plastic tarp. "Get in," she said. "We are leaving. Now."

Chapter 15: The Evacuation

They drove through the Vatican's Santa Anna Gate as the fire trucks arrived, their sirens wailing, their lights painting the walls red and blue. The Swiss Guard on duty was confused by the commotion — he waved them through without checking the vehicle, his attention fixed on the smoke billowing from the library roof. Hale drove south, away from the city center, past the Colosseum, past the Baths of Caracalla, toward the Via Appia Antica. The streets were empty at this hour. The streetlights cast pools of orange on the wet asphalt. Matteo watched the rearview mirror. No one followed. No headlights appeared behind them. "The fire was deliberate," he said. His voice was flat, hollow. "I know." "Someone wants the scroll destroyed." "I know." "We cannot go back. We cannot trust anyone." Hale glanced at him, her hands gripping the wheel. "I know, Matteo. I am an archaeologist. I have worked in countries where artifacts are worth more than lives. I have been shot at in Syria. I have been threatened in Egypt. I know how this works." She pulled over at a darkened gas station on the edge of the city and killed the engine. The silence rushed in. "We need to read the entire scroll and make a copy before they find us," she said. "After that, we decide what to do with it." "We could take it to the authorities." "The authorities started the fire," Hale said. "Think, Father. The library is the most protected building in Vatican City. A fire does not start in the archives by accident."

Chapter 16: The Decision

They sat in the car in the dark, the engine ticking as it cooled. The gas station was closed, its lights off, the pumps locked behind a metal grate. The only sound was the wind moving through the cypress trees along the road. "I have a colleague," Hale said finally. "Retired. Lives outside Rome in a village called Castel Gandolfo, on the lake. He has a library, a small lab, and no loyalty to the Vatican. He helped me with the Dead Sea Scrolls authentication in the nineties." "Can we trust him?" "He was my mentor. He taught me how to read Aramaic when I was twenty-two years old. If he is still alive, and if his mind is still sharp, we can trust him with our lives." Matteo thought about the Prefect's words: There are things the Church protects by not knowing them. He thought about the fire, the deliberate orange glow, the smoke that had nearly consumed the most important document ever found. "All right," he said. "Let us go see your mentor." Hale started the car and pulled back onto the road, heading east toward the hills. The sky was beginning to lighten in the distance, a pale gray band above the dark silhouette of the Alban Hills. Matteo turned and looked at the trunk where the scroll lay wrapped in Hale's coat. Two thousand years of secrets, written by a dead man in a cave, found by a junior librarian with a crowbar. And now three people knew about them. He wondered how long they would stay alive.

Chapter 17: The Refuge

Professor Aldo Vasari lived in a stone farmhouse on a hillside overlooking Lake Albano. The house was old, built in the seventeenth century, with walls three feet thick and a roof of terracotta tiles. The garden was overgrown with rosemary and lavender. He was eighty-one years old, bald, bearded, and irascible. He met them at the door in a stained dressing gown, a pipe clamped between his teeth, his eyes narrowed against the morning light. "Evelyn," he said, without warmth. "You look tired. And you have brought a priest." "Aldo, we need your help." "You always need my help. That is the only time you call. You never call to say hello, to ask how I am, to see if I am still breathing." He stepped aside and let them in. The house was cluttered with books, papers, pottery shards, and the smell of tobacco and old wood. Every surface was covered. Vasari led them to a back room that served as his study. A large oak table dominated the space, littered with academic journals and coffee cups. "Show me," he said. Hale unrolled the scroll across the table. Vasari stared at it for a long moment. He took off his glasses, wiped them on his dressing gown, put them back on. Then he sat down heavily in a chair, his knees cracking. "Where did you get this?" "Beneath the Vatican," Matteo said. "In a sealed vault that had not been opened since the Council of Nicaea." "I see." Vasari put his glasses back on and leaned over the scroll. "You have no idea what you have found. Or perhaps you do. That is why you are here, running, with smoke in your clothes." He looked at Hale. "They will kill you for this, Evelyn." "I know." "And you," Vasari said to Matteo. "They will burn you as a heretic. Metaphorically. These days they do it with excommunications and character assassinations. But the result is the same." "I know," Matteo said.

Chapter 18: The Translation

They worked through the day without pause. Vasari made coffee, strong and bitter, in a stainless steel moka pot. Hale transcribed the Aramaic into a notebook while Matteo translated into English, speaking the words aloud as he wrote. Vasari checked their work against his own references — a lexicon of Aramaic, a grammar of first-century dialects, a concordance of known Judas traditions. The gospel was longer than they had estimated. Nearly fifty columns of dense Aramaic script, covering events from the arrest in Gethsemane to the final years of Jesus in Rome. The tone was not devotional. It was reportorial. Clinical. Judas described conversations he had overheard between Jesus and his disciples, between Jesus and Pilate's agents, between Mary Magdalene and the women who followed her. There was no miracle at Cana in this account. There was no walking on water, no raising of Lazarus, no multiplication of loaves. Jesus was a teacher, a healer, a man of extraordinary charisma and political intelligence. He knew he was being used by the Sanhedrin. He played his role with precision. The crucifixion was a performance, staged for the masses, with a willing participant and a skilled physician standing by. The resurrection was a marketing campaign designed to consolidate the movement. The Church was a political organization, not a divine institution. The gospel did not attack faith. It exposed its machinery.

Chapter 19: The Genealogy

On the third column from the end, they found what they had been searching for. A list of names, forty-seven in total, written in a careful hand that stood apart from the rest of the text. The genealogy. Jesus and Mary had three children, according to Judas: a son named Yosef, a daughter named Sarah, and a second son named Mattai. The family line continued through Mattai, generation by generation, moving through Roman names, then Byzantine Greek names, then Italian names. The names that followed were familiar. They were names that appeared in the official histories of the papacy. The genealogy traced a direct bloodline from Jesus of Nazareth to the current occupant of the Chair of Saint Peter. Pope Clement XV — born Carlo Mancini, elected in 2013 — was a direct descendant of Jesus Christ. Hale stopped writing. Vasari set down his pipe. Matteo stared at the letters until they blurred, his vision swimming. "This cannot be right," he said. "It is what it says," Hale replied, her voice steady. "We need to verify the later names against papal records. But the early line is internally consistent. The names match known genealogical patterns." "If this is true," Vasari said, his voice barely a whisper, "then every pope for the last two thousand years has belonged to the same family. The Church is not the Bride of Christ. It is his bloodline. A monarchy disguised as a democracy."

Chapter 20: The Catacombs

The gospel contained references to specific locations in Rome — catacombs where the early community had buried their dead and hidden their documents. One reference stood out from the others. A chamber in the Catacombs of Priscilla, beneath the Via Salaria, marked with a fresco of a peacock with its tail spread in full display. "Peacocks symbolize immortality in early Christian art," Hale said, her finger tracing the reference. "They appear in funerary contexts throughout the second and third centuries. If there is a hidden chamber there, it may contain supporting material. Letters. Contracts. Evidence that corroborates the gospel." "We need to go," Matteo said. "Tonight." "Agreed," Vasari said. "The Custodians will have tracked the car by now. They know you left the city. They will be searching the satellite towns. You have perhaps twelve hours before they find this house." Matteo looked at the scroll, spread across the oak table. They had made digital photographs. They had made handwritten transcriptions. The scroll itself was still the original, the only physical evidence that could be authenticated. "We should bring it with us," he said. "No," Vasari said. "Too risky. If they catch you with it, everything is lost. Leave it here. I will hide it. If I am not here when you return, it will be in a place you will find." He wrote an address on a scrap of paper and handed it to Hale. "This is the entrance to the catacombs. Go now. Take only what you can carry."

Chapter 21: The Search

The Swiss Guard captain, a man named Rolf Baumann, stood in the burned wing of the Vatican library. The fire had been contained, but the damage was extensive. Smoke stains climbed the walls to the ceiling. The cabinets that had burned contained documents from the eighteenth century — valuable, but not irreplaceable. The fire had served its purpose as cover for what had been taken. Baumann's phone buzzed. He stepped into the corridor and answered. The Custodian's voice was flat, emotionless. "They left through the Santa Anna Gate at 2:32 AM. The security cameras tracked the car heading south on the Via Appia. We lost them near Castel Gandolfo." "Search the area," the Custodian said. "House by house if necessary. Find them. Bring me the scroll." "And the priest and the woman?" "Silence them. Use the lake. Make it look like an accident." Baumann ended the call and stood for a moment, staring at the smoke stains. He was a soldier, not a theologian. He had taken an oath to protect the Church, not to question it. He selected four men from his unit, men he trusted, men who understood that some orders were not written down. They drove south in an unmarked van, weapons concealed in a compartment beneath the floor, their faces hidden behind sunglasses and the visors of their caps.

Chapter 22: The Entrance

The Catacombs of Priscilla were closed to the public at sunset. Matteo and Hale approached through a side gate, using the key Vasari had given them — a heavy iron key that looked older than the lock it fit. The gate opened onto a staircase cut into the tufa stone, descending into absolute darkness. The air changed as they went down, growing cooler, damper, stiller. Hale had brought flashlights, high-powered LED models she used on digs. The beams cut through the dust-laden air, illuminating the narrow passageways lined with burial niches. Bones here and there, visible through broken seals. A skull catching the light. They walked for ten minutes, following the map Vasari had drawn from the gospel's geographical references, counting intersections, watching for the markers described in the text. The walls changed from rough-cut tufa to plastered surfaces painted with faded frescoes. A good shepherd with a lamb across his shoulders. A fish, the ichthys symbol. A vine heavy with grapes. And then a peacock, its feathers rendered in blues and greens that had survived the centuries surprisingly well, its tail spread in a full circular fan. "Here," Hale said. She ran her fingers along the edge of the fresco, feeling for irregularities. The plaster was loose at the bottom. She pressed, and a section of the wall gave way, pivoting on a hidden hinge with a sound like a breath. Beyond it lay a small chamber, no larger than a confessional, the air inside dry and still. On a stone shelf, resting as if placed there yesterday, sat a wooden box.

Chapter 23: The Second Scroll

The box was cedar, warped by two millennia of damp air but still intact, held together by brass corner plates that had oxidized to a soft green. The lid was not sealed — it rested loosely, held in place by gravity and the fit of the wood. Matteo lifted it carefully. Inside, wrapped in linen treated with beeswax that had hardened to a pale yellow shell, lay a second scroll. This one was shorter than the Judas gospel, perhaps two feet long, written in a different hand — a formal Greek uncial, the precise script of Roman bureaucrats and notaries. Hale unrolled it on the stone shelf, working the stiff linen aside. The text was a legal document. A marriage contract. The groom: Yeshua ben Yosef, of Nazareth, by trade a weaver. The bride: Miriam of Magdala, by trade a teacher. The witnesses: Lucius Gallio, centurion of the Second Legion; Claudia Procula, wife of Pontius Pilate, praefectus of Judea; and Judas Iscariot, called the Twin, of Kerioth. The date: the twentieth day of Nisan, in the consulship of Gaius Caligula and Gnaeus Sentius Saturninus — 37 AD. "This is evidence," Hale whispered, her voice reverent. "Not a gospel. A legal document. A marriage contract signed by Pilate's wife." "If this is real," Matteo said, his throat dry, "it corroborates everything. The journey to Rome. The marriage. The children." "It is real," Hale said. She pointed to the bottom of the scroll, where a seal in red wax, cracked but legible, bore the insignia of the Roman Senate. "This was registered. This is a certified copy of a marriage contract filed in the Roman archives. This is not a religious document. It is a legal one."

Chapter 24: The Footsteps

They heard the footsteps at the same moment. Both froze. A scrape of boot leather on stone, coming from the main corridor. A cough. A voice speaking German. Hale switched off her flashlight. The darkness was absolute, complete. Matteo pressed himself against the wall of the small chamber, his heart pounding so hard he was certain it was audible. The footsteps grew closer. Baumann's voice echoed, amplified by the stone corridors. He was speaking into a radio, reporting that the catacomb entrance showed signs of recent disturbance. The beam of a powerful flashlight swept past the hidden entrance. It stopped. Swept back. Matteo held his breath. The beam lingered on the peacock fresco. Baumann stepped closer. They could see his shadow moving across the light, the shape of his shoulders, the outline of a weapon holstered at his side. His flashlight illuminated the plaster wall, the faded colors, the cracks. He was looking at the peacock, studying it. But he did not see the hidden seam. He grunted, turned, and continued down the main corridor, his footsteps receding. Neither Matteo nor Hale moved for a full minute. Then Hale exhaled, a long shaky breath. "We need to leave. Now." Matteo wrapped the marriage scroll in his jacket, pressing it against his chest. They slipped out of the chamber, pushed the wall back into place, and moved in the opposite direction from Baumann, deeper into the catacombs, toward a second exit Vasari had mentioned — a service staircase that led to a convent garden above.

Chapter 25: The Narrow Passage

The second exit was a maintenance shaft, barely wide enough for a man of average build to pass. The staircase was spiral, cut into the living rock, the steps worn to shallow curves by centuries of use. There was no handrail. The drop into the darkness below was vertiginous. Hale went first, the marriage scroll in her pack, her flashlight held between her teeth. Matteo followed, the gospel translation folded in his inner pocket, his hands pressed flat against the damp stone walls. They climbed in silence. The air grew warmer as they ascended. The stone steps gave way to iron rungs set into the wall, rusted but solid. Then to a ladder bolted to the stone, its rungs slick with condensation. Above them, a metal grate. Hale pushed against it with her shoulder. The grate resisted, held by years of rust and disuse. She pushed harder, putting her full weight into it. It shifted with a screech of metal against stone that seemed impossibly loud. Fresh air flooded the shaft, cold and sweet. She hauled herself through and reached back for Matteo, her hand gripping his wrist. They emerged in a small walled garden, overgrown with rosemary and wild thyme, the plants brushing against their legs. A convent building stood to their left, dark and silent, its windows unlit. No alarms. No guards. They crossed the garden, their feet silent on the flagstones, and found a gate that opened onto a narrow street. Rome spread below them, a sea of orange lights against the dark hills, the dome of St. Peter's a silhouette against the stars.

Chapter 26: The Hunt

Baumann emerged from the catacombs empty-handed. He stood at the entrance, breathing hard, his flashlight still on. He reported to the Custodian by secure line, his voice tight with frustration. "They were here. They found something. A concealed chamber behind a fresco. It was empty when I reached it." "What did they take?" "I do not know. But they escaped through a service exit into a convent garden above." "Which convent?" "Santa Maria della Grazie." "I know it. The abbess is a friend of the Church. She will tell us if they sought shelter there. But they will not have done that. They are too smart." "What are your orders?" "Continue the pursuit. They will try to leave Rome, now that they know we are on their trail. Watch the airports, the train stations, the bus terminals, the highways. They have the scroll and they have a second document. They cannot be allowed to leave the city with either." Baumann acknowledged and ended the call. He divided his men into teams: Termini Station, Ciampino Airport, Fiumicino Airport, and the northern highways leading out of Lazio. He took the Via Appia himself, driving south in the unmarked van, scanning the dark countryside for a gray Fiat with a priest and a woman inside.

Chapter 27: The Safe House

Matteo's former colleague, a defrocked priest named Tommaso Neri, lived in a ground-floor apartment in the San Lorenzo district, near the university. Tommaso had been excommunicated in 2008 for officiating the marriage of a divorced Catholic to a Muslim man. He had not set foot inside a church since, though he still wore a crucifix beneath his shirt. He opened the door at three in the morning, saw Matteo's face, and without a single question, stepped aside to let them in. The apartment was small — a kitchen with a cracked tile floor, a bedroom barely large enough for a single bed, a bathroom lined with bookshelves holding theological texts that Tommaso could no longer read without pain. He made tea in a stained pot, setting mugs on the table. Hale spread the documents across the kitchen table, weighing the corners with salt and pepper shakers. "I need to see the rest of the gospel," she said. "We have the translation of the first forty columns. But there are ten more." Matteo pulled the folded pages from his pocket. They were crumpled, damp from sweat and the humidity of the catacombs. He smoothed them on the table. The final columns contained a single extended passage — a speech Judas attributed to Jesus, delivered in Rome in the year 40 AD, to a gathering of his closest followers. The tone was different from the rest of the gospel. It was not reportorial. It was reflective, personal, almost tender. It was the voice of a man who knew he was dying, speaking to the people he loved.

Chapter 28: The Veil

Matteo read the passage aloud. "After I am gone," Jesus said in Judas's transcription, "the Church will become a veil between the world and the truth. This is necessary. The world cannot bear the truth. The truth would set fire to every altar, every throne, every palace of the mind. The Church is a vessel, not an answer. It contains the mystery without revealing it. The priests will speak of resurrection. Let them. The people need the story. But you — you who hear these words — you know the story is not the truth. The truth is that I am a man, as you are men. I loved, as you love. I will die, as you will die. What matters is not what happened on the cross. What matters is what you do with the time that remains. The cross is a symbol. The living is the reality. Do not worship the symbol. Live the reality." When Matteo finished reading, no one spoke. The kitchen was silent except for the drip of the faucet. Tommaso broke the silence, his voice rough. "That is not the Jesus of the gospels." "No," Matteo said. "That is the Jesus of a man who knew he was going to be turned into a god and did not want the job."

Chapter 29: The Recording

The Custodian reviewed the security footage from the catacombs. The cameras were grainy, poorly positioned — the catacombs were not a high-security facility. But the timestamp showed Matteo entering the hidden chamber and emerging with a wooden box. The Custodian froze the frame, zoomed in on the box, enhanced the image. He recognized the cedar grain, the simple brass fittings, the proportions. It was the same box described in the Custodians' internal archive, the inventory compiled in 1520 under Pope Leo X. The repository of the marriage contract. The original. For eight hundred years, the Custodians had believed the contract was lost, destroyed during the Sack of Rome in 1527 when the Swiss Guard had burned a warehouse of papal archives to keep them from the hands of Charles V's army. But the contract had not been destroyed. It had been moved, hidden in the catacombs, preserved for nearly five centuries, waiting. And now this Jesuit and this Englishwoman had found it. The Custodian sat in the dark for a long time, his hands resting on the desk, his face illuminated only by the glow of the paused footage. Then he composed a message to Cardinal Marchetti: The marriage contract has been recovered by the targets. The situation has escalated. I request permission to use all necessary means.

Chapter 30: The Morning

Dawn came gray and wet over Rome. Rain streaked the windows of Tommaso's apartment. Matteo had not slept. He sat at the kitchen table, the translation spread before him, reading and rereading the final speech of Jesus as Judas had recorded it. It was not an argument against faith. It was an argument against certainty. Jesus, as Judas quoted him, did not want worship. He wanted understanding. He had accepted the role the Church would build for him — the divine son, the sacrificial lamb, the risen lord — because he knew that was the only language the world could understand. But among his true followers, he insisted on honesty between them. The Church would become a veil, woven over generations, thread by thread. The Custodians were the guardians of that veil. Their job was to prevent anyone from lifting it, to maintain the mystery at any cost. And they had done their job for two thousand years, killing when necessary, lying when useful, preserving the institution at the expense of the truth. Matteo had been a Jesuit for twenty-two years. He had taken vows of poverty, chastity, obedience. He had taught students that faith was a journey, not a destination, a process of continual discovery. He had never once considered that the Church itself was a deliberate construction, a necessary fiction, maintained by a secret society that burned libraries and silenced scholars to protect its secret.

Chapter 31: The Crisis

Hale woke on Tommaso's couch, stiff and irritable, a blanket tangled around her legs. She found Matteo at the table, still reading, still in the same clothes, the same position. A cup of cold tea sat beside him, untouched. "You have not slept." "No." "You need to sleep. You cannot translate if your brain is not working." "I cannot sleep. Every time I close my eyes, I see the scroll. I hear Judas's voice. He wrote this two thousand years ago, and he is speaking to me as clearly as if he were in this room." Hale sat across from him, pulling her knees up. "What do you think we should do?" "I do not know. The Church is my life. It is my family, my vocation, my identity. If I publish this, I destroy the Church. If I give it back to the Custodians, I destroy the truth." "If you do not publish it, you destroy the truth." "Is the truth worth destroying two thousand years of faith? Millions of lives built on belief? People find meaning in the Church. They find community, comfort, purpose. If I take that away, what do I give them in return?" Hale considered the question. "I am not a believer," she said. "I believe in evidence. I believe in letting the past speak for itself. But I also understand that people need meaning. If the Church gives them meaning, perhaps the literal truth of a two-thousand-year-old document is not worth the destruction it would cause." "That is a surprisingly compassionate position for someone who calls herself a secular humanist." "I am not a secular humanist," Hale said. "I am a scholar. And a scholar understands that some truths are dangerous."

Chapter 32: The Analysis

They spent the day analyzing the marriage contract, comparing it against known examples of first-century Roman legal documents. The Greek was formal, precise, the language of Roman jurisprudence. It named the parties, the witnesses, the date by the consular year. It described the dowry: a small house in Trastevere, a loom, thirty silver coins, a set of cooking vessels. Claudia Procula's signature was distinctive — a bold, practiced hand, the signature of an educated Roman woman of the upper class. Vasari had sent digital photographs of the genealogy to a colleague in Oxford, a professor of medieval prosopography named Emily Carter. She responded within hours. The genealogy from the first century to the tenth was consistent with known historical records. The names matched census data, property records, and funerary inscriptions. But after the tenth century, the pattern changed. The genealogy showed a direct father-to-son succession of popes, each name passing from parent to child, while the official history showed a series of elections by the College of Cardinals. "The elections were a cover," Hale said, spreading the analysis across the table. "The College of Cardinals was a performance. The actual succession was determined by blood, not by vote." "That would make the Church a monarchy, not a religious institution," Tommaso said from the stove, where he was boiling pasta. "It would make it a dynasty," Matteo replied. "A family business disguised as a divine calling."

Chapter 33: The Visit

The Custodian arrived at Tommaso's apartment building at eleven in the morning. He was not wearing clerical dress. He wore a gray suit, a white shirt, no tie. He looked like a mid-level bureaucrat on his way to a budget meeting, unremarkable, forgettable. He had traced the rental car through Rome's license-plate recognition system, a network of cameras that the Custodians had quietly integrated into their security apparatus. He had identified Tommaso Neri by cross-referencing Matteo's known contacts with the list of former priests living in Rome. He climbed the stairs to the third floor and knocked on the door, three times, evenly spaced. Silence from inside. He knocked again. "Tommaso. I know you are in there. I am not here to harm you. I am here to talk." The door opened a crack. Tommaso's eye appeared in the gap, bloodshot, wary. "Who are you?" "I am the person who decides whether you live or die today. Let me in." Tommaso held his gaze for a long moment. Then he opened the door. The Custodian stepped inside. He saw Matteo at the table, Hale standing by the window with her arms crossed, the documents spread between them. He took in the room with a single glance — the exits, the windows, the potential weapons. "Father Matteo," he said. "Professor Hale. We need to talk."

Chapter 34: The Offer

The Custodian did not sit. He stood in the center of the room, hands clasped behind his back, his posture relaxed but precise, every movement controlled. "You have done remarkable work," he said. "You have translated a difficult text with accuracy and insight. You have located a supporting document that our order believed lost for five centuries. You have demonstrated scholarship, courage, and resourcefulness. I am impressed." "Are you here to congratulate us," Hale said, "or to kill us?" "I am here to offer you a choice." The Custodian turned to Matteo. "You are a Jesuit. You took a vow of obedience to the Holy Father. You have spent your life serving the Church. I am offering you the opportunity to continue that service at a higher level." "By destroying the scroll." "By protecting it. The scroll will not be destroyed. It will be returned to the archive where it has been kept for centuries. You will be appointed to the Custodians. You will have access to knowledge that no living scholar has seen. You will become a guardian of the deepest mystery of the Church." "And if I refuse?" "Then I will take the scroll by force, destroy your translation and your notes, and remove you from the equation." "You mean kill us." "I mean remove you from the equation. How that happens is up to you." His voice was flat, matter-of-fact, as if he were discussing a budget line item.

Chapter 35: The Deal

Matteo looked at the gospel translation spread across the table. He looked at the marriage contract, the genealogy, the photographs, the notes. He looked at Hale, who shook her head slightly, a warning. He looked at Tommaso, who stood frozen by the door, his face pale. "I want twenty-four hours," Matteo said. "To finish the translation. To read the remaining columns. To make my decision." "You will not use the time to escape or to copy the documents." "I give you my word." The Custodian considered. He was a man who read people the way Matteo read ancient texts — by the details, the small tells, the things left unsaid. He saw the fatigue in Matteo's posture, the conflict in his eyes, the genuine moral struggle. He also saw the intelligence, the resolve, the steel beneath the exhaustion. "Twenty-four hours," the Custodian said. "You will remain in this apartment. I will post a guard outside. If you attempt to leave, the agreement is void. I will return at noon tomorrow. You will give me your answer and every copy of every document." He turned to Hale. "You will remain as well. No phone calls. No messages. No contact with the outside world." He walked to the door, paused, and looked back at Matteo. "You have until noon tomorrow, Father. Use the time wisely." He closed the door behind him. The lock clicked.

Chapter 36: The Return

The Custodian's footsteps faded down the stairwell. The apartment fell into a dense, breathing silence. Tommaso pressed his ear to the door, listening. "He is gone. But there is a man in the stairwell. I can hear him breathing." Matteo sat down heavily at the table. His hands were trembling. He pressed them flat against the wood to steady them. "You are not actually considering his offer," Hale said. It was not a question. "I am considering all options. That is what he gave me twenty-four hours to do." "He is a murderer, Matteo. He tried to burn you alive in the library." "He tried to burn the scroll. We were collateral damage." "That makes it better?" "It makes it calculated. I can work with calculated. I understand calculated." Hale stared at him. "You have changed in the last three days." "I have read a gospel that changes everything I thought I knew about my faith, my Church, my entire life's work. Yes. I have changed." Matteo looked at the papers on the table, the translation he had given everything to produce. "But I have not decided what I believe yet. The change is not complete."

Chapter 37: The Last Passage

Matteo returned to the final column of the gospel. He had translated it roughly, but there were phrases that had eluded him — dialect words, obscure idioms. He read it again, slowly, his finger tracing each letter. Jesus was speaking about the veil. The Church would weave it, generation by generation, thread by thread, until it became as solid as stone. But the veil was not a lie, Jesus said. It was a filter. A lens. The truth was too bright for human eyes. The Church softened it, made it bearable, gave it shape and form. Those who could not bear the light needed the veil. Those who could — the true followers, the ones who understood — would see through it. But even they would not speak of what they saw. The veil was necessary. The truth was necessary. Both could exist in the same world, as long as no one tried to tear the veil away by force. Matteo read the passage three times, letting the meaning settle. Jesus was not commanding secrecy. He was not establishing a conspiracy. He was describing a paradox. The Church was both true and false, both divine and human, both revelation and construction. The gospel was both authentic and beside the point. The God game — the structure of faith, the institution, the hierarchy, the rituals — was not a deception. It was a shelter.

Chapter 38: The Watermark

Hale spent the afternoon examining the marriage contract under a magnifying loupe that Tommaso had produced from a drawer full of broken clocks and old keys. She was looking for anomalies — erasures, overwriting, anachronisms in the script or the materials. She found one. At the bottom of the contract, beneath the main text, almost invisible to the naked eye, was a watermark impressed into the papyrus. It was a cross surrounded by four dots — a symbol that had no place in first-century Rome. The cross was a Christian symbol that did not become widespread until the fourth century. The four-dot pattern was a heraldic device associated with the Benedictine order, which did not exist until the sixth century. Hale checked her reference books, cross-referencing against known watermarks. The mark matched the insignia of the Scriptorium of Monte Cassino, active in the early twelfth century. "This document is a forgery," she said, her voice flat. "The marriage contract is a medieval copy, fabricated in the twelfth century." Matteo looked up from the gospel. The color drained from his face. "A copy of an original?" "No. A fabrication. A deliberate creation. The papyrus is old, but not first-century old. The ink has been chemically treated to simulate age. Someone in the twelfth century created this document to support the genealogy." "Why would anyone do that?" "The most likely reason is power. There was a papal schism in the twelfth century — multiple claimants to the throne of Peter. Someone created a bloodline to prove that their candidate was the legitimate pope."

Chapter 39: The Crisis Deepens

Matteo felt the ground shift beneath him. The marriage contract was a forgery. That meant the genealogy could be a forgery too. Or the genealogy could be real, and the contract was created to support it. Or both could be fabrications, layered on top of each other like a house of cards. The gospel itself — the Judas scroll — could be authentic, but the supporting documents were medieval inventions designed to weaponize it. "The Custodians may not know," Hale said. "They may have believed the contract was original. They have been protecting a forgery for eight hundred years." "Or they created it," Tommaso said. "The Custodians existed in the twelfth century. They could have commissioned the forgery to consolidate their own power." Matteo rubbed his eyes. They were dry, burning. He had not slept in two days. "We need to examine the gospel for similar marks. If the gospel is also a forgery — " "It is not," Hale said. "The carbon dating is definitive. The script is authentic first-century Aramaic. The ink matches known samples from the period. The gospel is real." "So the gospel is real, and the contract is fake. Someone found the real gospel and created fake supporting documents to prove a specific narrative." "That is the most likely scenario. The gospel is the truth. The contract is the spin."

Chapter 40: The Threshold

The Custodian waited in a cafe across the street from Tommaso's building. He drank espresso, one after another, and watched the apartment window through the plate glass. The rain had stopped. The street was quiet. He did not trust Matteo's word. He trusted leverage, pressure, the weight of institutions. He had spent forty years in the service of the Church, and he knew that faith was a variable, not a constant. Believers broke. They lost their way. They found their way back, or they did not. The only reliable force was institutional power, the slow accumulation of authority and control that had kept the Church intact for two millennia. The Church was the oldest continuous institution in the Western world. It had survived barbarian invasions, the rise and fall of empires, schisms, reforms, wars, and scandals that would have destroyed any other organization. It would survive a lost gospel. But the gospel could not be allowed to circulate. Not because it was true. Because it was true enough to cause damage, and damage was the only thing that mattered. The Custodian checked his watch. Sixteen hours remaining. He signaled to the guard in the stairwell to maintain position.

Chapter 41: The Final Lines

Matteo translated the last lines of the gospel. Judas had appended a personal note, separate from the main narrative, written in a more hurried hand, the ink darker, the letters less careful. I am old now. My hands shake. The ink spills across the page. I have told the truth as I saw it, as I lived it, as I remember it. I have hidden nothing. But I have also protected the secret, as Yeshua asked me to do. The secret is not that he survived the cross. The secret is that the cross does not matter. What matters is the living. The cross is a symbol, a tool, a story. The living is the reality that the story points toward. I have written this for the ones who come after me. Not for the Church, which will make its own stories. For the ones who need to know that the veil is woven by human hands, and human hands can pull it aside. For the ones who need to know that the truth is simpler than any doctrine, and harder. Love. Forgiveness. The work of your hands. The people at your table. That is the gospel. The rest is commentary. Matteo finished the translation and set down his pen. His hand was cramped, his vision blurry. The gospel was complete. He had read the entire account of Judas Iscariot, written in his own hand, preserved for two thousand years. And the final message was not a threat to the Church. It was an invitation to see beyond it, to find the human beneath the divine.

Chapter 42: The Confrontation

Matteo asked Tommaso to bring the Custodian upstairs. When the Custodian entered, Matteo was standing at the table, the complete gospel translation folded in his hands. The marriage contract and the genealogy lay beside him. "I have finished the translation," Matteo said. "And I have reached a decision." "Tell me." "The gospel is authentic. The marriage contract is a twelfth-century forgery, created at the scriptorium of Monte Cassino. The genealogy may be authentic or may be a later addition. I cannot determine that without further analysis." The Custodian's face revealed nothing. His eyes did not flicker. "And your decision?" "I will not join the Custodians." "Then you have chosen death." "No. I have chosen to publish the truth." The Custodian stepped forward, his hands dropping to his sides. "If you publish, I will ensure that you are excommunicated, discredited, and imprisoned. The gospel will be declared a forgery by the Church's scholars, and your professional reputation will be destroyed. You will die as a heretic and a fraud." "I know." "And you are willing to accept that?" Matteo looked at the gospel in his hands. He thought about Judas, dying in a cave in Damascus, writing his testimony for a world that would not be ready for it for two thousand years. He thought about the veil, woven by human hands, beautiful and necessary and false. "I took a vow of obedience to God," Matteo said. "Not to an institution. If the institution has built itself on a veil, my vow requires me to be honest about what I have seen."

Chapter 43: The Standoff

The Custodian did not respond immediately. He stood very still, his hands at his sides, his eyes fixed on Matteo. The silence stretched. Hale shifted her weight. Tommaso's breath was audible, uneven. "You are a fool," the Custodian said finally. "Perhaps. But I am not a coward." "There is a difference between courage and stupidity. You are holding a document that could destabilize the faith of billions of people. You are planning to publish it without context, without interpretation, without the safeguards that scholarship provides." "I am planning to publish it with an introduction that will explain the forgery and the watermark. I am planning to be honest about what we know and what we do not know." "That will not matter. People will read what they want to read. The gospel will become a weapon. Atheists will use it to attack religion. Fundamentalists will use it to attack the papacy. The truth will be lost in the war between narratives." "That is not my responsibility." "It is exactly your responsibility. You are a scholar. You know that context is everything. You know that a document without context is not truth — it is ammunition. If you publish without the Church's involvement, you are not revealing the truth. You are unleashing chaos, and you will not be able to control it."

Chapter 44: The Revelation

The Custodian took a breath. He looked older than he had twenty-four hours ago. The mask of control had slipped, revealing the weariness beneath. "There is something you do not know," he said. "About the forgery." "Tell me." "The Custodians did not commission it. We discovered it. We have known since the fifteenth century that the marriage contract was a medieval fabrication. We have also known who created it." "Who?" "A monk named Gerbert of Aurillac. The same monk who became Pope Sylvester II in the year 999." Matteo felt the air leave his lungs. The name was familiar — Sylvester II, the scholar pope, the mathematician, the man who introduced Arabic numerals to Europe. "Sylvester II created the forgery?" "He was a scholar, the most learned man of his age. He discovered the Judas gospel in the papal archives during his years as librarian. He recognized its authenticity immediately. But he also recognized its danger." "So he created a fake marriage contract and a fake genealogy to contain the real gospel." "He created a narrative that could be controlled. A story that could be managed, guided, shaped to protect the Church. He presented it to the papal court as a rediscovered original. The Church accepted it, and the Custodians were formed to guard the secret." "But why? Why not destroy the gospel?" "Because the gospel is true. Gerbert could not bring himself to destroy the truth. He could only build a cage around it."

Chapter 45: The Test

Matteo sat down. His legs would not hold him. The room spun. "The gospel is a test," he said. "Yes." "The Custodians have been testing people with it for centuries. When someone discovers it, you watch what they do." "Not sending it out, no. But when it has been found — and it has been found seven times in our history — we observe. We judge. We evaluate." "And what do you judge?" "We judge whether the discoverer can be trusted with the truth. Most cannot. They are silenced. A few are brought into the Custodians. They become guardians of the veil." "And me?" "You are the eighth. And you have passed the test. You refused to join us. You refused to destroy the gospel. You chose to publish, knowing the cost to yourself, your reputation, your faith." "I do not understand." "The test is not about loyalty to the institution. It is about integrity. Those who join us value the institution over the truth. Those who destroy the gospel value power over both. But those who choose to publish — those who choose the truth, regardless of the cost — those are the ones who understand what Jesus actually taught."

Chapter 46: The Choice

The Custodian extended his hand, palm open. "Give me the gospel." Matteo did not move. He held the translation against his chest. "I will not destroy it. I will return it to the archive. No one will read it again in our lifetime. But it will exist. It will wait. And someday, when the Church is ready, someone will find it again." "That is not publishing. That is burying." "It is preserving. Publishing now, in this climate, would cause chaos. The world is not ready for this truth. Preserving it allows the truth to emerge when the world can bear it." "How do I know you are telling the truth? How do I know you will not destroy it the moment I leave?" "You do not know. You have to trust me." "Why should I trust you?" "Because I am offering you the same choice I have offered every discoverer. Join us, destroy it, or trust me to keep it safe. Those are the only options. Choose one." Matteo looked at the gospel in his hands. He looked at Hale. She shook her head, her eyes pleading. He looked at Tommaso, who shrugged, his palms open. "It is your decision, Matteo," Tommaso said. "You are the one who has to live with it."

Chapter 47: The Decision

Matteo thought about the final lines of the gospel. The veil is woven by human hands, and human hands can pull it aside. Judas had written those words forty years after the crucifixion, in a cave, knowing he was dying. He had trusted that someone would find the gospel and understand it. He had not trusted the Church. He had trusted individuals, one by one, generation by generation. "I will give you the gospel," Matteo said. "On two conditions." "Name them." "First, I keep a copy of my translation. For my own records. For my own conscience." "Accepted." "Second, you tell me the truth. The complete history. Everything you know about Sylvester II, the forgery, the Custodians, and the gospel." The Custodian hesitated. His eyes searched Matteo's face. Then he nodded. "That is a fair condition. I will tell you everything. But not here. We will go to the archive. You will see the full history with your own eyes." "And then?" "And then you will decide what to do with what you have learned. That is the final test."

Chapter 48: The Archive

The Custodian led them through the streets of Rome, past the Colosseum, past the Forum, to a building Matteo had passed a hundred times without noticing. It was a Renaissance palazzo on the Via dei Serpenti, unmarked, the shutters closed, the door secured by a lock that required both a key and a code. Inside, the archive was a single large room, high-ceilinged, lined with floor-to-ceiling shelves that held leather-bound volumes in Latin. The Custodian walked to a specific shelf, pulled a volume, and opened it on a reading table. Inside was a history of the Custodians, written in the hand of Pope Sylvester II himself. "Gerbert of Aurillac discovered the Judas gospel in 997 AD," the Custodian said. "He was a librarian before he became pope. He recognized the gospel's authenticity immediately. He also recognized that it would destroy the Church if it were published in the political climate of his time." "So he created the marriage contract and the genealogy." "He created a structure. A narrative that could be controlled. He presented it as a rediscovered original. The Church accepted it. The Custodians were formed to guard both the gospel and the forgery, ensuring the secret never leaked." "And it worked for a thousand years." "It worked until a junior librarian with a crowbar found an amphora in a sealed vault." The Custodian closed the volume. "Now you know everything, Father. The full history of the God game."

Chapter 49: The Refusal

The Custodian made his offer one final time. "Join us, Father. You have the intellect, the integrity, the faith. You could be one of the great Custodians." Matteo shook his head. "I cannot." "Why?" "Because I believe in the gospel. Not the Judas gospel. The gospel of Jesus. The story of a man who taught love and forgiveness and died for his beliefs. If that story is a veil, I choose to live inside it. I choose faith over complete knowledge." "That is a contradiction. You know the truth now." "I know one truth. The gospel of Judas is a historical document. It records one man's perspective. But it is not the only truth. The faith of billions of people is also true, in its own way, in its own dimension. The veil is not a lie. It is a mystery. And I choose to live inside the mystery." The Custodian studied him for a long moment. "You are a rare man, Father Matteo." "I am a man who has found his faith again. Not in the institution. Not in the documents. In the space between them. In the uncertainty." "What will you do now?" "I will leave the priesthood. I will teach. I will write about the discovery without revealing the discovery itself. I will tell the story of a man who found a gospel and learned that the truth is more complicated than he ever imagined."

Chapter 50: The Departure

Matteo left the archive alone. Hale had already returned to London, her flight leaving that evening from Fiumicino. Tommaso had stayed in his apartment, the documents gone, the silence settling around him like dust. The Custodian had the gospel, the marriage contract, and the genealogy. They would be sealed in a vault beneath the Palazzo del Sant'Uffizio, not to be opened for another century. Matteo walked through the streets of Rome in the early evening. The rain had stopped. The light was golden, soft, casting long shadows across the cobblestones. The dome of St. Peter's glowed in the distance, catching the last of the sun. He stopped at a small church — Santa Maria in Trastevere — and sat in a pew near the back. The church was empty. Candles flickered before a mosaic of the Virgin. He did not pray. He sat in the silence, feeling the weight of what he had learned and what he had chosen. The gospel was real. The marriage contract was a forgery. The genealogy was a construction. The Church was a human institution, built on faith and fiction, hope and manipulation. And yet. Matteo felt the peace of the church around him. He heard the echo of a child's laugh from the piazza outside. He smelled incense and old stone and candle wax. The veil was woven by human hands. But it was also beautiful. He stood, walked to the door, and stepped out into the Roman evening. The God game was over. He had lost his faith and found it again, in a form he could not name, a shape he could not define. The gospel would wait. The truth would wait. And he would live his life between the veil and whatever lay beyond it, trusting that the space between was where faith was meant to be.
