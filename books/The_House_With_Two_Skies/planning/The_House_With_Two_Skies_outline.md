# The House With Two Skies

## Premise

After her mother Mair's death, Eluned Pryce — fifty-two, a translator, newly alone — returns to the remote hill farm in Powys where she grew up, to empty it for sale. The farmhouse stands on an old geological fault the locals never named. From the two small windows of the back bedroom, on certain still mornings, you can see two different skies: the grey Welsh overcast of this world, and something quieter and brighter through the other pane. Through that second window the farm is kept — the orchard alive, the kitchen lamp burning — and her mother is still there, alive, making tea. Eluned finds she can cross briefly into that other world, where a different version of herself made the choices she didn't: stayed on the farm, married the neighbour Tomos, never left for the city and the career and the marriage that slowly unmade her. Drawn deeper into the other house, Eluned must reckon with the grief she came home to avoid, the seduction of the life she didn't live, and what she owes the world and the people she actually belongs to — especially her daughter Carys, whose calls she keeps declining. The fault runs on a tide, and whoever lingers thins in their own world.

## Core Cast

- **Eluned Pryce, 52** — translator, divorced, the daughter who left; home to clear the house and sidestep grief, undone instead by the two windows.
- **Mair Pryce, 78** — her mother, dead in this world and alive in the other; sharp-tongued, tender in patches, carrying her own unfinished business with the daughter who left.
- **Carys, 27** — Eluned's adult daughter, in Cardiff; the real-world tether Eluned keeps setting aside, frightened of being needed and of needing back.
- **Tomos Llewellyn, 55** — the neighbouring farmer, widower in this world; quiet, decent, present; married to the other Eluned in the other world.
- **Branwen Cadwallader, 60** — Mair's oldest friend, keeper of the village's quiet knowledge about the fault; she lost her husband to it thirty years ago and warns Eluned of the tide and the toll.
- **The other Eluned** — the woman who stayed; glimpsed through the window first, then met face to face; unsettling, not a ghost but a life, fully lived, pulling in its own direction.

## Chapter Plan

### Part One: The House in Order (Chapters 1–10)

1. Eluned drives to the farm alone a week after the funeral; catalogue of the house, the smell of absence, her mother's coat still on the hook.
2. First morning: Eluned inventories the parlour, breaks a cup, cries for the first time since the death; Tomos arrives with eggs and the names of local skips.
3. The orchard — mossy, unpruned, half the trees toppled — and a memory of cider-pressing; Eluned takes stock of what the sale will require.
4. Eluned's sister called, her ex-husband's lawyer emailed: the real world presses in; she delays answers and starts to stay longer at the farm than planned.
5. Branwen comes to the farm, ostensibly with a casserole; she notices how Eluned lingers in the back bedroom and goes quiet; the first oblique warning.
6. Tomos brings the ewes down through the top field; Eluned watches from the fence; he tells her the back bedroom had a different name in the old deeds — "the still room."
7. A still morning: Eluned, insomniac, in the back bedroom at first light; she notices the two panes show different skies — she thinks she is imagining it.
8. The two-sky morning returns; Eluned sees movement through the second pane — a woman at the kitchen table — and recognises, with a lurch, her mother.
9. Eluned spends the day telling herself it was a trick of the glass; she cannot stop going back to the window; she does not phone Carys.
10. Third still morning: Eluned leans close and the second window opens like a door; she steps halfway through, hears her mother's voice, and steps back, shaking.

### Part Two: The Other House (Chapters 11–20)

11. Eluned crosses fully: the other kitchen, the other Mair alive at the table, the shock of touch, the smell of bread; she can only stay minutes before the light shifts.
12. Back in this world, disoriented; she checks for evidence — the photograph on the dresser shows the other house's kept orchard in the background, faint, as if double-exposed.
13. Second crossing: longer this time; Eluned sees the wedding photograph she did not take — herself and Tomos, 1997 — and understands the shape of the other life.
14. The other Mair speaks plainly of Eluned-who-stayed: "She worries about you, you know. She always has." Eluned does not know if this is said to her or to the other.
15. Tomos in this world comes to tell Eluned a ewe is stuck in the top ditch; they work together in the rain; he is careful with her, asks nothing she cannot answer.
16. Branwen returns, reads something in Eluned's face; over tea she says the fault has a tide — mornings after the wind drops — and that her husband Gareth crossed in 1993 and never fully came back.
17. Branwen explains the toll: those who linger thin in their own world — a name lost, an edge of recognition gone, small things first; Gareth could not hold his keys on the last morning.
18. Carys phones three times; Eluned listens to the voicemails but does not call back; she goes to the back bedroom instead.
19. Eluned meets the other Eluned face to face in the other kitchen, both women equally startled; a brief, strange conversation; neither knows what to call the other.
20. The other Eluned asks, simply: "Have you eaten?" It is entirely Mair's question; Eluned wets with it.

### Part Three: The Seduction of the Un-Lived Life (Chapters 21–30)

21. Eluned starts crossing every still morning; in this world the estate agent's calls pile up unanswered; the sale is effectively stalled.
22. She helps Mair make jam in the other kitchen — damson, the old recipe — a morning of such ordinary completeness it undoes her; she stays past the safe limit.
23. Back: she cannot remember the estate agent's name; she writes it down three times before it sticks; she tells herself it was tiredness.
24. Tomos, in this world, invites her to supper at his farm; she accepts; they eat plainly, talk about the hill, about what she did after she left Wales; she finds she cannot quite account for certain years.
25. A night she cannot sleep: she sits with the photograph albums, her mother's handwriting on the backs; the old argument surfaces — why Eluned left, who was right, what was never settled.
26. In the other house, Mair's lamp still lit after dark; Eluned risks an evening crossing; Mair has made cawl; they eat together; the conversation moves toward the old argument.
27. Eluned and Mair of the other world have the long-deferred reckoning: Eluned's leaving, Mair's silence, the letter that wasn't sent, the apologies that came too late to count; Mair's last word, gentle and unfair at once: "You always wanted the whole sky, cariad."
28. Eluned returns to this world and finds a photograph on the dresser has changed: Mair is absent from it now; the frame holds a summer day, the same field, but empty.
29. Carys texts: "Mum, I haven't heard from you in twelve days. I'm scared." Eluned reads it, sets the phone down, does not reply.
30. She stands at the window in the ordinary morning, trying to see which sky is the right one.

### Part Four: The Toll Deepens (Chapters 31–40)

31. The estate agent arrives unannounced; Eluned meets her at the gate and cannot finish a sentence; she reschedules and goes directly to the back bedroom.
32. Tomos finds Eluned sitting on the gate in the cold, not dressed for the weather, not certain what day it is; he takes her inside and makes tea; he says: "You're losing time."
33. Carys arrives at the farm; Eluned does not recognise her for one bad second at the door; the correction comes, but Carys saw it.
34. Carys and Eluned in the parlour; Carys asks what has been happening; Eluned tells her nothing, which is the lie it sounds like; they argue, old edges between them.
35. Branwen comes, sees Carys, takes Eluned aside and says what she could not say kindly before: "Gareth went so far he couldn't see me when he looked at me. It's the same in your face."
36. Branwen tells the full history: Gareth was in love with a version of their life that hadn't gone wrong — a daughter who didn't die — and he chose to stay near it; he thinned to a shell in six months.
37. Eluned, alone after Branwen leaves, tries to write down what she still knows of this world: Carys's birthday, her ex-husband's face, the name of the road she grew up on; some come slowly.
38. A still morning: Eluned crosses, intending to say goodbye; instead Mair asks her to sit and they are just together in the kitchen, the ordinary morning complete; Eluned cannot leave.
39. Eluned misses the whole day in this world; Carys cannot find her; Tomos checks the fields; at dusk Eluned is on the parlour floor, cold, not knowing how she got there.
40. Carys, frightened, sits with her mother all night; by morning Eluned knows clearly what she is risking and why.

### Part Five: The Turn (Chapters 41–50)

41. A clear, windless morning; Eluned goes to the back bedroom and looks through the second pane; she does not open it; she understands finally that "her mother, alive" is not the same as undoing loss.
42. She phones Carys — her daughter is in the kitchen downstairs — and says: "I need to tell you about the window." The telling, halting and strange; Carys listens.
43. Carys asks the question neither of them has asked: "What do you actually want from this world? Not that one. This one."
44. Eluned and Tomos walk the top field; he knows about the fault in the same half-knowing way the whole valley knows; he does not press; they stand at the wall and watch the light on the hill.
45. Eluned crosses once more, deliberately, to give her mother a true goodbye — the grief she came home to avoid, finally faced; she and Mair say what they mean, without the other world's softening distance.
46. The other Eluned appears at the other kitchen door; they look at each other; the other Eluned says: "Go home." It is not unkind. It is exact.
47. Eluned steps back through the window and, for the first time, latches it; she sits on the floor and cries properly for her mother — not the live Mair of the other world, but the dead Mair of this one.
48. Carys finds her mother on the floor; she sits down beside her; they are quiet together; then Carys begins to talk, and Eluned listens.
49. Eluned phones the estate agent, then, the next day, phones her back and says she won't sell — she will keep the farm, move in properly, live in it; a life toward the living, not away from it.
50. A single ordinary morning on the hill: Eluned feeding the ewes Tomos lent her, Carys still in the kitchen making tea the wrong way, one sky above the valley, the second window latched but still there, unmysterious now, just glass.
