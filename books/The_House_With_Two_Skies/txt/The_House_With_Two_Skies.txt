Chapter 1: The Coat on the Hook

Eluned drove up from Cardiff a week after the funeral, the back seat full of flat-packed boxes she had bought at a retail park off the M4, and she did not cry on the way, which she noted the way you note a symptom.

The farm was where it had always been, at the end of three miles of single-track road above Llanbrynmair, the house grey against a grey hill, the yard gate hanging the way it had hung for forty years, on a slant, dragging a quarter-moon scrape in the concrete. She let herself in with the key her mother had kept under the third slate. The kitchen smelled of cold ash and Sunlight soap and, faintly, of her mother, which was the worst of it, because you could not box up a smell and take it to a skip.

She walked through the rooms making her inventory. The parlour with its two chairs and the television no one had watched. The narrow stairs. The back bedroom that faced the hill, where she had slept as a girl and where the air was somehow always still. Her mother's room, the bed made, a glass of water gone cloudy on the nightstand. The kitchen again. On the back of the kitchen door, on the hook, her mother's coat — the green waxed one, cracked at the elbows, that Mair had worn to feed the hens for twenty years.

Eluned stood and looked at the coat for a long time. She did not take it down. She told herself she would deal with the coat later, when she had a system, and she went out to the car to bring in the boxes, and the hill watched her do it with the particular patience of a thing that has outlasted everyone who ever owned it.

Chapter 2: The Broken Cup

In the morning she started on the parlour, because it was the room with the least of her mother in it.

She wrapped the brass carriage clock in newspaper. She stacked the good plates that had only ever come out at Christmas. She found, in the sideboard, a biscuit tin full of buttons and a smaller tin full of teeth — milk teeth, hers and her sister's, kept for fifty years in a tobacco tin marked in pencil, El and Carys— no. El and Rhian. Her sister. She put the tin down and picked up a teacup from the dresser, one of the everyday ones with the gold rim worn to a ghost, and it slipped, simply slipped, and broke on the flagstone into three clean pieces.

And that was what did it. Not the coat and not the teeth but a cracked cup worth nothing, and she sat down on the cold floor among the newspaper and cried the way she had not been able to cry in the chapel or at the graveside or in the solicitor's office, ugly and without dignity, until her face ached.

She was still red about the eyes when Tomos Llewellyn knocked and came in without waiting, the way they all did up here, with a box of eggs in one hand.

"Saw the car," he said, and then saw her face, and set the eggs on the table and did not comment on it, which she was grateful for. "Brought you these. And I wrote down the skip hire in Machynlleth, and the man who'll take the furniture if you don't want the bother." He put a folded paper by the eggs. "No rush on any of it, Eluned. The hill's not going anywhere and neither's the house."

Chapter 3: The Orchard

The orchard was behind the house, six trees that her grandfather had planted and her mother had let go.

Eluned walked into it in her mother's wellingtons, two sizes too big, and saw what twenty years of not-pruning had done. Two of the trees were down, toppled at the root, their trunks gone soft and orange with rot, colonies of bright fungus stepping up the bark like little shelves. The standing four were a chaos of water-shoots and dead wood, the fruit they still made small and scabbed and falling unpicked into the long grass, where the wasps had them.

She remembered cider. She remembered her father — dead thirty years now, longer than her mother — turning the press in the September cold, the smell of it, the must and the bruise of apples, her own small hands sticky to the wrist. She remembered her mother carrying the demijohns down to the cellar, and the particular sound of the airlocks bubbling in the dark all autumn, a sound like the house thinking.

You could not sell a house with an orchard like this. You could not sell it with the gate on a slant and the gutters full and the back field gone to rush. She made a list in her head of what the sale would require and the list was long and she was, she realised, a little glad of its length, because a long list was a reason to stay, and she had not yet asked herself why she wanted a reason to stay in a cold house on a hill she had spent thirty years being glad to have left.

Chapter 4: The World Below

The world she had left did not leave her alone.

Rhian phoned from Shrewsbury, brisk and kind and already grieving in the organised way she did everything, wanting to know about the will, the headstone, whether Eluned was all right up there on her own, really all right. Eluned said she was fine. The estate agent in Newtown emailed about valuations and access. Her ex-husband's solicitor emailed about the final division of a flat in Pontcanna that Eluned no longer wanted and could no longer quite picture. There was a translation due, four thousand words of a Catalan novel about a marriage ending, which was either a cruelty or a joke, and she had not opened the file.

She answered none of it. She told herself she would answer all of it on Monday, and then it was Monday and she told herself Wednesday. She drove down to the Spar in the village for milk and bread and stayed three days on what she bought. She found that the hours up here did not behave the way hours behaved below. A morning would go into the cleaning of a single cupboard and she would look up and the light would be failing and she would not be able to say where the day had gone, except that it had gone gently, and she had not thought about the flat in Pontcanna or the marriage that ended or the daughter in Cardiff she kept meaning to ring.

She slept badly. The house was very quiet at night. The back bedroom, where she had moved her things, was quieter than the rest, as if the stillness pooled there, ran downhill into that one room and stood.

Chapter 5: Branwen's Casserole

Branwen Cadwallader came on the Thursday with a casserole in a tea towel and her late husband's old shooting stick to lean on going up the yard.

She had been Mair's friend for sixty years. She sat at the kitchen table while Eluned made tea and she talked about Mair the way the old talk about the dead, plainly, with the grief folded in among the practical, who would have the hymn books, what to do with the hens. She ate a biscuit. She watched Eluned more than she talked, in the way of someone taking a reading.

"You're sleeping in the back," Branwen said. It was not a question. "Your mam's room would be warmer. The chimney draws."

"I like the back," Eluned said. "It's quiet."

"It is that." Branwen turned her cup a quarter-turn on its saucer. "Quiet in the back. Always was. The still room, the old people called it." She looked at Eluned a moment too long. "You want to mind how much time you spend up there on your own, cariad. A grieving woman and a quiet room. It's not always a kindness, the quiet."

Eluned laughed, to be light about it. "I'm fifty-two, Branwen. I'm not going to come to harm sorting wardrobes."

"No," Branwen said, and did not smile, and changed the subject to the casserole, which wanted twenty minutes in a hot oven and no more, and would Eluned remember to actually eat it, and not just leave it on the side the way the grieving did, living on tea and air until they were half here themselves.

Chapter 6: The Still Room

Tomos brought the ewes down through the top field on the Saturday, and Eluned watched from the broken gate while his dog ran the flock down the slope in long clean arcs, the sheep pouring through the gap in the wall like grey water.

He came to lean on the gate beside her when they were penned. He smelled of lanolin and rain. He was a widower — his wife, Sian, had died of something fast and unfair five years back — and he farmed the two holdings now, his and the slice of theirs Mair had let him graze, and he carried his solitude lightly, the way men up here learned to or went strange.

"Your mam loved this field," he said. "Wouldn't sell it me, even when her knees went. Said she liked to see something growing she still owned."

"You can have it now," Eluned said. "Whatever you want of it. I can't farm a hill from Cardiff."

He nodded, but he was looking at the house, at the small dark window of the back bedroom under the eaves. "Funny old house," he said. "Sian never liked the back room. Wouldn't have it for the children, when we'd visit. The deeds call it something — I saw it once, when your mam had me witness a thing. Not 'bedroom.' An old word." He thought. "The still room. Like a dairy, maybe. Or—" He shrugged, a farmer's economy with what he couldn't explain. "Old houses. They keep their own names for themselves."

Chapter 7: Two Panes

She woke at four on a windless morning and could not get back under, and rather than lie there she went and sat in the chair by the back-bedroom window to wait for the light, wrapped in her mother's eiderdown.

The window was old, two panes in a wooden frame swollen shut with paint, the glass gone slightly violet with age and full of the little flaws and bubbles of old glass, so that the hill beyond it swam a little if you moved your head. She had looked through it ten thousand times as a girl. She knew its view the way she knew her own hand: the rise of the field, the black line of the forestry on the ridge, the sky above doing what the Welsh sky did, which was mostly the colour of a held breath.

The light came up grey and ordinary. And then she moved her head, and she saw — she thought she saw — that the two panes did not agree.

Through the left pane the sky was the held-breath grey she had known her whole life, the forestry black on the ridge, a buzzard going up on it. Through the right pane the sky was paler, and higher, and there was a wash of early gold along the bottom of it, the kind of clean dawn you got perhaps six mornings a year, and the ridge through the right pane had no forestry on it at all, only open hill.

She sat very still. She told herself it was the old glass, the violet flaws, the angle, her tiredness, the grief that the books all said could do strange things to the eyes and the appetite and the sense of the real. She told herself this for a long time. The two skies did not change to match each other. After a while the light came up properly and the difference thinned and was gone, and there was only the one hill, the one grey morning, and a woman in a chair who had not slept.

Chapter 8: A Woman at the Table

It happened again on the next still morning, and this time she did not tell herself anything, because there was a woman through the right pane, and the woman was her mother.

The right pane showed not the hill at all now but, impossibly, a room — as if the window had stopped being a window and become a doorway into another window across a lit kitchen. The other kitchen was their kitchen and was not: the same dresser, but the everyday cups all present and unbroken on it; the same flagstones, but a rag rug she did not recognise; the green waxed coat — but worn, in motion, alive, on the back of a woman who sat at the table with her hands round a cup of tea, looking out of her own window at a clean gold morning, not seeing Eluned at all.

It was Mair. Not Mair as she had been at the end, folded small in the hospital bed, but Mair at perhaps seventy, upright, her hair still half dark, her jaw set in the particular way she'd had when she was thinking and didn't want to be disturbed. She lifted the cup and drank and set it down and said something over her shoulder to someone Eluned could not see, and laughed at the answer, a short dry laugh Eluned had not heard in two years and felt in her chest like a hand closing.

Eluned made a sound. The morning shifted, the gold thinned, the kitchen folded back into hill and held-breath grey, and her mother was gone, and Eluned sat in the cold with her heart going and understood that she had just watched her dead mother laugh, and that she was not, whatever else was true, imagining it.

Chapter 9: Tricks of the Glass

She spent the whole of that day being a reasonable woman.

She made strong coffee. She opened the laptop and looked at the Catalan novel for the first time and translated a paragraph badly and deleted it. She walked down to the village and back to put the blood moving and the air through her, four miles, and told herself the entire way that grief and insomnia and old glass between them could make a kitchen out of a hillside, that the mind shown a familiar shape would finish it the way it finished a word from its first letters, that there was a name for it, pareidolia, the seeing of faces in the random, and that she of all people, who made her living being precise about meaning, was not going to be undone by a trick of the light in a cold farmhouse.

It was a good argument. She believed it with the front of her mind and not at all with the back, and the back was where she lived, that day, going up to the bedroom four times on invented errands — a jumper, a book, to see if she'd left the tap running — and each time standing at the window in the wrong light, the ordinary daylight, the two panes agreeing perfectly on one dull hill, and feeling the disappointment go through her like cold water, which told her everything the argument did not.

She did not phone Carys, who had left a voicemail she half-listened to, something about a weekend, something about coming up. She told herself she would phone tomorrow. She went to bed early, on purpose, so that she might wake at four, on purpose, and she lay in the still room listening to the house think and waited for it to be windless and pale.

Chapter 10: Halfway Through

The third still morning, she did not wait in the chair. She crossed the cold floor and put her face close to the right pane, close enough to feel the chill coming off the glass, and the other kitchen was there, lamplit against the gold dawn, and this time her mother was at the range with her back turned, and the window — Eluned understood it without deciding to — was not shut. The swollen frame, painted shut for thirty years, stood open a hand's breadth on the other side, the way a window stands open on a morning that has promised to be warm.

She put her fingers to the gap. There was no glass. There was air, and it was warmer than her own, and it carried the smell of toast and of the inside of a living house, and somewhere under that the green smell of an orchard in leaf.

She did not decide to. She leaned, and her head and one shoulder went through into the warm, into the other kitchen, into the place where her mother stood alive at the range with the kettle coming to the boil, and Mair — not turning, busy, ordinary, immortal — said, "Shut that window, cariad, you'll have the heat out," in the exact voice, the exact words, the small domestic scold of forty years, and Eluned's whole body convulsed with it, and she wrenched herself back into the cold dark still room and sat on the floor with her back to the wall, shaking, both hands pressed over her mouth so she would not wake a house that had no one in it to wake.

Chapter 11: The Other Kitchen

It took her two days to do it on purpose, and when she did she went all the way through.

She waited for the still morning and the open window and the warm air, and she did not let herself think, because thinking was what had pulled her back the first time. She put both hands on the sill and she climbed through, awkward, fifty-two and not limber, one knee on the cold sill of her world and then both feet on the warm flags of the other, and she stood up in the other kitchen with her heart slamming and the smell of baking bread all around her.

Her mother turned from the range.

For a moment Mair only looked at her, a small frown, the look of a woman placing a face. Then it cleared. "There you are," she said, as if Eluned had been out at the hens. "Sit down, you look perished. Have you come without a coat again?" She was already reaching two cups down from the dresser. She was solid. She moved with the small stiffness of seventy-odd and none of the ruin of the end. When she set a cup in front of Eluned her hand brushed Eluned's wrist, warm, dry, entirely real, the gold wedding ring loose on the finger the way it had always been, and Eluned made a sound she turned into a cough.

"I can't stay long," Eluned said. Her voice came out wrong.

"You never can," Mair said, comfortably, pouring. "Always halfway out the door, you. Drink your tea while it's in you."

The tea was too hot and tasted of their water, the soft hill water, and Eluned drank it scalding and watched her mother's face and did not speak, because there was nothing she could say that the morning would not take from her, and after a few minutes the gold light at the window began, very slightly, to thin, and she felt the pull of it, and she stood, and her mother said don't rush, and she rushed, back through the window into the cold, and sat down hard on her own floor, and the tea was still hot in her, the only proof.

Chapter 12: The Evidence

She became, that day, a person looking for evidence, because the alternative was to believe she had lost her mind, and she found she preferred the impossible to the mad.

She examined the back window in daylight, both panes, and they were only old glass, swollen shut, paint-sealed, no draught, no gap, the hill steady and singular through both. She put her palm flat to the right pane and it was cold and solid and gave her back nothing. Whatever the window was, it was not a window in the daytime, in the wind. It was only a window when the air went still and the light came gold, and then it was a door.

She went through the house looking for some mark the crossing had left, and she found one on the dresser in the parlour, in a photograph she had looked at a thousand times: her mother at sixty, standing in the orchard with a trug of apples, the trees behind her. Except that the trees behind her were wrong. In the photograph she remembered, the orchard was already going, two trees down. In the photograph on the dresser now the orchard stood whole and pruned and heavy with fruit, six trees in good order, a kept orchard, and her mother's face in front of it was the same and the light was the same but the world behind her had changed, faintly, as if a second picture had been printed over the first and not quite registered.

She took the photograph to the window and tilted it to the light. Under the kept orchard, very faint, like a watermark, she could just make out the ghost of the other one, the fallen trees, the going-to-ruin she knew was true. Both orchards were in the photograph now. She put it face down in a drawer and then took it out again and stood it back up, because she could not, it turned out, put her mother face down in a drawer, in any world, kept orchard or fallen.

Chapter 13: Nineteen Ninety-Seven

The second long crossing she let herself look around.

Mair had gone out to the hens — "shan't be a minute, mind the toast" — and Eluned stood alone in the other kitchen and let herself, for the first time, read the room as a room, the way she'd read a text for what it was telling her underneath. The differences from her own kitchen were small and total. The everyday cups all present. A man's cap on the second hook by the door, a working cap, not her father's. A pair of boots too big for Mair under the bench. A school photograph she didn't recognise, a dark-eyed boy, pinned to the dresser with the postcards.

And on the wall by the dresser, in a frame, a wedding photograph.

It was her. Fifty-two and she knew her own face at twenty-eight without a second's doubt, in a plain ivory dress, on the chapel steps in Llanbrynmair, squinting in the sun and laughing. And beside her, younger, broader, with all his hair, in a suit that did not fit him, was Tomos Llewellyn. There was confetti in the air. There was a date in silver pen along the bottom in her mother's hand: Eluned a Tomos, Mehefin 1997. June 1997. The summer, in her own world, that she had got on the train at Machynlleth with two suitcases and gone to Cardiff and not come back except to visit, the summer she had chosen the whole sky over the one hill, and had never once, in all the years, let herself wonder what the photograph would have looked like if she'd stayed.

Here it was. Her mother had framed it. Tomos had married her in this world, and they had a boy with dark eyes, and the orchard was kept. Eluned stood and looked at the life she had not lived hanging on the wall in a frame, and she heard the hens go up in the yard as her mother scattered the feed, and she had to sit down.

Chapter 14: She Worries About You

"She worries about you, you know," Mair said.

They were shelling peas. The other Mair set Eluned to small tasks the way she always had, as if work was the proper medium of company, and Eluned took it gratefully, her thumb running the pods open, the peas drumming into the bowl.

"Who does," Eluned said, carefully.

"You do." Mair did not look up from her own pod. "Worry about yourself. Down there in the city, on your own, that husband gone, that girl never seeing you." She said it as plain fact, not unkind, the way she'd always delivered the things that cut. "I see it on you when you come. You come in here with a face like a closed door and you sit and you drink your tea and you go again, and you think I don't notice, but I'm your mother, and I notice."

Eluned's thumb stilled on the pod. She understood, slowly, that Mair did not quite know which Eluned she was talking to — that to this mother there was one daughter, who lived sometimes here on the hill married to Tomos and sometimes, confusingly, came in cold and coatless from a city, and that the seam between those two ran straight through the woman at the table and Mair had simply, lovingly, papered over it the way she papered over everything she could not change.

"I'm all right, Mam," Eluned said, and the word came out of her before she could weigh it, Mam, and Mair looked up at that, and something passed over her face, recognition or its opposite, and she reached over and put her warm hand flat on Eluned's cheek, once, and said, "There's my girl," and went back to the peas, and Eluned shelled the rest of the bowl blind, because she could not see them for the water in her eyes.

Chapter 15: The Top Ditch

In her own world it rained for three days, and on the second a ewe of Tomos's got herself cast in the top ditch, and he came to the door wet through to ask Eluned's help, because the dog couldn't lift a sheep and two hands were better than one in a ditch.

She went. She was glad to go; the rain at least was certainly her own world's rain, cold and sideways and undeniable, and the ewe was certainly her own world's ewe, heavy and stupid and frightened, its fleece black with water, its legs going. They got down into the ditch together, the mud to their shins, and Tomos showed her where to take the weight and they heaved the animal over onto its feet, and it stood swaying and then bolted, ungrateful, up the field, and they were left laughing in the rain, filthy, breathing hard.

"You've still got the knack," Tomos said. "Hands remember."

"My hands remember a lot of things up here," Eluned said, and heard how it sounded, and he glanced at her, but he was not a man who chased a thing into the open.

They walked down together. At the yard he stopped. "You all right, Eluned? Up there on your own. You look—" He searched for it, a careful man with a careful word. "You look like you're keeping early hours. Your light's on at four. I see it from my place."

"I don't sleep well," she said. "It's the quiet."

"Aye," he said, and looked at the small dark window under the eaves, and then back at her, and let it go. "Well. You know where I am. Down the hill and turn left. Same as always." He went, and she stood in the rain a moment, glad of him, of his solidness, of a man who would not follow her into the open, and aware, distantly, that there was a version of this man in another kitchen who had followed her all the way to a chapel in June 1997, and that she had no idea what to do with knowing it.

Chapter 16: The Tide

Branwen came again on the Friday, and this time she did not bring a casserole, which Eluned understood, afterward, to have been the whole point.

She sat at the table and refused tea and looked at Eluned with her old pale eyes and said, "How many times have you been through."

Eluned opened her mouth to say through what, and could not. The lie would not come. Branwen was sixty years a friend of her mother's and the village's keeper of the things the village did not say aloud, and she had not climbed a wet hill on a bad hip to be told about old glass.

"A few," Eluned said.

Branwen nodded slowly, as if a fear had been confirmed and she had hoped to be wrong. "It runs on a tide, the back room," she said. "Always has. The still mornings, after the wind drops, when the light comes gold off the east — that's when it opens. Your mam knew. Her mam knew. They didn't use the room. There's a reason the bed was never in the back, cariad, and it wasn't the chimney." She folded her hands. "My Gareth went through. Nineteen ninety-three. You'd have been gone to Cardiff by then. He went through and he found a morning he wanted more than this one, and I couldn't follow him into it and I couldn't keep him out."

"What happened to him."

"He came back less each time," Branwen said. "That's what happens. I'll tell you the whole of it, because nobody told me, and I'd not wish that on you. But you'll make me a cup of tea first, after all, because it's not a thing I can say dry."

Chapter 17: The Toll

"It takes a toll," Branwen said, when the tea was made and going cold between her hands, untouched again. "Not all at once. By the inch. The more you're over there, the less of you holds here."

"Holds how."

"Small things first. A name you've had all your life, gone for a morning, then a day. The way to somewhere you've driven a hundred times. Then—" She turned the cup. "Gareth started losing the edges. He'd look at me, his own wife, thirty years, and there'd be a half a second where I was a stranger to him. He'd cover it. But I saw it grow, that half-second, until it was most of a second, until one morning it didn't pass at all and he looked at me over his own breakfast table the way you'd look at a woman come to read the meter."

The rain went on against the window.

"The last morning," Branwen said, "he couldn't hold his keys. Not couldn't find them — couldn't hold them. Picked them up off the hook and they went straight through his hand to the floor, like his hand had forgotten it was a hand. He looked at me and he was frightened, the only time, right at the end, and he said my name like a question. And then he went up to the back room for the still morning and he didn't come down, and there was no body, Eluned. That's the part they don't — there was no body. He just wasn't in the house, nor in the other one either, far as I could ever tell. He thinned until there wasn't enough of him left on either side to be anywhere." She looked up. "You've the same early hours in your face that he had. So I'm telling you. That's all I can do. I told nobody and I'd a husband to lose. You've a daughter. Mind that. You've a daughter down below who's still got all her edges and is going to want her mother to keep hers."

Chapter 18: Three Voicemails

Carys phoned three times that evening, and Eluned let the phone ring out on the dresser each time and listened to the voicemails afterward, standing in the dark kitchen, her thumb on the screen.

"Mum, it's me. Just checking in. You've gone a bit quiet. Ring me back, yeah?"

"Hi, it's me again. Look, I know it's a hard time, with Mamgu and everything, I just — I'd feel better if I heard your actual voice. Even just a text. Okay. Love you."

And then, later, shorter, with something underneath it that Eluned knew because she had put it there over twenty-seven years: "It's me. It's nearly eleven. Just — call me when you get this. Whenever. I don't care how late. Please."

Eluned stood with the phone warm in her hand and Branwen's voice in her head, you've a daughter down below who's still got all her edges, and she meant to ring. She composed the first sentence. She got as far as the green button. And then the house went very quiet around her, the particular stillness gathering, running downhill into the back room and standing, and she knew without going to the window that the wind had dropped and the morning would be a still one, and she set the phone down on the dresser, face up, the three missed calls glowing, and she went up the stairs to the still room to be ready for the light, and she told herself she would ring Carys after. She did not ring Carys after.

Chapter 19: Two of Her

She met the other Eluned on a morning she had not expected to.

She came through into the other kitchen and it was empty of Mair — gone to chapel, perhaps, or to Branwen's, the kettle just off the boil — and Eluned stood a moment in the warm and then heard a step on the other stairs, the wrong tread, not Mair's, and turned, and a woman came into the kitchen from the hall, and the woman was herself.

They stood and looked at each other across the rag rug. The other Eluned was fifty-two and was not: the same face, but weathered differently, browned across the nose from outdoor work, the hands stronger and the nails cut short and a man's old jumper on her over a work shirt. Her hair was longer and grey and tied back rough. She wore the green waxed coat — her mother's coat, except that here it was hers — and she stopped dead at the sight of Eluned and her face did the thing Eluned's own face must have been doing, the lurch, the recognition that refuses itself.

"Oh," the other Eluned said. Just that. And then, because one of them had to: "It's you, then. I wondered. Mam talks to the air sometimes by the back window. I thought she was going." A short laugh, dry, Mair's laugh, their laugh. "Going soft. But it's you she's talking to. You've been coming through."

"I'm sorry," Eluned said. "I didn't — I don't know the rules of it. I didn't know you'd—"

"Be here?" The other woman looked at her, not unkindly, taking the measure. "I'm always here. That's rather the point of me, isn't it. I'm the one who stayed."

Chapter 20: Have You Eaten

Neither of them knew what to call the other.

They could not both be Eluned, in the one kitchen, and there was no word for what they were to each other, closer than sisters and not sisters, the same root grown two ways. So they did not name it. They sat down at the table, the two of them, with the strangeness standing between them like a third person, and the other Eluned poured the tea because it was her kettle and her kitchen and her hand knew where the cups were without looking.

"You left," the other Eluned said. It was not an accusation. It was a woman confirming the geography. "Ninety-seven. Cardiff. The whole sky." She used the phrase as if it were common between them, which Eluned supposed it was; their mother had said it to both of them, in both worlds, the same words landing differently. "And I stayed. Married Tomos. Had Dafydd." A glance at the dark-eyed boy on the dresser. "Kept the orchard, kept the farm, kept Mam." She stopped. She turned her cup. "I don't envy you it, the sky. I used to. Long time ago. Then I stopped." She looked up, and her face was Eluned's face and the eyes in it had thirty years of a different weather behind them. "You look half-starved, mind. Whatever they feed you down there."

And then she said it, leaning back, entirely unconscious of it, in their mother's exact cadence, the question that had opened every visit of their whole shared childhood, the question that was love in the only grammar their family had ever owned:

"Have you eaten?"

And Eluned, who had crossed worlds and met her own self and seen her dead mother alive and held herself together through all of it, came apart quietly at the kitchen table over three small ordinary words, because they were her mother's and her own and they had been asked of her, at last, by someone, in a kitchen that smelled of bread.

Chapter 21: Every Still Morning

By the end of the second week she was crossing every morning the tide allowed, and there were more of them than the valley's six-a-year, because the tide did not keep the valley's books; it kept its own, and that autumn it ran high.

She arranged her life around the stillness. She went to bed at nine to be sure of waking at four. She kept her mother's eiderdown in the chair by the window so she would not be cold while she waited for the gold to come. She learned to read the night for it, the way the house hushed, the way the rush in the chimney died, and she was almost never wrong now, and when she was right she went through into the warm and stayed as long as the light would let her and came back emptied and full at once.

In her own world the post built up behind the door. The estate agent left three messages, then a letter, then a second letter on heavier paper. Rhian emailed twice, the second time only a line: Are you ok? Genuinely asking. The Catalan novel sat at four thousand words owed and not begun, the marriage in it ending in a language Eluned could still read but no longer felt much need to render into another. The skip Tomos had recommended was never hired. The furniture man was never called. The house she had come to empty filled instead, slowly, with the dust of her not-doing, and she moved through it lightly, on her way to the only room that mattered, and did not look hard at the rest.

Chapter 22: Damson

They made jam on a gold morning, she and her mother, and it was the most complete hour of Eluned's life, and that was how she knew it was dangerous.

The damsons had come on heavy in the other orchard, the kept one, and Mair had picked them the day before, and the kitchen when Eluned came through was already steam and sugar and the dark purple smell of the fruit going down in the big pan. Her mother put her to stoning, the old way, with the tip of a knife and a thumb, the stones into one bowl and the dark flesh into another, and Eluned's hands took up the rhythm they had learned at this table forty years before and never lost, and the two of them worked without much talk while the pan came to the boil and her mother skimmed the scum off pink into a saucer.

"Your nain made it tarter," Mair said. "Less sugar. I never had the patience for sour." She tested a blob on a cold plate, pushed it with her finger to see it wrinkle. "There. That's set." And they ladled it together into the warm jars, Mair holding, Eluned pouring, the way it took two people and always had, and they sealed the lids and stood the jars in a row on the windowsill, eight of them, dark as garnets against the gold morning light, and her mother looked at the row of jars with the plain satisfaction of a thing well made and said, "Now then. That's the winter sorted for sweetness," and Eluned looked at the eight jars she had helped fill in a world where her mother was alive and thought, with a lurch of pure animal wanting, I could stay for the winter. I could help eat the jam.

The light was already thinning at the window. She had stayed too long; she felt it, a faint drag behind the eyes, a thickness. She made herself go. At the window her mother said, "Take a jar," and she did, she took a jar of the other world's jam back through into the cold, and it was real in her hand, dark and heavy and sealed, and she could not afterward decide whether that was a comfort or the most frightening thing that had yet happened.

Chapter 23: The Name

In her own kitchen, that afternoon, with the jar of impossible jam on the table in front of her, she went to write the estate agent at last — to apologise, to make a date, to begin behaving like a woman selling a house — and she could not remember the estate agent's name.

She knew the office. Newtown, the one on the corner by the bank, green sign. She knew the woman's voice from the messages, brisk and young. She knew the name began with — she was almost sure it began with — and then it was gone, not on the tip of her tongue but simply absent, a hook with nothing on it, the way the word for a thing you have known your whole life will sometimes be just gone.

She found an old letter from the office in the pile by the door and read the name off the bottom — Hannah Pugh — and felt the relief of it land, of course, Hannah, obviously Hannah, and she wrote it on the back of her hand in biro so as not to lose it again. Then she could not be sure the relief was real, so she wrote it on the pad by the phone as well, Hannah Pugh, estate agent, Newtown, and then a third time, smaller, in the corner, pressing hard, as if pressing harder would fix it.

It was tiredness, she told herself. She was keeping early hours; Tomos had said as much; anyone who slept in two-hour pieces and woke at four would mislay a name. It was tiredness and grief and the strangeness of the autumn. She looked at the name written three times in her own hand and did not let herself think the other word for it, the word Branwen had used, the toll, by the inch, small things first.

Chapter 24: Supper at Tomos's

Tomos asked her to supper, and she went, partly out of loneliness and partly because some careful animal part of her knew she ought to be among the living, and his kitchen was warm and ordinary and entirely of this world, and that, for an evening, was a relief.

He cooked plainly — chops, potatoes, a cabbage from his own row — and they ate at the table where he had eaten alone for five years, and he asked her, gently, about the life she had lived away. Cardiff. The translating. The marriage. He listened the way he did everything, without hurry, refilling her glass, and she found herself telling him things, the shape of it, the years.

And the years would not quite come.

She could give him the headlines. She had married a man named Geraint, an architect; it had lasted nineteen years; it had ended without drama, by erosion. She had translated forty books. She had raised Carys, mostly well. But when Tomos asked her, idly, what she had done the summer Carys finished school, a particular summer, she reached for it and found a blur where the season should have been — she knew it had happened, she knew she had been there, but the texture of it, the actual days, would not assemble. It was like reaching into a drawer you know holds a thing and finding the drawer warm and empty.

"I don't — that's funny," she said, covering it the way Branwen had said Gareth covered it. "It's gone clean out of my head. I must be more tired than I thought."

Tomos looked at her across the chops, and he was a slow man but not a stupid one, and she saw him decide, kindly, not to ask again. "Happens to us all," he said, and passed her the potatoes, and changed the subject to the price of feed, and let her be, and she was grateful, and underneath the gratitude something cold turned over, because the summer Carys finished school was four years ago, not thirty, and it should not have been a blur, and it was.

Chapter 25: The Backs of Photographs

She could not sleep, and rather than wait at the window she went down and got the photograph albums out of the sideboard, the ones with the tissue between the pages, and sat up with them at the kitchen table.

Her mother had written on the backs of the loose ones, in pencil, in the small upright hand: Eluned, Sulgwyn 1971. Y ddwy ferch, 1968. The two girls. Herself and Rhian on the yard wall, squinting, scabbed at the knee. Her father with a lamb. The orchard in fruit, before it went. And among them, near the bottom of the box, an envelope, unsealed, addressed in her mother's hand to Eluned, Caerdydd — to herself, in Cardiff — and never sent, the stamp never stuck, a letter her mother had written and not posted, sometime, in some year of the long quiet between them.

She did not open it. She sat with it in her hand and the old argument came up in her the way damp comes up a wall, slow and total. The argument was thirty years old and had no single battlefield. It was the leaving itself. It was her mother's silence when she announced Cardiff — not anger, which Eluned could have fought, but a closing, a turning back to the sink, a "well, you'll do as you do," that had followed Eluned down the hill and onto the train and stayed in her for three decades like a stone swallowed. It was the visits that got shorter. It was the things neither of them said, the apology Eluned owed and would not pay because her mother would not ask, the apology her mother owed and would not pay because she did not believe she owed it. It was a whole life of love conducted in the negative, in the unsaid, in casseroles left on doorsteps and phone calls that ended too soon.

She held the unsent letter and did not open it, because the woman who had written it was alive, eight feet above her head, through a window, on the still mornings, and Eluned thought: I will not read it cold from a dead hand. I will ask her what it says.

Chapter 26: The Lamp After Dark

She had never crossed at night. The tide, Branwen had said, ran in the still mornings; but that evening, late, going up to bed with the unsent letter in her dressing-gown pocket, Eluned saw a light under the back-bedroom door, and went in, and the right pane was gold — not dawn-gold but lamp-gold, the warm low light of an evening kitchen — and through it her mother sat alone at the table with a bowl, and the window stood open.

It should not have been open after dark. Eluned understood, dimly, that this was the tide running wrong, running long, the way Branwen had warned it ran for those who pulled at it; that the more she crossed, the more the crossing offered itself, until the door that opened six mornings a year stood open for her whenever the wanting was strong enough. She understood it and she climbed through anyway, into the lamp-warm, because her mother was eating alone and there was a bowl already set at the second place as if she had been expected.

"Cawl," Mair said. "Sit. It's better the second day, this, you know that." She ladled it without asking, the leek and the lamb and the swede, the broth her mother had made every cold week of Eluned's childhood, and Eluned sat down in the lamplight and ate it, and it was exactly, exactly right, the taste going down into some root cellar of herself she had kept locked for thirty years, and the two of them ate together in the evening quiet of a kitchen in a world that should not exist, and Eluned felt the letter in her pocket against her side, and knew she was going to ask.

Chapter 27: The Whole Sky

"Mam," Eluned said, when the bowls were pushed back. "I found a letter. In the albums. One you wrote me and didn't send. To Cardiff."

Her mother went still, the ladle halfway to the pan. For a moment the lamplit kitchen held its breath.

"You weren't to find that," Mair said.

"I haven't read it. I wanted to ask you what's in it." Eluned's voice was steady and her hands were not. "We never said it, Mam. Any of it. I left and you turned to the sink and we did thirty years of casseroles and short phone calls and never once said the actual thing, and now—" she stopped herself before now you're dead, because here her mother was not, "—now I've a chance to, and I'm asking. What did the letter say."

Mair set the ladle down. She was quiet a long time, and when she spoke she did not look at Eluned but at the dark window, her own reflection, the two of them doubled in the black glass.

"It said I was sorry for the sink," she said at last. "For turning my back when you told me. You wanted me to fight you, I think, or to bless you, one or the other, and I couldn't do either, because if I fought you you'd have gone angry and if I blessed you you'd have gone for good, and I wanted—" Her voice caught, and held. "I wanted you to feel you could come back. So I said nothing, and nothing was the wrong thing, nothing was the worst thing, and I knew it while I was doing it and I did it anyway. That's what the letter said. I wrote it the week your father died and I never sent it because I thought, she'll think I only want her home for the help. So I kept it." She turned from the window. Her eyes were wet and her jaw was set in the old way. "You always wanted the whole sky, cariad. And I only ever had the one hill to give you, and I was too proud to say the hill would miss you. That's all it was. Thirty years of it, and that's all it ever was."

Chapter 28: The Empty Frame

Eluned came back through into the cold with her face wet and her chest cracked open and a kind of terrible peace in her, the peace of a thing finally said, and she did not at first notice the photograph.

It was the one on the dresser, the one that had grown a second, kept orchard behind her mother's shoulder. She passed it on her way to bed and something snagged her eye and she went back and picked it up and held it to the lamp.

Her mother was gone from it.

The orchard was there — the kept one now, fully, the fallen ghost-trees underneath it faded almost to nothing — and the light was there, a summer day, the same field, the trug of apples sitting in the grass where it had been set down. But the woman who had stood in front of it, her mother at sixty with her hand on the trug, was not in the frame. The photograph was of an empty orchard on a summer day, the apples picked by no one, the trug abandoned, and at the bottom, in her mother's pencil, the caption that had always been there: Yr ardd, a Mair. The orchard, and Mair. Except there was no Mair. There was only the garden, and a space where a woman had been, the grass very slightly flattened where she might have stood.

Eluned put the frame down with a hand that had gone cold. She understood what it meant and she made herself say it plainly in her own head, the way she made herself do everything now, one word at a time: the more I am over there, the less she is over here. I am pulling her through. Or I am pulling myself through. One of us is thinning out of this world, and I cannot any longer tell which, and that is exactly what Branwen said happened to Gareth at the end. He could not tell which.

Chapter 29: Twelve Days

The text from Carys came at quarter past nine, and Eluned read it standing in the dark kitchen with the cold frame still in her hand.

Mum, I haven't heard your voice in twelve days. I've rung 9 times. Tomos messaged Auntie Rhian that you seem "not yourself." I'm not angry, I'm scared. Please. Just tell me you're alright. One word.

Twelve days. Eluned read it twice. It did not feel like twelve days. It did not feel like any number of days; the days up here had stopped being countable, had run together into a single long still morning broken by gold crossings, and she would have said, if asked, that she had been at the farm perhaps a week. Twelve days. Carys had rung nine times. Tomos had messaged Rhian. The living world had been trying to reach her for twelve days and she had been making jam in another one.

She looked at the message and she meant to answer it. She got as far as the keyboard. One word, Carys had asked for, just one, alright, and it would have cost Eluned nothing, four letters and a daughter's mind eased, and she sat down at the table and put her thumbs to it and—

The house hushed. The chimney went quiet. Even now, even with the frame gone cold in front of her and her daughter's fear glowing on the screen, the still came down through the house and pooled in the back room, and Eluned felt the wanting rise in her like water rising in a well, and she set the phone down face up on the table with the message unanswered, and she told herself, the oldest lie now, worn smooth as a step, that she would answer it after, and she went up the stairs to the room with the two skies, and she did not answer it after.

Chapter 30: Which Sky

But the morning, when it came, was not a clear one.

She woke in the chair at four with the eiderdown round her and waited for the gold, and the gold did not come. The wind had not dropped. Rain came on instead, ordinary rain, ticking on the slates, and through both panes of the back window there was only the one hill, the one held-breath grey, the forestry black on the ridge, a single sky doing the single thing it did.

She sat and looked at the two panes showing her the same dull morning and she found she could not, for a long and frightening stretch of minutes, remember which of them was supposed to be the real one. Left or right. She knew that one pane was her own world and one was the other; she had known it for weeks; and now, tired, thinned, four in the morning, she looked from the left pane to the right pane and back and they were identical, two squares of grey hill, and the knowledge of which was which had gone the way the estate agent's name had gone, off its hook, and she sat there in genuine terror trying to reason it back — the right one opens, the right one is the door, so the left one must be — no, the door is the other, the door is — and could not be sure, could not be sure of the most basic geography of her own salvation, which sky was the one she had to live under and which was the one that would eat her if she let it.

The rain saved her. The rain meant no crossing, and no crossing meant a morning spent in her own world whether she chose it or not, and by full daylight, with coffee in her and the lamp on, the knowledge came back — the right pane, of course, the right is the door — and she could have wept with the relief of knowing her own house again. But she had had the warning now, plainly, in her own body. She had stood at the window and not known which sky was hers. Branwen's Gareth had ended at a window not knowing his own wife. She was not at the end. But she could see it now from where she stood, the shape of the end, and it was a still morning and an open window and a woman who could no longer tell which world she was supposed to want.

Chapter 31: At the Gate

Hannah Pugh came on the Tuesday, unannounced, because a fortnight of unanswered calls will bring an estate agent up a hill in person eventually, and Eluned met her at the gate and could not finish a sentence.

She heard the car and went down the yard, and she knew the young woman getting out of it was Hannah Pugh, the name written three times in her own hand, and she meant to say something ordinary and managing — so sorry, it's been a difficult few weeks, shall we go in — and the words came out of her in pieces that would not join. "You've come about the — for the — it's the house, isn't it, you've come, I've been meaning to, only with my mother, the, you know how it—" and she heard herself doing it, heard the sentences break off and not resume, and saw Hannah Pugh's professional smile tighten with something that was not impatience but a young person's alarm at an older one come adrift.

"Mrs Pryce, are you alright? You seem—"

"I'm tired," Eluned said, and that sentence at least came whole, because she had said it so many times it ran on rails now. "I'm so tired. Could we — another day. I'll ring you. I have your name." She held up the back of her hand, where Hannah Pugh was still faintly legible in old biro under three days of washing, and saw the young woman see it, and understood how it must look, a grey woman at a falling gate with a stranger's name written on her own skin, and did not care, because over the woman's shoulder, up under the eaves, the back window was catching a thin bar of light, and the wind was dropping, and Eluned wanted only for the car to be gone so that she could go up to it.

Chapter 32: Losing Time

Tomos found her on the gate two mornings later, sitting on the top bar in her nightdress and one wellington, at half past eight, with a frost on the ground.

He had come up to check the ewes and he saw her from the lane and he did not call out; he came up quietly the way you come up on a beast that might bolt, and he lifted her down off the gate, and she was cold through, her hands blue, her feet — one bare, one booted — without feeling, and she looked at him with a vague pleasantness, as if he had joined her at a bus stop.

"How long have you been out here, Eluned?"

"I came out to," she said, and stopped, and frowned. "I came out to feed the — was it the hens? There aren't any hens." She looked at her own bare foot with mild interest. "I've lost a boot."

He carried more than walked her into her own kitchen and set her by the range and lit it and put the kettle on and got her feet into a basin of warm water, swearing softly the whole time, not at her, and when she had a cup of tea in both blue hands and some colour coming back he crouched down in front of her and made her look at him.

"What day is it," he said.

She did not know. She could see him wanting her to know and she reached for it and there was nothing, no Tuesday, no Friday, no anchor, only the long still morning the days had all dissolved into. "It's—" she said. "It's autumn." And Tomos closed his eyes a moment, and opened them, and said, in his slow careful way, the thing nobody up here said: "You're losing time, Eluned. I've watched it three weeks. You're losing time the way the old people lose it, and you're fifty-two, and it's not natural, and I don't know what's doing it, but something is, and I'm going to ring your Carys, because you need someone here who isn't down a hill and turning left."

Chapter 33: One Bad Second

Carys came the next day, drove up from Cardiff in four hours flat, and Eluned opened the door to her own daughter and for one bad second did not know her.

It was the worst second of the whole autumn. A young woman stood on the step in a wet coat, dark-eyed, anxious, holding a bag, and Eluned's mind reached for her and produced — nothing — a stranger, a caller, the meter, and then the second turned over and broke and there she was, Carys, of course, Carys, her own girl, and Eluned's face must have shown the whole journey of it, the blank and the lurch and the late arrival of love, because Carys's own face, watching it happen, crumpled.

"You didn't know me," Carys said. She did not come in. She stood on the step in the rain with the truth of it between them. "Just then. At the door. You looked at me like — Mum, you looked at me like you'd never seen me before in your life."

"I knew you," Eluned said. "I know you. Cariad, of course I know you, come in out of the wet." And she did know her, now, completely, achingly; she could have listed every birthday, the chickenpox at six, the school in Cardiff, the year Carys wouldn't speak to her after the divorce, the slow climbing-back since — she had it all, now, at her fingertips, the whole archive of her daughter. But she had not had it for one second on the step, and they both knew it, and Carys came in past her into the cold dusty house her grandmother had died in, and put her bag down, and turned, and said, "What is going on up here. What is actually going on. And don't tell me you're tired."

Chapter 34: The Parlour

They had it out in the parlour, among the half-packed boxes, the brass clock wrapped in newspaper, the teeth in their tin.

Carys asked, and Eluned told her nothing, and the nothing sounded exactly like the lie it was, and Carys, who was twenty-seven and frightened and had driven four hours, did what frightened people do, which was get angry instead.

"You've been up here a month," Carys said. "You said a week. You stopped answering your phone. The house isn't packed, it's worse than when Mamgu was alive, there's a jar of jam on the table with no label and frost in the back bedroom and you were sitting on a gate in your nightie, Tomos said, in your nightie, Mum, and you won't tell me why, you're just — you've got that face on, that closed face, the one you do, the one you did the whole time after Dad—"

"Don't," Eluned said.

"—the one where you go somewhere I can't follow and you shut the door and you leave me on the outside of it," Carys said, the old wound opening clean, "which is what you have done my whole life, Mum, whenever it got hard, you go somewhere else, and I am so tired of standing outside the door of you knocking—"

And it was so exactly the shape of the old argument, her own argument with her own mother, the leaving, the closed back, the love conducted in the negative, handed down whole to the next pair of women in the next decade, that Eluned heard it land and could not answer it, because the answer was upstairs through a window, and she could not say to her daughter, I have found a door to a kitchen where my mother is alive and I am thinning out of this world to be in it, because it was mad, and because it was true, and because saying it would mean choosing, and she was not, yet, ready to choose.

Chapter 35: The Same in Your Face

Branwen came on the Thursday, as she did, and found Carys's car in the yard, and stopped in the doorway and looked at the two of them, mother and daughter at opposite ends of the cold parlour, and read the whole situation off them the way she read the weather off the hill.

She was kind to Carys. She had known her as a baby. She said the right things, sorry for your grief, you're the image of your nain, sit down, I'll not stop. And then she said, mild as anything, "Eluned, come and help me to the car, my hip's bad," which fooled no one, and Eluned went out with her into the yard, and Branwen took her arm — to lean, she said — and walked her out of Carys's hearing, and stopped, and turned, and her old face was not mild any more.

"Your girl drove four hours," Branwen said. "And you didn't know her at the door. Tomos told me. One second, he said, but a whole second." She tightened her grip on Eluned's arm. "I'm going to say the thing now that nobody said to me, and you're going to hate me for it, and I don't care. Gareth went so far in the end that he couldn't see me when he looked at me. His own wife. He'd look right at me and there'd be nobody home behind his eyes for me, because all the home in him had gone over to the other side, to the morning he wanted more than this one." She brought her face close. "It's the same in your face, cariad. Right now. This minute. The same thing that was in his. You looked at your own daughter on your own doorstep and for one whole second there was nobody home for her. And that is how it starts. Not how it ends. How it starts."

Chapter 36: Gareth's Morning

"You never told me what he wanted," Eluned said. "Over there. What morning Gareth wanted so much he'd give himself up for it."

Branwen leaned on the car. The rain had stopped; the hill steamed faintly where a weak sun touched it.

"We had a daughter," she said. "Before your time, or when you were small. Gwen. She died at four, of the meningitis, in a week, the way they did then, before you could do anything." She said it flatly, a grief worn down to bedrock over thirty years. "And Gareth never — you don't, do you. You don't get over the one that's small. He carried her his whole life. And then he found the back room, the still mornings, and he found a world through it where Gwen hadn't died. Where the week of the meningitis went the other way and she lived. She was grown, over there. Married. There were children — our grandchildren, that we never had on this side. And Gareth would go through and sit in a kitchen and be a grandfather, and then come back to me and our empty house, and you can see, can't you, you of all people can see now, how a man would choose the kitchen with the grandchildren over the house with the empty chair."

"How long," Eluned said.

"Six months, from the first crossing to the last. Six months to thin a strong man down to nothing. And the cruel joke of it—" Branwen pushed herself off the car. "The cruel joke is the daughter over there wasn't ours. She was theirs. The other Gareth's, the other Branwen's. He gave up the wife he had to go and be a grandfather to a girl who already had a father, who didn't need him, who never even knew which of the two old men at her table was the one that bled to death wanting her. That's what the back room sells you, Eluned. A life that belongs to somebody else, for the price of the one that's actually yours."

Chapter 37: What I Still Know

That night, alone, while Carys slept in Mair's old room with the door open to listen for her, Eluned sat at the kitchen table with a pad and a pen and tried to write down what she still knew of her own world.

She did it like an inventory, the way she had inventoried the house that first day, because an inventory was a thing she knew how to make. She wrote: Carys, born the 3rd of — and stopped. The third of. A March baby, she knew that, the daffodils, she had carried her home past the daffodils. The third of March. She wrote it and was not sure and left it. She wrote: Geraint, and tried to bring his face up, the man she had been married to for nineteen years, and got the architect's glasses and the shape of him and a particular grey jumper but not, quite, the face, not the eyes, the centre of it gone soft like the centre of a photograph left in the sun. She wrote: the road I grew up on — and this came, at least, this came clean and whole, because she was on it, she had never really left it, the single-track three miles above Llanbrynmair, and she wrote the name of it and felt steadier.

But the list frightened her more than it steadied her, because she could see, plainly, in her own hand, which things were going and which were holding. The farm held. The hill held. Her mother held — her mother was the most vivid thing in either world, gold-lit, alive, asking had she eaten. It was the down-below that was thinning. Geraint's face. Carys's birthday. The estate agent. The whole sky she had chosen at twenty-eight was fading out of her, pane by pane, and the one hill was getting brighter, and she understood at last, pen in hand, that this was not an accident of grief. This was the trade. The back room was taking her real life in payment and giving her the kept orchard in exchange, and she was, on the current terms, losing.

Chapter 38: Sit With Me

She had decided to say goodbye. That was the thing she told herself when she went up on the next still morning, while Carys still slept: she would go through one last time and tell her mother it had to stop, and latch the window, and come back and be a mother to the daughter she had downstairs. She had decided. She believed she had decided.

She went through into the gold, and the kitchen was warm, and Mair was at the table, and Eluned opened her mouth to begin the goodbye, and her mother looked up and said, "Oh, good, you're here. Sit with me a minute. Don't rush off. You always rush." And she patted the chair beside her, not across the table but beside her, close, and Eluned sat, because how do you not sit, and Mair did not make tea or set her to a task or talk; she just sat, in the gold morning, beside her daughter, with her hand over her daughter's hand on the table, the loose ring warm, and was quiet.

That was all. There was no reckoning to have; they had had it. There was no jam to make. There was just a mother and a daughter sitting together in the morning light with nothing that needed saying, the thing Eluned had wanted her whole life and never once had, this ordinary completeness, her mother's hand over hers and the kettle not even on, and Eluned felt the goodbye she had come to say dissolve in her like sugar in the scalding tea, and she did not say it, she could not say it, she sat in the gold with her mother's hand over hers and let the minutes go, and the light thinned and she did not rise, and it thinned further and she did not rise, sit with me a minute, and she sat, and she sat, and somewhere a very long way below her a daughter woke in a cold house to an open back door and an empty chair.

Chapter 39: On the Parlour Floor

She lost the whole day.

She knew nothing of it. Inside it was one long gold morning beside her mother, the hand over hers, the kettle never boiling, time gone soft and complete; outside, in the world that owned her, the hours of an entire day ran out one after another with no Eluned in them. Carys woke to the open door and the cold and her mother gone, and rang Tomos, and the two of them walked the fields in the rain calling, and checked the orchard and the barn and the lane down to the village, and rang Rhian, and rang, at last, the police, who said a grown woman missing eight hours on her own land was not yet a matter, give it till dark.

It was Carys who found her, at dusk, back in the house they had already searched, on the parlour floor between the half-packed boxes, cold, her eyes open, not knowing how she had come to be there or that a day had passed or that her daughter had spent it screaming her name into the wind. Carys got down on the floor with her and took her face in both hands and said Mum, Mum, look at me, it's me, and Eluned looked at the dark-eyed young woman holding her face and for a long terrible moment the gold morning was still in her eyes and the young woman was no one, was the meter, was a caller — and then it broke, again, late, but it broke, and Eluned came back into her own cold body on her own parlour floor and saw her daughter's terrified face an inch from hers and understood, all at once and completely, what she had nearly done, and what it would cost the girl holding her, and she began, finally, to be afraid of the right thing.

Chapter 40: All Night

Carys sat with her all night.

She got her mother up off the floor and into the warm by the range and into dry clothes and would not leave her, would not so much as go upstairs for a blanket without taking Eluned with her, holding her hand on the dark stairs like Eluned had once held hers. She made tea Eluned didn't drink. She talked, to keep them both there, about anything, about Cardiff, about her job, about a man she'd been seeing and stopped, about the daffodils she remembered being told she'd been carried home past, the third of March, was that right, Mum, the third? — and Eluned said yes, the third, and held to it, the third of March, the daffodils, her daughter's birthday, a fact in this world she would not let go of again.

And toward morning, with the window black and singular and no gold in it, Eluned told her. Not all of it, not the mechanics, but the shape: that there was a way the back room opened, on the still mornings, into a place where Mamgu was still alive; that she had been going there; that it took something from you each time, time, names, the edges of people; that she had got lost in it; that she had not known Carys at the door, twice now, and that this was the most frightening thing that had ever happened to her, more frightening than the door itself, because Carys was the thing she could not afford to lose the edges of.

Carys listened to all of it without once saying her mother was mad, which Eluned had braced for, and at the end she only held her mother's hand tighter and said, "Then we latch the window. Today. Together. And we go home." And Eluned, who had not been able to latch it alone, who had gone up four times to say goodbye and sat down instead, felt the difference of the word together go through her like the first warmth after the cold ditch, and knew that this, a living hand in hers in the black morning, was the only thing strong enough to pull against the gold.

Chapter 41: Just Glass

The next morning was clear and windless, a true still morning, the gold coming up clean off the east, the best tide of the autumn — and Eluned went up to the back room alone, before Carys woke, to prove something to herself.

She stood at the window. The right pane was already warming, the hill beginning to soften toward the other kitchen, and she could feel the pull of it in her chest, the old wanting, the gold and the warm and her mother at the table. She put her hand flat to the pane, the way she had a hundred times. And she did not press. She held her hand there against the warming glass and she made herself think the thing through to the end, plainly, one word at a time, the way she had learned.

Her mother was dead. Mair Pryce had died in a hospital bed in Aberystwyth on a Tuesday in September with Eluned holding her hand, and that was true, and it was the truest thing in either world, and no gold morning could make it not true. The woman through the window was alive, and was her mother, and was also not — was a mother who had a daughter who stayed, a mother whose grief and whose love had gone a different road, a complete and real woman who belonged to a complete and real life that was not Eluned's. Going through to her did not undo the death. It only postponed the grief, at compound interest, paid in names and days and the edges of a living daughter. You could not visit your way out of a loss. You could only either grieve it or be eaten by the refusing of it, and those were the two doors, and the gold one was the second.

She took her hand off the glass. The pull was still there; it did not go; she suspected it never would. But she did not press, and the morning thinned, and the kitchen folded back into hill, and it was just glass, two old violet panes in a swollen frame, and she stood and looked at the one grey hill through both of them and did not cross, and that not-crossing was the hardest thing she had done in thirty years, harder than leaving, harder than the funeral, and she had done it, once, and she would have to do it again tomorrow, and the day after, but she had done it once.

Chapter 42: The Telling

She woke Carys and brought her up to the back room in the full daylight, when the tide had ebbed and the window was safe, and told her the whole of it.

It came out halting and strange and she did not try to make it sound sane, because it wasn't, and pretending would only frighten Carys more. She showed her the window. She showed her the photograph from the dresser with the empty orchard and the flattened grass where a woman had stood. She showed her the jar of damson jam with no label, sealed, dark as a garnet, that neither of them had made in this world. She told her about the still mornings and the gold and the warm air and the smell of bread; about her own self in the other kitchen, browned from the work, the one who stayed; about the cawl and the peas and the jam; about the unsent letter and the reckoning and her mother's hand over hers with the kettle never boiling. She told her about Branwen's Gareth, and the six months, and the daughter who lived over there and belonged to someone else.

Carys listened to all of it sitting on the edge of the old bed, her face going through fear and disbelief and, slowly, something else, something Eluned recognised because she had felt it herself at that window — wanting. She watched her daughter look at the safe daytime glass and she saw the wanting wake in her, Mamgu, alive, and Eluned took her daughter's face in both hands the way Carys had taken hers on the parlour floor and said, "No. Listen to me. Do you see it in yourself, just now, the wanting? That's it. That's the whole of it. It doesn't feel like danger. It feels like love. That's why it's dangerous. Promise me you'll never come up here on a still morning. Promise me on the third of March."

"I promise," Carys said, frightened back to herself. "Mum. I promise. On the third of March."

Chapter 43: This World

They sat in the back room a long while, and then Carys asked the question that turned the whole autumn.

"What do you actually want," she said, "from this world. Not that one. I know what that one's got — Mamgu, and the orchard, and the version of you that didn't make a mess of things. I get it. I do." She looked at her mother steadily. "But this one. The real one. What do you want out of it? Because Mum, honestly, watching you this month — I don't think you've wanted anything from this world in years. Not since Dad. Maybe not since before. You translate other people's books and you lived in a flat you can't remember and you let me knock on the outside of you and you came up here to empty a house and instead you found a door out of your own life and you went straight through it. So." She didn't flinch. "What do you want. Here. Give me one thing."

And Eluned, who had not been asked that question by anyone, ever, who had not asked it of herself since she was twenty-eight on a train with two suitcases, sat in the cold room and reached for an answer and found, to her surprise, that she had one, and that it was small, and that it was entirely of this world.

"This," she said. "You. In a kitchen. The wrong tea." Her voice went. "I want to be in a kitchen with you and not be somewhere else behind my own eyes. I want to know you on the doorstep, every time, the first second, for the rest of my life. That's the one thing. I had it just now and I nearly traded it for a morning that belongs to a dead woman, and I want it back, and I want to not be the kind of mother you knock on. That's what I want from this world." She wiped her face with the heel of her hand. "It's not much, is it. Thirty years and that's the whole of my list."

"It's enough," Carys said, which was what her great-grandmother might have said, and her grandmother, the women of that hill, down all the years. "Mum. It's more than enough. It's the only thing on anyone's list, when you get down to it."

Chapter 44: The Top Field

In the afternoon she walked the top field with Tomos, because she needed an hour in the plain world among plain things, and he was the plainest good thing the hill had.

He had brought the dog and they walked the wall line while the dog quartered the rushes, and he asked after Carys and after her, and she told him, in part — that she'd been unwell in a way she didn't fully understand, that Carys was staying, that she was better. He took it the way he took weather. And then, at the top wall, looking down the whole drop of the valley to the grey village and the river, he said, not looking at her, "The back room. You want to keep out of it, on the still mornings." It was not a question. "My Sian wouldn't have it for the children. Your mam slept at the front sixty years. Branwen's man—" He stopped. "The valley knows about that room, Eluned. Knows and doesn't say. The way it knows where the bad ground is on the mountain, where the bog'll take a tractor. You don't put a fence round it and you don't talk about it. You just know, and you keep off, and you tell the ones that don't know to keep off too." He looked at her then. "I should have told you the first week. I thought — sell up, gone by Christmas, no harm. I'm sorry. I should have told you to keep off."

"You did," she said. "In your way. The still room. You told me its name."

He almost smiled. They stood at the wall and watched the light come and go on the far side of the valley, the cloud-shadows running over the hill, the river catching it silver where it bent, and there was nothing of the gold in it, nothing of the other world, only this one, wet and grey and moving and entirely real, and Eluned found that she could stand and look at it and want it, the actual hill, the actual weather, and that wanting the real thing was a muscle she had not used in thirty years and that it ached, using it, the way the leg aches that you finally stand on after a long time off it.

Chapter 45: A True Goodbye

She crossed one last time, and she did it the right way, which was the only safe way: she did it with a hand to hold on to.

Carys would not let her go at all, at first; they had a hard hour about it; but Eluned was certain, and in the end they made it as safe as such a thing could be made. Carys sat on the floor of the back room with her back to the wall and both hands locked around her mother's left wrist, braced, and Branwen came and sat in the doorway with her watch, because Branwen knew the tides and had timed a man's crossings before and failed to pull him back and would not fail twice. Ten minutes, they agreed. Branwen would call it at ten, and Carys would pull, and Eluned would come, no matter what, no matter who was at the table or what was being said.

Eluned leaned through into the gold with her daughter's grip hard around her wrist anchoring her to the cold floor of the real world, and her mother was at the table.

"I've come to say goodbye, Mam," Eluned said, and this time she said it, the words came, because there was a living hand around her wrist and a clock running and a daughter on the floor breathing hard, and the saying of it did not dissolve. "I can't come again. It's taking me from Carys. You'd not want that. You of all people. You held your back to me for thirty years so I'd feel I could come home, and I'll not let your being here cost me my own girl, you'd never forgive me it, you'd be ashamed of me."

Mair looked at her a long moment, and her face was not surprised, and Eluned understood that this mother, whoever and whatever she was, had always known the visits would end, had perhaps always known what they were. "No," Mair said. "I'd not want that. Go to your girl." She reached and put her warm hand once more against Eluned's cheek, the loose ring cool against the skin, and said, "There's my girl," and then, dry, herself to the last, "and eat something, for the love of God, you're all edges," and Eluned laughed and wept at once, and from the doorway Branwen said, low and steady, "That's nine, cariad," and Carys's grip began to draw her back.

Chapter 46: Go Home

The other Eluned came to the kitchen door as the light began to thin.

She had been out at the orchard; she came in with the work on her, the browned hands, the green coat, and she stopped in the doorway and looked at the two of them, the mother at the table and the half-there woman leaning in out of the cold, and she understood the whole of it in a glance, because she was, after all, Eluned, and Eluned understood things in glances.

"You're ending it," the other Eluned said. "Good."

"I'm sorry," Eluned said. "For coming through. For—"

"Don't." The other woman came and stood by her mother's chair and put a hand on Mair's shoulder, a small claiming gesture, this is mine, this kitchen, this morning, this woman, and it was not cruel, it was only true. "She's mine to keep and to lose, in the end, same as yours was yours. You don't get to grieve her twice by borrowing mine. It's not how it works. It's not fair on her and it's not fair on you and it's costing that girl on the floor her mother." She looked at Eluned, her own face, weathered the other way, steady. "Go home. You've got a daughter holding your wrist. I'd give a great deal for a daughter holding my wrist; I've a son who'll be off to Aberystwyth in a year and gone, and that's my grief coming, the ordinary kind, and I'll grieve it in my own world and not go looking through any window for a boy who stayed. Go home, Eluned. Live in your one. It's enough. It has to be. It's all anyone gets."

Go home. It was not unkind. It was exact. And from a long way below, in the cold, Branwen said "Ten," and Carys pulled, hard, both hands, her whole weight, and Eluned let go of the table and let herself be drawn back, back through the thinning gold, her eyes on her mother and her self and the kept kitchen to the last, until the window closed them off and there was only the cold room and the grey light and her daughter's arms hard around her, the two of them gone over backward onto the floorboards in a heap, breathing.

Chapter 47: The Latch

She latched the window herself.

It was a small thing and it had been beyond her for a month. The latch was an old iron catch, painted over, stiff; she had to work it loose with the edge of a knife, the paint flaking, while Carys and Branwen watched from the doorway and did not help, because they understood, both of them, that this had to be her own two hands. She scraped the catch clean and she turned it down into the keep and it bit home with a small iron sound, final, and the right pane was just a pane now, swollen shut and latched besides, double-shut, a window like any window, looking out on the one wet hill.

And then the thing she had not been able to do for the whole autumn, the thing she had crossed worlds to avoid, came up in her all at once with the latch turned and no door left to go through: she grieved. She sat down on the floor under the latched window and she cried for her mother — not the gold mother of the other kitchen, alive and asking had she eaten, but Mair Pryce of this world, who had died holding her hand in Aberystwyth, whose green coat was on the hook downstairs, whose handwriting was on the backs of the photographs, whose unsent letter was in the sideboard, who had turned her back at the sink thirty years ago because she loved her daughter too much and too clumsily to do anything else. She cried the way she had not been able to cry at the chapel or the graveside, the way the broken cup had only started and the gold mornings had stopped up — the whole of it, at last, on the floor of the still room, with the window latched, in the real and only world, for the dead and only mother, who was gone, who was actually gone, and would not be at any table, behind any glass, ever again.

Carys came and sat down on the floor beside her and did not try to stop it. She just stayed, with her arm around her mother, and let her cry it all the way out, which was the one thing that had been needed all along and that no window had been able to give.

Chapter 48: Now You Listen

When it had run its course, the two of them stayed on the floor of the back room, spent, leaning on each other, while the grey afternoon failed in the window.

And then Carys began, quietly, to talk. Not about the window or the grief or any of it. About herself. About the man she'd been seeing and why she'd ended it. About the job that wasn't what she'd wanted and the city that wore her down and the flat she couldn't afford and the way she'd felt, this past year, more than once, the same closing-of-the-door her mother did, the same going-somewhere-no-one-could-follow, and how it had frightened her to feel it, because she'd watched her mother do it her whole life and sworn she never would. She talked, the way a daughter talks when she has finally got her mother in a room with all the doors shut and nowhere else to be.

And Eluned listened.

That was the whole of it, and it was everything: she listened. She did not fix it or close herself off from it or drift somewhere gold behind her own eyes. She sat on the cold floor with her grown daughter and she listened to her, completely, present, every word landing, and when Carys finished she did not offer an answer, because there wasn't one needed; she only said, "I'm here. I'm not going anywhere behind my eyes. Tell me the rest," and Carys, who had spent a lifetime knocking on the outside of her mother, found the door open for once, and went in, and they sat in the dark of the still room with no light on, two women on a floor, talking, which they had not done in twenty years, and which was worth, Eluned thought, more than every gold morning the window had ever sold her.

Chapter 49: I'm Not Selling

She rang Hannah Pugh on the Monday and apologised, properly, in whole sentences, the name no longer needing to be written on her hand.

And then, the next day, she rang her back, because in between she had decided, and she said: "I'm not going to sell after all. The farm. I'm sorry to waste your time on the valuation. I'm going to keep it." And Hannah Pugh, professional, a little wary of the grey woman with the name on her hand, said of course, these decisions were hard after a bereavement, take all the time she needed, the offer of a valuation stood whenever — and Eluned said, no, you misunderstand, I'm not undecided. I'm deciding. I'm going to live here. I'm going to move up properly and live in it.

She meant it, and it was not the old wanting in another shape, and she had tested it against herself hard before she lifted the phone, because she of all people knew how a longing could dress itself as a choice. But this was not a retreat into the hill the way the window had been a retreat into the gold. She was not coming to the farm to escape her life. She was coming to it to have one. There was the orchard to bring back — the real orchard, the fallen one, two trees to clear and four to prune, years of work, this-world work, with this-world hands. There was Tomos down the hill and turning left, in no hurry, and time, maybe, to see what that was, slowly, like everything up here. There was room for Carys to come at weekends and learn the hill, if she wanted it, and bring whatever life she made. There was the Catalan novel to finish, owed and overdue, four thousand words of a marriage ending that Eluned found, now, she could feel again, and render into English, and send. There was a green coat on a hook that she would, this week, take down at last, and wear, to feed hens she would buy in the spring.

Chapter 50: One Sky

A morning in spring, windless and clear, the kind of still morning the tide would once have used.

Eluned was out in the top field at first light, feeding the half-dozen ewes Tomos had lent her to start a flock, the grass coming green and the hill loud with larks going up, and the orchard behind the house showing the first hard buds on the four pruned trees, the two dead ones cleared and burned over the winter, the ground turned for new whips in the autumn. She wore the green coat. The elbows were still cracked; she had not mended them; she rather liked them cracked.

Through the kitchen window she could see Carys, up for the weekend, making tea — too strong, the bag left in, the way she had always made it and the way Eluned had given up correcting — and the lamp was on against the early gold, and it looked, from the field, very like another kitchen Eluned had known, a warm window in a grey morning, a woman at the range. But it was this one. It was her own kitchen and her own daughter and her own one life, and there was only the one sky over the valley, doing what the spring sky did, the larks in it and a buzzard going up off the forestry on the ridge that the other world had never grown.

The back window was still there, under the eaves. Latched. She had not boarded it or replaced it; that would have been its own kind of haunting, treating it as a danger to be sealed rather than a fact to be lived alongside. It was just a window now, swollen shut and latched besides, and on the still mornings, sometimes, if she happened to be up there, she could feel the faint old pull of it, the gold wanting to come, and she let it come and let it go, the way you let an old grief come and go, without crossing, without latching her heart against it either, just a woman standing at a window in the world she had, watching the one hill under the one sky, with her tea going cold the way her daughter made it, called down at last to breakfast in a voice she knew, every morning, the first second, by heart.
