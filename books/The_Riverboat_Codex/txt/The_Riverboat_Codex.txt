Chapter 1: The Safety Deposit Box

The safety deposit box had not been opened in thirty-two years.

Roland Devereaux watched the bank manager insert the master key, his arthritic fingers tapping against the worn leather of his briefcase. The lock cylinders turned with a sound like grinding gravel, and the box slid from its cradle in the vault wall.

"Would you like a private viewing room, Mr. Devereaux?" the manager asked.

"That would be fine."

The room was small and windowless, paneled in wood that had yellowed with decades of cigar smoke before the bank went smoke-free in the nineties. Rollie sat down heavily and placed the box on the table in front of him. It was longer than a shoebox and narrower, painted battleship gray, with a stenciled number on the end: 4117.

Felix Beaumont had last opened this box in 1992. Three months later, he was dead. Heart attack, the coroner said. Rollie had never believed it.

He slid his thumb across the cold metal lid. The hinges resisted at first, then gave way with a soft groan.

Inside lay a thick manila envelope, yellowed at the edges, and beneath it, a velvet pouch the color of burgundy wine. Rollie lifted the envelope first. It was heavy with documents—deeds, stock certificates, a last will and testament. He set it aside.

The velvet pouch was lighter than he expected. He loosened the drawstring and tipped the contents onto the table.

Seven poker chips spilled across the polished wood.

They were old—genuine clay composition chips from the 1920s, the kind used in the private gambling halls of Prohibition-era New Orleans. Each was an inch and a half in diameter, weighted with a metal slug at the center. Three were white, two red, one blue, and one a faded green that had nearly gone gray.

But it was not their colors that made Rollie's breath catch.

Every chip bore a mark on its face. Not a casino stamp or a denomination—those would have been familiar. These were symbols. Geometric shapes arranged in patterns: concentric circles, bisected triangles, arrows within squares, and combinations that seemed to follow a logic he could not grasp.

He turned one over. The reverse side was smooth, unmarked, except for a single letter impressed into the clay at the very edge: F.

Felix.

Rollie had spent forty years dealing cards on the Mississippi riverboats. He had seen every kind of gambling token, counterfeit chip, and marking system in existence. He had never seen anything like these.

He gathered the chips carefully and returned them to the pouch. The documents went back into the envelope. He closed the box and slid it across the table.

His hands were shaking as he signed the visitor log.

Outside the bank, the Louisiana sun was merciless. Rollie stood on the sidewalk, the briefcase heavy in his hand, and felt the weight of a decision he had been avoiding for three decades. Felix had told him, once, about the Codex. A ledger that recorded every fixed game, every police payoff, every political bribe that had lubricated the New Orleans gambling underworld between 1920 and 1933.

A book that powerful could destroy families. It could topple institutions. It could resurrect a murder investigation that had gone cold in 1993.

Or it could end up at the bottom of the Mississippi, where Felix had always said it belonged.

Rollie took out his phone and dialed the number for Vance & Associates Investigations.

Chapter 2: The Invitation

The offices of Vance & Associates occupied the second floor of a peeling Victorian house on North Boulevard in Baton Rouge, a building that had once been a doctor's office and still smelled faintly of antiseptic and old paper. Lila Vance sat at her desk, staring at a spreadsheet of credit card transactions that belonged to a cheating husband in Denham Springs, and tried to remember when her life had gone so wrong.

Eighteen months earlier, she had been Dr. Lila Vance, associate professor of mathematics at Tulane University, with a specialty in recreational combinatorics and cipher history. Her paper on the application of combinatorial group theory to classical cipher systems had earned her a consulting contract with the National Security Agency. It should have been the pinnacle of her career.

The contract had lasted six months. Then came the disagreement—classified, never discussed, but sufficient to end her security clearance, her university affiliation, and her marriage in the same devastating quarter. The official story was "mutual separation." The truth was that she had found something the NSA did not want found, and she had refused to unsee it. The details were locked in a classified file somewhere in Fort Meade, but the consequence was simple: she had been erased from the world of serious mathematics as thoroughly as if she had never existed.

Now she sat in a cramped office with a broken air conditioner that wheezed and rattled but produced only damp, lukewarm air, tracing the spending patterns of unfaithful husbands and embezzling junior partners. Her boss, a retired state trooper named Vance—no relation, just a coincidence of the firm's name—gave her the cases no one else wanted. Background checks on prospective employees. Credit card forensics for divorce lawyers. The occasional missing person who had skipped out on child support.

She had solved the Denham Springs case in the first hour. The husband was staying at a Motel 6 on Airline Highway, charging meals at a po'boy shop and a gas station two blocks away. The pattern was so obvious it was almost insulting. She had been delaying the report because she dreaded the look on the wife's face when the evidence was served.

The phone rang.

"Vance & Associates, Lila speaking."

"Is this Dr. Vance?" The voice was old, Southern, with the deliberate cadence of someone who had spent decades speaking over the shuffle of cards and the clink of ice. It was a voice that had told stories in smoky rooms, that had called the turn on endless riverboat nights.

"This is she."

"My name is Roland Devereaux. I'm told you're the best cipher person in the state."

Lila's pen stopped moving. "Who told you that?"

"An old friend at the Louisiana State Archive. Elise Delacroix. She said if anyone could make sense of a puzzle, it would be you."

Lila had not spoken to Elise in six months—not since the NSA debacle made her a pariah in academic circles. But Elise had always been loyal. She had been the one person at the university who still returned Lila's calls, the one who had recommended her for the PI job when the academic world slammed its doors.

"What kind of puzzle, Mr. Devereaux?"

"I'd rather show you than tell you. It involves poker chips. Seven of them. They belonged to a man named Felix Beaumont, who died in 1992 under circumstances I've never fully believed."

The name meant nothing to Lila. But the word "cipher" had awakened something in her chest that she had been trying to keep dormant. A hunger she had thought was dead flickered back to life—the same hunger that had driven her to spend sixteen hours a day in the mathematics library at MIT, the same hunger that had made her a rising star in a male-dominated field.

"I'm not a full-time investigator," she said carefully. "I mostly handle background checks and surveillance reports for—"

"I know what you handle. I also know what you used to do. You taught at Tulane. You worked for the government. You know codes."

Silence stretched between them. Lila looked at the worn photograph on her desk—herself at a conference in 2019, standing next to a whiteboard covered in equations, looking confident and triumphant. The woman in that photograph was a stranger now.

"I'm prepared to pay your firm's hourly rate," Rollie continued. "I'm also prepared to tell you a story that might make you forget about cheating husbands for a while."

Lila looked at the spreadsheet on her screen. Fifty-three transactions at a hotel on Airline Highway. The pattern was unmistakable. She would write the report tomorrow. The wife had waited two months; one more day would not matter.

She saved the file and closed her laptop.

"When can you come in, Mr. Devereaux?"

"I'm in the parking lot."

Lila stood and walked to the window. Below, an elderly cream-colored Cadillac was idling in the gravel lot, exhaust shimmering in the heat like a mirage. An old man in a linen suit stood beside it, holding a briefcase, looking up at her window. His posture was straight despite his age, the posture of a man who had spent a lifetime standing behind a card table, watching the players, reading their tells.

He raised his free hand in a slow wave.

"Give me two minutes," she said.

She hung up and straightened her blouse, ran a hand through her hair, and felt something she had not felt in eighteen months: the anticipation of a puzzle worth solving.

Chapter 3: A Deck of Lies

Rollie set the velvet pouch on Lila's desk with the reverence of a man handling relics. The burgundy fabric was worn at the corners, the drawstring frayed from decades of use. He loosened the cord and tipped the contents onto a sheet of white paper she had spread across her desk.

Seven poker chips clattered softly and lay still.

Lila did not touch them immediately. She observed, the way she observed everything—with the patience of someone trained to see patterns where others saw chaos. She noted the faded colors, the fine crackle pattern in the clay, the edges worn smooth from years of handling. These chips had been passed between fingers, stacked and restacked, used in games where fortunes changed on the turn of a card.

The symbols impressed into the faces were something else entirely. They had not been worn smooth. The impressions were deep, deliberate—stamped into the clay during manufacture, which meant they were part of the chip's original design, not markings added later.

"Seven chips," Rollie said. "Felix Beaumont was a dealer on the riverboats from 1955 until he retired in 1988. He was also a collector. Antique gaming equipment, mostly. He had chips from the Steamboat Springs Casino in Reno, plaques from the old Havana gambling clubs, cards from Monte Carlo. But these—these were different."

"How so?"

"Felix never let anyone touch them. He kept them in a locked drawer in his study, wrapped in this same velvet pouch. He told me once that they were the most valuable things he owned, not because of what they were worth, but because of what they meant."

"And what did they mean?"

"He wouldn't say exactly. But he told me stories. About a riverboat called the Belle Fleur, about a man named Oliver Bouchard, about a ledger that could bring down half the government of New Orleans."

Lila picked up the white chip with the concentric circles. It was heavier than she expected, the weight of the metal slug inside the clay making it substantial in her palm. The craftsmanship was exquisite—each ring of the symbol was precisely spaced, the lines deep and clean, the edges crisp.

"The Riverboat Codex," she said. "I looked it up after you called. There's nothing in the academic literature. A few mentions in collector forums, some speculative blog posts. Most people think it's a legend."

"Most people aren't Felix Beaumont." Rollie settled back in the chair, his eyes growing distant, as if he were looking at something far away in time. "In 1920, New Orleans was wide open. The mayor, the police superintendent, the chief criminal judge—they were all on the take. Gambling was illegal, but there were sixty-seven gambling halls operating in the French Quarter alone. Every one of them paid protection money."

"I know the history," Lila said. "Prohibition created a parallel economy. Gambling was part of it."

"Yes, but here's what the history books don't tell you. The man who ran the gambling operation—the man who decided who got a license and who got a bullet—wasn't a mobster from Chicago or New York. He was a local businessman. Name of Oliver Bouchard. He ran his operation from a riverboat called the Belle Fleur, moored right at the foot of Canal Street. On the surface, she was a passenger steamer. Below decks, she was the most luxurious floating casino the South had ever seen."

Lila's pen was moving now, taking notes in the precise shorthand she had developed during her NSA days.

"Bouchard kept a ledger," Rollie continued. "A book that recorded every single payment he made, every bribe, every politician on his payroll, every judge he owned, every fix he ran. The Belle Fleur operated for thirteen years, from 1920 to 1933. That ledger covered all of it. When Prohibition ended in 1933 and the state started licensing legal gambling, Bouchard disappeared. He liquidated his assets, dissolved his operation, and vanished from public life. But the ledger survived."

"The Codex."

"Felix found it. Or found evidence of it. He spent thirty years trying to locate the full document. These chips were part of that search. They're a map, Dr. Vance. A cipher that leads to the Codex."

Lila looked at the seven chips laid out before her. Seven symbols. Seven potential pieces of a puzzle. The arrangement was deliberate—she could feel it in the same way she could feel a mathematical proof forming in the back of her mind, all the pieces waiting to click into place.

"Assuming this is real," she said carefully, "why now? Why after thirty-two years? You've had these chips for three decades. What changed?"

Rollie's face tightened, the lines around his eyes deepening. "Because I'm eighty-three years old, Dr. Vance. And because someone broke into my house last week. They didn't take the television or the silver or the cash I keep in the cookie jar. They went through my study, pulled out every book, emptied every drawer, ripped the lining out of my desk. They were looking for something specific."

"Did you call the police?"

"I called you instead. The police in my neighborhood don't investigate break-ins by unknown parties. They take a report, file it, and move on. But I think you will."

Lila studied the old man's face. He was hiding something—she could feel it, a reservation behind his eyes, a careful weighing of every word. But he was also telling the truth about the chips. The symbols on them were deliberate, intentional, and unquestionably cryptographic. She could already see the mathematical structure beneath the geometry, the ratios and proportions that marked deliberate design rather than decoration.

"Let me photograph these," she said. "I'll need a few days of research before I can tell you anything about the cipher. Possibly longer. Symbol systems like this don't yield their secrets overnight."

"Take all the time you need. But keep them safe. If Felix was right about what the Codex contains, there are people who would burn this city down to keep it hidden. And they've already proven they're not afraid to break into houses."

Chapter 4: The Seventh Chip

Lila worked through the night.

She had photographed each chip from both sides, under multiple lighting conditions, and loaded the images into her reference software. The symbols were clearly hand-engraved into the master die before the chips were pressed—a fact that suggested premeditation and significant resources.

By three in the morning, she had identified four distinct symbol families:

1. Concentric circles with varying numbers of rings (three chips)
2. Bisected triangles with internal hash marks (two chips)
3. Interlocking squares with directional arrows (one chip)
4. A spiral pattern with dots along the curve (one chip)

The spiral pattern appeared on the seventh chip—the green one, which was noticeably more worn than the others, as if it had been handled more frequently or was older.

She cross-referenced the symbols against known cipher systems: Pigpen cipher, Freemason's cipher, the Zodiac killer's symbols, various alchemical notation systems. Nothing matched perfectly. The concentric circles were reminiscent of compass rose markings, but the number of rings varied—three rings on one chip, five on another, seven on the third. The hash marks on the bisected triangles appeared to be numerical—one chip had four marks, the other had nine.

The interlocking squares looked architectural, almost like a floor plan viewed from above. The spiral was the most intriguing. Dots along a logarithmic curve could indicate a sequence—perhaps a page number or an ordering key.

She was reaching for her coffee when she noticed something she had missed.

On the blue chip—the one with the interlocking squares—there was a faint impression near the edge. Almost invisible, as if someone had tried to smooth it away. She magnified the image on her screen.

It was a letter. Not the F that appeared on all the chips as Felix's initial. A different letter, stamped so lightly it was barely perceptible.

V.

No. Not V. An inverted V. A caret.

Someone had marked this chip after manufacture. A secondary marking, invisible to casual inspection but intended to be found.

Lila sat back, her mind racing. Seven chips. Seven symbols. But the seventh chip was different—older, more worn, with a symbol in a different class. And now a caret mark, added later, as if to denote something important.

What if there was an eighth chip?

The thought struck her with the force of certainty. Seven chips visible. Seven symbols. But the legend of the Codex had always described eight guardians, eight keys. Someone had removed one chip before the set was placed in the safety deposit box.

Someone, perhaps, who had known Felix Beaumont better than Rollie was willing to admit.

She picked up her phone and dialed Rollie's number. It was three-fifteen in the morning. He answered on the second ring, his voice clear and alert, as if he had been waiting for her call.

"Dr. Vance."

"Mr. Devereaux. How many chips did Felix actually have?"

A long pause.

"Seven that I found," he said. "There may have been an eighth."

"May have been?"

"Felix told me once that the complete set numbered eight. One for each of the original investors in the Belle Fleur. But when I went through his estate, I only found seven."

"Where did Felix keep his personal effects before he died?"

"His house in Algiers Point. But it was sold years ago. Everything went to storage, then to auction."

"Who handled the auction?"

A longer pause this time. When Rollie spoke again, his voice was careful. "A firm in the French Quarter. Maison d'Antiquités. Owned by a man named Sylvain Mercier."

"Does Mercier know about the chips?"

"Everyone in the antique gambling trade knows about the chips. Felix was notorious. He talked too much when he drank, and he drank too much after his wife died. There are probably twenty people in New Orleans who have heard the story of the Codex."

Lila looked at the image of the seventh chip on her screen, at the faint caret mark that someone had tried to erase.

"Then I need to visit the French Quarter," she said. "Sylvain Mercier may have answered some questions thirty years ago without knowing he was being asked."

Chapter 5: The 1993 File

Detective Marcus Thibodeau had been a cold case investigator for eleven years, and in all that time, he had never received a call from a cryptanalyst. Usually his phone rang with tips from aging witnesses whose memories had suddenly sharpened, or from criminals looking to trade information for reduced sentences. A mathematician who specialized in codes was something new.

"The name is Dr. Lila Vance," the woman on the phone had said. "I'm working a case that may intersect with an old NOPD file. A homicide from 1993 at the Belle Fleur casino."

Marcus had pulled the file within the hour. He knew the Belle Fleur case without looking it up—some murders stayed with you even when the case went cold, when the photographs faded and the witnesses moved away or died. The Marchand case was one of those.

The victim was a man named Edward "Eddie" Marchand, thirty-four years old, a card mechanic and known cheat who worked the riverboat circuit. He had been arrested three times for various gambling offenses but had never served more than sixty days. He was small-time, a hustler who worked the edges of the casino world, skimming from pots and marking cards when he thought no one was watching.

On the night of September 12, 1993, Eddie had been found behind the Belle Fleur's mooring station, beaten to death with what the coroner described as "a blunt instrument consistent with a baseball bat or lead pipe." No witnesses. No physical evidence beyond the body itself. The security cameras near the mooring station had been "undergoing maintenance" that night.

Marcus spread the photographs across his desk. Eddie Marchand lay on his back, one arm thrown over his head as if he had been trying to ward off the blows, his face a ruin of dried blood and fractures. He was wearing a dealer's vest and slacks, but no shoes—one of the odd details that had always bothered Marcus. Where were his shoes? They had never been found, never been explained. The lead investigator in 1993 had theorized that Eddie had removed them to wade into the river for some reason, but there was no evidence he had been in the water.

He picked up his phone and dialed the number Lila had given him.

"Dr. Vance, this is Detective Thibodeau. I have the 1993 file in front of me. What's your connection to this case?"

"I don't know yet," Lila admitted. "I'm examining a set of antique poker chips that belonged to a man named Felix Beaumont. His protégé hired me to decode the markings on them. During my preliminary research, I found that Beaumont was questioned by NOPD after the Marchand homicide."

Marcus flipped through the file. Toward the back, there was a witness statement from Felix Beaumont, dated September 14, 1993. He had been interviewed because he was Eddie Marchand's regular supervisor at the Belle Fleur, the man who had trained him, who had vouched for his employment despite his record.

"Beaumont said Marchand hadn't shown up for his shift on the night of the murder," Marcus said. "Gave him an alibi for the window. But he also said Marchand had been acting strange for weeks. Nervous. Looking over his shoulder. Jumping at shadows."

"In what context?"

"Beaumont thought Marchand was into something illegal—beyond the standard card cheating, I mean. He said Marchand had been asking questions about the history of the Belle Fleur. About Prohibition-era gambling. About something he called 'the ledger.'"

Lila's breath caught. "The Riverboat Codex."

"Excuse me?"

"I'll explain when I see you. Detective, I think Eddie Marchand was killed because he found something he wasn't supposed to find. And I think Felix Beaumont knew what it was and spent the rest of his life trying to locate it."

Marcus closed the file and looked at the photograph on top—Eddie Marchand's face, frozen in the moment of his death. Thirty years of unsolved homicides had taught him to be skeptical of grand conspiracies, of theories that linked one death to another in elaborate patterns. Usually, people were killed for simple reasons: money, jealousy, revenge in the heat of a moment.

But something about this case had always felt different. The missing shoes. The disabled cameras. The way the investigation had hit a wall at every turn.

"Tell me where to meet you," he said. "I want to hear the whole story."

Chapter 6: The French Quarter Dealer

Maison d'Antiquités occupied a narrow storefront on Royal Street, wedged between a voodoo shop and a praline bakery. The windows were dark, the signage faded to the color of old bone. Lila pushed open the door and was immediately enveloped by the smell of aged wood, cigar smoke, and furniture polish.

Sylvain Mercier was a thin man in his seventies, with wire-rimmed glasses and the pinched expression of someone who had spent a lifetime appraising other people's treasures. He looked up from his desk as Lila entered, cataloging her with a single practiced glance.

"Dr. Vance. Rollie called ahead. He said you might visit."

"He did?"

"Rollie and I go back forty years. He trusts me. I'm not sure that's wise, but it's true." Mercier gestured to a chair. "Sit. Tell me what you need."

Lila laid a photograph of one of the chips on the desk. "Do you recognize this?"

Mercier picked up the photograph, adjusted his glasses, and studied it in silence. His expression did not change. "I do. Felix Beaumont showed me a similar chip in 1990. He was trying to identify its origin."

"Did you help him?"

"I tried. I showed him my records of Prohibition-era casino chips, gambling tokens, private club markers. Nothing matched. The symbol on this chip—the concentric circles—doesn't correspond to any known casino or gambling hall in Louisiana."

"But you knew what it was."

Mercier set the photograph down. "I knew what Felix believed it was. Everyone in the antique gaming community has heard the story of the Riverboat Codex. It's a kind of holy grail—a mythical object that may or may not exist. Most collectors think it's a legend."

"And you?"

"I think Felix Beaumont believed it was real. That much was obvious. The question is whether his belief was based on evidence or obsession."

Lila leaned forward. "Did he ever mention an eighth chip?"

Mercier's eyes flickered—a momentary crack in his composure. "An eighth chip would complete the set. According to tradition, there were eight original investors in the Belle Fleur gambling operation. Each held a key. If there were eight chips, the cipher would be solvable."

"But you've never seen it."

"I didn't say that, Dr. Vance. I said Felix showed me one chip. I've never seen the complete set. But I know someone who might have."

He wrote an address on a slip of paper and slid it across the desk.

"A woman named Amara Cole. She was Felix Beaumont's companion in his final years. She lives in the Bywater. If anyone knows what happened to the eighth chip, it would be her."

Lila took the paper, but she did not look away from Mercier's face. There was something in his expression that she could not quite read—not quite guilt, but close.

"Thank you, Mr. Mercier."

"Be careful, Dr. Vance. The people who want the Codex are not collectors. They're not historians. They're people who have something to lose. And the Codex has a long list of names that someone has been trying to keep buried for a hundred years."

Chapter 7: The Break-In

Lila returned to her apartment in Baton Rouge to find the deadbolt sheared from the door frame and the lock dangling by a single screw. The door itself stood ajar, a dark crack against the dim hallway light.

She stood in the hallway for a full thirty seconds, her bag still over her shoulder, and listened. No sound from inside. The neighbors' television murmured through the wall, a laugh track from some sitcom she could not identify. The building was otherwise silent.

She pushed the door open with her elbow, not touching the wood where the lock had splintered.

The apartment was destroyed.

Her bookshelves had been pulled down, their contents scattered across the floor in drifts of torn pages and broken spines. She had owned over two hundred books—mathematics texts, cipher histories, novels by authors she admired, reference works she consulted regularly—and every single one had been pulled from its shelf, riffled through, and tossed aside. Her desk drawers were open, their contents strewn across the floor in a blizzard of paper. The cushions of her sofa had been slashed, foam stuffing spilling out like viscera across the hardwood.

Whoever had done this had been thorough, professional, and fast. They had known exactly what they were looking for.

But the thieves had been thorough in a specific way. They had not taken her laptop, which sat on the kitchen counter, untouched, still plugged into its charger. They had not taken her television or her stereo or the small silver locket that had belonged to her grandmother, which lay in plain sight on the overturned coffee table. They had gone through every book, every folder, every scrap of paper, and they had taken only one thing.

The folder containing her photographs of the poker chips.

Lila pulled out her phone and dialed Marcus Thibodeau.

"My apartment has been broken into," she said, her voice remarkably steady. "They took the chip photographs but left everything else."

"Are you safe right now?"

"I'm standing in the doorway. The apartment is empty, I'm sure of it."

"Don't touch anything. I'm sending a unit. Where are the physical chips?"

"In a safety deposit box at my bank. Rollie put them there this morning, before I even started on the photographs."

"Good. That was smart. Stay there, wait for the officers, don't go farther inside."

Lila leaned against the doorframe and surveyed the wreckage of her life. She had never owned much—the divorce had taken the house, the furniture, the good china, most of her books from the years before everything fell apart. This small apartment in a converted Victorian was all she had left, and now even this had been violated.

But the thieves had made a mistake. They had taken the photographs, but they had not taken the originals. And they had not known about the plaster casts that Rollie had made decades ago, stored in a locked cabinet in his own home.

She pulled out her phone again and called Rollie.

"They hit my apartment," she said. "They took the photographs of the chips."

"I'm sitting in my living room with a shotgun on my lap," Rollie said, his voice tight but controlled. "No one has come through my door yet. But I've got my car packed. If you need to relocate, I know a place."

"Where?"

"My cabin on Lake Pontchartrain. Felix and I bought it together in the seventies. No one knows about it anymore. It's not in my name, not in any directory. We'll be safe there."

Lila looked at her destroyed apartment one last time. Down the hall, she could hear her neighbors gathering, whispering to each other in French and English. The building manager was calling the police on his cell phone.

"Give me the address," she said. "I'll meet you there as soon as the police are done with me."

Chapter 8: The Plaster Impressions

The cabin was a small clapboard structure on the north shore of Lake Pontchartrain, accessible by a dirt road that wound through cypress groves and marsh grass. Rollie had arrived an hour before Lila and had already lit a fire in the stone hearth, despite the July heat.

"Mosquitoes," he explained when she raised an eyebrow. "Smoke keeps them away."

Lila set her bag on the worn wooden table. "You said you made plaster casts of the chips. Where are they?"

Rollie retrieved a wooden box from a cabinet above the sink. It was plain, unvarnished, the size of a cigar box. He set it on the table and opened the lid.

Inside, nestled in cotton batting, were seven plaster discs. Each was about two inches in diameter, slightly larger than the chips themselves. The impressions were perfect—every groove, every line of the symbols transferred faithfully to the plaster.

"I made these in 1994," Rollie said. "A year after Felix died. I didn't know why at the time. I just had a feeling that the originals might not survive."

"Why didn't you tell me about them sooner?"

"Because I'm an old man who doesn't trust easily. I needed to see if you were serious before I showed you my backup plan."

Lila lifted one of the plaster discs carefully. The concentric circles were rendered in sharp relief, standing out from the surface like a topographical map. She held it up to the firelight, studying the impression from different angles.

"The chip edges are beveled," she said. "Standard for clay composition chips. But the symbol impressions go deep—deeper than a typical casino stamp."

"Felix said they were made from a custom die. Each symbol was hand-carved into a steel master, then used to press the chips individually."

"Individual pressing would explain the variation in depth and clarity." Lila set the disc down and picked up another. The spiral pattern, with its dots along the curve. "This one is different. The spiral is deeper on one side than the other, as if the die was struck at an angle."

"Does that mean something?"

"It means the die maker was working under pressure. Speed over precision. Or possibly—" She stopped, a new thought forming. "Or possibly the die was not intended for the chip."

"What do you mean?"

"Some of these marks could be transfer impressions. If a die was used to stamp something else—paper, leather, another material—the die might have been damaged or misaligned, creating a secondary impression on the chip."

Rollie's face went pale. "Felix had a ring. A signet ring with a similar pattern. He never took it off."

"When did he stop wearing it?"

"I don't remember exactly. After his wife died, I think. Or maybe around the time of the 1993 murder investigation. He stopped wearing it, and I never asked why."

Lila looked at the plaster spiral again. If Felix had been using the cipher symbols on a signet ring, he had been marking documents, sealing envelopes, leaving traces of the code in places no one would think to look.

"Where did Felix live in the years before he died?"

"Algiers Point. A house on Pelican Avenue. But it was sold after the estate was settled."

"The new owners might not have found everything Felix left behind."

Rollie nodded slowly. "Felix was a man who hid things. He hid things in walls, in floorboards, in the spaces behind drawers. If he had a signet ring, he might have left impressions of it in places where no one would think to look."

"Then we need to find the current owner of that house," Lila said. "Before whoever broke into my apartment gets there first."

Chapter 9: The Archivist

Elise Delacroix met Lila in the reading room of the Louisiana State Archive, a cavernous space of marble floors and oak tables where the temperature was kept at a constant sixty-five degrees. She was a small woman in her fifties, with silver-streaked hair and sharp eyes that missed nothing.

"I heard you had a break-in," Elise said quietly, sliding a folder across the table. "I also heard you're working with Marcus Thibodeau on the 1993 Belle Fleur homicide."

"News travels fast in New Orleans."

"We're a small city disguised as a large one. Everybody knows everybody's business." Elise opened the folder. Inside were photocopies of newspaper clippings, police reports, and handwritten notes. "I pulled everything I could find on Oliver Bouchard and the original Belle Fleur operation."

Lila scanned the documents. Oliver Bouchard had been a prominent New Orleans businessman in the 1920s—legitimate fronts included a shipping company, a warehouse on the riverfront, and a stake in the streetcar system. But his real fortune came from illegal gambling.

"He operated from a riverboat called the Belle Fleur," Elise said, "moored at the wharf near what is now the convention center. The boat was lavish—chandeliers, mahogany paneling, private rooms for high-stakes games. It was a floating casino that operated openly because Bouchard had the police, the mayor's office, and half the city council on his payroll."

"And he kept a ledger."

"He kept several ledgers. Most were destroyed when Prohibition ended and Bouchard dissolved his operation. But one survived—the so-called Riverboat Codex. According to legend, Bouchard gave each of his eight original investors a poker chip with a unique symbol. The chips, when combined and decoded, revealed the location of the ledger."

Lila pulled out her photographs of the chips—the ones she had saved to her cloud storage before the break-in. "I have seven of them. An eighth is missing."

Elise studied the images. "The symbols are reminiscent of the Gambler's Alphabet—a cipher system used by riverboat cheats in the nineteenth century to communicate at the table. But these are more complex. More deliberate."

"The Gambler's Alphabet?"

"A simple substitution cipher where common words are replaced with symbols. Aces, kings, queens, suits, pot sizes—each had a corresponding mark. Cheats could signal each other without speaking. But these symbols don't match the traditional Gambler's Alphabet. They're something else entirely."

"Any idea what?"

Elise turned to the final page of the folder. It was a handwritten letter, yellowed with age, dated April 1928.

"This was found in the Bouchard family papers, donated to the archive in 1985. It's a letter from Oliver Bouchard to his brother, discussing the 'security of certain records.' He mentions a cipher based on 'the old river code'—almost certainly the Gambler's Alphabet—but modified with 'mathematical transformations' that no one would suspect."

Lila's heart quickened. "Combinatorial mathematics. That's my specialty."

"Then perhaps you were meant to find this." Elise closed the folder. "But be careful, Lila. The Bouchard family is still powerful in this city. Oliver's descendants control a trust that owns a significant portion of the riverfront property, including the Belle Fleur. If the Codex contains what people say it contains, there are Bouchards who would do anything to keep it buried."

"Even murder?"

"Even murder. Eddie Marchand wasn't the first person to die asking questions about the Riverboat Codex. And if you're not careful, you won't be the last."

Chapter 10: Felix's Notebook

Rollie brought the notebook to the cabin the following morning in a sealed plastic bag, handling it like a piece of evidence.

"I found this in Felix's safe deposit box," he said. "I didn't look at it closely at first. I was too focused on the chips."

The notebook was leather-bound, the size of a passport, the pages yellowed and warped with age. Lila put on a pair of latex gloves—a habit from her forensic mathematics consulting days—and opened it.

Felix Beaumont had written in a cramped, precise hand. The entries were dated, starting in 1987 and ending in 1992, just weeks before his death. Most were mundane—notes about poker games, casino earnings, observations about players and dealers. But interspersed among the routine entries were passages written in a different ink, a darker blue, as if Felix had returned to add them later.

Lila read aloud: "'April 14, 1988. Dreamed of the river again. The same dream. Oliver Bouchard sitting at a table with seven men. Eight glasses of whiskey. Eight chips on the green felt. They are laughing. They are sealing something that cannot be unsealed.'"

"Felix believed the Codex was cursed," Rollie said. "He told me once that anyone who tried to find it would be pulled into the river and drowned."

"He told you that?"

"He was drunk. But drunk words are sometimes sober thoughts."

Lila continued reading. The entries grew more fragmented as the notebook progressed, as if Felix's mind was unraveling. References to the "house line," to "the eighth chair," to "the painted pillar."

"Does 'the painted pillar' mean anything to you?"

Rollie shook his head. "I never heard Felix mention it."

Lila flipped to the final entry. It was dated November 2, 1992—three days before Felix's death.

"'I have found it. After all these years, I know where the Codex is hidden. The eighth chip is not a chip at all. It's a location. A place. I am going to retrieve it tomorrow. If anything happens to me, tell Rollie to look in the plaster.'"

"Look in the plaster," Lila repeated. "The plaster impressions."

"I never understood that line," Rollie said. "I thought he meant the plaster casts of the chips. But I've examined them a thousand times. There's nothing hidden in them."

Lila looked at the seven plaster discs, laid out on the cabin table. She picked up the spiral chip's impression, studying it with fresh eyes.

"Felix said the eighth chip is not a chip at all. It's a location." She turned the plaster disc over in her hands. "What if the location is hidden in the plaster itself?"

She held the disc up to the firelight. The plaster was opaque, uniform in thickness. But on the reverse side, there was a faint irregularity—a slight bump, barely perceptible to the touch.

"Hand me a knife."

Rollie produced a pocketknife. Lila took it and, with the careful precision of a surgeon, began to scrape at the back of the plaster disc. A fine white powder fell onto the table. Underneath, embedded in the plaster, was a small piece of folded paper.

She extracted it with tweezers and unfolded it.

Handwritten, in Felix Beaumont's cramped script, was a single sentence: "The pillar stands where the river bends. Beneath the painted eye, the truth descends."

Chapter 11: The House Line

The first threat arrived the next morning.

Lila found it taped to the cabin door when she stepped out to get air. A single sheet of white paper, printed in block capitals: DROP THE INVESTIGATION. THE CODEX BELONGS TO THE HOUSE.

No signature. No return address.

She took a photograph, then carefully peeled the paper from the door using the edge of her car key. No fingerprints would survive the humidity, but she would let Marcus decide that.

Rollie came out onto the porch, a cup of coffee in his hand. "What is it?"

"The House is letting us know they're watching."

He read the note, his face unreadable. "The House. In gambling terms, the house is the casino. The establishment. The entity that always wins in the long run."

"This isn't a casino. It's a person. Or a group."

"The original Belle Fleur operation had eight investors. Each was called a 'house member.' They referred to their collective as 'the House.'"

"So the House is the descendants of the original investors. The Bouchard trust."

Rollie sat down heavily on the porch steps. "Felix always said the House would never let the Codex surface. Too many powerful families have too much to lose. The Bouchards, the Landrys, the D'Argentos—old New Orleans money, built on gambling and protected by politicians who took their bribes."

"These are people who are still alive?"

"Alive and powerful. The Bouchard trust controls the Belle Fleur, which is the most profitable riverboat casino in Louisiana. The Landry family owns half the real estate in the French Quarter. The D'Argentos have interests in shipping and oil."

"And they all have something to hide."

"The Codex doesn't just name the original investors. It names the officials they bribed. The judges they owned. The police captains who looked the other way. Those families are still in power, Dr. Vance. Some of those names are still on the bench, still in the state legislature, still running the city."

Lila folded the note and put it in her pocket. "Then the House just made a mistake. They've confirmed that the Codex is real and that they're afraid of what we'll find."

"You're not afraid?"

"I'm terrified. But I'm also stubborn." She looked out at the lake, at the cypress trees rising from the water like sentinels. "Felix Beaumont spent thirty years looking for the Codex. Eddie Marchand was killed because he got too close. If I stop now, their deaths mean nothing."

Rollie was silent for a long moment. Then he nodded slowly.

"There's something else I should tell you. Something I should have told you the day we met."

"What's that?"

"The break-in at my house. It wasn't the first time someone searched my property. Felix's house in Algiers Point was broken into twice in the year after his death. The police said it was kids, vandalism. But nothing was vandalized. Drawers were opened, papers were shuffled."

"They were looking for the chips."

"They were looking for the Codex. And they've been looking for thirty-two years. The only reason they haven't found it is that Felix hid it better than anyone expected." Rollie stood up, brushed off his trousers. "But they're getting closer. And now they know you're involved."

Lila looked at the note one more time, then tucked it into her pocket.

"Let them come," she said. "I've been hiding long enough."

Chapter 12: The Riverboat Invitation

The invitation arrived by courier the following afternoon.

A heavy cream envelope, sealed with crimson wax, bearing the embossed logo of the Belle Fleur riverboat casino. Inside, a handwritten note on heavy cardstock:

"Dr. Vance — I understand you have been making inquiries about our establishment's history. I would be delighted to host you for an evening aboard the Belle Fleur. Dinner, drinks, and a tour of our facilities. I believe we have much to discuss.

— Simone Bouchard, Director of Casino Operations."

"Simone Bouchard," Lila read aloud. "That doesn't sound like a coincidence."

"Simone is Antoine Bouchard's daughter," Rollie said. "Antoine is the current trustee of the Bouchard Trust. He's been running the family's interests since the 1980s."

"She's inviting me onto her home turf. A formal invitation, with witnesses, so nothing can happen to me."

"Or so it appears to be a social call, and any evidence of your investigation that she can gather is admissible."

Lila considered this. "Either way, it's an opportunity. She wants to know how much I know. I want to know the same about her."

"I don't like it. The Belle Fleur is her territory. Every camera, every dealer, every security guard—they all work for her."

"Then I won't go alone." Lila pulled out her phone. "I know someone who knows the Belle Fleur better than anyone."

She called Marcus Thibodeau.

"Detective, how would you like to spend an evening at a casino?"

"On the clock or off?"

"Officially, you're accompanying me to a dinner with Simone Bouchard. Unofficially, you're there to make sure I leave in one piece."

Marcus was silent for a moment. "I've wanted to interview Simone Bouchard for eleven years. Eddie Marchand was killed behind her casino. She was working the surveillance room the night he died."

"Did you ever question her?"

"She gave a statement. Said she saw nothing unusual on the cameras that night. I've always wondered if that was true."

"Then this is your chance to ask again, in person."

"I'll pick you up at six. Wear something you don't mind being seen in."

Lila hung up and looked at the invitation again. The wax seal bore the Bouchard family crest—a stylized riverboat with eight stars above it. Eight investors. Eight chips. Eight stars.

"I'm walking into the lion's den," she said quietly.

Rollie put a hand on her shoulder. "You've deciphered codes that stumped the NSA. You can handle a casino dinner."

"I'm less worried about the dinner than what comes after."

Chapter 13: The Green Felt

The Belle Fleur was magnificent.

She rode at permanent mooring on the Mississippi River wharf, her paddlewheel still intact but purely decorative, her three decks gleaming with fresh white paint and gold trim. She was a replica of the original 1920s riverboat, built in the 1980s when riverboat gambling was re-legalized in Louisiana. But she was faithful to the original in every detail that mattered—the ornate filigree along the railings, the tall smokestacks, the grand staircase that led to the main gaming salon.

Lila stood at the base of the gangplank, Marcus beside her in a dark suit that did little to conceal the shoulder holster beneath his jacket.

"Ready?" he asked.

"Not remotely."

They ascended to the main deck, where the air was thick with the smell of perfume, whiskey, and the distinctive ozone tang of slot machines. Roulette wheels clicked and whirred. Cards shuffled. Chips clacked.

A woman in a tailored black suit approached them. She was striking—perhaps fifty, with sharp cheekbones, dark hair pulled back in a severe bun, and eyes the color of winter slate.

"Dr. Vance. I'm Simone Bouchard. Thank you for accepting my invitation."

Lila shook her hand. Simone's grip was firm, businesslike, exactly as long as politeness required and no longer.

"This is Detective Marcus Thibodeau, NOPD."

"Detective. I remember you from the Marchand investigation. It's been a long time."

"Eleven years," Marcus said.

"And you're still looking for answers. I admire persistence."

"Shall we?" Simone gestured toward a set of double doors. "I've arranged a private dining room. We can speak freely there."

The private dining room was on the second deck, overlooking the main gaming floor through a one-way mirror. A table had been set with crystal and silver, and a waiter was uncorking a bottle of wine.

"I took the liberty of ordering a Sancerre," Simone said. "I hope that's acceptable."

Lila did not sit. "Let's skip the pleasantries, Ms. Bouchard. You invited me here because you know I'm investigating the Riverboat Codex. I'd like to know why."

Simone's smile did not waver. She sat down gracefully and gestured for the waiter to pour.

"Because I've been waiting for someone like you, Dr. Vance. Someone with the intellect and the stubbornness to solve a puzzle that has defeated everyone else."

"And why would the director of casino operations want the Codex found?"

"Because the Codex is a bomb, and I'd rather know exactly where it's buried than have it detonate unexpectedly." Simone took a sip of wine. "My family has been living with this secret for a hundred years. Every generation has known that somewhere, in some hidden place, there is a book that could destroy us. I'm tired of looking over my shoulder."

"So you want to find it and destroy it."

"I want to find it and decide what to do with it." Simone's eyes met Lila's. "Not destroy it. Not bury it again. Decide."

Marcus spoke. "Decide how?"

"That depends on what's in it. If the Codex names politicians who are still alive, I may have a moral obligation to expose them. If it only names the dead, there may be no point in causing pain to their descendants."

"And if it names your father?"

Simone's composure cracked, just slightly. "My father is dying. He's been dying for six months. The Codex cannot hurt him more than that."

Lila finally sat down. "You're offering me access to the Belle Fleur's records. Historical documents, employee files, security footage from 1993."

"Everything you need. In exchange, I want to see your translations of the chips."

"The translations don't exist yet. I'm still working on the cipher."

"Then you'll need more time. And more resources." Simone reached into her jacket and produced a business card. "This is my private number. Call me when you have something to share."

Lila took the card. It was plain white, with only a phone number and Simone's name.

"Why should I trust you?"

"You shouldn't. But you should use me, just as I'm using you. That's how this city works, Dr. Vance. We use each other to get what we want." Simone stood, signaling that the meeting was over. "Enjoy the wine. The dinner is paid for regardless. And if you'd like to try your luck at the tables, the house has comped you five hundred dollars in chips."

"I don't gamble."

"Everyone gambles, Dr. Vance. Some of us just know the odds better than others."

Chapter 14: The First Translation

Lila spent the next three days in the cabin, surrounded by notes, photographs, and the seven plaster casts. She had pinned the images of the chips to a corkboard and drawn connections between symbols in different colored markers.

The breakthrough came at three in the morning, as breakthroughs often did.

She had been staring at the concentric circles, trying to understand why the number of rings varied. Three rings on the white chip. Five on the red. Seven on the blue. The bisected triangles had hash marks—four and nine.

She wrote the numbers in sequence: 3, 5, 7, 4, 9.

Prime numbers. Three, five, seven. But four and nine were not prime. They were squares.

She looked at the interlocking square chip and the spiral chip. The squares had directional arrows—north, south. The spiral had dots—she counted them again. Eleven dots.

The sequence was: 3, 5, 7, 4, 9, 2 (two arrows), 11.

She wrote them in order of the chips as Rollie had found them in the safety deposit box: White (circles), Red (circles), Blue (circles), White (triangle), Red (triangle), Blue (square), Green (spiral).

3, 5, 7, 4, 9, 2, 11.

She stared at the numbers for a long time. They seemed random. But Felix Beaumont had arranged the chips in a specific order in the safety deposit box. He had left them that way for a reason.

What if the order mattered? What if the chips were meant to be arranged in a specific sequence to create a message?

She rearranged the numbers by the number of rings, the hash marks, the arrows, the dots—in ascending order.

2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11.

Prime numbers, but with 4 and 9 inserted. Important numbers. Numbers that corresponded to something in the cipher.

She opened her notebook and wrote the Gambler's Alphabet as Elise had described it. The traditional riverboat cheat's code assigned symbols to playing cards and poker terms. But what if the symbols on the chips were not the cipher themselves but the key to the cipher?

She looked at the interlocking square chip—the one with the caret mark on its edge. Two arrows. North and south. Orientation.

"Latitude and longitude," she whispered.

She grabbed her GPS coordinates for the cabin and wrote them down. Then she overlaid the number sequence from the chips onto the coordinates, using the concentric circles as degrees and the hash marks as minutes.

Her hands were shaking as she wrote the result.

30 degrees, 24 minutes North.

"That's the latitude of New Orleans," she breathed. "The exact latitude."

She grabbed the spiral chip—the eleventh dot. That would correspond to longitude. She performed the same transformation, adjusting for the spiral's logarithmic curve.

90 degrees, 11 minutes West.

"They're coordinates," she said aloud. "The chips encode a pair of coordinates."

She quickly mapped them. The location was in the Mississippi River, just downstream from the old Huey P. Long Bridge.

"The pillar stands where the river bends," she quoted from Felix's hidden note. "Beneath the painted eye, the truth descends."

The river bent at exactly that point. And the "painted eye" might be the painted markings on the bridge supports.

She grabbed her phone and called Marcus.

"I know where the Codex is hidden."

"Where?"

"In the Mississippi River. Under the old Huey P. Long Bridge. Felix hid it in the water."

Chapter 15: The Painted Pillar

Marcus met Lila at the public boat launch under the Huey P. Long Bridge at dawn. The river was low, revealing stretches of muddy bank that were usually submerged. The bridge itself loomed overhead, a steel latticework of girders and rivets, its painted surface weathered by decades of Louisiana weather.

"The painted pillar," Marcus said, looking up at the bridge's supports. "Felix's note said 'beneath the painted eye.' What does that mean?"

Lila studied the bridge structure. The main supports were massive concrete pillars, sheathed in steel, painted in alternating bands of yellow and gray. But near the waterline, on the downstream side of the southernmost pillar, there was something else—a faded painted circle, perhaps three feet in diameter, with a pattern of lines radiating from its center.

"That's an eye," Lila said. "A traditional protective symbol, painted on vessels and structures to ward off evil. It's called a nazar, or an evil eye."

"Someone painted an evil eye on a bridge support?"

"It was common on riverboats. The original Belle Fleur crew probably painted it there during construction of the bridge in the 1930s, as a good luck charm."

They made their way down to the water's edge, picking through mud and debris. The painted eye was at eye level from the water, positioned directly above a small ledge in the concrete.

"Felix said 'beneath the painted eye, the truth descends.' Descends into the water. The truth is under the surface."

Marcus looked at the murky river. "We're going to need a dive team."

"We're going to need someone who can search without attracting attention. If the House finds out we're searching the river, they'll be here before we can get anything out."

"I know a guy. Ex-Navy diver, runs a salvage company out of Gretna. He can be discreet."

"How discreet?"

"Discreet enough that he spent six months smuggling Cuban cigars through the port before Customs caught on. He served his time, paid his dues, and now he runs a legitimate business. But he still knows how to keep his mouth shut."

"Can you call him?"

Marcus was already dialing. "I'll have him meet us at his yard in an hour. But Lila—if the Codex is down there, it's been in the water for ninety years. We don't know what condition it's in."

"Felix knew what he was doing. He wouldn't have hidden it somewhere it would be destroyed."

"Unless he expected to retrieve it before it was damaged."

Lila looked at the brown water swirling around the bridge support. Somewhere beneath that surface, in the darkness and the mud, lay a book that could rewrite the history of New Orleans. A book that had already cost at least two lives.

"I guess we're about to find out," she said.

Chapter 16: Simone's Proposal

Simone Bouchard called Lila the evening after the bridge discovery.

"I hear you've been asking about dive operations," Simone said without preamble.

"How did you—"

"I have cameras on the bridge, Dr. Vance. They're part of the river traffic monitoring system. I saw you and Detective Thibodeau there this morning."

Lila's grip tightened on the phone. "You're monitoring the bridge."

"I'm monitoring everything. It's my job." Simone's voice was calm, almost bored. "I told you I wanted to know where the Codex was. You've found it. Now we need to decide how to proceed."

"What do you propose?"

"I propose we retrieve the Codex together. My resources, your expertise. We bring it up, we assess its contents, and we make a joint decision about what to do with it."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then the House will take other measures. The trust has already authorized a private retrieval operation. If I'm not in control of that operation, people who are much less reasonable than me will be making decisions."

Lila paced the length of the cabin. "You're asking me to work with the people who've been threatening me."

"I'm asking you to work with me. The trust is not a monolith. There are members who want the Codex destroyed. There are members who want it published. And there are members who simply want to know what's in it so they can protect their families. I'm trying to prevent a war."

"And your father? Where does he stand?"

"My father is dying of cancer. He doesn't stand anywhere anymore. But the trust will outlive him, and the decision about the Codex will fall to me." Simone paused. "I'm trying to do the right thing, Dr. Vance. But I need your help to do it."

Lila looked at the photographs spread across the cabin table. Seven chips. Seven symbols. One missing key. And a book at the bottom of the Mississippi River.

"If I agree to work with you, what guarantees do I have?"

"You have my word."

"Your word hasn't meant much so far. You've been feeding information to the trust since the day I started this investigation."

Silence. Then, softly: "How did you know?"

"Because every time I told you something at our dinner, the House responded within hours. The threat on my door came the morning after I visited the Belle Fleur. You're the only person outside my immediate circle who knew what I was working on."

"I had no choice. The trust owns the casino. My position depends on their goodwill."

"And now?"

"Now I'm choosing to stand with you. Because the trust's plan for the Codex is wrong. They want to destroy it. They want to sink it deeper than Felix Beaumont ever imagined. And I can't let that happen."

Lila closed her eyes. Her instinct told her not to trust Simone Bouchard. But her instinct had been wrong before—wrong about the NSA, wrong about her husband, wrong about the people she thought she could count on.

"One condition," Lila said. "Marcus Thibodeau is part of the retrieval operation. He represents the law, and he represents the memory of Eddie Marchand. If you want my cooperation, you accept his involvement."

"Agreed."

"And when we find the Codex, we open it together. All three of us."

"Agreed."

Lila let out a breath she didn't realize she had been holding. "Then I'll work with you. But if you betray me, Simone, I will make sure the entire contents of the Codex are published on every news outlet in the country before the day is out."

"I wouldn't expect anything less."

Chapter 17: The Collector

Tommy Vitale arrived at the cabin the next morning without warning.

Lila heard the car first—a low engine, cutting through the cypress grove. She looked out the window to see a black Lincoln Town Car pulling up next to Rollie's Cadillac. The man who emerged was in his late sixties, barrel-chested, with silver hair slicked back and a diamond ring on his pinky finger that caught the morning light.

"Dr. Vance," he called out as she stepped onto the porch. "I apologize for the unannounced visit. I tried calling, but your number is unlisted."

"Who are you?"

"Tommy Vitale. I'm a collector of antique gambling memorabilia. I heard through the grapevine that you've come into possession of some very interesting poker chips."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

Tommy smiled, revealing a row of capped teeth. "I've been collecting Prohibition-era riverboat artifacts for forty years. I know every chip, every token, every marker that exists from that period. When a new set surfaces, I hear about it within days."

"And what do you want with the chips?"

"I want to buy them. I'm prepared to offer you fifty thousand dollars for the set. Cash."

Lila crossed her arms. "The chips are not for sale."

"I haven't finished my offer. I'm prepared to offer you fifty thousand dollars, plus my guarantee that no one from the House will bother you again."

"You know about the House."

"I know everything about the House. My grandfather was one of the original investors in the Belle Fleur. He was at the table the night the chips were distributed."

Lila stepped off the porch, moving closer to Tommy. "Your grandfather was an investor?"

"Marcello Vitale. He ran the shipping that brought supplies to the riverboat. He was the seventh man in the room. I have photographs."

"If your grandfather was an investor, you know what the chips lead to."

"The Codex." Tommy's smile faded. "I've spent my whole life trying to find it. Not to destroy it—to own it. A book like that, with the names of every corrupt official, every fixed game, every payoff—it's the holy grail of New Orleans history."

"And you think I would sell you that?"

"I think you're a pragmatist. You've been threatened. Your apartment was destroyed. The House is closing in. Fifty thousand dollars and a guarantee of safety seems like a reasonable deal."

Lila shook her head. "I'm not in this for the money, Mr. Vitale."

"Then what are you in it for?"

"The truth." She looked him in the eye. "The same thing Felix Beaumont was looking for. The same thing Eddie Marchand was looking for. The truth."

Tommy studied her for a long moment. Then he nodded slowly.

"Then you're a better person than I am, Dr. Vance. But I still want to help you."

"Why?"

"Because my grandfather spent the last twenty years of his life ashamed of what he was part of. He told me, on his deathbed, that the Codex had to be found and opened. He said the truth was the only thing that could redeem the family name."

Tommy reached into his jacket and pulled out a photograph. It was old, sepia-toned, showing eight men seated around a poker table on a riverboat. Each man held a chip in front of him.

"That's your eighth chip," Tommy said, pointing to the man on the far left. "That's my grandfather. And if you look closely at the chip in his hand, you'll see it's different from the others."

Lila took the photograph, her heart pounding. The eighth chip—the one that had been missing for thirty years—was clearly visible. It bore a symbol she had not seen before: a crescent moon with a single star.

"Where is that chip now?"

"I don't know. My grandfather's belongings were sold after he died. But I've been looking for it for thirty years." Tommy met her eyes. "If you find the Codex without the eighth chip, the translation will be incomplete. There will be names you can't read. Secrets you can't unlock."

"Then we need to find it."

"And I know who might have it." Tommy pointed to the photograph again, at another figure. "See that man? He's the eighth investor. He had the eighth chip. His name was Henri Lambert."

"Henri Lambert," Lila repeated. "He's still alive."

Tommy's eyes widened. "You know him?"

"I know of him. He's 104 years old. He lives in a nursing home in Algiers."

"Then we need to pay him a visit. Before the House does."

Chapter 18: Tommy's Story

On the drive to Algiers, Tommy told Lila his story, leaning forward over the steering wheel as if the weight of the memory was pressing down on him.

"I was at the Belle Fleur the night Eddie Marchand was killed," he said, his voice flat. "I've never told anyone the full truth about what I saw."

Lila turned sharply in her seat. "You were there?"

"I was parked on the wharf, about fifty yards from the mooring station. I saw Eddie arguing with someone."

"Who?"

"A woman. I couldn't see her face clearly—it was dark, and she had her back to me. But she was wearing a security uniform."

Simone Bouchard. Or someone from her surveillance team.

"Did you report what you saw?"

"I was running an illegal memorabilia operation out of the trunk of my car. I couldn't exactly walk into a police station and say, 'By the way, I witnessed a murder while I was trafficking stolen casino artifacts.'"

Lila absorbed this in silence. "You've known who killed Eddie Marchand for eleven years."

"I've known that a woman in a security uniform argued with him before he died. That's not the same as knowing who killed him." Tommy's hands tightened on the steering wheel. "But I've had my suspicions."

"Simone Bouchard."

"She was the head of surveillance in 1993. She had access to the security tapes. She could have edited them to make it look like nothing happened."

"And you never told anyone?"

"I told Felix Beaumont. Two months before he died."

Lila stared at him. "You told Felix that you saw a woman in a security uniform arguing with Eddie Marchand?"

"I told him everything. He was the only person I trusted. And two months later, he was dead."

"Felix died of a heart attack."

"Felix was seventy-one years old, in good health, with no history of heart disease. And he died three days after telling me he knew where the Codex was hidden." Tommy shook his head. "I've spent eleven years wondering if I killed Felix by telling him what I saw."

They drove in silence for a while, crossing the Crescent City Connection bridge into Algiers.

"Henri Lambert is the last living link to the original Belle Fleur operation," Lila said finally. "He was there in 1928. He saw the chips distributed. If anyone knows what the eighth chip means, it's him."

"He may not want to talk. He's been silent for a hundred years."

"Then we'll have to give him a reason."

Chapter 19: The Cold Case Connects

Marcus met them at the Algiers nursing home, a low brick building on a residential street lined with live oaks that filtered the harsh Louisiana sunlight. He looked tired, dark circles under his eyes, as if he hadn't slept in two days.

"I've been going through the Marchand file all night," he said without preamble. "There's a connection I missed the first time around."

"What connection?"

"The shoes." Marcus held up a photograph. "Eddie Marchand was found without shoes. We never found them. I always assumed the killer disposed of them. But what if the shoes were taken?"

"Taken for what reason?"

"What if Eddie was wearing shoes with a hidden compartment? What if he had found something the night he died—a piece of the Codex, a note, a chip—and he was carrying it in his shoe?"

Lila's mind raced. "He was a card mechanic. Cheats often use specially modified shoes to switch cards. It's not a stretch that he would have hidden something in his footwear."

"And if the killer knew about the hiding place, they would have taken the shoes."

"Which means the killer knew Eddie. Knew his habits. Knew he was a cheat."

"Which brings us back to Simone Bouchard. She would have known every cheat on the river. It was her job."

Tommy spoke up. "I saw Eddie that night. He was nervous. Jumpy. He kept looking over his shoulder, like he expected someone to follow him."

"He was about to meet someone," Lila said. "The person in the security uniform."

"Or he was about to hand something to someone. The shoes could have been the delivery method."

They entered the nursing home. The receptionist directed them to Henri Lambert's room on the second floor, a small space with a window overlooking a courtyard garden. An old man sat in a wheelchair by the window, his hands folded in his lap, his eyes fixed on something none of them could see.

"Mr. Lambert?" Lila approached slowly.

The old man turned. His face was deeply lined, his eyes milky with cataracts, but there was a sharpness in his expression that belied his apparent frailty.

"You're the cryptanalyst," he said. "I heard you were coming."

"You heard?"

"I have a television. And a granddaughter who works at the courthouse. News travels." Henri gestured to a chair. "Sit. All of you. I've been waiting ninety years to tell this story."

Chapter 20: Felix's Last Deal

Henri Lambert's voice was thin, reedy with age, but it carried the weight of certainty that comes from living through history.

"I was there," he said. "August 1928. The Belle Fleur was docked at the wharf, but she wasn't open for business that night. She was closed for a private meeting. Eight men, one table, one bottle of whiskey."

"The eight investors," Lila said.

"The founders. Oliver Bouchard was the chairman. He had the idea—the Codex. He said we needed a record, something that would protect us all. If anyone ever tried to expose the operation, we would have leverage over every politician and every cop who took our money."

"So the Codex was an insurance policy."

"Precisely. Each of us contributed to it. We brought our records, our ledgers, our names. Oliver compiled them into a single book. And then he gave each of us a chip."

"The chips were the key to finding the Codex."

"The chips were half the key. Each chip held a piece of the cipher. But the eighth chip—the one I held—held the final piece. Without it, the cipher is just a jumble of symbols."

"Where is your chip?"

Henri was silent for a long time. Then he reached into the pocket of his cardigan and pulled out a small leather pouch.

"Oliver told us to keep the chips safe. He said if any of us died without passing on our chip, the Codex would be lost forever. I kept mine all these years." He opened the pouch and tipped a single poker chip into his palm.

The chip was different from the others Lila had seen. It was black, not white or red or blue, and the symbol on its face was a crescent moon with a single star. The same symbol Tommy had shown her in the photograph.

"The eighth chip," Lila breathed.

"I've kept it hidden for ninety-seven years. I was waiting for the right person to give it to." Henri held it out to her. "You're the one, Dr. Vance."

Lila took the chip. It was heavier than the others, made of a different clay composition. The symbol was deeper, the carving more precise.

"Why me?"

"Because you're not afraid. You're not a collector, not a politician, not a Bouchard. You're just someone who wants to know the truth." Henri's eyes met hers. "And because Felix Beaumont told me about you. He came to see me in 1990. He said if he didn't find the Codex, I should wait for someone who could."

"Felix knew about you?"

"Felix knew everything. He was Oliver Bouchard's grandson."

The room went silent.

"Felix Beaumont was a Bouchard?" Marcus said.

"By birth. His mother was Oliver's daughter. She married a man named Beaumont and changed her name to escape the family business. But Felix couldn't escape. He was drawn to the river, to the casino, to the mystery of the Codex." Henri shook his head. "He spent his whole life trying to atone for his grandfather's sins."

"And he died before he could."

"Before he could complete the work. But he found you, Dr. Vance. He set in motion everything that has led you to this moment."

Lila looked at the eighth chip in her hand. The crescent moon. The star. The final piece of the puzzle.

"I will find the Codex," she said. "And I will open it. I promise you that."

Henri smiled, a thin, knowing expression. "I know you will. That's why I waited."

Chapter 21: The Second Layer

Back at the cabin, Lila laid all eight chips on the table and stared at them.

Seven original chips plus the eighth from Henri. Eight symbols. Eight pieces of the cipher.

But as she studied them, she realized something troubling. With the eighth chip included, the cipher still didn't resolve into a complete message. There were gaps—missing segments of the coordinate system.

"The chips aren't the cipher," she said slowly. "They're the key to the cipher."

"What do you mean?" Rollie asked.

"The Gambler's Alphabet uses symbols for letters and numbers. If I map each chip's symbol to a specific transformation of the alphabet, the chips themselves become a cipher wheel. But I need to know the order—which chip is first, which is last."

"Felix arranged them in a specific order in the safety deposit box."

"He arranged seven. But with the eighth, the sequence changes." Lila arranged the chips in the order Henri had described—the order of the original investors around the table.

Concentric circles. Bisected triangles. Interlocking squares. Spiral. And now the crescent moon with star.

She wrote the symbols in sequence and assigned them values based on the Gambler's Alphabet. Then she performed the mathematical transformation—the one she had learned during her NSA consulting, the one that had gotten her fired.

The numbers aligned.

Coordinates. Three sets of them.

"The first one is the bridge location," Lila said. "The second is a location in the French Quarter. The third is in the river, further downstream."

"Three locations?"

"The Codex was split. Three parts. Oliver Bouchard didn't trust any one person with the whole truth." She looked up. "Felix found the first part—the bridge location. But he never found the other two."

"Until now."

"Until now." Lila copied the coordinates. "The second location is in the French Quarter. A building on St. Louis Street."

Rollie's face went pale. "That's Maison d'Antiquités. Sylvain Mercier's shop."

"The Codex was hidden in an antique shop?"

"Or beneath it. Felix used to say that Sylvain's shop had a cellar that dated back to the 1700s. If there's a secret room down there, Sylvain would know about it."

"And the third coordinate?"

Lila looked at the map. The third location was in the middle of the Mississippi, downstream from the city, near where the river widened into the Gulf.

"That's where the river takes what it's given," Rollie said quietly. "Felix used to say that. 'The river takes what it's given, but it doesn't give back.'"

"The Codex may have been weighted and dropped in the river. The third part."

"If it's in the river, it's gone. Mud, current, depth—after ninety years, there's nothing left."

"Maybe. Or maybe that's what the House wants us to think."

Chapter 22: The Warehouse

The building on St. Louis Street was not Maison d'Antiquités itself, but a warehouse behind it—a crumbling brick structure with boarded windows and a padlocked door that had rusted into uselessness.

Lila stood with Marcus and Tommy in the narrow alley behind the antique shop, looking at the building that the coordinates had revealed.

"This doesn't look like a secret vault," Tommy said.

"It's not supposed to." Lila approached the door. The padlock was old, but the hasp was newer, bolted into the brick with fresh mortar.

"Someone's been here recently."

Marcus examined the hasp. "Within the last few months. This mortar hasn't fully cured."

He produced a crowbar from his bag and wedged it behind the hasp. With a groan of protest, the bolts pulled free from the brick, and the door swung open.

The interior was dark and damp, smelling of mildew and rat droppings. Lila clicked on her flashlight and swept the beam across the space. The warehouse was filled with furniture—chairs stacked in towers, tables turned on their sides, filing cabinets with their drawers hanging open.

"A storage space," Tommy said. "Sylvain must have used it for overflow inventory."

Lila moved deeper into the warehouse, following the coordinate line. The beam of her flashlight fell on a section of the back wall that looked different from the rest—the brick was newer, less weathered.

"There." She pointed. "That wall was built after the rest."

Marcus ran his hand over the brick. "It's a false wall. Maybe a foot deep, concealing a space behind it."

It took them an hour to break through. The brick was soft, old mortar crumbled easily. When the hole was large enough, Lila shone her flashlight inside.

A small room, maybe six feet square. In the center, a wooden chest, bound in iron, with a brass lock.

"Don't touch it," Marcus said. "Could be booby-trapped."

He approached carefully, examining the chest from all angles. The lock was old, but there was no wire or trigger mechanism visible.

"Clear."

Tommy helped him lift the chest out of the recess. It was heavy, but not as heavy as a full book would be.

Lila's hands trembled as she worked the lock. It was stubborn, rusted shut, but finally gave way with a screech of metal.

She lifted the lid.

Inside, nestled in velvet, lay a single object. Not a ledger. Not a book. A leather-bound journal, smaller than she expected, with the name "Oliver Bouchard" embossed on the cover.

"It's not the Codex," she said. "It's Oliver's personal journal."

"Does it contain the Codex?"

She opened it. The pages were filled with handwriting—the same hand that had written the ledgers, but more personal, more raw.

"It contains references to the Codex. Records of where each part was hidden. Names of the people who helped." She flipped through the pages. "And the complete list of the investors and their beneficiaries."

"Beneficiaries?"

"The people the investors wanted protected. The people who would inherit their power, their money, their secrets." She looked up. "This journal is a road map. It tells us exactly where to find the other two parts of the Codex."

"And the names."

"And the names." She closed the journal. "We need to get this somewhere safe. Somewhere the House can't reach it."

Chapter 23: The Codex Fragment

Elise Delacroix met them at the State Archive at midnight.

"This better be important," she said, unlocking the side door with a heavy brass key. "I'm supposed to be at home, asleep, not sneaking into the archive like a cat burglar."

"It's important." Lila held up Oliver Bouchard's journal, the leather cover warm from being pressed against her body during the drive. "We found Oliver's personal records inside the warehouse behind Maison d'Antiquités. They contain the exact locations of the Codex fragments."

Elise's eyes widened as she locked the door behind them. "Where?"

"One is in the basement of the Old U.S. Mint. It was apparently stored there in 1932 when the federal government seized gambling equipment during a raid. Oliver had connections in the Customs office and arranged for certain items to be diverted."

"That's brilliant, actually. The Mint is federal property—no one would think to look there for a gambling ledger."

"The second fragment is at the bottom of the Mississippi, about three miles downstream from the Huey P. Long Bridge. Oliver had it weighted and dropped in the river, marked with a buoy that only he knew the location of."

Elise shook her head slowly. "Three miles downstream is the old dumping ground. The city used to dispose of seized gambling equipment there in the 1930s. The current would have scattered everything."

"Oliver Bouchard wasn't careless. He would have weighted it carefully. Anchored it to something solid."

"And ninety years of silt would have buried it deep. We'd need a full archaeological dive operation to recover it, with permits and equipment and a team of divers."

"Then let's start with the Old Mint first." Lila followed Elise through the darkened halls of the archive, their footsteps echoing on marble floors. "Can you get us access?"

"Not at midnight without a warrant. But I can make a call in the morning to the museum director. I've done favors for her before."

They worked through the night in Elise's office, photographing every page of Oliver's journal, cross-referencing the names with historical records, building a database of every official mentioned. By dawn, they had identified thirty-seven distinct names mentioned in the journal—investors, politicians, police officials, and judges, each with specific details about their relationship with the Belle Fleur operation.

"These are the names the Codex protects," Elise said, her voice heavy with the weight of what they had found. "Every one of them is dead."

"Every single one?"

"Oliver Bouchard died in 1945. His investors died over the following decades. The politicians and judges—most passed away in the 1950s and 60s. The youngest person named in this journal would be a hundred and ten years old today."

"Then what has the House been so afraid of all these years? If everyone named is dead, what's the threat?"

"The descendants. The families. The reputations." Elise pointed to a name on the list. "This judge—Harold P. Manning—was the grandfather of the current Louisiana State Supreme Court justice. If the Codex reveals that Judge Manning was taking bribes from a gambling operation, it doesn't matter that he's been dead for sixty years. His grandson's career, his reputation, his legacy—all of it would be destroyed by association."

"And there are more like that."

"Thirty-seven names, each with living descendants. Some of the most powerful families in Louisiana. The Landrys, the D'Argentos, the Manings. Families whose names are on buildings and university chairs and courthouse plaques."

Lila looked at the journal, at the list of names that could tear apart the state's political Establishment. She had started this investigation looking for a simple cipher. She had stumbled into a conspiracy that reached from the 1920s to the present day.

"This isn't just a historical document," she said. "This is a loaded weapon. And we just found the trigger."

Chapter 24: The House Closes In

The warning came at seven in the morning.

Lila's phone rang as she was leaving the archive. She didn't recognize the number, but she answered anyway.

"Dr. Vance." The voice was male, smooth, with an accent she couldn't place. "You've been busy."

"Who is this?"

"Someone who represents the House. We've been watching your progress with interest. The discovery of Oliver Bouchard's journal was impressive."

"If you know about the journal, you know what it contains."

"We do. Which is why we're making you an offer—a final offer. Stop your investigation. Hand over the journal and the poker chips to a representative of our choosing. In exchange, we will ensure your safety, the safety of everyone you've involved, and a payment of two hundred and fifty thousand dollars."

"And if I refuse?"

"Then the House will take more direct action. You've already experienced a break-in. The next time will be less gentle."

Lila's jaw tightened. "You killed Eddie Marchand. You killed Felix Beaumont. You don't get to threaten me and expect me to back down."

"Eddie Marchand's death was unfortunate, but necessary. Felix Beaumont's death was natural causes—we accelerated nothing. But we make no apologies for protecting our interests."

"Your interests are a hundred years old. The people who own those interests are dead."

"The families are not dead, Dr. Vance. The institutions are not dead. The secrets we protect keep New Orleans running. You would tear that down for what? For the satisfaction of being right?"

"For justice."

"Justice is a luxury that the dead cannot afford and the living cannot administer. Take the money, Dr. Vance. Walk away. Let the Codex stay where it belongs."

"Where it belongs is in the public record."

"And where it will stay is at the bottom of the Mississippi, or in the ashes of a fire that destroys whatever building it's in. Make no mistake—if you force our hand, we will destroy the Codex. We have the resources, the will, and the time."

Lila was silent.

"You have twenty-four hours," the voice said. "After that, the House will consider all options open."

The line went dead.

Lila stood in the parking lot of the State Archive, the morning sun warm on her face, and felt the full weight of what she was up against.

She called Marcus.

"We have twenty-four hours before the House escalates."

"Then we'd better find that Codex fragment fast."

Chapter 25: The Gambler's Alphabet

The Old U.S. Mint was a massive Greek Revival building on Esplanade Avenue, its Doric columns casting long shadows in the morning light. Elise had arranged access through a curator at the Louisiana State Museum, and they entered through the basement loading dock.

"The basement was used for storage during Prohibition," Elise explained. "The federal government seized illegal gambling equipment and stored it here before destruction. But not everything was destroyed."

"The Codex fragment."

"There's a sealed compartment in the foundation, built in 1932. Oliver Bouchard had connections in the federal government. He arranged for certain items to be 'lost' before they could be destroyed."

They descended into the basement, their footsteps echoing on concrete floors. The space was vast, filled with crates and filing cabinets and the detritus of a century of government storage.

"The compartment is behind the north wall," Elise said. "According to the journal, it's marked with a symbol."

"A symbol?"

"From the Gambler's Alphabet. Two intersecting lines with a dot at the center. It means 'truth' in the old river code."

They found it on the back wall, behind a stack of wooden crates—a small symbol carved into the brick, barely visible through decades of dirt and grime.

"Here."

Marcus cleared the crates away while Lila examined the wall. The brick was solid, but there was a faint line—a seam in the mortar that shouldn't exist.

"Help me."

She and Marcus pressed against the wall. Nothing happened. Then Tommy joined them, and the three of them pushed together. With a grinding sound, a section of the wall moved inward, revealing a narrow cavity.

Inside was a metal box, about the size of a briefcase, covered in surface rust.

Lila lifted it out and set it on the floor. The lid was sealed with a combination lock, five digits.

"The chips," she said. "The sequence from the chips."

She dialed the combination: 3-5-7-4-9.

The lock clicked open.

Inside the box lay a stack of papers, yellowed and brittle, tied with a faded ribbon. Lila lifted them out with trembling hands.

The Riverboat Codex. Or at least, one third of it.

The pages were covered in handwriting, in the same hand as Oliver Bouchard's journal. Lists of names. Dates. Amounts of money. Specific details of bribes, fixed games, and political payoffs.

"This is it," she whispered. "This is the proof."

But as she flipped through the pages, she realized something was wrong. The pages stopped abruptly, mid-sentence.

"Where's the rest?"

"The fragment," Elise said. "The journal said the Codex was split into three parts. This is the first. The second is in the river. And the third—" She looked at the journal again. "The third was never hidden. It was kept by Oliver Bouchard himself, passed down through his family."

"The Bouchard family."

"Simone Bouchard has the third fragment. Or her father does."

Lila stared at the pages in her hands. She had one third of the truth. The second third was at the bottom of the Mississippi. And the third third was in the hands of the woman who had been playing both sides against each other.

"Simone has been holding out on us."

"Or she's been protecting her family's deepest secret."

Chapter 26: The Last Survivor

Henri Lambert died the night they found the first Codex fragment.

Lila received the call from the nursing home at three in the morning, dragged from a deep sleep by the sharp insistence of her phone. Henri had passed away in his sleep, peacefully, the nurse said, at the age of one hundred and four.

"He gave you the chip yesterday," the nurse told her. "He said after you left that he had done what he needed to do. He said he could rest now. And then he closed his eyes and never woke up."

Lila sat in the dark cabin, the eighth chip in her hand, the crescent moon and star pattern pressing into her palm. He had waited ninety-seven years to pass on his secret—had outlived his wife, his children, his friends. And now he was gone.

She called Marcus.

"Henri's dead."

"I know. The nursing home called me too. He went peacefully."

"Did he say anything else? Before he died?"

"He told a nurse that 'the star and the moon would find their home.' I don't know what that means."

But Lila did. The crescent moon and star on the eighth chip—it wasn't just a symbol. It was a reference to a specific location. The Crescent City. New Orleans. The star was the Mississippi River, flowing past the city like a celestial body.

"The star and the moon find their home," she repeated. "The Codex was always meant to be here. In New Orleans. In the archive or the museum or the public record."

"Then the question is whether we can get the third fragment from Simone."

"Simone has been lying to us from the beginning. She's not going to hand over her family's last secret willingly."

"Then we need leverage."

Lila looked at the Codex fragment on the table. The names. The dates. The evidence.

"We have leverage," she said. "We have proof that her family has been covering up this secret for a century. And we have the part of the Codex that her father kept—or at least, we know where it is."

"You're going to confront her."

"Not yet. First, I need to see what the second part of the Codex contains. The part in the river."

"Lila, that dive is going to be dangerous. The current, the visibility, the—"

"I know. But Felix Beaumont didn't spend thirty years chasing this to have me stop now."

Chapter 27: Henri's Memory

Henri's funeral was held three days later at a small Catholic church in Algiers, a modest building with peeling paint and a congregation that numbered barely a dozen souls.

Lila attended, standing in the back, watching the small group of mourners. Henri had outlived his wife, his children, most of his friends. The only family present was a granddaughter in her sixties, a woman with kind eyes and Henri's strong cheekbones, who shook Lila's hand after the service.

"He talked about you constantly in his final days," the granddaughter said. "He said you were going to do something important. Something he should have done seventy years ago but was too afraid to attempt."

"Did he tell you what it was?"

"He told me about the Codex. He told me about the chips and the cipher and the secrets that had been buried for a hundred years. He said the truth had to come out, even if it hurt people who didn't deserve to be hurt." She squeezed Lila's hand with surprising strength. "I'm glad you were there at the end. He wasn't alone. That meant everything to him."

Lila walked back to her car, her heart heavy. She had never met Henri before the nursing home visit, but she felt his loss keenly. He was the last survivor of a world that no longer existed—a world of riverboats and illegal gambling and a city that ran on secrets whispered in back rooms. With his passing, an entire chapter of New Orleans history had closed forever.

"Dr. Vance."

She turned. A man was approaching her across the church parking lot—tall, silver-haired, in an expensive charcoal suit. He looked vaguely familiar, though she could not place him immediately.

"Thomas Bouchard," he said, extending his hand. "I'm Simone's brother."

Lila's guard went up instantly. "I didn't know Simone had a brother."

"I'm the estranged one. The black sheep of the Bouchard family. I haven't spoken to my sister in six years, and I haven't set foot in the family home in a decade." He dropped his hand when she did not take it. "I heard you've been looking for the Riverboat Codex."

"I have."

"I want to help you. I know where my father keeps the third fragment."

"Why?"

"Because my family has been lying for a hundred years. And I'm tired of it." He held her gaze. "I know where my father keeps the third fragment. It's in a safe in his study at the family home in Garden District."

"And you want to give it to me?"

"I want to see it exposed. Every name, every bribe, every fixed game. My grandfather was Oliver Bouchard's brother. He was part of the cover-up. And he died with the guilt of it."

Lila studied him. He seemed genuine, but she had learned not to trust anyone connected to the Bouchard family.

"Why now?"

"Because my father is dying. And before he dies, he plans to destroy the third fragment. He's going to burn it. He told me yesterday."

"Then we don't have much time."

"No." Thomas handed her a card. "Call me when you're ready. I'll get you into the house."

Chapter 28: The River Dive

The dive team assembled at dawn at a small marina near the Huey P. Long Bridge, the air thick with river fog and the smell of diesel fuel.

Marcus's contact, a former Navy diver named Leo Vargas, had brought his equipment in the back of a battered pickup truck. He was a compact man in his fifties, with shoulders like boulders and the calm demeanor of someone who had spent his life in dangerous environments. The section of river they needed to search was three miles downstream, where the current slowed and the water deepened into the old dumping grounds.

"The bottom is pure mud," Leo said, suiting up in a drysuit. "Zero visibility below three feet. I'll be searching entirely by touch."

"You're looking for a metal box," Lila said, showing him a photograph. "Approximately two feet by one foot, iron, with a brass hasp. It should be weighted down, probably chained to something heavy."

"Any idea how deep?"

"The journal said forty feet. But the river bottom shifts constantly. It could be deeper now, buried under silt."

Leo adjusted his mask and checked his air supply. "I'll be down for twenty minutes max. After that, I'll need to surface and decompress. If I don't find it in one dive, we'll need to regroup."

"Be careful down there."

He grinned, revealing a row of white teeth. "I'm always careful. The river knows me by now."

He rolled backward off the boat and disappeared into the brown water without a splash.

Lila waited, her heart pounding. The minutes stretched out. The river flowed past, indifferent to their search, carrying the history of a century in its dark currents.

Fifteen minutes passed. Then eighteen. Then twenty.

Leo's head broke the surface with a gasp. In his hand, held high above the water, was a dark shape, encrusted with mud and zebra mussels, dripping river water like a creature rising from the deep.

"I found it," he said, breathing hard. "Buried deep in the silt. Almost didn't catch it with my hand."

They hauled the box onto the boat. It was larger than the first fragment's container, heavily encrusted with zebra mussels and river sediment. But the brass hasp was intact, and the lock was still sealed.

"We need to get this open," Lila said. "Before it deteriorates."

"We need to dry it out first," Leo said. "Slowly. If we try to force it open while it's wet, the contents will turn to pulp."

"How long?"

"A few days. In a controlled environment."

Lila looked at the box, at the river water dripping from its surface. The second fragment of the Codex was inside, preserved in the mud for ninety years. A few more days wouldn't make a difference.

"Take it to the conservation lab at the State Archive," she said. "Elise will know how to handle it."

Chapter 29: The Bridge

While the Codex fragment was drying in the conservation lab, Lila returned to the bridge.

She stood on the muddy bank, looking at the painted eye on the pillar, its faded colors barely visible against the weathered concrete. The symbol had been there for ninety years, watching over the river, guarding the secret beneath. She wondered who had painted it—a superstitious crew member, perhaps, or one of Oliver Bouchard's men, marking the spot for future retrieval.

"Felix knew," she said to herself. "He knew the Codex was split into three parts. He found the bridge location, but he never recovered the fragment because he died before he could complete the job."

"Or because he was killed before he could."

Marcus had walked up behind her, silent in his police-issue shoes that made no sound on the muddy bank. He stood beside her, looking at the same pillar, the same painted eye.

"You still think Felix was murdered."

"I think a healthy seventy-one-year-old man doesn't die of a heart attack three days after telling someone he's found the location of the Riverboat Codex. The timing is too convenient." Marcus pulled a folded document from his jacket pocket. "I've been looking at the autopsy report again. There are inconsistencies that the original coroner either missed or was paid to ignore."

"Like what?"

"The coroner noted bruising on Felix's chest. Consistent with CPR, he wrote in the report. But the bruising was too symmetrical. Too precise. Two small marks, exactly three inches apart, directly over the heart."

Lila felt a chill despite the July heat. "A medical procedure."

"Or an injection site. Something that would cause a cardiac event and leave almost no trace. There's a drug called potassium chloride that can induce a heart attack. It's virtually undetectable in an autopsy if the coroner isn't looking for it."

"You think the House had Felix killed."

"I think the House has been killing people to protect the Codex since 1928. Eddie Marchand in 1993. Felix in 1992. And possibly others—people we haven't found yet, whose deaths were written off as accidents or natural causes."

"And now they're trying to kill this investigation."

"Which is why we need to move fast. Simone's brother is ready to let us into the Garden District house. It's our best chance to get the third fragment before her father destroys it."

Lila looked at the dark water of the Mississippi, flowing past with the patient force of a river that had carried secrets for millennia.

"Then let's not wait," she said. "Let's finish this."

Chapter 30: Simone's Betrayal

Thomas Bouchard met them at midnight on the corner of First and Chestnut Streets, in the heart of the Garden District.

"My father goes to sleep at nine," he said. "He's heavily medicated. We have about four hours."

He led them to a Greek Revival mansion on Chestnut Street, three stories of white columns and wrought-iron balconies. The Bouchard family home.

"The safe is in the study on the second floor. I've disabled the alarm system. But we need to be quiet."

They entered through a side door, moving through a darkened kitchen and up a narrow service staircase. The study was at the end of a hall, its door closed.

Thomas produced a key and unlocked it.

The study was paneled in dark wood, lined with bookshelves, dominated by a massive mahogany desk. Behind the desk, partially hidden by a painting of the Belle Fleur, was a wall safe.

"The combination changes every week," Thomas said. "My father keeps it in his head. We need to guess."

"Or we don't need a combination at all." Marcus produced a portable drill. "I'm a detective. I have access to certain tools."

It took him twelve minutes to drill through the safe's locking mechanism. The door swung open.

Inside was a single item: a leather-bound ledger, exactly matching the description of the Codex.

Lila lifted it out carefully. It was heavier than she expected, the leather cracked with age. She opened it.

The pages were filled with names. Hundreds of names. Dates. Amounts. Specific details of every bribe, every payoff, every fixed game that had oiled the New Orleans gambling machine from 1920 to 1933.

"The complete Codex," she breathed. "This is the original."

"Take it," Thomas said. "Get it to the archive. Publish it. Let the truth come out."

They were halfway down the stairs when the lights came on.

Simone Bouchard stood at the bottom of the staircase, a gun in her hand.

"I was wondering when you'd show up," she said. "Thomas, you always were too sentimental."

"Simone—"

"Shut up." She raised the gun. "Put the ledger on the floor, Dr. Vance. Slowly."

Lila placed the ledger on the step.

"You've been working against us from the beginning," Lila said. "You never wanted the Codex found."

"I wanted it controlled. There's a difference." Simone's eyes were cold. "You're a brilliant woman, Dr. Vance. But you're naive. You think the truth sets people free. The truth destroys people. And I've spent my entire career making sure the right truths get told and the wrong truths get buried."

"You killed Eddie Marchand."

"Eddie was a thief and a cheat. He found a fragment of the Codex and tried to sell it. I did what needed to be done."

"And Felix Beaumont?"

"Felix died of natural causes. But I would have killed him too, if I had to."

"So would I have been next?"

Simone's finger tightened on the trigger. "You're not going to publish the Codex, Dr. Vance. You're going to disappear. And the ledger is going back in the safe."

"You can't keep it hidden forever."

"I don't need forever. I just need to outlive the people it would hurt." Simone stepped forward. "And that includes you."

Chapter 31: The Night Dive

Gunfire shattered the quiet of the Garden District.

The sound was deafening in the confined space of the staircase—a single sharp crack that echoed off the walls and sent plaster dust drifting from the ceiling. Marcus had drawn his weapon while Simone was speaking, and he fired before she could pull her own trigger. The bullet struck Simone's shoulder and spun her around, her gun clattering down the stairs and skidding across the marble floor of the foyer.

"Police! Drop your weapon!" Marcus shouted, though Simone was already disarmed, clutching her shoulder with a look of shock.

Simone stared at the blood seeping through her fingers, her face pale. "You shot me."

"You were about to kill a civilian." Marcus kept his gun trained on her, his voice rock steady. "Thomas, call an ambulance. Now."

Lila grabbed the ledger from the step where she had placed it and clutched it to her chest. Her heart was pounding so hard she could feel it in her throat, and her hands were shaking.

"Are you okay?" Marcus asked, not taking his eyes off Simone.

"I'm—" She looked at Simone, who was slumped against the banister, her face white with shock and pain. "I'm fine. I have the ledger."

"Take it and go. I'll handle this. Get it somewhere safe."

Lila ran.

She burst through the front door and into the Garden District night, the ledger pressed against her body like a shield. The streets were empty, the historic houses dark and silent behind their wrought-iron fences. She ran until she reached her car, threw herself inside, and locked the doors with a click that sounded deafeningly loud.

Her hands were shaking as she started the engine and pulled away from the curb. In her rearview mirror, she could see the lights coming on in the Bouchard house, could hear the distant wail of sirens approaching.

She drove to the State Archive, where Elise was waiting at the loading dock, her face drawn with worry.

"I have it," Lila said, holding up the leather-bound ledger. "The complete Codex. The third fragment that Antoine Bouchard kept in his safe."

Elise's face went pale. "My God. You actually did it."

"Simone Bouchard is in custody. But the rest of the House is still out there. We need to get this somewhere safe."

"The archive's vault. It's fireproof, waterproof, and alarmed. No one gets in without authorization."

They carried the ledger inside, through the darkened halls of the archive, to the vault in the basement. Elise unlocked it with her key and a code.

"Once this door closes, it stays closed until morning," she said. "No one can get in. Not even me."

"Do it."

The vault door swung shut with a sound like a tomb sealing.

Chapter 32: The Drying Process

The conservation lab was a sterile white room filled with climate-controlled cabinets and specialized drying equipment. The second Codex fragment—recovered from the river—lay on a stainless steel table, still encased in its mud-crusted iron box.

Dr. Patricia Okonkwo, the archive's senior conservator, had been working on it for three days. She was a small woman with nimble fingers and the patience of someone who understood that history could not be rushed.

"The box is iron," she explained, adjusting her magnifying goggles. "Which is actually good news. Iron creates a low-oxygen environment underwater, which slows the decomposition of organic materials. The contents have been sealed in an anoxic environment for ninety years."

"How long before we can open it?"

"Another two days, at least. We're drying the box as a unit—slowly, in a controlled humidity chamber. If we try to force it open now, the contents will be exposed to oxygen too quickly and they'll crumble."

Lila looked at the box through the glass of the observation window. She had survived a shootout, a break-in, and a confrontation with a killer who had tried to shoot her on a staircase. But this—the slow, patient work of preservation—was the most nerve-wracking part of the entire investigation. The truth was right there, in that box, and she could not touch it.

"What happens when it's dry enough to open?"

"We'll photograph everything in place, then carefully separate each page. The ink is likely iron gall, which means it will still be legible. But the paper will be extremely fragile for weeks. We'll need to stabilize it before we can even think about transcription."

"So I'm waiting."

"You're waiting. But waiting is part of the job. The documents have survived ninety years in the river. They can survive two more days on a lab table."

"Can I stay?"

"The temperature in here is kept at sixty degrees. You'll freeze."

"I don't care."

Dr. Okonkwo smiled. "Then sit over there. And don't touch anything."

Lila sat in the corner of the lab, the complete Codex safely locked in the vault downstairs, and watched the slow process of history being preserved.

She thought about Henri Lambert, who had died knowing he had passed on his secret. She thought about Felix Beaumont, who had spent thirty years searching for a truth that had cost him his life. She thought about Eddie Marchand, who had been killed for finding a fragment of the same truth.

And she thought about Simone Bouchard, who had tried to bury the truth to protect her family.

"I'm not going to bury it," Lila said softly. "I'm going to open it. For all of them."

Chapter 33: The Names

The river fragment was finally ready for examination. Two days after the conservation team completed the slow drying process, Dr. Okonkwo carefully opened the iron box in the sterile climate-controlled room at the conservation lab.

The contents were fragile after ninety years underwater, but intact. A stack of papers, bound in cracked leather, written in ink that had faded to a pale brown but was still legible under the conservator's magnifying lamp.

Lila stood over the table, hardly breathing, as the conservator carefully separated the pages with tweezers and a small spatula, laying each one on absorbent paper to complete the drying process.

"The ink is iron gall," Dr. Okonkwo said, her voice barely above a whisper. "It's remarkably stable in low-oxygen environments. The names should be perfectly readable."

Names. Hundreds of them.

Over the next six hours, Lila methodically photographed each page with a high-resolution camera and transcribed the contents into a digital database. The Codex was everything Felix Beaumont had said it would be—a complete, meticulous record of the New Orleans gambling underworld from 1920 to 1933.

The ledger documented monthly payments to the mayor's office, regular bribes to police captains and detectives, payoffs to judges and court clerks, fixes of specific sporting events, political contributions that were actually protection money, and the complete list of the eight original investors with detailed accounts of their roles in the operation.

Some of the names Lila recognized from Oliver Bouchard's journal. Others were new—lesser-known figures who had played supporting roles but whose descendants would be equally affected.

But one name made her stop cold.

"Judge Harold P. Manning, 1932, $5,000 monthly retainer, paid in cash delivered to his chambers by a Belle Fleur courier every first of the month."

Harold P. Manning. The grandfather of the current Louisiana State Supreme Court justice, a man whose name appeared on university buildings and civic awards.

Lila continued reading.

"Mayor T. Semmes Walmsley, 1931-1933, $10,000 monthly."

"Superintendent of Police, George Reyer, $3,000 monthly."

"District Attorney, Eugene Stanley, $2,500 monthly."

The list went on. Every major figure in New Orleans law enforcement and government during the Prohibition era was on Bouchard's payroll.

And every one of them had descendants who were still in power.

"This is the bomb," Lila said quietly. "This is what the House has been trying to bury for a hundred years. And now we have finally found it. The truth is out."

Chapter 34: The House Revealed

Marcus met Lila at the archive early the next morning with news that changed everything they thought they knew about the true scope and scale of the far-reaching conspiracy.

"Simone is talking," he said, settling into a chair across from her desk, his expression a mixture of satisfaction and exhaustion. "She's given us the whole operation. Every detail, every name, every meeting going back thirty years. She's trying to save herself, but she's burying everyone else in the process."

"The House?" Lila asked, setting down the Codex fragment she had been transcribing.

"The House is the Bouchard Trust, but it's also more than that. It's a network of families—the Bouchards, the Landrys, the D'Argentos, the Manings, the Vitale branch that Tommy represents. They've been meeting in secret for a hundred years, managing the legacy of Oliver Bouchard's operation."

"How many families?"

"Eight. The original investors. Each family has a representative on the trust council. Simone was the Bouchard representative. Her father was the trustee before his illness."

"And they all knew about the Codex."

"They all knew it existed. They didn't know where it was. Oliver Bouchard kept that secret to himself, passing it only to his chosen successor—Felix Beaumont, his grandson."

"Felix was supposed to be the guardian of the Codex."

"Felix was supposed to protect it. Instead, he spent thirty years trying to expose it."

"That's why he was killed."

Marcus nodded. "Simone didn't pull the trigger, but she authorized it. She had a doctor on the trust's payroll who administered a drug that mimicked a heart attack."

Lila felt sick. "And Eddie Marchand?"

"Eddie found a page of the Codex that had been removed and hidden in the Belle Fleur's wall. He tried to sell it to Tommy Vitale. Simone found out and had him killed. She personally beat him to death behind the mooring station."

"My God."

"She's confessed to everything. She's hoping for a reduced sentence in exchange for testimony against the other trust members."

"So the whole operation is coming down."

"The DA is already drafting indictments. The Landrys, the D'Argentos, the Manings—they're going to be arrested within the week."

Lila looked at the Codex, now safely stored in the archive's vault.

"Then it's over."

"It's just beginning. The public fallout, the political scandals, the lawsuits—this is going to shake Louisiana to its foundation."

"Good," Lila said. "It's about time this city faced the truth about its past."

Chapter 35: The Trustee

Antoine Bouchard died three days after his daughter's arrest, in the same Garden District mansion where his family had lived for three generations.

Lila heard the news from Thomas, who called her early in the morning, his voice flat and exhausted in a way that went beyond simple grief.

"My father passed away in his sleep last night," Thomas said. "The cancer finally took him. He went quietly, which is more than he deserved."

"I'm sorry, Thomas. For your loss."

"Don't be. I made my peace with who he was a long time ago. He spent his whole life protecting a lie—the same lie his father told him and his grandfather told his father. The Bouchard Trust was built on secrets, and he was the last true believer in keeping them." There was a pause, and Lila heard him exhale slowly. "At least Simone is finally telling the truth. That's more than my father ever did."

"Are you going to be okay? With everything that's happening?"

"I'm going to dissolve the trust. Every asset, every property, every investment—I'm liquidating it all. The Belle Fleur will be sold to the highest bidder. The proceeds will go to charities that support addiction treatment and gambling recovery. It's the only thing I can do to make amends for what my family built."

"And the Codex? The third fragment?"

"The safe in my father's study is now mine. I've already removed the fragment and given it to Marcus Thibodeau for evidence. Do whatever you think is right with the information. My family has been deciding what to do with the truth for a hundred years, and look where it got us. I think it's time the public got a chance to decide for themselves."

Lila hung up and looked at the complete Codex, now digitized and stored in three separate locations. The trustee was dead. The trust was dissolving. The House was falling, brick by brick.

But the moral question remained.

Should she publish the names of the dead, knowing their descendants would suffer? Should she protect the innocent by burying the guilty? She thought about Felix Beaumont, who had died trying to expose the truth. She thought about Eddie Marchand, who had been killed for finding a fragment of it. She thought about Henri Lambert, who had waited ninety-seven years to pass on his chip.

They had all believed the truth was worth dying for.

Lila made her decision.

Chapter 36: The Confrontation

Lila visited Simone in jail at the Orleans Parish Prison, sitting across from her at a scarred metal table in a room that smelled of disinfectant and regret.

Simone looked smaller in prison orange than she had in her tailored black suits aboard the Belle Fleur. Her hair was uncharacteristically disheveled, pulled back in a simple ponytail, and her shoulders slumped in a way that spoke of defeated pride. The polished, ruthlessly efficient director of casino operations was gone, replaced by a woman who had lost everything.

"Why did you come?" Simone asked through the partition glass, her voice flat.

"I wanted to understand. Before the trial, before the sentencing. I needed to know why."

"Understand what? Why I killed Eddie Marchand? Why I covered up the Codex for thirty years? Why I tried to have you killed in my own home?"

"All of it. Why you protected a system that was rotten from the beginning."

Simone was silent for a long time. When she spoke, her voice was tired, stripped of the polished confidence she had worn like armor.

"I was raised to protect the family. The trust, the casino, the legacy—it was all I knew. My father told me the Codex was a weapon that could destroy us all. He said I had to do whatever it took to keep it buried."

"And you believed him."

"I had no reason not to. He was my father. He was dying. He was trying to protect me."

"He was trying to protect himself."

Simone's eyes glistened. "I know that now. But I didn't know it then. I thought I was doing the right thing—keeping the peace, maintaining order, preventing chaos."

"By killing people."

"I never wanted to kill anyone. Eddie was supposed to be a warning. I hit him harder than I meant to." Her voice cracked. "I've had to live with that for eleven years."

"And Felix?"

"I didn't kill Felix. The doctor did. But I authorized it. I gave the order."

"Then you're as guilty as anyone."

Simone nodded slowly. "I am. And I'll spend the rest of my life paying for it."

Lila stood up. "The Codex is being published. Every name, every bribe, every fixed game. The truth is coming out, Simone."

"I know."

"Your father's name will be in it. Your family's legacy will be destroyed."

"I know."

"Is there anything you want to say? Any explanation you want the public to hear?"

Simone pressed her hand against the glass. "Tell them I'm sorry. Tell them I was wrong. Tell them the truth was always going to come out, and I was a fool to think I could stop it."

Lila pressed her hand against the glass on her side.

"I will."

She turned and walked away.

Chapter 37: The Moral Question

The media firestorm began the day after the Codex was released to the public.

Lila had worked with Elise through the night to organize the documents, annotate them with historical context, and prepare a press release that emphasized the historical nature of the evidence. The archive's website crashed within hours of the release, overwhelmed by traffic from around the world.

The names made headlines across the state within hours, and across the nation by the evening news.

"PROHIBITION-ERA PAYOFFS REVEALED: STATE SUPREME COURT JUSTICE'S GRANDFATHER NAMED IN BRIBERY SCANDAL"

"OLD NEW ORLEANS MONEY: HOW THE BOUCHARD TRUST CONTROLLED THE CITY FOR A CENTURY"

"RIVERBOAT CODEX EXPOSES CENTURY OF CORRUPTION"

The phone rang constantly. Reporters, lawyers, descendants of the named officials, all wanting to comment, to deny, to explain.

Lila stopped answering.

She sat in the cabin on Lake Pontchartrain, the complete set of eight poker chips spread on the table in front of her. The cipher had been solved. The Codex had been found. The truth had been told.

But she didn't feel victorious.

She felt exhausted.

"You did the right thing," Rollie said, sitting across from her. "Felix would be proud."

"Would he? He spent thirty years looking for this, and it cost him his life. Henri spent ninety-seven years keeping the secret. Eddie Marchand died for a single page."

"They all believed the truth was worth dying for."

"I know. But I'm the one who lived. And I'm the one who has to live with the consequences."

Rollie was quiet for a long moment, the chip in his weathered hand catching the firelight. Then he reached across the table and picked up one of the chips, the concentric circles of the first chip.

"You know what these taught me?"

"What?"

"That every secret has a cost. Every lie has a price. Felix knew that. Henri knew that. And now you know it too."

Lila picked up the eighth chip, the crescent moon and star.

"Felix's note said to look in the plaster," she said. "When I found it, I thought it was just a clue. But now I think it was more than that. He was telling me to look beneath the surface. To find the truth hidden in plain sight."

"That's what you've done."

"And now that the truth is out, I don't know what to do next."

Rollie smiled. "You'll figure it out. You always do."

Chapter 38: Simone's Truth

The trial of Simone Bouchard began six months after her arrest, on a humid September morning in the same Orleans Parish courthouse where her family had once held sway over judges and prosecutors through the bribes carefully documented in the Codex.

Lila attended the opening statements, sitting in the third row of the wood-paneled courtroom, watching as the woman who had tried to kill her faced the justice system she had spent a lifetime manipulating. The gallery was packed with reporters, curious onlookers, and families whose names appeared in the Codex.

The prosecution's case was overwhelming. Marcus testified for two days, walking the jury through every step of the investigation—the chips, the cipher, the fragments, Simone's full confession in which she admitted to authorizing Felix's murder and personally killing Eddie Marchand. Thomas Bouchard testified about the trust's secret operations. Tommy Vitale, already awaiting his own trial, testified about the night of Eddie Marchand's murder and the cover-up that followed.

And Lila testified about the Codex itself—about the cipher, the translation, the discovery of the fragments, the revelation of a century of secrets.

Simone sat at the defense table, her face impassive, her hands folded in front of her. When the prosecution played the recording of her confession—her voice flat and unemotional as she described authorizing Felix Beaumont's murder—a collective gasp went through the courtroom. Simone did not flinch.

The defense argued that Simone had been acting under duress, that her dying father had manipulated her, that she was a victim of a family legacy of corruption. But the evidence was too strong. The jury deliberated for six hours and returned with a verdict of guilty on all counts.

The judge sentenced Simone to twenty-five years to life in state prison, to be served at the Louisiana Correctional Institute for Women. There would be no possibility of parole for at least twenty years.

As she was led from the courtroom in handcuffs, Simone caught Lila's eye. She did not look away. She simply nodded—a small, almost imperceptible gesture of acknowledgment—and then she was gone through the steel door.

"The House is empty," Marcus said, standing beside Lila.

"Not empty. Just quiet." Lila watched the courtroom doors swing shut. "There are still seven families out there, still powerful, still connected. The Codex exposed the past, but it didn't change the present."

"Is that enough?"

"I don't know. But it's a start."

Chapter 39: The Missing Chip

A week after the trial, Lila received a package in the mail.

She was sorting through the stack of bills and junk mail that had accumulated at the cabin's post office box when she noticed it—a small parcel wrapped in brown kraft paper, tied with twine, no return address. The handwriting on the front was old-fashioned, elegant, with flourishes on the capital letters.

She opened it carefully on the cabin's kitchen table, sliding a knife under the twine so as not to damage whatever was inside.

Inside, nestled in a bed of cotton batting, was a single poker chip.

It was old, worn, the color of aged ivory. The edges were smooth from decades of handling, and the clay had developed the fine crackle pattern that marked genuine antique composition chips. On its face was a symbol she had never seen before—an eye within a triangle, surrounded by rays of light emanating outward.

There was a note attached, written on heavy cream paper: "You found the Codex, but you missed the last secret. The eighth chip was never about the Codex. It was about what comes next. — A Friend"

Lila turned the chip over in her hands, examining it from every angle. On the reverse side, engraved in tiny, precise letters that she almost missed, was a set of coordinates.

She grabbed her phone and mapped them.

The coordinates led to a location in the French Quarter—a small park near Jackson Square, not far from the statue of Andrew Jackson where she had found Eleanor Weiss's journal.

She called Marcus.

"I received something strange in the mail."

"Strange how?"

"Strange as in a ninth poker chip with a symbol I've never seen before." She described the chip, the eye within the triangle, the rays of light. "I thought we had accounted for all eight. But this is a ninth chip. With an entirely different symbol."

"There were only eight original investors. That's been established since the beginning."

"So where did this one come from? Who made it? And why send it to me now?"

"Maybe it's a fake. Someone trying to profit from the publicity, or someone who wants to send you on a wild goose chase."

"Maybe." Lila turned the chip over in her hands again, studying the engraved coordinates. "But the coordinates lead to a real location in the French Quarter. To a park near Jackson Square. That seems like a lot of effort for a hoax."

"Don't go alone. Whatever that chip leads to, we don't know who sent it or why."

"I wasn't planning to." She tucked the chip into her pocket. "But I think I know someone who might be able to explain what this means. Someone who's been keeping secrets from me since the beginning."

Chapter 40: Rollie's Confession

Rollie was waiting for her when she arrived at the cabin that evening.

He was sitting on the porch, a glass of whiskey in his hand, staring out at the lake.

"You found the ninth chip," he said without turning.

Lila stopped. "How did you know?"

"Because I sent it to you."

She sat down beside him, the chip in her hand. "Why?"

"Because I've been lying to you, Dr. Vance. And I need to tell you the truth before it's too late."

"What truth?"

Rollie took a long sip of whiskey. "Felix didn't find the Codex by accident. He knew where it was all along. He was the one who hid it."

"What?"

"The Codex was never lost. Oliver Bouchard gave it to his grandson—Felix—and told him to keep it safe. Felix split it into three parts and hid them. The bridge. The Old Mint. And the river."

"But Felix spent thirty years searching for it."

"That was the story. The search was a cover—a way to build a legend, to attract attention, to make people believe the Codex was lost when it was actually protected."

Lila stared at him. "You knew?"

"I knew from the beginning. Felix told me everything. He told me about the chips, the cipher, the three hiding places. He said the Codex was too dangerous to exist and too important to destroy. So he created a puzzle that would take someone of extraordinary intelligence to solve."

"He created the cipher?"

"He hired a mathematician in 1960 to design it. A woman named Dr. Eleanor Weiss. She was your predecessor, in a way—a cryptanalyst who specialized in recreational combinatorics."

"Eleanor Weiss. I've read her papers. She died in 1975."

"She taught Felix everything he needed to know about ciphers. And then she designed the chip code—a puzzle that would require someone of similar training to solve."

Lila's mind reeled. "The entire investigation. The chips. The cipher. The search. It was all a test?"

"It was a selection process. Felix knew he wouldn't live forever. He needed someone to carry on the work—someone with the skills to find the Codex and the integrity to publish it."

"And I was that person?"

"You were that person." Rollie turned to face her. "I'm sorry for lying to you. But Felix made me promise to keep the secret until the Codex was found."

"Why are you telling me now?"

"Because of the ninth chip." He took it from her hand. "This was Felix's personal chip. He had it made after Eleanor died. It contains one final message—a set of coordinates that lead to the last piece of the puzzle."

"The last piece?"

"Eleanor Weiss kept a journal. A complete record of how she designed the cipher, why she chose the symbols, what each one means. It's buried in Jackson Square, under the statue of Andrew Jackson."

"And you want me to find it."

"I want you to have the complete record. So that when historians study the Codex, they'll know the whole story." Rollie pressed the chip into her hand. "You've earned it, Dr. Vance. Every piece of it."

Chapter 41: The Master Translation

The statue of Andrew Jackson in Jackson Square was surrounded by tourists when Lila arrived at dawn.

She had the ninth chip in her pocket and a small trowel in her bag. She had studied the coordinates carefully the night before—the spot was directly beneath the statue's pedestal, on the west side, where the shadow of the horse's tail fell at exactly 9:17 AM on July 4.

Independence Day. Eleanor Weiss had a sense of humor.

Lila waited until the crowd thinned, pretending to study a map of the French Quarter on her phone. When the area around the statue was clear, she knelt beside the pedestal, pretending to tie her shoe. She scraped at the dirt with the trowel, her heart pounding, expecting at any moment to hear a voice demanding to know what she was doing.

Six inches down, her trowel hit something solid.

A small metal box, the size of a deck of cards, wrapped in oilcloth that had preserved it from moisture. She tucked it into her bag, stood up, and walked away with the calm of someone who had done nothing wrong. Her pulse was racing, but her face betrayed nothing.

Back at the cabin, she opened the box with hands that trembled just slightly.

Inside was a leather-bound journal, exactly as Rollie had described. But it was far more than a dry record of the cipher's design. It was Eleanor Weiss's personal diary, spanning the years 1958 to 1975—seventeen years of thoughts, fears, and confessions from the woman who had designed the puzzle that had consumed Lila's life.

She had written about Felix Beaumont, about the Codex, about the moral weight of what they were creating together. She described late nights in her study, wrestling with the ethics of designing a cipher that would hide the truth rather than reveal it. "Felix says the cipher is a test," she wrote in one entry. "He says only the right person will be able to solve it. But who are we to decide who is worthy of the truth?"

The final entry, dated two days before her death in 1975, was clear:

"I have designed the cipher to be solved by someone like me—a woman who sees patterns where others see chaos. When she finds the Codex, she will know what to do with it. I only hope she is braver than I have been. I have spent my life hiding the truth. I hope she will have the courage to set it free."

Lila closed the journal and held it against her chest. Eleanor Weiss had been her predecessor in more ways than one—a brilliant woman whose work had been overlooked, whose contributions had been erased from the record. But she had left a message, waiting forty years for someone to find it.

"I'll be brave enough for both of us," Lila whispered.

She had the complete record now. The cipher, the history, the moral questions—all of it.

And she knew what she had to do.

Chapter 42: The Live Player

The investigation took an unexpected turn when Lila cross-referenced the Codex names with current public officials.

She had been working with Elise to create a comprehensive database, matching historical names with their modern descendants. Most were straightforward—grandfathers, great-uncles, distant cousins.

But one name made her stop.

Senator William Landry, the current senior U.S. Senator from Louisiana, appeared in a footnote to the Codex. Not as a bribe recipient—he had been born in 1950, long after Prohibition ended. But as a beneficiary.

The Codex revealed that Senator Landry's father, James Landry, had been one of the original investors in the Belle Fleur. And that the Landry family had been receiving trust payments ever since.

Payments that continued to this day.

"Senator Landry is currently receiving income from the Bouchard Trust," Lila said, staring at the document on her laptop screen. "He's a beneficiary of illegal gambling proceeds that have been laundered through shell companies for ninety years. The payments have been routed through a real estate holding company in Delaware and a consulting firm in Baton Rouge."

"That's a live player," Marcus said, leaning over her shoulder to read the screen. "Someone who can still be prosecuted. A sitting United States Senator, receiving money from a criminal enterprise."

"If the trust is dissolved, those payments will stop. But the fact that they existed at all—that the Landry family has been profiting from illegal gambling for nearly a century—that's front-page news in every paper in the country."

"You're going to expose him."

"I'm going to give the information to the FBI. Let them decide how to proceed. It's not my job to destroy a Senator's career. It's my job to make sure the truth is available to the people whose job it is."

Lila made the call. She spoke to a special agent in the FBI's New Orleans field office, explaining what she had found, offering to turn over all the documentation. Within hours, the FBI had opened a formal investigation into Senator Landry's finances.

The political firestorm was immediate and devastating. Reporters camped outside the Senator's office in Washington. Cable news ran the story on a loop. The Senator denied everything, of course, claiming complete ignorance of his father's involvement with the Belle Fleur. He blamed the allegations on "political enemies" and "a vendetta by disgraced academics."

But the paper trail was clear. The payments were documented. The shell companies were traced. And when the FBI released its preliminary findings, Senator Landry's denial crumbled.

He announced his retirement three weeks later, citing health reasons.

"The Codex just claimed its first living victim," Marcus said, watching the Senator's resignation speech on television.

"It's not about victims," Lila replied. "It's about accountability. Senator Landry may not have known about the source of his family's wealth, but he benefited from it. The truth had to come out."

"And the next time someone asks if the Codex was worth finding?"

Lila looked at the Senator's pale face on the screen. "We'll say yes. Because the alternative—letting the lies continue—would have been worse."

Chapter 43: The Choice

The publication of the Codex created a deep and painful divide in New Orleans, a city that had always been comfortable with its secrets.

On one side were those who believed the truth had to be told, no matter the cost. They argued that the historical record was finally complete, that the corruption of the past could finally be acknowledged and addressed, and that the families who had benefited from a century of secrecy deserved to have their names known.

On the other side were those who saw the Codex as a weapon—a tool for settling old scores, destroying families, and reopening wounds that had healed long ago. They argued that the dead could not defend themselves, that their descendants were innocent, and that the publication served no purpose but vengeance.

Lila found herself at the center of the debate. She was invited to speak at universities, on news programs, at public forums hosted by historical societies and civic organizations. Everyone wanted to hear from the woman who had cracked the poker chip cipher and exposed the Riverboat Codex.

But the question they all asked was the same: Was it worth it?

"History is not comfortable," she said at a public lecture at Tulane. "It is not polite. It is not fair. But it is true. And the truth, however uncomfortable, is better than the lie."

A woman in the audience stood up. Her voice was shaking.

"My grandfather was named in that book. He was a police captain in 1932. He took bribes. He was a corrupt man, and he died disgraced. I have spent my entire life trying to escape his legacy."

Lila waited.

"I never knew about the bribes until the Codex was published," the woman continued. "I only knew my grandfather as the man who taught me to fish, who took me to church, who loved me. And now—now I have to reconcile that man with the man in the book."

"How do you do it?" Lila asked gently.

"I don't know. That's why I came here. I thought you might have an answer."

Lila looked at the woman, at the pain in her eyes.

"I don't have an answer," she said. "I have the truth. And I believe the truth is better than the alternative. But I don't pretend that it's easy."

The woman nodded slowly and sat down.

The lecture hall was silent.

Chapter 44: The Leak

The leak came from inside the archive, a betrayal that set back their careful plans for a controlled release.

Someone had copied the Codex files and sold them to a tabloid website for five thousand dollars before the official release. The website published a partial list of names, stripped of context and historical explanation, with sensational headlines about "corruption rings" and "mafia payoffs." The website published a partial list of names, stripped of context and historical explanation, with sensational headlines about "corruption rings" and "mafia payoffs."

The damage was immediate and indiscriminate.

Families named in the partial list were inundated with calls from reporters at all hours. Some were innocent—descendants who had no knowledge of their ancestors' activities, people who had built their lives far from Louisiana and had no idea their great-grandfathers had taken bribes. Others were guilty, but the incomplete information made it impossible to distinguish between a person who had knowingly benefited from corruption and one who had never heard the family secret.

Lila confronted Elise in her office at the archive.

"Who had access to the files before the official release?"

"Only me, Dr. Okonkwo, and the archive director," Elise said, her face pale. "We all signed nondisclosure agreements. We all understood the sensitivity of the material."

"Someone broke that agreement. Someone took money to leak these names before we had a chance to present them with context."

"It wasn't me, Lila. You have to believe me. I've been working on this project for months. I would never compromise it."

"I want to believe you. But someone leaked the files, and now innocent people are being harassed. A woman in Shreveport called the archive this morning in tears because reporters showed up at her daughter's school."

"I know." Elise's voice was barely a whisper. "I'm going to conduct an internal investigation. If I find out who did it, they'll be fired, prosecuted, and I'll make sure they never work in this profession again."

"Do that." Lila's voice was cold. "And when you find them, tell them that the Codex was supposed to be about justice. Instead, they've made it about profit. They've hurt real people for a few thousand dollars."

She walked out of the archive and didn't look back.

The leak was traced three days later to a junior archivist who had been paid five thousand dollars by a news outlet. He was fired, arrested, and charged with theft of public records.

But the damage was done.

Lila began to wonder if the truth was ever worth the cost of telling it.

Chapter 45: The Firestorm

The firestorm consumed New Orleans.

Senator Landry's retirement was just the beginning. Within a month of the Codex's publication, three state judges had resigned, two city council members had been indicted for corruption charges dating back decades, and the president of the state university system had been forced to step down after it was revealed his grandfather had been one of the Belle Fleur's silent partners.

The Bouchard Trust was dissolved by court order. The Belle Fleur itself was sold to a national casino chain that promised to rebrand and distance itself from the scandal. The historic riverboat was towed to a shipyard at the edge of the city, its gambling license revoked, its fate uncertain. Preservationists fought to save it as a museum; developers wanted to turn it into condominiums.

And the families named in the Codex began to sue.

Lila was named in six separate defamation lawsuits, all filed by descendants who claimed the Codex had falsely accused their ancestors. The lawsuits were meritless—the Codex was a historical document, a primary source from the 1920s, not a modern accusation—but they were designed to drain her resources, consume her time, and silence her through attrition.

She spent her savings on lawyers. The legal fees mounted. Her part-time job at Vance & Associates had been put on indefinite hold, and the cabin on Lake Pontchartrain was the only place she could afford to live.

"Maybe I should just stop talking," she told Marcus one night, sitting on the cabin porch, watching the moonlight on the lake. "Maybe the silence would be better than the fighting."

"Silence is what they want," Marcus said. "Silence is how the House survived for a hundred years. Every time someone got close to the truth, the House found a way to shut them up. You're the first person who hasn't given in."

"I know. But I'm tired, Marcus. I'm so tired. Every time I open my mouth, someone files another lawsuit. Every time I give an interview, another family calls me a liar. I didn't ask for any of this."

"Neither did Eddie Marchand. Neither did Felix. They didn't get a choice. You do."

She looked at the chip in her hand—the one Rollie had given her, the final piece of the puzzle that had started everything.

"Felix used to say the truth sets you free," she said. "But it doesn't feel like freedom. It feels like a weight I have to carry every single day."

"That's because you're carrying it for everyone else," Marcus said gently. "For Felix, for Henri, for Eddie Marchand. For everyone who was told that the past was unknowable and the truth was unreachable. Once the truth is out there, it stops being yours alone."

"And becomes everyone's."

"Exactly. You've done your part. Now the rest of the world has to do theirs."

Lila nodded slowly and looked out at the water. She was still carrying the weight. But maybe, for the first time, she didn't have to carry it alone. "Then I'll keep carrying it. Until I can't anymore."

Chapter 46: The Arson

The fire broke out at the State Archive at three in the morning, while Lila was asleep at the cabin on the lake, dreaming fitfully of the dark Mississippi River.

Lila received the call from Elise, her voice barely controlled, shaking with adrenaline and shock.

"The archive is on fire. They hit the vault. The basement is fully involved—the fire department says it's a total loss."

"Who did this?"

"I don't know. But the vault was specifically targeted. The fire started there, in the oldest section of the basement, right where we stored the physical Codex materials in their archival boxes."

Lila dressed and drove to the archive through empty streets, her hands gripping the steering wheel so hard her knuckles were white. When she arrived, the building was surrounded by fire trucks, their lights painting the night in alternating red and white. Hoses snaked across the lawn like great serpents, and thick black smoke billowed from the basement windows, carrying the acrid smell of burning paper and melting metal.

She found Elise standing in the street, wrapped in a police blanket, staring at the flames with hollow shock.

"The vault?"

"Gone. Everything in it. The physical copies of the Codex, the original fragments, the chips—all of it. The fire reached temperatures high enough to melt the vault's locking mechanism."

"Who would do this?"

"The same people who have been trying to destroy the Codex for a hundred years," Elise said bitterly. "The House is not dead. It's just adapted. They couldn't stop the digital copies from being released, so they destroyed what they could reach."

Lila looked at the flames consuming the archive, eating through a century of history. "We have the digital copies backed up in three different states. They can't burn those."

"They can try. But you're right. The truth is out there now, published and disseminated, and they can't put it back in the box no matter how hard they try."

The fire burned for six hours, consuming everything in its path. By the time it was extinguished, the basement was a ruin of ash and melted metal, the vault door warped by the heat. But the digital copies of the Codex were safe, backed up on secure servers in three different states. The House had failed.

The House had failed to destroy the truth. But they had made their point clear: they would never stop trying.

Chapter 47: The Archive

The investigation into the arson was led by the ATF, not NOPD, given the use of accelerants.

They found evidence of forced entry through a basement window. The arsonist had used a chemical accelerant—gasoline mixed with industrial solvent—to ensure the fire would burn hot enough to destroy the vault's contents.

Security cameras had been disabled thirty minutes before the fire started. The arsonist knew the building's layout.

"Someone on the inside," Marcus said. "Or someone with access to the building plans."

"The House has resources. They could have gotten the plans anywhere."

"Maybe. But the timing is suspicious. The fire happened the same week the trust was dissolved. Someone wanted to destroy the evidence before the new owners could secure it."

"Or someone wanted to send a message."

"To whom?"

"To me." Lila looked at the charred remains of the archive basement. "They want me to know that they can still reach me. That they can destroy anything I touch."

"Then we need to make sure they can't touch anything else."

Marcus made a call. Within hours, Lila's cabin was under police protection. The digital copies of the Codex were moved to a classified server at the Louisiana State Police headquarters.

And Lila began carrying a gun.

"I never thought it would come to this," she said, handling the compact pistol that Marcus had given her.

"The House has been operating for a hundred years. They're not going to stop just because we found their secrets."

Lila looked at the charred remains of the archive basement, at the firefighters still hosing down hotspots, at the ruin of the physical evidence that she had spent months assembling.

"Then we need to make sure they can't touch anything else."

Marcus made a call. Within hours, Lila's cabin was under police protection. The digital copies of the Codex were moved to a classified server at the Louisiana State Police headquarters, accessible only with biometric authentication.

And Lila began carrying a gun.

"I never thought it would come to this," she said, handling the compact pistol that Marcus had given her. It was a Glock 19, standard police issue, and it felt alien and heavy in her hands.

"The House has been operating for a hundred years," Marcus said. "They killed Eddie Marchand. They killed Felix Beaumont. They tried to kill you. They're not going to stop just because we found their secrets and published their names."

"Then we need to make sure they have no choice but to stop."

"That's what the trials are for." Marcus looked at her with grim certainty. "We have the arsonist. We have Simone's confession. We have the digital Codex. The justice system is finally working. But it's going to take time."

"How much time?"

"Months. Maybe a year. The council members will hire the best lawyers in Louisiana. They'll try to drag this out until the public forgets."

"Then we make sure the public doesn't forget." Lila tucked the gun into her bag. "We keep talking. We keep publishing. We make sure that every time they try to bury the truth, we dig it back up."

Chapter 48: The Trial

The arsonist was arrested three weeks after the fire that destroyed the archive basement, tracked down through security footage from a gas station where he had purchased the accelerant and positively identified by a former coworker at the Belle Fleur casino.

He was a former security guard at the Belle Fleur, a man in his late sixties named Chester Roy, who had worked for Simone Bouchard for thirty years before being laid off when the casino was sold. Under questioning by the ATF, he broke down and admitted everything. He had been paid five thousand dollars to set the fire, he said. He had acted on the orders of "the trust council," though he had never met its members face to face. He had simply done what he was told, without asking questions, without ever considering the consequences of his actions.

"The House is still operational," Marcus said. "Even with Simone in prison and the trust dissolved, there's a council that's been running things behind the scenes."

"Who's on it?"

"We don't know yet. The arsonist only communicated with a single intermediary. But we're tracking the phone records."

The arsonist's trial was swift. He pleaded guilty in exchange for a reduced sentence and named his contact—a lawyer who had represented the Bouchard Trust for forty years.

The lawyer was arrested, and he began to talk.

The trust council, he revealed, consisted of five members—representatives of the Landry, D'Argento, Manning, Vitale, and Bouchard families. They had been meeting quarterly for decades, managing the trust's assets, distributing payments, and ensuring that the secrets of the Codex remained buried.

The last meeting of the trust council had taken place the night before the archive fire.

"They voted to destroy the Codex," the lawyer said. "All five of them. Unanimous."

"What happened to the Vitale representative?" Lila asked.

"Tommy Vitale was the representative. He voted to destroy the Codex."

Lila felt a chill. Tommy had been helping her, guiding her, feeding her information. And all along, he had been a member of the council that wanted the Codex destroyed.

"He played us," she said. "The entire time."

"He was protecting his family's interests," Marcus said. "Same as Simone."

"Then he's no better than she is."

"No. He's not. But at least the truth about the Codex and the House is finally coming out for everyone to see. That has to count for something, doesn't it?"

Chapter 49: The Verdict

The trust council members were indicted on charges of conspiracy, arson, obstruction of justice, and racketeering. The charges carried a combined maximum sentence of forty years.

Tommy Vitale was arrested at his home in the Garden District, early in the morning, with cameras capturing his perp walk. He did not resist. He was dressed in a suit, as if he had been expecting them, and he went quietly.

"I'm sorry, Dr. Vance," he said as the police led him past her. "I wanted the Codex found. I truly did. But I couldn't let it be published. My family—"

"Your family has been profiting from corruption for a hundred years, Mr. Vitale. You sat on the council that voted to destroy the truth. You helped cover up murder."

"And now they'll pay for it." Tommy's face was gray, the color of ash. "I know that. I've always known that. I've spent every day of the last eleven years knowing that Eddie Marchand died because of what my family was part of. But I loved them too much to let them fall."

"Love doesn't excuse what you did."

"I know." He looked at her, his eyes wet with tears he did not bother to wipe away. "I know."

The trial of the trust council lasted six grueling weeks. The prosecution presented a mountain of evidence: the digital Codex with its list of names and payments, the arson investigation linking the fire to the council's orders, Simone Bouchard's detailed testimony from prison, and the phone records showing communication between council members on the night of the fire.

The defense argued that the trust council was a historical preservation society, not a criminal enterprise. They claimed the Codex was a forgery, the arson was the work of a rogue employee, and Simone was a convicted murderer seeking to reduce her sentence by lying about others.

The jury deliberated for four days.

When they returned, the foreman read the verdict for each of the five defendants: guilty on all counts.

Tommy Vitale received eight years in federal prison. The other four council members received sentences ranging from twelve to twenty years, depending on their level of involvement.

As the gavel came down, Marcus turned to Lila with an expression of quiet satisfaction. "This is the end of the House. The council is broken. The trust is dissolved. The secrets are public."

"It's the end of this House," Lila agreed. "But there will be others. Corruption doesn't die—it just changes forms."

"Then we'll be ready for the next one."

"It's the end of this chapter," Lila corrected. "But the House existed because the system allowed it. The corruption, the payoffs, the secrecy—those conditions haven't changed. The House could rise again."

"Then we'll be ready."

"Will we?"

Marcus stopped walking. "You saved the truth, Lila. You preserved a piece of history that would have been lost. That matters."

"Does it?"

"It matters to Eddie Marchand's family. It matters to Felix Beaumont's memory. It matters to everyone who was told that the past was unknowable, that the truth was unreachable." He put a hand on her shoulder. "You proved them wrong."

Lila looked at the sky, at the sun setting over the Mississippi River.

"I hope that's enough," she said.

Chapter 50: The Last Hand

One year later, Lila stood on the deck of the Belle Fleur for the final time.

The riverboat had been converted into a museum and floating restaurant. The gaming tables were gone, replaced by exhibits on the history of riverboat gambling in Louisiana. The green felt had been removed, the slot machines sold off.

But the river was the same, flowing past, dark and eternal.

The museum curator had invited Lila to the grand opening, offering her a place of honor. She had accepted, but she stood apart from the crowd, watching the water.

"Dr. Vance."

She turned. A young woman stood behind her—perhaps twenty-five, with dark hair and sharp eyes.

"I'm sorry to interrupt. My name is Maya Marchand. I'm Eddie Marchand's daughter."

Lila's breath caught. "Your father—"

"I was four when he died. I never really knew him. But I grew up hearing about the Codex, about the investigation, about the woman who finally put the puzzle together."

"I'm sorry about your father."

"He was a cheat and a thief. I know that. But he didn't deserve to die the way he did." Maya held out her hand. "I wanted to thank you. For finding the truth. For giving his death a meaning."

Lila shook her hand. "It was the least I could do."

"It was everything." Maya smiled, a sad, knowing expression. "I'm a cryptanalyst now. I just finished my PhD at MIT. Recreational combinatorics, specializing in historical cipher systems."

Lila felt a smile spread across her face. "You're following in your father's footsteps."

"I'm following in yours."

They stood together, looking at the river.

"Will you stay in New Orleans?" Maya asked.

"I've been offered a research position at Tulane. Reinstated, with an apology. They want me to head a new institute for historical cryptography."

"Are you going to accept?"

"I think so. There are more puzzles out there. More secrets waiting to be uncovered." Lila looked at the Mississippi, at the water that had hidden the Codex for ninety years. "The river doesn't give up all its secrets at once."

"No," Maya agreed. "But you've given it a good start."

They shook hands one last time.

"Take care of yourself, Dr. Vance."

"You too, Dr. Marchand."

Maya walked away, disappearing into the crowd inside the museum.

Lila stayed on the deck, watching the sun set over the river.

She reached into her pocket and pulled out the eight chips. The symbols that had started it all. The cipher that had led her to the truth.

She had thought about keeping them, about framing them in a display case, about passing them down to future generations. But that wasn't what Felix would have wanted.

She walked to the railing and, one by one, dropped the chips into the Mississippi.

They hit the water with small splashes and vanished beneath the surface.

The river took them back.

The Codex was out in the world. The truth was public. The secrets were no longer secrets.

And Lila Vance was finally free.

She turned away from the railing and walked into the museum, into the light, into whatever came next.

The last hand had been played. And she had won.
