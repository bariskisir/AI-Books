Chapter 1: Red Initials

Lena Orsay arrived at the concert hall archive with her field case in one hand and the habit of distrust in the other. The matter before her was simple only from a distance: the disputed full score is presented under glass, its oxblood title leaf too handsome for the archive around it. Up close it had edges, and every edge had to be named before anyone could claim certainty. Rain tapped the high windows and made the archive lamps seem warmer than they were. The the red initials waited in the center of the work, asking for belief with the confidence of something recently arranged.

foundation chair Ivor Pell stood nearby, close enough to watch but not close enough to touch. "Beautiful," foundation said. Lena looked at the object, then at the room around it. "Maybe," she answered, or wanted to answer, depending on how useful restraint seemed. Lena did not answer at once. In cultural property disputes, the first performance was almost always innocence. Everyone admired the thing. Everyone regretted the conflict. Everyone hoped the paperwork would be kind.

She had learned not to flatter old things. Age deserved care, not romance. A stain could be a century of hands or last Tuesday's careless tea. A trimmed edge could mean rescue or theft. Beautiful objects often asked to be believed before they deserved belief, and Lena's profession existed to disappoint them politely.

She began with the physical facts. Light from the table lamp moved across the surface and found what ordinary admiration missed: a pressure mark near one edge, a color sitting high on the fiber, a correction shaped by habit rather than age. Lena measured, photographed, and measured again. The tape on her ruler had been replaced twice; the caution behind it had not. The table light flattened every object into fact: fiber, ink, pressure, shadow. She did not speak while she worked because talk made premature patterns.

When she finally asked questions, she kept them short. Who had opened the room. Who had requested the box. Who had described the item before it was seen. Who had money waiting on the other side of authenticity. The answers came with pauses, and the pauses were useful. It was the sort of detail that looked minor until it was alone with a lamp. A genuine object could survive inconvenience. A false one needed people to tidy the inconvenience away.

Petra never raised her voice in the lab. She did not need to. Solvents, scopes, and fiber samples gave her all the authority she wanted. When she doubted something, she made the doubt measurable. When she trusted something, she still tested it twice.

The day's argument narrowed around the red initials. It was not enough for the object to look old, or beloved, or plausible under glass. It had to explain itself among neighbors: catalog entries, rehearsal marks, donor language, invoices, old hands, newer hands, and the choir's stubborn history of use. Lena set each neighbor beside it in her notes. The comparison did not make the matter simpler. It made it honest.

By late afternoon, the others had begun to understand the shape of the problem. foundation stopped defending the first impression and started watching the evidence accumulate. The room's mood changed from ceremony to labor. That change mattered. Fraud preferred ceremony. Labor asked where the thread entered, why the stamp vanished, when the photograph was taken, and who benefited if nobody asked another question.

By the time Lena closed her notebook, the chapter of evidence had acquired a direction.

Ivor tapped the glass with one nail until Lena looked at his hand. She asked him not to do it again. The request embarrassed him more than a reprimand would have. He stepped back, smoothing his tie, and spoke of donor confidence while she noted the fresh mat board, the new cradle, and the way the manuscript had been staged to face visitors before anyone had finished asking what it was.

The chair cleared his throat, but the glass case had already lost its power to command the room.

Before she left, Lena wrote a final line beneath the chapter title in her notebook: Red Initials. It was not literary; it was a label for the pressure the day had found. When they left the room, the object remained under light, less mysterious and more dangerous.
