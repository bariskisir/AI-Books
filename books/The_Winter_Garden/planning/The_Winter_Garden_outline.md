# The Winter Garden — Outline

## Premise

A modern landscape architect, Alice March, is hired to restore a derelict walled garden on a Suffolk estate. As she peels back layers of neglect, she uncovers evidence that the garden was designed in 1916 by Captain James Ashford, a young soldier on his final leave before returning to the Western Front. The garden was planned as a gift for his fiancee, Eleanor Grey — but Ashford never came home, and Eleanor vanished from history. Through Ashford's letters buried in the soil and the garden's coded plantings, Alice pieces together a love story cut short by war, while confronting her own retreat from the world.

## Core Cast

- **Alice March** — A landscape architect in her late thirties, grieving a divorce and a stalled career. She has fled London for the Suffolk countryside.
- **Captain James Ashford** — A talented amateur botanist and garden designer, 25 years old in 1916. Commissioned into the Suffolk Regiment. Gentle, observant, haunted by what he has seen in France.
- **Eleanor Grey** — James's fiancee, a botanist's daughter. Quietly fierce, scientifically curious. She understands the garden's language better than anyone.
- **General Charles Ashford** — James's father, a retired general. Cold, pragmatic. He commissioned the walled garden as a wedding gift but sealed it after the war.
- **Margaret Holt** — The estate's elderly housekeeper, who remembers fragments of the old story.
- **Tom Wainwright** — A local historian and amateur archaeologist who helps Alice decipher the garden's secrets.

## Chapter Plan

### Part I: The Ruin (Chapters 1-12)

1. Alice arrives at Wynchwood Estate, Suffolk. The walled garden is a mess of brambles and collapsed stone. She nearly leaves.
2. The General's great-nephew, now the heir, explains the garden was sealed after the Great War. No one has touched it since 1919.
3. Alice begins clearing the south wall. She finds a fragment of china — a teacup painted with a forget-me-not motif.
4. In the potting shed, she discovers a rusted trowel and a notebook with botanical sketches signed "J.A. — July 1916."
5. Margaret Holt tells Alice about "the captain's garden" — a story the family never speaks of.
6. Alice finds a glass jar buried near the central fountain. Inside are letters, water-damaged but legible.
7. The first letter: James to Eleanor, March 1916. He describes the garden he plans to build for her. A winter garden — plants that bloom in the cold.
8. Alice reads the second letter. James writes from the front, April 1916. The trenches are quiet. He thinks of hellebores.
9. Alice photographs the letters and sends them to Tom Wainwright, the local historian. He is intrigued.
10. Tom visits the estate. He knows the Ashford family history but has never heard about the garden designer.
11. Alice and Tom trace the original planting plan from the notebook. The garden was laid out as a clock — each bed corresponds to a month.
12. Alice realizes the garden is not just decorative. It is a coded message, a calendar set to a specific date.

### Part II: The Design (Chapters 13-24)

13. Timeline shifts to April 1916. James is home on medical leave. He walks the empty walled garden with Eleanor.
14. James explains his vision: a garden that defies winter. Snowdrops, winter jasmine, hellebores, witch hazel. Eleanor laughs at his obsession.
15. James's father, the General, disapproves. A garden is a frivolity in wartime. Eleanor defends it.
16. James and Eleanor choose the first trees: a silver birch at the north gate, a yew at the south.
17. James returns to France. Eleanor takes over the planting. She writes to him every week.
18. In the trenches, James sketches new beds. He hides the sketches in his tunic.
19. Eleanor receives a letter with pressed snowdrops. James says they remind him of her gloves.
20. Summer 1916. The garden grows. Eleanor works in it every morning before the heat.
21. James is wounded at the Somme. He is evacuated to a field hospital. He writes about the garden to stay sane.
22. Eleanor receives word James is alive. She plants a row of lavender — his favorite — along the west wall.
23. The General writes to James, telling him the garden is a waste of the estate's resources. James does not reply.
24. Autumn 1916. James is invalided home. He sees the garden for the first time since spring. It is wild and beautiful.

### Part III: The Promise (Chapters 25-36)

25. James and Eleanor walk the garden together. He names each plant. She remembers the Latin.
26. They plan a wedding for spring 1917. The garden will be in bloom.
27. James's leave ends. He returns to France in November 1916. Eleanor gives him a pressed hellebore.
28. Alice, in the present, finds a wedding veil buried in a tin box under the yew tree. She cries.
29. Tom discovers Eleanor's diary in the county archives. He brings it to Alice.
30. The diary entries: Eleanor waiting. Letters that stop coming. The garden as her only comfort.
31. January 1917. Eleanor receives a letter from James's commanding officer. James is missing, presumed dead near Arras.
32. Eleanor does not tell the General. She tends the garden through the winter.
33. Alice traces Eleanor's story. She learns Eleanor never married. She lived on the estate until 1925, then vanished.
34. Tom finds Eleanor's death certificate. She died in a London hospital in 1927. Cause: pneumonia. She had been working as a nurse.
35. Alice and Tom discover the garden's secret: the planting calendar points to a specific date — February 14, 1917. The wedding date.
36. Alice realizes the garden was never finished. The center bed — the climactic heart of the design — was left unplanted.

### Part IV: The Reckoning (Chapters 37-50)

37. Alice decides to complete the garden. She researches what James intended for the center bed.
38. In James's notebook, she finds a pressed seed pod. Labelled "Winter Aconite — for the center."
39. Alice sources winter aconite bulbs. She plants them in the bare earth.
40. The General's great-nephew is skeptical but allows it. The estate needs the garden restored for a heritage grant.
41. February arrives. The aconites bloom — a carpet of yellow gold against the frost.
42. Alice holds a small ceremony. She invites Tom, Margaret, the heir. She reads one of James's letters aloud.
43. In the final letter (dated January 1917, never sent), James writes: "If I do not return, the garden will wait for you. It is my only proof that I lived."
44. Alice finds a memorial plaque in the estate chapel. James Ashford, 1891-1917. No mention of Eleanor.
45. Alice commissions a second plaque. Eleanor Grey, 1893-1927. She who tended the winter garden.
46. The garden opens to the public. Visitors walk the clock beds. The aconites are in full bloom.
47. Alice reflects on her own healing. The garden has saved her, too.
48. She receives a letter from a woman who saw the garden's story online. She is Eleanor's great-niece.
49. The great-niece visits. She brings Eleanor's photograph. Alice sees the garden through her eyes.
50. Alice plants one more tree — a crab apple, for hope. Winter ends. The first green shoots appear.
