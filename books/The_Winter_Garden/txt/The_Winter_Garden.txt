Chapter 1: The Ruin

The satnav gave up three miles back. Alice pulled the hire car onto the grass verge and killed the engine. Suffolk stretched around her, flat and grey under a low January sky.

She had expected a gate. A sign, at least. Instead there was a gap in the hedge and a track of mud and flint. She walked it with her bag over one shoulder, her boots sinking.

The estate appeared slowly. First the trees — a line of ancient oaks. Then the house, red brick and sagging, its windows boarded. And beyond it, the wall.

Twenty feet high, built of the same brick, stretching a hundred yards in each direction. Ivy had claimed it. Birds nested in the cracks.

Alice found the iron gate. It was locked. The key was in the letter she had been sent: a heavy Victorian thing, rusted but usable. She worked it into the lock and pushed.

The gate swung open on screaming hinges.

She stepped inside and stopped breathing.

The garden was a wreck. Briars stood six feet high. The paths were gone under nettles and sow thistle. A fountain lay on its side, a stone woman drowned in moss. The glass of a cold frame had shattered, and saplings grew through the frame.

A blackbird flew from a bush. It perched on the wall and watched her.

Alice stood in the centre and turned slowly. The walls enclosed everything. No house visible. No sky except the strip above. It was like standing in a sunken ship, looking up at a distant surface.

She counted the beds. Twelve. Arranged in a circle around the empty centre. Someone had planned this carefully. Someone had built a dream here.

She should turn around. Drive back to London. Call the solicitor and say no.

Instead she picked up a fallen brick and set it back on the wall.

Chapter 2: The Heir

Nigel Ashford met her in the kitchen of the main house. He was sixty, soft-handed, dressed in a cashmere jumper that had seen better days. He poured tea from a silver pot and did not apologise for the dust.

"The garden has been locked since 1919," he said. "My great-uncle's orders."

Alice stirred her tea. "Why?"

"My grandfather inherited the estate after the war. He was a practical man. The garden served no purpose."

"Who designed it?"

Nigel hesitated. "I was told it was built for a wedding that never happened."

"A wedding?"

"My great-uncle James was engaged. He died in France. The girl—" He waved a hand. "She left. The family doesn't talk about it."

Alice looked at the wall calendar in the kitchen. It was still on December of last year. "You want me to restore it?"

"I want a report. Costings. Feasibility. The estate needs the heritage grant. If the garden is viable — "

"The garden is a ruin."

"Yes." Nigel did not blink. "That's why I called you."

She walked back to the walled garden after dark. The moon was thin. She stood at the gate and listened to the wind move through the brambles. A bird called once and fell silent.

She thought about the wedding that never happened. The girl who left.

She took out her notebook and began to sketch.

Chapter 3: The China

Alice started at the south wall. The brickwork was soft, crumbling. She cleared ivy with her bare hands, pulling it down in great dusty curtains.

She worked for three hours. Her shoulders ached. Her fingernails were black.

She found the teacup at the base of the wall, buried under a drift of leaf mould. It was bone china, hand-painted, cracked but whole. A spray of forget-me-nots decorated the rim. The inside was stained dark amber — tea, left to dry a century ago.

She turned it over. Nothing on the base. No maker's mark.

She set it on the wall and kept digging.

By midday she had uncovered a section of paving. York stone, laid in a herringbone pattern. The original path. She swept it clean and saw the lines of the beds beyond.

The garden had been formal once. Symmetrical. Carefully plotted.

She picked up the teacup and carried it to the house. Margaret, the housekeeper, was in the larder.

"Does this mean anything to you?"

Margaret took the cup. Her hands were old and steady. She turned it in the light.

"The captain gave a set of these to his young lady," she said. "Before he went away."

"Captain?"

"James Ashford. He was the general's son. He died in the war."

"And the young lady?"

"Eleanor." Margaret set the cup down. "She stayed on after. Worked in the garden every day. Then one morning she was gone."

Chapter 4: The Notebook

The potting shed leaned against the north wall. Its roof had collapsed inward and the door hung from one hinge. Alice pushed through brambles to reach it.

Inside was dark and damp. A workbench rotted into the earth. Flowerpots stacked in pyramids, most broken. A rusted watering can. She lifted it and a spider ran across her hand.

The notebook was wedged behind a loose board. Leather-bound, swollen with moisture. She opened it carefully.

The pages were covered in ink drawings. Botanical sketches — hellebores, snowdrops, winter jasmine. Each one labelled in a precise hand. The Latin names, the planting depth, the preferred aspect.

On the final page, a plan. The walled garden drawn to scale. Each bed numbered. Arrows indicating sight lines. At the bottom, in pencil:

"For E. If I cannot give you spring, I will give you winter."

Alice sat on the shed floor and read the notebook twice. The handwriting was elegant, patient. A man who took his time.

She turned to the front cover. Inside, in faded ink: "J.A. — July 1916."

Captain James Ashford.

She thought about him sitting here, sketching, planning. The war still raging. The girl he was building for.

She closed the notebook and held it against her chest.

Chapter 5: The Housekeeper

Margaret Holt had worked at Wynchwood for fifty-two years. She remembered the General — the old General, James's father — a tall man with a limp and no warmth in his voice.

"He never spoke of the garden," Margaret said. "Not once. We were told to keep the gate locked and the key in his desk."

They sat in the kitchen. Margaret had made soup. Alice ate without tasting it.

"But you know the story," Alice said.

"I know what the village remembers. The captain was a gentle boy. He loved plants more than people, they said. But he loved Eleanor."

"What happened to her?"

Margaret shrugged. "She was a shadow after he died. Walked the garden. Talked to the flowers. People said she lost her mind."

"Do you believe that?"

"I believe she lost her heart." Margaret picked up the teacup. "This belonged to her. She had a set. She used to take tea in the garden even in winter."

"Even in winter," Alice repeated.

"The captain designed it as a winter garden. Things that bloom in the cold. She said it was the only place she felt close to him."

Alice looked out the window. The walled garden was invisible in the dark. But she could feel it there, waiting.

Chapter 6: The Letters

Alice dug near the fountain. It was a guess — the plan showed a circular bed around a central feature. She took a trowel and worked slowly, scraping soil back in thin layers.

The jar was eight inches down. Glass, stoppered with cork and sealed with wax. She lifted it out and held it to the light.

Inside were letters. Dozens of them.

She carried the jar to the potting shed, now braced with a beam she had wedged against the wall. She worked the cork loose with a knife.

The paper was fragile. Water had crept in at the edges. But the ink was still legible.

She unfolded the first letter.

"My dearest Eleanor," it began. "I have been thinking about the garden. The east bed will be snowdrops — Galanthus nivalis. They flower in January, when everything else is dead. I want you to see them on the morning of our wedding."

Alice sat down. Her hands were shaking.

She read the letter through. Then she read it again.

James had written it in March 1916. He was on leave. He described every bed, every plant, every intention. The garden was not a gift. It was a promise.

She unfolded the second letter. April 1916. From the front.

"The trenches are quiet tonight. I have drawn a plan for the fern bed. Tell me if you like it."

Chapter 7: The First Letter

March 1916.

My dearest Eleanor,

I have been given leave. Three weeks. I arrive Thursday.

I have been thinking about the garden. Not the one my father wants — the one you and I spoke of. The winter garden. I have drawn plans. I have calculated the light.

The east bed will be snowdrops. Galanthus nivalis. They flower in January, when everything else is dead. I want you to see them on the morning of our wedding.

The west bed will be hellebores. Helleborus niger. The Christmas rose. They bloom through frost. I know you prefer them to roses anyway.

The centre will be winter aconite. Eranthis hyemalis. Yellow like the sun you say is always hiding in Suffolk.

I have spoken to a nursery in Colchester. They can supply the bulbs.

I know my father thinks this is foolish. A garden in wartime. But I need something to build. Something that will last.

When I am in France, I will think of snowdrops.

Yours always,
James

Alice folded the letter carefully. She pictured a young man in uniform, writing at a desk, looking out at a muddy garden.

She thought about her own life. The marriage that failed. The career that stalled. The years she had spent building nothing she could point to.

She put the letter back in the jar and took out the next one.

Chapter 8: The Second Letter

April 1916.

Dearest Eleanor,

I am writing this by candlelight. The dugout is damp. The men are asleep.

We have been in the line for six days. It is quiet, which is its own kind of terror. I prefer noise. Noise means you are still alive.

I think about the garden constantly. I have drawn a plan for the fern bed. Tell me if you like it. I thought Dryopteris for the shady corner, Polypodium where the wall protects them.

I have taught myself to identify plants by their Latin names. It passes the time. I repeat them like prayers. Galanthus. Helleborus. Eranthis.

One of the men asked me what I was writing. I said a letter. He said he writes to his wife about the harvest. Potatoes and barley. He said it keeps him sane.

I understand.

When I close my eyes I see the walled garden. I see you walking the paths. Your blue dress. Your hair pinned up.

I have not told you this, but I was afraid before the first engagement. I am still afraid. But I carry your pressed hellebore in my breast pocket. It is flat now and the colour is gone. But it is there.

I will be home soon. Wait for me.

James

Alice read the letter twice. Then she put it with the others and sat in the silence of the shed.

She had never received letters like that. She wondered what it would be like to be loved that completely.

Chapter 9: The Historian

Tom Wainwright arrived on Saturday morning. He was in his fifties, bearded, wearing a wax jacket that smelled of woodsmoke. He drove a Land Rover full of archive boxes.

Alice showed him the letters.

He read the first one in silence. Then the second. When he looked up, his eyes were bright.

"This is extraordinary," he said. "James Ashford. I know the name."

"You do?"

"He's mentioned in the county history. The general's son. Killed at Arras, 1917. But there's nothing about the garden. Nothing about Eleanor."

"I found her teacup."

"You found her what?"

Alice showed him the china. Tom turned it over, examined the forget-me-nots.

"This is hand-painted. Commissioned, I'd say. Probably a wedding gift."

"They never married."

"No." Tom looked at the letters. "But he built her a garden. In a war. That's the kind of thing a historian dreams about."

They walked to the walled garden. Tom stood in the centre and turned slowly, the way Alice had done on her first day.

"It's laid out as a clock," he said.

"What?"

"The beds. Twelve of them. Arranged in a circle. Each one corresponds to a month."

Alice looked at the plan in James's notebook. Twelve beds. She had counted them but not understood.

"He planted time," she said.

"He planted a calendar." Tom pointed to the centre. "And that empty space — that's the target. The date everything points to."

Chapter 10: The Calendar

Alice spread James's plan across the workbench. Tom had brought tracing paper and pencils.

They mapped the beds. Twelve segments, each one a month. January: snowdrops. February: winter aconite. March: crocus. April: hellebore. May: bluebells. June: roses. July: lavender. August: hydrangea. September: sedum. October: aster. November: winter jasmine. December: witch hazel.

"The planting order is deliberate," Tom said. "He chose plants that bloom in sequence. A year-round garden."

Alice traced the lines. "But the plants don't match the months."

"What do you mean?"

"Snowdrops in January — that's correct. But he planted the east bed with snowdrops, and the east bed is the January position. He thought in terms of the compass."

"And the centre?"

Alice looked at the empty circle. "Winter aconite. Eranthis hyemalis. It was supposed to go there."

"When does it bloom?"

"February. Mid-February."

Tom sat back. "Let me check something."

He pulled out his phone and searched. "Eleanor Grey's birth date was February 14. Valentine's Day."

Alice felt a chill. "He was building her a Valentine's Day garden."

"The centre bed was to be planted with winter aconite. It would bloom every February. For the rest of her life."

Alice looked at the empty circle. Bare earth. Weeds.

"He never finished it," she said.

"Or she never let anyone finish it."

Chapter 11: The Archive

Tom drove Alice to the county record office in Ipswich. It was a grey building with fluorescent lights and the smell of old paper.

He had requested the Ashford family files in advance. They were brought on a trolley: three boxes of deeds, correspondence, photographs.

Alice started with the photographs. Most were formal portraits. Stiff collars, unsmiling faces. A family that did not show emotion.

She found James in the third box. A cabinet card, mounted on board. He was in uniform, a lieutenant's star on his collar. His face was young, open. He was almost smiling.

She set it aside and kept searching.

There were no photographs of Eleanor.

"There's nothing," she said. "No mention of her in any official record."

Tom was reading a ledger. "That's not unusual. Women left little paper trail."

"But she lived here. She tended the garden for years."

"Unmarried. No children. No property." Tom closed the ledger. "She was invisible to history."

Alice looked at James's photograph. He was looking past the camera, into some middle distance. She wondered if he was thinking of her.

"There must be something," she said.

"There is." Tom pulled a folder from the bottom of the box. "I saved the best for last."

He opened it. Inside was a diary. Leather-bound, small enough to fit in a pocket. The cover was stamped with a gold E.

Eleanor's diary.

Chapter 12: The Date

Alice opened Eleanor's diary on the first page.

January 1, 1917. Eleanor had written three words: "The garden waits."

She turned the pages. Brief entries. Weather. Plants. Letters received. Letters sent.

February 14. Her birthday. "James sent snowdrops last year. This year, nothing."

March. "I walked the garden today. The hellebores are blooming. He would want me to see them."

April. "Mrs. Bell says I must not grieve forever. But how long is forever?"

Alice stopped. She was reading a woman's grief, recorded in small, precise handwriting. No self-pity. Just observation. The way a botanist records a dying plant.

"The planting calendar," she said. "James designed the garden to bloom in sequence. Month by month. Building to February."

"The centre bed," Tom said.

"Winter aconite. He never planted it. But he wanted it to bloom on her birthday."

Tom looked at the diary. "She kept coming back. Every year. She watched the garden cycle through its seasons."

"Waiting for a bloom that never came."

Alice closed the diary. She looked at James's photograph again.

"He didn't just design a garden," she said. "He wrote a love letter. And she spent eight years trying to read it."

Chapter 13: The Leave

April 1916.

James arrived at Ipswich station at dusk. He had not told his father he was coming. He wanted one night of freedom before the General's disapproval.

Eleanor met him on the platform. She wore a blue coat and a hat that did not quite fit. She looked thinner than he remembered.

"You look like a ghost," she said.

"I feel like one."

They walked through the town. The streets were quiet. The war had taken the young men and left the old.

"The garden is empty," Eleanor said. "Just walls and weeds."

"I know. I've been drawing plans."

"In the trenches?"

"Where else?"

They reached the edge of the estate. The walled garden was dark behind its high brick. James found the key in his pocket.

"Shall we?"

He unlocked the gate. They stepped inside.

The moon was half full. It lit the space enough to see. Eleanor gasped.

James had arranged for the first planting before his leave. Volunteers from the village. They had cleared the weeds and turned the soil. The beds were bare, but they were shaped. Ready.

"When did you—" she started.

"Write to me," he said. "Tell me what you want to grow."

Eleanor took his hand. "I want to grow old with you."

James said nothing. He held her hand in the dark and looked at his empty garden.

Chapter 14: The Vision

The next morning, James walked the walled garden with Eleanor. The sun was thin but present.

"I want it to bloom in winter," he said. "Snowdrops in January. Hellebores in February. Aconites in March."

"Why winter?"

"Because the rest of the world is dead. And this will be alive."

Eleanor laughed. "You always did things backwards."

"No. I do things deliberately."

They walked the perimeter. James pointed out where each bed would go. He had memorised the plan he drew in France.

"The north wall will be witch hazel. Hamamelis. It flowers in December. Yellow, like the sun we never see."

"The south wall?"

"Winter jasmine. It blooms from November to February. Reliable. Unkillable."

Eleanor stopped at the centre. "And here?"

James was quiet for a moment. "That's for my father."

"The General?"

"He wants something to remember me by. When I go back."

"You're not going back."

"I am. And I might not—" He stopped. "I might not come home."

Eleanor took his hand. "Then plant something that will outlive you."

James looked at the bare earth. A circle, twelve feet across.

"I'll plant aconites," he said. "Eranthis hyemalis. Golden. They'll bloom every February."

"For me?"

"For you."

Chapter 15: The General

General Charles Ashford was a tall man with a grey moustache and a voice that had never been questioned. He found James in the walled garden that afternoon.

"Your mother wants to see you," he said. "She has been crying for three days."

"I'll see her at dinner."

The General looked at the turned earth. "What is this?"

"A garden."

"I can see that. But why now? There is a war."

James wiped his hands on his trousers. "That's exactly why now."

The General's jaw tightened. "You have a duty."

"I know my duty, Father."

"And a fiancee. Who you have not formally introduced."

"Eleanor is not a formal person."

The General stepped closer. "You will bring her to dinner. Tonight. We will discuss the wedding."

"Father—"

"This is not a request."

The General turned and walked away. James watched him go. His hands were shaking.

Eleanor came out of the potting shed. "I heard."

"Everyone heard. That's how he operates."

"I'm not afraid of him."

James looked at her. "I know. That's why I love you."

They stood together in the empty garden. The afternoon was cold. The sky was white.

"Let's plant something," Eleanor said. "Before I lose my nerve."

"What?"

"Anything. The first thing. Let's make it real."

James handed her a trowel.

Chapter 16: The Birch

Eleanor chose the silver birch for the north gate. James had described it in his letters — a tree that would catch the light even in winter.

They dug the hole together. The soil was heavy clay. It took an hour.

"Are you sure about this?" James asked. "It's a tree. We have to live with it."

"We have to live with each other. The tree is easier."

James laughed. It was the first time he had laughed in months.

They planted the birch and watered it. Eleanor packed the earth around the roots with her bare hands.

"There," she said. "It's started."

"It's one tree."

"It's the first."

James looked at the sapling. Thin. Bare. Vulnerable.

"By the time it's tall," he said, "the war will be over."

"And we'll be married."

"And we'll sit under it."

Eleanor smiled. "You'll read botanical journals. I'll complain about the village gossip. We'll be boring and old."

"Perfect."

He put his arm around her. The birch leaned slightly in the wind.

It was the first thing they built together. It would outlast them both.

Chapter 17: The Letters Cross

June 1916.

Eleanor wrote to James every week. She told him about the garden. The birch had taken root. The snowdrops were dormant. She had planted the hellebores herself.

James wrote back. His letters were shorter now. He was tired. He did not say it, but Eleanor could feel it between the words.

"I planted the lavender today," she wrote. "Along the west wall. The whole row. You said it was your favourite. I hope you can smell it from France."

James replied: "The lavender reaches me. Some days it is all that keeps me standing."

July. The roses bloomed. Eleanor sent James a pressed petal.

August. The hydrangeas turned blue. Eleanor sat in the garden and read his letters again. She had memorised them all.

September. James wrote: "I have been promoted. More men to lead. More lives to answer for. I dream of the garden every night."

October. Eleanor's mother fell ill. Eleanor divided her time between the sickroom and the walled garden.

November. James wrote: "I have your hellebore. It is flat and faded. It is the most precious thing I own."

December. Eleanor sat in the winter garden alone. The witch hazel was blooming, bare branches covered in yellow stars. She picked one and pressed it.

She did not know if she would have someone to send it to.

Chapter 18: The Sketches

The trenches were quiet. James sat with his back against the mud wall and drew.

He drew the garden from memory. Each bed, each plant, each stone. He filled page after page in his notebook. The men thought he was writing letters.

"Drawing again, Captain?"

Private Shaw crouched beside him. A young man from Norfolk. He had been a gardener before the war.

"Just keeping my mind occupied."

Shaw looked at the sketch. "That's a proper garden, that is. My da had a garden. Grew vegetables."

"Vegetables are useful."

"Flowers are better." Shaw pointed at a hellebore. "What's that one?"

"Christmas rose. Blooms in winter."

"Never seen it."

"You will. After the war. I'll show you."

Shaw smiled. He had not smiled in weeks.

James tore out a page and gave it to him. "That's a plan for a cottage garden. Give it to your father."

Shaw took it like a holy relic. "Thank you, Captain."

"Thank me after the war."

That night, James wrote to Eleanor. "I have taught myself to draw from memory. I can see the garden perfectly. Every leaf. Every petal. It is the only world I want to live in."

He sealed the letter and tucked it into his tunic.

The next day, they went over the top.

Chapter 19: The Snowdrops

Eleanor received the pressed snowdrops in a letter dated July 1916. James had picked them from a French field. He wrote: "They remind me of your gloves."

She held them in her palm. They were brown and fragile. She did not throw them away.

She pressed them in her Bible, between the pages of Ruth. Where you go, I will go.

The snowdrops in the garden bloomed in January. She had planted them in a drift under the birch. They were the first thing she saw every morning.

Margaret had started bringing her tea in the garden. Even when it rained. Even when the frost lay thick on the paths.

"You'll catch your death," Margaret said.

"Then I'll die in the garden."

Margaret did not argue. She understood.

Eleanor drank her tea. The snowdrops nodded in the wind. She thought about James in France. She did not know if he was alive or dead. The letters came irregularly. Sometimes three in a week. Sometimes none for a month.

She talked to the snowdrops. "Bloom," she told them. "Bloom for him."

They did. Every year.

Chapter 20: The Summer

Summer 1916. The garden flourished.

Eleanor worked in it every morning, before the heat became unbearable. She weeded. She watered. She talked to the plants.

The lavender row had taken. It was waist-high and covered in bees. The roses — a variety James had chosen called Winchester Cathedral — were creamy white and heavy-headed.

She picked a bunch and took them to the house. The General was reading the newspaper in the drawing room.

"From the garden," she said.

He looked at the roses. "They are weeds."

"They are flowers."

"Flowers do not win wars."

Eleanor set the roses on the table. "No. But they remind us what we are fighting for."

The General looked at her. He did not speak. But he did not throw the roses away.

Eleanor went back to the garden. She knelt in the lavender and closed her eyes. The smell was overwhelming.

She pretended James was beside her.

"Look," she whispered. "Look what we made."

There was no answer. Only the bees.

She opened her eyes and went back to weeding.

Chapter 21: The Wound

August 1916. The Somme.

James was leading his men across no man's land when the shell landed. He did not hear it. He only felt the ground rise and fall, and then he was on his back, staring at a sky full of smoke.

His leg was wet. He looked down. Blood soaked through his trousers.

Private Shaw appeared. "Captain! Captain, you're hit."

"Keep moving."

"I'm not leaving you."

"That's an order."

Shaw ignored him. He dragged James into a shell hole and tore open his field dressing.

The pain arrived. James screamed.

He was carried to the aid post. Then to the field hospital. He drifted in and out. He dreamed of the garden. The birch. The lavender. Eleanor's hands.

He woke in a tent. A nurse was changing his bandages.

"You're lucky," she said. "The bullet missed the artery."

"My leg—"

"Will heal. You'll walk with a cane."

James closed his eyes. "Write to Eleanor Grey. 12 Church Lane, Wynchwood. Tell her I'm alive."

The nurse nodded.

James slept. He dreamed of snowdrops.

Chapter 22: The News

Eleanor was in the garden when the telegram arrived. Margaret brought it to her.

She opened it with shaking hands.

"Regret to inform you that Captain James Ashford has been wounded in action. He is in hospital in Rouen. Condition satisfactory."

She read it three times. Then she sat down in the lavender.

"He's alive," she said.

Margaret sat beside her. "Alive."

Eleanor started to cry. She had not realised she had been holding her breath for weeks.

"I knew it," she said. "I knew he would come back."

"Not yet. He's still in France."

"But he's alive. That's enough."

She went back to the garden that evening. The light was golden. The roses were in full bloom.

She picked one and pressed it. She would send it to him. A piece of the garden, crossing the channel.

She planted a row of lavender along the west wall. His favourite. When he came home, he would see it first.

She worked until the light failed. Her hands were sore. Her heart was full.

Chapter 23: The General's Letter

General Ashford wrote to James in the hospital.

"Your mother is distressed. The garden is consuming the estate's resources. I have told the girl she must limit her planting.

You will recover. You will return to duty. You will stop this foolishness.

I have arranged for you to be transferred to a convalescent home in Kent. When you are fit, you will report back to the regiment."

James read the letter twice. Then he folded it and put it under his pillow.

He did not reply.

Instead, he wrote to Eleanor.

"The General is cross with us. I find this encouraging. It means the garden is working."

Eleanor wrote back: "I have planted the lavender. It is thriving. The General can do nothing about it."

James laughed when he read it. The nurse asked what was funny.

"Nothing," he said. "Everything."

He looked at his leg. The wound was healing. He would walk again. He would go back to France.

But first — he would see the garden.

Chapter 24: The Return

November 1916.

James came home on a cold grey morning. He walked with a cane. His leg ached in the damp.

Eleanor met him at the gate. She wore the blue coat. She did not run.

He limped towards her. She walked to meet him. They stopped a foot apart.

"You're thin," she said.

"You're beautiful."

She laughed. It was a wet sound. "You're a terrible liar."

"I'm a terrible everything. But I'm home."

She took his arm. They walked to the walled garden.

The gate was unlocked. James pushed it open.

The garden was wild. The lavender had spread. The roses were bare. The birch was taller. The paths were covered in fallen leaves.

"It's a mess," Eleanor said.

"It's perfect."

James stepped inside. He touched the birch trunk. He knelt and put his hand on the soil.

"I dreamed of this," he said. "Every night."

Eleanor knelt beside him. "We have the whole winter."

"Then let's plant it."

They spent the afternoon in the garden. James showed her the changes he wanted. She showed him what she had done.

They did not talk about the war. They did not talk about February, when he would have to leave again.

They talked about witch hazel and hellebores.

Chapter 25: The Names

November became December. The days shortened. The frost hardened.

James and Eleanor walked the garden every afternoon. He named each plant for her. She remembered the Latin.

"Tell me again," she said.

"Helleborus niger. Christmas rose. Blooms in the snow."

"Which bed?"

"West. You planted them yourself."

"Did I do it right?"

James smiled. "They're thriving. You have a better touch than I do."

They reached the centre bed. Still empty. Bare soil.

"I saved this for you," Eleanor said.

"I know."

"Are you going to tell me what goes there?"

"Not yet."

"Why not?"

James looked at her. "Because I want you to have something to look forward to."

The winter jasmine had started blooming along the south wall. Small yellow flowers, brave against the frost.

Eleanor picked one and tucked it into his buttonhole.

"There," she said. "Now you're wearing the garden."

"I always am."

James looked at the empty centre bed. He knew what he would plant. He just needed to survive long enough to do it.

Chapter 26: The Wedding

They planned a wedding for spring 1917. April. The garden would be waking up.

Eleanor wrote to her cousin in London about a dress. James wrote to the nursery about the aconite bulbs.

The General did not approve. He did not forbid it.

"You will do what you want," he told James. "You always have."

"I learned from you."

The General said nothing.

They walked the garden in the evening, James and Eleanor. The witch hazel was blooming. The whole garden smelled sweet.

"In April," Eleanor said. "We'll be married in the centre bed."

"There's nothing there yet."

"Then we'll be married on bare earth. It's appropriate."

James took her hand. "I'm sorry it's not finished."

"It will never be finished. Gardens never are."

"That's true."

They stood in the centre of the garden. The winter was all around them. But they were warm.

James kissed her forehead.

"April," he said.

"April."

Chapter 27: The Last Leave

January 1917.

James's leave ended. He packed his kit in silence. Eleanor watched from the doorway.

"I'll write every day," he said.

"You say that every time."

"This time I mean it."

"You always mean it."

He crossed the room and took her hands. "I'll be back. The garden will be finished."

"Forget the garden."

"I don't want to forget the garden. It's the best thing I've done."

Eleanor pressed something into his palm. A pressed hellebore.

"Take it," she said. "For luck."

James put it in his breast pocket. Over his heart.

They walked to the station. The train was late. They stood on the platform in the cold.

"When I come back—" James started.

"Don't. Don't say it."

"When I come back, we finish the centre bed. Together."

Eleanor nodded. She could not speak.

The train arrived. James kissed her hand. Then he boarded.

Eleanor stood on the platform until the train was gone. Then she walked home, alone.

She went to the walled garden. She stood in the centre bed.

"Come back," she whispered.

The garden did not answer.

Chapter 28: The Veil

Alice found the tin box under the yew tree.

She had been clearing the north-east corner. The yew was overgrown, its branches trailing the ground. She cut them back and found the box half-buried in the roots.

She lifted it out. The tin was rusted but sealed.

She carried it to the potting shed and pried it open.

Inside was a wedding veil. Yellowed, fragile, folded with care. A small card lay on top.

"For E," it said. "For April."

Alice lifted the veil. It was delicate — lace and silk, hand-stitched. Someone had spent months on it.

She held it up. The light came through the holes in the lace.

Eleanor's wedding veil. Never worn.

Alice sat down. The tears came before she expected them. She cried for Eleanor. For James. For the wedding that never happened.

She folded the veil carefully and put it back in the box.

She would finish the garden. She would plant the centre bed.

She owed it to them.

Chapter 29: The Diary

Tom brought Eleanor's diary to the estate. They sat in the kitchen. Margaret made tea.

"There's more than I thought," Tom said. "The archive had two volumes."

Alice opened the first volume. It covered 1916 to 1918.

She read quickly. Eleanor's handwriting was small and neat. She recorded the weather, the plants, the letters she sent and received.

January 1917: "James has gone back. The house is empty. The garden is my only comfort."

March 1917: "No letters in three weeks. I go to the garden every day."

April 1917: "Mrs. Bell says I must not wait forever. But I have already waited this long."

May 1917: "I have heard nothing. I write to him anyway."

Alice turned the pages. The entries grew shorter. The handwriting looser.

September 1918: "The garden is all I have."

November 1918: "The war is over. James is not coming home."

Alice closed the diary. She looked at Tom.

"She stayed," Alice said. "She stayed in the garden."

"Until 1925. Then she went to London."

"She was a nurse."

"She died of pneumonia. Working in a charity hospital."

Alice opened the second volume. It began in 1926. Eleanor was in London. She wrote about patients, about the weather, about missing the garden.

One entry caught Alice's eye.

"Today a patient asked if I had been happy. I said yes. I did not tell her that my happiness is buried in a walled garden in Suffolk. She would not understand."

Alice closed the diary.

"Let's go outside," she said.

Chapter 30: The Vigil

1917.

Eleanor waited.

The letters stopped coming in February. She wrote to the War Office. They replied that Captain Ashford was missing, presumed dead.

She did not believe them.

She went to the garden every day. She knelt in the dirt and dug with her hands. She pulled weeds. She watered the hellebores.

The General found her there one evening. He stood at the gate and watched.

"Eleanor."

She did not look up.

"You must accept it."

"Accept what?"

"That he is gone."

Eleanor stood. Her knees were stained. Her hands were black.

"He is not gone. He is here."

She gestured at the garden. The birch. The lavender. The roses.

"This is him. Every leaf. Every petal."

The General said nothing. He turned and walked away.

Eleanor knelt again. She kept digging.

The garden was all she had. It was enough. It had to be.

She worked until the light failed. Then she walked home alone.

The next morning, she went back.

Chapter 31: The Letter

January 1917.

James wrote his last letter in a dugout near Arras. He knew the offensive was coming. He knew he might not survive it.

He wrote to Eleanor.

"My darling,

I am writing this in case I do not come home. Do not grieve too long. You are young. You will love again.

But first — promise me you will tend the garden. The aconites are ordered. The nursery will deliver them in February. Plant them in the centre bed. They will bloom every year on your birthday.

I have designed the garden as a calendar. Each bed corresponds to a month. The centre is February. Your month. Our month.

If I do not return, the garden will wait for you. It is my only proof that I lived.

Tell the General I was not afraid. Tell my mother I loved her.

And tell yourself — tell yourself every day — that you were the best thing that ever happened to me.

Yours always,
James"

He sealed the letter and gave it to Private Shaw.

"If I don't make it," he said. "See that she gets this."

Shaw took the letter. "You'll make it, Captain."

James looked at the dugout ceiling. "Maybe."

He tucked a pressed hellebore into the envelope. Flat. Faded. Still there.

Chapter 32: The Silence

February 1917.

Eleanor received no letters. She wrote every week. She received nothing.

She went to the garden. The snowdrops were blooming. She had planted them herself.

She knelt and touched them.

"Where are you?" she whispered.

The wind moved through the birch. The snowdrops nodded.

She stayed in the garden all morning. The sun was weak. The frost did not lift.

At noon, Margaret came with tea.

"Any news?"

"No."

Margaret set the tea down. "You must eat."

"I'm not hungry."

"You must eat anyway."

Eleanor drank the tea. She looked at the centre bed. Bare earth. Empty.

She had ordered the aconite bulbs. They had arrived. She kept them in the potting shed.

She could not plant them. That was James's job.

She would wait.

She waited all winter. She waited through spring.

The aconites stayed in their sack in the shed.

Chapter 33: The Vanishing

Alice and Tom traced Eleanor's life after the war.

She left Wynchwood in 1925. She sold nothing. She packed a single bag and took the train to London.

Margaret remembered it. "She said the garden was finished. She said she could not watch it anymore."

"Finished?"

"She had kept it for eight years. Eight winters. She planted the aconites eventually. They bloomed every February. But he never saw them."

Alice found Eleanor's death certificate in the London records. She died in St. Thomas's Hospital, November 1927. Cause of death: pneumonia.

Her occupation was listed as "nurse."

Her marital status: "single."

"Single," Alice said. "She never married."

"She didn't want to," Tom said. "She was already married to the garden."

Alice looked at the certificate. Eleanor Grey. Age 34.

"She was younger than me," Alice said.

Tom said nothing.

Alice folded the certificate and put it in her bag.

"I'm going to plant the centre bed," she said.

Tom smiled. "It's February."

"I know."

Chapter 34: The Discovery

Tom found one more document in the county archive. A letter from Eleanor to the War Office, dated 1919.

"Dear Sirs,

I write to request information regarding the final letter of Captain James Ashford, 7th Battalion, Suffolk Regiment. I have been told by Private Thomas Shaw, who served with the Captain, that a letter was entrusted to him on the night of 30 January 1917. I have never received it.

Please confirm whether this letter was ever dispatched.

Yours faithfully,
Eleanor Grey"

Alice read it. "She never got the last letter."

"Shaw kept it."

"What?"

Tom pulled out another document. "Private Shaw was killed at Arras, February 1, 1917. He never got to send the letter."

Alice sat back. "So the last letter was lost."

"For a hundred years."

"Until we found it."

Tom nodded. "In the jar. Under the fountain."

Alice thought about it. James wrote the letter. Gave it to Shaw. Shaw died. The letter stayed in his pocket. Someone — Eleanor, maybe — found it and buried it in the jar.

Or maybe Shaw had already hidden it. Maybe he knew.

"It doesn't matter how it got there," Alice said. "It matters that it survived."

"The garden survived."

"The garden survived."

Chapter 35: The Secret

Alice and Tom stood in the centre of the walled garden. The afternoon was cold. The sky was white.

"The planting calendar," Tom said. "Let me work through it again."

He laid out James's plan. Twelve beds. Twelve months.

"Each bed flowers in its corresponding month. January — snowdrops. February — aconites. March — crocus."

"And the centre?"

"Winter aconite. Eranthis hyemalis. The same as February."

Alice looked at the empty circle. "So the centre is February too?"

"No. The centre is February 14. Valentine's Day. Eleanor's birthday."

"How do you know?"

"The diary. She wrote about it. James told her the centre bed was for her birthday."

Alice knelt and touched the soil. "He designed a garden that would bloom year-round. Every month, something to see. But the climax — the centre — was February. Her birthday."

"Every February 14, the aconites would bloom. Gold petals against the frost."

"He wanted her to have flowers on her birthday. Forever."

"Even if he wasn't there."

Alice stood. "Then let's plant them. Finally."

She walked to the potting shed and found the sack of bulbs. They were still good. Dormant. Waiting.

"Now?" Tom said.

"Now."

Chapter 36: The Unfinished

Alice planted the aconite bulbs in the centre bed. She worked alone. Tom watched from the edge.

She dug each hole carefully. Measured the depth. Placed the bulb. Covered it. Pressed the soil firm.

She planted thirty-six bulbs. One for each month since James died.

When she finished, she sat in the centre of the bed and looked at the garden.

The walls. The birch. The lavender. The empty paths.

"He built this for her," she said. "Every stone. Every plant. And he never saw it finished."

Tom came and sat beside her.

"He saw the plan. He saw the beginning. That was enough."

"It wasn't enough. He wanted to be here."

"But she was here. For eight years. She kept it alive."

Alice looked at the bare earth. The bulbs were invisible. But they were there.

"She never got his last letter."

"No."

"She never knew he was thinking of her. Right at the end."

"She knew."

"How?"

Tom smiled. "Because she stayed. You don't tend a garden for eight years unless you know you're loved."

Alice said nothing. She stayed in the garden until the light failed.

Then she walked home, alone.

But she was not empty.

Chapter 37: The Decision

Alice decided to complete the garden.

She called Nigel Ashford and told him her plan. The heritage grant would pay for the restoration. She would oversee the planting. The garden would open to the public.

Nigel was quiet for a long time.

"My great-uncle would have wanted it," he said finally.

"I think so."

"The family has kept it locked for a hundred years. Maybe it's time to let it go."

Alice started work the next week. She hired a small team. They cleared the brambles. They repaired the walls. They restored the paths.

She followed James's plan exactly. Every bed, every plant. She found a nursery that could supply the varieties he had chosen.

She worked twelve hours a day. Her hands blistered. Her back ached.

She did not mind.

The garden was coming back to life. Slowly. Painfully. But surely.

She thought about James. About Eleanor. About the love they had built in this space.

She wanted to honour it.

She wanted to finish what they started.

Chapter 38: The Seed Pod

In the back of James's notebook, Alice found a pressed seed pod. It was labelled: "Eranthis hyemalis — for the centre."

She held it in her palm. A hundred years old. Still intact.

She had already planted the bulbs. But this — this was James's own hand. His own harvest.

She took the seed pod to the centre bed. She knelt and pressed it into the soil.

"There," she said. "Now you're here too."

She covered it and stood.

The garden was quiet. The wind moved through the birch.

Alice felt something shift in her chest. Something close to peace.

She had spent years running from her own life. Her failed marriage. Her stalled career. Her loneliness.

But here, in this garden, she had found something else. A purpose. A connection.

She was not Eleanor. She was not James.

But she was part of their story now.

And she would make sure it was told.

Chapter 39: The Bulbs

Alice sourced winter aconite bulbs from a specialist nursery in Norfolk. They arrived in a brown paper sack, labelled by hand.

She carried them to the walled garden. The centre bed was ready. Tilled. Weeded. Waiting.

She planted them one by one. Her fingers in the cold soil.

She thought about Eleanor. Did she plant the original bulbs? Or did she leave the sack in the shed, waiting for James to come home?

She would never know.

But she could finish the job.

She planted the last bulb and pressed the soil flat. Then she watered the bed from the restored fountain. The stone woman had been lifted upright. Water ran from her cupped hands again.

Alice sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the garden.

The snowdrops were blooming. The hellebores were budding. The birch was catching the low winter sun.

It was beautiful.

She took out her phone and photographed the centre bed. She sent the picture to Tom.

"It's done," she wrote.

His reply came a minute later: "No. It's beginning."

Chapter 40: The Doubt

Nigel Ashford visited the garden in late January. He walked the restored paths in silence.

"It's remarkable," he said.

"Thank you."

"The grant committee will be impressed."

Alice nodded. She had done what he asked. The heritage value was clear.

But she wanted more.

"I want to open it on February 14," she said.

Nigel raised an eyebrow. "Valentine's Day?"

"The centre bed will bloom in mid-February. That's the date the garden was designed for."

Nigel looked at the centre bed. Bare earth. No sign of the bulbs.

"How do you know they'll bloom in time?"

Alice smiled. "Because James designed it that way."

Nigel was quiet. Then he nodded.

"February 14 it is."

After he left, Alice stood in the centre of the garden. She was not sure the aconites would bloom. She was not sure of anything.

But she had done the work. She had planted the bulbs. She had restored the walls.

The rest was up to the garden.

She closed her eyes and listened to the wind.

Chapter 41: The Bloom

February 14.

Alice arrived at dawn. The ground was white with frost. Her breath clouded in the cold.

She walked to the centre bed.

The aconites had bloomed.

Hundreds of golden yellow flowers, each one no bigger than a coin, covered the circle. They glowed against the frost. They caught the first light.

Alice knelt. She touched a petal. It was cold and soft.

She stayed there for a long time.

Tom arrived at eight. He found her still kneeling.

"They bloomed," she said.

"They bloomed."

"James designed it. February 14. He knew they would bloom on her birthday."

Tom knelt beside her. "He planted aconites in his imagination. She planted them in the ground. And now here they are."

"One hundred years later."

"Love doesn't have an expiration date."

Alice laughed. It was a wet sound.

She stood and looked at the garden. The snowdrops were still blooming. The hellebores were opening. The witch hazel was covered in yellow stars.

But the aconites — the aconites were the heart.

The garden was complete.

Chapter 42: The Ceremony

They held a small ceremony at noon. Nigel Ashford, Margaret, Tom, a few villagers.

Alice read one of James's letters aloud.

"My dearest Eleanor. I have been thinking about the garden. The east bed will be snowdrops. They flower in January, when everything else is dead. I want you to see them on the morning of our wedding."

She stopped. Her voice broke.

She looked at the aconites. Golden. Brave.

"He never got to show her," she said. "But we can see them. And we can remember."

Margaret stepped forward. She held the teacup.

"This was hers," she said. "She drank from it every morning in the garden. Even in winter."

She set the cup on the wall.

"We drank from it every morning," Eleanor said."

Alice did not correct her. It felt true enough.

Tom opened a bottle of champagne. They toasted the garden. They toasted James and Eleanor.

The aconites nodded in the wind.

Alice looked at the birch. The lavender. The roses.

She thought about love. About loss. About the things we build that outlast us.

She thought about James, sitting in a trench, drawing flowers.

She thought about Eleanor, kneeling in the dirt, keeping the garden alive.

And she thought about herself. Alone, but not empty.

The garden had saved her.

She was ready to go home.

Chapter 43: The Last Letter

Alice gathered everyone under the birch. She took out the last letter — the one James wrote in January 1917, the one that was never sent.

"James wrote this on the night before Arras," she said. "He gave it to a private named Shaw. Shaw was killed the next day. The letter was lost. We found it in a jar under the fountain."

She unfolded it.

"If I do not return, the garden will wait for you. It is my only proof that I lived."

She stopped. The garden was silent.

"I think it's proof enough," she said.

She folded the letter and tucked it into the branches of the birch.

"Read it," she said. "The garden is open now. Anyone can come."

The small crowd dispersed. Alice stayed.

She stood under the birch and looked at the aconites.

"Thank you," she whispered.

The wind moved through the branches.

It sounded like yes.

Chapter 44: The Plaque

Alice found the memorial plaque in the estate chapel. It was small, brass, mounted on the wall.

"Sacred to the memory of Captain James Ashford, 7th Battalion Suffolk Regiment. Born 1891. Died 1917. Beloved son."

No mention of Eleanor.

Alice stood before it for a long time.

"She deserves to be remembered," she said.

She commissioned a second plaque. Silver birch wood, hand-carved.

"Eleanor Grey. 1893-1927. She who tended the winter garden."

She mounted it on the wall beside James's plaque.

Margaret saw it and cried.

"She would have liked that," Margaret said.

"I hope so."

"She was invisible, you know. After she left. No one spoke of her."

"Until now."

Margaret nodded. "Until now."

Alice looked at the two plaques. James and Eleanor. Together at last.

She thought about all the invisible women. The ones who loved and lost and were forgotten.

She could not save them all.

But she could save Eleanor.

Chapter 45: The Opening

The garden opened to the public on February 15.

A slow trickle of visitors came. Old couples. Young families. A school group.

Alice led tours. She showed them the snowdrops, the hellebores, the aconites. She told them about James and Eleanor.

"He built this for her," she said. "He planned every bed. Every plant. He never saw it finished."

A woman asked: "Did she ever forgive him?"

Alice thought about it. "I don't think there was anything to forgive. He died. She grieved. But she kept the garden alive. That's not forgiveness. That's love."

The woman nodded.

Alice led the group to the centre bed.

"These are winter aconites," she said. "Eranthis hyemalis. They bloom in February. They were planted for Eleanor's birthday."

The children knelt and looked at the tiny flowers.

"It's like gold," one said.

Alice smiled. "Yes. It is."

She watched them walk the paths. She watched them touch the birch. She watched them read the letters displayed in the potting shed.

The garden was alive again.

And she had been the one to wake it.

Chapter 46: The Healing

Alice stayed at Wynchwood through March. She had no reason to return to London. No job waiting. No flat.

She rented a room in the village. She walked to the garden every morning.

She watched the garden change. The crocuses bloomed in March. The bluebells in April. The roses in June.

She planted new things. She followed James's plan, but she added her own touches. A herb garden near the potting shed. A bench under the birch.

She thought about her marriage. It had ended because she had stopped talking. She had retreated into silence.

The garden had taught her to speak again.

She called her mother. They talked for an hour.

She wrote to an old friend. She apologized for disappearing.

She started sketching. Just plants at first. Then the garden. Then the sky.

She was not healed. Not completely. But she was better.

She was growing.

Like the garden.

Like the aconites, year after year.

Chapter 47: The Letter

Alice received a letter in March. It was from a woman named Rachel Grey.

"Dear Ms. March,

I am Eleanor Grey's great-niece. A friend sent me the article about the winter garden. I have photographs of Eleanor. I would like to bring them to you.

Yours,
Rachel Grey"

Alice wrote back immediately.

Rachel Grey arrived on a Saturday. She was in her forties. She had Eleanor's eyes.

She brought a photograph. Eleanor in the garden, 1918. She was standing under the birch. She was smiling.

Alice held the photograph carefully.

"She's beautiful," Alice said.

"She was. My grandmother always spoke of her."

"Did she ever talk about the garden?"

Rachel shook her head. "She never came back. But she kept a pressed hellebore in her Bible. Until the day she died."

Alice opened the potting shed. She showed Rachel the notebook. The letters. The teacup.

Rachel touched the cup gently.

"She drank from this," Rachel said.

"Every morning. In the garden."

Rachel sat on the workbench. She was quiet for a long time.

"I never knew," she said. "My family never spoke of it."

"Now you know."

"Now I know."

Alice took Rachel to the centre bed. The aconites were fading. But the leaves were green.

"He planted these for her," Alice said.

"Every February."

"Yes."

Rachel knelt and touched the soil.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For finding her."

Chapter 48: The Photograph

Rachel gave Alice a copy of the photograph. Eleanor under the birch, 1918.

Alice framed it and hung it in the potting shed.

She looked at it every morning.

Eleanor was wearing a white dress. Her hair was pinned up. She was smiling, but her eyes were sad.

She was twenty-five years old. The war was over. James was dead.

But she was in the garden. The garden he built for her.

Alice thought about all the mornings Eleanor had walked these paths. All the evenings she had sat under the birch.

She thought about the loneliness. The waiting. The grief.

But she also thought about the love. The love that built a garden. The love that tended it for eight years.

The love that survived.

Alice touched the photograph.

"I see you," she said.

She went outside. The garden was quiet. The roses were blooming.

She sat on the bench under the birch and watched the light move across the wall.

She was not Eleanor. She was not James.

But she was here. In the garden. Alive.

And that was enough.

Chapter 49: The Great-Niece

Rachel Grey came back in April. She brought her daughter, a girl of twelve named Lily.

Alice showed them the garden. Lily ran ahead, touching everything.

"She's a gardener," Rachel said.

"She looks like Eleanor."

Rachel smiled. "That's what my grandmother said."

They walked the paths. Lily stopped at every flower.

"What's this?" she asked.

"Hellebore. Christmas rose."

"It blooms in winter?"

"Yes. Even in the snow."

Lily knelt and looked at it closely. "How does it survive?"

"I don't know. It just does."

Lily nodded, as if this made perfect sense.

Rachel and Alice sat on the bench under the birch.

"She would have loved this," Rachel said.

"I hope so."

"I know so."

They sat in silence. The garden was full of birds.

Lily came running back. "Can I plant something?"

Alice smiled. "Of course."

She found a packet of snowdrop bulbs in the shed. She showed Lily where to plant them. Lily dug with her hands, the way Eleanor had.

Alice watched her. The next generation. Tending the winter garden.

It would survive.

Chapter 50: The Spring

Alice planted one more tree.

A crab apple, Malus sylvestris. She put it near the north gate, beside the birch.

She dug the hole. She placed the roots. She covered them with soil.

"Helen of the garden," she said quietly. "This one is for hope."

She watered it and stood.

The garden was changing. The hellebores were fading. The snowdrops were gone. But the roses were budding. The lavender was sending up new shoots.

Spring was coming.

Alice walked the paths one last time. She touched the birch. She touched the yew. She knelt at the centre bed.

The aconites were dormant now. But she knew they would bloom again next February. And the February after that. And every February, as long as the garden lived.

She thought about James. About Eleanor. About the hundred winters that had passed.

She thought about her own winter. The years of silence. The years of hiding. The marriage she let die because she did not know how to fight for it.

It was over.

She was not the same woman who had arrived in January.

She stood and looked at the garden. The walls. The paths. The beds. The tree she had just planted.

"We're going to be all right," she said.

The wind moved through the birch.

The garden answered.
