# The Thief of Small Things — Outline

## Premise

Lena has a peculiar gift: she can only steal intangible things. A stranger's memory of a childhood summer, a musician's perfect pitch, a gambler's lucky streak — she lifts them like pocket change, leaving her targets vaguely unsettled but never quite sure what they have lost. She has built a small, quiet life around this curse, taking only what she needs to survive. But when her mother falls into a mysterious wasting illness — a slow fading of spirit that no doctor can name — Lena discovers the one thing she cannot steal is the very thing her mother needs most: the will to live. Desperate, she enters the orbit of The Collector, a man who deals in stolen intangibles on a grand scale, and who has been watching Lena for years. The Collector offers Lena a deal: one final heist, the biggest she has ever attempted, in exchange for the secret to stealing the will to live. But every bargain with The Collector comes with a hidden cost, and Lena must decide how much of herself she is willing to trade for her mother's life.

## Core Cast

- **Lena** — A woman in her early thirties. She steals intangible things: memories, talents, luck, emotions. The theft leaves her targets hollowed in small, nearly invisible ways. She has never stolen anything large, anything that would truly matter — until now. Pragmatic, guarded, capable of great tenderness she rarely shows.
- **Mama** — Lena's mother. A warm, worn woman who raised Lena alone above her secondhand shop in the village of Marcheux. She is fading slowly, her body healthy but her spirit withdrawing like a tide. She does not know about her daughter's gift.
- **The Collector** — A man of indeterminate age and impeccable dress. He runs a hidden trade in stolen intangibles — emotions, talents, memories — from a warehouse at the edge of the city. Cold, patient, and deeply curious about Lena's unique limitation. He sees in her a puzzle he has not yet solved.
- **Duvray** — The local clockmaker, one of Lena's occasional clients. He buys stolen patience and steady hands for his trade. Gruff but protective of Lena.
- **Sister Celeste** — A nun who runs the village hospice. Kind, observant, quietly suspicious of Lena's comings and goings.

## 50 Chapter Plan

### Act I: The Touch of Empty Hands

1. Lena wakes in her childhood room above Mama's shop and senses something is wrong — the house feels hollowed out, as if a vital warmth has drained away overnight.
2. Mama cannot get out of bed. She stares at the ceiling and does not speak. The doctor finds nothing physically wrong.
3. Lena steals the memory of a good day from a neighbor to give to Mama, hoping it will spark her back. It does not work.
4. Duvray the clockmaker warns Lena that people have noticed her mother's condition — and that strange questions are being asked in the village.
5. Sister Celeste visits Mama and tells Lena that the spirit can sometimes leave a body before the body fails. She has seen it before.
6. Lena experiments with her gift, testing what she can and cannot steal. She realizes will is not a small thing — it is a fortress.
7. A stranger arrives in Marcheux. He wears a fine coat and watches Lena from café tables.
8. The stranger approaches Lena outside Duvray's shop. He knows what she is. He introduces himself only as "someone who collects rare things."
9. The Collector demonstrates his power: he shows Lena a bottled aurora, a stolen talent for languages, a sealed jar of pure grief. He offers tea and conversation.
10. Lena refuses The Collector's invitation but cannot shake his final words: "You cannot steal what you do not understand, and you do not understand the will to live because you have never had to fight for yours."

### Act II: The Inventory of Loss

11. Lena remembers the first thing she ever stole — her father's love for her mother, lifted when she was seven years old, an accident that shattered their marriage.
12. She has been afraid of her gift ever since. She uses it only in small ways, controlled ways, never for anything that matters.
13. Mama rallies briefly, sits up, asks for tea. Lena dares to hope. By evening, Mama has sunk back into silence.
14. The Collector sends a message: a small velvet box containing a stolen sunset. No note. Lena understands it is a lure.
15. Lena visits the hospice where Sister Celeste works. She steals the peace from a dying man's final hours — and feels it settle in her own chest like ash.
16. The theft leaves Lena shaken. She returns to Mama and tries to press the peace into her, but it will not transfer. Stolen things cannot be regifted.
17. Duvray confesses that he told The Collector about Lena. He was paid in stolen patience — three years' worth — for his trembling hands.
18. Lena is furious but understands. Everyone makes bargains. She decides to learn what The Collector knows.
19. She travels to the city, to the address on The Collector's card. The warehouse is vast, dim, filled with glass jars of light and shadow and color.
20. The Collector offers her a tour. She sees a library of stolen childhoods, a gallery of forgotten talents, a vault of luck both good and bad. He calls it the Inventory.

### Act III: The Cost of Wanting

21. The Collector explains the rules of his trade: intangibles lose potency over time, cannot be regifted, and every theft leaves a mark on the thief.
22. Lena asks directly: can the will to live be stolen? The Collector smiles and says it is the rarest commodity. He has never managed it himself.
23. He offers Lena a deal: steal something for him — a specific memory from a specific man — and he will tell her everything he knows about the will to live.
24. Lena accepts. The target is a retired judge who lives in the city. The memory: the judge's first ruling, the one that made his reputation.
25. Lena stalks the judge for three days, learning his habits, his rhythms, his vulnerabilities. She feels like a common pickpocket.
26. She steals the memory during a crowded charity gala. The judge staggers, confused, as a lifetime of confidence unravels in his chest.
27. Lena returns to The Collector with the memory in a jar — a golden thread of light. She feels sick.
28. The Collector keeps his word. He explains that the will to live is not an object to be stolen. It is a fire. You cannot steal a fire. You can only borrow an ember — and the borrowing costs the lender.
29. Lena asks what he means. The Collector says: someone must choose to give it. Willingly. And that choice will destroy them.
30. Mama worsens. Sister Celeste sends word that the end may be weeks away. Lena burns the velvet box with the stolen sunset. She will find another way.

### Act IV: The Ember Trade

31. Lena returns to Marcheux and sits with Mama day and night. She talks to her, reads to her, plays music she remembers Mama loving.
32. Mama speaks once: "You were always reaching for things that weren't yours." Lena does not know if Mama knows, or if it is fever.
33. The Collector arrives in Marcheux. He offers a different deal: he knows someone who might give their will to live willingly — but only if Lena brings him something first.
34. The something is Lena's own first memory. The Collector wants to know why she can steal only intangibles. He believes the answer is in her earliest memory.
35. Lena refuses. Her first memory is her own. It is all she has that has never been stolen or traded.
36. Duvray falls ill. His stolen patience is wearing thin, and his hands shake worse than ever. Lena steals him steadiness from a sleeping child. She hates herself.
37. Mama has a lucid hour. She tells Lena she is not afraid. She tells Lena she has always known her daughter was different. She tells Lena to let her go.
38. Lena cannot let go. She steals grief from a widow in the village to see if she can understand loss better. The grief sits in her like a second heart, cold and heavy.
39. Lena returns to The Collector and agrees to his terms. She gives him her first memory in a jar — a memory of rain on a tin roof, her mother's voice singing, the smell of bread.
40. The Collector examines the memory and is fascinated. He sees in it the origin of Lena's gift: a child who sensed her parents' emotions too keenly and learned to take them just to survive.

### Act V: The Last Small Thing

41. The Collector keeps his word. He arranges a meeting with a woman named Elara, who is dying of the same slow fading as Mama. Elara is willing to give her will to live — but only to someone who will use it well.
42. Lena meets Elara in a sunlit room at the hospice. Elara is serene, ready. She asks Lena one question: "What will you do with what I give you?"
43. Lena tells the truth: she will give it to Mama, and Mama will live. Elara asks if Mama would want to live on borrowed fire. Lena does not answer.
44. Elara places her hand on Lena's chest. Lena feels the transfer — a warmth like sunlight pouring through her ribs, a fierce and terrible desire to exist.
45. The will to live burns in Lena now. She races back to Marcheux, desperate to give it to Mama before it fades.
46. Mama is sleeping. Lena presses her hands to her mother's chest and tries to push the fire through. It will not go. Stolen things cannot be regifted. Borrowed things cannot be regifted either.
47. Lena screams. She has traded her first memory for nothing. Mama stirs, opens her eyes, and looks at Lena with something like recognition.
48. Mama speaks: "You carried me inside you once. Now I carry you." She reaches up and touches Lena's face. In that touch, something passes between them — not stolen, but given. Mama takes Lena's borrowed will to live and makes it her own.
49. Mama sits up. She is weak, thin, but present. The fire has found its home. Elara passes away that same night, at peace. The Collector disappears from Marcheux, leaving behind a single jar: Lena's first memory, returned.
50. Lena opens the jar. The memory of rain and bread and singing fills the room. She sits with Mama and lets it wash over them both. She has stolen many things, but this — this was given. She decides that from now on, she will only take what is freely offered. She closes the empty jar. Outside, the rain begins to fall.
