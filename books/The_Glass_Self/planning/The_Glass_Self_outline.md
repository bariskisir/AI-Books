# The Glass Self — Outline

## Premise

After surviving a near-fatal car accident on a wet November highway, psychiatrist Dr. Vincent Crane develops Cotard's delusion — the rare neurological and psychiatric condition in which the sufferer believes they are dead, have lost their organs, or have ceased to exist. The precision of Vincent's situation is almost unbearable: for twenty years he has treated Cotard patients with professional distance and clinical authority, writing papers, presenting at conferences, and reassuring families that the condition is treatable. Now he inhabits the very delusion he described in textbooks, certain that the man walking his hospital's corridors is a corpse performing the motions of life. His wife Elena watches her husband become a stranger who denies his own heartbeat. His colleague Dr. Sarah Ng must treat the man who trained her, navigating both ethics and grief. His son Patrick comes home to find a father who insists, with perfect logical consistency, that they are speaking to no one. And Vincent himself — brilliant, methodical, entirely lucid about everything except the central fact of his own existence — must decide whether his conviction is a symptom to be corrected or a revelation the accident tore loose.

---

## Core Cast

- **Dr. Vincent Crane, 48** — Consultant psychiatrist, dry wit, exacting, loved by patients for his patience. Survived a T-bone collision on the Westbrook bypass. Now believes he died in that accident and has been walking dead ever since. His knowledge of Cotard's makes him a uniquely cooperative and uniquely resistant patient.
- **Elena Crane, 45** — Architect, precise thinker, deeply practical. Has spent twenty years translating Vincent's emotional opacity into liveable family life. Now faces the possibility that the man she loves may not recover, or may not be willing to.
- **Dr. Sarah Ng, 38** — Registrar turned consultant, trained partly under Vincent. Assigned as his treating psychiatrist because no one else will do it. Brilliant, careful, privately terrified of failing her mentor.
- **Patrick Crane, 22** — University student, reading history. Home for the first time since the accident. Treats his father's condition with a mixture of dark humor and brittle tenderness that masks genuine fear.
- **Dr. Oliver Marsh, 65** — Department head, administrator, political survivor. Likes Vincent professionally, finds this situation professionally inconvenient, and handles both feelings badly.
- **The Mirror** — Not a character. A motif. Vincent checks every mirror he passes because Cotard patients sometimes report seeing nothing. He always sees himself. This disturbs him more than absence would.

## Chapter Outline

### Part One: The Accident and Its Aftermath (Chapters 1–10)

**Chapter 1: Before the Glass**
Vincent drives home through rain. He is on the phone with Elena, distracted, clinical even in conversation. He describes a patient's delusion to her before he rounds the bend. The crash.

**Chapter 2: The White Room**
Vincent wakes in ICU. He catalogs his injuries with professional detachment. He tells the nurse his heart stopped for forty-three seconds. Something in him notes: it never started again properly.

**Chapter 3: Elena at the Window**
Elena's point of view. She waited in the family room for four hours. She describes the hospital from the outside in, the way an architect reads buildings: structure, load, the places that might fail.

**Chapter 4: Discharge**
Vincent is released. He walks through the hospital to the car. He pauses at a polished floor section and stares at his reflection. He says nothing. Elena notices.

**Chapter 5: The House**
Back home. Patrick is still at university. Elena has prepared the spare room because Vincent said he preferred not to sleep in their bed. He can't explain why. He lies in the spare room staring at the ceiling and understands, with quiet certainty, that he is not breathing unless he thinks about it.

**Chapter 6: First Consultation**
Vincent returns to work too early. Sarah is covering his caseload. He reviews her notes in his office and finds them technically correct and emotionally inert. He writes a correction in the margin. He does not tell her about the spare room.

**Chapter 7: The Mirror Begins**
Vincent starts checking mirrors. In the hospital bathroom before his first ward round back. He sees himself. He makes a note in his personal journal: "The reflection proves nothing. A dead man casts a reflection. This is the error in the myth."

**Chapter 8: The Journal**
We read from Vincent's journal. Entries dated three weeks after discharge. His prose is controlled, diagnostic, applying clinical terminology to his own experience. He does not call it Cotard's. He calls it "the clarification."

**Chapter 9: Patrick Comes Home**
Patrick arrives by train for the mid-term break. Vincent meets him at the station. The reunion is stilted. Patrick makes a joke about the car; Vincent doesn't respond to the joke. He responds to the logical content.

**Chapter 10: Sarah Notices**
Sarah finds Vincent standing outside a patient's room, not entering. He's been there for eleven minutes. She asks if he's all right. He says: "I don't have a good answer to that question." She files a concern note. Marsh calls her into his office.

---

### Part Two: The Diagnosis Diagnosed (Chapters 11–20)

**Chapter 11: Marsh's Office**
Oliver Marsh explains the situation to Sarah: the Trust won't permit Vincent to keep seeing patients unless cleared. Sarah is asked — not ordered, Marsh is too cautious for orders — to conduct an informal assessment. She refuses. Then she agrees.

**Chapter 12: The First Session**
Sarah and Vincent meet in his office. He has rearranged the chairs so they face parallel rather than opposite, a standard technique for reducing confrontation. He is doing the thing he would do. She asks about the accident. He answers in the third person.

**Chapter 13: Cotard's, Technically**
Vincent explains Cotard's delusion to Sarah with academic precision, as though giving a lecture, as though she hasn't attended those lectures. He then says: "You'll note I'm not claiming I have it." He pauses. "I'm claiming I died."

**Chapter 14: Elena and Sarah**
Sarah calls Elena. Elena has been waiting for this call. She describes the changes: the spare room, the mirrors, Vincent's refusal to eat unless reminded, his certainty that food serves no biological purpose for him now. She says: "He's not frightened. That's what frightens me."

**Chapter 15: The Body as Evidence**
Vincent undergoes a physical examination. His heart is fine. His ECG is normal. His neurological examination is unremarkable. He reviews each result and explains, with a surgeon's patience, why normal results are consistent with post-mortem metabolic continuation.

**Chapter 16: Night Ward**
Vincent cannot sleep. He walks the hospital at 2 a.m. He sits in an empty consultation room. A cleaner finds him there, asks if he's lost. He says: "No. I know exactly where I am."

**Chapter 17: Patrick and Elena**
Mother and son at the kitchen table. Patrick is trying to fix things by understanding them. Elena is trying to hold things together by not understanding them too clearly. They talk around Vincent for most of the conversation and then Patrick says: "Is he going to get better?"

**Chapter 18: The Medication Question**
Sarah proposes antidepressants — Cotard's often responds to SSRIs and antipsychotics. Vincent engages with the pharmacology thoughtfully, asks about efficacy studies, agrees to a trial. He tells her he's doing it for Elena, not for himself, because a dead man has no need of medication.

**Chapter 19: The Letter**
Vincent writes a letter to his patients. Not sent, just written. He explains, as though dictating a clinical note, that their doctor is no longer available. He leaves it in his desk drawer. Sarah finds it during a risk assessment.

**Chapter 20: Sectioning**
Marsh, Sarah, and the hospital trust solicitor meet. The question of involuntary admission is raised. Sarah argues against it: Vincent is not a danger, he is compliant, he is engaged in treatment. She wins the argument. She is not certain she should have.

---

### Part Three: The Interior Country (Chapters 21–30)

**Chapter 21: The Glass Self**
Vincent writes the longest journal entry. He describes what it feels like from inside: not grief, not depression, but clarity. He is like a glass figurine — perfect form, no interior warmth, no continuation. He writes: "I am not suffering. That is the symptom."

**Chapter 22: Elena's Blueprint**
Elena draws architectural plans for a house they will never build. She does this when she needs to feel competent. She designs around the assumption that Vincent will improve. She doesn't tell him she's doing this.

**Chapter 23: Ward Round Without**
Sarah runs a ward round Vincent would normally lead. She finds herself making decisions the way he would. She is briefly angry about this. Later she is grateful.

**Chapter 24: The Support Group**
Sarah locates a rare Cotard's support group — for families. She suggests Elena attend. Elena goes once, sits in the back, does not speak. A woman there whose husband recovered describes the moment he said he felt warm again. Elena drives home and sits in the driveway for thirty minutes.

**Chapter 25: The Logic**
Vincent engages Sarah in a philosophical argument: if subjective experience is the proof of existence, and he experiences certainty of non-existence, then he exists only as a kind of error — a persistent process with no subject. He is genuinely curious what she thinks. She says: "I think you're doing what you always do. Turning the argument into a room with no doors."

**Chapter 26: Patrick's Walk**
Patrick and Vincent walk along the canal path near their house. Vincent describes the canal's drainage system. Patrick says: "Dad, I miss you." Vincent looks at him. He says: "I'm right here." He means it as a correction. Patrick hears it as a cruelty. They finish the walk in silence.

**Chapter 27: The Paper**
A colleague from Edinburgh sends Vincent a draft paper on Cotard's. He reads it annotating margins. His notes are lucid, clinical, helpful. He emails back seventeen suggestions. The colleague thanks him effusively. Vincent notes in his journal: "The dead can still edit."

**Chapter 28: The Medication Takes Effect**
Three weeks on medication. Vincent tells Sarah in session that he has noticed a softening — not in the belief, but in the certainty's texture. He says: "The conviction is the same. It is slightly less loud." She writes this down carefully.

**Chapter 29: The Smell of Rain**
Vincent walks to the car park at 7 p.m. It has rained. The smell of wet tarmac is what he remembers from the accident. He stands for a long time. He goes back inside and tells a nurse the corridor smells of rain. She says it doesn't. He thanks her.

**Chapter 30: Elena's Ultimatum**
Not harsh — she is not a harsh woman. But Elena tells Vincent that she cannot sustain the current arrangement indefinitely. She does not want a divorce. She wants him to stay in treatment and to come back to their bedroom. He sits with this. He says: "Give me until the end of the month."

### Part Four: The Treatment and the Test (Chapters 31–40)

**Chapter 31: The Photo**
Patrick finds a box of photographs from Vincent's childhood. He shows them to his father. Vincent looks at the photographs of himself as a child and says, without cruelty: "That person died in the accident too." Patrick does not speak to him for three days.

**Chapter 32: Full-Dose**
Sarah increases the antidepressant. Vincent experiences nausea, insomnia, a week of what he describes as "heightened static." He continues to come to sessions. He continues to discuss his condition with clinical interest.

**Chapter 33: Marsh Intervenes**
Marsh visits the house. Social call, he says. Elena makes coffee. Marsh talks about the hospital, the department, the new funding cuts. He does not mention what he came to say until he is putting his coat on: that the Trust needs a decision by the end of the month. Sarah calls this a professional ambush.

**Chapter 34: The Dream**
Vincent dreams. He doesn't often, or hasn't since the accident. He is in the car again, and the impact comes, and then he is standing on the road in the rain looking at the crashed car and the figure inside it. He walks to the car. The figure inside is him. But it's also not him — younger, more frightened, more alive. He wakes at 3 a.m. and sits with the image.

**Chapter 35: The Return**
Vincent tells Sarah he wants to return to clinical work on a supervised basis. She says they will need to discuss this with Marsh. She does not say no. He takes this as a significant thing.

**Chapter 36: Sarah's Doubt**
Sarah talks to her supervisor — a therapist she's been seeing for two years, separate from the hospital. She describes the case without names. She says: "My patient is the most self-aware person I've ever treated. I don't know if that helps or makes it worse."

**Chapter 37: The First Patient Back**
Supervised return. One patient, a woman with severe health anxiety. Vincent is careful, warm, precise. He gives her exactly the right thing. Afterward he sits in his office and Sarah comes in and he says: "It's still there. But it moved when I needed it to."

**Chapter 38: Elena's Project**
Elena is awarded a commission: a new residential building in the city center. She tells Vincent at dinner. It's the first dinner they've eaten together in two months. He says: "Tell me about the load-bearing problem." She does. For an hour they talk about her work. It is the closest thing to normal they've had.

**Chapter 39: The Mirror Shifts**
Vincent checks a mirror in the hospital corridor. He sees himself. Then he notices: he's been checking the mirror after good things now. Not to verify absence but to verify presence. He writes in his journal: "I looked to confirm rather than to catch. This may be the difference."

**Chapter 40: The Crisis**
A patient of Vincent's — one who was returned to his caseload — presents in crisis at 11 p.m. Vincent is on call. He handles the crisis correctly, stays until 2 a.m., calls the right people, makes the right decisions. On the drive home he begins to cry. Not dramatically — just tears, without a clear reason. He calls Elena from the car park and she stays on the phone until he is ready to drive.

### Part Five: The Return and the Remainder (Chapters 41–50)

**Chapter 41: The Bedroom**
Vincent moves back into the bedroom. Not as a declaration — he just appears in the doorway one night and Elena lifts the duvet and he gets in. He lies on his back. She puts her hand on his chest, over his heart. He says: "I can feel that." She says: "Good."

**Chapter 42: Marsh's Resolution**
Vincent meets with Marsh and the Trust. He presents his own recovery clearly: partial remission, continuing treatment, willing to accept further supervised practice. Marsh, to his credit, fights for him. The Trust agrees to a six-month supervised return.

**Chapter 43: Patrick's Essay**
Patrick has written a university essay on Cotard's. He shows it to his father — not to make a point, but because he doesn't know how else to say he's been trying to understand. Vincent reads it carefully. There are things wrong with it. He corrects them with a pen. Then he writes at the bottom: "Good work."

**Chapter 44: The Rain Again**
Vincent drives the Westbrook bypass for the first time since the accident. Elena doesn't know. He stops at the exact spot. The hedges have been cut back. There's no sign anything happened there. He sits for a while. He drives on.

**Chapter 45: Sarah's Last Official Session**
The supervised treatment formally concludes. Sarah and Vincent have their last structured session. She asks him where he is now with the belief. He says: "It's like a scar. I know what it is. I can feel where it was. I wouldn't say it's gone."

**Chapter 46: The Conference**
Vincent presents a paper at a psychiatry conference. Not on Cotard's — he is not ready for that. On something else, something technical. Afterward a colleague says: "You seem different. Better." He says: "I had some time to think."

**Chapter 47: Elena's Building**
Elena's building is complete. She takes Vincent to see it before the official opening. They stand in the atrium looking up at the light coming through the roof. He says: "You made something." She says: "Yes." He says: "That's real." She doesn't say anything, but she takes his arm.

**Chapter 48: The Journal Ends**
Vincent writes the last journal entry. He describes the condition with clinical precision one final time. Then he writes: "I am not certain I'm alive. I am certain I am here. For now, I will accept that the difference may not matter." He closes the journal. He does not throw it away.

**Chapter 49: Patrick's Return**
Patrick goes back to university. The goodbye is different from the arrival. Vincent hugs him — initiates it, which he hasn't done in months. Patrick says: "Call me, yeah?" Vincent says: "Yes." Both of them know it isn't certain. Both of them mean it.

**Chapter 50: The Glass Self**
Vincent alone in the hospital corridor at night after a late call. He passes a mirror and stops. He looks for a long time. The reflection looks back. He says, quietly, to no one: "Still here." He doesn't know what he means by that. He walks on.
