Chapter 1: The Broken Hunt

The mammoth did not die fast enough.

Ayla drove her spear into its flank, the ivory tip grating against rib. The beast wheeled, trunk lashing. Snow exploded around her feet.

Behind her, someone screamed.

She turned. The boulder was already falling. It had been loosened by the mammoth's charge, a slab of frozen stone the size of a child. It struck Old Mother across the legs.

The old woman did not scream. She simply folded, her bone-drum clattering across the ice.

Ayla ran to her. The mammoth was stumbling now, the poison on the spear tip working through its blood. It took three more steps and sank to its knees. The other hunters finished it with stones.

But Ayla did not watch. She knelt beside Old Mother, whose legs lay at a wrong angle beneath her.

"Stay still," Ayla said.

Old Mother's face was the color of ash. She gripped Ayla's arm with surprising strength.

"The bone is broken through," she said. "I can feel it."

Ayla looked at the legs. The right one was bent sideways below the knee. The left was straight but swollen, the skin already dark.

Daron arrived, breathing hard. He looked at Old Mother and said nothing.

"Help me carry her," Ayla said.

Daron did not move. "She is the clan's heart."

"She is still alive. Help me."

They made a litter from spears and hide. The other hunters wrapped the mammoth meat in its own skin, preparing to haul it back to camp. The sun was already low, a pale disc behind veils of cloud.

The first howl came as they lifted Old Mother onto the litter.

It was distant, thin, carried on the north wind. Ayla felt it in her chest before she heard it.

The wolves had found the blood.

Daron looked east. "They will follow."

Ayla nodded. "We need to move."

Chapter 2: Blood on the Snow

They carried Old Mother across two miles of frozen ground.

The litter bumped and swayed. Every jolt made the old woman gasp. Ayla walked at her side, one hand steadying the frame, watching the snow for blood.

The wound on Old Mother's leg had soaked through the wrappings. Drops fell dark against the white. There was no stopping it. The clan's winter cave was still half a day's walk.

Kaya met them at the ridge. She had the infant strapped to her chest, his face buried in the fur of her tunic.

"I heard the howls," she said.

"We all heard them," Daron muttered.

They reached the cave as the last light bled from the sky. The entrance was low, hidden behind a curtain of icicles. Inside, the fire had burned down to coals.

They laid Old Mother on her sleeping furs. Ayla built up the fire while Kaya unwrapped the wound. The leg was purple from knee to ankle. The bone had not broken through the skin, but something inside was crushed.

Kaya looked at Ayla. Her eyes said what her mouth did not.

"She will not walk," Kaya whispered.

Ayla took the medicine pouch from around Old Mother's neck. She did not know what to do with it. The old woman had never taught her the healing ways. There had always been time.

The howl came again, closer now. Not one voice. Several.

Renn appeared in the cave mouth. His one eye was fixed on the darkness behind him.

"They are on the blood trail," he said. "They will be here by morning."

Chapter 3: The Shaman's Leg

Old Mother woke at dawn.

The fire had burned low. Ayla sat beside her, unmoving, having watched through the night. The cold had crept into the cave. She could see her breath.

Old Mother touched her leg. Her fingers explored the swelling, the heat, the wrongness of the bone beneath.

"It is dead," she said. "The leg is dead below the knee."

Ayla shook her head. "We can bind it. I can—"

"You can lead them." Old Mother's voice was quiet but firm. "I have been leading them since before you were born. Now my body is finished. But you are not."

"I am not the shaman. I am a hunter."

"You are both." Old Mother struggled to sit up. Ayla helped her, propping furs behind her back. "I have watched you. You read the snow. You read the animals. You read people. That is what a leader does."

The cave was waking. Daron stood at the entrance, speaking with Renn. Kaya was feeding the infant from a skin of melted snow.

"I do not know the way across the plain," Ayla said.

"South and east. Follow the dying sun. Three hands of days, maybe four. There is a valley where the ice does not reach. The animals go there in spring."

"How do you know?"

"I have never been. But my mother told me, and her mother before her." Old Mother took Ayla's hand. "The stories are the map. You carry them now."

Ayla looked at the old woman's leg. The skin was mottled, cold to the touch. She had seen this before, on hunters who stayed out too long in the freeze. The flesh was dying.

"We need to leave today," Ayla said.

"Then leave." Old Mother closed her eyes. "Wake me when it is time."

Chapter 4: The Gathering

Ayla called the clan together.

There were twelve of them. Thirteen counting the infant. Old Mother could not stand, so they gathered around her sleeping furs. The fire crackled between them.

Daron spoke first. "We cannot travel with her like this. She will die on the plain."

"She will die if we stay," Ayla said. "The wolves will find us. The winter is not done. The mammoth meat will last ten days, maybe twelve."

"So we eat the meat and wait for spring."

"Spring is two moons away. The meat will rot before then. We will starve."

The others shifted. Renn leaned on his spear. Kaya held the infant close. The two other hunters, Torv and a man named Gred, stared at the fire.

"The old woman said there is a valley to the east," Renn said. "Past the frozen plain. Green ground, open water."

"She has never seen it," Daron said.

"She has the stories." Ayla met his eyes. "That is all we have. Stories and our feet."

"Your feet," Daron said. "Your plan. You are young, Ayla. You have never led."

"No. But I have never lost a hunt. I have never abandoned a clan member." She stood. "We leave at midday. We take the meat, the furs, the fire-starters. We travel light. We travel fast."

"And the wolves?"

"We watch them. We do not challenge them. We let them follow until we reach the valley."

"And if they attack?"

Ayla picked up her spear. The ivory tip caught the firelight. "Then we fight."

No one spoke. Daron looked away first.

They began to pack.

Chapter 5: The Pack Arrives

Renn was the first to see them clearly.

He had climbed the scree slope above the cave, the same watch-point he had used for thirty winters. His one eye was good still. Better than most men's two.

"There are six of them," he said when he came down. "Maybe seven. Hard to count when they move."

Ayla stood at the cave entrance. The sun was high, the sky the pale blue of deep winter. The plain stretched east, white and featureless.

"Where are they now?"

"Half a mile north, circling. They found the mammoth bones. They are feeding."

"How long before they come here?"

Renn scratched his beard. "They have fed. They will not hunt tonight. Tomorrow, maybe. The scent is strong."

Ayla looked back into the cave. The clan was nearly ready. Furs were bundled, meat wrapped in hide, fire-starters packed in a watertight pouch. Old Mother lay on the litter, her dead leg wrapped in fur.

"I want to see them," Ayla said.

She walked north, past the cave's shelter, out onto the open snow. Renn followed at a distance. The wind bit through her tunic.

She saw them then, against the white. Six shapes, grey and lean, moving in a loose pack. They were not running. They were drifting, flowing over the snow like smoke.

One stopped and turned its head.

It was larger than the others. Its coat was almost white, with a dark stripe down its back. It stood still, watching her. Even at this distance, she could see its eyes.

The wolf did not growl. It did not bare its teeth. It looked at her the way she looked at prey, measuring.

Behind her, Renn cleared his throat.

"The pack leader," he said.

Ayla nodded. She held the wolf's gaze for a long moment, then turned and walked back to the cave.

Chapter 6: Leaving the Caves

They left the winter cave at midday.

The sun was a white coin behind cloud. No shadows. The world was flat and featureless, a sea of snow without shore.

Ayla walked at the front. Behind her came the litter bearers, Renn and Torv, with Old Mother wrapped in furs. Then Kaya with the infant, then Gred hauling the meat sled, then the others. Daron walked at the rear, watching their back trail.

The snow was crusted on top, hard enough to hold a man's weight. Good walking, if it lasted. Old Mother had said three hands of days. Four at most.

Ayla had not told them that she did not know how they would find the valley. The sun was the only guide, and it hid behind the clouds. She walked south and east, toward the place where the sky looked thinner.

After two hours, she stopped to check on Old Mother. The old woman was awake, her eyes fixed on nothing.

"Water," she said.

Ayla put a skin of melted snow to her lips. Old Mother drank.

"The wolves?" she asked.

"Following."

"Of course. They know." Old Mother closed her eyes. "The pack leader has done this before. He has followed dying things across the white. He knows how it ends."

"It ends with us in the valley."

Old Mother did not answer.

Ayla walked on. The sun shifted behind the clouds. The wind picked up, carrying ice crystals that stung the face. Behind them, invisible but present, the pack moved through the white.

Chapter 7: First March

The first night on the plain was the coldest Ayla had ever known.

They made camp in a depression between two low ridges. There was no wood for a proper fire, only a handful of dry twigs they had brought from the cave. Ayla nursed a small flame in a hollow of stone, just enough to melt snow for drinking.

The clan huddled together for warmth. Old Mother lay in the center, wrapped in every fur they could spare. Her breathing was shallow but steady.

Kaya sat beside Ayla, the infant asleep against her chest. She was young, two winters younger than Ayla, but her face was old.

"How far have we come?" Kaya asked.

"Half a day. Maybe eight miles."

"Is that enough?"

"It is a start." Ayla poked the fire. "We need to go faster. The weather will turn."

Daron appeared out of the darkness. He had been walking the perimeter, his spear in hand. He sat down heavily.

"They are closer," he said.

"How close?"

"Close enough." He held his hands to the fire. "They are not hunting. They are waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For one of us to fall behind." Daron looked at Old Mother. "It will not be long."

Ayla said nothing. She had seen the same thing. The pack was patient. They knew the plain. They knew that everything on it eventually grew weak.

She lay down that night with her spear beside her. The wind moaned across the ice. Somewhere in the darkness, a wolf howled.

It was answered by another, and another. A chorus of hunger.

Ayla closed her eyes and tried to sleep.

Chapter 8: Old Mother's Fever

Old Mother woke them with her shaking.

Ayla felt it first. The old woman's body trembled against hers, a vibration that traveled through the furs. She sat up and touched Old Mother's face. It was burning.

"Fire," Old Mother whispered. "There is fire in my bones."

Ayla uncovered the leg. The swelling had spread. The skin was black in patches, cracked and weeping. The smell was wrong, sweet and rotten.

Kaya held a torch close. When she saw the leg, she turned away.

Ayla packed snow around the wound, trying to cool it. Old Mother hissed through her teeth.

"Leave it," she said. "The heat is from inside. Snow will not reach it."

"Then what will?"

"Nothing." Old Mother coughed. "The leg is poison now. It is killing me slowly."

Daron stood apart, watching. "She needs to stay still. Travel will make it worse."

"We cannot stay," Ayla said.

"Then she dies."

"She dies either way." Ayla's voice was flat. "But the clan lives. We move at dawn."

Kaya looked at her with something like fear. Ayla did not meet her eyes.

She sat with Old Mother through the rest of the night, changing the snow on her leg, wiping the sweat from her face. The fever burned. The old woman muttered in a language Ayla did not know.

Just before dawn, Old Mother opened her eyes.

"Ayla."

"I am here."

"You must leave me."

"No."

"I am dead already." Her voice was thin but clear. "The leg is dead. The poison will reach my heart soon. When it does, cut me open. Take the medicine pouch. Take the stories."

"I do not know the stories."

"You will learn them." Old Mother squeezed her hand. "But first, you must live."

Chapter 9: The Wolf's First Kill

The pack took the reindeer at dawn.

Ayla saw it from the ridge. The reindeer had been separated from its herd, a young buck with a lame foreleg. It stood alone on the white, head low, knowing.

The wolves came from three directions. The alpha led, moving straight at the buck's throat. The others flanked.

It was over in heartbeats.

The alpha's jaws locked on the throat. The reindeer kicked once, twice, and went down. The pack swarmed over it, tearing, feeding.

Ayla watched. The wolves fed until their bellies swelled. Then they lay in the snow, sleeping in the sun.

Renn came up beside her. "They will not follow for a while. Full bellies make slow hunters."

"We should use the time. Push harder."

Renn nodded. "There is a frozen river ahead. If we cross it before night, we gain a day."

"How do you know?"

"I hunted this plain when I was young. Before the ice took my eye." He tapped his temple. "The land is in here. The way is south and east, past the black rocks."

Ayla looked where he pointed. She could see nothing but white.

"You see rocks?"

"I see everything." Renn smiled. "One eye sees more than two, sometimes."

They moved. Ayla pushed the clan harder, keeping the pace fast. The meat sled cut deep grooves in the snow. Old Mother's litter swayed. Her fever had broken, but she was weaker now, barely conscious.

The wolves did not follow that day. They stayed with the reindeer kill, gorging and resting.

But Ayla knew the alpha would find them again. He always did.

Chapter 10: Daron's Doubt

They made camp that night beside the frozen river.

It was wide, maybe two hundred paces across, its surface a sheet of blue-white ice. The current moved beneath it, a dark sound in the silence.

Daron stood at the edge, testing the ice with the butt of his spear.

"It is thick enough," he said. "We can cross in the morning."

"Good," Ayla said.

Daron turned to face her. The firelight caught the hard lines of his face. "No. Not good. We are three days out. Old Mother is dying. The wolves are waiting. And you have no answer for any of it."

"I have answers. You do not like them."

"You have luck. That is all." He stepped closer. "You led us away from the cave because you were afraid. You did not think about what happens after."

"I thought about it. I thought about staying in that cave until the meat ran out, until the wolves found us cornered, until we starved." Ayla did not step back. "The plain is dangerous. The cave was death."

"Old Mother is dying on this ice because you moved her."

"Old Mother was dying in the cave because a rock crushed her leg." Ayla's voice was hard. "Do not put her blood on me."

Renn stepped between them. "Enough. The river is the argument. Not each other."

Daron looked at Ayla for a long moment. Then he turned and walked away.

Kaya came to Ayla later, the infant sleeping.

"He is afraid," she said.

"We are all afraid."

"He is afraid of you. Because you are not afraid."

Ayla looked at her. "I am afraid, Kaya. I am afraid every moment. I just do not let it stop me."

Chapter 11: The Frozen River

The crossing began at first light.

Renn went first, his spear testing the ice with every step. The surface was smooth, almost polished. Ayla could see the darkness of the water below, deep and cold.

"Follow my steps," Renn called. "The ice is not even. There are thin places."

The clan moved in single file. The meat sled came next, then the litter bearers with Old Mother. Kaya walked behind them, the infant strapped tight. Daron brought up the rear.

The ice groaned under their weight. Ayla felt it through her feet, a deep sound like the earth yawning.

Halfway across, the ice cracked.

It was a sharp sound, a whip crack. Everyone stopped. Ayla held her breath.

The crack ran across the surface, a dark line spreading from the sled's runners. Gred, pulling the sled, went still.

"Do not move," Renn said.

The ice held. The crack did not widen.

"Slowly," Renn said. "One step at a time. Do not stop."

They moved. Ayla counted her heartbeats. Each step was a risk. Each groan of the ice was a warning.

The far bank came closer. Twenty paces. Ten. Five.

Ayla stepped onto solid ground. She turned and watched the others follow. Gred came next, pulling the sled, the crack following him like a thread. Then the litter bearers, moving carefully. Old Mother did not open her eyes.

Kaya came last, her face pale. When she stepped onto solid ground, she sat down in the snow, shaking.

Daron was the final one. He crossed without looking down.

On the far bank, three grey shapes stood watching. They had not followed onto the ice. They did not need to. They knew the river was a barrier, and barriers did not last.

Chapter 12: Through the Ice

The wolves did not cross that night.

But in the morning, one of them was dead.

Ayla found it on the river, half-submerged, its body caught in a crack in the ice. It had tried to cross in the dark and broken through. The water had taken it, frozen it, held it.

It was one of the adolescents. A grey yearling, thin-ribbed, its legs splayed in the ice.

The pack stood on the far bank. The alpha was at the front, watching. He did not howl. He did not move. He simply looked at the dead wolf, then at Ayla.

She understood. The pack was smaller now. One less mouth to feed. One less hunter to help. The balance had shifted.

"We should take the meat," Daron said.

"No," Ayla said.

"It is fresh. The ice killed it, not disease."

"The pack is watching. If we take it, they will attack."

"They will attack anyway."

"Not today." Ayla turned from the river. "We move."

They left the wolf in the ice. The pack did not follow. They stayed with their dead, circling, sniffing, learning what the ice could take.

Ayla pushed the clan hard that day. They crossed the plain at a pace that left them gasping. The sun moved across the sky, always in front of them, always to the east.

Old Mother woke once, briefly.

"Where are we?" she asked.

"South of the river. Still on the plain."

"Good." Her voice was barely a whisper. "The mountain is close. Three sleeps. Maybe two."

"The mountain?"

"At the edge. You will see it. Black against the white."

Her eyes closed. Her breathing slowed.

Ayla walked on, looking east. She saw nothing but ice and sky.

Chapter 13: The Lost Hunter

Torv disappeared during the second watch.

No one saw him go. One moment he was there, sitting by the fire, rubbing his hands together. The next, he was gone.

Kaya noticed first. She had been feeding the infant and looked up to ask Torv for more snow. He was not there.

"Torv?" she called.

The wind answered.

Ayla got up. She walked the perimeter, looking for tracks. They led away from camp, heading north, back the way they had come.

"Did he say anything to anyone?" Ayla asked.

"He was talking about the caves," Gred said. "Before he slept. He said the caves were safe. The plain was not."

"Was anyone with him?"

"Only the wolves."

Ayla looked north. The tracks were already filling with snow. She could see a dark shape in the distance, moving slowly across the white.

"He is going back," Daron said.

"He will not make it."

"His choice."

Ayla gripped her spear. Half the clan looked at her. The other half looked north, at the shrinking figure.

"We do not go after him," she said.

Daron raised an eyebrow. "The great leader leaves a man to die?"

"I lead the living. He chose to leave. I will not risk the clan for a choice he made against my word."

Kaya said nothing. The infant cried. Ayla turned away from the north.

They broke camp in silence.

That night, a wolf howled from the direction Torv had gone. It howled once, and then there was silence.

Ayla did not sleep.

Chapter 14: Smoke on the Plain

On the fifth day, Ayla saw smoke.

It was thin, barely visible, a grey thread against the white sky. It rose from somewhere ahead, maybe two miles east.

The clan stopped. Everyone saw it.

"Fire," Renn said. "Someone else is on the plain."

"Friend or enemy?" Kaya asked.

"There is only one question on the plain," Renn said. "Hungry or fed. Everything else is a story."

Ayla studied the smoke. It rose steadily, not a campfire. A cooking fire, sustained, tended.

"We approach carefully," she said. "Spears ready. No threats. We see who they are before they see us."

They moved in a spread formation, the sled and litter in the center, hunters on the flanks. The smoke grew closer. Ayla could see the shape of a shelter now, a low hide tent half-buried in the snow.

A man emerged. He was thin, bearded, dressed in patched furs. He carried no weapon.

He saw them and raised his empty hands.

"I have nothing to fight with," he called. "I have nothing to steal. Only my family and my hunger."

Ayla walked forward alone.

"We are the Broken-Claw Clan," she said, using the old name. "We are crossing to the eastern valley."

"The valley." The man laughed, a dry sound. "I have been trying to reach that valley for twenty days. I have not found it."

"Old Mother says it is there."

"Old Mother is a story." He gestured behind him. "I have a wife and a daughter. We are out of food. We have been eating snow for three days."

Behind him, a woman appeared in the tent opening. She held a child to her chest.

Ayla made her decision.

"Bring them," she said.

Chapter 15: The Strangers

The man's name was Herran. His wife was Sella. The child was a girl of perhaps four winters, too young to have a proper name yet.

They had been hunters from a southern clan, driven north by a war over hunting grounds. They had been wandering for months.

"We have meat," Ayla said. "Enough to share for a few days."

Herran looked at the sled, at the wrapped joints of mammoth. His eyes were hollow.

"How much do you want in return?"

"Nothing. We do not trade for survival."

Daron made a sound in his throat. Ayla ignored him.

They set up camp together. The strangers had no fire, no dry wood. Ayla gave them a coal from their own fire and a strip of meat each.

The little girl ate too fast and vomited. Her mother held her while she wept.

Kaya sat beside them. She gave the girl a piece of softened hide to chew.

"What is her name?" Kaya asked.

"Mar," the mother said. "It means 'small bird.'"

"She is beautiful."

The mother smiled, but her eyes did not.

That night, around the fire, Herran told them about the land ahead.

"The plain goes on for days. Then the ground rises. There are black rocks, then a pass through the mountains. That is where the green is."

"Have you seen it?" Ayla asked.

"No. But a trader told me. He said the valley is wide, with a river that does not freeze. Fish in the water. Deer in the hills."

"How far?"

"Seven days. Maybe eight. If the weather holds."

Ayla looked at Old Mother. The old woman had not spoken since morning.

Seven days. They had meat for four.

Chapter 16: Sharing the Fire

After the strangers had eaten, Daron pulled Ayla aside.

"You are giving away our food."

"I am keeping us alive."

"They will slow us down. The child cannot walk. The woman is weak. The man is half-starved."

"They know the way. We do not."

Daron shook his head. "You find reasons for every risk."

"I find reasons to survive." Ayla kept her voice low. "They have been on this plain longer than us. They know where the ice is thin. They know where the animals are. We need them."

"We need food."

"Then we hunt." Ayla turned back to the fire. "Tomorrow, I hunt. Renn comes with me. The rest stay here, rest, gather strength."

"And the wolves?"

"They will wait. They have learned patience."

Daron said nothing. He walked away and sat with his back to the fire, facing the darkness.

Ayla returned to the fire. Herran was showing Kaya how to set a snare with sinew and a bent twig. The little girl Mar was asleep in her mother's lap.

Renn watched Ayla.

"You did the right thing," he said.

"I do not know if I did."

"That is what right feels like." Renn picked up his spear and tested the edge. "Wake me when it is time to hunt."

Ayla sat by the fire. The wolves were out there, somewhere beyond the light. And somewhere ahead, the valley waited.

She touched the medicine pouch at her neck. Old Mother's pouch. Her pouch now.

Chapter 17: Old Mother's Story

They stayed an extra day to rest.

The sky was clear, the wind low. Ayla set snares along a rabbit trail while others repaired the sled and re-wrapped Old Mother's leg.

In the afternoon, Old Mother woke.

She looked around the camp slowly, taking in the strangers, the fire, the meat sled.

"More people," she said.

"They are joining us. They know the way."

Old Mother nodded. "Sit with me."

Ayla sat. The old woman's hand found hers. It was cold.

"I want to tell you the story," Old Mother said. "The one about the first wolf."

"I have heard it."

"You have heard the words. Now you need to hear the meaning."

Old Mother took a breath. Her voice was thin but steady.

"The first people did not have fire. They lived in the dark, afraid of everything. The wolves had fire in their eyes. The people wanted that fire."

She paused, coughing.

"The first woman went to the wolf leader. She said, 'Teach me your fire.' The wolf said, 'I will teach you, but you must feed me first. You must feed me even when you are hungry. You must share your kill with me, and I will share my fire with you.'"

Old Mother looked at Ayla.

"The woman agreed. And that is why wolves follow us still. Because we made a promise. We have been feeding them ever since. Keeping our word."

"The wolves out there did not make that promise."

"No. But you did." Old Mother smiled. "Your mother made it. Her mother before her. The promise passes down. That is why they watch but do not attack. They are waiting to see if you will keep it."

Ayla sat in silence. The fire crackled. Somewhere in the dark, a wolf howled.

Chapter 18: The Wolf Leader Watches

The alpha came close the next morning.

Ayla was checking the snares alone when she saw him. He stood on a rise fifty paces away, unmoving, his grey-white coat blending with the snow.

She stopped. He did not run.

They looked at each other across the white space. The wind moved between them. Ayla could see his ribs through his coat. He was thin. They were all thin.

He took a step toward her. Then another.

Ayla did not reach for her spear. She held her ground.

The wolf stopped ten paces away. His head was low, his tail down. Not a threat. Not submission. A question.

Ayla spoke to him, her voice low.

"I have no food for you yet. But I will keep the promise."

The wolf's ears shifted. He stood still for a long moment. Then he turned and walked away, disappearing into the white.

She stood there, heart pounding, until he was gone.

When she returned to camp, Renn saw her face.

"You saw him."

"Yes."

"Close?"

"Close enough to touch."

Renn nodded. "He is testing you. Measuring."

"I know."

"What will you do?"

Ayla picked up her spear. "I will keep moving. If the promise is real, he will stay back. If it is not, he will attack."

"And if he attacks?"

"Then I will know the story was wrong." She looked east. "We leave now. No more delays."

They broke camp. The strangers followed. The clan was fifteen now, plus the infant. Fifteen mouths. Fifteen sets of eyes on Ayla.

She led them east.

Chapter 19: Ayla's Dream

That night, Ayla dreamed.

She was running. The snow was soft beneath her feet, but she did not sink. She moved fast, faster than a person could, four legs pushing against the white.

She was a wolf.

The pack ran with her. She felt them beside her, behind her, their breath pluming in the cold. They were not hunting. They were moving, flowing over the ice like water.

The alpha ran at her side. In the dream, he was not a wolf. He was a man with grey eyes and a scarred face. He ran in silence.

They crested a ridge. Below them lay the valley, green and warm. Water ran. Grass grew. Animals grazed.

"This is where," the wolf-man said. "But you are not ready."

"Why?"

"Because you still think you are leading them." He looked at her. "You are not leading. You are serving. The clan does not follow you. They trust you. Trust is not follow."

Ayla woke with a start.

The fire had burned low. The camp was quiet. She could hear Kaya's breathing, the infant's soft whimpers.

She sat up and looked east. In the moonlight, the plain stretched forever.

She did not sleep again.

In the morning, she told no one about the dream. But she walked differently. Softer. As if she was listening instead of watching.

Chapter 20: The Storm

The blizzard hit without warning.

One moment the sky was clear. The next, the wind rose from the north, carrying a wall of white. Ayla had time to shout before the world disappeared.

"Stay together! Hold hands!"

The wind ripped the words from her mouth. Snow filled her eyes, her nose, her lungs. She grabbed Kaya's arm and pulled her close.

The clan huddled together. Ayla found a dip in the ground, a shallow scoop where the snow had not drifted deep. They pressed into it, bodies tight, furs pulled over their heads.

The storm raged for hours. Ayla could not see, could not hear. She felt the others against her, their heat, their shaking. The infant cried, a thin sound swallowed by the wind.

At some point, Old Mother's hand found hers.

"I am still here," the old woman said.

"Stay with me."

"I will stay as long as I can."

The storm passed as suddenly as it had come. The wind dropped. The snow stopped falling. The sky cleared to a deep, cold blue.

They dug themselves out. Sella was blue-lipped and shivering. The little girl Mar was unconscious but breathing. One of the meat sled's runners had broken.

And Herran was gone.

Chapter 21: When the Wind Dies

They found Herran's body fifty paces from camp.

He had wandered into the storm, trying to find firewood. The wind had taken his sense of direction. He had walked in circles until the cold took him.

Sella sat beside him in the snow. She did not cry. She held his hand and spoke to him in a language no one else understood.

Mar woke and asked for her father. Kaya carried her away.

"We bury him," Ayla said.

"There is no time," Daron said.

"There is time for this."

They dug a shallow grave in the snow. It was the only grave they could make. The ground was frozen a thousand hand-depths down. They laid Herran in the snow and covered him with stones.

Renn spoke. He did not know the stranger's gods, so he spoke to the sky.

"Your journey is over. The rest of us will carry your name."

Sella did not move from the grave until Mar pulled at her hand.

Ayla looked at the clan. They were fewer now. Fifteen had become fourteen. And Old Mother was fading.

"East," she said. "We keep going east."

No one argued. They packed what remained and walked.

Behind them, the wolves found Herran's grave. Ayla heard them digging through the stones, but she did not look back.

Chapter 22: Counting Food

Kaya took inventory at the midday rest.

She had been keeping count on a strip of hide, marking each portion with a scratch. The numbers were not good.

"Four days of meat," she said. "Maybe three if we eat full portions."

"And the strangers?"

"They eat what we eat."

Ayla looked at the clan. They sat in small groups, chewing dried mammoth, conserving energy. Sella held Mar in her lap. The little girl had not spoken since her father died.

"The wolves are still following," Renn said. "They did not eat well from Herran."

"They are waiting."

"They are starving."

Ayla walked to the edge of the camp, where the snow dropped into a shallow ravine. The pack was visible, a cluster of grey shapes lying in the snow. They were conserving energy too.

The alpha sat apart, facing the camp.

Ayla felt the weight of it. She was leading hungry people across an empty land, pursued by hungry wolves. Every choice was a calculation. Every meal was a subtraction from the days they had left.

"We need to hunt," she said.

"Where?" Kaya asked. "There is nothing here."

"There is always something. You just have to find it."

Ayla picked up her spear. She chose Renn to come with her. The others would stay, rest, watch Old Mother.

"Do not go far," Daron said.

"I will go as far as I need to."

She walked into the white.

Chapter 23: The Huntress Goes Out

Ayla and Renn walked south for an hour.

The plain changed as they moved. The flat snow gave way to hummocks, low ridges, patches of frozen grass poking through. Animal signs.

Renn found the tracks first. Small, delicate, the imprints of a hare.

"Fresh," he said. "Passed here maybe an hour ago."

They followed the tracks north and west. Ayla moved silently, her spear balanced, her breath slow.

The hare was feeding behind a mound of snow, nibbling at frozen moss. It did not see them.

Ayla raised her spear. She threw.

The spear struck the hare through the middle. It kicked once and went still.

Renn grunted. "Good throw."

"It is not enough. One hare for fourteen people."

"Then we find more."

They reset the spear and kept walking. Ayla's snares from the night before had yielded nothing. The plain was empty. The animals had gone east, or south, or somewhere she could not follow.

By the time the sun began to sink, they had the hare and nothing else.

The wolves had watched them the whole time. Ayla saw their shapes at the edge of sight, drifting, waiting.

She carried the hare back to camp. Fourteen people. One hare.

That night, the meat was divided into portions smaller than Ayla's palm.

No one complained. There was no point.

Chapter 24: The Hare

The division of the hare was a quiet ceremony.

Kaya cut the meat, her knife thin and sharp. She gave each person a piece, carefully measured. The bones went into a skin of water to make broth. The marrow was saved for Old Mother.

Ayla watched them eat. She did not eat herself.

"You need to eat," Kaya said.

"I will eat when we have enough."

Kaya held out her own portion. "Take some of mine."

"No."

"Then I will not eat either."

Ayla looked at her. Kaya's face was stubborn, hard.

"You would starve for me?"

"I would not let you starve for me." Kaya pressed the meat into Ayla's hand. "You are the leader. Leaders need strength."

Ayla ate. The meat was tough, stringy, barely enough to coat her tongue. But it was something.

The wolves had gathered closer to camp. Ayla could see their eyes in the darkness, green reflections at the edge of the firelight.

"They are hungry too," Renn said.

"So are we."

"Who will break first?"

Ayla did not answer. She looked at the fire, at the dying coals, at the faces of the clan around her.

They were all waiting. For food. For the valley. For something to change.

She was waiting too.

Chapter 25: Daron's Plan

Daron came to her that night.

"The hunting ground," he said. "The caves where we came from."

"What about them?"

"We should go back. The wolves will not stay. They will follow the easier prey."

"There is no easier prey. We are all starving."

"There is meat in the caves. We left hanging haunches. They will be frozen, preserved."

"We cannot carry it. That is why we left it."

"Then we go back and eat it there. Wait out the winter. Try again when the thaw comes."

Ayla shook her head. "Old Mother said the thaw is two moons away. We do not have two moons of food."

"We have caves. Shelter. We can hunt the local game."

"The local game is gone. That is why we left."

Daron's face tightened. "You are young, Ayla. You do not know when to turn back."

"I know when dying is certain. Going back is certain death. Going forward is chance."

"Chance is not a plan."

"It is the only plan we have."

Daron stood. He looked down at her, his fists clenched. For a moment, Ayla thought he might strike her.

"You are killing them," he said.

"I am trying to save them."

"Same thing, at the end." He walked away, into the dark.

Ayla sat alone. The fire popped. The wind moaned.

She thought about the caves. About the warm darkness, the frozen meat, the safety of stone walls.

She thought about the valley. Green. Warm. Alive.

She did not know which was real and which was a story.

Chapter 26: The Pack Hunts

The wolves brought down a bison at dawn.

Ayla heard the struggle before she saw it. The bellow, the snarling, the wet sound of flesh tearing. She ran toward the noise, Renn behind her.

The pack had cornered a bull bison against a rock outcropping. It was old, its ribs showing, one horn broken. It had been separated from the herd, weak, ready.

The alpha had its jaws locked on the bison's throat. The others tore at its flanks, its belly. The bison fought, kicking, throwing one wolf against the rocks. But there were too many.

It went down.

The pack fed. They tore into the bison's belly, eating the warm organs first. Blood soaked the snow.

Ayla watched from a distance. The meat was there, a hundred pounds of it, maybe more.

"We take it?" Renn asked.

"Not now. They are too close. Too hungry."

"We wait?"

"We wait."

They sat in the snow, watching the wolves eat. The pack gorged themselves, their bellies swelling. When they finally pulled away, stumbling and full, the carcass was half-eaten.

Ayla approached. The wolves watched her, too full to move.

She cut what she could carry. Strips of meat from the hindquarters, the liver, the tongue. Enough to feed the clan for two days.

When she returned to camp, no one asked where she had been.

They just ate.

Chapter 27: Old Mother's Breath

Old Mother's breathing changed on the eighth night.

Ayla heard it first. The rhythm had shifted. Each breath was a labor, a pause, a struggle to begin again.

She lit the fire and checked the old woman's leg. It was black now, the rot spreading past the knee, reaching toward the hip. The smell was thick, sweet, something that belonged in a dead body.

"Old Mother," Ayla said.

The old woman opened her eyes. They were clear. Clearer than they had been in days.

"The valley," she said.

"We are going. We will make it."

"You will make it." Old Mother coughed. "I will not."

"Please."

"Do not beg. It does not suit a leader."

Ayla took her hand. It was cold, thin, the bones sharp under the skin.

"The wolves will not touch me," Old Mother said. "I am too old. Too tough."

"Do not joke."

"I am not joking." She smiled. "I am telling you what happens. I stay here. You go on. That is the way."

"I will not leave you."

"You will. Because the clan needs you. Because the valley is real. Because I have seen it in my dreams, and I am telling you it is there."

Old Mother squeezed her hand.

"The season turns. The snow will end. You will see green."

Ayla wept.

Old Mother closed her eyes. Her breathing slowed. But she did not stop.

Chapter 28: The Left Hand

Ayla's right hand was frostbitten.

She had not noticed it happening. The cold had crept in slowly, numbing her fingers, turning the tips white. She only noticed when she tried to grip her spear and could not feel the shaft.

She unwrapped the hand. The skin was hard, waxy, the fingers stiff.

Kaya examined it, her face careful.

"Can you move them?"

Ayla tried. The fingers bent, barely. Pain shot through her hand, sharp and hot.

"You need to warm them slowly. If you warm them too fast, you will lose them."

"I cannot lose them. I need to hunt."

"Then do not hunt with this hand." Kaya wrapped the hand in soft fur. "Use the other."

"My left hand cannot throw."

"Then learn."

Ayla looked at her left hand. It felt clumsy, weak, a child's hand.

She spent the rest of the day practicing. Throwing stones, then small sticks, then her spear. The first throws went wide. The second went farther. By evening, she could hit a target at ten paces.

It was not enough. But it was something.

The wolves watched her practice. They sat at the edge of the camp, silent, patient.

That night, Sella approached Ayla. She held something in her hand. A bone needle, thin and sharp.

"For the hand," she said. "Keep the blood moving. Sew the fur tight."

Ayla took it. "Thank you."

Sella nodded. She did not speak much, but she had not stopped moving. She gathered wood, tended the fire, watched her daughter.

They were all learning to use what they had.

Chapter 29: The Huntress Fails

Ayla saw the deer at midday.

It was a young doe, grazing at the edge of a frozen pond. Its coat was thick, winter-white. It had not seen the clan.

Ayla signaled to Renn. She moved downwind, her spear in her left hand. The grip felt wrong, the balance off.

She got within twenty paces. The doe lifted its head, ears twitching.

Ayla threw.

The spear flew wide. It struck the ice behind the doe and skittered away. The deer bolted, white tail flashing, gone into the white.

Renn said nothing.

Ayla stared at the empty space where the deer had been. She picked up her spear. The shaft was undamaged. The failure was hers.

"It is the hand," she said.

"It is the mind," Renn said. "You are thinking about missing. You are not thinking about hitting."

"How do I stop thinking about missing?"

"You miss more." He shrugged. "That is how learning works."

They walked back to camp empty-handed. The clan looked at them, hoping, then looked away.

In the afternoon, Kaya served broth. It was thin, mostly water, with scraps of dried meat floating in it.

Ayla did not drink hers.

Daron watched her. "The hunter returns without food."

"Tomorrow will be different."

"Tomorrow will be the same. It will keep being the same until you admit you cannot do this."

Ayla met his eyes. "I will not admit that."

"Then we all starve."

"We will not starve."

But her left hand ached, and her stomach was empty, and she did not know if she believed herself.

Chapter 30: Kaya's Courage

Kaya found Ayla before dawn.

"I will hunt," she said.

Ayla sat up. Kaya stood in the firelight, her face set. She held a spear, a short one that had belonged to Torv.

"You have never hunted," Ayla said.

"Then teach me."

"Kaya, it is dangerous. The wolves are—"

"The wolves are starving. I am starving. The infant is starving. I would rather die trying than sit here waiting."

Ayla looked at her. There was something in Kaya's eyes she had not seen before. Not desperation. Determination.

"Your brother," Ayla said. "If you die, who takes him?"

"Sella. I asked her. She said yes."

Ayla stood. She picked up her own spear, holding it in her good hand.

"All right. I will teach you."

They walked out together, into the grey pre-dawn light. Renn watched them go, a small smile on his face.

The wolves watched too. Kaya held her spear tight.

"They are watching," she said.

"Let them. They need to see that we are not prey."

"What are we?"

"Hunters."

Ayla stopped at a ridge and pointed at the snow.

"Look. Tracks. What made them?"

Kaya bent, studied the print. "Deer. Young, from the size."

"Which way?"

"East. Toward the rising sun."

"Good." Ayla felt the ghost of a smile. "Let us follow."

Chapter 31: Two Hunters

They tracked the deer for two hours.

Kaya was quiet, watchful. She learned fast. She read the ground, noticed the broken twigs, the disturbed snow. She moved without sound, her weight balanced, her breath slow.

Ayla watched her and remembered learning the same skills. Her father had taught her. He had been patient, exacting.

"You see it?" Kaya whispered.

Ayla followed her gaze. The deer was ahead, bedded down in a hollow between two drifts. Its head was up, alert, but it had not seen them.

"Your throw," Ayla whispered. "From here. Aim behind the shoulder."

"I cannot. It is too far."

"You can. The spear will carry."

Kaya raised the spear. Her hand shook.

"Breathe," Ayla said. "Throw on the exhale."

Kaya breathed. She held. She threw.

The spear flew straight. It struck the deer in the flank, not deep, but enough. The deer lurched up, bleeding, stumbling.

"Again," Ayla said. "Quickly."

Kaya did not have another spear. She looked at Ayla, panic in her eyes.

Ayla threw her own spear. It hit the deer in the throat.

The deer went down.

They stood in silence, breathing hard. The deer kicked once, twice, and was still.

Kaya walked to the deer. She touched it, her fingers coming away red.

"I did it," she said.

"We did it." Ayla pulled her spear free. "Now we carry it back."

They dragged the deer to camp together. They arrived as the sun reached its peak.

The clan had not eaten meat in three days.

Chapter 32: The First Blood

They butchered the deer with reverence.

Kaya did the cutting. Her hands were steady. She had watched a thousand butcherings, but this was the first time the blood was on her hands.

She worked carefully, saving every part. The hide, the sinew, the bones for broth. The organs went into a separate pile, to be eaten first.

The meat was divided equally. Everyone got a portion. Even the infant got broth, rich and warm.

Sella held her daughter as the girl drank. Mar's color had been bad, grey around the mouth. The broth brought pink back to her cheeks.

Old Mother could not eat. Ayla held broth to her lips, but most of it ran down her chin.

"Drink," Ayla said.

"I am past needing it." Old Mother's voice was a thread. "I have eaten enough in my life. Let the others have mine."

"Old Mother—"

"Do not argue. It wastes energy."

Ayla divided the old woman's portion among the others. Daron ate his in silence. He did not look at Ayla, but he did not argue either.

That night, for the first time in days, there was no howling.

The wolves were quiet. Perhaps they had found their own deer. Perhaps they were planning something else.

But for one night, the clan slept with full stomachs.

Kaya sat by the fire, her spear across her knees. She was watching the darkness the way Ayla watched it.

A new hunter had been born.

Chapter 33: The Feast

The morning after the kill, Ayla set snares along a game trail.

When she checked them at midday, two held hares. One was still alive, kicking, its leg caught in the sinew loop.

She killed it quickly. A snap of the neck. No suffering.

The clan ate well again that night. There was enough for everyone, and enough left over for the morning. They roasted the hares on sticks, the fat dripping into the fire.

Sella and Mar sat closest to the flames. Mar had started speaking again, small words, asking for more.

"You are feeding them like they are clan," Daron said.

"They are clan now."

"They have been with us five days."

"Five days is enough. We have shared food. We have shared cold. We have shared death." Ayla turned to him. "That makes them clan."

Daron looked at her. His face was hard, but something in his eyes shifted.

"She would have died. Her daughter too."

"Yes."

"You took them in with nothing."

"Yes."

"That is either wisdom or foolishness."

"I do not know which yet."

Daron sat down across from her. He took a piece of meat from the fire and chewed slowly.

"I was wrong about Old Mother," he said. "She can lead. Through you."

Ayla said nothing.

"I do not trust your plan," Daron said. "But I trust you."

It was the closest he had come to acceptance.

They ate in silence, the fire crackling between them. Somewhere in the darkness, the wolves watched.

Chapter 34: The Wolf Takes a Deer

The alpha stole the deer carcass the next night.

Ayla had left it hanging from a rock outcropping, high enough to be out of reach. But the alpha had jumped, pulled it down, dragged it away.

They found the spot in the morning. The carcass was gone. Only the head remained, and the bones.

Kaya stared at the empty space. "All of it?"

"All of it."

"How?"

"He jumped. Climbed." Ayla traced the claw marks on the rock. "He wanted it."

Daron kicked the snow. "That was half our remaining food."

"I know."

"We should have guarded it."

"We could not guard everything." Ayla's voice was even, but her jaw was tight. "We needed to rest."

The clan gathered. They looked at the empty rock, at the tracks leading east.

Sella spoke. "He is telling you something."

Ayla turned to her. "What?"

"The wolf wants you to know he is stronger. He is sending a message."

"Then I will send one back."

She walked east, following the tracks. The clan watched her go.

She found the alpha on a ridge, half a mile away. The deer carcass lay beside him, half-eaten.

He watched her approach. He did not run. He did not growl.

Ayla stopped twenty paces away.

"Keep the meat," she said. "We will find more."

The alpha's ears shifted. He lowered his head to the carcass and tore another mouthful.

Ayla turned and walked back.

The message had been sent. Received.

Now they both knew where they stood.

Chapter 35: Renn's Last March

Renn died on the eleventh day.

He was sitting by the fire, sharpening his spear. The motion stopped. His hands went still. The spear fell into the snow.

Ayla felt it before she saw it. A silence where his breath had been.

"Renn?"

He did not answer.

She turned. He was sitting upright, his one eye open, staring at the horizon. His face was peaceful.

She knew he was gone before she touched him.

The clan gathered. They stood in a circle around the old tracker. No one spoke.

Kaya started to cry, quiet tears she wiped away quickly.

"He was the best of us," Daron said.

"He was the last of the old ones." Ayla knelt beside him. "He knew the land. He knew the stories. He knew how to read the ice."

She closed his eye.

"We bury him like the old ones. A stone for every season he saw."

They built his grave through the afternoon. They gathered stones from the outcropping, carried them in their arms, piled them over his body.

Daron found a flat stone for his head. Ayla scratched a line across it with her knife. A single line, meaning a journey ended.

She spoke over his grave.

"Renn of the Broken-Claw. Tracker. Hunter. Keeper of the plain. You walked this land before we were born. You will walk it after we are gone. Go now to the green valley, where the game is plenty and the winter never comes."

She did not know if the words were true. But they felt true.

Chapter 36: Burying the Tracker

After Renn's burial, Ayla called the clan together.

"We are eleven now," she said. "Eleven who can walk. The infant does not count. Old Mother cannot walk. The rest of us must carry them."

She looked at each face. Daron. Kaya. Gred. Sella. Mar. Two other hunters, thin and silent. The infant, unknowing.

"We have six days of food. Maybe five if the portions are small. The valley is three days ahead, maybe four."

"How do you know?" Daron asked.

"Old Mother told me. And Renn told me, in his way. The ground is rising. The snow is thinning. We are close."

Gred spoke. "The wolves are close too."

"We let them be close. We do not fight them. We do not feed them. We ignore them."

"And when they attack?"

"We cross that risk when it comes."

The clan dispersed. Ayla stayed by Renn's grave.

Kaya came to her.

"You are lying to them," Kaya said. "About the valley."

"I am not lying. I do not know for certain. But I am not lying."

"How do you know when we are close?"

"The mountain." Ayla pointed east. "See there, where the sky is darker? That is the land rising. The valley is on the other side."

Kaya looked. She nodded slowly.

"Three days," she said.

"Maybe four."

"We will make it."

They walked on, into the white.

Chapter 37: The Split

Daron left on the twelfth day.

He took Gred with him. And two of the other hunters, the thin ones who never spoke their names.

They left at dawn, while Ayla was checking the snares. When she returned, they were gone. Their sleeping furs were empty. Their share of the meat was missing.

Kaya was waiting for her.

"They went north. Back toward the caves."

Ayla set down her snares. They were empty. Nothing to show for the morning.

"Did they say anything?"

"Daron said to tell you he was sorry. But he could not follow a woman to a story."

Ayla looked north. The tracks were fresh, clear in the snow.

The pack had split. Three wolves followed Daron. The alpha stayed with Ayla.

"They will not make it," Kaya said.

"Daron is a good hunter."

"The caves are six days back. They have three days of food."

"They know that."

Kaya shook her head. "He would rather die his way than live yours."

Ayla said nothing. She felt the loss like a wound. Not for Daron. For what his leaving meant. The clan was smaller now. Weaker.

She turned to the east.

"Come. We still have a valley to find."

They walked. The mountains were closer now, dark against the pale sky. The ground rose beneath their feet.

Behind them, the alpha followed.

Chapter 38: The Wolves Follow Daron

The alpha did not follow Daron.

Ayla had expected him to go after the larger group. Five people moving north, wounded and afraid. Easier prey.

But the alpha stayed with Ayla.

She did not understand until Kaya pointed it out.

"He is not hunting them. He is hunting you."

"Why me?"

"Because you are the leader. Because you are the threat."

Ayla looked at the wolf, a grey shape moving at the edge of sight. He was thinner now, his ribs visible, his coat patchy. But his eyes were the same. Unblinking. Patient.

"Three wolves went north," Sella said. "The yearlings. The alpha stayed, and his mate, and one other."

"The yearlings will kill Daron."

"Or they will die trying."

Ayla did not know which outcome she hoped for. She had wanted Daron to stay. She had wanted him to see the valley.

But he had chosen his path.

That night, they made camp at the base of the mountain. The ground was hard, rocky, dotted with patches of frozen moss. The wind was different here, less sharp.

Old Mother coughed. The sound was wet, deep.

"Tomorrow," she said. "Tomorrow we see the valley."

Ayla held her hand.

"Tomorrow."

Chapter 39: Old Mother's Gift

Old Mother gave Ayla the medicine pouch at dawn.

It was a small thing, made of soft leather, worn smooth by years of handling. Inside were dried herbs, a bone needle, a single feather.

"It was my mother's," Old Mother said. "And her mother's before that."

Ayla took it. It was heavier than it looked.

"What are the herbs for?"

"Pain. Wounds. Sleep." Old Mother coughed. "The feather is for the spirit. When you reach the valley, burn it. The smoke will carry your thanks to the sky."

"I do not know the words to say."

"You do not need words. The fire knows."

Ayla tied the pouch around her neck. It rested beside her own medicine bundle, the one Renn had given her.

"There is more," Old Mother said. "The valley. I have seen it."

"In your dreams?"

"I have never left this plain. But my mother described it to me. And her mother before her." Old Mother's eyes were bright, fever-bright. "The river runs from the mountain. The fish are thick. The grass is tall. The deer come in herds. It is everything we need."

"Then it is real."

"It is real. You will find it."

Old Mother closed her eyes. She was quiet for a long time.

When she spoke again, her voice was barely a whisper.

"Promise me."

"Promise what?"

"Promise you will live. Promise you will see the valley."

"I promise."

Old Mother smiled. Her hand went slack in Ayla's.

Chapter 40: Old Mother Dies

Old Mother died before noon.

She went quietly, without struggle. One breath did not follow the next. Her chest rose and fell and then was still.

Ayla sat with her for a long time.

Kaya came and sat beside her. She did not speak.

Sella brought Mar. The little girl looked at the old woman and seemed to understand. She placed a small stone on Old Mother's chest.

"For the journey," she said.

Ayla laughed. It was a strange sound, cracked and broken.

"Yes. For the journey."

They buried Old Mother at the foot of the mountain. They could not dig deep. The ground was too hard. They covered her with stones, like Renn, like Herran, like all the others.

Ayla kept the medicine pouch. She kept the bone-drum, the one that had clattered across the ice when the boulder fell.

She did not speak over the grave. There were no words left.

She stood and looked east.

The mountain rose before them. Beyond it, according to the stories, was the valley. Green. Warm. Alive.

She did not know if the stories were true.

But she was going to find out.

There were three of them now. Ayla. Kaya. Sella and Mar. And the infant, strapped to Kaya's chest.

The rest were dead or gone.

Ayla picked up her spear.

"We go," she said.

Chapter 41: Leading Alone

The climb was steep.

The mountain's flank was covered in loose stone and patches of ice. Each step was a risk. A wrong foot could send them sliding back to the plain.

Ayla led. She tested each step before committing. Kaya followed, the infant quiet against her chest. Sella came last, carrying Mar on her back.

The wolf pack followed at a distance. The alpha was visible, climbing the same slope, finding his own path.

"He is still with us," Kaya said.

"He wants to see the valley too."

"Or he wants to see us die trying."

Ayla did not answer. She kept climbing.

The sun moved across the sky, a pale disc behind thin cloud. The wind picked up, cutting through their furs.

At the ridge, Ayla stopped.

Below her, the world opened.

The valley stretched east, wide and deep. A river ran through its center, dark water moving fast. The banks were lined with grass. Not brown grass. Green grass. Living grass.

Trees grew along the riverbanks. Willows, their branches tipped with buds. The air was warmer here, the snow thin on the valley floor.

"It is real," Ayla said.

Kaya reached the ridge and looked down. She made a sound, half-laugh, half-sob.

"It is real. Old Mother was right."

Sella came last. She set Mar down. The little girl stared at the green.

"Grass," she said.

"Yes," Sella said. "Grass."

They stood on the ridge, the wind in their faces. Behind them, the plain stretched white and endless. Below them, the valley waited.

The alpha reached the ridge. He stood fifty paces away, watching.

Ayla looked at him.

"Come," she said. "If you want."

She started down into the green.

Chapter 42: The Wolf Returns

The descent took the rest of the day.

The path was steep, winding through rock and scrub. But the air grew warmer with every step. The snow gave way to mud, then to grass.

They reached the valley floor as the sun touched the western ridge.

The river was wide, maybe thirty paces across. It moved fast, clear, the water cold but not frozen. Ayla knelt and drank. The water tasted of stone and earth, not of ice.

She washed her face. The feeling of water on her skin was foreign, almost painful.

Kaya knelt beside her. She let the infant touch the water. He laughed, a sound Ayla had never heard from him.

"He is happy," Kaya said.

"He has never seen water move."

They made camp by the river. There was wood here, dry driftwood and fallen branches. Sella built a fire, the first real fire they had made in days.

Ayla lay on the grass and looked at the sky.

The stars were coming out, one by one. They looked the same here as they had on the plain. But the air was softer. The wind carried the smell of earth.

Kaya sat beside her.

"What happens now?" Kaya asked.

"Now we live."

"Is it that simple?"

"It is that simple." Ayla looked at her. "We find shelter. We hunt. We plant. We make a life here."

"As a clan?"

"As a clan. Four of us. Five counting the infant."

"It is not many."

"It is enough."

Kaya was quiet for a moment. Then she lay back on the grass.

"The grass is soft," she said.

"Yes."

"I had forgotten."

They lay there, watching the stars.

Chapter 43: The Last Deer

On the first morning in the valley, Ayla hunted.

She used her left hand. The frostbite had not healed, but the pain was less. She could grip the spear. She could throw.

The valley was full of life. Bird tracks in the mud. Fish in the river. Deer tracks along the bank, fresh and deep.

She followed the tracks upstream. The deer had been drinking, feeding, moving slowly. Not afraid.

She found them in a clearing. Three does and a fawn, grazing on new grass. They lifted their heads when they saw her, but did not run.

They had not learned to fear humans.

Ayla chose the oldest doe. The one with the dull coat and the slow step. She threw her spear.

It struck true.

The deer stumbled, ran three steps, and fell.

Ayla knelt beside it. She touched its flank, still warm.

"Thank you," she said.

She butchered it by the river, saving every part. The meat she carried back to camp in strips, draped over her shoulders.

When she returned, Sella was gathering greens by the riverbank. Kaya was teaching Mar to throw stones.

They saw the meat and stopped.

Ayla laid it out on the grass.

"We eat," she said.

They ate.

The wolves came down from the ridge that evening. They did not approach the camp. They stayed at the edge of the valley, watching.

Ayla left them a haunch of the deer on a flat rock.

Kaya watched her do it.

"You are feeding them," she said.

"I made a promise."

Chapter 44: The Birth

The infant grew weaker on the third day.

He had been quiet since the descent, sleeping more, feeding less. Kaya tried to wake him, but his eyes stayed closed.

"He is cold," she said.

Ayla touched his forehead. It was warm, too warm.

"The fever," she said.

"We have no healer."

"I have Old Mother's pouch."

Ayla opened the medicine pouch. She did not know most of the herbs. She smelled them, tasted them, tried to remember what Old Mother had said.

There was a bitter root. Feverroot, Old Mother had called it. "For when the body burns too hot."

Ayla crushed it and mixed it with water. Kaya held the infant's mouth open. Ayla dripped the liquid in.

The infant coughed, sputtered, swallowed.

They watched him through the night. Kaya held him against her skin, sharing her warmth. Ayla kept the fire high.

Sella brought water. Mar brought a handful of flowers, laying them beside the infant.

"He needs a name," Ayla said.

Kaya looked at her. "Name him."

"No. You. He is your brother."

Kaya looked at the infant. His breathing was shallow, his face flushed.

"Ren," she said. "After Renn. So he carries the tracker's sight."

Ayla nodded.

"Ren," she said. "Welcome to the valley."

The infant opened his eyes.

He looked at Kaya, at the fire, at the sky above.

And he lived.

Chapter 45: The End of the Plain

On the fifth day, Ayla climbed back to the ridge.

She wanted to see the plain. Wanted to know it was behind her.

The climb was easier now. Her body was stronger, fed on meat and fish and greens. The frostbite had not spread. Her hand was healing.

She reached the ridge and looked west.

The plain stretched to the horizon, white and endless. The wind moved across it, carrying snow. It looked the same as it had always looked.

But it was different now. She had crossed it. She had survived it.

The bones of the clan lay somewhere out there. Renn. Old Mother. Herran. Daron, maybe, if the yearlings had found him.

They had died on the white. But she had carried them across.

The alpha stood on the ridge, fifty paces away.

He was thinner. His mate was not with him. The pack was smaller now, reduced to him and the young one that stayed close.

He looked at Ayla. She looked at him.

"Where is your mate?" Ayla asked.

The wolf did not answer. But he looked east, toward the valley.

Ayla understood. The pack had crossed too. Not all of them.

"We are both alone now," she said.

The wolf's tail moved, once.

She turned and walked back to the valley.

Chapter 46: The Stand

The wolves surrounded the camp on the seventh night.

Ayla had felt it coming. The alpha had been circling closer, the young one with him. They had not taken the meat she left. They were past the point of offerings.

She woke to the sound of movement. Soft pads on grass. Low growls.

She sat up, spear in hand.

The fire had burned to coals, casting a dim red glow. The shadows moved beyond the light.

Kaya woke beside her. "They are here."

"I know."

Sella pulled Mar close. The infant, Ren, slept on, unaware.

"Build up the fire," Ayla said. "Fast."

Kaya threw dry wood onto the coals. The flames rose, casting light.

The wolves were at the edge of the ring. The alpha stood directly ahead, his eyes fixed on Ayla. The young one circled left. A third wolf, one Ayla had not seen before, circled right.

They had come for the kill.

Ayla stood, spear raised.

"Stay behind me," she said.

Kaya picked up a burning branch. "I can fight."

"Protect the child."

The alpha took a step forward. Then another.

Ayla could see his ribs, his dull coat, the hunger in his eyes. He was starving. They were all starving.

He crouched, muscles coiling.

Ayla braced.

"Come, then," she said.

Chapter 47: The Wolf in the Firelight

The alpha did not attack.

He stopped at the edge of the firelight. He sat down, his tongue lolling, his chest heaving.

He looked at Ayla. Not with hunger. With something else.

Recognition.

"You came all this way," Ayla said. "You followed us across the plain. You lost your pack."

The wolf's ears shifted.

"We are the same, you and I. Leaders who lost their people."

The wolf lowered his head. He lay down in the grass.

Ayla took a step forward. Kaya grabbed her arm.

"Do not."

"He is not going to attack."

"How do you know?"

Ayla did not know. But she walked forward anyway.

She stood over the wolf. He was bigger than she had realized. His shoulder came to her hip. His jaws could crush her arm.

But he lay still.

She knelt beside him. She could see the wound on his leg, a deep gash, infected.

"This is why your mate is gone. You tried to protect her."

The wolf looked at her. His eyes were yellow, ancient.

Ayla opened the medicine pouch. She took out the bitter root, the healing moss.

"This will hurt," she said.

She cleaned the wound. The wolf did not move. He lay still, his breath slow, his eyes on hers.

When she finished, she sat back.

"That is all I can do."

The wolf got up. He stood over her, close enough to touch.

Then he turned and walked into the darkness.

The other wolves followed.

Chapter 48: Ayla's Choice

In the morning, the camp was quiet.

The wolves were gone. Their tracks led east, deeper into the valley.

Ayla sat by the fire, her hand resting on the medicine pouch.

Kaya brought her water.

"He could have killed you," Kaya said.

"He could have."

"Why did he not?"

Ayla thought about it. About the dream she had, the wolf-man with grey eyes. About Old Mother's story, the promise between people and wolves. About the wound she had cleaned, the trust in the alpha's eyes.

"Because we made a promise," she said. "A long time ago. And he remembered."

"Is that true?"

"It is a story. But stories are true enough."

Kaya sat beside her. They watched the sun rise over the valley.

"What do we do now?" Kaya asked.

"Now we build."

Ayla stood. The valley stretched before them, green and wide. The river ran clear. The fish were thick. The deer were many.

She looked at her clan. Kaya. Sella. Mar. Ren.

Five of them. It was not many.

But it was enough.

"We find a place to make shelter," she said. "Somewhere near the river. Somewhere with good ground."

Sella pointed east. "There, where the trees are thick. Good wood. Good shelter."

They walked.

Behind them, the plain stretched white. Before them, the valley opened.

Ayla did not look back.

Chapter 49: The Truce

The wolves came to the camp once more, three days later.

Ayla was working on the shelter, lashing branches together, when she felt their presence. She turned.

The alpha stood at the edge of the clearing. Behind him, two others. The young one, and a new wolf Ayla had not seen before. A female, grey-coated, her belly swollen with pups.

The alpha had found a new mate.

He looked at Ayla. He carried something in his mouth. A hare, freshly killed.

He dropped it on the ground. Then he stepped back.

Kaya stared. "Is he... giving us food?"

"I think so." Ayla approached the hare. The wolves watched, still.

She picked it up. It was warm, the blood still fresh.

"Thank you," she said.

The alpha dipped his head. Then he turned and led his pack into the trees.

Mar clapped her hands. "The wolf brought us a gift!"

"Yes," Ayla said. "He did."

That night, they ate the hare. It was the sweetest meat Ayla had ever tasted.

She looked out at the trees, where the wolves had disappeared.

The promise was kept. On both sides.

The valley was big enough for all of them.

Chapter 50: The Green World

Spring came to the valley in a rush.

One morning the air was warm, and the snow was gone from the valley floor. The river swelled with meltwater. The grass grew tall. Flowers appeared, yellow and white, spreading across the meadow.

Ayla stood at the door of the shelter. It was a good shelter, built from willow and pine, chinked with mud. Kaya had woven a door from reeds.

Sella was planting seeds in the soft earth by the river. Mar helped, patting the dirt with her small hands. Ren crawled on a fur, grabbing at grass.

The clan was alive.

Ayla touched the pouch around her neck. Old Mother's pouch. She had not burned the feather yet. She was waiting for the right moment.

She walked to the river.

The water was clear, cold, running fast. She knelt and drank. The taste was different now. Green. Alive.

She took the feather from the pouch.

It was grey, small, from a bird she did not know. Old Mother had said to burn it when she reached the valley. The smoke would carry her thanks.

Ayla built a small fire on the riverbank. She held the feather over the flames.

It curled, blackened, turned to smoke.

The smoke rose, straight and thin, into the blue sky.

"Old Mother," Ayla said. "We made it."

The smoke scattered on the wind.

Ayla watched it go. Then she stood and walked back to her clan.

Above the valley, on the ridge that overlooked the plain, a grey shape stood watching. The alpha had made his own shelter in the rocks above. His mate was in the den, nursing pups.

He saw Ayla walk to her shelter. He saw her pause, look up.

He did not howl. He simply watched.

Then he turned and went into the den, where the new wolves were waiting.

The winter was over.
