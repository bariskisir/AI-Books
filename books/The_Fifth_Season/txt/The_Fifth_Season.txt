Chapter 1: The Ferry

The ferry arrived at Nissos on a Tuesday in November. Eleni stood at the railing with her hands buried in her coat pockets, watching the island rise from the gray sea like a sleeping animal stirring. The whitewashed buildings clung to the hillside, their shutters pulled tight against the coming winter. The dock was small, barely more than a concrete slab with rusted cleats.

She was the only passenger getting off.

The dock boy caught her suitcase when she tossed it down. He said something in Greek. The wind took his words. She nodded anyway. The air smelled of salt and wet stone and the iodine tang of dried seaweed. The wind came from the north, cold and clean, carrying nothing from the mainland. Eleni closed her eyes and let it hit her face. She had forgotten what real wind felt like, wind that had not passed through exhaust pipes and city canyons.

She opened her eyes. A woman stood at the top of the ramp, gray hair pinned back, black dress pressed and clean. Her arms were crossed. She watched Eleni climb the cobblestones.

"You are the meteorologist," the woman said. Not a question.

"Eleni Vakrinos."

"I am Maria. The room is ready."

Maria turned without waiting for a reply and walked toward the village. Eleni followed. Her suitcase wheels rattled over the uneven stones. Behind her, the ferry horn sounded, already pulling away. The vibration carried up through her feet.

The village was a single street of stone buildings. A taverna. A church. A row of houses with blue shutters. No one else was outside. The season was over.

Maria stopped at a painted sign: Petridou Taverna. She pushed the door open.

"You eat dinner here. Breakfast too, if you wake before noon."

Eleni looked up. Her window faced the sea. The ferry was gone.

"Thank you," she said.

Maria was already inside.

Chapter 2: The Room

The room was small and white. A bed with a thin mattress and a wool blanket folded at the foot. A wooden table scarred by years of use. A single chair. A wardrobe with one metal hanger inside.

Eleni set her suitcase on the floor at the foot of the bed. She looked at it for a long moment. She did not open it.

She sat on the edge of the mattress instead. It creaked under her weight. She faced the window. The glass was old, wavy, distorting the sea into something dreamlike, the horizon bending and reforming as she shifted her head. She watched the slow pulse of the waves for a long time, tracking nothing, counting nothing.

The heater clicked on, rattled, and stopped. The room went quiet again. The silence was so complete she could hear her own pulse in her ears.

She had been here for ten minutes and she could already feel the shape of the days ahead. Empty. Quiet. Measurable. She had come here for this. For the absence of schedules, of demands, of phone calls she did not want to answer. She had come because she needed to be somewhere that had no memory of her husband.

She pulled out her notebook and opened it to a blank page. She wrote: Nissos. November 14. Barometric pressure: 1013. Wind: NNW, 12 knots.

The pen scratched against the paper. She closed the notebook.

The ceiling had a crack running from corner to corner, a dark vein in the white plaster. She followed it with her eyes. It branched near the center, one fork reaching toward the window, the other toward the door. She traced its path until the light changed outside, shifting from gray afternoon to the deeper gray of dusk.

Someone knocked. Maria: "Dinner in one hour."

Eleni said okay. The footsteps retreated down the stairs.

She looked at her suitcase. She looked at the window. She stayed in the chair until the light was gone, letting the room fill with darkness.

Chapter 3: The Weather Station

The path to the headland was overgrown, barely visible through the dry brush. Eleni pushed through with her arms raised to protect her face. The branches caught at her coat. Her boots sank into the thick, wet mud. The climb took longer than she had expected, her thigh muscles burning by the time she reached the top.

The station sat on the headland like a forgotten concrete cube. The metal door was rusted at the hinges. The paint peeled from the frame in long strips. The wind sensor on the roof was missing three of its four cups, a single remaining cup spinning uselessly in the breeze.

She had requested access through the university. The Greek meteorological service had granted it reluctantly. A note on the permit, written in clipped official Greek, read: Equipment not maintained since 2019.

She worked the door open with her shoulder, forcing it past the swollen frame. Inside, the air was stale and still. Dust coated every surface. A dead fly lay on the windowsill. A desk. A chair. A computer monitor from another decade, its screen gray and lifeless. The barometer on the wall still had mercury in its glass, silver and alive.

She touched the instrument face. The glass was warm from the sun through the window. The needle wavered under her touch. She felt something shift in her chest, a small movement toward purpose.

She spent an hour taking inventory. The thermometer was accurate within half a degree. The hygrometer was off by twelve percent, the hair mechanism degraded. The rain gauge was clogged with pine needles and debris washed in by the wind.

She cleaned what she could with a cloth she found in the desk drawer, a relic of the last person who had worked here. She made a list of supplies on the back of an old chart: replacement cups, desiccant, cleaning solvent, a new bulb for the desk lamp.

Outside, the wind picked up. She stood in the doorway and watched clouds build over the eastern ridge, dark and heavy on the horizon. A front was coming. She could feel the pressure drop in her inner ear, the same way she had felt storms coming since she was a child. The feeling was familiar, almost welcome.

She wrote the prediction in her notebook: Storm by Friday.

For the first time in months, she had a reason to wake up.

Chapter 4: The First Meal

Maria brought the lamb stew to Eleni's table with both hands, a ceramic bowl that steamed in the cold air of the dining room. The smell filled the room: tomato, cinnamon, slow-cooked meat. Eleni's stomach responded before she could stop it.

She sat alone at the table near the window. The other tables were empty, chairs stacked upside down on some of them. The dining room had the hollow feel of a place built for crowds that had already gone home.

"Eat," Maria said. "You are too thin."

Eleni picked up the spoon. The stew was rich, the lamb falling apart at the touch of her fork. She took a bite. The flavors spread across her tongue. Her stomach contracted. She realized she could not remember the last time she had eaten something that had been cooked with attention. Sandwich wrappers. Instant noodles. Food consumed standing up.

The door opened. A man walked in.

He was tall and lean, with dark hair that curled at his collar, still wet. He wore a wet suit peeled down to his waist. He carried a pair of fins under one arm, his bare feet silent on the floor tiles.

He nodded at Maria and sat at the bar, a single stool pulled close to the wood. He ordered coffee in Greek, the words quick and low.

Maria brought the coffee. She said something that made him glance at Eleni. Their eyes met for a second. He looked away first.

Eleni ate her stew, the silence stretching between them. She could hear him sip his coffee. The clock on the wall ticked. A chair creaked.

They did not speak. The silence was not uncomfortable. It was the silence of two people who had forgotten how to begin.

Eleni finished her meal. She set the spoon down. She stood and walked toward the door. Her coat brushed the frame. She felt his eyes follow her out.

She did not look back.

Chapter 5: The Diver

Nico came to the taverna every evening at the same hour, just as the light failed. He ordered coffee and drank it slowly, the cup cradled in both hands. He never ordered food.

Eleni watched him from her table by the window. He watched the window, the darkening sea beyond the glass. They had exchanged nothing but glances for three days. The pattern was a ritual now: she ate, he drank, Maria moved between them, and no one said anything that mattered.

On the fourth evening, Eleni sat at the bar instead of her table. The stool was hard and the wood was worn smooth by years of use. Maria raised one eyebrow but said nothing.

Nico was two stools away. His coffee sat in front of him, untouched, cooling. He stared at the surface of the liquid like it contained something he was trying to read.

"The coffee gets cold," Eleni said.

Nico looked at her. His eyes were dark, almost black in the dim light. "I like it cold."

She nodded. She ordered tea from Maria, who placed the cup in front of her with a small grunt of approval.

Eleni wrapped her hands around the warm ceramic. The heat traveled up her wrists. She felt her shoulders drop a fraction of an inch.

"You are the meteorologist," Nico said.

"Dr. Vakrinos." A pause. "Eleni."

"Nico."

"I know. Maria told me."

He almost smiled. The corner of his mouth shifted. Almost.

"Your coffee," she said.

He picked it up and drank it.

Chapter 6: The Signal

Eleni spent the morning rewiring the anemometer, her fingers stiff with cold. The wind laughed at her efforts, whipping the wires before she could secure them. She climbed onto the roof of the station, the metal slick with dew. She balanced on the ridge, her heels pressed against the edge of the concrete.

Her hands shook as she worked the new cups into place.

The first gust spun them. A full rotation. She watched the numbers climb on the handheld reader. Thirteen knots. Fifteen. Eighteen.

She felt something like joy. The feeling startled her. She had not expected to find it here, on a wet roof with bruised fingers.

She climbed down carefully and went inside. The station was still ugly, still neglected, the same dust on the same surfaces. But the instruments were alive now, their displays lit, data streaming onto the screen. She sat at the desk and watched the numbers change in real time.

A low was forming in the Adriatic. She could see it on the pressure chart, the telltale spiral of isobars. It would track southeast. It would hit Nissos in thirty-six hours.

She calculated the pressure gradient with a pencil on the back of an old printout. The numbers were clean. This would be a proper winter storm. Not dangerous. Substantial. The kind of weather that reminded you the world was still working.

She wrote her first report in careful English, addressed to the university archive. Dry. Professional. She attached the preliminary data.

When she finished, she sat back in the creaking chair. The wind moaned through the cracks in the walls. She thought about her husband. The way he used to tease her for talking to the weather. The way he said she loved the atmosphere more than she loved him. She thought about the phone call. The sound of water.

She stood up and walked outside. The sky was clear. The sea was flat and gray.

The storm was still two days away.

She had time.

Chapter 7: The Beach

Eleni walked the shoreline in the gray afternoon. The sand was wet and packed hard by the tide. The sky was the same color as the water, the horizon line blurred, the island wrapped in a soft haze.

She found Nico's towel first. Blue fabric folded on a flat rock above the tide line. Then the weights: three lead rings stacked neatly, their handles aligned.

She looked out at the water. No one. Flat surface. No disturbance.

She stood at the edge of the dry sand and scanned the horizon. Nothing. The sea was a sheet of pewter. She waited. A minute. Two.

She was about to turn back when a dark shape broke the surface, far out where the water turned deep blue. A head. Shoulders. A man breathing, his chest heaving.

Nico raised one hand. Not a wave. An acknowledgment. Then he was gone again, pulled under by his own weight.

Eleni stayed on the beach. She did not know why she stayed. Something about the waiting felt necessary.

He surfaced closer this time, closer to shore. He swam in with steady, economical strokes. When the water was shallow enough, he stood and walked up the sand. Water streamed from his hair, his shoulders, the waistband of his suit.

He looked at her. Water dripped from his chin.

"Your towel," she said.

"I know."

He picked up the blue fabric. He dried his face. He did not put his shirt on, just stood there in the wet suit, bare-chested, the cold air raising goosebumps on his arms.

"The water is warm," he said. "Down deep."

He walked past her toward the path. She watched him go. The dark shape of his back disappeared into the trees.

She stayed on the beach until the light failed, the horizon turning from gray to charcoal to nothing, the sea and sky becoming the same dark thing.

Chapter 8: The Question

Maria hiked up the path to the weather station carrying a basket. She did not knock. She pushed the door open and surveyed the room with a critical eye.

"You work too much," she said.

"It needs calibration."

"Tomorrow it will still need calibration. Eat."

She produced bread from the basket, still warm, wrapped in a cloth. A wedge of hard cheese. A thermos of coffee that had been poured into a ceramic cup.

Eleni ate. The bread was good. The cheese was sharp and tasted of sheep and mountain herbs. She had not realized how hungry she was.

Maria sat on the edge of the desk. She picked up a chart, squinted at it, put it down. She was not pretending to understand. She simply did not care.

"Why this island?" Maria said.

"I told you. The weather station."

"You told me the data." Maria leaned forward, her face serious. "I am asking why this island."

Eleni chewed. The bread turned heavy in her mouth.

"Because it is quiet."

"Quiet places are for hiding."

"Or for working."

Maria shrugged. "Same thing."

Eleni did not answer. She stared at the bread in her hands.

Maria studied her for a long moment. She straightened her dress, a faint sigh escaping her lips.

"Nico lost someone too," she said. "Two years ago. His dive partner."

She walked to the door. She stopped with her hand on the frame.

"You are both drowning. This island is a small sea."

She stepped out. The door clicked shut behind her.

Eleni sat alone with the bread and the cheese and the thermos that was still half full. The instruments hummed. The wind sensor spun.

She did not eat the rest of the bread.

Chapter 9: The Chart

Sleep would not come.

Eleni sat at the small table in her room. The lamp cast a yellow circle on the open notebook. The rest of the room was dark. Outside, the wind was building, a low steady sound that pressed against the windows.

She drew isobars with a mechanical pencil, the lines precise and even. She calculated pressure gradients in her head before writing them down. She mapped the storm's trajectory on a blank page, the curve of the weather system arcing southeast across the Ionian.

The numbers were clean. Satisfying. The storm would arrive Friday evening. Wind from the southeast. Waves of four to five meters. Rain heavy but brief. She could predict it, name it, measure it. That was the beauty of her work. The world was legible if you knew how to read.

She wrote the forecast in English. Then she translated it into Greek, the unfamiliar professional terms clumsy on her tongue.

She checked her phone. No signal. The island had limited coverage, and the room was in a dead spot. She put the phone face-down on the table.

The crack in the ceiling caught her eye. It seemed longer than before. She followed it with her gaze, the darkness of the room making it hard to track.

She looked at her suitcase. Still closed. She had been here for six days. Six days and she had not unpacked a single item.

She opened the notebook to the first page. A photograph was tucked there, held in place by the spine. Her husband, Alex, smiling at the camera, a river behind him. He had been young in that photo. They both had been.

She had forgotten the photo was there.

She closed the notebook quickly.

Outside, the wind rose further. The loose shutter banged against the wall.

She turned off the lamp and lay in the dark, listening.

She did not sleep.

Chapter 10: The Offering

Nico came to the taverna kitchen door just after dawn. He stood in the doorway, wet suit dark with seawater, a string of silver fish hanging from his hand. The fish were fresh, their scales catching the first light, blood still on his fingers.

Maria took them without comment.

"They are for dinner," he said. "If you want."

He was not looking at Maria. He was looking past her, into the kitchen, where Eleni stood wrapped in a blanket with her hair uncombed.

Maria cleaned the fish at the sink, running water over the silver bodies. She did not look up.

"Did you sleep?" Nico asked.

"No," Maria said for Eleni. "She did not."

Eleni said nothing. She was not in the habit of being spoken for, but she did not correct Maria.

"The storm," she said finally. "I was tracking it."

"It will be here tomorrow."

"I know."

He nodded. The fish hung from his hand, dripping onto the stone floor.

"These are for dinner. Come down."

He left.

Maria turned from the sink. "You heard him. Go shower. Put on clean clothes. You will eat what he caught."

Eleni went upstairs. She stood under the hot water for a long time, letting it run over her. She put on a clean shirt. She ran her fingers through her hair, not bothering with a brush.

She came down for dinner.

Chapter 11: The Table

Maria had set two places at the same table. Side by side, facing the window. The table was covered with a cloth. Two glasses. Two forks. A candle that had not been lit.

Eleni stopped in the doorway. Nico was already seated. He was watching her, his hands still on his lap.

"Maria," Eleni said.

"Sit," Maria called from the kitchen. "The fish will be cold."

Eleni sat. Nico pushed a bottle of wine toward her. She did not take it.

The fish came to the table on a platter, grilled with lemon and oregano. The skin was crisp and golden. The aroma rose with the steam.

They ate in silence. Forks on plates. The wind outside, pressing against the shutters. The tick of the wall clock. Eleni drank water. Nico drank wine.

Halfway through the meal, Nico spoke.

"She does this. Decides things. You do not get a vote."

"I noticed."

"It is easier to let her win."

"I noticed that too."

He almost smiled. That same almost-smile, the one that barely moved his mouth but changed his eyes entirely.

They finished eating. Nico cleared the plates. Eleni watched him stack them, his movements unhurried.

Maria emerged from the kitchen. She looked at the empty plates. She looked at them. A satisfied nod.

"Good. Tomorrow the same."

Chapter 12: The Name

Eleni was at the station, her back to the door, when she heard someone step inside. She did not turn around. She had not heard footsteps on the path.

"The wind," she said. "I did not hear you coming."

Nico leaned against the door frame. "You have no lock."

"There is nothing to steal."

His eyes moved across the room. The dusty equipment. The cracked window. The single chair. The monitor that flickered when it was turned on.

"You are wrong," he said.

She turned to face him.

He picked up a barometer weight from the desk, turned it in his hands. The brass was tarnished. He seemed to study it.

"I am Nico."

"I know."

He set the weight down precisely where it had been. He looked at her, his expression unreadable.

"Now I know your name. And you know mine. Maria will not have to introduce us."

"You planned this."

"I planned nothing. She told me you were here."

Eleni turned back to her instruments, fiddling with a dial that did not need adjustment. Her hands were steady.

"The storm. It will hit tonight."

"I know. I can feel it."

"Most people cannot."

"I am in the water every day. I feel pressure changes in my sinuses."

She looked at him. He was not joking.

"That is actually accurate."

"I am not stupid."

"I did not say you were."

He leaned against the door frame and looked at the sky through the greasy window. The clouds were building.

"Your name," he said. "Eleni. It suits you."

Then he was gone, his footsteps fading on the gravel.

Chapter 13: The Lesson

The storm came that night, not violent but steady, a long gray exhale that lasted until morning. They ate dinner at the taverna. Maria made a soup of beans and greens, drizzling olive oil over each bowl before serving.

Nico joined them without being invited. He sat at the table across from Eleni and picked up his spoon.

"The water will be dirty for days," he said.

"The swell will drop by Tuesday."

"You calculated that."

"It is my job."

He stirred his soup, watching the pattern form and dissolve. "When the water clears, I will teach you to dive."

Eleni put her spoon down. "I did not ask for lessons."

"You look like you need to hold your breath."

Maria snorted into her wine.

Eleni stared at Nico. He did not look away.

"I do not trust the water."

"The water does not care if you trust it. It only cares about density and temperature."

"Then why would I go in?"

"Because the surface is where the noise lives. Underneath, there is only pressure and dark and silence. Some people find that peaceful."

Eleni thought about the phone call. The sound of water on the line. The way Alex's voice had cut off.

"Tuesday," she said.

Nico nodded and went back to his soup.

Chapter 14: The Answer

Tuesday morning was clear. The sky had been scoured clean by the storm. The light was thin and sharp.

Eleni met Nico on the beach. He had brought equipment: fins, a mask, a snorkel, a small float. She brought a towel and a fear she was trying to hide.

"The first rule," he said. "Breathe. That is the only rule."

"I know how to breathe."

"Then you are ahead of most beginners."

He showed her how to clear the mask, how to use the snorkel, how to equalize pressure in her ears. He did not touch her. He demonstrated on himself, each movement clear and deliberate. She watched his hands.

She put the mask on. The rubber was tight against her face. It smelled of salt and sunblock and years of use.

"Float face-down. Look at the sand. Nothing else."

She walked into the water. The cold hit her ankles, her knees, her waist. She kept walking until she could float.

She put her face in.

The world collapsed into quiet. The only sound was her own breath through the snorkel, a rhythmic marine sound. The sand below was rippled in patterns. A fish moved past, small and silver.

She stayed.

Nico floated beside her. She could see him in the edge of her vision, a dark shape. He was not watching her. He was watching the horizon.

She floated for ten minutes. When she stood, her legs were shaking.

"The cold," she said.

"No. Not the cold."

She did not argue.

Chapter 15: The Map

Eleni brought her charts to the taverna and spread them across the table, weighing down the corners with salt and pepper shakers. The isobars were drawn in blue ink, the wind vectors in red arrows. Pressure ridges marked with dashed lines.

Nico leaned over them. He traced the mountain ridgeline with his finger, following the topography she had sketched from satellite data.

"Here. The wind accelerates through the saddle. Between the peaks."

"That is not on the chart."

"It is on the island."

She found a pencil and made a note in the margin. She felt his eyes on her handwriting.

"The current shifts here," he said, moving his finger to a point past the headland. "Eastward."

"How do you know?"

"I swim it. Every day."

She looked at him. He was not a scientist. He had no formal training. But he knew things she did not, things the satellite imagery could not capture.

"Show me."

He looked up. "The water?"

"The currents. From above. From the headland."

They walked up the path together. At the top, the wind hit them. Nico stood at the edge and pointed into the blue expanse.

"There. You can see the line."

She saw it. A subtle shift in the surface. A seam in the water, a line where the current changed direction.

She wrote it down.

Nico stood beside her. She felt his attention shift. He was no longer looking at the water.

He was looking at her hands.

Chapter 16: The Permission

Eleni did not sleep. She lay in the narrow bed and thought about the water. The silence beneath the surface. The way her heartbeat had sounded in her own ears.

In the morning, she walked to the beach. Nico was already there, sitting on the sand, facing the sea. He did not turn when she approached.

"You came."

"I said I would."

"You said Tuesday. It is Wednesday."

She sat beside him. The sand was cold through her jeans.

"I was thinking."

"About the water."

"Yes."

He waited.

"I agreed to snorkel. Not to dive."

"Snorkeling is diving. Just shallower."

"I cannot hold my breath."

"You can. You do not know yet."

She looked at the water. Green near the shore, deepening to blue farther out. Wind ripples across the surface.

"Today. Shallow only."

He stood and handed her a mask.

She walked into the water. The cold was familiar now. She put the mask on. It leaked. She adjusted it. It leaked again.

Nico did not reach for her. He explained again, slowly, how to seat the rubber against her face.

She did it right this time.

She floated face-down. She breathed. She looked at the sand.

She surfaced after ten minutes, calm.

She did not cry this time.

Chapter 17: The Float

The fourth time she went out, she stayed longer.

The sun was thin through the clouds, a pale disk that barely warmed the air. The water was clear, visibility good. She floated in the shallows, her arms spread, her body suspended.

She heard her own heartbeat. Slow. Steady.

She thought about nothing. For the first time in two years, she thought about nothing. The relentless loop of memory and guilt and what-if quieted. She was aware only of the water temperature, the pressure of the mask against her face, the small sounds of her own breathing.

She surfaced and pulled the mask up.

Nico was twenty meters away, floating on his back. He was looking at the sky, his arms out, completely still. He looked like he belonged there, like he was part of the water.

She watched him for a minute. He did not look at her.

She put her face back in.

The sand rippled below. A school of tiny fish passed, their bodies catching the light. She followed them with her eyes until they disappeared into deeper water.

When she came out, her fingers were pruned. She was shaking. Not from cold.

Nico was on the beach, drying his hair with his shirt.

"Good?" he said.

She nodded. She could not speak.

He did not ask questions. He walked her to the taverna door and left before she could find the words to thank him.

She went to her room. She sat on the edge of the bed and let the tears come.

Chapter 18: The Walk

Nico found her on the path. She was sitting on a stone wall, her hands in her lap. The light was going. The sea had turned dark. The wind was picking up again.

"You missed dinner," he said.

"I was not hungry."

He sat beside her. Not close. Far enough to be polite. Close enough that she could feel his warmth.

"Maria will be angry."

"Maria is always angry about something."

He almost smiled. She was beginning to recognize that almost-smile, the way it moved only the left corner of his mouth.

"The floating," he said. "It is hard the first time."

"It was not the first time."

"The first time you let it work."

She looked at him. His face was hard to read in the twilight.

"How did you know?"

"Because I watched you. When you came out, you looked like someone who had just remembered something they had lost."

She turned away.

His name was Alex."

Nico said nothing.

"We were on the phone. He drove into the water. I heard it."

He was quiet. The wind moved between them.

He reached over and put his hand on the stone wall, close to hers but not touching.

She looked at his hand. It was brown and scarred, a working hand.

She did not move away.

Chapter 19: The Call

Eleni's phone rang just after noon. She stared at the screen. The photo of Dora, her sister, in a café in Athens. Two years old. She had not updated it.

She answered.

"You sound terrible," Dora said.

"Thank you."

"When are you coming back?"

"I just got here."

"It has been two weeks. You are hiding on an island with no one."

"There is a woman named Maria. She owns the taverna. And a diver named Nico."

"A diver."

"Free-diver. He teaches."

A pause. Eleni could hear Dora processing, searching for the right question.

"Is he handsome?"

"Dora."

"Fine. Are you okay?"

Eleni looked out the window. The sea was gray. The sky was gray. The horizon had blurred into a single wash of color.

"I am fine."

"Liar."

"I am better."

Dora was quiet. "That is something."

Eleni pressed the phone to her ear. She wanted to say more. The words would not come.

"I have to go. The station."

"The station. Of course."

"I will call next week."

"You will not. But call anyway."

Eleni hung up. She held the phone in her hand, its weight familiar. She put it in the drawer and closed it.

Chapter 20: The Bottle

Maria brought a bottle of raki to the table. Three glasses. She poured without asking.

"Tonight we drink," she said.

"Why?" Nico asked.

"Because it is Thursday and the fish was good."

She raised her glass. Eleni and Nico raised theirs.

The raki was strong. It burned going down. Eleni coughed. Maria refilled.

"My first husband," Maria said, "drowned."

The word sat on the table.

"He was a fisherman. He went out in weather he should not have. They found the boat. They did not find him."

Eleni looked at her glass. The liquid was clear, faceted by the light.

"I was twenty-three. I did not leave this island for three years. Not because I was trapped. Because I could not imagine anywhere else being different."

She drank.

"Eventually I married again. A good man. He sold bread. He died in his bed. Boring." She laughed. "I prefer the boring death."

Nico laughed. A short, surprised sound.

Maria refilled the glasses.

"You two. You are like a before and after photograph. Same picture. Different times."

She stood and walked to the kitchen.

Eleni and Nico sat with the raki and the silence. The bottle was half-empty. The night stretched ahead.

Chapter 21: The Confession

Eleni found Nico on the dock. He was sitting at the edge, his feet hanging over the water. She sat beside him.

"I have something to tell you."

He waited.

"Alex, my husband. He died in a flood. He drove into a washed-out road. The rain had been falling for six hours. The road collapsed under his car."

She paused.

"I was on the phone with him. I heard the water."

Nico did not move.

"I study storms. I knew the road was dangerous. I did not tell him."

"You could not have known."

"It is my job to know."

He was quiet. The water lapped at the dock supports.

"I carry my phone in a waterproof case. When I dive. In case I find something I want to photograph."

He looked at the water.

"Yorgos and I were exploring a cave. He went first. He stirred up the silt. I could not see. When it cleared, he was gone."

He touched the water with his foot.

"I searched for three days. I never found him. I still look. Every time I dive, I look."

Eleni put her hand on the dock between them.

Nico looked at it. He did not take it.

But he did not pull away.

Chapter 22: The Symmetry

"Yorgos was not my partner," Nico said. "Not like that. He was my dive partner. We had been diving together for twelve years."

They were still on the dock. The sun was setting, the horizon turning orange and then pink and then gray.

"He had a wife. Two children. He was a better diver than me. Calmer. More patient."

"But you survived."

"That is not the same as living."

Eleni nodded. She knew the difference.

"Alex was a geologist. He studied river systems. He knew the flood plains better than anyone. He drove into that road because he thought he could read the water."

"Pride."

"Trust. He trusted his knowledge."

"And your knowledge told you the road was dangerous."

"Yes."

"But he did not ask."

"He did not ask."

Nico looked at her directly. "That is not your fault."

"I know."

"Do you?"

She did not answer.

The last light left the sky. The dock creaked beneath them, wood expanding in the cooling air.

"I am tired," Eleni said.

"Then sleep."

"I cannot."

"Neither can I."

They sat in the dark, side by side. Neither moved to leave.

Chapter 23: The Sea

They dove together for the first time on Saturday morning.

Nico rowed them out in a small boat, the oars dipping and rising. The water was flat. The sky was pale. A perfect winter morning.

"Today you go down."

"I have been down."

"Three meters. Today, ten."

She looked at the water. Dark blue. Deep. The surface reflected nothing.

"I will be with you. Breathe. Equalize. Do not rush."

She put on the fins, the mask, the weight belt. The lead was heavy at her waist.

"Trust the equipment. Trust your body."

She sat on the edge of the boat.

"One more thing. When you come up, you will feel like you are breaking the surface of another world. That feeling is real."

She fell backward into the water.

The cold took her. She let it. She equalized. She descended. Nico was beside her, a dark presence in her peripheral vision.

At ten meters, the light turned green. The reef spread below her like a submerged city, rock formations and coral and the slow movement of fish. She hung in the water. She breathed. She was breathing underwater.

Nico pointed. A grouper, big and old, watched them from the shadow of a rock. Unmoving.

She watched it back.

When she surfaced, she was laughing. The sound startled her.

Nico was laughing too.

In the boat, she could not stop shivering. She did not care.

Chapter 24: The Shell

Eleni found the sand dollar on the empty beach.

It was whole. Perfect. The pattern on its surface was a five-pointed star etched into the pale calcium. She picked it up carefully. It was light and fragile, the edges thin enough to see through.

She walked to where Nico was coiling a rope, his movements slow and methodical.

"This is for you."

He took it. He turned it over in his hands, examining it from every angle.

"Sand dollars are not dollars."

"I know."

"They are skeletons. The remains of something that lived."

"I know that too."

He held it carefully, like it might break at any moment.

"Why this?"

"Because I found it. And I wanted to give you something."

He looked at her for a long moment. His eyes moved across her face, reading something she could not name.

He put the sand dollar in his jacket pocket.

"I will keep it."

"It will break."

"Then I will keep the pieces."

He went back to coiling the rope.

Eleni walked back toward the station. She did not turn around.

But she wanted to.

Chapter 25: The Kitchen

Maria taught Eleni to make moussaka.

"Eggplant first. Salt it. Let it weep."

"Why?"

"Because the eggplant holds bitterness. The salt draws it out."

Eleni sliced the eggplant into even rounds. Maria watched her hands.

"My mother made it with potatoes."

"Your mother was wrong."

"She was Greek."

"So am I. I am still right."

Eleni layered the ingredients in the dish. Eggplant. Meat. Béchamel. Each layer smooth and even.

"You cook like a scientist. Precise. No feeling."

"It gets the job done."

"Food is not a job. It is an argument."

Eleni put the dish in the oven. She wiped her hands on the towel.

"You should talk to him," Maria said.

"About what?"

"About staying. About leaving. About the thing you are both pretending is not happening."

Eleni folded the towel. "Nothing is happening."

Maria laughed. "Nothing is the loudest thing on this island."

She opened the oven and checked the moussaka. It was not ready.

Neither was Eleni.

Chapter 26: The Fever

Eleni woke shivering. Her head ached. Her throat was raw. The room spun when she tried to sit up. She lay back down and stared at the ceiling crack.

Maria found her hours later. She put a hand on Eleni's forehead and made a sound of disapproval.

"Fever. You are stupid for going in the water in winter."

"I am a meteorologist. I understand thermoregulation."

"Then you understand you are an idiot."

Maria brought soup. She brought blankets. She stood over Eleni until she ate every spoonful.

Nico came in the evening. He stood in the doorway, a jar of honey in his hands.

"My uncle's hives. For the tea."

"He does not need honey. She needs antibiotics."

"Antibiotics do not cure colds."

Maria and Nico argued over Eleni's bed, their voices competing. Eleni watched them from the pillow, two people who cared in different languages.

"I am fine," she said.

Neither listened.

Nico put the honey on the table. He looked at her, his face softening.

"Stay in bed."

"The station—"

"Will survive one day without you."

She wanted to argue. She did not have the strength.

He left. The honey stayed. She fell asleep to the sound of Maria washing dishes downstairs.

Chapter 27: The Threshold

Eleni woke in the dark. Someone was in the room.

Nico. Sitting in the chair by the window, his body outlined by moonlight.

"Your fever broke," he said.

"How long have you been there?"

"An hour. Maybe two."

"You should go home."

"I know."

He did not move.

She watched him in the dim light. His face was half-lit, half-shadow. He was not looking at her. He was looking at the window.

"Why are you here?"

"Because I could not sleep. And because Maria said you were alone."

"I am always alone."

"That is the problem."

She did not tell him to leave. She turned onto her side, facing the wall. She listened to him breathe, a steady rhythm in the dark.

After a long time, she heard him stand. She heard him walk to the door. His hand on the frame.

"Eleni."

"Yes?"

"Tomorrow. The water will be calm."

Then he left.

She stared at the wall. The crack in the ceiling ran from corner to corner.

She did not sleep.

Chapter 28: The Recovery

Eleni returned to the station. Nico joined her without asking, appearing in the doorway as she was wiping dust from the instruments.

The equipment had gathered a fine layer in her absence. She cleaned it. He handed her tools.

"The barometer needs adjustment."

He watched her work.

"You talk to the equipment."

"It listens better than people."

"Is that your professional opinion?"

"It is a data-driven conclusion."

He handed her a wrench. She used it to tighten a fitting.

They worked until midday. The station hummed with repaired instruments. The computer displayed live readings. The anemometer spun freely.

"Thank you," she said.

"For the wrench?"

"For not asking questions."

He leaned against the wall. "I have questions. I am just not asking them."

"You can ask one."

He considered the offer. "What are you afraid of?"

She looked at the screen. The pressure was steady.

"That the answer is nowhere. That I left my life and there is nothing to fill the space."

He nodded.

"Now you."

He was quiet. "That I will stop looking for Yorgos. That I will stop and there will be nothing left of him."

They stood in the quiet station. The only sound was the wind sensor spinning.

Chapter 29: The Village

The supply boat arrived on Thursday. It brought three passengers.

Eleni watched them come down the ramp. Young. German, perhaps. They wore expensive outdoor gear and looked slightly lost, consulting a phone that had no signal.

Maria met them at the top of the ramp.

"You know it is winter?"

"We wanted quiet," one of them said.

"You came to the right place."

Maria put them to work. The young man fixed a shutter that had been hanging loose. The two girls swept the church steps, raising clouds of dust.

The village felt different. Fuller. The sound of voices carried through the narrow streets.

Nico watched from the dock. He did not approach.

"Tourists," he said.

"In winter."

"Lost. Or running. Usually one or the other."

"Which one are you?"

He looked at her. "Both."

The tourists ate at the taverna that night. They were loud, their laughter carrying through the dining room. They ordered wine and retsina, asked Maria questions she answered with single words.

Eleni and Nico sat at the bar. They did not laugh. But they were not silent.

Something had shifted.

Chapter 30: The Revel

The tourists brought wine. Three bottles of cheap red. They opened them at the table and poured freely.

Maria lit a fire in the hearth. The flames threw shadows across the walls.

"Music," one of the tourists said. "There must be music."

Maria produced a small radio from behind the bar. She tuned it to a station playing Greek love songs from the 1980s, tinny and sentimental.

The tourists loved it. They danced between the tables, arms linked, singing along to words they did not know.

Maria pulled Eleni up from her stool.

"I do not dance."

"Everyone dances."

Maria pushed her into the middle of the room. The tourists clapped. Eleni stood there, stiff and self-conscious.

Then Nico was in front of her. He did not take her hand. He just stood there and moved to the music, a simple sway.

She followed.

They danced without touching. Two bodies responding to the same rhythm. Eleni felt awkward and then forgot to feel awkward.

The song ended. Another started.

Nico walked her outside. The night air was cold on her flushed face.

"You are not bad," he said.

"I am terrible."

"But you tried."

He stepped closer.

"Eleni."

She waited.

He did not say anything else. They stood in the dark. The music played inside.

Chapter 31: The Kiss

Nico walked Eleni to her door. The village was dark. The tourists had stumbled to their rooms. Maria was washing glasses in the kitchen.

At the door, Eleni stopped.

"Thank you."

"For what?"

"For the dancing. For the lessons. For the honey."

He almost smiled.

"You are welcome."

She kissed him.

It was not gentle. It was urgent, two years of silence compressed into the press of her mouth against his. He responded, his hand finding her waist, his other hand touching her face.

When she pulled back, they were both breathing hard.

"That was—" he started.

"Don't."

She opened the door.

"Come inside."

He followed.

The room was small. The bed was narrow. They did not talk.

In the morning, light through the wavy glass fell across the floor in a pattern of gold.

Nico was gone.

She lay in the bed and touched the empty space beside her.

Chapter 32: The Aftermath

Eleni did not leave her room for two days.

She told herself she was working. She opened her notebook. She stared at blank pages. The data would not come.

Maria left food outside her door. Eleni ate some of it.

She could still feel his hands on her face. The weight of him beside her in the dark. The way he had said her name.

She could not write. She could not think. She could only replay the kiss, the door, the room, the way he had left before the sun was fully up.

On the second afternoon, she heard him in the taverna. His voice, low, answering Maria's questions. She could not make out the words.

She stayed upstairs.

She told herself she was a scientist. Scientists did not behave this way. Scientists did not fall apart over a single kiss.

But she was not acting like a scientist. She was acting like a woman who had forgotten she was capable of feeling.

She opened her notebook. She wrote: Barometric pressure: 1021. Wind: Variable. Conditions: Clear.

She closed it.

She heard Nico's footsteps retreat.

She stayed in the room.

Chapter 33: The Convergence

The winter storm arrived on Saturday.

Eleni had forecast it. She had tracked its approach for days, watched it build over the Adriatic. She knew when it would hit and how hard.

She went to the station anyway.

The wind was already strong when she left the taverna. Maria shouted at her to stay inside. Eleni ignored her, leaning into the gusts as she climbed the path.

The station was cold, colder inside than out. She checked the instruments. She recorded the data, her handwriting steady.

The storm was stronger than she had predicted. The wind gusted to sixty knots. The building shook. The windows rattled in their frames.

She should have stayed at the taverna.

The door blew open. Nico stood there, soaked, furious.

"Maria sent you."

"No. I came because you are being stupid."

"The storm—"

"Is going to kill you if you stay here."

He grabbed her wrist. "We are leaving."

"The equipment—"

"Can be replaced. You cannot."

He pulled her out the door. The wind hit them. Rain came sideways, sharp and cold. She could barely see.

Nico held her hand. He did not let go.

They made it to the taverna. Maria was waiting with towels and hot wine.

"You are both idiots," Maria said.

Eleni dripped on the floor.

Nico was still holding her hand.

Chapter 34: The Shelter

The power went out at midnight.

The taverna went dark. Maria found candles, three of them, and placed them on the bar. The flames threw long shadows.

The wind howled. The rain hammered the windows. The old building groaned.

Nico and Eleni sat at the bar. Maria had gone to bed.

"The roof will hold," Nico said.

"I know."

"The station will be fine. I tied it down."

"You did?"

"After I brought you back. I went up again."

She looked at him. His hair was still damp. His hands were raw from the rope.

"Why?"

"Because it is yours. And I did not want you to lose it."

They sat in the candlelight. The flames flickered.

Eleni reached across the bar and took his hand.

"I am sorry."

"For what?"

"For disappearing. For the kiss. For not knowing what to do."

He held her hand. "None of us knows."

He lifted her hand and pressed it to his lips.

"You came," she said. "In the storm."

"I will always come."

The candles flickered. The wind rattled the windows.

They did not let go.

Chapter 35: The Facts

In the morning, the storm had moved east. The sky was clear. The island glittered with wet light.

Eleni stood in the taverna doorway. Nico came up behind her.

"The statistics," she said. "Of two people meeting on an island. Of both being widowed. Of both being here in winter."

"High or low?"

"Infinitesimal."

"Then we are lucky."

"I do not believe in luck."

He stood beside her. "Neither do I. But here we are."

"I have to go back to Athens. The university needs me for spring term."

He did not react. "When?"

"January."

"A month."

"Yes."

He nodded. He looked at the horizon.

"A month is enough time to fall in love."

"Is that what we are doing?"

He turned to face her. "I do not know. Tell me what we are doing."

She could not answer. The words stuck in her throat.

He walked down to the beach. She watched him go, his figure growing smaller against the sand.

Chapter 36: The Morning

Eleni found Nico on the beach before dawn.

He was sitting on the sand, facing the water. His knees were drawn up, his arms resting on them. The sky was just beginning to lighten.

She sat beside him.

"I did not sleep," she said.

"Neither did I."

"I was thinking about what you said. About a month."

He waited.

"I am afraid of starting something I cannot finish."

"I am afraid of finishing something that has not started."

She looked at the horizon. A line of gold was appearing at the edge of the sea.

"What if we stop thinking about it?"

"Can you do that?"

"No."

He laughed. A real laugh, the first she had heard from him.

"Neither can I."

They sat together as the sun came up. The water turned gold, then blue. The light grew.

Nico stood and held out his hand.

"Come. I want to show you something."

She took his hand.

He led her to the water.

Chapter 37: The Swim

Nico took her to the reef.

It was past the headland, in water so clear it seemed like air. The rock formations rose from the seabed, ancient and encrusted with coral. Colors she did not have names for: deep purple, rust red, a blue that was almost green.

They swam side by side. He pointed. She followed. A moray eel in a crevice. A school of sardines turning as one. A sea star clinging to the rock.

He had never shown anyone this place.

She understood without being told.

They surfaced in a small cove. The cliffs rose around them, sheer and white. The water was still, protected from the wind.

"There is a cave behind the waterfall."

"I cannot go—"

"I will be with you."

She followed him. The cave mouth was narrow, barely wider than his shoulders. Water dripped from above. The sound of the sea echoed.

Inside, the light was blue. The walls were covered in tiny shells, fossils embedded in the rock. The air was cool and damp.

Nico treaded water. Eleni floated beside him.

"This is where I come. When I need to remember why I am alive."

She reached for him. He came to her.

They held each other in the blue light. The water held them.

Chapter 38: The Curse

Maria was waiting when they returned. She was sitting at the taverna with a glass of wine. It was not yet noon.

"Did you swim?"

"Yes."

"Did you go to the cave?"

Nico stopped. "Who told you about the cave?"

"Everyone knows about the cave. I am not asking about the cave. I am asking if you took her there."

He did not answer.

Maria sighed. She poured two glasses.

"The island legend. Anyone who falls in love here during the fifth season stays forever. The winter is the fifth season. The season between seasons."

Eleni laughed. "I do not believe in legends."

Maria did not laugh.

"The legend is not the problem. The problem is that you might want to stay. And that is the one thing you cannot decide."

She pushed the wine across the bar.

Eleni looked at the glass. She did not touch it.

Nico did not say anything.

He picked up his glass and drank.

Chapter 39: The Callback

The email arrived on Tuesday.

Eleni read it three times, the cursor blinking at the bottom of the screen. Her department head. Spring term. A new research grant requiring her presence.

She closed the laptop.

She walked to the beach. The wind was cold. The water was gray.

Nico was in the water, swimming. She watched his arms cut through the surface, steady and strong. He was a long way out.

She sat on the sand and waited.

When he came out, he knew immediately. He read it in her posture, in the way she did not stand to meet him.

"When?" he said.

"January. They need me for a grant. I have to go."

"Then go."

"It is not that simple."

"It is exactly that simple. You go. You come back."

She looked at the water. "What if I do not come back?"

He did not answer.

She stood and walked away.

He stayed on the beach, watching the horizon.

Chapter 40: The Fracture

Eleni packed her notebook. She went to the station and measured the wind one last time. She wrote a final report and closed the file.

She did not see Nico.

Three days. She ate in her room. She worked at the station until dark. She avoided the taverna, taking the back stairs to her room.

On the third night, she found a note under her door. A single word, written in a hand she recognized.

Why?

She read it twice. She folded it and put it in her pocket.

She did not answer.

In the morning, she walked to the headland. The station was quiet. The instruments recorded data she would never analyze.

She thought about Alex. The phone call. The sound of water.

She thought about Nico. The cave. The blue light.

She packed her things. She was leaving in four days.

Chapter 41: The Empty

Her last week began. She took her final measurements. She recalibrated the instruments one last time. She wrote a maintenance schedule for the next person. There would not be a next person. The schedule was for herself, for the fiction that someone would care.

She swept the station floor, the dust rising and settling. She organized the desk. She wiped the windows.

She worked until her hands ached.

She did not see Nico.

She asked Maria if he was all right.

"He is diving," Maria said. "He dives when he cannot speak."

Eleni nodded.

She went to her room. She looked at the crack in the ceiling. She had been here six weeks. She had not unpacked. She had fallen in love.

She had not said it.

She had one week left.

She sat on the edge of the bed and tried to find the words. They would not come.

Chapter 42: The Message

Maria found Eleni in the kitchen. She was sitting at the prep table, a cold cup of coffee in her hands.

"Tell me," Maria said.

"I do not know what to do."

Maria sat across from her. The table was covered in flour.

"My second husband was a good man. But he was not my best love. My best love was a fisherman. He left this island when I was twenty-two."

Eleni looked up.

"He left because he was afraid of staying. I stayed because I was afraid of leaving. Two fears. Neither one wrong."

"What happened to him?"

"He married a woman in Piraeus. He had children. He sent me a letter once. He said he was happy."

She reached across the table and took Eleni's hand.

"Here is the truth. You can leave and be sad. You can stay and be scared. You will feel the missing either way. The question is which missing you can live with."

Eleni did not answer.

But she stopped packing.

Chapter 43: The Cave

Nico did not come back from his dive.

Eleni did not know. She was in her room, staring at the ceiling.

Maria knocked at sunset. "Is Nico with you?"

"No."

"He went out this morning. He has not come back."

Eleni stood. The fear was cold and immediate.

"Which direction?"

"The headland. The cave."

Eleni ran. She took the path without feeling her feet. She reached the beach. The light was fading. The boat was there, tied to the rock. His fins were on the sand.

But he was not.

She stood at the water's edge. The sea was dark.

"Get the coast guard," she said.

Maria was already on the phone.

Eleni looked at the horizon. The sun was gone. The water was black.

She thought about Alex. The waiting. The phone. The not knowing.

She could not do this again.

Chapter 44: The Search

The coast guard arrived in forty minutes.

Eleni met them at the dock. She had a flashlight. She was holding his wet suit.

"He went to the cave. Past the headland. The current shifts eastward."

The officer looked at her. "How do you know?"

"I am a meteorologist. I study currents."

She pointed at the map he carried.

"The wind was from the northwest today. The surface current pushes east. If he surfaced in trouble, he would be carried toward the cliffs."

The officer looked at his crew.

"Take her."

They took her. The boat motored past the headland, the engine loud in the quiet dark. The searchlight cut across the water. Eleni stood at the bow, her hands gripping the rail.

She watched the water. She watched the rocks.

"Farther east."

The helmsman adjusted.

The light swept across a rocky outcrop. Something moved.

"There."

Nico. Half in the water. Unconscious. His hand on a rock.

They pulled him in.

Chapter 45: The Rescue

They pulled him from the water. He was cold. Too cold. His lips were blue. His eyes were closed.

Eleni knelt beside him.

"Nico."

No response.

She pressed her hand to his chest. He was breathing. Shallow. Slow.

"Get blankets. Get the heater."

They wrapped him in thermal blankets. They moved him to the cabin. Eleni held his hand.

"Stay. Stay."

His fingers moved. Barely.

At the dock, an ambulance waited. They took him to the clinic. Eleni rode with him. She did not let go of his hand.

In the clinic, the doctor worked. Eleni waited in the hall. She watched the door.

After an hour, the doctor came out.

"He will be fine. Mild hypothermia. Exhaustion. He needs rest."

Eleni went in.

Nico was awake. His eyes tracked her as she crossed the room.

"Eleni."

She sat in the chair beside his bed. "Do not do that again."

"I found the cave."

"The cave."

"Yorgos's cave. I found it. I let him go."

She took his hand. "Good."

"Then I could not surface. The current."

"You almost died."

"I know."

He squeezed her hand.

"You found me."

"I used science."

"You always use science."

She was crying. She rested her forehead on their joined hands.

Chapter 46: The Recovery

Nico stayed in the clinic for two nights.

Eleni did not leave. She sat in the chair by the bed. She read him weather reports.

"Wind from the northwest. Twelve knots. Sea state two."

"Beautiful."

"Barometric pressure steady at 1023 millibars."

"You have a voice for weather."

"I am a professional."

He smiled with his eyes closed.

On the second day, the doctor said he could go home. Eleni walked him to the taverna. Maria made soup and stood over him until he ate every spoonful.

"You are not to dive for a week," Maria said.

"A month," Eleni said.

"A week," Nico said.

"Two weeks."

"Two weeks," Nico agreed.

Maria looked at them. She shook her head and went back to the kitchen.

Nico reached for Eleni's hand.

"Thank you."

"For what?"

"For coming after me."

She looked at their hands. "I will always come."

He heard what she meant. The words settled between them.

She did not take her hand back.

Chapter 47: The Conversation

They sat on the dock. The water was calm. The air was cold but still.

"The cave," Eleni said. "What happened?"

Nico looked at the horizon. "I found it. The entrance was where I remembered. I went inside."

He paused.

"I had not been there since the accident. I was afraid. But I went in. I swam to the chamber where we stopped."

"And?"

"He was not there. No bones. No equipment. I searched the whole chamber. Nothing."

"Did that help?"

"I did not know. I thought finding nothing would be worse. But it was better. The cave had taken him. The sea had kept him. I could stop looking."

He looked at her.

"I stopped looking. I said goodbye in my head. Then I tried to surface too fast."

"Nico—"

"I know. It was stupid. I got confused. The light. The cold. I ran out of air."

She took his hand.

"Next time, tell me before you go."

"Next time, I will take you with me."

She leaned against his shoulder. He put his arm around her.

The dock creaked. The water lapped at the supports.

They sat like that as the sun went down.

Chapter 48: The Decision

Eleni called her department head. She sat on her bed. The phone was heavy in her hand.

"Dr. Vakrinos. Good to hear from you."

"I need to request a change to my posting."

"Change?"

"A remote research position. Nissos. Six months."

A pause. "The station is not equipped for long-term—"

"The station is operational. I have calibrated all instruments. I can transmit data from here. I have already written a proposal."

Another pause. "Let me see this proposal."

She had not written it. She would write it tonight.

She hung up. She walked to the station. She wrote the proposal in two hours, the words coming easily. Justified. Scientific. Necessary.

She sent it.

Then she walked to the beach.

Nico was there. He was sitting on the sand.

"I asked for six months," she said.

"Remote?"

"Yes."

"Will they agree?"

"I do not know."

He stood. "Then we wait."

"Yes."

He put his hand on her face. "Six months is a long time."

"It is a start."

She turned and walked back to the village.

Nico stayed on the beach.

He was smiling.

Chapter 49: The Spring

The first tourist ferry arrived on a Sunday in March.

Eleni watched from the headland. The boat was full, deck crowded with people in bright jackets, children running. The ferry horn echoed across the water.

The island was waking up.

She walked down to the taverna. Maria was preparing for the season, directing a young man who was painting the shutters blue.

"You are still here," Maria said.

"I am."

"The proposal?"

"Approved."

Maria nodded. She did not smile. But something in her face softened.

Nico came in. He was sunburned. He had been diving.

"The tourists are here."

"I saw."

"Your quiet season is over."

"My work does not care about tourists."

He sat beside her. "Neither do I."

He took her hand under the table.

Maria brought breakfast. Eggs. Bread. Honey. Coffee.

They ate together.

The ferry horn sounded again. The tourists spilled into the village.

The island filled with noise.

Eleni did not mind.

Chapter 50: The Season

Eleni went to her room. She opened her suitcase. She took out her clothes and put them in the wardrobe. She placed her books on the shelf. She put her notebook on the table.

She hung her coat on the hook behind the door.

The room was no longer a room. It was a home.

She walked to the window. The sea was blue. The sky was clear. The wind was light. She had measured it that morning. Five knots. Northeast.

Perfect conditions.

She went downstairs.

Nico was waiting at the door.

"The water is warm."

"Show me."

They walked to the beach together. The sand was full of tourists, the water full of noise. Children splashing. Couples floating.

Nico took her past the swimmers. Past the shallows. To where the water turned deep and quiet.

"Ready?"

She put her mask on. "I am ready."

They dove.

Under the surface, the world went quiet. The noise cut off like a door closing. The light turned blue.

Eleni looked at the reef below. The fish moving in currents, the coral, the light filtering through layers of water.

Nico was beside her. She reached for his hand under the water.

He held it.

They stayed down as long as they could.

When they broke the surface, the world was still there, still bright, still full of sound. But it was different. The colors seemed sharper. The air tasted clean.

She was different.

She was staying.
