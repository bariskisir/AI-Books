
ARC THREE: THE GREY HARVEST


CHAPTER 21: The King's Summons

The Royal Summons arrived on a morning of cold rain.

Elara stood in Marta's garden, soaked to the bone, watching the thistle's purple bloom droop under the weight of water. The soil had gone grey. Not black like the blighted plants, but grey—the color of ash, of old bones, of extinguished fires.

The messenger was a captain of the Royal Guard, armored in silver and blue. He dismounted and knelt before her, water streaming from his helmet.

"Master Botanist Songweaver, His Majesty King Aldric requests your presence at the capital. Immediately."

"The Collegium—"

"Fallen." The captain's voice was grim. "The Grey Silence consumed it three days ago. Dean Harrow is dead. The archives are destroyed."

Elara's knees buckled. She caught herself on the garden wall.

"The archives," she repeated. "Everything we knew about music-plants—"

"Gone." The captain stood. "You are the last expert, Master Botanist. The King needs you."

Elara looked at the thistle. It hummed, steady, unwavering.

"I can't leave," she said. "The thistle—"

"Can wait." The Thistle Keeper stepped out of the rain. "The king cannot."

The Keeper had never left the wood before. But now they stood at the edge of Marta's garden, staff in hand, cloak streaming.

"The Grey Silence is coming for the thistle," they said. "It knows where the last song is buried. It will consume this forest in a matter of days."

"Then we need to move the thistle."

The Keeper laughed, a bitter sound. "You cannot move a tree that has grown for nine hundred years."

"Then we move a cutting. A seed. Something." Elara turned to the captain. "Give me two hours. I will be ready."


CHAPTER 22: The Last Cutting

Elara approached the thistle with her specimen knife.

Her hand shook. She had never taken a sample from the thistle, never cut even a single leaf. The plant was sacred. Inviolate. It was Seraphine's grave marker, her final song, her legacy.

But the Grey Silence was coming. And if the thistle died, every piece of it would die.

"I'm sorry," she whispered, and she cut.

The blade sliced through a single stem, near the base. Black sap welled up, not like the blighted plants' burned-honey stench, but sweet. Clean. Like honey and rain and something older, something that remembered the beginning of the world.

The thistle's hum faltered for a single beat. Then it resumed, unchanged.

Elara wrapped the cutting in damp moss and cloth. It was small. Fragile. A finger-length of thorn and leaf.

"Is it enough?" she asked the Keeper.

"It must be."

Elara packed the cutting in a wooden box lined with soil, and strapped the box to her back.

She was ready.


CHAPTER 23: The Road to Ruin

The journey to the capital took five days.

Five days of rain and mud and silence. The roads were empty. The villages were abandoned. The music-plants had turned to ash, and the fields lay fallow and grey.

Elara rode beside the captain, the box containing the thistle cutting pressed against her spine. The Thistle Keeper walked behind them, a shadow in moss-green.

"Where did everyone go?" Elara asked.

"North," the captain said. "The Grey Silence moves south. They're fleeing to the mountains."

"And the king?"

"The king remains in the capital. He will not abandon his throne."

They passed a village where the music-plants had been the first to die. The melody-trees that had once lined the main street were blackened skeletons. The song-bushes that had filled the air with harmony were dry and brittle.

Without the music-plants, the soil had lost its fertility. Without their songs, the villagers had lost hope.

"We're running out of time," Elara said.

"We've run out," the Keeper corrected. "We're just still moving."


CHAPTER 24: The Capital's Last Song

The capital—Aria—was a city built on music.

Its walls were grown from singing stone, a rare mineral that vibrated in sympathy with the earth's deep hum. Its towers were tuned to specific pitches, creating harmonies as the wind passed through them. The streets were paved with resonance tiles that played soft chords beneath the feet of pedestrians.

Elara had loved this city. It was a living instrument.

Now it was dying.

The singing stones were silent. The towers made no sound. The resonance tiles were cracked and dull. The citizens moved through the streets like ghosts, their faces grey with fear.

The Royal Palace sat at the city's heart, a structure of white stone and silver spires. King Aldric met them in the throne room—a tall man, his beard streaked with grey, his eyes hollow with exhaustion.

"Master Botanist," he said. "I am told you found the Last Bard's legacy."

"I did, Your Majesty."

"Can it save us?"

Elara opened the box and showed him the cutting. "This is a fragment of the thistle that grows on Seraphine Vale's grave. It holds her final song. It is immune to the Grey Silence."

The king leaned forward. "Can you grow more?"

"I don't know yet."

"Then find out." The king's voice was hard. "You have the full resources of the kingdom. Laboratories. Assistants. Whatever you need. Find out how to grow these thistles, or we all die."


CHAPTER 25: The Royal Greenhouse

The king gave Elara a greenhouse in the palace gardens.

It was the finest in the kingdom, heated by geothermal springs, lit by crystal lenses that focused sunlight into a warm golden glow. The soil was imported from the eastern valleys, the water from the mountain springs.

Elara planted the cutting in a pot of her own making, filled with soil from Whisperwood, mixed with ash from Marta's garden.

For three days, nothing happened.

The cutting sat in the soil, its leaves unchanged, its spines sharp and defiant. It did not grow roots. It did not grow new leaves. It simply existed, patient as stone.

"Maybe it needs to be planted on a grave," the Thistle Keeper said. "Maybe it only grows where something died."

"Then we need a grave."

"Whose?"

Elara had no answer.

She spent her nights with Seraphine's journal, searching for clues. She spent her days beside the cutting, listening to its faint hum, trying to understand what it needed.

On the fourth day, she noticed something. The hum was changing. It was weakening.

"Something is wrong," she told the Keeper. "The cutting is dying."

"Then we've failed."

"No." Elara touched the cutting's leaves. "The thistle in Whisperwood is still strong. The cutting is too far from its source."

"Then we need to return it."

"Or we need to plant it." Elara looked up. "On a grave."


CHAPTER 26: The Body in the Garden

Elara found a grave in the palace garden.

It was small, unmarked, hidden beneath a rose bush that had long since died. The soil above it was black, blighted, but the stone beneath was old. Ancient.

She asked the gardener about it.

"Old King's daughter," the gardener said. "Died of the wasting fever a hundred years ago. They buried her here because she loved the roses."

"Does she have a name?"

"Princess Lyra."

Elara knelt beside the grave. She could feel the thistle cutting pulsing against her back, responding to something in the soil.

"She was a musician," the gardener added. "Played the harp. Beautiful voice. They say the roses bloomed whenever she sang."

Elara understood.

She planted the cutting at the foot of Princess Lyra's grave, pressing it into the soil with her bare hands.

The cutting's hum changed. Deepened. Strengthened.

Within an hour, it had grown new roots.


CHAPTER 27: The First Bloom

The cutting grew with unnatural speed.

By the next morning, it had doubled in height. By evening, it had produced new leaves, their spines sharp and silver-tipped. By the third day, it had flowered.

The bloom was pale, smaller than the thistle in Whisperwood, but it was a bloom. And it hummed.

Elara wept.

She had done it. She had grown a second thistle.

The Thistle Keeper stood beside her, silent, their face unreadable. "It's smaller," they said. "Weaker."

"It's alive."

"It is." The Keeper's voice was quiet. "And it is the last hope of the kingdom."

The news spread quickly. The thistle had been propagated. There was hope.

But even as the palace celebrated, Elara noticed something troubling. The new thistle's hum was slightly different from the original. A quarter-tone flat. Almost imperceptible, but she heard it.

The song was not exactly the same.

She did not tell the king.


CHAPTER 28: The Grey Tide

The Grey Silence reached the capital on the same day the new thistle bloomed.

It came not as a wave but as a deepening silence. The birds stopped singing first. Then the wind stopped rustling the leaves. Then the palace's singing stones fell silent, one by one.

Elara stood in the greenhouse, watching the new thistle tremble. Its hum flickered, wavered, but held.

"The original is stronger," the Keeper said. "This one may not survive."

"We need more," Elara said. "We need an entire field of them."

"We need more graves."

Elara looked at the Keeper. "The Grey Silence is killing people now. Not just plants."

"I know."

"Then we'll have graves." Her voice was cold. "We'll have more graves than we know what to do with."

The Keeper met her eyes. "Are you willing to plant thistles on the bodies of the dead?"

"If it saves the living."

"And if the thistles only grow on the graves of musicians?"

Elara had no answer. She looked at Princess Lyra's grave, where the new thistle drew its strength from a hundred-year-old corpse.

"Then we find the musicians," she said. "And we plant them there."


CHAPTER 29: The Seeding

Elara organized the planting of a dozen more cuttings.

The palace grounds became a graveyard. The king gave permission for any fallen musician to be buried in the gardens, their graves marked by thistle blooms. The citizens of Aria brought their dead to the palace gates, hoping the thistles would grow.

Some did. Most did not.

Elara discovered that only the graves of those who had known song—true song, the kind that came from the soul—could sustain the thistles. A passing hobby or casual talent was not enough. The thistles needed devotion. They needed sacrifice.

"Thirteen," she counted. "We have thirteen thistles."

"Against a Silence that stretches across the entire kingdom," the Keeper said.

"They're growing. They're spreading roots." Elara touched one of the new thistles. Its hum was still slightly off, still a quarter-tone flat. "If we can cover enough ground with them—"

"We can't. Not fast enough."

"Then we need to speed up the growth."

"How?"

Elara did not know.


CHAPTER 30: The Keeper's Sacrifice

The Thistle Keeper came to Elara in the middle of the night.

"I've made a decision," they said.

Elara looked up from her journals. "What decision?"

"I'm going to give my life to the thistles."

"No."

"I have two hundred years of song in my bones. Marta kept the song alive for nine centuries. I have less, but I have enough." The Keeper's voice was calm. "If I am buried beside the smallest thistle, it will grow strong. It may even match the original."

"I won't let you."

"Elara." The Keeper stepped closer, and for the first time, they reached out and took her hand. "I have guarded the thistle for two centuries. I have watched the song fade, watched the Silence spread, watched hope die. I am tired. This is the only gift I have left to give."

"They are not ready. I am not ready."

"You are." The Keeper's grip tightened. "You have the knowledge. You have the song. You have Seraphine's memory in your blood. You don't need me anymore."

Elara's eyes burned with tears. "I need your guidance."

"You have it." The Keeper touched her chest, just above her heart. "I will always be in the song. In every thistle that grows, in every note that rings. I will be the roots."

They left before dawn.

Elara found them at dawn, lying beside the weakest thistle, their body already growing cold. Their hands were clasped around the thistle's stem, and their face was peaceful.

She buried them where they lay.

By noon, the thistle had grown three feet.

END OF ARC THREE
