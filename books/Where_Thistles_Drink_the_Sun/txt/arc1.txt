WHERE THISTLES DRINK THE SUN

A Novel

---

ARC ONE: THE WITHERING SONG

---

CHAPTER 1: The Botanist's Lament

Elara Songweaver knelt in the damp soil of Whisperwood, her fingers tracing the black veins that spiderwebbed across the silverbell leaves. The plant had been healthy yesterday. Today it wept a thin black sap that smelled of burned honey.

She pressed her ear to the ground and heard nothing. The silverbells should have been humming their low C-sharp, the root-song that anchored the grove's harmony. Silence.

"Another one," she whispered.

She had mapped 147 species of music-plants in the Verdant Kingdom. Each produced a unique tone, a note in the symphony that kept the land fertile and the air sweet. The silverbells were the bass line. Without them, the higher registers would drift, untethered.

Elara scraped a sample of black sap into her glass vial. The blight had appeared three weeks ago at the kingdom's southern edge, devouring the reedflutes first. Now it moved north, and she moved with it, always a day behind.

She had named it The Quieting.

At twenty-seven, Elara was the youngest master botanist in the Collegium of Natural Harmonies. Her peers called her obsessive. They were correct. Music-plants were not her profession. They were her purpose.

She stood and brushed soil from her leather apron. The Whisperwood stretched around her, a cathedral of green and brown, its canopy filtering sunlight into golden shafts. Birds moved between branches, but their calls seemed muted, as if the forest itself held its breath.

Elara walked deeper into the wood, following the old game trail she knew would lead to the ridge. From there she could survey the damage. The blight followed streams, she had observed. Water carried the black corruption downstream, infecting roots, silencing one plant at a time.

Near the ridge's crest, she found something new.

A cluster of moonpetals—delicate white flowers that sang a soft F at dusk—stood untouched. Perfect. Healthy. Their leaves were green, their petals unblemished. And at their center, wrapped around their stems like a strangler, grew a thistle.

Elara frowned. Thistles did not grow in Whisperwood. The soil was too rich, the canopy too dense. Thistles were plants of the roadside, the wasteland, the places where nothing else would grow.

But this thistle was healthy. Vibrant. Its spines caught the sunlight like needles of polished silver.

And it hummed.

She bent closer. The sound was faint, almost below hearing, but it was there—a single note, pure and steady, like a distant bell across still water.

Elara reached for her notebook.


CHAPTER 2: The Note That Should Not Be

The thistle's hum measured at G-sharp below middle C. Elara recorded this in her field journal, then twice more to be certain. Her tuning fork confirmed the pitch.

This was impossible. Thistles had no vocal apparatus. Their cell structure lacked the resonant chambers that allowed music-plants to produce sound. They were botanically mute.

Elara circled the moonpetal cluster, examining the thistle from every angle. Its stalk was thick, woody, almost gnarled—unusual for a thistle of its apparent age. The leaves were broader than the common spear-thistle, and their spines were tipped with a reddish-brown that looked almost like dried blood.

She did not recognize the species.

Her fingers hesitated above her specimen knife. The Collegium would want samples. Living tissue, pressed specimens, seeds if she could find them. Protocol demanded collection.

Instead, she sat.

Something about the thistle commanded stillness. Its hum was not merely a sound but a presence, a vibration that resonated in her chest, in the hollow behind her breastbone. She had studied music-plants for a decade. She knew their songs. This was different. This was not a plant singing.

This was a plant remembering.

The thought unsettled her. She pushed it away.

"Get a grip, Elara," she muttered. "It's a weed with unusual properties."

But she did not take the sample.

She sketched instead. Detailed drawings of leaf venation, spine arrangement, flowering head. The bloom was a deep purple, nearly black, surrounded by spiny bracts that curled inward like cupped hands. She noted the soil composition, the angle of sunlight, the proximity to water.

Then she noticed the ground beneath the thistle.

A slight depression. Rectangular. About six feet long and three feet wide, overgrown with grass that had grown thicker and darker than the surrounding turf. A grave.

Elara's pencil stopped moving.

Someone was buried here. Someone whose body had fed this thistle. Someone whose bones had become its roots.

And the thistle sang.


CHAPTER 3: The Last Bard

The old woman in the cottage at the wood's edge knew about the grave.

Her name was Marta, and she had lived in the shadow of Whisperwood for eighty-two years. Her hands were twisted with age, her eyes milky with cataracts, but her voice carried the rhythm of someone who had once sung.

"They call it the Bard's Grave," Marta said, gesturing toward the wood with a gnarled finger. "Though I reckon most folk have forgotten why."

Elara sat at Marta's kitchen table, a cup of nettle tea cooling before her. "Who is buried there?"

"Don't you know?" Marta's blind eyes found Elara's face with unsettling accuracy. "The Last Bard. The one who sang the sun into the sky and the moon into the sea."

Elara had heard the stories. Every child in the kingdom had. The Last Bard who had woven the world into harmony, who had taught the first music-plants to sing. Fairy tales. Myths.

"The stories say the Last Bard died a thousand years ago," Elara said carefully.

"Close enough." Marta nodded. "Nine hundred and seventy-three years, if you count by the old calendar. I know. I was there."

Elara's tea sloshed over the rim of her cup. "You were there? That's—you would be—"

"Old?" Marta laughed, a dry rasping sound. "I am old, child. Older than I look. Older than I have any right to be." She tapped her chest, just above her heart. "The song keeps you alive, you see. The Last Bard's song. A thread of it, woven into my bones when I was a girl."

"What song?"

"The Song of Ending." Marta's voice dropped. "The one she sang as she died. The one that planted the thistle on her grave."

Elara leaned forward. "The thistle is growing on the Last Bard's grave?"

"She planted it herself. With her own hands. With her own blood." Marta's blind eyes glistened. "She told me, before she went into the wood for the last time: 'When I am gone, the thistle will sing. And when the world forgets the old songs, someone will find it. Someone will remember.'"

"She told you this."

"I was her apprentice."

Elara sat back. The air in the cottage seemed to thicken, pressing against her ears. "You were apprenticed to the Last Bard."

"I was." Marta smiled, and for a moment her face was young. "I was twelve years old. She was dying, you see. Already fading. The Blight had taken most of her song by then. But she had enough left for one last planting."

"The Blight." Elara's blood chilled. "What Blight?"

"The same one you're chasing now, girl." Marta's smile faded. "It's come back. Just like she said it would."


CHAPTER 4: The Song of Ending

Marta told Elara the story over three cups of tea, as the sun traced its arc across the sky and the shadows lengthened outside the cottage window.

The Last Bard's name was Seraphine Vale. She had been the greatest musician the Verdant Kingdom had ever known, a woman whose voice could shape the weather, whose harp could coax flowers from stone. She was the last of a line that stretched back to the world's beginning, when the First Bard had sung creation into being.

"I was her apprentice for only three months," Marta said. "Three months at the end of her life. But in those months, she taught me everything."

"And the Blight?"

"Seraphine called it the Grey Silence. It came when the songs grew weak, when the people stopped listening to the music of the world. It was the disease of silence, the rot of forgotten things." Marta's hands trembled as she lifted her cup. "It killed the music-plants one by one. The same as it's doing now."

"How did Seraphine stop it?"

"She didn't." Marta shook her head. "She slowed it. She held it back with her own song, poured everything she had into keeping the Grey Silence at bay. But it wasn't enough. It never is."

Elara felt cold, despite the warmth of the cottage. "Then how did the kingdom survive?"

"Because Seraphine planted the thistle." Marta set down her cup. "On her own grave. With her own blood. She knew the Grey Silence would return. She knew the music-plants would need a seed to grow from, a single note to rebuild the symphony."

"The thistle."

"The thistle." Marta nodded. "It's her last song, child. Her final note. As long as it grows, the music can be reborn."


CHAPTER 5: The Thistle Keeper

Elara returned to the grave that evening, when the sun hung low and the wood filled with amber light.

The thistle was unchanged. It stood at the head of the grave, its purple bloom dark against the fading sky, its hum steady and true. But now Elara heard something else in its song—a thread of melody, a pattern she had missed before.

Someone was watching her.

She turned slowly. A figure stood at the edge of the clearing, half-hidden in the shadow of an oak. Tall, wrapped in a cloak the color of dried moss, face obscured by a hood.

"You should not be here." The voice was low, genderless, resonant. "The grave is forbidden."

Elara's hand moved to her knife. "Who are you?"

"The Thistle Keeper." The figure stepped forward, and Elara saw the staff in their hand—a gnarled length of wood, carved with spiraling patterns that seemed to move in the fading light. "I guard the Last Bard's rest. I protect the seed."

"You've been watching me."

"Since you found the grave." The Thistle Keeper stopped a few paces away. "I thought you would take a sample. I was prepared to stop you."

"Why didn't you?"

"You didn't." The hood tilted slightly. "You sat. You listened. You understood, at least a little, what the thistle is."

Elara swallowed. "I'm a botanist. I study music-plants. The blight is killing everything in the forest. The thistle is immune."

"The thistle is not immune." The Thistle Keeper's voice hardened. "It is resistant. There is a difference. It can endure the Grey Silence because it was planted in a moment of perfect song. But it is not invulnerable."

"How do you know about the Grey Silence?"

"I know many things." The Thistle Keeper raised their staff and pointed it at Elara. "Leave this place. Take your knowledge and your notes and do not return. The thistle is not ready."

"Not ready for what?"

The Thistle Keeper did not answer. They turned and walked into the trees, and within three steps, they were gone.

Elara stood alone in the clearing, heart pounding, the thistle's hum vibrating through her bones.

She had spent her life studying the quiet magic of music-plants. She had never encountered anything like this. She had never encountered magic at all.

Now she stood at the grave of the Last Bard, and she understood nothing.


CHAPTER 6: The Collegium

Elara returned to the Collegium of Natural Harmonies the next morning. The journey took four hours by coach, through rolling hills and past villages where the music-plants were already showing signs of the blight. The reedgrass along the road had gone black at the roots. The windflowers in the meadows had stopped swaying in rhythm.

The Collegium was a sprawling complex of stone buildings and glass greenhouses, built into the slope of Mount Verity. Elara had studied here for eight years, taught here for four. It was, for all practical purposes, her home.

She went straight to the library.

The Archivist was a man named Corbin Vale, a distant descendant of Seraphine's line, though the name had faded into obscurity. He was tall and thin, with spectacles perched on a nose that had been broken twice.

"The Last Bard," Elara said, sliding into the chair across from his desk. "I need everything you have."

Corbin raised an eyebrow. "That's a broad request."

"Seraphine Vale. Specifically."

Corbin's eyebrow climbed higher. "You know the name. Most people don't."

"I know more than the name." Elara leaned forward. "I know about the Grey Silence. I know about the thistle. I know she had an apprentice named Marta."

Corbin was silent for a long moment. Then he stood, walked to the locked cabinet behind his desk, and produced a key from a chain around his neck.

"You've been to the grave," he said, not a question.

"Yes."

"Then you've met the Thistle Keeper."

"I have."

Corbin opened the cabinet and withdrew a leather-bound volume, its pages yellowed with age. "This is Seraphine's journal. The last one she wrote before she died. I was told to give it to the one who found the thistle."

He placed the book in Elara's hands.


CHAPTER 7: Seraphine's Journal

The journal was fragile. Elara handled it with the care she would give a wounded bird.

The entries were written in a flowing hand, the ink faded to brown, the pages spotted with age. The first entry was dated three years before Seraphine's death.

"In the beginning, there was sound," she read. "The First Bard sang the world into being, note by note, chord by chord. The mountains rose at the sound of his bass. The rivers carved their courses at the trill of his flute. The music-plants grew from the melody he left behind, each one a fragment of the original song."

Elara turned the page.

"The Grey Silence is not a disease. It is an absence. A forgetting. When people stop listening to the music of the world, the music begins to fade. The Grey Silence is the silence between notes, given form and hunger."

She read faster.

"I have held the Silence back for thirty years. My voice grows weaker. My song fades. Soon, the Silence will have me.

"But I have prepared a seed.

"The thistle grows in wastelands. It thrives where nothing else will. It is the plant of last resorts, of desperate places. And it remembers. Its roots hold memory. Its spines hold pain. Its bloom holds hope.

"I will plant a thistle on my grave. I will pour my last song into its roots. And when the Silence returns—as it will, as it must—the thistle will sing the world back into being."

Elara closed the journal.

She understood now. The thistle was not merely a plant. It was a seed, yes, but not a seed of new growth. It was a seed of memory. A seed of song.

And the Grey Silence was coming to consume it.


CHAPTER 8: The Blight's Whisper

The blight reached the Collegium three days later.

Elara was in the greenhouse when she heard the change. The resonance pipes—tall brass cylinders that amplified the music-plants' songs—had fallen silent. The greenhouse, always vibrant with layered harmonies, was quiet as a tomb.

She ran to the main chamber and found the staff gathered around the central fountain. The fountain's water was black, and the music-lilies that grew in its basin had turned to ash.

"Master Botanist," said the Collegium's Dean, a severe woman named Harrow. "Your blight has arrived."

"It's not my blight," Elara said, but the words felt hollow.

"The music-plants in the eastern wing are dead. The resonance pipes in the tower have gone silent." Harrow's face was stone. "The Collegium is the heart of the kingdom's harmony. If the heart stops—"

"We need to find a cure."

"There is no cure." Harrow's voice cracked, just slightly. "I've read the old records. The Grey Silence has no remedy. It must be endured."

Elara thought of the thistle. The thistle that sang. The thistle that remembered.

"There's a plant in Whisperwood," she said. "Immune to the blight. If I can study it, understand its resistance—"

Harrow's eyes narrowed. "The thistle."

Elara blinked. "You know about it."

"Every Dean of the Collegium has known. It's the last card, Master Botanist. The final hope." Harrow's voice dropped to a whisper. "But it is not a plant to be studied. It is a song to be sung. And none of us know the melody."


CHAPTER 9: The Grey Silence

That night, Elara could not sleep.

She sat in her quarters, Seraphine's journal open on her lap, the words blurring before her eyes. The Grey Silence was not a disease. It was an absence. A forgetting.

The people of the Verdant Kingdom had forgotten the old songs. They had stopped listening to the music of the world. They had filled their ears with chatter and commerce and the clatter of daily life, and somewhere in that noise, they had lost the melody.

And the melody was dying.

Elara looked out her window at the city below. The streetlamps glowed amber, and the people moved through the streets, and nobody noticed that the night was unusually quiet. The evening-blooming jasmine had not sung its twilight lullaby. The cricket-vines had not begun their nightly chorus.

They had forgotten to listen.

She thought of the Thistle Keeper, standing guard at the grave. She thought of Marta, alone in her cottage, the last apprentice of the Last Bard. She thought of Seraphine, pouring her dying song into a thistle's roots.

And she understood what she had to do.

She had to learn the song. The song that Seraphine had planted. The song that the thistle remembered. She had to learn it well enough to sing it herself.

She closed the journal and packed her bag.


CHAPTER 10: The Road to the Grave

Elara left the Collegium before dawn.

She took only what she could carry: her field journal, Seraphine's journal, a change of clothes, a water skin, and her tuning fork. The rest could wait.

The road to Whisperwood was empty at this hour. The blight was spreading faster now—she could see it in the hedgerows, where the melody-brambles had gone dark and still. The hawthorn whistlers, usually a bright G, were silent.

The world was losing its voice.

She walked faster.

By mid-morning, she reached Marta's cottage. The old woman was sitting on her porch, as if she had been expecting her.

"You're going back," Marta said.

"Yes."

"You've read the journal."

"Yes."

Marta nodded slowly. "Then you know what you have to do."

"I think so." Elara sat on the step beside her. "I have to learn the song. The one the thistle remembers."

"No." Marta turned her blind eyes toward Elara. "You have to become the song."

Elara waited.

"Seraphine didn't just plant her song in the thistle," Marta said. "She planted herself. Her voice. Her memory. The thistle is not a vessel that contains the song. The thistle is the song. Every spine, every leaf, every root. To learn the song, you must become part of the thistle. Part of Seraphine."

"How?"

Marta smiled. "I'll show you."

And so Elara Songweaver began her true apprenticeship.

END OF ARC ONE
