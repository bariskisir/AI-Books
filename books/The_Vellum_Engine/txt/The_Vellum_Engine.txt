Chapter 1: The Drawing

Elian Mott received the vellum in a flat box tied with cotton tape at nine in the morning, when the patent archive still smelled of floor wax and rain-soaked wool. The box looked too new for the thing it claimed to contain. Its corners were sharp. Its label had been typed on a machine that left no bite in the paper.

Mira Saye stood on the other side of his worktable, coat still buttoned, a thin line of water darkening the hem. She was the estate solicitor, though she never used that phrase as armor. Her files were clean, her questions direct, and her patience with decorative old-world nonsense was famously short.

"They need an answer by Friday," she said.

Elian loosened the tape with a bone folder. "Old machines dislike Friday answers."

"So do courts."

"Courts can issue orders. Machines only issue consequences."

She gave him the kind of look that asked whether he planned to be useful before lunch. He lifted the lid. Inside lay a sheet of pale vellum under tissue, its surface slightly cockled, its ink a careful brown. The title block named the Marrow Engine, a brass calculating machine sealed for thirty years behind glass in the old Harrick Works demonstration hall. The engine was famous in the small way technical marvels become famous: known deeply by a few people, misunderstood by many more, and valuable whenever someone needed a story to attach to property.

The patent seal at the foot of the sheet was clean. Too clean, perhaps, but cleanliness alone proved nothing. Elian pulled on cotton gloves and weighted the corners with silk bags. The vellum relaxed under the light. A faint animal smell rose from it, dry and old, mixed with something sharper.

Mira watched him bend closer. "Tell me what you see."

"Ink, ambition, and a machine drawn by someone with excellent hands."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the first answer."

He followed the frame, the title, the arrangement of shafts and wheels. Whoever had drawn the engine knew how to make authority visible. The lines were steady. The annotations were neat. The signature had the right flourish for an inventor who had died before photography became kind to old men.

Then he reached the central gear train.

One brown line made his breath stop. A forty-tooth gear transferred force into a pinion that could not be where it was shown unless brass had learned mercy. There should have been a shaft through the second bearing. There was none. Instead the drawing offered air, inked with the confidence of metal.

Mira noticed the change in him. "Problem?"

"Not yet." He took a brass loupe from the drawer and leaned until the lens almost touched the surface. "A problem is honest. This may be something else."

Outside the reading room, a cart rolled over a threshold with a soft clatter. Someone laughed in the corridor and then lowered their voice, remembering where they were. The archive carried those sounds carefully, as if noise itself required cataloging.

Elian straightened. "Where did this come from?"

"A locked desk in the estate house, according to the heirs. It was found during valuation."

"Found by whom?"

"An appraiser retained by Jarin Pike."

The name landed with a small thud. Pike handled estate assets for families who wished to turn heirlooms into liquidity without admitting they needed money. He had a gift for making sales look like rescue.

Elian looked back at the vellum. The false shaft, if false it was, sat at the center of the claim. If the drawing was authentic, title to the Marrow Engine might pass from the workers' trust to the inventor's descendants. If it was not, someone had placed a lie on old skin and brought it into a room built to honor evidence.

"Friday," Mira said again, softer now.

"Friday is possible for a first opinion."

"And for certainty?"

He covered the vellum with tissue. "Certainty is expensive. Someone seems already to have paid for the appearance of it."

Chapter 2: Wrong Teeth

By noon the rain had thinned to a silver haze, and the reading room lamps had become the main weather. Elian made three photographs of the central train, one with raking light, one with color scale, one with a thin metric ruler laid above the title block. He did not touch the ink. He did not trust first impressions. Fraud, like innocence, deserved method.

He counted the teeth on the principal wheel with a pencil point held above the image. Forty. He counted again from another spoke, because a tired eye could make a gear generous. Forty again. The pinion beside it held twenty-one teeth. Elegant, slightly irregular, and useless in the arrangement shown.

Mira returned with two paper cups of tea from the archive cafe. "You have the expression."

"Which expression?"

"The one my father had when a witness said something tidy."

Elian accepted the tea and forgot to drink it. "The ratio is elegant and impossible."

"Explain that as if I bill in six-minute increments."

He turned the photograph toward her. "This wheel has forty teeth. This pinion has twenty-one. That alone is not fatal. But the pinion is drawn as if carried on a shaft that is not drawn, not numbered, and not supported by any bearing. If it existed, it would pass through this plate and collide with the reset arm. If it does not exist, the gear floats."

"Could the drawing omit a hidden part?"

"Patent drawings omit covers, screws, decorative nonsense. They do not omit the thing that lets the machine turn."

Mira sat, her coat now over the chair, sleeves rolled twice. She had solicitor's hands, careful and ink-stained at the side of one finger. "Could it be a draft?"

"Drafts make mistakes. This makes a sale."

The words stayed between them. Elian disliked drama in professional rooms, but he disliked politeness toward bad evidence more. He took a second sheet from his drawer and sketched the gear train as drawn, then the gear train that would have to exist for motion to pass. The first sketch ended in contradiction. The second required an extra bracket, a deeper plate, and a different cam profile.

Mira watched the lines form. "The estate position is that the engine was held for display but never transferred to the workers' trust."

"Then the drawing must show the original protected design."

"Yes."

"This does not show a working original."

"Could it show a later rebuild?"

"That would damage their claim."

She understood at once. If the vellum described the machine after later factory labor had changed it, then the sheet could not be the inventor's foundational patent. It would be, at best, a copy after the fact. At worst, a deliberate attempt to dress a later machine in earlier clothes.

Elian opened the box again and studied the whole sheet. The title was too assertive. The seal too conveniently placed. The drawing had the stage manners of a witness who knew the answer before the question.

"Who benefits?" he asked.

"The estate, directly. A private collector, if sale proceeds. Pike's firm, by commission."

"And who loses?"

"The workers' trust loses the engine, the hall, possibly the archive. Their maintenance endowment is tied to possession."

He wrote that down. Possession. Endowment. Sale. Friday. He liked nouns. They behaved better than motives.

Mira rose and walked to the window, where wet stone buildings stood in a row of gray planes. "I need to advise the trustees whether to seek emergency relief."

"Advise them to preserve everything."

"That is not the same as accusing anyone."

"No. Accusation comes after proof. Preservation comes before proof disappears."

He closed the vellum box, but his hand stayed on the lid. The old skin had already changed in his mind. An hour earlier it had been an object. Now it was an event. Someone had scraped, drawn, dated, carried, and timed it. Someone had chosen Friday, as if truth could be forced to keep business hours.

"I want to see the engine," he said.

"The hall is locked."

"Then unlock it."

Mira picked up her file. "That may require charm."

"Bring a locksmith instead."

For the first time that day she smiled. It was brief and useful. "I know one with a court stamp."

Chapter 3: Deadline

The archive cafe occupied a former sorting room with high windows and iron radiators that knocked like old pipes in a ship. By half past two it had filled with students, conservators, and office clerks sheltering from the weather. The tables were too small for legal files, so Mira spread the estate papers across two of them and dared anyone to object.

Elian sat opposite her with his notebook open. He had eaten half a sandwich without remembering the act. The bread tasted of chalk and mustard. The tea had gone cold again.

"The inventor was Lucien Harrick," Mira said, tapping the first page. "Died 1911. His company continued under family ownership until 1979. Workers formed the Harrick Works Trust after closure. The demonstration hall, engine, workshop records, and certain designs were transferred for preservation."

"Certain designs."

"The phrase is doing too much work."

"Legal phrases often develop large appetites."

She slid a copy of the sale agreement across the table. The private collector's name had been redacted, but money had a smell even through black ink. The figures were large enough to make everyone virtuous in public and nervous in private.

"The descendants claim the newly found vellum proves the Marrow Engine was never among the transferred designs," Mira said. "They argue the trust had custody, not title. If the drawing is authentic, title belongs to the estate. If title belongs to the estate, the sale can close Friday at five."

"And the trust?"

"Receives a courtesy viewing arrangement for five years."

Elian read the clause. "Courtesy is what people offer after theft when they still want invitations."

Mira's mouth tightened. "The trustees are divided. Some want to fight. Some want to negotiate a payout. Some are tired."

"Tired is useful to Pike."

"Yes."

Outside the cafe glass, archive staff moved between buildings under black umbrellas. Their shoes made dark commas on the pavement. Elian imagined the engine in the factory hall: brass under dust, a machine made by many hands, waiting while lawyers reduced it to an asset line.

"Who has inspected the vellum before us?" he asked.

"Pike's appraiser. A private paper consultant hired by the estate. No one for the trust."

"Convenient sequence."

"Everything about this has been convenient. The drawing appeared after the heirs accepted preliminary terms but before title review. The estate notified the trust with four business days to respond. Pike's letter says delay will expose the trust to damages."

"Threats are also provenance."

She looked up. "What?"

"They tell you where the document wants to go."

Mira wrote the phrase in the margin of her pad, though whether as evidence or warning he could not tell. "The trustees can seek a temporary injunction. I need enough to convince a judge there is a serious issue."

"The gear train is enough for suspicion."

"Suspicion is not enough for an order."

"Then we add witnesses."

"Who?"

"Someone who knows the engine as metal, not as property. Someone who knows patent notation. Someone who knows vellum better than ink knows how to lie."

Mira leaned back. "You already have names."

"I have habits."

He listed them. Otto Brann, former maintenance engineer, retired but not softened. Dr. Iona Bell, historian of mechanical computation, intolerant of sloppy chronology. Celia Voss, conservator, whose reports could make a judge understand collagen without metaphor.

Mira closed one file and opened another. "Can they move quickly?"

"Truth can move quickly. It just dislikes being shoved."

"Elian."

"I will call them."

She nodded, satisfied. For several minutes they worked without speech. She assembled legal chronology. He made a technical chronology beside it. Patent. Factory rebuild. Trust transfer. Closure. Appraiser visit. Discovery. Sale deadline. The two timelines lay side by side like rails.

At the counter, the cafe machine hissed steam. A spoon fell. Someone apologized too loudly. Ordinary sounds persisted even when fraud entered a room.

Mira gathered the papers. "If the drawing is authentic, the trust loses the engine."

"If it is false?"

"The sale collapses."

Elian stirred his cold tea. The spoon made a small hollow ring against the cup. "Then someone has made Friday expensive."

"And us inconvenient."

"That is a respectable calling."

She tucked the sale agreement into her folder. "I will draft the injunction papers. You make your calls."

He watched her leave through the cafe doors, walking fast, coat open, one hand already raising the phone. Elian remained a moment longer with the timelines. The vellum's false shaft had become a door. Behind it were people, payments, missing records, perhaps simple greed, perhaps something uglier dressed as legacy.

He wrote one more word at the bottom of the page: custody.

Then he underlined it twice.

Chapter 4: Otto

Otto Brann arrived the next morning before the archive opened, because retired factory men kept hours that made institutions look lazy. He came with a cane, a cap folded into one pocket, and machine oil in the wool of his coat despite the fact that he had not officially maintained a machine in years. His beard had gone white in uneven patches. His eyes had not retired at all.

"You dragged me across town for a photograph," he said to Elian, not as complaint but as accounting.

"A photograph of a drawing."

"Worse."

Mira signed him into the consultation room. "Thank you for coming, Mr. Brann."

"If this is about selling the engine, I have nothing polite."

"Good," Elian said. "Save time."

They placed enlarged images of the vellum on the table. Otto did not sit. He bent over them with both hands planted beside the sheets, cane hooked over one wrist. At first he said nothing. His lips moved slightly as he followed the train. Elian recognized the look. Otto was not reading a drawing; he was putting his body back in the machine, feeling where knuckles scraped, where a lamp had to be held, where a bolt could be reached only by inventing new forms of patience.

At the third photograph he snorted.

"There," Elian said.

"That bracket is from the eighties rebuild."

"Nineteen eighties?" Mira asked.

Otto looked at her as if she had asked whether rain was damp. "Unless old Harrick traveled forward for spare parts."

He tapped the print with a blunt finger. The nail had a dark crescent of oil no washing had persuaded away. "Original bracket was cast iron with two ribs. Cracked in seventy-eight when fools from the council tried to turn the crank without warming the bearings. We cut a brass support from scrap and fitted it after lunch. See that corner? I filed it because the cover wouldn't seat."

Elian leaned close. The drawn bracket showed the filed corner, rendered with careful little hatching. It was not a generic support. It was Otto's scar.

"Who had access to see this?" Mira asked.

"Anyone who stood by the glass after the cover came off. Not the public most days. Appraisers. photographers. Trustees. Men with clean shoes who said they loved industry."

"Do you have records of the rebuild?"

"There was a service notebook."

The word altered the air.

Elian kept his voice level. "Was?"

Otto's jaw worked. "Black cover. Grease on the corners. We wrote what drawings missed. Ada Marr started it. I kept it after her. It lived in the maintenance cabinet."

"Where is it now?"

"If I knew, I would have brought it instead of my temper."

Mira wrote quickly. "When did you last see it?"

"Before the hall closed for asbestos work. Maybe nine years ago. The trust moved things around. I was told records were boxed."

"By whom?"

"Thom Kest."

He said the trust chair's name without accusation, but fatigue lay beneath it. Old institutions did not need villains to lose things. They only needed keys, committees, damp basements, and time.

Elian showed him the central gear train. "Would the engine run as drawn?"

Otto laughed once, dry and sharp. "It would complain before it failed, and then it would break something expensive."

"That is a useful answer."

"It is the only answer."

He finally sat. The chair creaked under him. For a moment he looked less angry than wounded. "They want to take it?"

"The estate says it owns it," Mira said.

"The estate never oiled it. The estate never scraped mouse nests out of the printer carriage. The estate never stayed late because schoolchildren were coming in the morning and the carry arm stuck at seven."

No one answered. Some testimony arrived before court and did not need oath to become real.

Elian slid the photograph back into its sleeve. "Whoever drew this knew the machine after it was repaired, not when it was born."

Otto nodded. "They knew what to copy."

"But not what it meant."

"Meaning costs more."

He stood, slower now, cane finding the floor. At the door he turned back. "Find the black book."

"We will try."

"No." Otto's eyes fixed on Elian. "Find it. That engine has been spoken for by men who never listened to it. The book listened."

After he left, the consultation room retained the faint smell of oil. Elian did not mind. It was the first honest thing the case had brought in.

Chapter 5: Scrape

Celia Voss disliked being watched while she worked, but she disliked unexamined vellum more, so she allowed Elian and Mira to stand behind the yellow line in her conservation studio. The room was narrow, clean, and brighter than weather. Brushes lay in size order. Scalpels nested in cork blocks. The air held the dry mineral scent of paper, paste, and restraint.

The vellum rested on a padded table under low raking light. In that light the smooth authority of the drawing broke apart. The surface rose and fell in shallow ridges. Old pores caught shadow. A title panel that had seemed merely pale now showed a net of fine scratches.

Celia bent over it with a binocular microscope. She was small, severe, and had the gift of making silence feel like instruction. "Modern blade," she said.

Mira uncapped a pen. "How modern?"

"Sharp, stainless, impatient."

Elian smiled despite himself. "Can impatience be cited?"

"In my report, no. In my mood, yes."

She adjusted the light and invited them closer one at a time. Elian leaned over the eyepiece. The title block became a landscape of damage. Fibers had been shaved down, not abraded by ordinary handling. The cuts crossed old surface grime and stopped beneath newer ink. Someone had thinned away writing, then placed a claim in the wound.

"The vellum itself may be old," Celia said. "The skin has age. That helps the fraud. People see an old support and lend charity to everything on it."

"What was there before?" Mira asked.

"Too early to say. A label, perhaps. A display panel. The scraping is concentrated where title and origin would sit."

Elian looked at the inked name of the Marrow Engine. The letters had seemed proud yesterday. Under Celia's light they looked planted.

She took a minute fiber from an already loose edge of ink with a dampened microspatula so fine it appeared more idea than tool. She sealed it in a glassine packet and marked the location on a diagram. Every movement was small. Nothing in the room was hurried except the people who had brought the problem.

"Can you prove it?" Mira asked.

"The skin will prove it. I only have to listen without flattering it."

"What will you test?"

"Ink composition. Fiber disturbance. Heat history, if visible. Erased writing under infrared. I also want to know whether the surface was artificially aged after scraping."

"Cooked?" Elian asked.

"Possibly. Warmed, humidified, dried, pressed. Fraud often has kitchen habits."

Mira wrote that down, then crossed out kitchen habits and substituted controlled heat. Celia noticed and approved with one raised eyebrow.

At the far table a junior conservator repaired a torn map with wheat starch paste. The quiet work continued around them, proof that most damage in the world had no conspiracy behind it. Some things simply tore. Some were torn.

Celia moved the lamp again. Along the lower border, beneath decorative ruling, something faint interrupted the surface. "There may be erased pencil here."

Elian felt the case shift by a fraction. "Inventory?"

"Maybe. Or a mount note. I will use infrared after lunch."

Mira's phone vibrated. She read the message, expression flattening. "Pike is asking whether the vellum can be returned to the estate by tomorrow."

Celia did not look up. "No."

"He says delay may damage value."

"Then he should have brought it before someone scraped it with a fresh blade."

Elian watched Mira type. "That is not how solicitors usually phrase things."

"It is how conservators phrase things when being polite."

Celia covered the vellum with a clean sheet and turned off the raking light. The room seemed suddenly ordinary again, though nothing had become ordinary. The false document had acquired a body. It had cuts, layers, chemicals, heat perhaps. It had suffered in service of a claim.

At the door Celia stopped Elian. "Do not let them move it without my report."

"I have no power to stop them."

"Then borrow some from a judge."

Mira, still typing, said, "That is already in progress."

Outside, the rain had ended. The pavement shone. Elian carried the image of the scraped title panel with him like grit under the fingernail. Fraud was often discussed as intellect. Here it was labor. Someone had sat at a table and patiently injured a thing until it could lie.

Chapter 6: Pressure

Jarin Pike arrived at the archive shortly after three with a leather folder, polished shoes, and a smile designed for rooms with witnesses. He signed the visitor book in a hand so large it nearly entered the next line. The receptionist directed him to the small conference room, where Mira had already arranged herself behind a stack of papers that made hospitality unlikely.

Elian came in last and took the chair nearest the door. He preferred exits when men like Pike wished to talk.

"Thank you for seeing me at short notice," Pike said.

"You did not give notice," Mira replied.

His smile adjusted, not enough to vanish. "The estate is concerned."

"The trust is concerned."

"Naturally. These matters produce feeling." He opened the folder but did not remove anything. "What we require now is certainty."

"Truth," Elian said.

Pike looked at him as if noticing a tool left on a dining table. "Mr. Mott, yes. The archive consultant."

"Archivist."

"Of course. Truth in archives is often a negotiated thing."

Mira's face cooled. "Not in this archive."

For a moment the room held the hum of old fluorescent lights. Pike placed both hands on the folder. They were well-kept hands, the nails buffed, the skin pale where a ring had been recently removed or often turned. "Let us not become theatrical. The family has preserved this material for generations."

"They found it last week," Elian said.

"In a preserved desk."

"Convenient furniture."

Mira gave him a look that meant leave sarcasm to counsel. He folded his hands and let her proceed.

"The vellum is under conservation examination," she said. "The trust will seek interim protection until authenticity and title are determined."

Pike sighed. He had practiced the sigh. It conveyed disappointment in other people's failure to be adults. "Delay damages value. The collector is patient but not indefinitely so. The estate may pursue costs if obstruction is frivolous."

"Then do not make us litigate frivolity," Mira said. "Produce the chain of custody."

"We have provided a summary."

"A summary is what people offer when documents are absent."

The smile thinned. "Some records are private family papers."

"You are using private family papers to remove a public trust asset. Privacy has limits."

Pike turned to Elian. "You have examined the drawing. I understand you found mechanical questions. Surely one cannot expect a century-old patent sheet to satisfy every modern technical prejudice."

"The prejudice is against impossible shafts."

"Machines are complex."

"This one is. The drawing is less so."

Pike shut the folder at last. The soft leather made a sound like a book closing in a chapel. "Be careful, Mr. Mott. Expertise is persuasive until it becomes advocacy."

"Evidence survives advocacy."

"If handled properly."

Mira stood. "This meeting is over."

Pike rose with her, still measured, still public. At the door he looked once toward the secure room where the vellum had been taken after Celia's first examination. "The estate expects its property returned intact."

"So does the trust," Mira said.

After he left, she remained standing until his footsteps faded beyond reception. Then she sat and rubbed the bridge of her nose.

"He is scared," Elian said.

"He is calculating."

"Fear with an abacus."

"Do you have to phrase everything as if a nineteenth-century clerk is taking dictation?"

"Only under stress."

She almost smiled. Then she opened the folder he had left no papers from and began writing a note of the meeting. "Threat about costs. Attempt to frame conservation as delay. Refusal to produce chain of custody."

"Comment about truth being negotiated."

"Already written."

Elian added it to his own notebook. Threats were also provenance. So were evasions. So was the way Pike had glanced toward the storage room, as if the vellum mattered less as evidence than as hostage.

Mira's phone rang. She listened, spoke twice, and ended the call. "Judge Quill can hear us tomorrow morning."

"Good."

"Do not say she will like impossible shafts."

"I would never flatter a judge with a shaft."

"Elian."

"I will prepare diagrams."

He went back to the consultation room and laid out the photographs. For court, absurdity had to be made plain without becoming childish. He drew the engine as claimed, the engine as built, and the collision between them. Lines, arrows, labels. No adjectives. Let the machine testify first.

Late light entered the high windows and touched the table in pale bars. The archive settled into evening around him. Somewhere below, a cleaning cart rattled. Somewhere across town, Pike was likely making calls, smoothing panic into strategy.

Elian sharpened his pencil with a knife and kept drawing.

Chapter 7: Dormant

The Harrick Works stood at the bend of the canal, its brick walls blackened by a century of coal smoke and river damp. The letters over the gate had lost their gilt but not their stubbornness. HARRICK CALCULATING INSTRUMENTS. Beneath them a newer brass plaque named the workers' trust in smaller type, as if the present had been allowed to sign below the past.

Mira arrived with a temporary inspection order folded in her coat pocket. Otto arrived with keys on a ring large enough to restrain a small gate. Elian arrived carrying a camera case, two lamps, and the sense that old factories watched the people who came to value them.

"No heroics," Mira said as they crossed the yard.

Otto grunted. "Machines do not reward heroics."

"Nor do judges."

The demonstration hall occupied the front of the building, where visitors had once been shown polished certainty. Now dust lay in even gray sheets on the floor. Display cases held micrometers, brass calipers, lettered instruction cards, and a row of payroll clocks stopped at unrelated hours. At the center of the hall, behind a glass enclosure, the Marrow Engine stood under a canvas cover.

Otto unlocked the case. The key resisted, then turned with a dry snap.

"Do not touch the escapement," he said.

"I was raised properly," Elian replied.

"I have met raised people."

They removed the canvas together. Dust lifted and drifted in the lamplight. The engine appeared by degrees: columns, plates, drums, gear stacks, cams, a narrow printer carriage at the right side. It was not beautiful in the polished manner of museum machines. Its brass had darkened where oil and hands had known it. Its steel rods bore the faint bloom of age. Here and there repairs announced themselves without shame.

Elian felt the mood of the room change. Even Mira, who valued objects chiefly when they became evidence, stepped closer with care.

"There it is," Otto said.

The words were plain, but his voice was not.

Elian set the vellum photograph on a portable stand and began comparing. The actual gear train curved away from the drawing like a road refusing a bad map. The bracket Otto had named was there, filed corner and all. Behind it, half-hidden by a plate, the missing shaft existed in the real machine but not where the vellum placed motion. Whoever had drawn the false sheet had looked through glass, seen surfaces, guessed functions, and guessed wrong.

Mira pointed. "Could a viewer see enough from outside the case?"

"Enough to copy shapes," Elian said. "Not enough to understand sequence."

Otto opened a side access panel with a screwdriver from his pocket. "From here you see the carry arm. Public never saw this unless I was showing off."

"Did you show off?"

"Only when deserved."

He held a lamp while Elian photographed the internal train. The air inside the case smelled of dust, old oil, and cold metal. Elian noted screw heads, witness marks, wear arcs. Causality lived in such things. A polished path told where force had passed. A clean corner told where it had not.

Behind them the building ticked and settled. Wind moved through a cracked upper pane. Old places made small statements, and this one had been speaking for years to people too busy to listen.

Mira's phone vibrated. She stepped away, read, and returned. "Pike's counsel objects to inspection beyond visual review."

Otto closed the panel with exaggerated gentleness. "Tell him to object to physics next."

"I may phrase it differently."

Elian took one last photograph of the whole engine, head-on, with the false drawing in the foreground. The comparison was almost cruel. The vellum looked elegant. The engine looked competent. Competence, in machines and people, often had less charm and more use.

As they prepared to leave, Otto remained by the glass. He rested one hand on the edge of the case, not touching the engine, just the boundary around it.

"It ran payroll tables first," he said. "Then tide tables during the war. Then school demonstrations. People think invention is the day a man signs a patent. It isn't. It is every day the thing still works."

Mira listened, and Elian saw her legal theory sharpen around the sentence.

Outside, the canal was dull green under evening light. The factory windows reflected the sky in broken panes. Elian looked back once before the gate closed. The Marrow Engine was dormant, not dead. The difference mattered more than any document Pike had brought.

Chapter 8: Notation

Dr. Iona Bell kept her office in the mathematics faculty basement because, as she said, mechanical computation belonged near pipes. The room was narrow and overfull. Gear models sat beside chalk. Books leaned in towers that appeared unstable only to people without faith. A poster of Ada Lovelace watched from above a filing cabinet with one drawer permanently open.

Iona read the patent annotations with a red pencil in her hand and increasing offense in her shoulders. She was tall, gray-haired, and had the plainspoken severity of someone who had spent decades watching elegant machines described badly by confident men.

"No," she said at last.

Elian waited.

"Also no."

Mira, perched on the only chair not occupied by books, asked, "Can you expand that for court?"

Iona circled a symbol in the margin of the vellum photograph. "This mark for carry transfer was not in use when Harrick filed his original patents. It appears in teaching notes about twenty years later, after Beldon's notation simplified multi-stage mechanical diagrams."

"Could Harrick have devised it early?" Mira asked.

"He could have devised anything. But then his students, assistants, and rivals would not all have used the older notation in every surviving notebook while he alone used a symbol that became convenient after his death."

Elian leaned over the desk. "Modern historian's convenience?"

"Precisely. Someone who learned the machine from later diagrams backfilled that language into an earlier document."

She pulled a volume from the shelf without looking and opened to a plate marked with paper slips. Harrick's published 1894 specification showed dense little arrows, cross-references, and numbered operations. The vellum sheet had cleaner symbols, more generous spacing, and the seductive clarity of hindsight.

"Good for readers," Iona said. "Bad for authenticity."

"The estate may argue the vellum is a presentation drawing," Mira said.

"Presentation to whom? A time traveler? The notation community had not agreed with itself yet." Iona pushed the book toward them. "Look at the reset mark here. Hooked tail. On the vellum it is squared off. That convention arrives after commercial tabulators start standardizing training manuals."

Elian made notes. "Can you show this visually?"

"I can show it insultingly simply."

"Judges appreciate simplicity when it is not insulting."

"Then I will be polite to the judge and rude to the forgery."

She cleared a patch of chalkboard and drew three columns: Harrick period, transitional period, modern teaching notation. Under each she placed symbols for carry, reset, transfer, and print. The vellum marks fell not in the first column but in the third, with one awkward attempt to imitate the first. The pattern was more damning than any single error. It showed a hand trying to sound old in a language learned from new books.

Mira photographed the board. "Would you testify?"

"Gladly, if no one asks me to pretend uncertainty where there is only politeness."

"Courts require measured language."

"I can measure contempt."

Elian laughed, and Iona smiled for the first time. It softened her face without reducing its force.

They spent another hour with the annotations. Iona identified two references to later component names, one abbreviation from factory service records rather than patent practice, and a note about tolerance written in decimal form inconsistent with Harrick's shop books. Each point alone might be explained. Together they formed a crowd.

At the door, Iona handed Elian a photocopy of an old article. "There was a lecture in 1972 about the Marrow Engine rebuild. Professor Kline used slides from the factory. I do not know where the slides are, but if someone copied the outer frame of a real drawing, Kline may have shown it."

"Where would they be?"

"University audiovisual basement, if the damp has not eaten them. Ask for mechanical history overflow. They will deny it exists. Insist."

Mira tucked the article into her file. "Everyone in this case seems to have hidden something in a basement."

"Basements are where institutions put memory until money returns," Iona said.

Outside the faculty building, the rain had stopped and the pavement steamed faintly. Students crossed the quad with headphones and bright bags, carrying futures too immediate to notice old machines. Elian held Iona's chart under his arm. The vellum now failed in metal, skin, and language.

Fraud had many ways to be wrong. The trick was making each wrongness speak plainly enough that no one could mistake it for age.

Chapter 9: Thom

Thom Kest met them in the trust office above the factory canteen, a room with peeling green paint, metal filing cabinets, and a kettle that clicked off every six minutes whether water had boiled or not. He was broad-shouldered, bald, and wore a tie loosened as if he had been arguing since breakfast. A framed photograph behind him showed factory workers in 1932 standing around the Marrow Engine with the stiff pride of people who had built something difficult and knew it.

"I did not lose the patent file," Thom said before sitting.

Mira closed the door. "Who says you did?"

"Everyone who wants the engine sold."

Elian looked at the cabinets. Some had paper labels gone brown at the edges. One drawer stood open, empty except for dust and two dead staples.

Thom followed his gaze. "There was a trust copy. Blue wrapper. Ada Marr's note on the front. I saw it when I took over from Felton."

"When was that?" Mira asked.

"Twelve years ago. We inventoried the hall after the roof repairs. Engine documents were in cabinet C, second drawer. Service notebook, patent copy, maintenance receipts, correspondence with the council."

"And now?"

He opened the drawer fully. "Now we have air."

Elian knelt. Dust outlined the shape of a missing box. The rectangle was clean at the center, furred along the edges. Something had sat there for years and been removed recently enough for the surrounding dust to remember.

"Who had keys?" he asked.

Thom grimaced. "Trust officers. Otto had maintenance keys. Estate appraiser was given supervised access. Council heritage officer. Cleaning contractors for the common rooms, not cabinets."

"Supervised by whom?"

"Me, first visit. Pike, second visit."

Mira's pen stopped. "Pike supervised an estate appraiser inside trust records?"

"He said the family needed to identify personal papers. I objected. He waved some letter about cooperation. We were trying not to start a war."

"The war had already started," Elian said.

Thom looked at him, tired anger moving under his face. "Easy to say now."

"Yes. It is."

The admission did more than apology would have. Thom sat and rubbed both hands over his scalp. "The trust is broke. Roof, insurance, fire system, accessibility lift. Every year we choose which important thing to neglect. Pike arrived with money noises. Some trustees thought partnership. Some thought trap. I thought we could hear them out and keep control."

Mira softened only slightly. "We need visitor logs, key records, emails, minutes."

"You can have them."

"All of them."

"I said you can have them."

He pulled a cardboard archive box from under the table. Inside were folders labeled in block capitals. Thom had prepared for accusation by becoming useful. Elian respected that.

They reviewed the appraiser visits. First visit: Jarin Pike, appraiser Lena Corb, Thom Kest present. Second visit: Corb present, Pike listed as estate representative, supervision column blank. Third visit appeared in an email but not the log. The date matched the week before the vellum drawing surfaced.

"Why no log?" Mira asked.

Thom's face changed. "There was no third visit."

Elian turned the email around. Pike's assistant thanked Thom for accommodating "further document review." Thom read it twice, then shook his head.

"I was in hospital that week. Gallbladder. Everyone knew."

"Who covered for you?"

"Deputy chair. Maren Vale."

"We will need to speak to her."

Thom stood abruptly and walked to the window. Below, the demonstration hall roof sloped toward the canal. "They will make this my fault."

"They are trying," Mira said.

"Will it work?"

No one answered too quickly. Law did not reward comfort given ahead of evidence.

Elian closed the empty drawer and noted the dust rectangle. "There was a file. It vanished after estate access. That is not your acquittal, but it is a fact."

Thom nodded. His shoulders lowered a fraction.

Before they left, he handed Elian a photocopy from an old newsletter. It showed Ada Marr in overalls beside the engine, hair pinned back, one hand resting on the frame. The caption called her senior maintenance engineer and guardian of the trust copy.

"She distrusted owners," Thom said. "Said paper should have escape routes."

Elian looked at Ada's unsmiling face. "Where would she hide one?"

"Places only maintainers open."

Downstairs, beyond the office floor, the engine waited under glass. The missing file was no longer absence. It was direction.

Chapter 10: Notebook

Otto mentioned the service notebook again only after Mira had gone to file supplemental papers and Thom had been called into a trustee meeting loud enough to be heard through two doors. Elian found him in the demonstration hall, sitting on a bench opposite the glass case with his cane across his knees.

"You are brooding," Elian said.

"I am remembering. Brooding is for people with better knees."

Elian sat beside him. The hall was closed to visitors. Late sun came through high windows and made the dust on the floor look intentional. The Marrow Engine stood half in shadow, its brass dulled to the color of old honey.

"Tell me about the black book."

Otto rubbed his thumb over the cane handle. "Ada started it before I was hired. She said official drawings were written for owners, not maintainers. Owners wanted to know what a thing cost. Maintainers needed to know how it failed."

"What was in it?"

"Everything. Bearing replacements. Gear chatter. Who dropped a wrench into the print carriage in 1956. Which bolts were original and which were cut from scrap. Notes about demonstrations. Arguments in the margins. Ada had strong margins."

"And the trust copy?"

"Blue folder. Sometimes kept with the book, sometimes separate. Ada moved things when she smelled lawyers."

"Did she smell them often?"

"She believed all corridors eventually produced one."

They watched the engine in silence. Elian had learned that silence beside an old craftsman was not empty; it was where detail gathered courage.

Otto finally said, "There was an oil cabinet under the south stair."

"Thom showed us cabinet C."

"Cabinet C was for people who asked where records were. Oil cabinet was for people who knew why records mattered."

Elian turned toward him. "You think it may still be there?"

"I think Ada built false backs in places because she had a low opinion of inheritance. I also think I am old and may be turning memory into architecture."

"Those are not mutually exclusive."

Otto snorted. "You talk like a footnote."

"Occupational damage."

They crossed the hall to the south stair. Beneath it stood a row of gray metal cabinets, their doors labeled LUBRICANTS, RAGS, BELTS, WASTE. The oil cabinet smelled faintly rancid when Otto opened it. Inside were two modern plastic bottles, a tin funnel, and a stack of absorbent pads still in their wrapping.

Otto knelt with difficulty. Elian offered a hand; Otto ignored it until the last possible second, then used it without acknowledgment. He ran his fingers along the back panel. "This has been replaced."

"When?"

"After my time. Too clean."

He tapped. The sound was flat, not hollow.

Elian examined the screws. Phillips heads, bright under grime. "Modern."

"Someone found one hiding place."

"Or maintenance replaced a panel."

"Maintenance does not use four different screws unless drunk or dead."

Elian photographed the cabinet and screws. The missing notebook had become both more possible and less reachable. If someone had searched the old hiding places, they knew Ada's habits. That narrowed the field to maintainers, trustees, or someone with advice from one.

Otto closed the cabinet harder than necessary. The clang traveled up the stairwell.

"If the book is gone," he said, "they have the service history."

"Or they destroyed it."

"That would be like burning a witness because you disliked his accent."

Elian wrote the phrase down. Otto noticed.

"Do not quote me in court."

"I will paraphrase respectfully."

"Do not paraphrase me either."

From upstairs came the muffled rise of voices. The trustees were arguing over injunction costs, insurance, loyalty, survival. Institutions made noble decisions only after making practical ones loudly.

Elian looked back toward the engine. "Machines keep secrets badly."

Otto followed his gaze. "Men are worse."

"Ada hid copies."

"If she had time."

"She had distrust. It often works faster than time."

Otto's face settled into something like approval. "Then look where a maintainer would open because no owner wanted dirt on his cuffs."

"Oil cabinets, access panels, crawl spaces."

"And the head assembly."

Elian raised an eyebrow. Otto shook his head. "No. Not without order. You open that wrong, you misalign the carry train."

"I was raised properly."

"Raise yourself further."

They left the south stair with photographs but no book. Still, Elian felt the case had gained a pulse. The fraud had not controlled everything. It had missed old habits, or feared them. Somewhere in the factory, perhaps, a black notebook waited with grease on its corners and the stubborn memory of hands.

Chapter 11: Inspection

Judge Mara Quill held emergency applications in a courtroom that had once been painted cream and had since matured into the color of old envelopes. She was small, unsmiling, and read every page as if the paper had personally asked for her time. By the time Mira rose to speak, the judge had already marked three tabs in the bundle.

Pike's counsel, a silver-haired man named Ravel, began with courtesy and moved quickly to alarm. The estate, he said, faced irreparable commercial harm. The trust had produced speculation dressed as expertise. The vellum was old, the family reputable, the sale time-sensitive.

Judge Quill looked over her glasses. "Most sales are time-sensitive to the seller, Mr. Ravel. That does not make them sacred."

Mira kept her face still. Elian, seated behind her, admired the sentence privately.

When her turn came, she laid out the matter without heat. A newly discovered document had appeared inside a disputed chain of custody. The machine it purported to describe did not match the drawing in material respects. The support showed signs of alteration. Trust records were missing after estate access. The sale would remove or transform the asset before evidence could be tested.

"We ask for limited inspection, preservation of the engine and related records, and restraint on sale pending full hearing," she said.

Ravel rose again. "The estate has no objection to visual inspection, provided no parts are touched, disturbed, photographed beyond agreed angles, or subjected to invasive conservation."

"That would make the order decorative," Mira said.

Judge Quill turned to Elian. "Mr. Mott, you have provided a preliminary statement. Can the mechanical issue be assessed without dismantling?"

Elian stood. "Partly, Your Honor. The visible train already contradicts the vellum. Certain wear patterns and internal alignments require access panels to be opened, but not removal of parts."

"Risk?"

"Low if performed by Mr. Brann, who maintained the engine, and documented throughout."

"And the vellum?"

Celia, seated two rows back, rose when called. "Non-destructive imaging and micro-sampling from already loose ink. No flattening, no washing, no removal from support."

The judge asked four more questions, each practical, each aimed at preventing later complaints. Who would hold the vellum? Who would log access? How would photographs be shared? Could the trust afford preservation? Mira answered. Ravel objected. Pike, sitting at the estate table, wrote notes without looking at anyone.

At last Judge Quill dictated the order. "No sale, transfer, alteration, removal, private conservation, or transport of the Marrow Engine or documents purporting to establish title until further order. Limited inspection under joint protocol. No dismantling. No private testing. No helpful transport."

Ravel stood. "Your Honor, the estate is concerned that preservation may become interference."

"Preservation is not disappearance," the judge replied. "If either side confuses the two, they may return here and explain themselves."

Elian liked her at once, though liking judges was professionally dangerous. It encouraged faith in systems that still required proof, money, and luck.

Outside the courtroom, Pike approached with Ravel a step behind him. "A temporary inconvenience," he said to Mira.

"Then endure it temporarily."

"The heirs will remember who increased their costs."

"They should remember who found the document."

His eyes moved to Elian. "You are enjoying this."

"No."

The answer seemed to irritate him more than wit would have.

Elian continued, "A false document wastes everyone. Even the people using it."

Pike's face showed, for the first time, a clean flash of anger. Then the public smile returned. "Be careful with old things, Mr. Mott. They break."

"So do stories."

Mira touched Elian's sleeve, not warning exactly, but timing. They let Pike pass.

In the corridor, Celia joined them with her case in one hand. "I heard no private conservation. Good."

"You also heard no dismantling," Otto said, arriving from a bench by the lift where he had pretended not to listen.

"I heard access panels," Elian said.

"Panels are not dismantling if opened by someone with sense."

"The order says joint protocol," Mira reminded them. "We draft it today. Inspection tomorrow."

The courthouse doors opened onto wet steps and taxi noise. Elian held the stamped order while Mira called the trust. The paper was thin, ordinary, and suddenly load-bearing. Behind its clauses the engine had gained time. Time was not truth, but truth often needed it as a room needs light.

Chapter 12: Cam Wear

The inspection began with too many people in the hall. Ravel sent an associate. Pike sent an appraiser who said little and photographed everything except the places Elian asked her to note. The trust sent Thom, pale from lack of sleep. Mira stood with the court order on a clipboard. Celia came to observe handling conditions, though metal was not her kingdom. Otto arrived with a tool roll older than the appraiser's career.

"Names in the log," Mira said. "Times in and out. No one touches the engine except Mr. Brann. Questions through me."

Otto opened the glass case and waited for everyone to finish breathing over his shoulder. Then he rolled back the left access panel with the slow care of a man opening a sleeping eye. The smell of old lubricant deepened at once.

Elian set lamps at low angles. Brass and steel became topography: ridges, polish, dust, dark arcs where parts had moved thousands of times. He photographed the cam assembly first. The vellum drawing showed a narrow cam profile, almost delicate, with a steep rise meant to lift the follower quickly. The actual engine's follower had worn a broad shining path across the cam face.

"This machine ran thousands of cycles," Otto said.

"Not with that cam," Elian replied.

"Not for one turn."

Ravel's associate cleared his throat. "Could the cam have been replaced?"

Otto looked at him over the rim of his glasses. "Everything can be replaced if you own a hammer and lack shame. The question is whether it was."

Elian pointed to the witness marks. "The mounting screws show long-term seating. The oil staining is continuous. No fresh tool marks. If replaced, it was replaced before decades of use, and the vellum still shows a profile inconsistent with this wear."

The associate wrote quickly, perhaps capturing only the parts that looked helpful. Mira watched him do it.

They moved through the train. At each stage, the machine gave small, stubborn answers. A shaft centerline differed by several millimeters from the vellum. A reset lever had clearance the drawing failed to allow. A stop pin in the drawing would have blocked the carry arm in the engine. The false sheet had copied appearances and misunderstood motion.

Celia, standing back from the case, said, "It is strange how much damage confidence can do."

"In machines?" Mira asked.

"In everything."

At the right side, Otto removed a dust cover over the printer carriage. Beneath it lay a row of type wheels, blackened at the edges. Elian took close photographs. The vellum drawing included the carriage but simplified the escapement so severely that it could not advance paper cleanly.

"Why include that detail if wrong?" Thom asked.

"Because a visible carriage looked important," Elian said. "The forger knew what a buyer expected to see."

"A buyer, not a machinist," Otto said.

The appraiser flushed but said nothing.

After two hours, Elian's knees ached from crouching. His notes filled six pages. He had expected contradiction; he had not expected the emotional force of accumulated error. Each wrong line on the vellum now seemed less like a mistake and more like indifference. The forger had not merely lied about ownership. He had failed to care how the engine lived.

They closed the panel, logged the time, and resealed the case with a numbered tag. Mira made everyone sign.

Pike appeared as they were packing. He had not been present for the work but entered at the moment of conclusion, which told Elian he understood performance better than evidence.

"I trust the inspection was satisfactory," Pike said.

"It was informative," Mira replied.

"Different word."

"Better one."

Otto was winding his tool roll. "Your drawing gives the engine a bad leg, a broken hand, and no lungs."

Pike looked at Mira. "Is this part of the official record?"

"It can be, if you like."

He declined by silence.

Elian placed the cam photographs in a hard case. Brass remembered use more honestly than families remembered ownership. The phrase came to him fully formed, but he did not write it down yet. Some sentences needed to earn their place in a report.

Outside, in the yard, Thom stopped him. "Is it enough?"

"It is good evidence."

"That is not the same thing."

"No."

The trust chair nodded as if he had expected no comfort and respected the absence of it. Across the yard, Pike's car pulled away, its tires hissing through shallow puddles. Elian watched it go, then looked back at the factory. The machine had spoken in wear, clearance, and oil. Now they had to translate that speech for court before someone translated it into money.

Chapter 13: Ink

Celia's ink report began with restraint and ended with a quiet blade. She invited Elian and Mira to the studio after the first tests, warning them that preliminary results were not verdicts. Then she placed three sample charts on the light table and let the colors condemn themselves.

"The brown line contains a modern stabilizer," she said.

Mira leaned closer. "Modern as in recent?"

"Late twentieth century at earliest in this formulation. It was chosen to look old. Iron gall behavior, brown tone, slight feathering. But the binder tells a different story."

Elian studied the magnified images. Under visible light the gear lines and title letters shared a family resemblance. Under analysis, they separated. The title ink sat in the scraped panel like a later tenant. The gear ink contained another mixture, older perhaps, or artificially aged differently.

"One drawing has become two acts," he said.

Celia nodded. "Old skin. New claim. Possibly intermediate decorative ruling."

"Can you explain that without sounding poetic?"

"I can, but it will be less accurate."

Mira made a note. "Could the estate argue restoration? That old ink was reinforced?"

"They may argue anything. Reinforcement follows original lines. Here the title panel was abraded, then rewritten. The new ink crosses cut fibers. Also, the pressure differs."

She showed them a side-lit image where the title letters sat slightly proud, their edges crisp in the damaged skin. The gear ink, by contrast, had settled into older surface irregularities. The support had a history. The claim had an entrance date.

Elian felt a familiar anger, professional and cold. He did not mind copies when they confessed to being copies. He did not mind restoration when it marked its work. But this drawing had used age as disguise, and disguise as weapon.

Celia passed Mira a draft paragraph. "Do not quote from it until final. I need confirmatory spectroscopy."

Mira read. "The title inscription is materially inconsistent with the support and with other inked elements. Surface preparation indicates deliberate removal of prior text."

"Good severe words," Elian said.

"Severe is earned," Celia replied.

At a side bench she had arranged comparison samples: nineteenth-century iron gall, twentieth-century drafting ink, modern conservation-safe pigment. Each behaved differently under light and solvent. Celia spoke of them without romance. Ink was chemistry before it was handwriting. It aged, migrated, cracked, and betrayed.

"The forger had money," she said.

"And taste?" Elian asked.

"Taste would have made them less greedy."

Mira looked up from the report. "What do you mean?"

"A modest false label may pass because no one profits enough to inspect it. A title document attached to a machine worth millions invites tools."

"Greed increases magnification," Elian said.

"Exactly."

The studio phone rang. Celia answered, listened, and said, "No." A pause. "Because the court order says no." Another pause. "Because I can read." She hung up with visible care.

"Pike?" Mira asked.

"His office. They would like an estate representative present for all testing."

"They can observe agreed procedures, not supervise them."

"I told them less elegantly."

Elian took photographs of the charts for his own working file. The title ink differed from the gear ink. The stabilizer placed one part of the drawing in the wrong century. The scraped fibers placed it after an injury. Soon, with infrared, perhaps the old erased purpose would surface.

As they left, Celia turned the vellum lights off one by one. The sheet lay under its cover, guarded by darkness and protocol.

In the corridor Mira said, "Every new answer makes the fraud more elaborate."

"Or more desperate."

"Which is more dangerous?"

"Desperation. Elaborate people make plans. Desperate people improve them badly."

She considered that, then checked her phone. "Pike has filed a demand for expedited return of estate property."

"After hearing about the ink?"

"Before, I think. But he will know soon."

Elian looked back through the studio window. Celia had already returned to the microscope, head bent, hands steady. The vellum had entered a room where lies were not shouted down. They were thinned, lit, sampled, and named.

Chapter 14: Inventory

The probate annex occupied the oldest wing of the courthouse, a place of narrow stairs, dim bulbs, and clerks who knew the location of every file but resented being asked quickly. Mira obtained access with a stamped request and the expression of a woman prepared to wait longer than bureaucracy could bear.

Elian carried the document camera. He had come for handwriting, paper, sequence, and the small human lapses that altered records usually left behind. Fraud in official files was rarely theatrical. It preferred margins.

The Harrick estate inventory lay in a gray folder tied with tape. The original filing listed furniture, securities, land parcels, factory shares, and a line for "Harrick Works demonstration engine and associated drawings, held in trust." In the amended copy, the phrase had changed. "Held by estate." Two words removed, two substituted. A machine moved across legal reality by the pressure of a pen.

Mira set both pages side by side. "Different pen."

"Different century?" Elian asked.

"Different conscience."

The amendment bore no initials. No clerk stamp. No cross-reference to an order authorizing correction. The page number had been typed originally, but the altered line sat slightly lower than its neighbors, as if inserted on a machine whose carriage did not share the first machine's memory.

Elian photographed the page under normal light, then oblique light. The ink shine differed. The amended words had not sunk into the paper with the same age. A faint erasure shadow sat where "in trust" had been.

A clerk named Ms. Denholm watched from the doorway. "You find something?"

"An untidy correction," Mira said.

Ms. Denholm came closer and peered over her half glasses. "We dislike those."

"So do we."

"Amendments should have initials. Even old ones. Especially old ones. Dead people create enough confusion without clerks helping."

Elian liked her immediately. "Do you have an amendment ledger?"

"We have three, because one burned and one was reconstructed by a man with handwriting like rain."

She led them through a side room stacked with ledgers. Dust rose in a patient cloud. For an hour they searched dates around the supposed correction. No entry. The estate inventory had changed without the office admitting it had been touched.

Mira's voice stayed even. "Who requested the file recently?"

Ms. Denholm produced a retrieval slip. Two requests in the past month: Jarin Pike's firm, then Mira's. Before that, nothing for seven years.

"Was the file supervised?" Elian asked.

"All files are supervised," the clerk said, offended. Then honesty won. "In theory. We had two staff out that week."

"Could someone substitute a page?"

Her mouth tightened. "Someone could try. Someone would have to be arrogant or stupid."

"Those qualities travel together," Mira said.

They scanned the amended page at high resolution and requested certified copies of prior microfilm. Ms. Denholm disappeared into an office and returned with a reel box. "Old imaging from before the records moved. If your line says something different there, I want a copy of your report."

The microfilm reader hummed like a tired insect. Elian threaded the reel while Mira dimmed the lamp. Frames flickered: wills, receipts, inventories, signatures of people long reduced to flourishes and debt. Then the Harrick page appeared, grainy but clear.

Held in trust.

The words sat on the old image without hesitation.

Mira exhaled. "There."

Elian captured the frame. Probate records were full of grief and arithmetic. This one had acquired ambition after filing.

Ms. Denholm folded her arms. "I will be making an internal note."

"Please make it durable," Mira said.

On the courthouse steps later, the certified request receipt in Mira's file, Elian watched buses move through late traffic. The case had shifted again. The vellum was not alone. A false drawing, a missing trust file, an altered inventory. Different materials, same direction.

"The paper moved toward sale," he said.

Mira nodded. "And away from trust."

"That is a path."

"It is also a pleading."

She looked tired but sharpened by anger. Elian had seen that state in good lawyers and good machinists. Fatigue burned away decoration. What remained was purpose.

They walked back toward the archive in the blue hour, carrying copies of a line that had been changed because someone believed no one would look backward carefully enough. It was a common mistake. Archives existed almost entirely to punish it.

Chapter 15: Second Letter

Jarin Pike produced the provenance letter at four-thirty on a Thursday, which told Mira nearly as much as the letter itself. Late documents had a talent for arriving after the day's patience had been spent. He sent it by courier in a cream envelope with the estate crest embossed on the flap, as if paper lineage could improve content.

They opened it in the archive consultation room with Iona present by chance and Celia by invitation. The letter was dated 1938. It purported to be from a private collector named Alistair Fenn to Lucien Harrick's grandson, confirming that the vellum drawing had remained in a family desk since the inventor's death and had never passed into the workers' trust.

Mira read it once, then placed it flat on the table. "Convenient."

"That word keeps returning," Elian said.

Iona adjusted her glasses. "The prose is wrong."

"How?"

"No one in 1938 who collected mechanical drawings wrote like a catalog entry from 1995."

Celia was already looking at the paper, not the words. "May I?"

Mira nodded. Celia lifted the sheet with a spatula and held it toward the window. The winter light passed through the paper and revealed a watermark: MARCH & FENN ARCHIVAL BOND.

Elian saw the problem before anyone spoke. "Fenn?"

"Paper mill," Celia said. "Founded 1964."

Iona made a small satisfied sound. "Bad luck."

"No," Elian said. "Bad homework."

Mira photographed the watermark and sent a preservation notice to Pike before the kettle in the corner finished boiling. Then she called his office on speaker.

Pike answered with warmth too ready to be real. "Ms. Saye. I trust the letter clarifies matters."

"It raises them. The paper bears a watermark from a mill founded in 1964."

Silence arrived cleanly.

Then Pike said, "It may be a replacement copy."

"Your covering note calls it original correspondence."

"I will have to confirm with the family."

"Please also confirm why a replacement copy contains a fresh signature in blue ink and no notation that it replaces anything."

"You are taking an adversarial tone."

"The document is taking an adversarial date."

Elian looked at Iona, who mouthed, Good.

Pike ended the call with promises to revert, a verb that often meant retreat under varnish. Mira immediately drafted a request for the original, chain of custody, and identity of the person who located the letter. Celia bagged the sheet. Iona began listing historical wording issues in the margins of a copy with visible pleasure.

"The collector's archive may survive," Iona said. "Alistair Fenn donated papers to Halver University. I used a catalogue years ago."

"Can we get appointment books?" Mira asked.

"If they were kept. Collectors kept records of lunch with people they disliked. It made them feel permanent."

Elian read the letter again. It was over-explanatory. It answered legal questions no one in 1938 would have framed. It named the workers' trust before the trust existed in its later form. It smelled, not physically but intellectually, of a document written backward from a dispute.

"Why produce something this weak?" Celia asked.

"Because they need more than the vellum," Mira said. "Our inspection hurt them."

"Or because they assume old-looking things pass if delivered with confidence," Elian said.

The courier envelope remained on the table. Elian examined it, then the letter's fold pattern. The creases were fresh, too shallow for a document supposedly kept in a desk for decades. There were no rust marks from old clips, no dust shadows, no correspondence scars. It had lived a short, careful life.

At six, the archive lights shifted to evening mode, dimming along the far wall. Staff moved through closing routines. The consultation room, with its bagged letter and photographs and handwritten objections, felt like a small court already in session.

Mira's phone chimed. Pike had sent a one-line email: The estate reserves all rights regarding disputed interpretations of private documents.

"That is not a chain of custody," she said.

"It is a fog machine," Iona replied.

Elian placed the letter copy beside the vellum photographs. False skin. False title. False provenance. The case was acquiring layers, but the layers did not strengthen it. They made the construction visible.

Mira capped her pen. "I will ask the court to require production of originals."

"And if there are none?"

"Then we ask why copies keep arriving when truth was requested."

Outside, dusk pressed against the windows. Elian gathered his notes. The second letter was meant to close a gap. Instead it had opened another door, and beyond it he could hear the machinery of fraud running faster than its maker understood.

Chapter 16: Hidden Copies

The next morning Otto met Elian at the factory with a paper bag of pastries and a list of places no owner had ever willingly inspected. The pastries were for stamina, he said, not hospitality. They ate standing under the south stair while dust from the treads sifted down with each footstep above.

"Ada Marr believed in duplicates," Otto said. "Not copies for convenience. Copies as insurance against men in suits."

"Men in suits appear often in your theology."

"Too often in my life."

They began with the least invasive places. The oil cabinet had already disappointed them. Behind the belt rack they found a mouse nest made partly from a 1987 visitor brochure. Inside a hollow plinth beneath a display case they found three rusted screws, a bus ticket, and a pencil labeled HARRICK WORKS STORES. In the old pattern room, now used for folding chairs, Otto showed Elian where Ada had once taped keys under drawer runners.

"She sounds paranoid," Elian said.

"She was accurate."

Mira joined them near noon, carrying coffee and a copy of Pike's latest objection. "He says unsupervised searches threaten estate property."

Otto looked around at the cracked plaster and dusty gauges. "He is welcome to supervise the spiders."

Elian, who was photographing a maintenance hatch, said, "We are not removing anything. We are documenting trust storage locations."

"I know," Mira said. "I enjoy watching objections become silly when placed near facts."

They moved to the engine hall after lunch. The court order barred dismantling but allowed inspection of surrounding records and fixtures. Otto walked the perimeter slowly, touching nothing at first. He was listening with his eyes. Elian had seen conservators do the same with bindings, machinists with lathes, nurses with patients before taking a pulse.

"There," Otto said at last.

He pointed not to the engine but to a narrow service cupboard built into the wall behind the demonstration platform. Its door had been painted shut at least twice. Thom found a key after twenty minutes of muttering in the office. The lock resisted. Otto refused a crowbar, worked the key gently, and opened the cupboard with a sound like paper tearing.

Inside were lamp bulbs, old cleaning cloths, a cracked enamel sign, and a wooden box marked PROJECTOR. No notebook. No blue folder.

But the back of the cupboard was false.

Elian saw it when Otto held the lamp low. The grain ran wrong. Two nail heads had been painted, not driven. Otto did not pry. He photographed, measured, and called Mira over.

"We need court permission to open it," Mira said before anyone asked.

Otto's face hardened. "It is a cupboard."

"It may contain evidence."

"That is why cupboards exist."

"That is why judges exist."

The old man stared at her, then laughed once. "You argue like Ada when she was right."

Mira filed an email request from her phone, attaching photographs. Judge Quill's clerk acknowledged receipt with the kind of speed that suggested the judge had begun to expect trouble from this case.

While they waited, Elian examined the projector box. It contained glass slides from factory demonstrations: payroll tables, gear diagrams, a portrait of Harrick, a badly faded image of Ada Marr beside the engine. Not the service notebook, but another thread. He logged each slide.

"Paper should have escape routes," Otto said quietly.

Elian looked up.

"Ada said that after the first trust dispute. The family wanted to borrow drawings for an anniversary exhibition. She sent them photographs and hid the originals for a month."

"Where?"

"In the boiler flue. Wrapped better than sandwiches."

Mira's phone chimed. Limited opening permitted, provided photographed and witnessed. Otto's mood improved by three degrees.

They removed the false back with thin wedges. Behind it lay a shallow cavity, empty except for a strip of blue paper caught on a nail.

Mira bagged it. Otto closed his eyes.

Elian looked at the torn scrap. Blue wrapper. Trust copy, perhaps. Or bait. The cavity had held something; someone had found it first.

"Not empty," he said.

Otto opened his eyes, angry again because anger was easier than grief. "Empty enough."

"No. It tells us Ada hid copies. It tells us someone knew where to look. And it tells us they missed at least one fiber."

Mira nodded. "We add it to the application."

The cupboard smelled of old dust and dry wood. Elian photographed the cavity until the light failed. Hidden copies were no longer legend. They were absence with blue edges, and absence, properly handled, could still testify.

Chapter 17: Pencil

Infrared light changed the vellum from an object into a confession that had not yet found words. Celia worked in darkness except for the monitor glow, her hands moving over the controls with unhurried precision. Elian and Mira watched from behind her, faces reflected faintly in the black screen.

At first the image showed only the familiar title block: MARROW ENGINE, ORIGINAL PATENT DESIGN, LUCIEN HARRICK. Then Celia adjusted the wavelength, and the proud letters faded. Beneath the lower border, where scraped fibers had thinned the skin, older marks began to surface.

A number appeared first. Faint. Broken. 72-D-14.

Then a word: DISPLAY.

Mira leaned forward. "Not a patent file."

"Not originally," Celia said.

More letters emerged under the erased title panel. They were not part of a legal instrument. They looked like an exhibition mount note: vellum demonstration sheet, gear sequence display, possibly from a museum or factory lecture. Someone had taken an old educational object, scraped away its modest purpose, and promoted it into title evidence.

Elian felt the case settle into a clearer shape. "Fraud with a career change."

Celia captured the image and saved three exposures. "The inventory number may correspond to a museum deaccession or internal display catalogue."

"Can we trace it?" Mira asked.

"If the catalogue survives. Numbers are more loyal than people."

Elian copied the number carefully. 72-D-14. The 72 might refer to 1972, which matched Iona's lecture slide lead. D for demonstration. Fourteen for a sequence in an educational set. Or none of those things. Archivists loved patterns and feared them for the same reason.

Celia moved to ultraviolet. The scraped title panel fluoresced differently from the surrounding vellum. The damage became a pale wound. The newer ink appeared dark and assertive.

"There," she said. "Surface abrasion before inscription. The infrared gives us prior use; ultraviolet gives us sequence."

Mira's pen scratched steadily. "Can this be in your final report by Monday?"

"Yes, if no one calls me again to ask whether court orders are optional."

"They are not."

"I know. I keep explaining this to people who own expensive phones."

Elian studied the ghost word DISPLAY. It had humility. It told viewers what they were seeing. It made no claim to own anything. The forger had chosen it precisely because its material age could be borrowed. Old vellum, old ink perhaps, old factory association. Then scraping, heat, new title, new chain, new money.

The monitor flickered as Celia changed exposure. For a moment the false title and erased word overlapped, two purposes occupying the same skin. It was almost unbearable in its neatness.

"Who had access to deaccessioned display materials?" Mira asked.

"Museums, conservation brokers, private dealers, universities clearing storage," Elian said. "And anyone who knew to ask."

"Pike?"

"Pike would not scrape vellum himself. He would buy the ability."

Celia saved the final image and turned on the room light. Everyone blinked. The vellum on the table looked innocent again under ordinary illumination. That was the insult of good forgeries. They asked normal light to be their accomplice.

Mira's phone rang. She stepped into the corridor. Through the glass, Elian saw her expression change from attention to anger contained by training. She returned after two minutes.

"Thom has been suspended by the trustees pending an anonymous complaint."

"What complaint?"

"Sale of archival materials."

Celia said a word too softly to enter any report.

Elian looked back at the vellum. The erased inventory number, the missing blue folder, the empty hiding place, Thom suspended at the moment his testimony mattered. The case was no longer only about proving a document false. It was about keeping people from becoming substitutes for missing paper.

"They are making him the drawer," he said.

Mira understood. "Then we find the drawer."

"And the catalogue behind that number."

Celia handed them the printed infrared image. The word DISPLAY hovered beneath the false title like a witness seen through fog.

Elian placed it in a folder and wrote the number on the tab. For once, the past had left not a grand statement but a small inventory mark. That was enough. Small marks were harder to intimidate.

Chapter 18: Calculation

Iona preferred chalk to slides because chalk made error visible. In the seminar room she reserved for the afternoon, she drew the Marrow Engine's true calculation path across three boards: input, carry, reset, transfer, print. Her lines were spare and exact. Each gear became a circle, each cam a rise, each shaft a horizontal certainty.

Elian sat with Mira in the front row. Otto took a chair near the aisle, arms folded. Thom had come despite suspension, or perhaps because of it. He looked hollow around the eyes but alert. Celia stood at the back, unwilling to sit in a room where dust fell from the ceiling vents.

"The machine performs a sequence," Iona said. "Not a gesture. Sequence matters because mechanical time is unforgiving."

She turned the crank on a small teaching model. One wheel advanced. A pawl caught. A carry arm lifted at the right moment. The next wheel moved. It was slow, visible, and satisfying.

"Now the vellum path."

On the third board she drew the false arrangement. At first, to an untrained eye, it looked plausible. Wheels met wheels. Lines connected parts. The notation smiled. Then she marked the missing shaft in red and the impossible collision in blue.

"Here it locks at the third operation."

Otto made a sound like a verdict.

Iona continued. "If forced, the drive would either strip teeth from the pinion or bend the reset arm. The printer carriage would never receive a clean carry signal. This is not an alternate design. It is a nonfunctioning diagram made by someone who saw components but did not understand timing."

Mira photographed the three boards. "Can this be simplified for court?"

"This is simplified."

"Further."

Iona looked offended, then practical. "I can build a demonstration model with removable shafts. True path turns. Vellum path locks."

"Good."

"It will click when it fails."

"Even better."

Elian walked to the board and compared the chalk with the vellum photograph. The forger had made an intelligent mistake, which was more useful than a foolish one. Intelligent mistakes revealed source material. The false drawing copied the engine as seen after the rebuild, then translated visible parts into a patent-style logic. The result failed because the hidden sequence had not been available to sight.

"They worked from photographs," he said.

Otto shook his head. "Photographs and a visit. You would not draw that bracket so neatly from public pictures."

"Then someone opened the case for them."

No one spoke. Thom's suspended status sat heavily in the room.

"Not Thom," Otto said.

Thom looked at him, startled.

"You forget to oil door hinges," Otto said. "You could not guide a forger through that case without leaving fingerprints on your own forehead."

It was perhaps not the most graceful vote of confidence, but Thom took it as one. "Thank you, I think."

Mira wrote: access to engine interior. "Who besides Otto could open it?"

"Former maintainers," Otto said. "Two dead. One in Spain and too honest from laziness. Trustees with keys could open the glass but not interpret. Pike's appraiser may have had a consultant."

"The conservation workshop," Elian said. "If they handled mechanical frames, they may have someone who understands just enough."

Iona underlined the red missing shaft until chalk dust fell. "Just enough is the birthplace of many bad diagrams."

They spent another hour refining the explanation. Iona translated motion into courtroom language. Elian translated courtroom language back into mechanical sequence when it became too vague. Mira marked the places where opposing counsel would try to widen uncertainty. Otto corrected every sentence that made the engine sound delicate rather than stubborn.

By the end, the boards carried a clean argument. The true machine made a mechanical sentence. The vellum interrupted itself mid-verb.

Outside the seminar room, students gathered for an evening class and glanced at the chalked gears without interest. Their ordinary indifference comforted Elian. Not every old thing needed drama. Most deserved accuracy, shelter, and use.

Thom lingered after the others left. "If the engine proves the drawing false, why do we need the notebook?"

"Because false is one question," Elian said. "Ownership is another. The notebook may show the trust's work, repairs, and custody."

"And clear me."

"Yes."

Thom nodded, not asking for certainty. He had learned the cost of that word.

Elian took one final photograph of Iona's boards. Sometimes absurdity needed a diagram. Sometimes justice did too.

Chapter 19: Suspended

Thom stood outside the factory gate with his keys in a brown envelope and his coat collar turned against the wind. The trustees had suspended him that morning after an anonymous complaint accused him of selling archival material from the Harrick Works collection. The gate behind him was locked, though he had opened it for others for twelve years.

"They are making me the missing drawer," he said when Elian arrived.

"Then we find the drawer."

"Easy sentence."

"Hard work."

Mira joined them from the street, carrying a folder under one arm. She looked at the envelope in Thom's hand. "Did you surrender all keys?"

"Yes. They inventoried them as if I might steal the building by habit."

"Do not speak to trustees alone for now. Anything you say will become weather."

Thom laughed without pleasure. "I used to be weatherproof."

They walked to a cafe near the canal because Thom was no longer allowed in the office. The place smelled of frying onions and wet coats. A radio played low behind the counter. Thom ordered tea and did not drink it.

He handed Elian a list of appraiser visits. "I made this after the first request. Dates, names, who had access, what rooms."

Mira spread it beside the official log. Jarin Pike's name appeared twice where the visitor log showed once. The third visit, the one Thom denied, had a purchase-order reference in Pike's own correspondence but no trust entry.

"Who signed the purchase order?" Mira asked.

"Estate side. Initials J.P."

"For what service?"

"Document review and display assessment."

Elian underlined display. The erased word on the vellum had acquired company.

Thom watched him. "What?"

"The vellum was once a display item. The invoice language may matter."

"Everything matters after it is too late."

"Not too late."

Thom looked out the window at the canal. A narrow boat moved under the bridge, its engine coughing. "They believe I took money. People I have known for years looked at me and left room for it."

Mira's voice was firm but not unkind. "Fear makes bad accountants."

"So does poverty. The trust needs money. Everyone knows I argued with Maren about the sale. Everyone knows I said we should hear the estate offer. Now I look bought."

"Who is Maren Vale?"

"Deputy chair. She covered when I was in hospital. She wants partnership with private capital. Says heritage must be practical."

"Did she give Pike access?"

"Maybe. She says she followed procedure. Maren always says procedure when she means decision."

Elian wrote the name. Maren Vale. He disliked adding suspects because suspicion could become a cheap solvent, dissolving trust without revealing truth. Still, the access path mattered.

Mira asked for emails, text messages, trustee minutes. Thom had brought copies on a flash drive and printed the most important pages. He had prepared for defense like a man assembling sandbags in rain.

"One more thing," he said. "The anonymous complaint quotes from a storage inventory only trustees have."

"Can you prove that?"

"I know the wording. 'Loose vellum fragments, educational display.' I wrote that line from Ada's old list. It was in the internal drive."

Elian felt the threads tighten. Educational display. Loose vellum. The false patent support. "When was that inventory created?"

"During roof repair. Before my chairmanship. I updated it."

"Did it include location?"

"Yes. Service cupboard behind platform."

Mira exchanged a look with Elian. The cupboard with the false back and torn blue scrap. Anyone with the internal inventory could have found it. Anyone with access could have removed what Ada hid.

Thom finally drank his tea, grimaced at its coldness, and set it down. "Tell me the truth. Am I useful to you or only damaged?"

"Both," Mira said.

Elian added, "Damage is not guilt."

Thom nodded slowly. The distinction seemed to give him something small to hold.

When they left the cafe, the wind had risen and the factory chimney cut a dark line into the sky. Thom did not try the gate. He stood across the street and looked at the building as if it had turned its face from him. Elian wanted to promise return, restoration, vindication. He did none of those things.

"Keep your phone on," Mira said. "We will need you."

Need was not comfort, but it was better than pity. Thom accepted it and walked away along the canal, keys no longer in his pocket, memory still in his hands.

Chapter 20: Cleared Room

The storeroom was empty in a way that made emptiness feel performed. Elian reached it just after eight, called by Otto, who had found the door open and the shelves naked under fluorescent light. The room had held gauges, ledgers, cracked wooden patterns, broken display mounts, and the kind of industrial leftovers that become precious only when someone decides they are inconvenient. Now it held dust, screw holes, and four rectangles on the floor where crates had stood.

Mira arrived ten minutes later with wet hair and no patience. "Who authorized this?"

"Cleanup," said Maren Vale from the doorway.

She was neat, composed, and dressed in a dark suit that looked expensive without admitting it. Her hair was silver at the temples, her voice low. She carried a ring of trust keys and the confidence of a person who had never misplaced blame.

Otto turned on her. "Cleanup is when dirt leaves. This is memory leaving."

"The room was unsafe," Maren said. "Loose materials, fire load, pest damage. We have insurer concerns."

"At night?" Mira asked.

"Contractors came when available."

"During a preservation order?"

Maren's expression did not change, but her eyes moved to the clipboard in Mira's hand. "No protected engine components were removed."

"Related records and materials are protected."

"These were debris."

Elian knelt by the threshold. The dust there had been disturbed not by sweeping but by scraping. Pale powder clung in the seam between floorboards. He took a clean sample envelope from his kit.

Celia arrived with her field case, summoned by a photograph. She did not greet anyone. She crouched beside Elian, touched the powder with a soft brush, and looked at the bristles under a hand lens.

"Vellum dust," she said.

Maren folded her arms. "That seems unlikely."

"Unlikelihood is not a test result."

Mira stepped closer. "Who were the contractors?"

"A storage firm."

"Name."

"I will provide it."

"Now."

Maren hesitated just long enough to become interesting. Then she gave the name. Mira wrote it down and immediately called the number. It rang to voicemail.

Elian examined the shelving. One shelf had a fresh gouge at the edge, as if a crate had been dragged quickly. The wall behind it showed a clean rectangle, protected from dust by something recently removed. He photographed everything: floor, shelves, threshold, door lock, fluorescent tubes, Maren's polished shoes standing just outside the powder line.

"What was stored here?" he asked.

Maren looked toward Otto, as if hoping he would answer in anger.

He did, but accurately. "Old display mounts. Pattern boxes. A crate marked D-14 if my memory is not lying. Projector stands. Broken glass from the flood. Ledgers from stores."

"D-14?" Mira asked.

Elian wrote it beside the infrared inventory number. 72-D-14. The room seemed to tilt.

Maren's composure finally showed a crack. "Many crates had old numbers."

"Then this one will be easy to trace," Mira said.

The police arrived because Mira had called them before entering, a habit Elian appreciated more each day. The responding officer looked around with the weary caution of someone asked to distinguish crime from committee work. Mira handed him the preservation order. Celia explained the dust. Otto explained the room with too much history and not enough brevity.

Jarin Pike called it cleanup by email within twenty minutes. His message said the estate had no involvement and urged all parties to avoid inflammatory allegations. It arrived before anyone had accused the estate, which made it useful.

Celia sealed the dust sample. "Someone worked here."

"Or destroyed what worked," Elian said.

The empty room had the loudness of a confession without grammar. It did not yet say who. It said hurry. It said knowledge. It said the false vellum had been prepared or at least stripped near the very machine it was meant to steal.

Mira stood in the center of the room and looked at Maren. "No further movement of materials. Not a box. Not a broom. If the insurer complains, send them to me."

Maren's mouth tightened. "You do not run this trust."

"No," Mira said. "But the court currently runs your discretion."

Otto made a sound dangerously close to approval.

As they left, Elian looked back at the bare shelves. Absence had texture now: dust, gouges, numbers, timing. The forger had tried to clear a room. Instead they had furnished the case with urgency.

Chapter 21: Dust

Celia processed the dust in a temporary workspace on the factory office table because the sample was too suggestive to wait for the studio. She laid down clean paper, labeled slides, and made everyone stand back except Elian, who was allowed near because he knew how not to breathe on evidence. Otto objected to being treated like weather. Mira told him weather was often disruptive.

Under the portable microscope, the pale powder became a field of fragments. Calfskin fibers, translucent and twisted. Pumice particles, angular and gray. Brown ink flakes, thin as burnt sugar. Celia adjusted focus and let Elian look.

"Scraping debris," she said.

"From our vellum?" Mira asked.

"Likely from a vellum support prepared in the same way. I will compare fiber and pigment. But the mixture is consistent with removal of old writing from animal skin."

Otto leaned over the table despite being told not to. "They scraped it here?"

"Someone scraped vellum here," Celia said. "The rest awaits proof."

Elian thought of the storeroom at night. A lamp. A blade. A sheet pinned flat. The forger shaving away a display label while the real engine stood across the hall under glass. The intimacy of the insult struck him harder than he expected.

"They did not use a distant studio," he said.

"Why would that matter?" Thom asked from the doorway.

He had come despite suspension because Mira had called him as a witness to the room's prior state. He looked at the table but did not enter without permission.

"It means access," Elian said. "Comfort. Knowledge of rooms. Confidence no one would interrupt."

Mira added, "And connection to whoever cleared the room."

Celia transferred a portion of dust into a vial for laboratory comparison. "There is pumice. Used for abrasion. Not ordinary storage dust. Also small particles of brown ink similar in color to the erased undertext."

"Could old display materials shed like this?" Maren asked from the hall.

No one had invited her in, but she remained close enough to hear. Celia looked at her. "Old materials shed from decay. This is cutting and abrasion."

Maren did not answer.

Elian photographed the slide through the microscope. The image was not dramatic to an untrained eye. It would look like debris, because it was debris. But debris had grammar. Vellum fibers from scraping did not gather at a threshold unless work had been done and hurriedly cleaned. Pumice did not fall from honest shelves in useful company with ink.

Mira's phone buzzed. "The storage firm denies attending last night."

Maren's face changed. Not much. Enough.

"Who moved the materials?" Mira asked.

"I was told they came," Maren said.

"By whom?"

"Pike's office coordinated estimates. I assumed..."

She stopped. The word assumed had opened beneath her feet.

Otto's anger sharpened. "You gave estate people keys."

"Temporary access for assessment."

"To trust rooms under court order?"

"They said the order concerned the engine."

Mira's voice was quiet. "Did you read it?"

Maren looked at the floor. That was answer enough.

Thom, still at the doorway, said, "You suspended me for selling records while you handed them a key."

Maren turned toward him. "I tried to save the trust."

"From what? Ownership?"

The room held too many injuries at once. Mira intervened before the argument consumed the evidence. "We need a written statement from you, Ms. Vale. Now. Every communication with Pike's office. Every key issued. Every room accessed."

Maren nodded once, the movement stiff.

Celia sealed the last slide. "The dust goes to the lab. I can have preliminary comparison by tomorrow."

Elian looked again through the microscope. The fragments lay lit and enlarged, small casualties of a document forced to pretend. He felt the insult before the anger, then the anger after all. Not hot. Durable.

Outside the office, the factory hall was quiet. The engine behind glass had not moved. Yet the case around it was accelerating. False documents were no longer arriving from nowhere. They had a room, a threshold, a key path, and people who had mistaken cooperation for stewardship.

Mira gathered the statements. "We go back to Quill."

"With dust," Otto said.

"With evidence."

"Dust can be evidence."

Celia closed her case. "Most evidence is dust if you wait long enough."

No one argued. The factory had begun to yield what it knew, in particles small enough to ride a careless sleeve and strong enough, perhaps, to stop a sale.

Chapter 22: Watermark

The provenance letter returned to court inside a clear sleeve, looking thinner than its own claims. Judge Quill had ordered production of originals or a sworn explanation for their absence. Pike produced the letter and no explanation worth the name. Ravel called it a family copy, a preservation copy, and at one point a contemporaneous transcription, which caused the judge to remove her glasses.

"Mr. Ravel," she said, "those are three different things."

"The estate's position is that the document records an older truth."

"Documents do not become true because they are useful."

Mira rose with the watermark enlargement. March & Fenn Archival Bond, founded 1964. Letter dated 1938. Ink inconsistent with age. Fold pattern fresh. No chain of custody. No original from which this alleged copy had been made.

Ravel attempted dignity. "Families often preserve documents by copying them."

"Replacement of what?" Mira asked.

There was no answer, only procedural fog. Ravel spoke of private papers, inherited desks, wartime disruptions, and the difficulty of reconstructing every household decision across generations. Some of it was reasonable in isolation. None of it explained why a document first described as original had become a series of adjectives with no parent.

Judge Quill cut through it. "Produce the original or stop using the letter as if it were one."

Pike sat behind counsel, expression blank. His pen moved in small strokes. Elian wondered whether he was taking notes or drawing escape routes.

Mira then introduced the amended probate inventory and the microfilm copy. Held in trust before alteration. Held by estate after. No ledger entry. No authorization. The judge studied the images side by side for a long moment.

"Who accessed the probate file?" she asked.

"Pike's firm requested it last month," Mira said. "Before the sale agreement was finalized."

Ravel stood. "There is no evidence my client altered a court record."

"No one has said your client held the pen," Judge Quill replied. "I asked who accessed the file."

The distinction landed. Ravel sat.

Elian had been in enough hearings to know when a room changed temperature. Judges rarely announced belief. They adjusted the weight they gave silence. Pike's silence had grown heavier.

Celia testified briefly about the watermark and paper. She avoided flourish. "The sheet cannot predate the mill that made it. The ink shows no characteristics consistent with a 1938 original. It may be a later copy, but there is no physical evidence establishing what it copies."

"Could it copy a genuine lost letter?" Ravel asked.

"Many things could exist unseen. My evidence concerns this sheet."

"So you cannot exclude the possibility."

"I cannot exclude a document you have not produced."

Judge Quill wrote that down.

After the hearing, in the courthouse corridor, Ravel approached Mira with a settlement suggestion: standstill, shared display, private ownership unresolved, no admissions. Mira listened without expression.

"The trust keeps the engine?" she asked.

"For now."

"The sale withdrawn?"

"Paused."

"False documents withdrawn?"

"Disputed documents."

"Then no."

Ravel looked tired. "You may win less than you think."

"That is true in most litigation," Mira said. "It does not make your offer better."

He left them by the stairwell. Otto, who had attended despite being told his testimony was not needed that day, watched him go.

"He knows the letter is bad," Otto said.

"He knows the judge knows," Elian answered. "That is different."

Mira checked her phone. "Quill expanded preservation. All trust records, storage areas, appraiser communications, and conservation materials. No access without written agreement."

"Maren?"

"Providing statement this afternoon."

Elian looked back toward the courtroom doors. The watermark issue had seemed almost comic at first: a paper mill founded after the letter's date. But comedy had its place in fraud. Bad homework revealed haste. Haste revealed pressure. Pressure revealed value.

Outside, the courthouse steps were dry for once. Sun struck the stone and made every cigarette end visible. Mira stood in the light, reading the order again.

"The fog is thinning," she said.

"Fog usually does when paper catches fire."

"Please do not say that near probate records."

They walked back toward the archive with the new order in Mira's file and the false letter reduced to what it had always been: not provenance, but performance.

Chapter 23: Crate

The number on the erased vellum border, 72-D-14, led first to a dead catalogue, then to a retired museum registrar in a bungalow full of ferns, and finally to a crate receipt from a conservation workshop outside the city. The workshop occupied a former dairy, whitewashed and discreet, with frosted windows and a brass bell by the door. It smelled of varnish, linen, and money trying not to announce itself.

Its owner, Leonard Greaves, received Elian and Mira in a reception room where gilt frames leaned against walls like aristocrats awaiting judgment. He was narrow, soft-spoken, and wore magnifying glasses on a cord around his neck.

"We do not discuss client material," he said.

Mira handed him a copy of Judge Quill's order. "Now you do."

Greaves read it slowly. "This concerns the Harrick engine?"

"It concerns documents and materials related to it."

"We have not received engine records."

Elian looked down. A brass shaving lay caught in the coir mat by the door. It was small, curled, and bright at one edge.

"You repair machines too?" he asked.

"Frames," Greaves said too quickly. "Clock cases. Scientific instruments sometimes. Cosmetic stabilization."

"May I see your intake log?"

Greaves looked at Mira, who looked at the order. He led them through a corridor into the workroom. Benches held frames, paperweights, a cracked barometer case, and a wooden transit box. At the far end, beneath a cloth, something angular sat on a padded trolley.

Elian did not move toward it. He had learned the value of letting silence invite mistakes.

Mira photographed the visitor book. Jarin Pike had signed in under an estate purchase order on the date between the unlogged factory visit and the vellum's appearance. A second entry used the initials L.C., identified as "materials consultant." The address line was blank.

"What was delivered?" Mira asked.

"Display materials for assessment."

"From where?"

"The estate."

"The estate did not possess trust display materials."

Greaves swallowed. "I was told title was under review."

"That phrase has done a great deal of work in this matter."

Elian asked about crate 72-D-14. Greaves denied recognizing it until Mira placed the retired registrar's receipt on the table. Then he adjusted his glasses, buying seconds.

"We received an old display vellum and mount fragments," he said. "For proposed exhibition reproduction."

"Where is it?"

"Returned."

"To whom?"

"Mr. Pike's courier."

"Was it intact?"

"It had degraded inscriptions."

"Was it scraped?"

Greaves's face closed. "We cleaned surface accretions."

Elian stepped closer to the bench. On a tray lay pumice powder, two micro-scalpels, and brown ink tests on vellum scraps. He did not touch them. He photographed from where he stood.

"Surface accretions shaped like words?" he asked.

Greaves said nothing.

From the far trolley came a faint smell of oil, wrong in a room of frames. Elian looked at the cloth. "What is under there?"

"A private instrument case."

Mira's voice hardened. "Lift the cloth."

Greaves hesitated. Then he did. Beneath it lay a padded crate containing a brass gear, a mounting bracket, and several steel fasteners. Not the Marrow Engine's head assembly, but parts of its periphery or models made from it. Each was tagged with an estate purchase order.

Elian photographed the gear from three angles. "These are not display vellum."

"They were provided for dimensional reference."

"By whom?"

"Mr. Pike's office arranged delivery."

"From the factory?"

Greaves's silence answered.

Mira called Judge Quill's clerk from the workroom. While she spoke, Elian examined the gear without touching it. The keyway showed chatter marks, irregular and human. Otto would know.

Greaves finally said, "I was told the trust had consented."

"By Pike?"

"By his consultant."

"Name?"

"Lena Corb."

The appraiser. The clean shoes. The quiet photographs during inspection.

Mira ended the call. "No materials leave this workshop. The court will issue directions. You will preserve logs, correspondence, invoices, CCTV, courier records, and all waste from work on vellum supports."

Greaves sat down as if his knees had received news late.

Outside, the workshop yard held a row of bins. Elian looked at them and wondered what had already been carried away: scrap vellum, old labels, failed ink tests, the first versions of a lie. But the workshop had kept enough. Fraud liked professional help. Professional help liked invoices. Invoices, unlike conscience, could often be subpoenaed.

Chapter 24: Brass Gear

Otto recognized the gear before Elian finished showing him the photograph. They were in the factory canteen, where the tables still bore scratches from decades of lunch tins and arguments. Rain ticked against the high windows. Mira stood by the tea urn, listening.

"That is ours," Otto said.

"How can you tell?"

"I cut that keyway after lunch on a Tuesday with a dull broach. See the chatter?"

Elian slid the enlarged photograph across the table. To him the keyway had looked irregular. To Otto it was autobiography.

"Here," Otto said, tapping the print. "Tool grabbed because I should have sharpened it. Ada would have sworn at me. She was dead by then, so I swore for her. We needed the gear in before a school group on Wednesday."

"Could there be another gear with similar marks?"

"There could be another man who made the same bad decision with the same dull tool, but I would like to meet him and apologize."

Mira noted the statement. "Was this gear part of the engine?"

"Spare transfer gear. Kept in the storeroom. Not installed after the eighties rebuild. We used it for demonstrations sometimes, to show tooth profiles."

"So it was trust property."

"It was factory property before the trust had a letterhead."

Elian showed him the bracket from the workshop crate. Otto's face darkened. "Reference bracket. We made it when the council wanted a teaching model. Also ours."

"Could those parts help someone draw the false vellum?"

"They could help someone pretend to understand. The gear dimensions, the bracket shape, tooth count. But without the service notebook, they still guess sequence."

"And guessed wrong," Elian said.

Otto leaned back, breathing through his nose. "They carried pieces out of this building."

"Likely."

"While Maren handed them keys and Thom took blame."

Mira said, "We do not yet know Maren's state of mind."

"I know her state of keys."

That was difficult to dispute.

They walked to the storeroom after the rain eased. The empty shelves seemed worse now that the workshop photographs had given absence names. Otto showed where the spare gear had hung on a peg board, wrapped in oiled cloth. The outline remained, a clean circle against dusty wood.

Elian photographed the peg board. "Can you make a declaration identifying the gear?"

"I can make a declaration, a speech, or a curse."

"Start with declaration."

"Coward."

Mira almost smiled. "Declarations survive cross-examination better."

Otto dictated while Elian typed on a laptop borrowed from Thom. Date of manufacture. Purpose. Storage location. Distinguishing chatter in keyway. Last observed before estate access. His voice grew steadier as detail accumulated. Memory became evidence when pinned to particulars.

Halfway through, Maren appeared at the door. She looked older than the previous day. "I gave my statement."

Mira closed the laptop slightly. "Thank you."

Maren looked at Otto. "I did not know they would remove parts."

"You did not ask hard enough."

"No." The admission cost her. "I didn't."

Elian watched her. Shame could be genuine without being complete. "Who suggested the storage room cleanup?"

"Pike. Through Lena Corb. They said loose materials were attracting pests and threatening insurance. They offered to arrange assessment at no charge."

"And the night clearing?"

"I authorized removal to a holding facility. I thought the storage firm came. I did not know Pike's courier took anything."

Mira's voice stayed neutral. "We will need all communications with Corb."

"I have given them."

"All."

Maren looked at the empty room. "I thought partnership meant help."

Otto said, "Help does not usually arrive with a false patent."

No one softened that for her.

Later, Elian sent Otto's declaration to Mira and attached the workshop photographs. A small human imperfection had become a signature stronger than a seal. The dull broach, the Tuesday lunch, the chatter in brass. Fraud preferred grand documents; truth often survived in tool marks.

As they locked the storeroom, Otto touched the empty peg where the gear had hung. "Ada would have hidden that too if she had known."

"She hid enough," Elian said.

"Not enough yet."

"Then we keep looking."

Otto nodded. Outside, the rain had stopped. The factory roof dripped steadily into the yard, counting seconds no court had ordered but everyone now needed.

Chapter 25: Frozen

Judge Quill froze the sale on a Friday morning that had been meant to close it. Her order arrived by email first, then by courier, then in the voices of trustees who gathered in the demonstration hall because the office no longer felt large enough for fear. No transfer, sale, removal, alteration, or encumbrance of the Marrow Engine or associated materials pending full evidentiary hearing. The words were formal. Their effect was not.

Someone cheered. Someone else began to cry and pretended to cough. Thom stood near the back, still suspended, hands in his coat pockets. Maren stood on the opposite side of the hall, alone under the payroll clock display.

"This is not victory," Mira said before celebration could grow legs. "It is time."

Otto lifted his cane. "Time beats sale."

"For now."

Elian watched the trustees absorb the distinction. They wanted relief, and deserved some, but the case had become more dangerous because it had begun to matter. Pike's commission, the estate's expectations, the collector's plans, the investor behind the collector if one existed: all of them had been halted by paper. Halted things often pushed back.

The collector withdrew his public congratulations by noon. His statement praised patience, preservation, and due process with the bland sincerity of a man whose lawyers had found a cliff in the path. Newspapers picked up the dispute by afternoon. LOCAL ENGINE SALE PAUSED AMID DOCUMENT QUESTIONS. The first article misspelled vellum. The second called the engine a "Victorian computer," which was not entirely wrong and not entirely useful.

Thom approached Elian after the trustees dispersed. "We have proof."

"We have evidence."

"Enough?"

"Never enough until the judge says so."

He nodded, disappointed but not surprised. Suspension had taught him caution faster than advice could have.

Mira joined them with her phone still in hand. "Pike's counsel wants a meeting."

"Settlement?" Elian asked.

"Containment. They will offer shared custody, nondisparagement, and a private authenticity review."

Otto, passing behind them, said, "Private means dark."

"Usually," Mira replied.

They refused the meeting unless it was recorded and all documents were produced first. Pike declined by not answering. His silence spread through the afternoon like damp.

At four, Celia's comparison report arrived. The dust from the storeroom contained fibers and ink particles consistent with the scraped area of the vellum. Not definitive identity, she wrote, but strongly supportive of a common preparation process. Pumice composition matched material found on the workshop bench in photographs. Elian read the paragraph twice, feeling the room narrow around it.

"The false patent was prepared inside the factory or from material worked there," he said.

Mira corrected him automatically. "Likely."

"Likely is doing good work."

"Likely is admissible if properly housed."

Outside, rain began again, tapping the factory windows like small type. The hall lights reflected in the glass around the engine. For once, the machine seemed less like an exhibit and more like a center of gravity. People moved around it differently now. They looked at it not as a thing saved or sold but as a witness that had survived long enough to be asked better questions.

Maren crossed the hall to Thom. Elian could not hear the first words. He saw Thom's shoulders stiffen. Then Maren handed him a folder. He did not take it. She placed it on the bench beside him and walked away. Thom opened it after a minute.

"What is it?" Elian asked when Thom came over.

"Emails. More than she gave Mira. Forwarded from Pike's assistant. They mention a conservation broker."

Mira's eyes sharpened. "Name?"

"Voss knows him, apparently. Not Celia's relative. Broker called Niall Rusk."

Elian wrote it down. Another professional. Another invoice waiting to betray someone.

The frozen sale had not stopped motion. It had changed its direction. Documents began to surface because fear shifted sides. People who had been careless became eager to be less guilty than the next person. It was not noble, but it was useful.

As evening came, the trustees left one by one. Otto stayed to check the case seals. Mira stayed to draft the next request. Elian stayed because leaving felt premature.

Through the glass, the Marrow Engine held its position in the dim hall. It had waited through bankruptcy, closure, neglect, and attempted conversion into an asset. It could wait through litigation. Waiting, Elian thought, was not passive when done under guard.

Chapter 26: Dead Collector

Jarin Pike blamed a deceased collector on Monday, which was bold even by the standards of a case that had already asked a paper mill to forgive time. The affidavit arrived with exhibits and careful sorrow. Alistair Fenn, now dead for twelve years, had allegedly handled the vellum drawing, advised the family informally, and created or preserved the disputed letter. Any irregularities, Pike implied without quite saying, belonged to a man no longer available for clarification.

Iona read the affidavit in the archive cafe and put it down as if it smelled.

"Convenient man," she said. "Quiet in cross-examination."

Mira had already requested Fenn's archive from Halver University. By midafternoon, an archivist named Pru Calder replied with scans from appointment books, acquisition ledgers, and correspondence indexes. Fenn had been meticulous. He had recorded lunch with dentists, quarrels with framers, and the purchase of a cracked barometer he later regretted in three separate notes.

He had never visited Harrick Works. He had never acquired vellum drawings related to mechanical computation. He had, in fact, written in one letter that calculating machines were "commercial contraptions without aesthetic consolation."

Elian admired the phrase despite its foolishness.

"The dead deserve better alibis," Mira said.

They called Pru Calder on speaker. Her voice crackled with archival cheer. "Fenn was many things, but silent about acquisitions was not one of them. If he had touched a Harrick vellum, he would have invoiced, indexed, and complained about storage."

"Could papers be missing?" Mira asked.

"Always. But his mechanical holdings are almost nonexistent. He collected scientific illustration, not machines. Botanical plates, anatomical teaching sheets, astronomical charts. The display vellum you describe sounds more like something deaccessioned from an educational collection in the seventies."

"Do you have records for 72-D-14?"

A pause. Paper moved near the phone. "Not in Fenn's personal archive. But the university museum used D numbers for demonstration material before reorganization. I can check."

Elian and Mira exchanged a look. Another door.

While they waited, Elian reviewed Pike's affidavit. Its sentences were careful but overbuilt. The dead collector appeared at every gap where a living witness would have been useful. He had advised, stored, copied, remembered, perhaps misplaced. He was less a person than a bridge made of fog.

"Pike is retreating from the family desk story," Elian said.

"Yes," Mira said. "Now the document passed through Fenn, which explains the bad paper if framed as later copying."

"But creates a new chain he cannot support."

"Fraud often expands when cornered."

Pru called back at six. The university museum had deaccessioned a group of demonstration vellums in 1981, including item 72-D-14: "Harrick Works gear-sequence display sheet, vellum, damaged, mount removed." It had gone to a surplus educational materials sale. Buyer listed: Greaves Conservation, acting as agent.

The workshop.

Mira asked for certified records. Pru promised them by morning and sounded delighted to be useful in a way that might irritate private dealers.

Elian leaned back. The vellum's career now had shape. Display sheet. Deaccession. Conservation workshop. Decades later, estate dispute. Scrape. New title. False patent. The dead collector had no place in it.

"Why use Fenn at all?" Iona asked.

"Because his surname matches the paper mill," Elian said. "Someone saw March & Fenn and made a story from half a watermark."

Iona closed her eyes briefly. "That is offensively stupid."

"Useful, though."

Mira drafted a response to the affidavit. No Fenn records supporting Harrick material. Evidence Fenn disfavored calculating machines. University deaccession record connecting inventory number to Greaves. Demand for communications concerning Fenn before affidavit preparation.

The cafe emptied around them. Chairs went up on tables. The staff began wiping counters with lemon cleaner that fought the smell of old paper and lost honorably.

Elian added another failed provenance to the file. The case no longer depended on proving one document false. It showed a series of invented histories, each created after the last one cracked. That sequence mattered. Honest provenance might have gaps. False provenance kept changing its shoes.

When they finally left, Iona walked with them to the archive door. "Fenn did write one good thing," she said.

"What?"

"He called bad restoration 'an insult performed with clean hands.'"

Elian thought of the scraped vellum, the polished shoes, the workshop bench. "He was not always wrong."

Chapter 27: Broker

Niall Rusk kept no public office, only a website with photographs of manuscripts under flattering light and a phone number answered by an assistant who gave nothing away except contempt. Celia knew his name and disliked it immediately.

"He can age materials beautifully," she said.

"Legally?" Mira asked.

"Beauty is not the legal part."

They met in Celia's studio because she had files on prior cases involving questionable restoration, and because she did not want Rusk's name spoken near the vellum without context. She pulled a folder from a locked cabinet and laid out articles, invoices from unrelated disputes, and a conference program where Rusk had once presented on "sympathetic integration of replacement surfaces."

Elian read the title. "That sounds like forgery wearing a clean shirt."

"It can be legitimate," Celia said. "Replacing losses in a binding, toning repair paper, integrating a fill. But Rusk understands the edge where restoration becomes persuasion."

Mira had obtained estate accounts through the expanded order. Payments to Rusk appeared as "framing consultation" and "display stabilization." The dates matched the storeroom clearing, the workshop delivery, and the vellum preparation. The amounts were high enough to purchase discretion but not high enough to purchase loyalty if prison entered the conversation.

"Who authorized payment?" Elian asked.

"Estate account. Pike approval."

"Purpose?"

"Framing services."

Celia snorted. "No frame costs that unless it includes a conscience."

Mira requested bank records and underlying invoices. Ravel objected before reading the request, which saved everyone time. Judge Quill ordered production by the end of the week with a line about euphemistic billing that Elian underlined for pleasure.

Rusk's invoices arrived badly scanned and heavily described. "Surface harmonization." "Historic toning." "Title panel integration." "Demonstration support adaptation." The last phrase made Mira sit very still.

"Adaptation," she said.

"There is your career change," Elian replied.

Celia read the technical notes with increasing anger. "He used humidity cycling. Warm press. Pigment testing. He knew exactly what he was doing."

"Does he say patent?"

"No. He says client-supplied inscription text."

"Client?"

"Pike's office, by purchase order."

They subpoenaed Rusk for a statement. He responded through counsel that all work was decorative, non-deceptive, and performed for proposed exhibition. Mira placed that beside the estate's use of the vellum as title evidence. The contradiction did not require ornament.

That evening, Elian and Mira went through bank transfers in the archive conference room. Numbers made a different kind of prose. Estate account to Rusk. Rusk to Greaves. Greaves to courier. Pike's firm reimbursed for "valuation expenses." Behind each payment sat a movement of material or labor.

"Fraud is administrative," Mira said.

"Most human vice is, once successful."

She stretched her hands. "Do you ever say anything cheerful?"

"The bank records arrived in searchable PDF."

"That is not cheer. That is mercy."

They worked until security reminded them of closing. Elian packed the documents slowly. The broker's role clarified motive and method but not yet the highest instruction. Pike could claim he sought an exhibition facsimile and others misused it. Rusk could claim he followed text provided. Greaves could claim conservation. Each person had left himself a small door. The case required closing them not with outrage but with sequence.

At the exit, Celia handed Elian a sealed envelope. "Rusk once taught a workshop on aging vellum repairs. Attendee list includes Lena Corb."

The appraiser again.

Mira took the envelope as if it were fragile and dangerous, which it was. "When?"

"Three years ago."

Elian looked back toward the studio where the false vellum lay under guard. The document had not been made by a lone hand in secret. It had been assembled through skills distributed among people paid to know less than they did.

Outside, the night air was cold and clean. Mira put the envelope in her case. "Tomorrow we ask about Corb."

"She will say valuation."

"Then we ask what she valued: history, or the price of making it."

They walked to the station under streetlights. The city had the hard shine it gets after rain, every surface reflecting something slightly broken. Elian thought of Rusk's phrase, title panel integration, and felt again the peculiar anger of his profession. Words could be tools. They could also be soft gloves over theft.

Chapter 28: Slide

The university audiovisual basement was exactly where Iona had promised: under a lecture hall, past two locked doors, beside a boiler room, and governed by a technician named Mr. Pell who believed all requests were attacks on order. He denied the mechanical history overflow existed until Iona recited three shelf marks, a retired professor's middle name, and the serial number of a slide projector purchased in 1969.

"You have been here before," Elian said.

"I have enemies in many basements," Iona replied.

Mr. Pell led them to a room lined with metal cabinets. The air smelled of dust, acetate, and warm wiring. Boxes of slides sat in drawers labeled by department and then by whatever system had briefly pleased a student assistant decades earlier. Mechanical History Overflow occupied two cabinets and part of a sink.

They searched for Professor Kline's 1972 lecture on the Marrow Engine rebuild. Iona worked fast, muttering at misfiled labels. Elian checked each box against the article she had given him. Mira photographed drawer fronts for chain of custody because by now no one trusted labels to remain labels.

After two hours, Elian found a gray box marked KLINE - HARRICK WORKS - COMPUTATION DEMO. The tape had dried and curled at the ends.

Iona took it from him with something close to reverence. "Do not drop that."

"I was raised properly."

"Everyone in this case claims that."

They used a light table. Slide after slide appeared: factory exterior, workers around the engine, close-ups of gear trains, Ada Marr pointing at a board with a steel rule, Professor Kline in a brown suit standing in front of a projected drawing. The slide was faded, the color shifted toward red, but behind him the true patent drawing filled the screen.

There was the missing shaft.

Elian bent closer. The outer frame resembled the vellum. The title block was different. The mechanism was clear enough to show the true train, not the false rebuilt copy. If the forger had seen a reproduction of this slide or the original drawing it depicted, he had copied the frame and ignored or replaced the difficult interior.

"There," Iona said. "The real design had been public once."

Mira's voice was low. "Can we digitize this?"

Mr. Pell, who had been pretending not to care, said, "Not on that machine. It scratches."

They all looked at him.

He sighed. "I have a better scanner upstairs."

For once, bureaucracy became useful. Mr. Pell scanned the slide at high resolution while Iona hovered and criticized dust handling. The image emerged slowly on the monitor. The true gear path sharpened. Kline's brown suit remained unfortunate. The drawing behind him was a gift.

Elian compared it to the vellum image on his laptop. Same decorative border. Similar scale. Different title text. Different central train. The false drawing had borrowed authority from a real visual tradition, then substituted the rebuilt machine's visible parts for the original working design.

"They saw enough to know what to imitate," he said.

"But not enough to understand what mattered," Iona added.

Mira requested a certification from the university for the slide's custody. Mr. Pell produced a form as if personally offended by the need but filled it out perfectly. He included cabinet, drawer, box label, scan time, and his full name in block capitals.

"Thank you," Mira said.

"Do not lose it," he replied.

"That is our general theme."

Before leaving, Elian looked again through the remaining slides. Ada Marr appeared in three. In one, she stood beside an open service panel, hand raised to stop someone outside the frame. Her expression was stern, almost amused. Behind her, on a small ledge, sat what might have been a black notebook.

He showed Otto the scan later. The old man looked at it for a long time.

"That's the book," he said.

"Where is she standing?"

"Head assembly side. Oil cabinet behind her. But that ledge is not there now."

"Removed?"

"Covered. During the museum refit."

The slide had given them more than a drawing. It had shown a place time had hidden under later carpentry.

Elian printed the image and marked the ledge. Ordinary neglect had misplaced the real design. Fraud had made it valuable again. Now neglect, in the form of a forgotten slide, had answered fraud with the quiet persistence of a box in a basement.

Chapter 29: Overlay

Elian built the overlay in the archive imaging room, where the monitors were calibrated and the chairs were designed by someone who disliked spines. He placed the high-resolution scan of Kline's slide beneath the vellum image and adjusted scale using the decorative border. The frames met almost perfectly. The false title sat over the true lecture title like a mask.

Mira stood behind him with a mug of coffee gone untouched. Iona leaned in too close. Otto sat across the room because he claimed computers made people careless and then asked every thirty seconds whether the lines matched.

"They match at the outer frame," Elian said.

"We heard that," Otto replied.

"You asked again."

"Because answers improve when repeated by people who are sure."

Elian reduced the opacity of the vellum layer. The true gear train emerged beneath the false one. For a moment both machines occupied the screen: one capable, one impossible. The missing shaft glowed in green. The false bracket sat in red, shifted from the rebuilt engine. Where the forger had copied the border, lines aligned. Where he had drawn mechanism, error bloomed.

"They saw the machine," Otto said. "They did not understand it."

"They understood ownership," Elian answered.

"That is not the same craft."

Mira took screenshots. "This will help the judge."

Iona nodded. "Visual argument. Simple enough for court and precise enough for truth."

They created three exhibits: border alignment, mechanism divergence, and functional lock point. Elian avoided dramatic colors. Red for vellum, blue for verified drawing, green for living engine. No shadows, no arrows shaped like accusations. Let the geometry do the work.

As he worked, he felt the strange satisfaction of making deception visible. The overlay did not shout. It simply allowed two claims to stand in the same space until one failed.

Mira's phone rang. She listened, said little, and ended the call. "Rusk will give a statement."

"Voluntarily?" Iona asked.

"Under threat of contempt, which has a way of awakening civic spirit."

"Will he name Pike?"

"He will name whoever makes him least alone."

Otto stood and came to the screen. His cane tapped twice. "Can you overlay the engine photograph too?"

"Yes, but the perspective differs."

"Try."

Elian loaded the inspection photograph and corrected for angle. It was imperfect but good enough to show the visible bracket and gear positions copied into the vellum. The forger had drawn from the rebuilt machine, not from the original patent. The overlay made Otto's first instinct measurable.

The old man stared. "That's my filed corner."

On the screen, the small bevel aligned exactly.

Elian saved the image. "Human imperfection, exhibit C."

Otto did not smile. "Ada used to say the machine would tell on us. Every bad cut, every rushed fit."

"It is telling on someone else now."

They printed the exhibits on matte paper. The archive printer hummed and released sheet after sheet, warm and faintly chemical. Mira labeled each immediately. Iona corrected one caption where Elian had used "transmission" too loosely. Otto objected to the word "component" because it sounded like something bought in a packet. They settled on "part."

By evening, the wall of the imaging room held the case in pictures: vellum, engine, slide, overlay, dust, watermark, inventory. It was no longer a collection of suspicions. It was a map of movement. Material moved from trust to workshop. Text moved from display to patent. Ownership moved in altered records. Money moved through invoices. Error moved from surface observation into mechanical impossibility.

Elian stood back. "This is enough to understand."

Mira capped a marker. "Understanding is not always enough to win."

"No. But it prevents losing stupidly."

"I will accept that as optimism from you."

They packed the exhibits into a case. Otto lingered by the print showing Ada's slide. "That ledge," he said. "We should look tomorrow."

"Court order?"

Mira sighed. "If we are opening carpentry near the engine, yes."

"Ada would hate all this permission."

"Ada is not the one answering to Quill."

Otto grumbled but did not argue. The overlay had given them a visual proof. The slide had given them a physical lead. Somewhere behind later paneling, perhaps, the black notebook had sat in a photograph for fifty years, waiting for someone to notice not the famous machine but the small dark rectangle beside it.

Chapter 30: Oil Cabinet

Judge Quill granted permission to inspect the refitted paneling beside the head assembly with a note that read, in full, "Carefully." Mira printed the order and taped a copy to the glass case before anyone opened anything. Otto approved of the adverb but not the tape.

The ledge from the 1972 slide no longer showed. In its place stood a smooth plywood backing installed during a museum refit in the 1990s, painted the same institutional cream as the wall. Behind it, according to Otto, had been a shallow service recess above the old oil cabinet, useful for lamps, rags, and notebooks when a maintainer needed both hands.

Thom came as witness, suspension lifted provisionally after Maren's supplemental statement undermined the complaint against him. He wore the same tired suit but stood straighter. Maren did not attend. She had resigned from the deputy chair position that morning.

Celia documented the wall before removal. Elian photographed screw heads. Otto selected tools with ceremonial irritation. "If any of you say treasure, I leave."

"No one will say treasure," Mira said.

"People think old things become treasure when they stop being useful."

"We are here because this one remained useful."

That satisfied him enough to begin.

The panel came away slowly. Dust fell in a soft sheet. Behind it lay the old recess, narrower than the slide had made it appear, lined with dark wood and smelling faintly of oil. At first Elian saw only a rag, stiff with age, and a glass bottle without a stopper. Then Otto reached in and stopped.

His hand rested on something black before he drew it out.

The service notebook was smaller than Elian expected, its cover cracked, its corners dark with grease. A cotton thread held it closed. The label had nearly worn away, but under the grime he could read SERVICE HISTORY - MARROW ENGINE. Tucked beneath it lay a folded packet tied with blue thread.

No one spoke.

Otto held the notebook as if it were both tool and bone. His thumb moved over the cover once. "Ada hid it."

Mira recovered first. "Place it on the clean sheet. We document before opening."

Otto obeyed. That, more than anything, showed the gravity of the find.

Celia examined the notebook exterior. "Stable enough to open with supports. No forcing. The packet separately."

Elian photographed every stage. The first page bore Ada Marr's handwriting, sharp and forward-leaning: This book records operational maintenance of the Marrow Engine held for the Harrick Works Trust and its successors. Drawings show intention. Service shows survival.

Otto turned away for a moment. When he turned back, his eyes were wet and angry about it.

Inside were dates, repairs, replacement parts, demonstration notes, names of workers, disputes with management, and sketches in three or four hands. Otto's younger writing appeared later, less elegant but precise. The 1978 bracket repair was there, with a small drawing of the filed corner. The spare gear with the dull-broach chatter was there. The trust transfer was referenced. The blue packet was cited twice: trust copy, do not lend.

"We wait to open the packet until Celia prepares supports," Mira said.

Otto nodded. "Some discoveries need witnesses more than wonder."

Elian looked at him.

"Ada said that too," Otto muttered.

They logged the recess contents: notebook, blue packet, rag, bottle, two obsolete oil labels, a pencil worn to an inch. The pencil bore teeth marks. It was absurdly moving.

Thom read Ada's first page and sat down on the bench. "This clears me."

"It helps," Mira said.

"Let me have the sentence."

She softened. "Yes. It helps clear you."

The notebook smelled of shop oil, dust, and old paper. It carried not just evidence but continuity. Each entry had been written because someone expected a future hand to need it. That expectation had survived owners, closures, refits, appraisers, and a false patent.

Elian closed the cover gently after Celia approved. The engine stood beside them, visible through glass, quiet in the careful commotion. The black book had been less rumor than invitation, and they had finally accepted it.

Outside, rain struck the roof and ran down the old gutters. Inside, the factory held its breath around a notebook that had listened when people did not.

Chapter 31: Trust Design

Celia opened the blue packet the next morning in the archive conservation studio, under supports and cameras and the combined attention of everyone who had learned to distrust easy paper. The thread came loose without cutting. The folds relaxed slowly under humidification, not enough to flatten memory out of them, only enough to read.

Inside lay a blue-paper copy of the true patent drawing, a transfer receipt, and three pages of Ada Marr's notes. The drawing matched Iona's slide and the living engine: the correct shaft, the correct carry path, the cam profile that belonged to actual wear. Across the back, in Ada's hand, were the words: Trust copy. Do not lend to estate men.

Otto laughed once, then covered his eyes.

Elian did not touch the paper. He stood close enough to see the fibers, the faded blue, the pressure of Ada's pen. The copy was not grand. It did not have vellum's pale authority or a seal meant to impress buyers. It looked like working paper. It was therefore beautiful in a way the false document could not imitate.

Iona read the mechanism and nodded. "There. That is a machine."

Mira photographed the back note. "Ada was direct."

"Ada had time-saving virtues," Otto said.

The service notebook placed the copy in context. Entry after entry recorded parts made by factory labor after Harrick's death: replacement cams, recalibrated pawls, redesigned brackets, printer carriage repairs, gear recuts, oiling protocols, public demonstration modifications. The engine in the hall was not one inventor's relic preserved under a bell jar. It was a workshop's continuing act.

"The engine is not one man's relic," Iona said. "It is a workshop."

Otto nodded once. "Always was."

Mira marked passages relevant to title. The trust transfer receipt named operational design copies and service records as held by the workers' trust for preservation and public education. A later note recorded a family request to borrow the copy for an exhibition and Ada's refusal. Another noted that "estate representatives confuse admiration with possession."

"She knew," Thom said.

"She anticipated," Elian replied. "Knowing came later."

Thom had been reinstated by emergency trustee vote that morning, though the damage to his name would take longer than minutes to repair. He held himself carefully in the studio, as if too much relief might disturb the evidence.

Mira read the transfer receipt aloud. The language was not perfect. Old agreements rarely were. But it placed custody, maintenance, and operational design with the trust. It named the engine as a continuing educational instrument, not a saleable family ornament.

"This changes the hearing," she said.

"How much?" Thom asked.

"Enough that Pike will try to settle before it."

"Should we?"

Mira looked at the blue copy, then at the notebook, then at Thom. "Not for anything that leaves ownership unclear."

Elian turned a page of the service notebook with a support card. Ada's note near the 1978 rebuild read: Machines are made again every time they are kept alive. If title is challenged, show the service history.

He copied the sentence carefully. It was almost too good, which made him suspicious for half a second, but Ada had earned it through grease and foresight. Some sentences survived because they were true enough to be useful.

Celia prepared housing for the blue copy. "This goes into a climate box after imaging."

"And the false vellum?" Thom asked.

"Also preserved," Elian said.

Otto frowned. "Why keep the lie?"

"Because future lies study old ones. We leave warnings."

Otto considered that and accepted it with a grunt.

By afternoon, the studio table held two histories: the false vellum on old skin, and the blue copy on plain paper. One had been made to impress. The other had been made to remain. Elian knew which would matter more to a judge, and which mattered more to him. They were, for once, the same.

Chapter 32: Thom Cleared

Thom read the receipt twice before anyone asked him to speak. They were in the trust office, not the studio, because evidence had to return to people eventually or it became another kind of locked thing. The receipt lay in a protective sleeve on the table. His own signature appeared on a later transfer log, confirming that he had received the blue trust copy into storage twelve years earlier. Beneath it, in another hand, was the appraiser's signature removing the file for "review" during Pike's first visit.

Thom touched the sleeve, not the paper. "So I did not lose it."

"No," Mira said. "You signed for it before someone else took it."

Relief made him look briefly ill. He sat down hard. Blame leaving the body was not always graceful. It had weight and hooks.

Otto stood by the window, pretending to study the yard. "Told you."

"You said I forgot hinges," Thom replied.

"Different matter."

Mira spread the chain on the table: Thom receipt, appraiser removal, lack of return entry, Maren's key authorization, workshop intake, Rusk invoice. It was not complete in every criminal sense, but for the civil hearing it had force. The trust copy had been stored, removed under estate access, hidden or displaced, then replaced in the case narrative by a false vellum.

"What happens to Lena Corb?" Thom asked.

"She has been compelled to attend the hearing," Mira said. "Her counsel says she acted within valuation instructions."

"Meaning Pike."

"Meaning she would like it to mean Pike."

Elian looked through Corb's photographs from the first inspection. She had captured the engine bracket, the spare gear peg board, the service cupboard, and three images of the storeroom shelf where display materials had been kept. A valuation appraiser had photographed like someone shopping for source material.

"She knew what to collect," he said.

Mira nodded. "And Rusk knew what to do with it."

Thom rubbed his face. "Maren resigned."

"Yes."

"The trustees want her blamed for everything because that is simpler."

"Simplicity is attractive and often false."

"Did she commit anything?"

Mira chose her answer carefully. "She breached the order and her duties by giving access she did not understand. Whether she knowingly participated in fraud is another question."

Thom looked toward the hall. "She thought money would save us."

Otto turned from the window. "Money was the bait. Saving was the hook."

No one improved the sentence.

They drafted Thom's witness statement that afternoon. Elian helped with chronology, not language. Mira insisted Thom use his own words. He described taking over the trust, seeing the blue copy, recording it, allowing supervised access, entering hospital, returning to confusion, and then being accused. The statement was plain and therefore strong.

At one point Thom stopped typing. "Can I write that I was ashamed?"

Mira said, "If it is true."

"I was. Even knowing I had not sold anything. Accusation finds rooms in you."

Elian looked down at his notes. That sentence did belong in a statement.

By evening, the office felt less besieged. Not safe. Safety would come later, if at all. But the empty drawer had acquired a chain; the missing paper had returned; Thom's name had stepped out from under the weight placed on it.

Before leaving, he walked to the demonstration hall and stood before the engine. Elian followed at a distance. Otto remained in the doorway.

"I almost voted for the sale," Thom said.

"Almost is not a vote."

"It feels close."

"Close matters in mechanics. In ethics, too. But it is not the same as contact."

Thom smiled faintly. "You always talk like that?"

"Under stress."

"This whole place is stress."

The engine reflected them in the glass: three men of different ages, none of them inventors, all of them held in some relation to a machine that had outlived simpler claims. Thom straightened the notice on the case seal. It did not need straightening. He did it anyway, because care often began where usefulness ended.

Chapter 33: Heat

Celia's final vellum report arrived in sections because the truth had become too large for one polite attachment. Scrape analysis. Ink chemistry. Infrared imaging. Ultraviolet fluorescence. Fiber comparison. Heat deformation. Each section removed another possible innocence.

They gathered in the archive conference room to review it before filing. Celia stood at the screen and spoke without drama. "The vellum support is old. The title panel is not. Prior inscription was mechanically abraded. New title and claim text were added after abrasion. The support was then exposed to controlled heat and humidity to reduce contrast between old and new surfaces."

"It was cooked," Otto said.

"Gently," Celia replied.

"That does not improve the verb."

She changed slides. Magnified edge images showed collagen contraction in a pattern inconsistent with ordinary storage. The edges had tightened, drawing slightly inward. Natural aging produced uneven histories: damp corners, handling grime, insect damage, light exposure. This sheet showed a more uniform attempt to persuade.

"Artificial aging?" Mira asked.

"Yes. Not crude. But visible when compared with untreated areas under the mount line."

Elian read the conclusion. The vellum had been forced to pretend. Celia had not used those words, of course. She had written, "The object presents evidence of intentional alteration intended to modify perceived date and purpose." It was a cleaner blade.

Iona reviewed the notation appendix. "Good. You included the carry symbol chronology."

"With your citation," Celia said.

"Then it is excellent."

Mira assembled the report with Elian's mechanical statement, Iona's notation statement, Otto's declaration, Thom's chain evidence, the overlay exhibits, bank records, and workshop materials. The hearing bundle had become a brick. It would take two people to carry and, with luck, one judge to understand.

Pike's response arrived near dusk. He denied knowledge of any false use. He had commissioned a display reconstruction for potential exhibition. He had relied on professionals. He had not instructed anyone to alter court records or remove trust property. He was shocked by suggestions otherwise.

"Shock is cheap," Mira said.

"And reusable," Elian added.

Rusk's statement contradicted him within an hour. The broker admitted preparing the vellum support for "client-provided title language" and aging it to "harmonize with existing material." He claimed Pike's office represented the work as a demonstrative exhibit. But attached emails from Pike's assistant referred to "title sheet," "estate claim text," and "urgent provenance completion before closing."

"There," Mira said. "Before closing."

Elian felt no triumph. The phrase was useful, not cleansing.

Celia closed her laptop. "Will the court understand the heat issue?"

"Yes," Mira said. "But the simple point is the same: old skin does not make new words old."

Otto approved. "Say that."

"I might."

They worked late, refining statements. Elian removed adjectives from his report until only necessary ones remained. He described ratios, shafts, cam wear, bracket chronology, and the functional impossibility of the vellum design. He attached photographs with labels. He resisted the sentence about brass remembering use, then placed it nowhere. Court was not the place for every true sentence.

At ten, the archive was quiet except for the ventilation and the occasional distant clunk of pipes. Mira printed the final index. Pages slid into the tray, warm and exact.

"Do you think Pike believed he could win?" Celia asked.

"He believed everyone would be too tired to make him lose," Mira said.

That seemed right. Fraud often relied less on brilliance than exhaustion.

Elian looked at the first page of the report. The case name sat above the title: Harrick Works Trust v. Harrick Estate. It made the matter sound symmetrical. It was not. On one side stood a machine, records, workers, repair, use. On the other, a set of documents trying to become history before anyone read them closely.

When he stepped outside, the night was cold. The city smelled of wet stone. He carried a copy of Celia's heat images in his case and thought of the vellum under warmth, pressed and aged like a face taught to lie. It had been damaged, but not silenced. Under the right light, even forced things spoke.

Chapter 34: Head Assembly

The van came at midnight because guilty errands preferred hours when honest people had gone home. Otto saw its lights from the flat above the old gatehouse, where he had been sleeping badly since the sale froze. He called Mira first, then the police, then Elian, in that order, which showed he had learned something from lawyers but not too much.

Elian arrived with his coat over a sweater and his shoes not fully tied. Mira was already at the loading door, hair pinned badly, phone in one hand, court order in the other. Two uniformed officers stood beside her. Otto blocked the doorway with his cane planted like a surveyor's mark.

At the van, three men in dark jackets held tool rolls and a padded crate. Jarin Pike stood under the security light, face pale with anger made formal.

"Emergency conservation," he said.

Mira held up the order. "Emergency contempt."

"We received information the engine's head assembly is at risk from improper storage conditions."

Otto laughed. It was not a pleasant sound. "It has survived worse conditions than your concern."

Pike turned to the officers. "We are acting to preserve estate property."

"No one removes the head but a machinist and a court order," Otto said. "You have neither."

Elian moved close enough to see the tools. Socket wrenches, padded clamps, lifting straps, a custom brace. Not a general inspection kit. A removal kit. The head assembly contained the most valuable and complex portion of the engine: printer logic, carry transfer, visible intelligence. Without it the body would remain, impressive and diminished. A collector could display the brain elsewhere and leave the trust with weight.

"Who authorized this?" Mira asked.

Pike's jaw tightened. "The estate retains residual rights under dispute."

"The order says no removal."

"The order presumes stable conditions."

"Do not invent exceptions at midnight."

One of the men by the van shifted. Elian recognized him from Greaves's workshop photographs: a mechanical technician, name unknown, standing near the padded trolley. He looked less convinced than paid.

The police sergeant asked for identification, work orders, and the crate manifest. Pike objected. The sergeant did not care. That was refreshing.

Inside the loading bay, the engine hall beyond lay dark except for emergency lights. The Marrow Engine stood under glass, case seal intact. Elian photographed the seal, the door, the van, the tools, Pike, the men, the license plate. Flash lit the brick walls in hard bursts.

"Stop photographing," Pike said.

"No," Mira replied.

The technician spoke quietly. "We were told the court approved removal."

"By whom?" Mira asked.

He looked at Pike.

Pike said, "Do not answer without counsel."

The sergeant raised an eyebrow. "That may be wise for later. Tonight, nobody touches the machine."

Otto stepped closer to the technician. "Do you know the head alignment procedure?"

The man hesitated. "We have diagrams."

"Which diagrams?"

No answer.

"If you lift that head cold, you warp the carriage guides. If you loosen the rear bolts before supporting the transfer shaft, you take the timing with you. Did your diagrams mention that?"

The technician looked at the tool roll as if it had betrayed him.

Mira called Judge Quill's clerk. Elian heard only one side: attempted removal, midnight, head assembly, police present, order. The clerk must have woken the judge, because Mira's posture changed after three minutes.

"Her Ladyship directs that all tools and materials be held pending further order," Mira said. "Mr. Pike and all attending persons are to provide statements by ten tomorrow."

Pike's smile failed to assemble.

The officers logged the tools. One wrench fit the head assembly bolts exactly. The padded crate's interior had been cut to the shape of the assembly from workshop measurements. This was not emergency conservation. It was extraction with a vocabulary.

As the van was sealed, Otto stood beside Elian and looked through the hall doors toward the engine.

"They would have taken the brain," he said.

"And left the body for the trust."

The old man's hand tightened on his cane. "Ada would have put a lock on Pike's mouth."

"Courts have less satisfying methods."

"We'll see."

By two in the morning, the loading bay was quiet again. The van remained under police control. Pike left in a taxi without looking back. Mira stayed until every seal was photographed. Elian retied his shoes at last.

The engine seemed less dormant now, not alive, but defended. Defense was not romance. It was paperwork, phone calls, old men awake at windows, and officers willing to stand in cold brick yards while rich people explained why orders meant less after midnight.

Chapter 35: Stopped

Morning made the attempted removal look worse. Daylight showed tire marks at the loading door, a scrape on the threshold where the padded crate had been set down, and the neat professionalism of the seized tools. Criminal foolishness often looked crude. This did not. It looked planned by people who expected paperwork to arrive after action, apologizing.

Mira convened everyone in the factory office. Pike did not attend. Ravel appeared in his place, carrying the expression of counsel who had spent the night learning facts from a client in the wrong order.

"My client acted under genuine concern for preservation," Ravel said.

Otto muttered, "My condolences to preservation."

Mira placed the tool inventory on the table. "Custom brace. Head bolt wrench. Crate cut to assembly dimensions. No conservator. No court notice. Midnight access."

"The hour was unfortunate."

"The hour was chosen."

Ravel did not answer that. He turned to the technician, who had come with separate counsel and the color of a man deciding how much loyalty he could afford. His statement was plain: Lena Corb contacted him for urgent removal of the Marrow Engine head assembly, representing that the estate had court approval and that Pike would be present with authority. Greaves's workshop had supplied measurements. The work order described "salvage of title-relevant operational unit."

Mira wrote the phrase slowly. "Title-relevant."

Elian felt the cold pleasure of a bad euphemism exposing itself. They had not been preserving the engine. They had been identifying the part most useful to ownership, display, and sale.

Judge Quill held an emergency remote hearing at eleven. Her face on the screen was composed in a way that made everyone sit straighter.

"I issued an order," she said. "Which word in it caused difficulty?"

Ravel stood in the factory office as if the judge could see his shoes. "Your Ladyship, my client misunderstood the scope in light of urgent conservation concerns."

"Then he should have applied to understand it before hiring a van."

Mira presented the tool log, photographs, technician statement, and crate manifest. Pike joined by phone after the judge ordered him personally present on the call. His voice had lost its public polish.

"I relied on advice," he said.

"From whom?" Judge Quill asked.

"Consultants."

"Names."

There was a pause.

"Ms. Corb and Mr. Greaves advised that removal might be prudent."

"Might be prudent is not authority."

The judge ordered Pike, Corb, Greaves, Rusk, and the estate to produce all communications concerning removal, conservation, sale, title documents, and engine components. She barred Pike from entering trust premises. She required police-held tools to remain sealed. She advanced the evidentiary hearing.

After the screen went dark, the office remained silent. Even Otto did not immediately comment.

Thom broke it. "They would have taken it."

"They were stopped," Mira said.

The difference mattered. It did not erase the attempt, but it prevented the empty case that might have greeted them by morning.

Elian went down to the hall alone for a moment. The engine stood behind glass, case seal intact. He checked the seal number against the log. He photographed it again because repetition in evidence was not a refrain; it was protection.

Otto joined him. "I dreamed once they took the head and left a note saying loan."

"This was close."

"Close matters."

"Yes."

The old man rubbed his thumb over the cane handle. "I used to think keeping a machine alive meant oil, temperature, and stopping fools from turning handles. Now it means lawyers."

"Only temporarily."

"You hope."

"I document."

Otto gave him a sideways look. "That may be your version of hope."

Perhaps it was. Elian looked at the engine's head assembly, all timing and restraint, still in place because suspicion had been converted into phone calls fast enough. The false vellum had tried to claim the machine through paper. The midnight van had tried to claim it through force disguised as care. Both had failed for the moment.

Outside, the factory yard was bright after rain. The tire marks would fade. The tool log would not. That was the work now: keep what mattered from fading before court could hear it.

Chapter 36: Evidence

Probate court heard the machine evidence first because Judge Quill said she preferred to know whether the disputed drawing described a machine or a fantasy before hearing why anyone owned it. The courtroom was full: trustees, estate heirs, reporters, Corb with counsel, Greaves with counsel, Rusk behind a face too blank to be innocent or guilty by appearance. Pike sat at the estate table, no longer smiling.

Elian took the witness stand with three folders and a glass of water he did not want. He gave his qualifications, which always felt like reciting the labels sewn inside one's own coat. Patent archivist. Mechanical drawings specialist. Prior provenance investigations. He spoke plainly and waited for the real questions.

Mira led him through the vellum drawing. Date claimed. Material. Title. Gear train. He explained the forty-tooth wheel and twenty-one-tooth pinion, the absent shaft, the collision with the reset arm, the cam profile inconsistent with wear, the bracket copied from the 1980s rebuild.

"In your opinion," Mira asked, "does the vellum depict the original patent design of the Marrow Engine?"

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because the design as drawn cannot perform the engine's required sequence, and because it includes features known to have been added decades after the inventor's death."

She showed the overlay exhibits. On the screen, the true patent copy, lecture slide, living engine, and false vellum aligned and diverged. The courtroom became attentive in the way rooms do when a complicated thing finally receives a clear picture.

Judge Quill leaned forward. "Mr. Mott, explain the missing shaft without assuming I know what a carry transfer is."

Elian pointed to the diagram. "Power must pass from this wheel to this pinion. For the pinion to rotate, it needs an axle held by bearings. The vellum shows the pinion in a location where no axle is drawn and where an axle would physically strike this reset arm. Without the axle, the part floats. With the axle, the machine jams."

"So either way?"

"It cannot work as drawn."

Ravel cross-examined with polished skepticism. Patent drawings, he suggested, could be schematic. Old machines changed. Experts could disagree. A missing line might be a drafting convention. Later repairs might reflect original intentions. Elian answered each point with the same patience.

"Could the inventor have anticipated the later bracket?" Ravel asked.

"He could have anticipated the need for support. He could not have anticipated Mr. Brann's filed corner from a 1978 repair."

A small sound moved through the courtroom. Judge Quill silenced it with a look.

Ravel tried again. "Is it your testimony that every discrepancy proves fraud?"

"No. A discrepancy proves discrepancy. The pattern, sequence, and material context support fraud."

"You are not a lawyer."

"No."

"You cannot opine on ownership."

"No."

"Yet you have formed views."

"I have formed views on whether a drawing describes the machine it claims to describe."

Mira did not smile. Elian could feel her not smiling.

Ravel moved to the midnight removal, perhaps unwisely. He suggested the head assembly's complexity justified emergency concern. Elian described the custom crate, the specific tools, the absence of conservation protocol, and the risk of removing the head without alignment procedure.

"Would removal have damaged the engine?"

"It could have. More importantly, it would have separated the most functionally significant assembly from the trust premises in violation of preservation."

Judge Quill wrote notes. Pike stared at the table.

When Elian stepped down, his shirt clung to his back. Testimony was not battle, whatever people said. It was controlled translation under pressure, and pressure left its own wear marks.

Otto patted his shoulder in the corridor during recess. "You made the impossible sound properly stupid."

"Thank you, I think."

"It was praise."

Inside the courtroom, the machine evidence had done its first work. The drawing was no longer merely suspect. It was mechanically illiterate in public.

Chapter 37: Inventory Chain

Mira handled the inventory chain herself. She preferred documents to perform without witnesses when possible, but this chain required explanation because its movement was the point. She called Ms. Denholm from the probate annex first. The clerk wore a navy cardigan and the offended dignity of a person whose files had been tampered with.

Under oath, Denholm described the original inventory, amendment procedures, ledger requirements, and microfilm copy. Mira placed the current file image beside the older microfilm on the screen. Held by estate. Held in trust. Two phrases, one line, one machine.

"Is there any authorized amendment changing this line?" Mira asked.

"No."

"Should there be?"

"Yes."

"Did Pike's firm request the file shortly before the amended copy was relied upon?"

"Yes."

Ravel objected to implication. Judge Quill allowed the answer and reminded everyone that implication would be addressed in submissions, not hidden from facts.

Next came the trust storage chain: Thom's receipt, appraiser removal signature, missing return entry, Maren's access authorization, workshop intake, Rusk invoice. Mira did not dramatize. She placed each document in order. The paper moved in one direction: toward sale.

Thom testified after Denholm. He looked nervous but not weak. He described the blue folder, the cabinet, the appraiser visit, his hospital absence, the later accusation. When Mira asked how suspension affected him, Ravel objected as irrelevant.

Judge Quill allowed a limited answer. "Credibility and sequence, Mr. Ravel. We are not machines here."

Thom said, "It made me doubt my own memory for a while. That was the worst part."

Elian, seated behind Mira, wrote the sentence down though it needed no report.

On cross-examination, Ravel pressed Thom about trust finances, his willingness to consider sale discussions, and gaps in storage controls. Thom admitted all of it.

"So records under your care were poorly secured," Ravel said.

"Yes."

"And you entertained sale discussions."

"Yes."

"And you cannot say what happened during your hospital absence."

"No."

Ravel paused, sensing damage.

Thom added, "But I can say I signed the blue copy into storage before your client's appraiser signed it out."

Mira looked down at her notes. This time she did smile, but only enough for the table to see.

Maren Vale testified next under subpoena. She admitted authorizing access she did not fully understand, relying on Pike's assurances, forwarding internal inventories, and approving removal of storeroom materials to what she believed was a neutral holding facility. Her shame was visible. So was her effort to place Pike in every sentence.

"Did you intend to assist fabrication of title evidence?" Mira asked.

"No."

"Did you read the preservation order before granting access?"

Maren closed her eyes briefly. "No."

"Why not?"

"Because I wanted the trust to survive, and I heard what I wanted from people offering money."

The courtroom was very quiet. It was not exoneration. It was something messier and more human, which often made better evidence.

Mira ended with the appraiser's photographs. Lena Corb's images showed the service cupboard, display inventory, spare gear, bracket, and storeroom. They predated the false vellum's appearance. Corb watched from the side benches, expression fixed.

"The chain begins with access," Mira said in closing that phase. "It continues through removal, alteration, and attempted use. No single document bears the whole burden. Together they show direction."

Judge Quill nodded slightly, not agreement but receipt.

During recess, Elian stepped into the corridor. Pike stood near a window with Ravel, speaking low. For the first time, Pike looked less like a man managing risk than a man inside it.

Mira joined Elian. "Tomorrow Iona and Celia."

"Today went well."

"Today went clearly. Well is for after judgment."

He accepted the correction. In the courtroom, documents had done what documents can do when handled carefully: they had made conduct visible without shouting motive. The paper moved in one direction. Everyone had seen it.

Chapter 38: Iona's Board

Iona brought a small gear model to court in a padded case that looked large enough for a musical instrument. She set it on the evidence table with the gravity of a surgeon laying out instruments. Ravel eyed it as if it might perform contempt.

"Demonstrative aid," Mira said.

Judge Quill looked interested despite herself. "Will it make noise?"

"Only if the false arrangement is used," Iona replied.

"Proceed."

Iona's testimony began with notation. She walked the court through Harrick-period symbols, later teaching conventions, and the vellum's anachronisms. She did not condescend. She did not hurry. She had the rare gift of making technical history sound like common sense that had been waiting for better lighting.

"A drawing may be beautiful," she said, "and still be a nonfunctioning machine."

Then came the model.

The true train advanced when she turned the handle. One gear carried to the next. A small arm lifted and fell. The printer indicator moved one position. The sound was modest: clicks, soft resistance, a clean stop.

"This is not an exact scale model," she said. "It demonstrates sequence."

She replaced one shaft and shifted the pinion to match the vellum. "Now the disputed arrangement."

She turned the handle. The first gear moved. The second caught. The false pinion jammed against the reset arm. A single click sounded in the courtroom, hard and final.

No paragraph of expert opinion could have improved on it.

Judge Quill leaned back. Ravel looked at his notes as if they might offer him another machine.

On cross-examination, he tried to suggest that models simplify. Iona agreed. He suggested simplification can mislead. She agreed. He suggested the actual engine was more complex. She agreed warmly.

"Then your model cannot prove the engine would fail," he said.

"The model demonstrates the geometric conflict. The actual engine contains the same conflict if built as drawn, plus additional timing conflicts. Complexity worsens the problem; it does not rescue it."

Ravel tried notation. Could Harrick have invented symbols before general use? Yes. Could a draftsman modernize a copy? Yes. Could the vellum be a later copy of an original? In theory, yes.

"But it is presented as original patent evidence," Iona said. "A later interpretive copy with modern notation and nonworking mechanism cannot do that work."

"You are not a title lawyer."

"No. I am a historian of machines. Your question concerns the machine."

Elian watched Judge Quill write again. Good witnesses did not avoid concession. They placed it where it belonged and refused to let it wander.

During a break, Iona stood in the corridor beside her model case and drank water from a paper cup.

"I dislike court," she said.

"You were effective."

"Those are compatible."

Otto approached, eyeing the model. "Your false click was too polite."

"I considered making it louder."

"Should have."

Iona's mouth twitched. "For the appeal, perhaps."

After lunch, Mira introduced the overlay exhibits through Iona. The true patent copy, the 1972 slide, the living engine, and the vellum aligned on screen. Iona explained how the outer frame could be copied while the internal logic was replaced by a misunderstanding of visible rebuilt parts. She identified the modern carry symbol, the later tolerance notation, and the service-record abbreviation.

Corb's counsel objected when Iona mentioned photographs taken by appraisers. Judge Quill allowed the limited point: the false drawing reflected visible later features, not hidden original design.

By the end of Iona's testimony, the courtroom had heard the engine fail in miniature and seen the false sheet fail in chronology. The vellum's elegance no longer helped it. If anything, it made the wrongness more offensive. Beauty without function had become evidence of performance.

As they left for the day, Iona handed the model case to Elian while she put on her coat.

"Careful," she said.

"I was raised properly."

"So was the forger, probably. Be careful anyway."

He carried the case down the courthouse steps, feeling the small weight of gears that had made a court understand motion. Behind him, Otto told Iona that her model needed better bearings. She told him his standards needed historical context. For a moment, under the cold evening sky, the case felt almost ordinary, and the ordinary felt worth protecting.

Chapter 39: Celia's Skin

Celia testified about vellum without romance. She began by saying animal skin was a working material, not a relic of mystery. It could be scraped, stretched, written, erased, heated, humidified, and made to mislead if someone understood its habits. The courtroom listened because she offered no ornament to resist.

Mira led her through the object. Old support. Scraped title panel. New inscription. Ink stabilizer. Infrared undertext. Ultraviolet abrasion. Heat contraction. Dust comparison. Each conclusion rested on an image, a test, or a physical observation. The screen showed magnified fibers like torn ropes, ink particles like dark islands, the ghost word DISPLAY beneath the false title.

"In your opinion," Mira asked, "was the vellum originally created as the patent title document it purports to be?"

"No."

"What was it?"

"An older display or demonstration support, later altered."

"Altered how?"

"Prior text was mechanically removed. New title and claim text were added. The surface was treated to harmonize the alteration with the older support."

Ravel rose for cross-examination with the careful courtesy people use before attacking expertise. "Ms. Voss, is the vellum old?"

"Parts of it."

"The support itself?"

"Yes."

"Then it could be authentic."

"An old door can have a new false sign."

Judge Quill's pen paused. Elian saw it. The sentence had entered the case.

Ravel tried to recover. "That is a metaphor."

"It is also the condition of this object."

He moved to ink. Testing was limited, samples small, conclusions probabilistic. Celia agreed. He suggested conservation treatments could introduce modern materials. Celia agreed, then explained why no legitimate treatment accounted for new ink in scraped fibers forming title language. He suggested heat exposure could occur in storage. Celia agreed, then distinguished uniform controlled contraction from attic damage.

"You cannot know the forger's intent," Ravel said.

"I can know the sequence of physical actions."

"But not intent."

"The sequence is consistent with making an old support appear to bear new evidentiary text from an earlier period."

"That is a long way of saying forgery."

"Yes."

The word sat plainly. No one moved.

Mira introduced the dust comparison. Celia explained that material from the cleared storeroom threshold contained calfskin fibers, pumice, and ink particles consistent with scraping work like that performed on the vellum. She did not overstate identity. Her restraint made the connection stronger.

Corb watched from the side benches with a face gone still. Greaves looked down. Rusk's counsel wrote constantly.

During redirect, Mira asked about ordinary old documents. "Do they often contain inconsistencies?"

"Yes. Age is messy."

"How is this different?"

"The inconsistencies serve a later claim. They are not random damage. They occur where title, date, and origin are asserted."

Elian thought of that long after. Fraud did not damage everything. It damaged the parts that needed to change.

When Celia stepped down, she looked tired for the first time. In the corridor she accepted tea from Mira and held it with both hands.

"I hate saying forgery in court," she said.

"You said it accurately."

"That is why I hate it."

Otto approached. "Your old door sentence was good."

"It was imprecise."

"Good things often are."

She gave him a look. "Do not tell anyone in conservation I said it."

Elian smiled. The day had been severe, but not joyless. Truth, when handled by people who cared about exactness, had its own dry humor.

Back inside, the false vellum lay in its protective sleeve on the evidence table. Under courtroom light it looked almost dignified. Celia's testimony had not stripped it of age. It had stripped it of authority. That distinction mattered. Old things could be damaged, repurposed, even made complicit by force. The task was not to despise the object. It was to stop the lie placed upon it from owning anything.

Chapter 40: Broker

Niall Rusk entered the witness box with the expression of a man who had decided contrition was safer than elegance. He wore a dark suit, no tie, and spectacles he removed whenever a question required discomfort. His counsel sat close, alert to every verb.

Mira began with his qualifications. Paper repair, vellum stabilization, exhibit preparation, historic surface integration. Rusk described legitimate work in a soft voice. He had restored charters, repaired bindings, toned fills so losses did not shout from across a gallery. Celia, seated behind Elian, listened without blinking.

Then Mira moved to the Harrick commission.

"Who engaged you?"

"Mr. Pike's office."

"For what purpose?"

"To prepare a damaged vellum display support for exhibition use."

"What text were you asked to place on it?"

Rusk swallowed. "Client-supplied title language."

"Was that language the title and claim text now visible on the disputed vellum?"

"Yes."

"Did you understand it would be used in court?"

"No."

"Did you ask?"

"No."

"Why not?"

He looked at his hands. "Because I was paid not to know."

The answer traveled through the courtroom slowly. It found every person who had ever treated ignorance as a service.

Mira let the silence work, then introduced emails. Pike's assistant had sent Rusk photographs of the engine, the erased inventory number, proposed patent title language, and the phrase "urgent provenance completion before closing." Rusk had replied with cost, timing, and a warning: "Full evidentiary reliance would require provenance support beyond material preparation." Pike had answered, "We are managing provenance separately."

"What did you understand that to mean?" Mira asked.

Rusk removed his spectacles. "That my work might be used as more than a display."

"Yet you proceeded."

"Yes."

Ravel cross-examined hard, trying to frame Rusk as the sole bad actor. Rusk had prepared the vellum. Rusk had aged the surface. Rusk had used client text without verifying. Rusk had the technical skill.

"You are seeking to reduce your own exposure by blaming Mr. Pike," Ravel said.

"I am seeking to describe what happened."

"After being threatened with contempt."

"That improved my memory."

Judge Quill looked up. "Mr. Rusk."

"Apologies, Your Ladyship."

But the answer had landed. Fear could clarify. That did not make the clarified thing false.

Mira on redirect asked about the workshop. Rusk confirmed Greaves received the display support and mechanical reference materials. Lena Corb supplied photographs and dimensions. Pike approved payments. The vellum was returned through Pike's courier. Rusk never dealt with the Harrick family directly.

"Did any instruction come from the trust?"

"No."

"From Thom Kest?"

"No."

"From Maren Vale?"

"No."

Thom lowered his head. Not weakness. Relief again, cleaner this time.

After Rusk, Greaves testified under similar pressure. He admitted receiving trust materials through estate channels, failing to verify authority, and allowing work on the vellum. He tried to hide behind client confidentiality until Judge Quill asked whether confidentiality had a habit of entering locked trust rooms. He answered more plainly after that.

Lena Corb's testimony came last. The appraiser was composed until Mira placed her workshop attendance certificate for Rusk's aging course beside her engine photographs. Corb admitted identifying useful display materials, photographing mechanical details, and coordinating with Pike. She denied intending fraud.

"What did you think would be done with the scraped display vellum?" Mira asked.

"A reproduction."

"Of what?"

Corb hesitated. "A patent sheet."

"A reproduction of an original you had not seen?"

"I relied on Mr. Pike."

The sentence had become a hallway everyone tried to run through at once.

By day's end, the professional scaffolding around the false vellum stood exposed. No single witness confessed to inventing the entire fraud. Each had done a piece, named it narrowly, and sent an invoice. The whole emerged not from one grand villainy but from paid compartments of incuriosity.

Outside court, Celia said, "He has made restoration sound dirty."

"Rusk did that," Elian said. "You made it legible."

She accepted that with a nod. Across the corridor, Pike stood alone for once, his counsel speaking to someone else. He looked at the witnesses he had paid, and none of them looked back.

Chapter 41: Investor

Money received a name on the fourth day of the hearing. Until then the private collector had remained behind redactions, statements, and polite references to patience. Bank records pulled him into daylight as Orson Vale, no relation to Maren, though the coincidence caused one reporter to make a note and three lawyers to frown. Vale was not merely a collector. He was an investor in industrial heritage properties, skilled at separating movable assets from inconvenient buildings.

Mira traced payments through estate accounts, Pike's firm, Rusk, Greaves, the courier, and the attempted head removal. Then she introduced a side agreement between Vale's acquisition company and the estate. The purchase price increased substantially if the engine's head assembly and "title-authenticating documents" were delivered free of trust claims.

Otto whispered, "They priced the brain."

Elian said nothing. He was looking at Pike.

Pike had stopped attending in person after Rusk's testimony, but Judge Quill ordered him back when the side agreement appeared. He entered late, face closed, and sat without greeting Ravel. The courtroom noticed.

Vale testified by video from an office with a harbor view. He had the calm of a man accustomed to risk belonging to other people. He described the transaction as lawful acquisition, the engine as underutilized heritage, the trust as financially incapable, and the disputed documents as estate matters.

Mira asked, "Did you plan to keep the engine in the factory?"

"We explored several display options."

"Did those include removing the head assembly?"

"Technical assessment suggested modular conservation might be appropriate."

"That was not my question."

Judge Quill looked at the screen. "Answer it."

Vale's jaw moved. "Yes."

"Before or after court approval?"

"Subject to advice."

Mira introduced emails between Vale and Pike. In one, Vale wrote: "Factory shell can remain sentimental. Value sits in operating head and title paper." In another: "Trust noise must be resolved before Friday or price adjusts."

The word noise did more damage than Vale seemed to understand.

Pike's counsel objected to privilege. Judge Quill ruled the emails commercial and producible under prior order. Ravel looked as if each page had become heavier in his hands.

Bank records tied Vale's company to reimbursement for Rusk's work, Greaves's workshop, and the attempted removal. The collector had planned to buy not a machine in place but a stripped asset with a story attached. The false vellum was not decoration. It was part of the purchase structure.

During recess, Otto stood in the corridor with Elian and watched Pike confer with Ravel.

"Coward," Otto said.

"Defendant," Mira corrected, arriving behind them.

"Both can share a coat."

The judge ordered Vale joined to the proceeding for purposes of relief and costs. Money, at last, had a chair in the courtroom. It did not sit comfortably.

In the afternoon, Mira recalled Thom briefly to identify the trust's public access obligations and maintenance endowment. He explained school visits, volunteer days, demonstration plans, roof costs, insurance troubles. He did not pretend the trust was healthy.

"Would sale have solved those problems?" Ravel asked.

"It would have ended the thing we were solving them for."

Elian saw Judge Quill write again.

Vale's counsel suggested a generous donation in exchange for transfer. Thom's answer was quiet. "We are not fundraising here. We are determining whether someone tried to take what we held."

By the time court adjourned, the case had widened beyond a false drawing. It included a business plan for extraction, professionals paid to prepare paper, appraisers gathering source material, and a midnight attempt to remove the most valuable assembly. Pike no longer looked like an overzealous estate manager. He looked like a hinge between money and method.

That evening, in the factory hall, trustees gathered around Thom as he reported. Maren attended as a member now, not officer, and stood near the back. When Thom read Vale's "trust noise" email aloud, the room reacted not with anger first but hurt. Noise was what powerful people called others when they wanted not to hear them.

Otto looked at the engine. "It heard worse."

"From whom?" Elian asked.

"Owners."

The hall settled into silence. Down the row of display cases, old tools lay under glass: calipers, gauges, files, a dull broach perhaps not the one but like it. They were not sentimental. They were instruments. The trust, for all its flaws, had kept them where work could still be seen. That, Elian thought, was the opposite of noise.

Chapter 42: True Copy

The true copy entered evidence near the end, not because it mattered least but because Mira wanted the court to understand everything that had tried to replace it. Celia carried it in a protective folder. Iona stood ready for mechanism. Otto sat behind them with both hands on his cane, prepared to dislike anyone who breathed too hard.

The blue paper looked modest on the evidence table. After days of false vellum, altered inventories, workshop invoices, and midnight tools, its plainness had force. It did not announce itself as treasure. It behaved like a working document that had expected to be useful and had waited.

Mira asked Celia to describe its material condition. Mid-twentieth-century blue copy paper. Stable ink. Fold patterns consistent with long storage. No evidence of recent alteration. Then Iona identified the mechanism. It matched the 1972 slide, the engine's functional sequence, and the service history.

"This is the operational design copy held by the trust," Iona said.

"In your opinion, does it depict a functioning version of the Marrow Engine?"

"Yes. More importantly, the version that corresponds with the living machine's sequence."

Mira turned to Otto. He was called to identify Ada Marr's hand and the service context. His testimony was rougher than the experts', but roughness suited it.

"Do you recognize the note on the back?"

"Yes. Ada Marr."

"How?"

"She wrote like she was late for a train and angry at the timetable."

A small laugh escaped somewhere. Judge Quill allowed it to die naturally.

"Please read the note."

Otto put on his glasses. His voice changed when he read. "Trust copy. Do not lend to estate men."

He removed the glasses and looked at Pike, not the judge. Mira gave him a second, then drew him back.

"Why would she write that?"

"Because estate men asked. Because she knew paper leaves politely and returns altered or not at all."

Ravel cross-examined cautiously. "Mr. Brann, you admired Ms. Marr."

"Yes."

"You share her distrust of the estate."

"I learned from evidence."

"Is it possible this note reflects personal bias rather than legal title?"

"It reflects the fact that people who wanted the copy were not to be trusted with it. Recent events have been kind to her opinion."

Judge Quill's pen paused again.

Mira introduced the service notebook entries: transfer to trust, repairs by factory labor, public education use, refusal to lend, location in the hidden recess. The notebook did not merely support title; it told a story of maintenance as ownership's daily proof.

Elian was recalled briefly to connect the true copy to the overlay. He explained that the false vellum copied the outer frame but diverged from the true mechanism. The blue copy and slide matched each other independently. The living engine matched them in function and wear. Three witnesses across time agreed: paper, photograph, machine.

Ravel suggested the blue copy could have been planted.

Elian answered, "Its existence is visible in a 1972 slide, its storage is referenced in entries predating the dispute, and its material condition is consistent with long-term concealment. Planting would require planting evidence decades before the controversy."

"Not impossible."

"Very little is impossible if chronology is ignored."

Judge Quill looked up. "We are not ignoring chronology."

That ended the line.

When the blue copy was returned to its folder, the courtroom seemed to exhale. Not dramatically. Just enough. The true document had not destroyed the false one by splendor. It had outlasted it by being connected: to notebook, slide, engine, witnesses, use.

In the corridor after adjournment, Otto sat on a bench and stared at his hands.

"You all right?" Elian asked.

"Ada should have been here."

"In a way, she was."

Otto frowned. "Do not become sentimental at me."

"I will file it as evidentiary presence."

"Better."

Mira joined them. Closing submissions would be the next day. She looked exhausted, but her eyes were clear.

"The past has been blunt," she said, glancing at the folder in Celia's hands.

"It needed to be," Elian replied.

The blue copy went back to the archive under seal. The false vellum remained in evidence. Both would be preserved. One as title. One as warning.

Chapter 43: Rejected

Judge Quill delivered judgment two weeks later in a courtroom made brighter by winter sun and collective lack of sleep. No one had expected a ruling from the bench after a case so heavy with exhibits, but she had circulated notice that she would give oral reasons and written reasons to follow. The room filled early.

Elian sat behind Mira with Otto, Thom, Celia, and Iona. Maren came and sat apart, then moved one seat closer to the trustees before proceedings began. Pike sat at the estate table beside Ravel. Vale's counsel appeared by video, his harbor view replaced by a neutral conference room wall.

Judge Quill began with the documents. The vellum, she found, was unreliable in every material respect. Its support was old, but its title claim was not. Its mechanism did not function. Its notation was anachronistic. Its erased undertext showed prior display use. Its preparation involved scraping, new inscription, and artificial aging. It could not establish estate title.

Pike stared forward. He had become very still.

She turned to the inventory. The altered probate line was unauthorized and inconsistent with the earlier microfilm. The provenance letter was unsupported and physically incapable of being what it first claimed. The court would give it no weight.

Then the trust evidence. The blue copy, service notebook, transfer receipt, maintenance history, and public use supported the trust's claim. The engine had been held not as a passive family relic but as a working heritage asset maintained by factory labor and successor trustees.

"The estate may not sell what the trust owns," she said.

The sentence landed without ornament. It had more torque than anger.

Ravel rose. "Your Ladyship, we ask for a stay pending appeal."

"Denied."

"Given the commercial implications-"

"The commercial implications arise from arrangements made before title was established and, in some respects, in defiance of preservation. They do not favor a stay."

Vale's counsel began to speak from the screen. Judge Quill raised one hand and the video voice stopped.

She ordered the private sale void as to the engine and associated trust materials. She maintained preservation orders pending transfer of disputed items back to trust custody under archival supervision. She referred the altered court record and attempted removal circumstances to appropriate authorities. Costs would be argued separately, but her preliminary view was clear enough to make Ravel sit down slowly.

When court adjourned, no one cheered. The silence felt too large and too serious. Then Thom bent forward with both hands over his face. Otto put one hand on his shoulder, awkwardly, as if comfort were a tool he had not used in years.

In the hall, reporters gathered. Mira gave a statement so brief it disappointed them: the trust was grateful, the engine would remain preserved for public access, written reasons would be reviewed. She did not say fraud. She did not need to. The judgment had done it more durably.

Thom held the order as if paper had become weight-bearing again. "We keep it."

"You keep it," Elian said. "Now you must keep it well."

"Yes."

The word carried more burden than celebration.

Pike passed them with Ravel, eyes fixed on the exit. Otto shifted as if to speak. Mira caught his sleeve.

"No."

"I was only going to say good morning."

"No."

He grumbled but stayed.

Celia looked at the courthouse doors. "What happens to the false vellum?"

"Stored with the reports," Elian said. "Not hidden. Not destroyed."

Iona nodded. "Teach the next person where to look first."

Outside, the air was cold enough to make everyone breathe visibly. Thom called the trustees from the steps. His voice broke once when he said rejected. Maren stood near him and did not ask to speak.

Elian looked back at the courthouse. Legal endings were never clean. Referrals, costs, possible appeals, insurance, governance repairs, conservation work: all of that remained. But the sale had been stopped, the false drawing rejected, and the engine returned in law to the hands that had kept it alive.

Mira came down the steps last, folder under her arm. "Well?"

"You won."

"The trust won."

"You argued."

"The evidence argued."

He accepted the correction because it was true and because she looked too tired to be contradicted. Together they walked toward the factory, where practical arguments waited: cleaning schedules, access plans, locks, humidity, funding, and the ordinary burden of keeping a thing saved after the drama had ended.

Chapter 44: Trust

The first trustees' meeting after judgment was held in the demonstration hall, not the office. Thom insisted. If they were going to argue about the engine, they would do it where the engine could embarrass them into seriousness. Folding chairs formed an uneven circle around the glass case. The winter light through the high windows made the dust visible in long, slanting bands.

Mira attended as legal adviser. Elian came for records. Otto came because no one could have kept him away without another court order. Celia and Iona sat near the back, officially observers, unofficially guardians of standards.

Thom opened with the judgment. He did not reread the parts everyone had memorized. He read the obligations: preservation, access, governance, records control, conservation plan. Winning had produced work. That was its honest shape.

"First vote," he said. "Do we reopen public demonstrations?"

Hands rose quickly, then slowed as Otto cleared his throat.

"After cleaning," he said.

"After conservation assessment," Celia added.

"After operational review," Iona said.

Thom looked around. "So: yes, after everyone tells us why not yet."

"That is how preservation works," Elian said.

The room relaxed enough to laugh.

They debated access. School groups. Monthly open days. Research appointments. Digital scans of the true copy. Display of the false vellum? That question divided them.

One trustee wanted it hidden to avoid shame. Another wanted it displayed as evidence of attempted theft. Maren, speaking carefully from the third row, said, "We should show it only with explanation. Otherwise it remains powerful."

Otto looked at her, surprised perhaps to agree. "A lie in a case needs a label bigger than the lie."

"We can do that," Elian said. "A teaching display. False document, true copy, engine parts, timeline."

"No spectacle," Celia said.

"No spectacle."

They voted to preserve and display the false vellum only after conservation housing and interpretive review. They voted to digitize the service notebook, restrict handling of originals, replace locks, install access logs, and create a records committee with someone who actually liked records. No one volunteered until Ms. Denholm from the probate annex, invited as a governance adviser, said she knew retired clerks who enjoyed righteous filing.

The biggest argument concerned money. Roof repairs remained. Insurance had risen. Legal costs would take time to recover. Vale's voided purchase left no windfall. Saving the engine did not save the trust from arithmetic.

Thom did not pretend otherwise. "We cannot be proud and broke in a way that lets the next Pike arrive."

Maren spoke again. "I can help fundraise. Not as officer. As penance, if you like."

"Penance is not a governance category," Mira said.

"Then as work."

Thom studied her. "Work we can discuss."

It was not forgiveness. It was better because it was less theatrical. Trust, once damaged, returned by tasks or not at all.

Elian watched them argue in practical terms. That was how ownership sounded when it meant care rather than extraction. Not speeches. Schedules, locks, budgets, humidity sensors, volunteer training, insurance clauses, school bookings, cleaning methods. The room filled with the unglamorous grammar of stewardship.

After the meeting, Otto unlocked the glass case under the new two-person protocol so Celia could take humidity readings. Thom stood beside him with the second key. The act was small, almost comic in its caution, and deeply satisfying.

"Two keys," Otto muttered. "Ada would laugh."

"Ada hid things because one key was not enough," Elian said.

Otto considered that. "She would laugh less."

Mira packed her files. "The court order requires a progress report in thirty days."

"We will have one," Thom said.

"With exhibits?"

"With photographs, logs, and fewer adjectives than Elian prefers."

Elian placed a hand over his heart. "Cruel but fair."

Outside, the canal reflected a pale sky. Inside, the trustees stacked chairs and kept talking. The engine remained under glass, but the hall around it had changed. It was no longer a room waiting for sale. It was a room preparing to open.

Chapter 45: Cleaning

They cleaned the engine over three weekends, not with heroics but with swabs, patience, and arguments about what not to improve. Celia wrote the protocol. Otto objected to three verbs. Iona objected to one diagram. Elian revised the labels until everyone disliked them equally, which he took as balance.

No bright polish. No theatrical restoration. Dust removed, loose debris documented, oil renewed where the maintenance history supported it, brass left honest where hands had worn it. The machine was not to be made young. It was to be made legible.

The first Saturday began cold. Volunteers arrived in coats and nervous shoes. Thom assigned tasks: floor dusting outside the case, cataloging loose display material, scanning labels, fetching tea. No one touched the engine except Otto, Celia, and two younger trustees trained for the work.

"Listen for drag," Otto told them as he demonstrated a hand movement over a bearing without turning it. "Feel before force. Machines forgive ignorance less than people."

"People have not been especially forgiving here," one trustee said.

"Machines are quicker about it."

Elian labeled parts with reversible tags. He wrote not just names but histories where space allowed: bracket, brass, fitted during 1978 rebuild; transfer gear, spare, dull-broach keyway; printer carriage, repaired 1962 and 1984. Each tag connected metal to record. No part floated free as the false vellum had tried to make it.

Iona brought students on the second weekend. They arrived whispering, as if entering a chapel, then became braver when Otto snapped, "It is a shop machine, not a saint." They asked good questions. Why not polish the brass? Why preserve the false document? How could a drawing be beautiful and wrong? Iona answered some. Elian answered others. Otto answered the rest with stories that began as corrections and ended as testimony.

Celia supervised cleaning of the glass case. "No ammonia near the seals. No leaning over open panels. No celebratory wiping."

"That last one is aimed at me," Thom said.

"At everyone with feelings."

Maren came each weekend and did whatever task was dullest: inventorying chair legs, scanning old newsletters, checking humidity log batteries. People spoke to her politely but not warmly. She accepted that. Work was the only apology the room could use.

On the third Sunday, they opened the printer side. The narrow paper path had accumulated dust and one dead moth. Otto removed the moth with tweezers and placed it in a waste envelope as if even that deserved procedure.

"Do we log it?" a student asked.

"We do now," Elian said.

The student wrote: biological debris, removed from printer carriage. Everyone laughed quietly, then returned to work. Care could be comic without becoming careless.

During lunch, they sat on folding chairs in the hall and ate sandwiches from paper bags. The engine stood open under covers. Sun found the brass through upper windows. Thom read aloud a draft announcement for public reopening. It avoided triumph and promised a demonstration by hand turn after conservation sign-off.

"Too many words," Otto said.

"You say that about most things."

"Most things have too many words."

Elian looked around the hall: trustees, students, experts, a solicitor eating crisps from the packet, a former deputy chair entering humidity data, an old maintainer pretending not to be moved. The case had been about ownership, but ownership here looked like people willing to spend a Sunday learning how not to damage what they loved.

At the end of the day, Otto applied fresh oil to one bearing under Celia's watch. The smell rose warm and mineral. He closed his eyes briefly, listening.

"Better," he said.

"You turned nothing," Celia replied.

"I heard the bearing accept it."

She opened her mouth, then chose not to argue.

Elian logged the oil type, amount, location, and time. The entry went into the new service record, copied digitally, backed up twice. Ada Marr's black notebook rested in a climate box now, retired from daily duty but not from meaning. The new record began where hers left off.

When they locked the hall, the engine looked almost the same to an untrained eye. That was success. Honest cleaning did not shout. It removed what obscured and left what history had earned.

Chapter 46: Catalog

Elian repaired the catalog record on a Tuesday when the factory was quiet and the rain returned in fine diagonal lines. He sat in the trust office with the service notebook scans on one screen, the old catalog on another, and a pot of tea Otto had made too strong because he believed weakness in tea led to weakness elsewhere.

The existing record was brief and wrong in the way underfunded records often were. Marrow Engine, brass calculating machine, circa 1890, inventor Lucien Harrick, on display. Associated patent drawing: estate. Provenance incomplete. It had been written by someone trying to be neutral without enough facts. Neutrality, Elian thought, could become a waiting room for error.

He did not make it lyrical. Catalog records were not memorial speeches. They needed titles, dates, creators, materials, custody, scope, conditions, related records, and warnings where warnings were earned.

Title: Marrow Engine.

Creator: Lucien Harrick, initial design; Harrick Works engineering staff and maintenance department, continuing operational development.

Custodial history: transferred to Harrick Works Trust; maintained by factory labor; disputed estate claim rejected by court order dated...

Associated records: blue-paper trust copy, service notebook, 1972 lecture slides, maintenance logs, false vellum drawing.

He paused at false vellum drawing. Description mattered. Too soft, and future readers might lend it dignity. Too harsh, and the catalog became argument rather than record. He wrote: Modern forgery on older vellum support, prepared from a former display sheet and falsely inscribed as original patent title evidence; rejected by court.

The words looked severe. He did not soften them.

Mira reviewed the legal note by phone. "Add judgment citation."

"Already."

"Do not say theft unless quoting the referral."

"I did not."

"Do not say Pike by name in the main object record."

"Cross-reference?"

"Restricted legal file."

He adjusted the fields. Archives were hospitable to uncertainty. They were not obliged to be polite to lies. But politeness and precision were not the same war.

Thom came in with a stack of access forms. "How long will this take?"

"Forever, if done properly."

"I meant today."

"Today, the main record. Later, item-level descriptions, authority records, digital repository links, handling notes."

Thom looked at the screens. "I used to think catalogs were lists."

"That is how they lure you."

Maren entered with scanned newsletters and placed them on the table. "These mention Ada's demonstrations. I marked pages."

Elian checked them. 1968 school visit. 1974 engineering society talk. 1981 rebuild fundraiser. Each note supported public use. He added related references.

"Thank you," he said.

She nodded and left before gratitude could become conversation.

By afternoon, the record had grown into a structure that could hold complexity without collapsing into story. The engine was not just inventor, not just trust, not just court dispute. It was design, labor, maintenance, display, attempted alienation, preservation. The catalog needed to show all of that and still help someone find the oil protocol.

Celia sent handling language for the vellum. Iona sent a paragraph on notation anachronisms that Elian cut by half and then, under protest, restored in a linked technical note. Otto refused to send anything by email and instead dictated: "Do not turn engine without two trained persons and a reason." Elian used it nearly verbatim.

At five, he saved the record and exported a preservation copy. Then he printed the summary page for the physical file. Paper still mattered. Not because digital records failed more often, but because redundancy was a moral position in this building now.

Otto came in as the printer worked. "Is it fixed?"

"Catalogs are never fixed. This one is corrected."

"Does it say the lie was a lie?"

"Yes."

"Good."

They stood by the printer while the page emerged. Outside the office window, the canal moved under rain. Inside, the record became less vulnerable to charm. Future readers would still need judgment. They always did. But they would not begin where Pike had wanted them to begin, with an old-looking sheet and a title that pretended to be enough.

Chapter 47: Hand Turn

The first hand turn was scheduled for a Thursday morning before public reopening, with only the conservation team, trustees, and two students present. Otto said machines disliked audiences when waking. Iona said machines disliked anthropomorphism. Otto said historians disliked useful language. The argument lasted long enough to settle everyone's nerves.

The hall had been cleaned. The case glass shone without looking new. The engine's brass remained dark in the hollows and bright where generations of hands had earned brightness. A narrow roll of test paper sat in the printer carriage. The new service log lay open on a table, pen uncapped.

Celia checked humidity. Elian checked tags. Thom checked the door, though it was already locked. Mira attended without legal necessity, which she denied by saying she wanted to confirm compliance. No one challenged her.

Otto stood at the crank. He had dressed as if for work: old waistcoat, clean shirt, sleeves rolled. His cane leaned against the case. For a moment he looked younger not in face but in relation to the room.

"Ready?" Thom asked.

"No," Otto said. "But we proceed."

He placed one hand on the crank and the other on the frame. He did not turn immediately. He felt. The hall seemed to draw in around the machine. Outside, a lorry passed on the road, then faded.

The crank resisted.

Otto did not force it. He moved back a fraction, then forward. Oil spread through contact. A gear accepted motion. Another answered. The resistance changed from refusal to weight.

Gears advanced. Cams lifted. A pawl clicked into place. The carriage shifted by one measured step.

No one spoke.

The machine did not roar. It made a careful shop sound, modest and exact: brass teeth meeting, steel pins rising, a spring taking and releasing load. Elian felt the sound in his chest more than his ears. It was not life. Machines were not alive. But it was continuity, and continuity had its own force.

Otto turned through half a cycle, then stopped. He listened.

"Again?" Iona asked softly.

"Full cycle," Otto said.

The second movement came easier. The carry arm lifted at the point Iona had drawn on her board. The printer prepared its mark. The paper advanced. A small black impression appeared, not yet meaningful to anyone but the machine.

Celia exhaled. "No unusual drag."

"There was a slight hesitation at the reset," Otto said.

"Log it," Elian said.

One of the students wrote: hand turn, full cycle, slight hesitation at reset, no abnormal sound, output mark produced. His handwriting shook a little.

Mira stood near the case with her arms folded. Her face had softened in a way she would later deny. "All that over a crank turn."

"All that so the crank turn happened here," Thom said.

That was exactly it.

They ran two more cycles, each logged. The paper strip emerged with marks aligned in a narrow column. Iona checked the sequence and nodded. "It still carries."

Otto touched the frame, not possessively, but as one worker acknowledging another. "Of course it does."

Elian thought of the vellum drawing's locked gear, its false certainty, the midnight crate shaped for a stolen head. The true machine gave a small answer and, in doing so, answered everything that mattered.

Afterward they closed the case and sealed it. The hall returned to ordinary light. People began speaking at once, too softly, then normally. The students asked whether the public demonstration would include a full cycle. Celia said perhaps. Otto said only if people behaved. Iona said behavior was difficult to define. Thom said tickets would be free but limited.

Elian made the catalog note before leaving: Successful hand turn under conservation protocol. Output produced. Engine remains operational by manual demonstration.

He saved it, printed it, and placed it in the file. The record complete? Not yet. Records were never complete while objects remained in the world. But the line mattered. It marked the day the Marrow Engine moved again after a court had been asked to believe a drawing that would have stopped it forever.

Chapter 48: Output

The public demonstration took place on a Saturday clear enough to make the factory windows shine. People queued along the canal: former workers, engineering students, parents with adult children, reporters, neighbors who had passed the building for years without entering. The trust limited numbers, but the line still bent around the yard. Heritage, once threatened, had become visible to those who had assumed it would remain.

Inside, the hall smelled of floor polish, paper, and faint oil. The false vellum sat in a side case under controlled light, labeled plainly. Beside it were the blue trust copy, a reproduction of Iona's overlay, and a short timeline. No spectacle. No accusations beyond those established by judgment. People lingered there longer than Elian expected.

"They used old skin?" one visitor asked.

"Yes," Elian said.

"That seems worse than just lying on new paper."

"It is a more expensive kind of lying."

The visitor nodded as if that explained several things beyond the case.

At noon, Thom welcomed the first group. His speech was short because Otto had edited it. He thanked the court, experts, volunteers, and workers past and present. He named Ada Marr. Otto looked down when he heard her name and pretended to adjust the crank cloth.

Iona explained the calculation sequence with the small model. This time the false arrangement was shown only as a still diagram; no theatrical failure. The point had been made in court. Here, the living machine deserved the attention.

Otto took his place at the crank. Two trained trustees stood by. Celia watched from the side with a checklist. Elian logged conditions. Mira stood near the back, not officially anything, listening.

"The engine will be turned by hand," Thom said. "It will not be polished into youth. It will not be run beyond conservation limits. What you hear is what remains because people kept it."

Otto turned.

The hall quieted at once. The machine's sound was small, almost private, yet it filled the room because everyone had chosen to hear it. Gears advanced. The carriage clicked. The paper strip moved. A black mark appeared, then another, then a number resolved in narrow type.

Iona leaned over the output and read it aloud. "There. It still carries."

Otto touched the strip with one finger. "Of course it does."

This time people laughed softly, not at him but with the relief of precision fulfilled. The machine had not performed a miracle. It had done what it was built and kept to do. That was better.

Reporters asked questions afterward. Why did the trust fight? How much was the engine worth? Would there be criminal charges? Thom answered carefully. Value was not only price. Legal matters remained ongoing. Public access would continue. Maintenance would be funded through donations, grants, and recovered costs if ordered.

An older woman approached Otto with a photograph. Her father had worked at Harrick Works in the 1950s. Otto recognized the man by nickname and told her he had once dropped a sandwich into a parts bin. She laughed and cried at the same time, which made Otto look alarmed.

Elian retreated to the side case. The false vellum lay under glass, diminished by explanation. It still had beauty. That was important. Lies often borrowed real beauty. The label did not ask viewers to sneer at it. It asked them to look harder.

Mira joined him. "Good turnout."

"Threat improves attendance."

"So does survival."

They watched visitors move from the false vellum to the blue copy, then to the engine. The sequence taught without a lecture: claim, proof, living thing.

At the end of the day, Elian helped rewind the output strip. Iona kept the first correct public output in a labeled sleeve. It would be stored with the new service record and a digital scan.

"A small answer," she said.

"Small answers are underrated."

Outside, late sun caught the canal. Inside, volunteers folded chairs and swept the hall. The engine rested after three controlled demonstrations. Otto checked the case seal twice, then once more because no one stopped him.

Elian placed the output sleeve in the temporary file. The true machine had spoken in marks narrow enough to fit on a strip of paper. It had answered greed, error, false title, and the polished hunger of collectors not by grandeur but by carrying correctly from one column to the next.

Chapter 49: Estate Closed

Mira closed the estate without the engine in a room that smelled of toner, old carpet, and restrained disappointment. The Harrick heirs sat across from her with their own counsel. They looked less villainous than tired, which was often harder to dislike. They had expected money, then vindication, then perhaps rescue from blame. They received instead a revised account, lower figures, legal costs, and a letter explaining that certain matters had been referred elsewhere.

Elian attended only for the records transfer schedule. He sat near the wall and watched arithmetic take the place of fantasy.

"There will be no collector's plaque," Mira said.

One heir, a nephew named Simon Harrick, rubbed his forehead. "We were told the trust had no real claim."

"By whom?"

He looked at his counsel, then at the table. "Pike."

"You retained him."

"We retained expertise."

"Expertise is not a substitute for instructions you understand."

The sentence was severe, but not cruel. Mira had no interest in making the heirs into masterminds because evidence had not made them that. They had wanted a sale and believed the person who made it sound easy. Wanting was not forgery. But wanting had made them available to it.

Another heir, Clara, spoke quietly. "My grandfather always said the factory swallowed the family."

Elian looked at her. She was in her sixties, hands folded tight. "What did he mean?"

"Money went in. Time. Men. Pride. Then the workers got the hall and everyone acted as if something had been stolen from us."

"Was it?"

She shook her head after a long pause. "I don't know. I think we inherited grievance because it cost nothing to keep."

Mira allowed the silence after that. It was not legal, exactly, but it belonged in the room.

The settlement of remaining estate assets took two hours. Factory land not held by the trust would be sold separately, subject to heritage restrictions. Family papers unrelated to the engine would remain with the heirs, but copies of relevant correspondence would go to the trust archive. The estate withdrew all claims to the Marrow Engine and associated operational records.

When the heirs left, Mira leaned back and closed her eyes for three seconds. "They are angry."

"At us?"

"At arithmetic, mostly."

Elian understood. Arithmetic had a way of taking sides when property pretended to be history.

She smiled tiredly. "I prefer honest subtraction."

"Will Pike face charges?"

"That is for others now. Civilly, costs and sanctions first. Professionally, likely trouble. Criminally, we wait."

"Waiting again."

"Law is mostly waiting with deadlines."

They packed the records for transfer. Each box had a contents list, seal number, and destination. Elian checked them against the catalog: family correspondence copies, estate valuation files, appraiser communications, sale agreement, voided side agreement, court orders. The false provenance letter would be stored with the dispute file, not returned to a drawer where it could acquire new myths.

As they carried boxes to the archive van, Simon Harrick returned alone. He held a small envelope.

"This was in my aunt's things," he said. "Not engine title. Just photographs. Factory picnic, I think. If the trust wants copies."

Elian opened the envelope enough to see black-and-white prints: workers beside long tables, women in summer dresses, men in shirtsleeves, a younger Ada Marr scowling at someone outside the frame. He closed it.

"They will."

Simon nodded. "I am sorry."

Mira answered before Elian could. "Put that in writing if you mean it. Institutions remember better than conversations."

He gave a small, chastened laugh. "Of course."

After he left, Elian placed the envelope in a temporary sleeve. Not every family paper was a weapon. Some were only memory waiting for the right custodian.

By late afternoon, the estate file was closed as to the engine. There would be less money, fewer congratulations, no private display in a collector's controlled room. At the factory, there would be roof leaks to fix and school visits to schedule. Honest subtraction left work behind. That was one way to know it was honest.

Chapter 50: Stored

Elian stored the false vellum on a clear morning in early spring, after Celia approved the housing and Judge Quill's final written order released the evidence back to the trust under archival conditions. The sheet lay in a custom folder with inert supports, its reports in a companion file, its images duplicated in three systems and one printed binder because redundancy had become local religion.

He did not hide it. He did not destroy it. Lies kept properly could teach future archivists where to look first.

The folder label was plain: Altered vellum display support falsely inscribed as Marrow Engine patent title document. Associated litigation and conservation reports. Access restricted. He had considered shorter titles. None had done enough work.

The true patent copy rested in a blue folder in the next drawer, not touching the false vellum, not made theatrical by proximity. The service notebook sat in a climate box that still faintly smelled of oil despite all proper materials. Ada Marr's first page had been digitized, transcribed, and quoted in the new display with permission from the trust: Drawings show intention. Service shows survival.

Downstairs, visitors moved through the demonstration hall. Elian could hear footsteps and the occasional murmur rising through the floor. The engine would be turned once that afternoon for a group of apprentices from a technical college. Otto had complained about their shoes in advance, which meant he was looking forward to it.

Mira came into the archive room with two coffees. She placed one beside him at a legally safe distance from all paper.

"Final costs order came in," she said.

"Good?"

"Good enough. Pike, estate, and Vale's company share most costs. Referrals continue. Rusk's regulator has opened an inquiry. Greaves is cooperating loudly."

"Loud cooperation is still cooperation."

"Sometimes."

She looked at the drawer labels. "You really are keeping the false one beside the truth."

"Near, not beside. Context, not equality."

"Archivists have exact prejudices."

"We call them standards."

They drank coffee by the worktable. Sun fell across the floor in a pale rectangle. The archive smelled of board, paper, and the faint dust that no cleaning ever fully defeated.

"Will this happen again?" Mira asked.

"Forgery? Yes."

"That was not comforting."

"I was not attempting comfort."

"Clearly."

He closed the drawer and entered the storage location in the catalog. Box number, shelf, access conditions, related case file, conservation note. The record accepted each field with indifferent calm. Systems did not know when a story ended. They only knew when data fit.

At noon, Otto came upstairs, out of breath but unwilling to admit it. "They are early."

"The apprentices?"

"They have questions. Better than being late."

Elian took the new service log from the shelf. Mira followed them down. In the hall, a dozen adults in work jackets stood around Iona, who was explaining the carry sequence with her model. Thom adjusted a display label. Maren checked the visitor count at the door. Celia corrected a student's distance from the case with one raised finger. The room had become itself again, but not the same self as before.

Otto took the crank when the time came. He looked at the group. "This is not magic. If you want magic, you will be disappointed. If you want work, listen."

He turned the engine through one measured cycle. The gears answered. The carriage clicked. A mark appeared on the paper strip, narrow and exact.

Elian stood at the back with the service log open. He wrote the date, time, conditions, operator, output, and note: public educational demonstration, successful hand turn, no abnormal drag.

The record was complete for that moment. It would be incomplete again as soon as the next hand touched the next page. That was proper. Completion in archives was not an ending; it was a condition maintained against loss.

After the visitors left, Elian returned upstairs and turned off the stack light. The false vellum slept in its folder. The blue copy slept in another. The black notebook rested in its box. Downstairs, the Marrow Engine waited for the next group, the next question, the next careful turn.

Elian locked the archive door and left the record complete enough to be trusted.
