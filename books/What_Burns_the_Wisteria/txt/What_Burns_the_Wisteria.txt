What Burns the Wisteria

A Novel

---

Arc One: The Bloom

---

Chapter 1: The First Petal

Yuki Tanaka woke to the smell of wisteria.

She had lived above her flower shop for fifteen years, and the scent had never once reached her bedroom window. The floristry was on the ground floor — Fleurs de Kyoto — and the wisteria grew in the back garden, a dense tangle of gnarled trunk and hanging violet clusters that her grandmother had planted when Yuki was a child. The vine was old, older than Yuki herself, and it bloomed reliably every May. But it had never announced itself through a closed window on the second floor in early April.

Yuki sat up. The alarm clock read 5:47 AM. Pale gray light filtered through the curtains, and the smell was unmistakable — thick, sweet, almost cloying. Wisteria.

She crossed to the window and pushed the curtain aside.

The vine had grown.

It had been a cold spring. The buds had only just begun to swell, and she had expected another two weeks before the first flowers appeared. But there they were — heavy cascades of purple blossoms hanging from every branch, covering the trellis that arched over the garden path. The wisteria was in full, impossible bloom.

Below, her daughter's window faced the garden. Hana was fifteen, still asleep at this hour, her room dark behind a half-drawn blind. Yuki looked down at the vine and felt something cold settle at the base of her spine.

A single tendril had wound itself around the frame of Hana's window.

Yuki blinked. It was there — not a shadow, not a trick of the light. A thin green shoot, no thicker than a finger, curled around the wooden casement like a question mark. It had not been there last night. She was sure of it.

She went downstairs in her robe, the floorboards cool against her bare feet. The shop was dim and quiet, the empty vases waiting on the shelves. She unlocked the back door and stepped into the garden.

The cold air hit her, but the wisteria was warm. She felt it before she touched it — a faint heat radiating from the vine, as if the plant were generating its own life, indifferent to the season. She reached out and brushed a cluster of blooms. They were soft, perfect, each tiny petal a shade of purple so deep it was almost black at the center.

The tendril on Hana's window had grown further since she'd looked from above. It now traced the full length of the frame, curling at the corners as if measuring.

"Yuki."

She turned. Her grandmother stood in the kitchen doorway, wrapped in a faded housecoat. Etsuko was eighty-three, her back curved like a comma, her eyes still sharp.

"The wisteria is blooming," Yuki said.

"I know." Etsuko came down the single step into the garden, her bare feet sure on the cold stone. She looked at the vine for a long moment. Then she looked at the tendril on Hana's window.

"It's early," Yuki said.

"Yes."

"The buds weren't even open yesterday. It's not possible."

Etsuko was quiet. The morning birds had started, their calls thin in the cool air. A neighbor's dog barked once and fell silent.

"When I was a girl," Etsuko said slowly, "before the war, my mother told me something about this vine. She said it came from her mother's garden in the countryside. And that her mother's mother had tended it, and her mother before that."

Yuki had heard the story before — the wisteria had been in the family for generations, a cutting carried from one home to the next. But her grandmother had never said more than that.

"She told me," Etsuko went on, "that the wisteria knows. It knows what's coming."

"Knows what?"

Etsuko pointed at the tendril around Hana's window. Her finger was thin, the joints swollen.

"The vine blooms when someone in the family is going to die."

The words hung in the cold air. Yuki stared at her grandmother. The old woman's face was unreadable.

"That's a superstition," Yuki said. "It's a plant. It blooms when it's ready."

Etsuko lowered her hand. She looked at Yuki with something like pity.

"When your grandfather died," she said, "the wisteria bloomed in November. There was frost on the ground, and the vine was covered in flowers. I went outside and picked one, and it was warm, like it was alive. And then I went inside, and your grandfather was gone."

Yuki shook her head. "That was a coincidence."

"Maybe." Etsuko turned and walked back inside. At the door she paused. "But the vine has never wound around a window before."

She disappeared into the darkness of the house, and Yuki was left alone in the garden, the sweet smell of wisteria pressing against her from all sides.

She looked up at her daughter's window. The tendril had moved. She was certain it had moved — it had been tracing the frame, and now it lay across the glass, a green vein against the dark pane.

The vine was reaching for Hana.

Yuki went inside and started the rice. She checked on her daughter before she left for school. Hana was awake, scrolling through her phone, her dark hair spread across the pillow. She looked young and ordinary and alive.

"Mom. Why does it smell like flowers?"

"The wisteria bloomed early."

Hana frowned. "It's April."

"I know."

"That's weird."

"Yes."

Hana shrugged and returned to her phone. Yuki stood in the doorway for a moment longer, then went downstairs to open the shop.

She spent the morning arranging lilies for a funeral and tried not to think about purple blossoms. But the scent followed her everywhere, clinging to her clothes, her hair, her skin. It was in the shop when customers came and went. It was in the kitchen when she ate lunch alone. It was in her bedroom when she finally lay down that night, exhausted.

She dreamed of vines growing through windows, wrapping around furniture, pulling everything into the earth. She woke gasping at 3 AM, the smell of wisteria thick as smoke in her throat.

She went to the window. The vine had grown across Hana's entire window now, a lattice of green and purple that covered the glass like a hand.

Yuki pulled out her phone and called her grandmother's room. There was no answer. She went downstairs and found Etsuko sitting in the dark kitchen, a cup of tea cold in her hands.

"It covered her window," Yuki said.

"I know," Etsuko said. She did not look up. "It's begun."

Chapter 2: The Inventory of Loss

The next morning, Yuki called in sick for the first time in three years.

She told herself it was exhaustion. A bad night's sleep. The peculiar stress of an unseasonal bloom. But when she pulled back the curtain and saw the wisteria still pressed against Hana's window like a lover at the glass, she knew she was not going to open the shop.

She made tea. She sat at the kitchen table. She watched the light change over the garden while the kettle boiled and cooled and boiled again.

Etsuko came down at eight, moving slowly. She did not mention the wisteria. She prepared her own breakfast — rice, pickles, a piece of grilled fish — and sat across from Yuki, eating with the deliberate patience of someone who had learned that speed changed nothing.

"Should I cut it back?" Yuki asked.

"No."

"Then what do I do?"

Etsuko set down her chopsticks. "Have you told Hana?"

"There's nothing to tell."

"There is everything to tell." Etsuko's voice was flat. "This family has lived with the vine for a hundred years. Your mother knew. I knew. You need to tell her."

Yuki's hands tightened around her cup. "I don't even believe it myself."

"Belief doesn't matter. The vine does what it does whether you believe or not. It bloomed for your mother. It bloomed for your grandfather. It bloomed for my brother, who died in the war before I was born — my mother told me she went outside and the vine was white, covered in white blossoms, and she knew before the telegram came."

"White?"

"Depending on who it's for." Etsuko picked up her chopsticks again. "Purple for an older member of the family. White for a younger one. Pink for someone who will die in violence or sudden. At least, that was how my mother explained it."

Yuki stared at the garden door. Through the glass, the wisteria was a riot of purple. "It's purple."

"Yes."

"Then it's not for Hana."

Etsuko said nothing.

"Grandmother. It's purple. You said purple is for an older member of the family. Hana is fifteen."

"Your mother was thirty-seven when the vine bloomed for her," Etsuko said quietly. "The flowers were purple. She died of a heart defect no one knew she had, three days after the first blossoms appeared."

Yuki remembered her mother. A blur of warmth and jasmine perfume, a laugh that filled rooms. She had collapsed at the grocery store on a Tuesday afternoon. Yuki was twenty at the time, away at university, unable to get home before her mother was already gone.

"Those are the rules?" Yuki could hear her own voice rising. "There are rules for how the death vine communicates?"

"It is not a death vine." Etsuko's voice was sharp. "It is a wisteria. It blooms. That is all it does. The meaning is ours to carry."

"Then why did it wind around Hana's window?"

Etsuko looked down at her hands. "I don't know. It has never done that before."

The admission was more frightening than any explanation. If her grandmother did not know, then no one knew. The vine was doing something new, and there was no family wisdom to interpret it.

Yuki cleaned the kitchen. She swept the shop floor even though it was clean. She arranged flowers that did not need arranging. She avoided the garden.

Mr. Kobayashi came at noon.

He was the owner of the bookstore next door — sixty-seven years old, a widower, with silver hair that fell across his forehead and reading glasses he was always pushing up his nose. He had been a fixture on this street for forty years, and he had been bringing Yuki coffee every Tuesday since her divorce, eight years ago.

He entered through the back — the door between their shops had been cut through during the war, Etsuko had said, when both buildings were a single establishment. He carried two cups of takeout coffee and a paper bag from the bakery.

"Shop's dark," he said. "You never close."

"I needed a day."

He set the coffee on the counter. His eyes went to the garden door, where the wisteria was visible, a cascade of impossible color. "That's early."

"Yes."

"I've never seen it bloom like this." He pushed his glasses up. "It's covered the whole trellis. And the windows."

He had seen the tendril on Hana's window. Yuki felt a flash of something — shame, or fear, she could not tell.

"It's a strange year," she said.

Mr. Kobayashi studied her for a moment. Then he handed her the paper bag. "Croissant. From the French place. You need to eat."

She took the bag. "Thank you."

He did not leave. He stood by the counter, looking at the arrangement she had been working on — white chrysanthemums and blue delphinium, a funeral piece.

"Who is that for?"

"Mrs. Fujimoto. From the corner."

"I heard. She was ninety-two." He paused. "That's a good age."

"It is."

"You should open your shop, Yuki. People need flowers. Even when things die, or maybe especially then. They need something that looks like life."

He left through the connecting door, and Yuki heard the bell of his own shop ring as a customer entered.

She ate the croissant. She drank the coffee. She opened the shop.

The afternoon passed in a blur of small transactions — a retired couple buying a potted orchid, a salaryman picking up a bouquet for his wife's birthday, a teenager buying a single rose that Yuki suspected was for a confession of love. Ordinary life, continuing. The wisteria was there at the edge of her vision, but she did not look at it directly.

Hana came home at four-thirty. She dropped her bag in the entryway and went straight to the kitchen, where Etsuko was preparing vegetables for dinner.

"Grandma. The vine is on my window."

"I know."

"I can't open my curtains. It pressed against the glass. There are petals in the crack."

"I'll clean it."

"But it's weird." Hana's voice was tight. "It wasn't there two days ago. How does a vine grow that fast?"

Etsuko continued cutting carrots. "It is an old plant. Old plants do surprising things."

"It's not normal."

"No," Etsuko agreed. "It is not normal."

Hana stood in the kitchen doorway, waiting for something more. When it did not come, she went upstairs and closed her door.

Yuki watched her grandmother's hands. Steady, precise, unhurried. But there was a tension in her shoulders that Yuki had never seen before.

That night, Yuki went to Hana's room after she was asleep. She stood in the darkness, listening to her daughter breathe. The vine cast shadows across the curtain, a lacework of leaves and blossoms.

She reached out and touched the glass. On the other side, a tendril pressed back, as if responding.

Yuki pulled her hand away.

Chapter 3: What Mothers Pass Down

Three days passed.

The wisteria continued to bloom. The flowers did not fade or fall. The vine grew thicker, darker, more insistent. It had covered the back wall of the house now, creeping up toward the second-floor eaves. The air around the garden was heavy, perfumed, almost suffocating.

Yuki kept the shop open. She filled orders for weddings and funerals and anniversaries. She smiled at customers. She did not tell anyone about the vine.

But people noticed. Mrs. Yamamoto from the fabric shop commented on the smell. Mr. Endo, who delivered the fish, asked if she had planted something new. The postman remarked that he had never seen wisteria so early.

"It's just a lucky season," Yuki said each time, and each time the lie felt heavier.

On the fourth day, Etsuko called her upstairs.

Her grandmother's room was at the front of the house, facing the street. It was spare and clean — a futon rolled in the corner, a low dresser, a single alcove with a scroll and a vase. Etsuko sat on the floor, an old wooden box open before her.

"Sit down."

Yuki sat.

Her grandmother lifted objects from the box one by one. A photograph of a young man in military uniform, his face serious. A lock of black hair tied with red string. A jade comb. A letter, yellowed, the ink faded to brown.

"This is my mother's box," Etsuko said. "She showed it to me when I was your age. She showed me everything, so I would know what the vine had taken."

"Taken?"

"Every death in this family is marked in this box." Etsuko touched the photograph. "My uncle Kenji. The vine bloomed white three days before his plane was shot down over the Pacific." She touched the lock of hair. "My older sister, Yumi. She was six. The vine bloomed pink, and she died of a fever the same night."

"Why have you never shown me this?"

"Because I hoped it would end with me." Etsuko looked up. "I thought if I did not show you, if I did not pass it on, the vine's power would fade. That it was a belief, not a truth. That if no one believed, it would stop."

"But it didn't stop."

"No. It is waiting for Hana."

Yuki's chest tightened. "You don't know that."

Etsuko opened the letter. The handwriting was elegant, the characters precise. "My mother wrote this to me before she died. She said the vine chooses who it shows itself to. It wrapped around my window before my grandmother passed. It wrapped around your mother's window before I fell ill — the illness that nearly killed me when you were a child. And now it has wrapped around Hana's."

"I don't remember that."

"You were seven. I told you it was a construction accident. The vine had damaged the wall and I had it repaired. You didn't question it."

Yuki remembered nothing. She had been a child, absorbed in her own world. She had trusted her grandmother.

"When my mother was dying," Etsuko said, "I asked her why the vine did this. Why it bloomed, why it chose windows, why it seemed to know. She said she did not know. She said the vine had always been with the family, longer than anyone could remember. It was not a curse. It was not a gift. It was simply there, like the attic, like the foundation. A fact of the house."

"But Hana—"

"Is young. And the vine is interested in her in a way it has never been interested in anyone before." Etsuko closed the box. "I do not know what that means. But I know we cannot ignore it."

That night, Yuki could not sleep. She went downstairs and stood in the garden, barefoot on the cold stone. The wisteria was luminous in the moonlight, the purple blossoms glowing like low fire.

She reached out and touched the main trunk. It was warm, as before. She could feel something under her palm — not a pulse, exactly, but a vibration. A deep, slow hum, like a cello string held too long.

"I don't understand you," she whispered.

The vine did not answer. But the vibration grew stronger, and she felt it travel up her arm, settling in her chest.

She pulled her hand away and went back inside.

In the morning, Hana was gone.

Chapter 4: The Bookstore Oracle

Yuki found her at Mr. Kobayashi's shop.

She had run through the house first, calling her daughter's name, her heart hammering. She had checked the garden, the bathroom, the closets, as if a fifteen-year-old might be hiding in the coat closet. She had woken Etsuko, who had risen without alarm and said only, "Check next door."

Mr. Kobayashi's bookstore was called The Paper Raven. It was a narrow storefront crammed with shelves from floor to ceiling, the kind of shop where books migrated across surfaces in slow tectonic drift. Yuki found Hana in the back corner, sitting on the floor with a book open on her lap.

She was reading. She was fine.

"What are you doing?" Yuki's voice came out harsher than she intended.

Hana looked up, startled. "Reading."

"You left without telling me."

"It's next door. I texted you."

Yuki checked her phone. There was a message: At Mr. Kobayashi's. Reading. Sent thirty minutes ago. She had not heard it arrive.

"I'm sorry," Yuki said. "I didn't — I'm sorry. I woke up and you weren't there."

Hana studied her with the unsettling directness of teenagers. "You're acting weird, Mom. You've been acting weird since the flowers bloomed."

Mr. Kobayashi appeared at the end of the aisle, adjusting his glasses. "She's been coming by after school. Reads in the poetry section. I don't mind."

Yuki had not known this. Hana had never mentioned it.

"I should get back to the shop," Yuki said. "Hana, come home when you're done."

She left quickly, but she did not go to her shop. She stood in the alley between the two buildings, in the shadow of the wisteria, and pressed her hands against her face.

The vine knew something. Her grandmother knew something. Her daughter was reading poetry in a bookshop, oblivious, and the vine was covering her window like a burial shroud.

Mr. Kobayashi came out the back door of his shop. He did not seem surprised to find her there.

"You look like you need to talk."

"I don't know what I need."

He leaned against the wall. The morning sun was just clearing the roofline, casting long shadows. "I've known your family for forty years. I knew your mother. I knew your grandmother when she was younger than you are now. I know about the wisteria."

Yuki turned to face him. "What do you know?"

"Not enough. But I know it bloomed when your mother died. I remember because I brought flowers to the house — I was still a young man, I had just bought the shop — and the garden was full of wisteria blossoms, even though it was autumn. I thought it was strange, but I didn't say anything."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because Etsuko asked me not to." He pushed his glasses up. "She said the vine was private. A family matter. And I respected that."

"But you're telling me now."

"Because Hana came to my shop this morning and asked me if I knew anything about the wisteria on her window. She said you wouldn't tell her the truth."

Yuki felt the ground tilt slightly. "She asked you?"

"She's a smart girl. She knows something is wrong. She came looking for answers." He paused. "I didn't give her any. I told her to ask you. But she won't wait forever, Yuki. Children never do."

Yuki went back into her shop. She stood at the counter, staring at the flowers, seeing nothing.

At noon, Hana came home for lunch. She sat at the kitchen table across from Yuki and said, "Tell me about the vine."

Etsuko was at the sink. She did not turn around.

"It's an old plant," Yuki said. "It blooms early sometimes."

"That's not true." Hana's voice was flat. "You're lying. Grandma is lying. Everyone is lying except maybe Mr. Kobayashi, and he told me to ask you."

Yuki looked at her daughter. Hana was young, yes. But she was not a child. Her face had the sharp lines of someone who had already learned to spot deception.

"Finish your lunch," Yuki said. "We'll talk tonight."

"If you don't tell me, I'll find out myself."

"Hana—"

"I mean it. I'll look in the attic. I'll read grandma's box. I'll search online. There's always a way."

Etsuko turned from the sink. Her eyes met Yuki's, and there was something in them — acceptance, maybe. Or resignation.

"Tell her," Etsuko said. "She deserves to know."

Yuki closed her eyes.

"The wisteria blooms when someone in the family is going to die," she said. "And it wrapped around your window."

The silence stretched. Hana's face went through several expressions — disbelief, confusion, fear — before settling on something Yuki could not read.

"That's insane," Hana said finally.

"Yes."

"It's not—" Hana stopped. "You actually believe this?"

"Your grandmother believed it. Her mother believed it." Yuki's voice was tired. "I didn't believe it either, until three days ago."

"But it's a plant." Hana's voice was rising. "It can't know things. Plants don't know things."

"Then explain it."

Hana opened her mouth. Closed it. Opened it again.

"I don't know," she said. "But there has to be a normal explanation. Something scientific. Maybe the soil changed. Maybe there was a chemical spill. Maybe it's global warming."

"Maybe."

"So why are you acting like it's magic?"

Yuki did not answer. She could feel the vine's presence outside, even through the walls. She could smell it. She could feel its warmth, its slow pulse.

"Because I touched it," Yuki said. "And it felt alive. Not like a plant. Like something else."

Hana stared at her. Then she stood up, left her lunch half-eaten, and went to her room.

Yuki heard her window open — it took effort, the vine had sealed it shut — and then the sound of tearing.

She ran upstairs. Hana had forced the window open and was pulling at the tendrils, breaking them, snapping the green shoots with her bare hands.

"Hana, stop!"

"It's just a plant!" Hana was crying now, the tears running down her face. "It's just a stupid plant and I'm not going to let it kill me!"

She ripped a handful of blossoms from the vine and threw them on the floor. The petals scattered, purple against the wood.

And then she stopped. She was holding a single flower, and it was changing color.

The purple was draining from it, from its center outward, replaced by a pale, shimmering white.

Hana dropped it. The flower lay on the floor, white as bone.

"It changed," she whispered.

Yuki picked it up. The petals were warm, and they were white. Pure white.

The color for a young member of the family.

Chapter 5: The Silence of Roots

The white flower did not revert.

Yuki placed it on the kitchen windowsill, and it stayed white for the rest of the day, and the day after, and the day after that. The other flowers remained purple, but the one Hana had touched was bleached of all color, as if the vine had marked her.

Hana did not touch the wisteria again. She kept her window closed, the curtains drawn. She went to school, came home, ate dinner, did homework. She did not speak about the flower, or the vine, or what Yuki had told her.

The silence was worse than the questions.

On the fifth day, Yuki called her ex-husband.

Takeshi lived in Osaka with his new wife and a baby boy. He answered on the second ring, his voice distracted — he was at work, an office manager for a logistics company, always busy.

"Yuki. Is everything okay?"

"No. Can you come to Kyoto?"

There was a pause. She could hear him moving to a quieter space. "What happened?"

She told him about the wisteria. She did not soften it, did not frame it as a question. She told him the vine was growing over Hana's window, that it bloomed impossibly, that a flower in her daughter's hand had turned white.

He was quiet for a long time.

"Yuki. You know how that sounds."

"I know."

"Are you sleeping?"

"I know how it sounds, Takeshi. But I'm asking you to come and see for yourself."

Another pause. She heard him exhale. "I'll come this weekend. I'll stay at a hotel."

"Hana needs to see you."

"She can see me. I'm not—" He stopped. "I'll be there Saturday morning."

He hung up. Yuki held the phone for a long time, listening to nothing.

Saturday came. Takeshi arrived at nine, a duffel bag over his shoulder. He was broader than he had been during their marriage, softer around the edges, but his eyes were the same — practical, skeptical, unwilling to be deceived.

He looked at the wisteria first. Mr. Kobayashi had trimmed the front walkway so he could see the full sweep of the vine from the street. Takeshi stood with his hands in his pockets, staring at the mass of purple blossoms covering the back of the house.

"Okay," he said. "That's not normal."

"No."

He walked to the garden. He touched the trunk. He frowned. "It's warm."

"Yes."

"It's April. The ground is still cold. The plant should be dormant."

"I know."

He looked at Hana's window. The vine had regrown what she had torn away, denser than before. The glass was barely visible.

"She's inside?"

"Asleep. She's been sleeping more than usual."

Takeshi turned to face her. "Yuki. What do you think this is?"

"I don't know. But the vine has been in my family for generations. My grandmother says it blooms when someone is going to die."

"And you believe her?"

"I didn't. But then Hana touched it and the flower turned white in her hand. And now I don't know what to believe."

Takeshi looked back at the vine. "There has to be an explanation."

"Then find it. You're the practical one."

He took out his phone and started taking photos. He took samples of the leaves and blossoms, placing them in plastic bags. He scraped a bit of bark from the main trunk. He noted the weather conditions, the soil moisture, the angle of the sun.

Yuki watched him work and felt nothing. She had hoped — what? That he would confirm her fears? That he would dismiss them? She did not know.

That afternoon, Hana came downstairs. She saw her father and stopped on the bottom step.

"Dad."

"Hana." He crossed to her and hugged her. She did not resist, but she did not return the embrace either. "How are you holding up?"

"Fine."

"The vine—"

"I know about the vine. Mom told me."

Takeshi looked at Yuki over Hana's head. "I'm going to take some samples to a botanist friend at Kyoto University. He'll tell me what's going on."

"Nothing is going on." Hana's voice was flat. "It's just a plant."

"Hana—"

"It's just a plant." She pulled away from him. "And I'm going to my room."

She went back upstairs. They heard her door close.

Takeshi sat down at the kitchen table. Yuki sat across from him. Etsuko was in the garden, pruning the lower branches of the wisteria with careful, deliberate cuts.

"She won't talk to me," Takeshi said.

"She won't talk to me either."

"It's the teenage thing, right? It's normal?"

"Maybe." Yuki looked toward the garden. "Or maybe she knows something we don't."

Chapter 6: The Botanist's Doubt

Takeshi's contact at Kyoto University was a man named Dr. Ryo Hayashi — a plant physiologist with a specialty in floral development. He was young for a professor, early forties, with a runner's build and the kind of intense focus that made people uncomfortable.

He received the samples on Tuesday. By Thursday, he had called Takeshi and asked to see the vine in person.

He arrived on Saturday, two weeks after the first bloom. He carried a digital camera, a handheld spectrometer, and a leather satchel full of instruments Yuki could not identify.

The vine had grown further. It now covered the entire back wall of the house and part of the roof. The blossoms had not faded. The air was thick with their scent.

Dr. Hayashi spent two hours examining the vine. He took samples of the soil, the flowers, the leaves, the bark. He measured the temperature of the trunk at different points. He scraped mycorrhizal samples from the roots. He photographed every angle.

Then he sat down at the kitchen table, accepted a cup of tea, and said, "I don't have an explanation."

"What do you mean?" Takeshi asked.

"I mean, by every metric I can measure, this plant should not be blooming. Its phenological schedule is off by six weeks. The floral development is accelerated beyond anything I've seen. And the pigmentation — the concentration of anthocyanins in the petals is statistically anomalous. It should not be this purple."

"But it's still a wisteria," Yuki said. "It's a normal plant."

Dr. Hayashi set down his tea. "The cellular structure is normal. The DNA sequence is consistent with Wisteria floribunda. But the metabolic activity—" He shook his head. "This plant is photosynthesizing at three times the normal rate. It's drawing nutrients from the soil at a depth I can't account for. The root system, I suspect, has extended far beyond what is typical, possibly hundreds of meters."

"Hundreds of meters?" Takeshi leaned forward. "How is that possible?"

"I don't know. I would need to do a full excavation to confirm, and I can't do that without damaging the plant. Or the house, for that matter."

"So it's a mystery," Yuki said.

"It's a phenomenon." Dr. Hayashi looked at her with what seemed like genuine curiosity. "There may be a perfectly natural explanation. A subterranean thermal vent, perhaps. A mineral deposit that triggers early flowering. A unique genetic mutation passed down through vegetative propagation."

"But you don't think so."

"I'm a scientist. I deal in evidence, not belief. But I will tell you: I have never seen anything like it. And the rate of growth—" He paused. "If it continues at this pace, the vine will cover the entire house within a month."

The silence that followed was broken only by the sound of Etsuko's shears in the garden.

Dr. Hayashi left his card. He said he would run more tests. He said he would be in touch.

Yuki saw him to the door. At the threshold, he turned back.

"Mrs. Tanaka. One more thing."

"Yes?"

"The flowers. They're warm. I don't know why. But they're consistently three degrees above ambient temperature." He looked at her with an expression she could not read. "That should not be possible."

He left. Yuki closed the door and leaned her forehead against the wood.

Takeshi was packing his bag in the living room. He had taken three days off work, but he had to return. His wife was calling. The baby was sick.

"I'll come back next weekend," he said. "I'll bring more equipment."

"It won't matter."

He stopped. "What do you mean?"

Yuki turned from the door. "The vine is not a scientific problem. It's a family problem. It has been in this family for a hundred years. It knows us. It knows Hana. And I don't think there's a machine in the world that can explain it."

Takeshi looked at her for a long moment. Then he closed his bag and said, "I'll call you tomorrow."

He kissed her forehead — a gesture from their marriage, a habit he had never quite broken — and left.

Yuki stood in the empty living room. Through the back window, she could see Etsuko still pruning, her movements slow and steady, as if she had all the time in the world.

But the vine was growing faster now. She could see it moving, just at the edges, the way you could see a clock's minute hand if you stared long enough.

It was climbing toward the roof.

It was spreading toward the front of the house.

It was finding new windows.

---

Chapter 7: Kobayashi's Crossing

Mr. Kobayashi found Yuki in the garden at midnight.

She could not sleep. The vine's presence was a pressure in the air, a constant reminder. She had come outside to breathe, but the wisteria's scent was everywhere — sweet, heavy, inescapable.

"You're out late," he said, appearing at the back gate.

"I could say the same."

He carried a bottle of sake and two cups. "I couldn't sleep either. Old men have restless nights."

They sat on the stone step overlooking the garden. Mr. Kobayashi poured sake into the cups. The liquid caught the moonlight.

"Tell me about Hana's father," he said.

"You know Takeshi."

"I know he's a good man who wasn't right for you. But I don't know when you knew."

Yuki drank. The sake was warm in her throat. "I knew before we married. I ignored it."

"Why?"

"Because I was pregnant. Because my mother had just died. Because I thought marriage would fix things that marriage couldn't fix."

Mr. Kobayashi nodded. He did not offer advice, which was why she trusted him.

"Etsuko told me about the vine's history," he said. "Not all of it. Enough."

"How much is enough?"

"That it bloomed for your mother. For your grandfather. For others. And that it has never wound around a child's window before."

Yuki looked at him. His face was lined, familiar. "What do you think it means?"

"I think the vine is trying to tell you something. But I don't think it's telling you that Hana will die."

"What else could it mean?"

Mr. Kobayashi refilled her cup. "In the old stories — the real old stories, from before the war — wisteria was a bridge. It connected this world and the other world. People planted it at the boundaries of their homes to keep the spirits out, or to welcome them in, depending on the story."

"Grandmother never told me that."

"Your grandmother's family kept the vine for a reason. They could have cut it down, moved away, stopped the cycle. But they didn't. They tended it. They passed it down."

"Because they couldn't destroy it."

"Because they didn't want to." He looked at the vine, its blossoms dark against the night sky. "It was part of them. Part of the family. The blooms, the deaths — they were connected to the same root system."

Yuki was quiet. The sake was loosening something in her chest, a knot she had been carrying for weeks.

"Hana asked me yesterday if I thought she was going to die."

Mr. Kobayashi's expression did not change. "What did you tell her?"

"I told her no. I told her I would protect her. But I don't know how to protect someone from a plant."

"You protect her the same way you protect her from anything. You stay. You pay attention. You tell her the truth when you can."

"Is that enough?"

"It's all there is."

They finished the sake in silence. The vine hummed in the dark, warm and patient.

When Yuki went inside, she checked on Hana. Her daughter was asleep, her face peaceful in the dim light. The vine cast shadows across her walls, moving slightly, as if breathing.

Yuki sat beside her bed until the sun came up.

---

Chapter 8: The Weight of White

Hana woke with petals in her hair.

She found them when she was brushing her teeth — three white wisteria petals tangled in the dark strands. She held them under the bathroom light, turning them over.

White. Not purple.

She came downstairs with the petals in her palm. Yuki was making coffee, Etsuko was setting out breakfast.

"These were in my hair," Hana said.

The three women looked at the petals. Three white petals, fresh, as if they had just fallen.

"Were your windows open?" Yuki asked.

"No. They were closed. The vine is sealed against them."

"They must have come through a crack."

"You know that's not true."

Etsuko reached out and took one of the petals. She held it to her nose, then pressed it to her lips.

"They're for you," she said. "The vine is marking you."

Hana's face went pale. "Why?"

"I don't know. But it started when you touched it. You changed it. You made it white."

"I didn't do anything."

"You touched it." Etsuko's voice was soft, almost reverent. "You touched it, and it responded. It has never responded to anyone like that before."

"What does that mean?"

"I think the vine has chosen you."

Hana stared at her great-grandmother. Then she dropped the petals in the sink and walked out of the kitchen.

Yuki found her in her room, packing a bag.

"I'm leaving."

"Where will you go?"

"I don't know. Dad's house. A friend's. Anywhere but here."

"Hana—"

"It's in my hair, Mom. It's growing through the walls. I could feel it last night, moving, pressing against my bed. It wants me. And I don't know why."

Yuki sat on the edge of the bed. "If you leave, it might follow."

"How can a plant follow me?"

"Your grandmother said it's connected to the family. If you're part of the family, it's connected to you."

Hana stopped packing. She looked at Yuki with an expression that was half fear, half anger.

"Then what do I do?"

"I don't know. But I think running won't help."

Hana sat down on the floor, her back against the wall. The vine was visible through the window, pressed against the glass, patient and waiting.

"How long has it been?" Hana asked.

"Since the first bloom? Fifteen days."

"How long do we have?"

Yuki did not answer. She could not.

---

Chapter 9: Etsuko's Confession

That evening, Etsuko asked them to gather in the garden.

It was the first time they had all sat together among the wisteria since the bloom began. Yuki brought chairs. Hana came reluctantly. The three generations sat in a triangle, the vine arching above them, the blossoms brushing their shoulders.

"I need to tell you something I have never told anyone," Etsuko said.

Her voice was thin in the evening air. She was smaller than she had been a month ago, as if the vine were drawing something from her.

"When I was sixteen, the vine bloomed for my mother. I knew what it meant. I had seen it before, for my uncle, for my sister. But this was different. This was my mother, and I was not ready to lose her."

She paused. The wisteria swayed, though there was no wind.

"I went into the garden at night. I was angry. I wanted to hurt the vine the way it was going to hurt me. I took a knife and I cut into the main trunk."

Yuki inhaled sharply. "You cut the vine?"

"I cut deep. I wanted to kill it. I thought if the vine died, my mother would live."

"And did it work?"

Etsuko shook her head. "The vine healed overnight. The wound closed as if it had never been made. But the next morning, my mother was worse. The doctors had no explanation. They said she had taken a sudden turn."

"What happened to her?"

"She died three days later. I always believed I made it worse. That my violence toward the vine hurt her somehow."

Yuki reached for her grandmother's hand. "You were a child. You didn't know."

"I knew. And I did it anyway." Etsuko's eyes were wet. "I tell you this because you need to understand: the vine does not respond to force. It responds to attention. To acknowledgment. It is not an enemy. It is a witness."

"Witness to what?" Hana's voice was barely a whisper.

"To the family. To the life that passes through these walls. It has been here longer than any of us, and it will be here after we are gone. It is not punishing us. It is recording us."

The three women sat in silence. The vine's blossoms glowed in the twilight, purple and white together.

That night, Hana dreamed she was roots.

She dreamed she was underground, extending through dark soil, feeling the weight of the house above her. She felt the family's history in the dirt — layers of grief and joy and ordinary days. She felt the bones of ancestors, the water of old tears, the heat of buried anger.

She woke with dirt under her fingernails.

---

Chapter 10: The Trembling Earth

On the eighteenth day, the ground moved.

It was a small earthquake — a 3.2 on the scale, common enough in Kyoto. Dishes rattled. Picture frames shifted. The house groaned.

But when it was over, the garden was different.

The wisteria had grown during the quake. Not much, but enough to notice. The main trunk had thickened. The tendrils had extended. The blossoms were denser, heavier, as if the shaking had fed them.

And the root system had surfaced.

Along the back wall of the garden, the earth had cracked, and beneath it, the roots of the wisteria were visible — not the thin, fibrous roots of a normal plant, but thick cables of wood, some as wide as a human arm, pulsing with faint light.

Hana was the one who found them. She had come outside to assess the damage and saw the glowing roots in the fresh cracks of the earth.

"Mom! Grandma! Come look!"

Yuki and Etsuko joined her. The three of them stood over the exposed roots, watching the faint luminescence pulse in slow rhythm.

"It's alive," Hana whispered.

"It's always been alive," Etsuko said. "We are just seeing it now."

Yuki knelt and touched a root. It was warm, like the rest of the vine. She could feel the pulse beneath her fingers, slow and deep, like a heartbeat.

It matched her own.

She looked up at Etsuko. Her grandmother's face was pale in the dim light.

"How deep do the roots go?" Yuki asked.

"I do not know."

"Under the house?"

"Yes. Probably."

"Under Hana's room?"

Etsuko did not answer. She did not need to.

Yuki stood. Her hand was still on the root, and she could feel it moving, growing, extending further into the earth.

"We need to dig," she said.

"Dig where?" Hana asked.

"Under your room. I need to see what the vine is doing beneath us."

Hana hesitated. Then she nodded.

Yuki fetched a shovel from the shed. The earth was soft, broken by the quake. She dug alongside the foundation, the blade cutting into the soil with wet, heavy sounds.

The roots were everywhere.

Beneath the topsoil, the ground was a lattice of wisteria roots, interwoven so densely that the soil itself seemed secondary. They glowed faintly in the dark, a network of light running beneath the house.

And at the center, directly below Hana's window, the roots converged on something.

Yuki dug deeper. The shovel struck something hard. She cleared the soil with her hands.

It was a bone.

Human.

Long and white, curved like a rib, embedded in the root system as if the vine had grown around it, through it, claimed it.

Yuki sat back, her hands covered in dirt, the bone pale in the glowing light of the roots.

"What is that?" Hana's voice was high, thin.

Etsuko stepped forward. She looked down at the bone. Her face was very still, very old.

"That," she said, "is what the vine has been hiding."

--- End of Arc One ---

Arc Two: The Roots

---

Chapter 11: What Lies Beneath

Yuki did not sleep that night.

The bone sat in a wooden box on the kitchen table, cleaned of soil, pale and silent. It was a rib — Dr. Hayashi confirmed it by phone, after Yuki sent him photographs. Human. Adult. Could not determine age or sex without further analysis.

"Where did you find it?" he asked.

"Under my house. In the root system of the wisteria."

The pause on his end was long enough to become its own kind of answer.

"I'm coming back to Kyoto," he said. "Do not disturb anything else."

But Yuki had already disturbed everything.

She sat at the table with the box, her tea cooling beside her. Etsuko had gone to bed without comment. Hana had retreated to her room but had not slept — Yuki could hear her moving, pacing, opening and closing drawers.

The bone was not the first.

If the wisteria had grown around one, it had grown around others. The root network beneath the house was vast, interwoven, and patient. The question was not whether there were more bones.

The question was whose.

Mr. Kobayashi arrived at dawn. He let himself in through the connecting door and found Yuki still at the table, the box open.

"Etsuko called me," he said.

"She told you?"

"She said you found something." He sat across from her, his movements careful. "A bone."

Yuki pushed the box toward him. He looked inside, his face unreadable.

"Rib," he said.

"Yes."

He was quiet for a long time. Then he said, "Your grandmother's mother died in this house. Your great-grandmother. She was the one who planted the wisteria, or so the story goes."

"Planted it, or brought it?"

"There's a difference?"

"I think so. I think the vine was already here. I think my great-grandmother did not plant it so much as accept it."

Mr. Kobayashi closed the box. "What are you going to do?"

"Dig."

"Under the entire house?"

"If I have to."

"That would take weeks. And the vine—" He looked toward the garden, where the wisteria was visible through the window, dense and dark. "The vine will not let you."

"It's a plant. It doesn't have a choice."

"It has been growing for a hundred years. It has roots under this house, under the street, under the neighboring buildings. It knows what it wants."

Yuki looked at him. "What does it want?"

"I think it wants to be known. All of it. Not just the flowers, but the roots. The history. The bones."

"Then why hide them?"

"Because we were not ready to see." He pushed his glasses up. "But the earthquake changed something. The vine revealed itself. It is no longer content to wait."

Hana came downstairs, her face pale, dark circles under her eyes. "I dreamed about the roots again."

"What did you see?" Yuki asked.

"I saw them under the whole street. Under Mr. Kobayashi's shop. Under the corner store. Under the shrine at the end of the block." She sat down heavily. "They're everywhere, Mom. The whole neighborhood is built on top of them."

Yuki felt the floor beneath her feet differently. The solidity of the house, the street, the city — all of it resting on a network of roots that had been growing for a century, perhaps longer.

"We need to dig," she said. "All of it."

"Where do we start?" Hana asked.

Yuki looked at the box, the bone, the silent witness of the vine.

"At the beginning," she said. "Where my great-grandmother is buried."

---

Chapter 12: The Cemetery of Roots

The temple cemetery was three blocks from the shop, a quiet corner of old Kyoto where the dead were stacked in generations. Yuki had been there for funerals, for Obon, for the ordinary rituals of remembrance. She had never gone looking for a grave that might not exist.

Her great-grandmother's name was Kiyo Tanaka. She had died in 1945, according to family records — the last year of the war. But when Yuki reached the Tanaka family plot, the grave marker was different from what she remembered.

The stone was older. The characters were worn. And the name carved into it was not Kiyo.

It was Hisae Tanaka. Died 1943.

Yuki knelt in front of the grave. The moss had grown thick over the inscription. It had been undisturbed for decades.

"Who is Hisae?" Hana asked, standing behind her.

"I don't know. I've never heard that name."

Etsuko had not come. She had said she was too tired, but Yuki suspected it was something else. Her grandmother had been avoiding the garden, the kitchen, the back of the house. She had been staying in her room, door closed.

Yuki traced the characters with her finger. Hisae. A woman's name. A Tanaka name.

She walked the cemetery, searching for Kiyo. There was no grave with that name. Not in the Tanaka plot, not anywhere in the cemetery.

"Maybe she was cremated," Hana said. "Maybe she's in a columbarium."

"Maybe." But Yuki did not believe it.

She returned to the shop and called Dr. Hayashi. She asked him to find everything he could about Hisae Tanaka and Kiyo Tanaka in the municipal records.

He called back within two hours.

"Hisae Tanaka was born in 1910, died 1943. Cause of death: listed as 'complications from illness.' She was twenty-three years old."

"And Kiyo?"

"There is no Kiyo Tanaka in the records. Not in Kyoto, not in the prefecture. The name appears nowhere official."

Yuki sat down. "Then who was my great-grandmother?"

"That is a question I cannot answer from records alone." He paused. "But there is something else. Hisae Tanaka's death certificate lists her mother as 'Miyako' — no surname. And her address at time of death is listed as your current address."

"She died in my house."

"Yes."

"At twenty-three."

"Yes."

Yuki looked at the bone in the box. The rib of an adult. "Could Hisae have been buried under the house?"

"It is not uncommon in old Japanese families for certain members to be buried on the property. Especially in rural areas, or in families that wanted to keep their dead close."

"Was she buried under the wisteria?"

"I cannot confirm that without excavation. But it would explain the root growth around the bone you found. If the vine grew through her remains, it would have absorbed her into its structure."

Yuki closed her eyes. The vine had grown through a woman. A twenty-three-year-old woman, dead in her house, buried in her garden.

And there was no record of Kiyo at all.

She went to Etsuko's room. Her grandmother was sitting by the window, looking at the street. She did not turn when Yuki entered.

"Who was Kiyo?" Yuki asked.

Etsuko was silent.

"Grandmother. Who was Kiyo?"

"Kiyo was my mother," Etsuko said. "But she was not born a Tanaka. She was not born anything, really. She appeared."

"Appeared?"

"During the war. In 1944. She came to the house looking for shelter. Hisae — my older sister — was living here alone after our parents died. Kiyo arrived at her door, starving, without documents. Hisae took her in."

Yuki sat on the floor. "Hisae was your sister?"

"Yes. And Kiyo was her companion. Her partner. They lived together in this house, and the vine was already here, and it watched them."

"What happened to Hisae?"

"She died." Etsuko's voice was flat. "The vine bloomed pink, and she died. It was sudden. The doctors said it was a fever, but I knew. I was ten years old, and I knew."

"Pink?"

"Sudden death. Violence. The color for someone taken before their time."

Yuki thought of the white flower in Hana's hand. "And Kiyo?"

"Kiyo stayed. She raised me. She became my mother in every way that mattered. She took the Tanaka name, though she had no right to it. And when she died, I buried her."

"Where?"

Etsuko finally turned. Her eyes were old, and tired, and full of things she had never said.

"Under the wisteria," she said. "Where else?"

---

Chapter 13: The Witness of Wood

Dr. Hayashi arrived that afternoon with a ground-penetrating radar unit.

He spent three hours scanning the garden, the foundation, the alley between the shops. The results were written in colored bands on a screen, but even Yuki could read them.

The roots extended further than anyone had guessed. They ran beneath the entire block, a web of organic matter that connected every building on the street. At the center, under the house, was a dense mass that the radar could not penetrate.

"What is that?" Yuki asked, pointing at the dark area on the screen.

Dr. Hayashi zoomed in. "I don't know. The roots are too thick there. It's like a wall of organic material."

"How deep?"

"At least eight meters. Possibly more. The signal degrades beyond that."

"So there could be more bones."

"There could be a lot of things." He looked at her. "Do you want to dig?"

Yuki looked at the garden, the vine, the house where three generations of women had lived and died. The vine was patient. It had been waiting.

"Yes," she said. "But not yet. First, I need to talk to the street."

She spent the rest of the day visiting neighbors. She went to Mrs. Yamamoto at the fabric shop, to Mr. Endo the fishmonger, to the priest at the temple. She asked the same questions: How long have you lived here? Have you noticed anything strange about the wisteria? Has the vine ever done anything unusual?

Most of them said no. The wisteria was just a plant. It bloomed early sometimes. Nothing more.

But Mr. Endo remembered something.

"When I was a boy," he said, "my grandmother told me not to go near the Tanaka house at night. She said the vine could hear you. That it would remember what you said."

"Did she say why?"

"She said the vine held the dead. That it was a kind of graveyard, but alive. She said the Tanaka women were buried in the roots, and the vine was their tombstone."

Yuki thanked him and walked home slowly, the twilight settling around her like dust.

She found Hana in the garden, sitting at the base of the wisteria, her hand on the main trunk.

"It's humming," Hana said.

"What?"

"The vine. It's humming. Can you feel it?"

Yuki knelt and placed her palm on the trunk. The vibration was there, faint but unmistakable — a low note, almost below hearing, that resonated in her chest.

"It's singing," Hana said. "I think it's singing to me."

---

Chapter 14: Hana's Descent

That night, Hana did not come inside for dinner.

Yuki found her still in the garden, but she had moved. She was lying on the ground, her body stretched along the length of a root that had surfaced during the earthquake. Her eyes were closed. Her breathing was slow.

"Hana."

No response.

"Hana!" Yuki shook her shoulder. Hana's eyes opened, but they were different — distant, focused on something far away.

"Mom." Her voice was dreamy. "I saw her. Kiyo. She was standing in the garden, and the vine was growing through her. She was made of flowers."

Yuki pulled her upright. Hana swayed, blinking.

"What happened?"

"I don't know. I lay down, and I could feel the roots under me. They were warm. And then I was somewhere else."

"Where?"

"Under the house. In the dark. But I could see everything — every root, every bone, every person who had ever touched the vine. They were all there, Mom. All of them."

Yuki carried her inside. Hana was light, fragile in her arms. She put her to bed and sat beside her until she fell asleep.

Then she went downstairs and confronted Etsuko.

"The vine is taking her."

"It is showing her."

"I don't want it to show her anything. I want it to leave her alone."

Etsuko looked at her calmly. "That is not your choice. It was never your choice. The vine chooses, and the family responds. That is the way it has always been."

"Then change it. You're the oldest. You've been here the longest. Tell the vine to take you instead."

"I would, if I could. But the vine does not want me." Etsuko's voice was soft. "It wants her. She has the gift."

"What gift?"

"The gift of seeing. When I was young, I thought everyone could feel the vine. I thought the warmth, the pulse, the dreams — I thought they were normal. But they were not. They were because the vine had chosen me, too."

"Chosen you for what?"

"To bear witness. To remember. To pass the story on."

Etsuko reached out and took Yuki's hand. Her grip was stronger than Yuki expected.

"Your mother did not have the gift," Etsuko said. "You do not have the gift. But Hana — Hana has it stronger than anyone. She turned the flowers white with a touch. She hears the vine's song. She walks in the roots. She is the one the vine has been waiting for."

"Waiting for? For what?"

"I do not know. But I think we are about to find out."

---

Chapter 15: The City Beneath

The next morning, Hana did not wake up.

Yuki checked on her at seven. She was asleep, her breathing deep and regular. At nine, she was still asleep. At noon, Yuki could not rouse her.

"Hana. Wake up."

Hana's eyelids fluttered. Her mouth moved. But her eyes did not open.

Yuki called Dr. Hayashi. He arrived within the hour, checked Hana's vitals, shone a light in her eyes.

"She's in a kind of trance," he said. "Her brain activity is elevated — she's dreaming, but consciously. I've seen similar states in deep meditation, but never this prolonged."

"Can you wake her?"

"I can try. Physiologically, she should wake. The fact that she isn't suggests a psychological barrier. She may be somewhere she does not want to leave."

Yuki looked at her daughter, still and silent, her hands folded on her chest.

"Where is she?"

"I don't know. But if I had to guess, she is underground."

Yuki sat beside Hana's bed for three hours, holding her hand. The vine pressed against the window, patient and watchful.

At four in the afternoon, Hana spoke.

"Mom."

"I'm here."

"Don't be scared."

"I'm not scared." She was terrified.

"The roots go all the way down. Past the city. Past the old city. Past the bones. There's something at the bottom."

"Something?"

"A seed. A very old seed. Older than the house. Older than Kyoto. It's been waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For someone to find it. Someone who can see."

Hana's hand tightened around Yuki's. Her eyes remained closed.

"It knows your name," Hana said. "It's been waiting for you."

---

Chapter 16: The Excavation

Dr. Hayashi made the arrangements.

He brought a crew of three graduate students, a portable soil scanner, and a small excavator. By the time Yuki came downstairs, the garden was half-dug, the wisteria's roots exposed to the morning light.

"Are you sure about this?" Mr. Kobayashi asked. He stood at the back gate, watching the work.

"No," Yuki said. "But Hana is trapped. And I think the only way to free her is to follow the roots."

The excavation took two days.

They found bones. Many bones. Ribs, vertebrae, a skull. Dr. Hayashi catalogued each one, his face growing more troubled with each discovery.

"These are not all the same person," he said on the second afternoon. "I've identified at least six individuals."

"All Tanaka women?"

"I cannot confirm that without DNA testing. But the arrangement suggests intentional burial. The roots have grown around each set of remains, almost as if the vine was planted specifically to hold them."

Yuki watched the dig continue. The hole was three meters deep now, the roots forming walls around it. The vine had not resisted the digging. It had parted, making way, as if it wanted to be seen.

At the bottom of the hole, one of the graduate students called out.

"Dr. Hayashi. You need to see this."

They gathered at the edge. The roots at the bottom had formed a chamber — a hollow space, walled with interwoven woody tissue, lined with moss. In the center of the chamber lay a single object.

A seed.

It was the size of a fist, dark brown, veined with purple. It pulsed faintly, as if alive.

Dr. Hayashi reached for it.

"Don't touch it," Yuki said.

He stopped. "This is unlike anything I've ever seen. The seed morphology is not consistent with Wisteria floribunda. It may be an entirely new species."

"It's not a new species," Yuki said. "It's the original. The one the vine came from."

She climbed down into the hole. The roots did not stop her. They held steady, forming a path.

She knelt before the seed. It was warm, like the flowers. She could feel the pulse, the hum, the song that Hana had heard.

"What are you?" she whispered.

The seed did not answer. But the vine around her began to move, closing the hole above her, sealing her in darkness.

---

Chapter 17: The First Witness

In the darkness, Yuki saw nothing.

But she felt everything.

The roots pressed against her, not painfully, but firmly, as if holding her in place. She felt the weight of the earth above her, the network of the vine extending in all directions, the bones of the women who had come before.

And she felt the seed.

It was not a seed. It was a memory. A memory so old that it predated the house, the street, the city. A memory of a forest that had stood here before Kyoto was built, before humans had settled this valley. A vine that had grown in that forest, climbed its trees, bloomed in the ancient air.

The vine had been here first. The house had been built on top of it. The Tanaka women had not planted it — they had accepted it. They had made their peace with it.

And in return, the vine had held them.

Yuki saw them now — the women whose bones lay in the roots. Hisae, who had died young and been buried beneath the vine she loved. Kiyo, who had arrived without a past and found a home in the shadow of the wisteria. Others, older, their names lost, their faces unfamiliar but their presence unmistakable.

They were part of the vine now. Not trapped, but held. Remembered. The vine was not a tomb. It was a library of the dead, a living archive of the women who had lived and died in its shadow.

And at the center of it all was the seed. The first seed. The one from which all of it had grown.

Yuki reached out and touched it.

The light came.

---

Chapter 18: What Burns

The light was white and cold and it came from the seed.

It did not illuminate the chamber so much as replace it. Yuki was no longer underground. She was standing in a field, under an open sky, and the wisteria was everywhere — not as a single vine, but as a forest of blossoms, stretching to the horizon.

A woman stood before her. Young, dark-haired, dressed in simple cloth. She looked familiar.

"Kiyo?"

The woman smiled. "You found the seed."

"I don't understand."

"You will." Kiyo gestured at the forest. "This is what the vine remembers. The world before the city. Before the house. Before the bones."

"Why did the vine choose us?"

"Because you were here. Because you built your home on top of the seed. Because you tended the vine without knowing what it was." Kiyo's voice was soft, patient. "The vine does not choose. It grows. It accepts. It holds."

"Then why is it taking Hana?"

Kiyo's expression shifted. "It is not taking her. It is showing her what she will become."

"Which is what?"

"A root. A branch. A part of the vine." Kiyo stepped closer. "When I died, I became part of it. Hisae became part of it. Every Tanaka woman who has lived under this roof has joined the vine in death. That is the bargain. That is the gift."

"I don't want that for Hana."

"The vine does not ask. It simply is." Kiyo touched Yuki's face. Her fingers were warm. "But Hana is different. She can see, truly see. She can change the vine. She turned the flowers white. She felt the song. She is the first in generations to be awake in the roots."

"And that means?"

"It means she has a choice. The rest of us did not."

The light began to fade. The wisteria forest dissolved. Yuki felt herself rising, the roots releasing her, the earth giving way.

She opened her eyes in the garden. She was lying on the grass, the evening sky above her. Mr. Kobayashi was kneeling beside her, his face tight with worry.

"Yuki. You're back."

"How long?"

"Three hours. You went down into the hole and the roots closed around you. We couldn't reach you."

She sat up. Her hands were covered in soil. Her dress was torn. But she felt whole.

"Where is Hana?"

"Still asleep. But better — she woke briefly. Asked for you."

Yuki stood. She walked to the house on unsteady legs. In Hana's room, her daughter was sitting up, drinking water, her face pale but her eyes clear.

"Mom. You found it."

"I found it."

"What is it?"

Yuki sat on the edge of the bed. She took Hana's hand.

"It's the beginning," she said. "And the end. And I think it's been waiting for you to decide what happens next."

---

Chapter 19: The Neighbors' Dreams

The word spread.

Not through conversation, but through sleep. That night, everyone on the street dreamed of the wisteria.

Mrs. Yamamoto dreamed that the fabric in her shop had roots, that bolts of silk sprouted tendrils and wrapped around her shelves. Mr. Endo dreamed of a fish with wisteria blossoms instead of scales. The temple priest dreamed that the altar was made of living wood, and that the ancestors spoke from the grain.

They came to Yuki's shop the next morning, one by one, their faces troubled.

"I dreamed of your vine," Mrs. Yamamoto said. "It was in my shop. It was everywhere."

"Me too," said Mr. Endo. "I dreamed the roots had reached the harbor. The whole city was connected."

The priest came last. He was old, older than Etsuko, his back bent from decades of bowing.

"I dreamed of a woman," he said. "She was standing in your garden, and the wisteria was blooming through her body. She said to tell you: the fire is coming."

"Fire?" Yuki's blood went cold.

"The burning. She said the wisteria must burn before it can grow again. It is the cycle. The old must be consumed for the new to emerge."

He left. Yuki stood in the doorway, watching him walk back to the temple.

Fire.

She went to the garden. The wisteria was beautiful, impossibly beautiful, its blossoms heavy and sweet. But beneath it, she knew, the roots extended everywhere, under every building, under every life.

The vine had grown too large.

"It's not supposed to be this way," she said aloud.

Etsuko appeared behind her. "What did the priest say?"

"That there will be a fire. That the vine must burn."

Etsuko was quiet. Then she said, "He is not wrong."

"You knew?"

"I suspected. The vine has been growing faster, reaching further. It has been preparing."

"Preparing for what?"

"For the burning. For the renewal." Etsuko looked at the vine. "We are not the only ones who can see the future, Yuki. The vine sees it too."

---

Chapter 20: Hana's Choice

Hana recovered slowly.

She spent three days in bed, eating little, sleeping much. When she was awake, she stared at the ceiling, her hands folded on her chest.

Yuki stayed with her. She canceled orders, closed the shop, let the flowers wilt in their vases. The business could wait. Nothing mattered but Hana.

On the third evening, Hana spoke.

"I know what I have to do."

Yuki looked up from the chair beside the bed. "What?"

"The vine showed me. The seed showed me." Hana turned her head. Her eyes were clear, resolved. "I have to go into the roots."

"No."

"It's the only way. The vine is spreading. It's under the whole neighborhood. If I don't stop it, it will keep growing. It will cover everything."

"How will going into the roots stop it?"

"I don't know exactly. But the seed told me. It said I could change things. That I could end the cycle."

"What cycle?"

"The cycle of women being buried under the vine. The cycle of death feeding the roots. The vine has been using us, Mom. For a hundred years, it has been using our bodies to grow."

Yuki felt the words like a blow. "Your grandmother said the vine held us. That it remembered us."

"It does. But it also consumes us." Hana sat up. "I saw them. Hisae, Kiyo, all of them. They're part of the vine now. They can't leave. The vine won't let them."

"And if you go down there?"

Hana was silent for a long moment. Then she said, "I can open the seed. I can release them."

"How?"

"I don't know. But the seed showed me. It chose me." She looked at her hands. "I turned the flowers white. I heard the song. I'm the one who can do this."

"No." Yuki stood. "You're fifteen years old. You're not going to sacrifice yourself for a plant."

"It's not a plant. It's our family. Our history. And I'm the only one who can finish it."

"I won't let you."

Hana looked at her with an expression that was too old for her face.

"Mom. You don't have a choice. The vine has already decided."

--- End of Arc Two ---

---

Arc Three: The Fire

---

Chapter 21: The Gathering

The street met in Yuki's shop that night.

Mrs. Yamamoto came. Mr. Endo. Mr. Kobayashi. The temple priest. Two young mothers Yuki knew only by sight. A retired salaryman who lived three doors down. They filled the shop, standing among the wilting flowers, their faces drawn by shared dreams.

"The vine is under all of our homes," the priest said. "The dreams confirmed it. We are all connected to its roots."

"What do we do?" Mrs. Yamamoto asked. "Cut it down?"

"It will not die," Etsuko said. She stood at the back, leaning on her cane. "I tried to kill it when I was young. It healed overnight."

"Then burn it," Mr. Endo said.

"No fire," Yuki said quickly. "There are bones under the house. If we burn the vine, we burn them."

"Bones?"

"The women of my family. Buried in the roots."

A murmur went through the crowd. Mrs. Yamamoto crossed herself — she was Catholic, the only one on the street.

"This is not a natural plant," the priest said. "It is a boundary thing. A creature of edges. It belongs between life and death, and it has grown too far into our world."

"Then what?" Mr. Endo's voice rose. "We let it take over?"

The argument continued. Some wanted to dig, some wanted to burn, some wanted to do nothing. Yuki listened with half her attention. She was watching Hana, who sat apart from the group, her eyes distant, her fingers tracing patterns in the air.

The vine was speaking to her. Even now, in a room full of people, Hana was somewhere else.

Mr. Kobayashi came to stand beside Yuki. "This is getting nowhere."

"I know."

"Your daughter knows something. The rest of them are guessing."

Yuki looked at him. "She says she has to go into the roots."

"Into them?"

"Under the house. To the seed. She says she can end it."

Mr. Kobayashi was quiet. Then he said, "Can she?"

"I don't know. But she believes it. And the more time passes, the more I believe her."

The meeting ended without resolution. The neighbors left, their fear palpable, their questions unanswered. Yuki closed the shop and stood in the silence.

Hana was still tracing patterns in the air.

"What are you drawing?" Yuki asked.

"The root system. I can see it now. All of it." Hana's fingers moved. "There's a pattern. It's not random. The vine grew in a specific shape."

"What shape?"

"A spiral. Centered on the seed. Every root, every tendril, every bloom — it's all part of a single spiral, growing outward."

"A spiral is a circle."

"A spiral is a circle that changes. It grows. It returns but never to the same place." Hana looked at her hands. "I think the vine is trying to complete something. A cycle. A transformation."

Yuki took her daughter's hands. They were cold.

"Whatever you're planning, I'm coming with you."

"No, Mom."

"Yes. I am your mother. I will not let you go into the dark alone."

Hana looked at her for a long time. Then she nodded.

---

Chapter 22: The Preparation

They spent the next day preparing.

Dr. Hayashi brought safety harnesses and oxygen tanks. Mr. Kobayashi provided flashlights, rope, a first-aid kit. Etsuko prepared a meal — rice and fish and pickled vegetables — and insisted they eat.

"You will need your strength," she said.

Yuki could barely swallow. The food tasted like ash.

Hana ate with calm precision, as if she were preparing for an exam. She had not argued about Yuki coming. She had accepted it with a quietness that was more disturbing than resistance.

At dusk, they stood at the edge of the excavation.

The hole had been covered with a tarp. Yuki pulled it back, revealing the dark cavity below. The roots glowed faintly, their pulse visible in the dim light.

"Do you have a plan?" Mr. Kobayashi asked.

"Find the seed. Open it. Release the women trapped in the vine."

"Open it how?"

"I'll know when I see it." Hana stepped to the edge. "Mom. Are you sure?"

"Yes."

They descended.

The roots formed a natural ladder, sturdy and warm. Yuki followed Hana down, the light from above shrinking to a small circle, then a pinprick, then nothing.

They were in the dark.

But it was not true dark. The roots glowed, a soft purple luminescence that illuminated the chamber. The seed sat at the center, pulsing like a heart.

"It's bigger," Yuki said.

It was. The seed had grown since Yuki had seen it last. It was now the size of a human head, its surface veined with light, its pulse strong and regular.

"It's ready," Hana said.

"Ready for what?"

"For the burning."

Hana knelt before the seed. She placed her hands on its surface. The pulse quickened.

"Hisae," Hana said. "Kiyo. All of you. I know you're in there. I know you can hear me."

The roots trembled. A sound rose from the earth — not music, but something like it. A song made of wood and memory.

"I'm here to let you go," Hana said. "The vine has held you long enough. But it cannot continue. It's spreading. It's taking more than it should. I need you to help me end it."

The song changed. It became questioning, uncertain.

"The fire will come," Hana said. "But I need you to let go before it does. I need you to release the vine."

A figure emerged from the roots.

---

Chapter 23: Hisae's Face

She was made of wood and blossom, her body woven from tendrils, her face a mask of bark. But her eyes were human — dark and sad and unmistakably alive.

Hisae.

"You called," she said. Her voice was the rustle of leaves, the creak of branches.

"I called," Hana said.

"You wish to release us."

"Yes."

"We have been part of the vine for eighty years. We do not remember what it is to be separate."

"Then it's time to remember."

Hisae turned her wooden face toward Yuki. "You are her mother."

"Yes."

"She is brave. Braver than I was."

"What happened to you?" Yuki asked. "Really?"

Hisae was silent. The roots around her shifted, as if the vine was listening.

"I loved Kiyo," she said. "In a time when such love was not spoken of. We lived in this house together, tending the vine, hiding our life from the world. When the war came, I was afraid. I was afraid of so many things. The bombs. The soldiers. The neighbors who suspected."

She paused. The glow of the roots dimmed.

"And then I became sick. The vine knew before I did. It bloomed pink — the color of sudden death. I knew what it meant. I had seen it before."

"You died of an illness?"

"I died of a broken heart. I could not bear to leave Kiyo alone." Hisae's wooden face twisted. "But I did not truly leave. The vine caught me. It held me. And I have been here ever since, watching Kiyo grow old, watching the family continue, never able to speak."

"But Kiyo joined you," Hana said. "She's in the vine too."

"Because she chose to be. When she died, she asked the vine to take her. She did not want to be separated from me again." Hisae's voice broke. "And the vine obliged. It always does."

Yuki felt tears on her face. "Then why do you want to leave?"

"Because the vine is growing hungry. It has been feeding on the family for generations, and it wants more. It wants Hana. It wants a living host."

"No," Yuki said.

"The vine does not understand death as we do. To it, joining the roots is not an ending. It is a continuation. It believes it is offering Hana a gift."

"And if she refuses?"

"Then the vine will take her anyway. It has waited too long. It will not let her go."

---

Chapter 24: The Bargain

Hana stood before Hisae, her face calm.

"Let me understand," Hana said. "The vine wants me to become part of it. Alive. Not dead like the others."

"Yes."

"And if I do, it will stop growing?"

"It will stop expanding. It will remain as it is, contained within the property. It will not spread to the neighbors."

"And if I refuse?"

Hisae's wooden eyes were sorrowful. "Then it will take you by force. And it will continue to grow until the entire city is in its roots."

Hana looked at Yuki. "You see? I have to."

"No," Yuki said. "There has to be another way."

"There is." Hana turned back to Hisae. "The seed. I can open it."

Hisae's face flickered — surprise, fear, something else. "The seed has never been opened."

"I know. But I can do it. The vine showed me."

"Opening the seed will release everything. The roots will lose their anchor. The vine will die."

"Yes."

"And we will die with it."

Hana was silent. Then she said, "I know."

"No!" Yuki grabbed Hana's arm. "You can't. You'll kill them all."

"I'll free them," Hana said. "There's a difference."

"Your great-grandmother. Her sister. All of them. You'll erase them."

"They're already erased, Mom. They've been trapped in a plant for decades. That's not living. That's not being remembered. It's just being stored."

Yuki could not argue. She could see the truth in Hana's face — the certainty that had been growing since the first white petal.

"If you open the seed, what happens to you?"

Hana looked down at her hands. "I don't know."

"Then we're not doing it."

"Mom—"

"We find another way. Or we don't. But I am not losing you to this thing."

Hana's expression shifted — frustration, but also something else. Gratitude, perhaps. Or relief that someone was still fighting.

"Three days," Yuki said. "Give me three days to find another answer."

"Three days," Hana agreed.

They climbed back up into the light.

---

Chapter 25: The Research

Yuki spent the first day reading.

She went through Etsuko's box — the letters, the photographs, the locks of hair. She looked for patterns, for clues, for anything that might suggest another path.

The women in the photographs stared back at her, their faces serious, their lives reduced to paper and ink.

She found a letter from Kiyo to Hisae, written in 1944, when the war was at its worst.

"My dearest Hisae," it began. "I dream of the garden. I dream of the wisteria, in bloom, covering everything. I dream that we are both made of flowers, and that our roots are intertwined, and that nothing can separate us."

Yuki set the letter down. They had loved each other. They had wanted to be together forever. And the vine had granted that wish, in the most literal way possible.

She went to Mr. Kobayashi's shop. She found him in the back, sorting used books.

"What do you know about magical plants in Japanese folklore?"

He looked up. "That's a broad category."

"Wisteria specifically. Boundary plants. Things that connect this world and the other."

He led her to a section of the shop she had never explored — folklore, mythology, old religion. He pulled down a volume with a faded spine.

"This is a collection of folktales from the Kansai region, published in 1912. There's a story about a wisteria vine that grew in a village near Lake Biwa."

Yuki took the book. She read the story standing in the aisle.

It told of a family who lived beside a great wisteria. The vine bloomed when someone was about to die. It wrapped around the windows of the dying. It was considered a sacred plant, a messenger between worlds.

And at the end of the story, the eldest daughter of the family was given a choice: she could join the vine and become part of its memory, preserving the family's history forever, or she could burn it and let the past go.

She chose to burn it.

The vine burned for three days. The smoke was purple, and it smelled of honey. When the fire died, the family was free — but the eldest daughter was gone. She had become part of the fire.

Yuki closed the book.

"She burned with it," she said.

Mr. Kobayashi nodded. "That's how the story ends."

"But Hana—" Yuki stopped. "Hana is the eldest daughter. She's the one who can see. She's the one who turned the flowers white."

"The story is a story. It does not have to be prophecy."

"But what if it is?"

Mr. Kobayashi had no answer.

---

Chapter 26: The Second Night

Yuki did not sleep.

She spent the night in Hana's room, sitting on the floor, watching her daughter's chest rise and fall. The vine pressed against the window, its shadows dancing in the moonlight.

At midnight, Hana stirred.

"Mom? You're still awake."

"I can't sleep."

Hana rolled over to face her. In the dim light, she looked younger than fifteen. She looked like a child.

"What if I don't come back?"

"You will."

"But what if I don't?"

Yuki could not answer. The words would not form.

"I've been thinking," Hana said. "About the story. About the girl who burned with the vine."

"You know about that?"

"Mr. Kobayashi told me. He said I should know before I make a choice."

"He shouldn't have."

"He should have. I need to know what I'm walking into." Hana sat up. "I think that's what happens. When you open the seed, you become part of the fire. You burn with the vine."

"Then we don't do it."

"But if I don't, the vine keeps growing. It takes more women. More bones. More of the family, generation after generation. It never stops."

"Then we move. We leave the house. We go somewhere the roots can't follow."

"The roots are already everywhere. The street. The neighborhood. The whole block. Even if we left, the vine would still be here. It would still take people."

Yuki pressed her face into her hands. "I can't lose you, Hana. You're all I have."

"You have Grandma. You have Mr. Kobayashi. You have the shop." Hana's voice was soft. "You'll be okay."

"How can you be so calm?"

"I'm not calm. I'm terrified. But I'm also certain. For the first time in my life, I know exactly what I'm supposed to do."

They sat in silence as the night deepened.

"I love you, Mom."

"I love you too."

They held each other until morning.

---

Chapter 27: The Community Wakes

On the third day, the dreams returned.

Everyone on the street dreamed the same dream: a woman made of wisteria, standing in the center of a spiral, her arms outstretched. She was burning, but she was not in pain. She was smiling.

They came to Yuki's shop again, more of them this time. Word had spread to the surrounding blocks. People Yuki had never met stood in her shop, their faces a mixture of fear and recognition.

"We dreamed of your daughter," a woman said. "She was the one burning."

"Was she?" Yuki asked, her voice flat.

"She was glowing," the woman said. "Not suffering. Glowing."

The priest pushed through the crowd. "The dreams are a message. The vine is preparing for something. It is showing us what will happen."

"Or what could happen," Yuki said. "It's not decided yet."

"The vine has decided. Your daughter has decided. The only question is whether you will accept it."

Yuki looked at the crowd — her neighbors, her community, people who had known her for years. They were afraid. They were looking to her for answers.

"The vine will be dealt with," she said. "I promise. But I need time."

"Time is what we don't have," the priest said. "The vine is growing faster. It reached the next block yesterday. My parishioners are finding roots in their basements."

"Then dig them out."

"We have. They grow back overnight. Faster each time."

Yuki closed her eyes. The pressure was immense. The whole neighborhood was depending on her, on Hana, on a choice that should never have to be made.

"Tomorrow," she said. "Tomorrow, it ends."

---

Chapter 28: Etsuko's Goodbye

That evening, Etsuko called Yuki to her room.

The old woman was sitting by the window, the last light of sunset falling across her face. She looked smaller than ever, diminished, as if the vine had been drawing life from her as well.

"I am not going to be here much longer," Etsuko said.

"Grandmother—"

"It is not the vine. It is age. I am eighty-three years old, and I have lived a full life." She paused. "But I wanted to tell you something before I go."

"What?"

"I knew about the seed. I have always known."

Yuki stared at her. "You knew?"

"Hisae told me. Before she died, she told me everything. She said the seed was the heart of the vine, and that one day, a Tanaka woman would have to open it."

"And you never told me?"

"What would you have done with that knowledge? You were a child. Then you were a young woman with a husband and a baby. You were not ready."

"When would I have been ready?"

"Now." Etsuko's eyes were bright. "You are ready now. And so is Hana."

"You're saying you've been waiting for this."

"I have been preparing for it. Every story I told you, every lesson I gave you, every time I made you tend the garden — I was preparing you for this moment."

Yuki sat down heavily. "You used us."

"I protected you. There is a difference." Etsuko reached out and took Yuki's hand. "I did not want this for Hana. I wanted it to end with me. I prayed that the vine would lose its power, that the old magic would fade. But it did not. It grew stronger."

"And now Hana has to pay for it."

"Hana gets to choose. That is more than any Tanaka woman before her was given."

Etsuko released her hand. She looked out the window, where the last light was dying.

"I am proud of you," she said. "And I am proud of Hana. Whatever happens tomorrow, know that."

Yuki could not speak. She nodded, and left the room, and did not look back.

---

Chapter 29: The Descent

Dawn came cold and clear.

Yuki woke before the sun. She dressed in her oldest clothes — loose pants, a cotton shirt, sturdy shoes. She braided her hair. She made tea, but could not drink it.

Hana came downstairs at six. She was wearing white.

"Why white?" Yuki asked.

"It's what the dream told me to wear."

"Hisae's dream?"

"Yes. She said white is the color of beginnings."

Yuki wanted to argue, to delay, to find another way. But there was no other way. The vine had grown thicker overnight. The roots had broken through the kitchen floor. The house was groaning under their weight.

They walked to the excavation. The neighbors had gathered, standing in a silent circle around the hole. Mr. Kobayashi was there. Mrs. Yamamoto. Mr. Endo. The priest. Others Yuki did not know.

Dr. Hayashi was at the edge, his equipment packed away. "I can't come down with you. The roots are too dense now. I'd get stuck."

"Then we go alone," Yuki said.

Etsuko came to the edge, leaning on her cane. She looked at Hana for a long moment.

"You have the gift," she said. "Use it wisely."

"I will, Great-Grandmother."

Etsuko touched Hana's face. Then she stepped back.

Hana descended first. Yuki followed.

The roots closed above them, sealing them in darkness.

---

Chapter 30: The White Fire

The chamber had changed.

The seed was enormous now — the size of a grown person, pulsing with light. The roots pulsed in rhythm, a heartbeat that shook the earth.

Hisae was waiting for them. She was more solid than before, her wooden body detailed, almost human.

"You came back," she said.

"I made a promise," Hana said.

"Are you ready?"

"Yes."

"No." Yuki stepped forward. "I have a question first."

Hisae tilted her head. "Ask."

"Was there ever another way? Could the vine have been stopped without someone burning?"

Hisae was silent for a long time. The roots hummed around her.

"There could have been," she said finally. "If the family had stopped tending the vine. If they had moved away. If they had not buried their dead in its roots. But they did not. Generation after generation, they fed the vine with their lives, their deaths, their love. The vine grew strong because they made it strong."

"So this is our fault."

"Not fault. Destiny. The vine and the family are the same thing. You cannot separate them. You can only end them."

Hana stepped toward the seed. "How do I open it?"

"You touch it. You ask it to let go."

"That's all?"

"That is everything."

Hana placed her hands on the seed. The pulse quickened. The light grew brighter.

"Seed of the wisteria," Hana said. "I am Hana Tanaka, daughter of Yuki, granddaughter of Etsuko, great-granddaughter of Kiyo. I ask you to release the souls you hold. I ask you to let them go."

The seed trembled. Cracks appeared on its surface. Light poured out — white, pure, blinding.

And then the fire came.

It was not a physical fire. It was a fire of light, of memory, of release. It poured from the seed, filling the chamber, touching everything. Yuki felt it pass through her, warm and clean.

Hisae was burning. But she was smiling.

"Thank you," she said. "Thank you for freeing us."

The light consumed her. She dissolved into petals, into light, into nothing.

The roots began to wither.

"Come on," Hana said. "We have to go."

They climbed. The roots crumbled beneath their hands, but the light carried them upward, pushing them toward the surface. Yuki felt Hana's hand in hers, and she held on.

They broke through the earth into the morning sun.

The neighbors gasped. The wisteria was burning — not with flame, but with white light. Every blossom, every leaf, every tendril was glowing, shedding its color, turning to ash.

The vine was dying.

And Hana was still burning.

The white light was coming from her — from her skin, her eyes, her open mouth. She was the fire. She was the seed.

"Hana!" Yuki caught her as she fell.

Her daughter was light in her arms, weightless, barely there. Her eyes met Yuki's.

"I did it," she whispered.

"I know."

"Is it done?"

Yuki looked at the garden. The wisteria was gone. Nothing remained but ash and bare wood, the skeleton of the trellis, the memory of blossoms.

"It's done," Yuki said.

Hana smiled. Her body was cold.

And then the light faded, and Hana was still.

--- End of Arc Three ---

Arc Four: The Ash

---

Chapter 31: What Remains

They carried Hana inside.

Mr. Kobayashi lifted her, his old arms trembling with the effort. He laid her on the sofa in the shop, among the wilted flowers and empty vases. Her face was peaceful. Her chest did not rise.

Dr. Hayashi checked for a pulse. He listened for breath. He shone a light in her eyes.

"I'm sorry," he said. "She's gone."

Yuki did not scream. She did not cry. She sat on the floor beside the sofa and took Hana's hand. It was cool, but not cold. It was the temperature of the earth.

"She's not gone," Yuki said.

"Yuki—"

"Feel her hand. It's not cold."

Dr. Hayashi touched Hana's hand. His brow furrowed. "That's unusual. Ambient temperature would normally cause—" He stopped. "She should be colder."

"She's not dead."

"There's no pulse. No respiration. Brain activity is flat."

"You don't understand. She opened the seed. She became the fire. She's still—" Yuki struggled for words. "She's still here. She's just not in her body."

The neighbors gathered in the doorway, their faces uncertain. The vine was dead. The roots had withered. The danger was over. But Hana was lying on the sofa, and she was not breathing, and they did not know what to do.

"Give us space," Yuki said. "All of you. Please."

They left, one by one. Mr. Kobayashi stayed.

"I'll call the funeral home," he said.

"No."

"Yuki—"

"No funeral home. She is not dead."

He looked at her with pity. "I understand you don't want to accept it. But—"

"Feel her hand."

He touched Hana's hand. His expression changed.

"It's warm."

"Yes."

"How?"

"I don't know. But she is not dead. She is waiting."

"Waiting for what?"

"For the new vine to grow."

Mr. Kobayashi stared at her. "The vine is gone. It burned."

"Not all of it." Yuki looked toward the garden. "The seed is gone. But the roots are still there, beneath the earth. And the seed released something before it burned. Something that is still here."

"How do you know?"

"Because I can feel it. The same way I felt the vine. It's in the air now. In the soil. In Hana."

Mr. Kobayashi did not argue. He pulled up a chair and sat beside them.

"Then we wait."

They waited.

---

Chapter 32: The Vigil

The days passed.

Yuki did not leave the shop. She sat beside Hana, holding her hand, speaking to her in a low voice. She told her stories — about the first time she held her, about her first steps, about the day she was born and the wisteria was in full bloom and Yuki had thought it was a good omen.

Etsuko came each morning. She sat on the other side of Hana, her hands folded, her eyes closed. She did not speak, but her presence was a comfort.

Mr. Kobayashi brought food that Yuki did not eat. Mrs. Yamamoto brought flowers that Yuki did not arrange. The neighbors left offerings at the door — rice, fruit, incense. They did not understand what was happening, but they understood that something sacred was in progress.

Dr. Hayashi came daily. He checked Hana's vitals, recorded her temperature, noted the absence of decay. Her body remained warm. Her skin remained supple. Her hair continued to grow.

"This is not scientifically possible," he said on the fourth day.

"Neither was the vine," Yuki said.

He could not argue.

On the fifth day, Yuki noticed the first green shoot.

It was growing from Hana's palm — a tiny stem, pale green, with two small leaves. It had emerged from her skin as naturally as if she were soil.

Yuki did not remove it. She watched it grow.

By the sixth day, there were shoots from both hands, from her feet, from the crown of her head. Tiny, delicate, new.

Hana was becoming a garden.

---

Chapter 33: The Growth

The plants grew quickly.

By the end of the second week, Hana's body was covered in new growth — small leaves, tiny blossoms, the beginnings of a vine that trailed from her hands and wrapped around the sofa. She looked like a sleeping spirit, a forest goddess from an old story.

Dr. Hayashi documented everything. He measured the growth rate, tested the plant tissue, compared it to the original wisteria. The DNA was identical. The vine was regrowing from Hana's body.

"She's become the host," he said. "The new vine is growing through her."

"Is it the same vine?" Yuki asked.

"Genetically, yes. But it's different. It's not aggressive. It's not spreading. It's just—" He searched for the word. "Growing. Peacefully."

Because Hana is not afraid, Yuki thought. The old vine grew on fear and death. This one grows on her.

On the fifteenth day, Hana's eyes opened.

They were green. Not the brown they had been before — green, the color of new leaves, of spring growth.

"Mom."

Yuki was at her side instantly. "I'm here."

"I was in the seed. I was everywhere. I saw everything."

"What did you see?"

"All of them. Hisae, Kiyo, the others. They're not gone. They're in the new vine. They're free, but they're still here."

"Here where?"

"In the garden. In the air. In me." Hana blinked slowly. "I'm not the same."

"I know."

"I'm part of it now. The vine is growing through me. I can feel it."

"Does it hurt?"

"No." Hana looked at her hands, now covered in green. "It feels like belonging."

---

Chapter 34: Etsuko's Passing

Three weeks after the burning, Etsuko died.

She went in her sleep, peacefully, her face turned toward the window where the morning light fell. Yuki found her when she went to bring her breakfast. The old woman was still, her hands folded, her expression peaceful.

Yuki sat beside her for a long time.

"I should have been here," she said.

"You were," Mr. Kobayashi said from the doorway. "You were here every day. She knew."

"I didn't say goodbye."

"You said it every time you sat with her. She knew."

They buried Etsuko in the garden, where the wisteria had been. The ground was soft, the ash of the old vine mixing with the soil. Yuki planted a cutting from the new vine on her grave — a small shoot that Hana had grown from her hand.

"She would have wanted that," Hana said. She was in a wheelchair now, her body too covered in growth to walk easily. But she was alive. She was awake. She was present.

"She wanted to be part of the vine," Yuki said.

"She always was. She just didn't know it."

They stood in silence as the sun set over the garden. The new vine was small, barely a foot tall, but it was healthy. It had none of the old vine's hunger.

It was content to grow, and bloom, and be.

---

Chapter 35: The Adaptation

Hana's transformation continued.

The vine grew from her body at a steady pace, covering her limbs, her torso, her face. She did not seem to mind. She said it felt natural, like wearing clothes made of life.

"Doctors want to study me," she said one afternoon. "Dr. Hayashi has been getting calls. Research institutions. Universities."

"What do you want to do?"

"I want to stay here. I want to be in the garden."

"Then that's what you'll do."

But Yuki knew it was not that simple. The world had changed. The vine was gone, but something new had taken its place — something that lived in her daughter's body, breathed through her lungs, grew from her hands.

The neighbors were afraid. They did not say it, but Yuki could see it in their eyes when they passed the shop. They saw Hana in her wheelchair, covered in leaves and blossoms, and they crossed to the other side of the street.

Only Mr. Kobayashi came regularly. He brought books, tea, conversation. He treated Hana the same as before, as if she were not becoming something other than human.

"How do you do it?" Yuki asked him once.

"Do what?"

"Act like everything is normal."

He considered. "Because it is normal. The vine was always part of this street. Now it's part of Hana. That's a change, but it's not a tragedy. It's just a different way of being."

"You're not afraid of her?"

"I love her," he said simply. "Fear doesn't enter into it."

---

Chapter 36: The Roots of the City

In the months that followed, something strange happened.

The old vine had extended its roots throughout the neighborhood. When it died, those roots withered. But the new vine, growing from Hana, began to send out new roots — invisible, underground, connecting to the same network.

People noticed changes.

Mrs. Yamamoto's fabric shop began to grow flowers from the bolts of cloth. Not real flowers, but patterns — intricate, organic designs that appeared in the weave overnight. She started selling them as "wisteria silk" and could not keep them in stock.

Mr. Endo's fish developed a faint purple sheen, and customers said they tasted sweeter. He started charging double.

The temple's garden, which had been struggling for years, burst into impossible bloom. The priest declared it a miracle.

"The vine is still here," Hana said. "It's not a threat anymore. It's just... helping."

"Is that what you want?" Yuki asked.

"I don't control it. It's part of me, but it does what it wants. All I can do is guide it."

"Guide it where?"

"Toward life. Away from death." Hana looked at her hands, where small white blossoms had opened. "The old vine fed on dying. This one feeds on growing."

"That sounds like a good thing."

"It is. But it's also strange. I'm not sure what I'm becoming."

Yuki took her daughter's hand, careful not to disturb the blossoms.

"You're becoming yourself," she said. "That's all anyone can do."

---

Chapter 37: The Researcher's Return

Dr. Hayashi came back in autumn.

He had spent months analyzing data, writing papers, consulting with colleagues around the world. He had presented his findings at three conferences. The scientific community had received them with a mixture of fascination and skepticism.

"I'm being asked to provide proof," he said. "They want to see Hana."

"She's not a specimen."

"I know. I told them no. But there are others who will not ask permission."

Yuki felt a coldness settle in her chest. "What do you mean?"

"The government has become interested. The Ministry of the Environment. They've been asking questions about the original vine, the excavation, the bones. They want to classify the new growth as a protected species."

"Protected? It's my daughter."

"Legally, it's unclear. The vine is growing from her body, but it is a distinct organism. They could argue that the vine is a natural resource that belongs to the state."

"That's insane."

"It's bureaucracy. But it's real. I came to warn you."

Yuki looked at Hana, who was sitting in the garden, her body half-covered in green, blossoms in her hair.

"They will not take her," Yuki said.

"You may not have a choice."

"There is always a choice."

---

Chapter 38: The Visitors

They came the next week.

Three men in suits, with official badges and clipboards. They stood in the shop, looking around at the wilting flowers, the dusty shelves, the photograph of Hana on the counter.

"Mrs. Tanaka," the lead man said. "I'm Inspector Mori from the Ministry of the Environment. We've received reports of a botanical anomaly on this property."

"Reports from who?"

"I'm not at liberty to say. But we have authorization to inspect the premises."

Yuki did not move. "My daughter is not an anomaly."

"We understand this is sensitive. But the vine that grew here has been identified as a potential new species. Under the Law for the Conservation of Endangered Species, we have a duty to—"

"My daughter is not a species."

Inspector Mori's expression did not change. "We would like to see her."

"No."

"Mrs. Tanaka, we have a warrant."

He produced a document. Official stamps, signatures, the seal of the Kyoto District Court.

Yuki read it. Her hands were shaking.

"Please wait here," she said.

She went to the garden. Hana was sitting on a bench, her hands in her lap, covered in small purple blossoms.

"They're here," Yuki said.

"I know. I felt them coming."

"They have a warrant."

"I know."

"I won't let them take you."

Hana looked up. Her eyes were still green, calm, knowing.

"They can't take me, Mom. I'm part of the earth now. Part of the roots. They can try, but I'll always come back." She smiled. "Let me talk to them."

Yuki wheeled her into the shop. The inspectors stared.

Hana was beautiful and strange — her skin woven with green, blossoms growing from her hair, her hands covered in tiny leaves. She glowed faintly in the dim light.

"I'm Hana Tanaka," she said. "You wanted to see me."

Inspector Mori recovered himself. "We need to take samples. Tissue samples, plant samples. For analysis."

"I'll give you what I can. But I won't leave this property."

"That may not be possible."

"Then you'll have to carry me." Hana's voice was calm. "And I'm rooted pretty deep."

Something in her tone made the inspector pause. He looked at her more carefully.

"What are you?"

Hana held up her hand. A blossom opened in her palm, white and perfect.

"I'm what comes after," she said. "The new thing. The one the old vine was waiting for."

---

Chapter 39: The Standoff

The inspectors left, but they promised to return.

Yuki spent the night on the phone with lawyers, journalists, anyone who could help. Mr. Kobayashi sat with Hana in the garden, telling her stories about the old days.

"They're going to come back," Yuki said. "With more people. With police."

"Probably," Hana said.

"What do we do?"

"Let them come."

"Hana, they'll take you."

"They can try."

Yuki looked at her daughter — so calm, so certain. She wanted to be angry, but she could not find the anger.

"You're not afraid," Yuki said.

"I was afraid of the vine. I was afraid of dying. I'm not afraid of bureaucrats."

"But what if—"

"Mom." Hana took her hand. "The old vine was afraid of death. That's why it consumed. This vine is not afraid. I'm not afraid. And I think that makes all the difference."

In the morning, the street was different.

The neighbors had gathered overnight. They stood in a line in front of the shop — Mrs. Yamamoto, Mr. Endo, the priest, dozens of others. They held signs. They had called the news.

"Leave Hana alone," Mrs. Yamamoto said when Yuki came outside.

"What?"

"She's one of us. She's not a specimen. She's our neighbor's daughter."

Yuki looked at the crowd. They were not afraid anymore. They were angry — at the government, at the men in suits, at anyone who would take a child from her mother.

"Thank you," Yuki said.

"She did what the vine required," the priest said. "She freed us. Now we protect her."

---

Chapter 40: The Garden of Hana

The government did not return.

The press coverage was too intense. The public sympathy was too strong. The story of the girl who became a garden captured something in the national imagination, and the Ministry quietly withdrew its claim.

Life settled into a new rhythm.

Yuki reopened the shop. She sold flowers from the new vine — purple and white blossoms that stayed fresh for weeks, that brought luck and comfort and strange dreams. People came from across Kyoto to buy them.

Hana remained in the garden. She did not need to eat or drink. She absorbed sunlight and water through her skin, through the vine that grew from her. She spoke to visitors, told them stories, let them touch her leaves.

"You're happy," Yuki said one afternoon, finding her among the blossoms.

"Yes." Hana touched a flower. "I miss being normal. I miss walking. I miss school. But I don't miss being afraid."

"Are you still Hana?"

"Parts of me. The parts that matter."

Yuki sat beside her. The new vine was taller now, winding around the rebuilt trellis, its blossoms a mix of purple and white. It glowed in the afternoon light.

"I used to think the vine was a curse," Yuki said. "Something that took from us."

"It did take. But it also gave."

"What did it give?"

"Connection. Memory. A way of being that doesn't end." Hana looked at her. "I'll be here longer than you, Mom. I'll be here after the house is gone, after the street is gone, after Kyoto is gone. The vine remembers."

"Will you remember me?"

"Always. You're in my roots."

--- End of Arc Four ---

---

Arc Five: The Bloom (Reprise)

---

Chapter 41: The Nature of Time

The years passed in a way that felt both fast and slow.

Hana did not age. Her body remained fifteen, frozen at the moment of transformation. The vine grew from her, through her, around her, but she did not change. She was a fixed point in a moving world.

Yuki aged. Her hair grayed. Her face lined. Her hands grew stiff from years of arranging flowers. She watched her reflection in the shop window and saw her mother looking back at her.

Mr. Kobayashi grew slower, thinner, more fragile. He closed his bookshop at seventy-five and spent his days in the garden, reading aloud to Hana.

"You should write this down," he told Yuki one afternoon. "The story. All of it."

"What for?"

"Because people need to know. Because Hana's story matters. Because one day, you'll be gone, and she'll still be here, and someone will need to understand."

Yuki bought a notebook. She began to write.

She wrote about the vine, the seed, the bones. She wrote about Hisae and Kiyo, about love that defied death. She wrote about Hana's choice, the burning, the transformation. She wrote about Etsuko's last days, about the neighbors who had stood in front of the shop.

She wrote because remembering was the only way to make sense of what had happened.

"Are you telling the truth?" Hana asked, reading over her shoulder.

"I'm telling the story."

"There's a difference."

"Is there?"

"In a story, things have meaning. In truth, they just happen." Hana touched the page. "But maybe meaning is the same as truth. I don't know anymore."

---

Chapter 42: Mr. Kobayashi's Farewell

He died in winter, when the garden was bare.

Hana felt it before anyone told her. She called Yuki from her room and said, "He's gone."

Yuki ran next door. She found him in his chair, a book open on his lap, his glasses still on his face. His expression was peaceful.

Mr. Kobayashi had been a constant presence for forty years. He had been the one who brought coffee, who knew about the vine, who had never looked at Hana with fear. He had been the closest thing to a father Yuki had known since her divorce.

They buried him in the garden, beside Etsuko.

Hana grew a wisteria over his grave — not the vine that lived through her, but a separate plant, one she had cultivated from a cutting. It bloomed the next spring, purple and white together.

"He always liked the mixed colors," Hana said.

The bookshop passed to a young couple from Tokyo. They renovated it, painted it, filled it with new books. They did not know about the vine, about Hana, about the bones beneath the floor.

The street changed. Mrs. Yamamoto retired. Mr. Endo's son took over the fish shop. New families moved in. The old stories faded.

But the garden remained.

---

Chapter 43: Yuki's Vigil

Yuki was seventy when the dreams returned.

Not the vine's dreams — something gentler. Dreams of a girl in a white dress, walking through a field of wisteria. The girl turned, and it was Hana, unchanged, young, her arms full of blossoms.

"Are you ready?" Hana asked in the dream.

"Ready for what?"

"To let go."

Yuki woke with tears on her face.

She went to the garden. Hana was there, as she always was, sitting on the bench, her hands folded. The vine had grown thick around her, supporting her, holding her upright.

"I dreamed of you," Yuki said.

"I know."

"You asked if I was ready to let go."

"Yes."

"Let go of what?"

Hana looked at her with ancient eyes. "Me."

---

Chapter 44: The Unbinding

"I'm not dying, Mom," Hana said. "But I'm not staying the same, either. The vine is growing. It's reaching beyond this garden."

Yuki sat beside her. "Where is it going?"

"Everywhere. The roots have spread under the whole prefecture now. I can feel them. They connect to other gardens, other plants, other families. The vine is becoming part of the world."

"And you?"

"I'm the center. The heart. But I don't need to be in one place." Hana paused. "I've been thinking about leaving."

"Leaving?"

"Not forever. But the vine needs to be tended in other places. There are seeds that need to be planted. People who need to see."

"Who will tend them?"

"I will. Through the vine. Through the roots." Hana touched Yuki's face. "But that means I have to let go of this body. Most of it, anyway."

Yuki's heart clenched. "You'll die?"

"No. I'll become more vine. Less human. I'll still be here, in the roots, in the blossoms. But you won't be able to see me like this."

"Then stay."

"I can't. The vine needs to grow. And I need to let it."

Yuki looked at her daughter — frozen at fifteen for decades, a miracle and a prison.

"How long have you known?"

"A long time. I was waiting until you were ready."

"I'll never be ready."

"I know." Hana smiled. "But you'll let me go anyway. Because you love me."

---

Chapter 45: The Second Transformation

They had one more spring together.

Yuki closed the shop. She spent every day in the garden, sitting with Hana, talking, remembering. They went through old photographs. They listened to music. They watched the wisteria bloom.

The transformation was gradual.

Hana's body grew thinner, lighter, more translucent. The vine absorbed her, cell by cell, replacing flesh with wood and blossom. Her voice became softer, harder to hear, like wind through leaves.

On the last day of spring, Yuki woke to find Hana's body gone.

In its place was a wisteria tree — not a vine, but a proper tree, with a trunk and branches and roots that went deep into the earth. Its blossoms were white and purple, mixed together, perfect.

"Goodbye," Yuki whispered.

A breeze stirred the blossoms. One fell into her hand.

It was warm.

---

Chapter 46: The Garden Keeper

Yuki was alone.

She had been alone before — after her divorce, after her mother died — but this was different. This was a solitude that pressed against her from all sides, empty and full at the same time.

She opened the shop again. She arranged flowers. She greeted customers. She lived.

But she spent her evenings in the garden, under the wisteria tree, talking to Hana.

"Your father called today," she said one evening. "He wants to visit. He's old now, too. His wife died last year."

The branches stirred.

"I told him you were gone. I didn't know how else to explain it."

A blossom drifted down.

"He said he always knew you were special. From the moment you were born."

Yuki laughed, a dry sound. "He didn't know anything. None of us did."

She looked up at the blossoms. They were thick, healthy, full of life.

"I miss you."

The tree creaked. A root surfaced near her feet, glowing faintly in the dark.

"I'm still here, Mom." The voice was barely a whisper, carried on the wind. "I'm always here."

---

Chapter 47: The Life Cycle

Years passed. Decades.

Yuki grew old. She could no longer run the shop. She sold it to a young florist who promised to care for the garden.

She moved into a small room on the ground floor, the same room where Hana had slept as a baby. She could see the wisteria tree from her window.

The neighborhood changed completely. The old buildings were replaced. The new residents did not know about the vine, about the bones, about the girl who became a tree.

But the tree remained.

It grew taller each year. Its blossoms spread across the trellis, across the new buildings, across the street. The city pruned it back, but it always grew again, persistent and gentle.

People called it the Miracle Wisteria of Kyoto. Tourists came to photograph it. Couples were married beneath it. Children played in its shade.

No one knew its true name.

But sometimes, late at night, a woman would sit beneath it, and she would speak to it, and the blossoms would tremble, and the roots would glow, and the woman would remember.

---

Chapter 48: Yuki's End

She was ninety-four when she felt the vine call to her.

She had known it was coming. She had felt the pull for years — a gentle tug, like a hand reaching for hers. The tree wanted her. It wanted to hold her, the way it held all the Tanaka women.

She was not afraid.

She dressed in her best kimono. She combed her white hair. She walked to the garden one last time, her steps slow, her breath thin.

The tree was in full bloom. White and purple blossoms covered every branch, glowing in the twilight.

"Hello, Hana," she said.

The branches bent toward her. A root surfaced, curling around her ankle, warm and gentle.

"I'm ready," Yuki said.

She lay down beneath the tree. The roots rose around her, cradling her, drawing her into the earth.

She felt the warmth spread through her body. She felt the vine accept her. She felt her daughter's presence, close and familiar.

"Thank you," she whispered.

The blossoms rained down, covering her in white and purple.

And Yuki Tanaka joined the vine.

---

Chapter 49: The New Memory

The vine remembered everything.

It had grown for a hundred years before the Tanaka house was built. It had witnessed the rise of Kyoto, the wars, the rebuilding, the slow passage of generations. It had held the bones of women who had loved and lost and died.

And now it held Yuki.

She was not gone. She was in the roots, in the blossoms, in the sap. She was part of the memory, the same way Hisae was, the same way Kiyo was, the same way all the Tanaka women were.

They existed together in the vine's awareness — a chorus of voices, a library of lives.

"Is this what you wanted?" Yuki asked, her voice made of rustling leaves.

"Yes," Hana answered, her voice made of blooming flowers.

"And now?"

"Now we grow."

The vine continued to grow. It spread under the city, under the prefecture, under the country. Small shoots emerged in distant gardens — a wisteria in Tokyo, another in Osaka, another in a village in the mountains.

Each one was connected. Each one remembered.

The Tanaka family was gone — the last descendant had been Yuki. But the vine preserved them, their stories, their love, their grief.

It was not a curse anymore. It was a record.

A living memory of the women who had burned.

---

Chapter 50: The Wisteria Burns

In the end, it was not fire that took the vine.

It was time.

The wisteria tree stood for three hundred years after Yuki died. It became a landmark, a protected natural monument, a symbol of Kyoto's endurance. Pilgrims came from around the world to see it.

And then, slowly, it began to fade.

The blossoms came later each spring. The leaves grew smaller. The branches thinned.

The city's botanists studied it, tried to save it, but there was nothing to be done. The vine was old. It had lived longer than any wisteria had a right to. It was ready.

On the last day, a single blossom remained.

It was white, tinged with purple at the edges. It hung from the highest branch, facing the morning sun.

A young woman stood beneath it. She was sixteen, with dark hair and curious eyes. She had come from another city, drawn by a dream she could not explain.

She reached up and touched the blossom.

It fell into her hand, warm and alive.

The vine shuddered. The roots beneath the earth shifted. And something passed from the old tree into the girl — a seed, invisible, older than memory.

She felt it take root in her chest.

"What is your name?" a voice asked. It came from the air, from the earth, from the blossom in her hand.

"Hikari," she said. "Hikari Tanaka."

The vine fell silent. The last blossom crumbled to dust.

Hikari looked at her hand, where the dust had settled. A tiny green shoot was pushing through her skin, pale and new.

She was not afraid.

She was home.

--- The End ---
