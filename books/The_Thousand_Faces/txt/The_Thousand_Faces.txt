The Thousand Faces

by Barış Kısır

Chapter 1: Admission

The intake form said Devin Chen had a fear of heights. Under "nature of phobia" he had written, in very small letters: not heights exactly. the idea that I could jump.

Sonia Park read this at her desk in the east wing and then read it again. She pressed her thumb over the last word and felt the paper underneath — textured, institutional, faintly waxy. The idea that I could jump. Acrophobia with an intrusive-thought component, possibly overlapping OCD. She wrote her first note in the margin.

The Harmon Institute occupied a converted Victorian estate on three acres outside Millhaven, New York. In 1962 someone had added a clinical wing to the back, and in 1998 someone had added another. The original house remained as administration and staff quarters, its high ceilings and molded plaster absorbing whatever warmth the fluorescent corridors tried to emit. The grounds had once been beautiful and were now merely maintained.

Sonia had worked here for four years. She had her own office, a view of the herb garden, and a caseload of seven patients at present. She was good at this work. She knew that about herself without pride, the way you know the length of your own stride.

At nine-fifteen she walked to the reception wing to meet Devin Chen.

He was sitting beside the admissions desk with his hands folded on his knees in a way that looked practiced. Twenty-eight years old. Slight, with thick black hair combed more carefully than his jeans and flannel shirt suggested he'd combed it. He was watching the door as if calculating whether he could reach it in time if he had to.

"Mr. Chen," Sonia said. "I'm Dr. Park. I'll be your primary clinician while you're at Harmon."

He stood immediately. Too fast, then correcting for it. "Hi." A pause. "Sorry. Hi, Dr. Park."

"There's nothing to apologize for."

"That's what everyone says the first day." He looked at her with something that might have been humor or might have been assessment. Hard to tell. She noted it.

She walked him through the standard orientation — common areas, meal schedule, the rhythm of sessions, the visiting hours. He listened with the focused attention of someone who expected to be tested. When they reached the east corridor he glanced at the window at the far end.

"How high is that?" he said.

"Third floor. Approximately forty feet."

He nodded, looking at it. Not with fear exactly. With what she would later describe in her notes as vigilance — the way you watch something you know can hurt you but haven't decided whether it will.

"The windows are sealed," she said. "All of them."

He turned back to her. "Right." A small, rueful smile. "Sorry. Reflex."

"You mentioned in your intake form that the phobia isn't precisely about the height itself."

"The height is fine. It's like — I know intellectually I would never jump. I'm not suicidal. I just can't convince my brain that the height isn't a kind of invitation." He paused. "That's probably the tenth time I've said that exact sentence."

"It's a clear sentence. It means something."

He looked at her again with that assessment quality. Then he said, "I hope this works."

"Tell me what you're hoping for," Sonia said, and took out her notebook.

The rest of the morning was administrative. She reviewed his referral documents, confirmed his medication history — a low-dose SSRI, nothing remarkable — and filed the preliminary case notes before lunch. The intake report would be finalized after her first full session, scheduled for Thursday.

At one o'clock there was a department briefing in the small conference room beside Webb's office. The usual calendar items: one patient transitioning to outpatient, one new intake approved for next week, a scheduling change for the occupational therapy block. Sonia sat at the end of the table with her coffee going cold and half-listened.

Then Webb cleared his throat.

"One more item," he said. "I'll be presenting some information at Thursday's staff meeting about a new clinical enhancement we're introducing. I don't want to get ahead of the formal presentation, but I'd encourage everyone to come with open minds."

He smiled at the room. It was a practiced smile, calibrated to convey authority and benevolence simultaneously.

Sonia wrote Devin Chen's name at the top of a fresh page and started her treatment notes.

Chapter 2: The Protocol

The Thursday staff meeting ran from two to four. Webb had ordered the good coffee and arranged the chairs in a semicircle, which was his signal that he wanted the meeting to feel collaborative rather than hierarchical. Sonia recognized the staging and sat next to Liam Ferris near the back.

"Looks like a pitch," Liam said quietly.

"Yes," Sonia said.

There were eight clinical staff present: four psychologists including Sonia and Liam, two psychiatrists, and two licensed counselors. Nurse Rourke stood near the door in her way of attending without quite joining. Webb stood at the whiteboard with a clicker and a small stack of printed materials.

"What we're looking at," he said, "is what I'm calling the Harmon Protocol. It's an integrative approach combining two established intervention methods in a sequence that, to my knowledge, hasn't been formalized at the institutional level before."

He clicked through a title slide: HARMON PROTOCOL — ACCELERATED THERAPEUTIC CONVERGENCE.

"The first component is a refined pharmacological preparation — a supervised course of medication designed to lower the cognitive and limbic resistance that typically slows the early stages of therapeutic work. The compound itself has a solid safety profile. I'll provide the literature."

He passed around a stapled handout. Sonia opened hers to page two and scanned the reference list. Four citations, three of which were to Webb's own previous papers.

"The second component is targeted neurological assistance — micro-pulse deep-brain stimulation, non-invasive, transcranial. We've been cleared for the equipment, and Dr. Yuen in our neurology division has been trained in the procedure."

A hand went up. Dr. Cheung, one of the psychiatrists. "What's the clinical rationale for combining the pharmacology and the stimulation? What does the conjunction achieve that either method alone doesn't?"

"Excellent question. The pharmacological preparation sensitizes the limbic-cortical interface in a way that the TMS can then target very precisely. The idea is to reach emotional material faster, with less patient resistance. What typically takes six to twelve months of weekly therapy to surface can, in theory, emerge in six to eight sessions."

"That's an extraordinary claim," Sonia said.

Webb looked at her with his benevolent smile undimmed. "I know it sounds that way. That's why I'm being careful to say 'in theory' and to walk everyone through the supporting research before we begin."

"Who are the target patients?"

"Patients with phobia-based presentations initially. The material is most tractable — discrete, well-defined fear structures. Less confounding overlap with complex trauma or personality disorders."

Sonia looked down at her handout. She thought of Devin Chen and his intake form.

"Participation for patients will be voluntary, of course," Webb said. "And clinical staff will be invited but not required to participate in a parallel supportive track — a very low-dose protocol designed to heighten empathic attunement during sessions. Pure elective. You can opt out at any time."

Liam leaned close. "What does 'heighten empathic attunement' mean?"

"I don't know," Sonia said.

After the meeting Webb circulated with his stack of supporting materials and his easy confidence. Sonia took the handout packet and went back to her office.

She read for an hour. The pharmacological compound Webb had described was identified only as "Compound A-7" in the literature — a proprietary label that made tracing its composition difficult. The TMS component was more standard. The combination protocol had apparently been piloted at two other institutions, referenced in a footnote, but the footnote didn't include facility names.

She filed the handout in her desk drawer and made a note to look up the compound.

In the morning she had her first full session with Devin Chen. He sat across from her in the corner room with its single tall window, and he talked about the fire escape outside his apartment, and how he had stood on it once and had not been afraid of falling but of the way his hands had gripped the railing as if they already knew they were going to let go.

"Was that the moment you sought help?" Sonia said.

"No. That was the moment I realized the hands weren't mine." He said it simply, as a fact. "I made an appointment the next morning."

She wrote: intrusive-thought component strong. Ego-dystonic. No genuine suicidal ideation. Patient has high insight and strong observational capacity.

She wrote: patient could benefit from structured work. Patient does not need to be a test case for anything.

She did not yet know why she wrote that last line.

Chapter 3: First Breach

A week into Devin Chen's residency, Sonia was in session with him when it happened for the first time.

They were working on a graduated exposure hierarchy. Sonia had asked him to close his eyes and imagine standing on the third-floor landing he passed each morning on his way to breakfast — not the fire escape, not a rooftop, just a familiar height with a familiar railing. She was monitoring him with the kind of careful watchfulness that good clinical work requires: posture, breathing, the small visible tremors of discomfort.

He closed his eyes. His breathing slowed as he concentrated. And then Sonia felt it.

It arrived like a change in atmospheric pressure. The room seemed to tilt — very slightly, as if she were standing on a surface that had developed an almost imperceptible slope. She felt the sensation in her inner ear first, then in her hands: a cold, prickling awareness of depth beneath and around her. The window in the corner room was to her left. She did not look at it. She focused on her notepad, on the pen in her hand, on the scratched wooden arm of her chair.

It passed in perhaps eight seconds.

Devin opened his eyes. "That was bad. Not catastrophically, but bad."

"Tell me what you experienced."

"The usual. Vertigo cognition — I know the ground is there, but I can't reach the fact of it. Like the certainty has a hole in it."

Sonia wrote it down. Her hand was steady.

"That's useful information," she said. "You're mapping it. That's the first step."

After the session she sat for a moment in her empty office. The feeling had been so specific — the inner-ear tilt, the hand prickling. Not her own anxiety. She didn't have a fear of heights. She was not prone to vertigo. She had read enough clinical literature on countertransference to know that strong empathic resonance during exposure work was documented, but something about the specificity of the sensation made her pause.

She was fatigued. She hadn't been sleeping well. The new protocol meeting had left a residue of low-grade unease. She wrote the sensation in her clinical log under "personal observation" and categorized it as countertransference. Empathic resonance. Entirely within the range of known phenomena.

She had a supervision session with Webb that afternoon, a routine biweekly check-in. He asked about her caseload adjustments, reviewed the week's session notes in summary. She did not mention the sensation.

When she left his office, Nurse Rourke was in the corridor, pushing a medication cart.

"Dr. Park," Rourke said. She had a flat, observant face that gave nothing away unless she decided to give something.

"Agatha."

"How is Mr. Chen settling?"

"Well, I think. He's engaged with the work."

Rourke adjusted something on the cart without looking at it. "He's a good one to engage with." She began pushing the cart again. "Some of the new admissions aren't finding the adjustment so easy."

"Anyone in particular I should know about?"

"Not my patients to name." Rourke glanced back once before she rounded the corner. "You'll notice what you notice."

Sonia stood in the empty corridor for a moment.

She noticed she was noticing.

Chapter 4: The Observation Room

The one-way mirror in Observation Suite A was a rectangle of dark glass set into the east wall of a small monitoring room. Harmon used it for case consultations, for training, for the kind of clinical oversight that required distance. Sonia used it rarely. Most of her supervision was based on session recordings reviewed after the fact.

She was using it on a Tuesday afternoon to observe a session between Liam Ferris and one of his patients — a forty-year-old man named Trent Bassett, admitted for severe social anxiety. Webb had asked her to provide a secondary clinical perspective on the case as part of Bassett's updated treatment review.

The room on the far side of the glass was ordinarily appointed: two chairs, a low table, a floor lamp with warm light. Liam sat with his notebook on his knee. Bassett sat across from him and was talking, though Sonia couldn't hear the words — only the audio feed through the small speaker on the monitoring panel, which she had turned low.

She watched Liam.

He looked normal. He was holding the session correctly: open posture, minimal note-taking, appropriate eye contact. His responses to Bassett were measured and timed well. And yet something in Sonia's perception kept snagging on him without producing a clear finding — a wrongness she couldn't locate.

She turned up the audio slightly.

"...the group setting specifically triggers it?" Liam was saying.

"Any group," Bassett said. "Even a small one. The feeling is — I know they're looking at me, but I don't know what they see."

"Can you describe what you imagine they see?"

Bassett hesitated. "Everything I'm trying to hide."

Liam nodded. Normal response. She watched him nod and something shifted in her perception of it — the nod seemed very slightly off in timing, like a word spoken a half-beat before its cue. She blinked. Looked again. He was simply nodding.

She wrote in her notepad: Bassett session. Ferris standard technique. Nothing actionable. Possible observer fatigue on my part.

She added: note the nod. Then crossed it out.

After the session she went to the monitoring panel to switch off the equipment and noticed the recording light was off. The session had not been recorded.

Liam sometimes forgot to activate the recording before sessions. It was a known issue, mentioned in his last annual review. She noted it without concern and went to find him in the break room.

"Forgot to record again," she said.

He looked up from his coffee. "Christ. I'll write a summary note."

"Webb will want it."

"I know. I know." He rubbed his eyes. He looked tired — the specific tiredness of someone whose sleep had been invaded by something they weren't discussing. "How are you finding the new protocol brief? Webb mentioned you had questions."

"I have a lot of questions. The compound is proprietary, which makes independent verification difficult."

"He's been doing this for twenty years, Sonia."

"That's not an answer."

Liam looked at her for a moment. "No," he said. "It's not." He set his coffee down. "I told him I'd participate in the clinician attunement track. The supportive dose thing."

"When did you decide that?"

"Yesterday. It seemed — I don't know. He makes it sound very reasonable in person."

"He makes everything sound very reasonable in person," Sonia said.

Chapter 5: Calista

Calista Graves arrived on a Friday afternoon with two suitcases that matched and a paperback she had dog-eared past the halfway point. She was thirty-three, referred for extreme social anxiety with dissociative features by her psychiatrist in Manhattan. The referral letter was three pages and dense with clinical terminology, but the sentence Sonia kept returning to was near the end: patient demonstrates unusual capacity for observation and pattern detection, which may function as both a coping mechanism and a symptom driver.

Sonia met her in the intake room. Calista was sitting very straight, the paperback closed on the table beside her. She had dark eyes and a stillness that wasn't calm — it was the stillness of someone who had learned to hold themselves very still as a form of protection.

"Dr. Park," she said, before Sonia had introduced herself.

"Yes. You read the intake materials?"

"Staff directory. I looked up everyone I was likely to meet." She said it without apology. "It helps me."

"That's fine. A lot of people do that."

"I know." She paused. "The medical director is Dr. Marcus Webb, fifty-five years old. He completed his residency at Cornell Medical and his clinical training in London. He's published forty-two papers, the most recent being three years ago. One earlier paper was retracted."

Sonia sat down. "That's thorough."

"I have trouble with uncertainty." She turned the paperback so the spine faced up. Sonia couldn't see the title. "If I can structure the information, the anxiety is manageable."

They went through the intake protocol. Calista answered every question directly, completely, and with a self-awareness that most patients with anxiety presentations took months to develop. She described her symptoms with clinical precision: the dissociative episodes, the way she sometimes heard her own voice from a small distance, the sensation of watching herself in the third person that came with crowds.

"When did the dissociation begin?" Sonia said.

"After I left a job at a research firm." She was quiet for a moment. "I can say more about that later. I'd rather not on the first day."

"Of course."

Calista looked at the window. "Who else is currently admitted here?"

"I'm not able to share that information."

"I know." A small, private smile. "I thought I'd ask." She looked back at Sonia. "There's a young man I passed in the hall on my way in. He was at the window at the end of the corridor. He looked like he was trying to decide something."

Sonia wrote: patient is observant beyond the clinical norm. Intake presentation suggests high intelligence deployed in service of anxiety management. Later she wrote: assign Calista Graves to enhanced monitoring. Not because she was concerning, but because she was going to see things.

She was right about that. She could not have said, at that moment, how right.

Chapter 6: The Second Vision

The Harmon Protocol went live in its first phase on a Monday morning. Webb circulated a memo: three patients with phobia presentations had agreed to participate. Devin Chen was one of them. Sonia read the memo and went to find Webb, but he was in a procedure consultation and she was twenty minutes from her first session of the day.

She told herself she would speak to him after.

Her nine o'clock was a group session — a therapeutic conversation group of five patients. Sonia facilitated. The group usually covered what each member wanted to bring, with Sonia guiding the dynamics. It was a good group: honest, mostly, with occasional defensiveness that produced useful material.

Devin was in the group. Since the protocol had started — his first session under the new medication had been Friday — he seemed altered in a way she couldn't specify. More present, perhaps. The emotional material was closer to the surface. He sat leaning slightly forward.

The group ran for fifty minutes. Near the end, while a woman named Marguerite was describing the particular horror of driving on bridges, Sonia felt it again.

This time it was longer. The room stayed solid and she remained aware of her body and its position in the chair, but superimposed on her ordinary perception was a vivid overlay: a metal landing, open-grated, and below it an enormous drop. The sound of wind. The feeling in her palms and forearms of gripping a painted railing very hard. The interior sensation of a choice being weighed — not falling, not jumping, but the presence of the ledge itself as something that would accept her if she stepped wrong.

She was aware of herself in the room. She was aware of the group. She saw Devin, and Devin's face was perfectly calm.

It lasted perhaps twenty seconds. Then it withdrew like a tide pulling out.

Sonia said, "Marguerite, that's very useful. The bridge as a specific trigger — is it the height, the movement, or the water?"

Her voice was level. Her hands were level. No one looked at her.

After the session she went to her office and sat at her desk and wrote for eleven minutes. She wrote what she had seen and felt with the precision she brought to case notes. Then she put the notepad in the locked desk drawer where she kept material she wasn't ready to formalize.

She wrote one line at the bottom: Devin Chen is in the protocol.

She did not write what she was beginning to suspect. She did not have enough evidence to write it, and she was rigorously not in the habit of writing things she did not have evidence for.

But she sat for a long time afterward, looking at the herb garden through the window, not quite seeing it.

---

Chapter 7: Corridor Talk

Sonia found Liam in the hall between the east wing and the dietary suite at twelve-thirty. He was heading for lunch. She fell into step with him.

"How was your morning?" he said.

"Eventful." She considered. "I had another countertransference episode during group."

He glanced at her. "Another one?"

"The second one. Stronger."

"What kind?"

"Sensory. The patient I'm thinking of is on the protocol."

Liam was quiet for a few paces. They pushed through the double doors into the dining room, which smelled of soup and cleaning fluid. "You think there's a connection?"

"I don't think anything yet. I'm documenting."

"Heightened attunement is in Webb's stated mechanism of action for the clinician track," Liam said. "Even without the clinician dose. He suggested that proximity to protocol patients could have secondary effects on unsupported clinicians."

"He said that?"

"In the reading materials. Page seven of the second handout." Liam picked up a tray. "I thought you read them."

"I read them. I missed that part."

"He frames it as temporary and mild. An artifact of the therapeutic proximity, not a drug effect."

Sonia took a plate of salad she wasn't going to eat. "So I'm experiencing a drug effect from being near a drugged patient?"

"Or empathic resonance from doing good clinical work with someone who trusts you."

"You're being charitable to the protocol."

"I'm trying to be." He sat down. "Are you going to tell Webb?"

"I don't know yet."

"If you don't tell him and he finds out you've been documenting anomalous experiences without reporting, it becomes a conduct issue."

"If I tell him, I become data."

Liam put his fork down and looked at her across the table. He had a face that was usually easy to read — open, tired in the honest way, without strategy. Right now she couldn't read it.

"Sonia," he said.

"What?"

"Take care of yourself on this one."

She looked at him. "That's a strange thing to say."

He picked up his fork again. "It's what I'd want someone to say to me."

Chapter 8: Webb's Office

She went to Webb's office at three, between sessions. His assistant waved her in without checking. Webb was at his desk with three monitors on and his reading glasses halfway down his nose. He looked up with his reliable warmth.

"Sonia. Sit down."

She sat. She had prepared what she wanted to ask and she asked it directly: "What is the exact pharmacological composition of Compound A-7?"

His expression didn't change. That was, she noted, a data point.

"It's proprietary," he said. "The licensing structure doesn't allow me to share the composition in full, but the safety literature covers the relevant characteristics. Did you read the—"

"I read the materials. The safety literature is four studies, two of which you authored, one of which was conducted at a facility not named in the citation."

"The fourth is peer-reviewed, independent."

"The fourth is a rodent study."

Webb took his reading glasses off and set them on the desk. "Sonia. Tell me what's actually worrying you."

"I want to understand what my patients are receiving."

"They're receiving a licensed compound in a carefully calibrated dose, administered with full informed consent procedures, documented in their treatment files, and monitored daily by Dr. Yuen and nursing staff."

"Devin Chen is twenty-eight years old. He has an intrusive-thought component to his acrophobia that means his relationship with heights is partially ego-dystonic. He is not a simple phobia case."

"He was thoroughly assessed before enrollment."

"By you?"

A pause. Almost imperceptible. "By me, with Dr. Ferris's consultation."

"You consulted Liam on the enrollment decision?"

"As I do with all—"

"Liam hasn't mentioned this to me. We have shared notes on several of my patients." She paused. "Including Devin."

Webb leaned back. He put his hands flat on the desk in a gesture that looked relaxed and wasn't. "Sonia, I value your clinical instincts enormously. That's why I wanted you involved in this work. But I need you to trust the process here, at least long enough to see initial results."

"I trust processes I can verify."

"Everything is verifiable. The files are accessible. Speak to Dr. Yuen. Speak to Agatha. The protocol is documented at every step." He looked at her steadily. "Is there something else going on? Something clinical you'd like to discuss?"

She recognized the reframe. Patient the clinician. He was very good.

"Not at present," she said. "Thank you for your time."

She stood and left before he could structure another response. In the corridor she walked at her normal pace and did not allow herself to process anything until she had turned two corners and was safely out of his probable sightlines.

Then she stopped and breathed.

She had not mentioned the visions.

Chapter 9: Agatha

Nurse Agatha Rourke had worked at the Harmon Institute since 1999. She had worked under three medical directors, through two building renovations, one lawsuit, and several incidents she filed correctly and never spoke of socially. She was not difficult. She was not cold. She was, in the precise sense, reserved — a quality that in nursing could mean anything from professionalism to self-protection, and in Agatha's case meant both.

Sonia had worked alongside her for four years without having a real conversation. Clinical cooperation, corridor exchanges, the ordinary professional contact of shared institutional space. It was Sonia's doing as much as Agatha's; she was not particularly social inside the institute.

She found Agatha in the medication room on a Wednesday morning, doing the nine o'clock count.

"Can I have ten minutes?" Sonia said.

Agatha looked up from the tray. "I have a round in twelve."

"Ten is enough."

Agatha finished the count, made her notation, and closed the case. She turned to face Sonia with her flat, careful expression. Waiting.

"The patients on the new protocol," Sonia said. "How are they presenting on rounds? Mood, sleep, any behavioral anomalies?"

Agatha was quiet for a moment. "I'm not their primary clinician."

"I know. I'm asking for observational notes. The kind that don't necessarily make it into the formal file unless they reach the threshold."

"Are you asking me to share clinical information outside the chain of command?"

"I'm asking you what you see."

Agatha looked at the medication cabinet. She seemed to be weighing something with her whole body, not just her mind. "The patients on the new program," she said, very carefully, "sometimes need extra watching at night."

"What kind of watching?"

"They can have trouble settling. Sleep disruption, mild agitation." A pause. "The kind that resolves by morning, so it doesn't usually make the notes."

"How long has that been occurring?"

"Since the protocol started."

"Have you reported it to Dr. Yuen?"

"I mentioned it informally. He said it was expected. Adjustment period."

"Did that satisfy you?"

Agatha looked at her directly for the first time in the conversation. "No," she said. "It didn't."

"But you didn't escalate."

"I don't escalate on informal reports without a documented basis." She folded her hands. "I have a documented basis for one patient. It's at the threshold. I was planning to file this week."

"Which patient?"

"Mr. Chen." She said it quietly. "He was found at the window at two in the morning, three nights ago. He said he was sleepwalking. The window is sealed. He was not in danger. But I stayed with him for thirty minutes, and he was not sleepwalking."

"What was he doing?"

Agatha looked at the wall. "Waiting," she said.

---

Chapter 10: Night Rounds

Sonia arrived at the institute at eleven-thirty that night. She had not volunteered for rounds in eight months. She told the night desk she was covering for a colleague and signed in under the standard protocol.

The ward at night was different in the particular way that enclosed medical spaces are always different at night — quieter than the word quiet implies, the sounds that remain (the floor, the HVAC, the distant perimeter) somehow more present for the absence of human noise. The fluorescent lights in the corridors were dimmed to their overnight setting: a lower register of blue-white that made the walls look grey.

She started with the second floor. The three patients on the protocol were housed on three and four, but she walked the full circuit first, establishing presence, noting what was normal.

At twelve-forty she took the stairwell to three.

The corridor was empty. Both sides. She checked the notations on the medication door — nothing unusual — and continued to the end of the hall, where a tall sash window looked north toward the service road and the tree line.

She was three rooms short of the window when she saw Devin Chen.

He was standing at the window in a hospital-blue robe, his arms at his sides, looking out. He was completely still. She could see his reflection in the glass — faint, ghostlike, a face looking both ways at once.

She stopped. He had not heard her. Or had not reacted to her.

She walked toward him carefully and said his name at normal volume.

He turned. Not startled — a slow, deliberate turn, as if he had heard her from the beginning and had been waiting for her to say it. His eyes were open and focused. He was not sleepwalking.

"Dr. Park," he said.

"What are you doing up, Devin?"

"Couldn't sleep." He looked back at the window. "I keep coming here. I don't know why. The window is sealed. I know it's sealed. I've checked it." He touched the frame. "I just want to know it keeps being sealed."

She stood beside him and looked at the dark grounds below. The tree line was a black edge against the lighter sky.

"Let's get you back to your room," she said. "I'll sit with you for a few minutes."

"You don't have to."

"I know."

She sat with him until twelve-fifty, not talking, while he lay in his bed looking at the ceiling. His breathing gradually slowed. Before she left she checked the window in his room — sealed, as all windows were sealed, the latch secured and the seal intact.

In the corridor she wrote one note: Chen, Devin. 12:40. Third-floor window. Fully conscious. Presented oriented times three. No evidence of somnambulism. Clear, present affect.

Then she wrote: this is not adjustment period behavior.

She had the first session with Devin in the morning. She was already composing what she would say. She had questions about the medication, specific ones, the kind that required documentation she did not yet have.

She went down to the second floor and spent the rest of her rounds watching.

Chapter 11: The Fire Escape

Sonia had her first session with Devin at nine on a Tuesday. He was on time. He was dressed in his own clothes, which was usually a good sign, and he had his intake paperback folded into his pocket.

He said the medication made him feel alert in a way that had a slightly artificial quality — not bad, just precise. "Like the emotional material is clearer," he said. "Like the usual noise is lower."

"What kind of noise?"

"The ambient anxiety. The background. The thing that's always running." He looked at the window. "It's quieter now. I can hear specific things more clearly."

"What specific things?"

He was quiet for a moment. "The heights are clearer. I mean — I can see them more completely in my mind. Usually when I imagine a height I can only hold it for a second before the anxiety interrupts it. Now I can hold it longer." He paused. "Is that supposed to happen?"

"It's within the description of what the protocol is intended to do," she said carefully. "Bringing material to the surface."

"But you're not sure."

She looked at him. He had the high-insight quality she'd noted from the first day — a clarity about his own internal states that some patients developed after years of work. "I have more to learn about the mechanism," she said.

"But you're not sure," he said again, without aggression, as a simple observation.

They worked on the hierarchy. He was engaged, cooperative, productive. The session was, by every clinical metric, going well.

At two o'clock that afternoon the external line rang. Sonia was between sessions. One of the administrative staff told her there had been an incident.

Devin Chen had been found at the base of the exterior fire escape. The east-wing fire escape, third floor, which ran down the back of the building to a paved service area. He was found at eleven-forty-three a.m. by a maintenance worker. He was dead on arrival at the emergency assessment — severe head trauma, multiple fractures consistent with a fall from height. The fire escape door on the third floor had been found open.

Sonia put the phone down.

She sat at her desk and looked at the herb garden and looked at the notepad with this morning's session notes in her handwriting.

The session had been at nine. The incident was at eleven-forty-three. Between those hours he had been at breakfast and, according to the morning medication log Agatha confirmed for her thirty minutes later, had received his standard morning protocol dose.

She opened the locked drawer. She took out the notepad where she kept her personal observations.

She read what she had written about her second vision: a metal landing, open-grated. Below it an enormous drop. The feeling in her palms and forearms of gripping a painted railing very hard.

The east-wing fire escape had an open-grated metal landing at the third floor.

She closed the drawer. She sat very still.

Chapter 12: The Investigation Begins

Webb held a staff meeting the next morning. He said all the appropriate things: tragedy, unexplained circumstances, our sincere condolences to the family, investigation in cooperation with local authorities. He said the fire escape door's lock would be reviewed. He said patient safety was the institute's foremost priority.

He said all of it correctly, with the precise emotional register of someone who had given this kind of statement before.

Sonia sat in the meeting and said nothing.

After the meeting she went to the records room, signed out Devin Chen's file, and sat in the small alcove beside the filing shelves where no one would bother her.

She read the file from the beginning.

The official enrollment notes for the protocol were filed by Dr. Yuen. The medical notes were standard. But the consent form — the document that should contain the clearest description of the protocol's known risks — was a standard institutional form with the specific compound listed only as "pharmacological treatment component, Harmon Protocol." The consent language covered, in general terms, "neurological adjustment effects, possible emotional sensitivity during initial treatment period."

Nothing about nighttime agitation. Nothing about the windows.

She photographed the form with her phone. Then she photographed the medication records, the dosing schedule, the nursing observation notes that Agatha had filed — the ones that documented night disturbances as "mild" and "resolved."

She returned the file. She signed out.

In her office she began making a list. What she knew. What she suspected. What she would need to verify.

Under "what I know" she wrote: Devin Chen is dead. He was on the protocol. He was exhibiting nocturnal window-seeking behavior. I had a specific sensory vision of a fire escape while in session with him. The fire escape door was found open.

Under "what I suspect" she wrote nothing. Not yet.

Under "what I need" she wrote: composition of Compound A-7. Full list of patients on protocol. Incident records for previous protocol cohorts, if any. Webb's 2003 paper. The retraction notice.

She looked at the list and then she looked at the wall.

She thought about the six seconds during the group session, the specific cold grip of the railing, the specific feeling of the decision being present in the air. She thought about whether those six seconds were data or noise.

She was a clinician. She was trained to work in conditions of uncertainty. She was trained to distinguish between her own material and the patient's material, and she was trained to know that the distinction was sometimes imperfect.

She picked up the phone and called the pharmacology department at Columbia University, where she had a contact, a man named Dr. Grant Ashby, who was inclined to answer obscure questions about proprietary compounds for colleagues he respected.

"I need whatever you can tell me about a compound labeled A-7 in protocol literature," she said. "It's described as reducing limbic-cortical resistance in therapeutic settings."

"Can you send me the literature?"

"I'll scan it tonight."

"Call me Thursday," Ashby said.

She put the phone down and went to her afternoon sessions. She did what she was paid to do. She was careful and present and useful to the people who needed her.

She did not allow herself to think about Devin Chen until she was alone in her car at five-thirty, and then she allowed it completely, and then she drove home.

Chapter 13: Pharmacology

She sent Grant Ashby the scanned documents at nine-thirty that night. He called back Thursday at eleven with a voice that had the particular character of someone who has been reading carefully.

"Where did you get this?" he said.

"Clinical briefing document. Internal."

"The compound is described here in a way that's very carefully vague. 'Limbic-cortical sensitization' covers about eight possible mechanisms. But there's a mention in footnote four of the third document that caught my eye."

She found the footnote. It was four lines in small print. "Yes."

"The specific binding profile described there — high affinity for mirror-neuron circuits and the temporo-parietal junction — that's not a standard anxiolytic. That's something in the territory of research compounds designed to increase interpersonal mirroring and empathic identification."

She was quiet.

"The closest thing I know in the literature," Ashby said, "is a class of compounds that were explored in the early 2000s for what one research group called 'shared cognitive experience' facilitation. The idea was that you could lower the barriers between self and other, therapeutically — help people with autism, with alexithymia, with severe attachment disorders."

"What happened to the research?"

"It didn't go forward. The boundary problem was too significant — patients began experiencing difficulty distinguishing their own thoughts and feelings from others'. There were some serious adverse events in at least one trial."

She wrote: mirror-neuron hyperactivation. Temporo-parietal junction. Boundary dissolution.

"If someone were administering this compound to both patients and clinicians simultaneously," she said, "in a therapeutic setting—"

"Then you'd be engineering a situation where both parties' mirror-neuron systems were enhanced and the normal distinction between self and other would be chemically compromised." He paused. "The clinician would experience what the patient experiences. The patient might experience what the clinician experiences. You'd have a kind of shared psychological space."

"Would that be therapeutic?"

"For some things, possibly. For someone with severe phobia?" He hesitated. "You'd be amplifying the patient's fear response through the clinician's empathic channel. If the fear got intense enough, and the clinician's response was strong enough, you'd be running the phobia in both directions simultaneously." He paused. "You'd be making it worse, not better."

"And if the protocol dose was elevated—"

"The patient could lose the ego-dystonic quality entirely. The 'I would never jump' component you'd normally rely on. The intrusive thought becomes indistinguishable from intention."

She put her pen down.

"Sonia," Ashby said. "Are you all right?"

"A patient died," she said. "Two days ago. He had a fall."

A long silence.

"You need to report this," he said.

"I need more than I have."

"What you have is enough to mandate a report."

"What I have is a footnote and a sensory experience I can't explain clinically. That's not enough for a conduct report that would hold up."

"It's enough to protect the other patients."

"I know," she said. "I know it is. Give me seventy-two hours."

Chapter 15: The Mirror Room

On a Saturday when the clinical wing was minimally staffed, Sonia went looking.

She went looking in the way she did most things: methodically, without announcement, with a clear sense of what she was looking for and a willingness to revise that sense based on what she found. She started in the area she knew — the east wing, the consultation suite, the storage rooms at the end of the corridor.

She found the room through a door she had always assumed was a utility cupboard. It was, in fact, a utility cupboard — on the other side of that door was a second door, partially concealed by a shelving unit that had been placed just far enough from the wall to allow a person to pass.

The room beyond was perhaps twenty feet by fifteen. It had a one-way mirror occupying most of the wall it shared with — Sonia calculated from the building plan she held in her mind — Room 14. The room that had been assigned to Devin Chen.

She stood at the mirror and looked into Room 14. It was empty now. Made up and empty, waiting for the next admission.

The monitoring equipment in the room was not standard. The standard recording setup — a camera mounted high in the corner, a microphone — was present. But beside it was a second bank of equipment, sleeker, with branded panels she didn't recognize at first glance. She photographed it.

The server had a status light that was on. It was active.

V.

VCS. Veridian Cognitive Systems.

Chapter 16: Liam's Sessions

She reviewed the schedule twice, in case she was misreading it. She wasn't.

Liam Ferris had eighteen sessions listed in the protocol calendar for the past two weeks. Of those, six were standard sessions with his own patients. Twelve were listed under a category Sonia had not noticed before: "Protocol Enhancement Consultation — Facilitator: Ferris, L."

Chapter 17: The Compound

Grant Ashby sent a longer email on a Monday, with three attachments. The first was a summary of the compound's likely mechanism — he had tracked it through some informal channels she wasn't asking about. The second was a reference to a 2004 clinical trial, conducted in Europe, in which a compound with an identical binding profile had been used in a research context. The trial had been discontinued after three adverse events, two of which involved patients acting on fears they had previously been able to cognitively suppress.

She read this three times.

The third attachment was a molecular structure diagram and a paragraph that ended: this compound, in combination with transcranial stimulation targeting the temporo-parietal junction, would produce a pharmacological environment in which the clinician and patient share an enhanced mirror-neuron state while the patient's fear circuits are simultaneously stimulated. The probable effect in a phobia patient is the erosion of cognitive override capacity — the part of the system that knows the fear is irrational and acts on that knowledge.

She printed it. She put it in the folder with Calista's log and the photographs from the mirror room.

She thought about her own visions. The inner-ear tilt. The cold grip of the railing.

She had not taken the clinician attunement dose. Webb had offered it as elective and she had declined. Which meant the visions were either profound empathic resonance — the most intense she had ever experienced — or she had been dosed without her knowledge.

She opened her desk drawer. She took out the small journal where she logged her own medication — supplements, occasional ibuprofen, nothing clinical. Nothing beyond what she administered herself.

She looked at the water in the small pitcher on her desk. The standard-issue pitcher that the housekeeping staff refilled each morning from the central kitchen.

She stood up and poured the pitcher into the sink in the small bathroom attached to her office. She went to the dining room and filled her own water bottle from a sealed bottle she bought from the vending machine in the main foyer.

She told herself she was probably wrong. She told herself there were limits to what any institution could do and being secretly drugged through the water supply was well beyond those limits.

She kept her own water bottle from that day on.

Three days later she noticed the visions had not occurred since she changed her water source.

Chapter 18: Webb's History

She pulled the 2003 paper on a library database she accessed from home. It had been published in a specialist journal — the Journal of Integrated Neuro-Clinical Practice — under Webb's full name and those of two co-authors whose affiliations were, in 2003, listed as a research institute in Amsterdam that Sonia found no longer existed when she searched for it.

The paper was titled: "Toward a Model of Shared Cognitive Architecture in Clinical Settings: Preliminary Observations on Facilitated Mirror-State Therapy."

She read it in two hours.

It described exactly what Grant Ashby had described — a compound used to lower self-other boundaries in a clinical setting, applied to both patient and clinician simultaneously. The 2003 paper was more optimistic than the European trial data. It presented two case studies in which, Webb argued, "facilitated mirror-state therapy" had produced remarkable acceleration of therapeutic breakthrough.

The retraction notice, which she found in the same database under a supplementary search, was dated six months later. The stated reason was "methodological concerns regarding informed consent procedures." There was a note from the journal: following retraction, one of the co-authors contacted the journal to clarify that the informed consent documentation referenced in the paper had not in fact been obtained from both research participants.

Both research participants. Not patients, plural. Research participants.

She stared at the screen.

The paper was a theoretical framework based on, at most, two people. One of whom had apparently not been properly consented.

She wrote: Webb has been trying to do this for at least twenty years. The 2003 trial was a pilot. This is the continuation.

She wrote: what is Veridian buying?

She thought about that. The data from sessions in which both patient and clinician were neurochemically primed for maximal emotional engagement — recordings of fear responses in their most acute, unguarded form, with the clinician functioning as an amplifier. What would that data be worth to a neurological research firm?

She thought: quite a lot. She thought: to the right buyer, almost anything.

Chapter 19: Vision Cascade

She was in session with a patient named Marcus Delgado on a Wednesday — a man admitted for severe OCD, not on the protocol, a patient she had been seeing for eleven weeks. They were in the middle of a standard session, useful work, nothing extraordinary. She was, as she always was, carefully present.

The vision hit without the usual perceptible onset.

One moment she was in the room, attending to Delgado's account of his morning. The next she was entirely somewhere else.

She was standing in a kitchen she did not recognize, looking at the stove. Every burner was on. The blue flames were low, precise, efficient. In the vision she knew, with absolute certainty, that she had turned each burner on in turn and could not stop doing it — that she would have to go around again and check, and again, and that the checking would never relieve the certainty that one was still on, because in the checking you were also turning them on.

It lasted approximately four minutes. She was aware, dimly, of the room around her — Delgado's voice, the chair beneath her, the particular quality of the afternoon light from the window. But she could not get to those things. They were behind the vision like sound behind glass.

When she came back, Delgado had stopped talking and was looking at her.

"Dr. Park?"

"I'm sorry," she said. Her voice was even. She was not sure how. "I had a brief — a migraine precursor. It happens occasionally. Please continue."

He continued. She took notes in her regular hand, looking at her own handwriting as though it were evidence.

After the session she went to the bathroom and ran cold water over her wrists. She looked at herself in the mirror. Normal. Recognizably herself. No tremor.

She had not changed her water. She had been drinking from her own bottle for ten days and the visions had stopped for ten days and now they were back. Which meant either the compound was still in her system — a persistent effect — or she had been dosed through another vector she hadn't identified.

Or Delgado.

She stopped with her wrists under the cold tap.

Delgado was not on the protocol. He was not in the program. He was not receiving the compound, as far as she knew.

But she had been in sessions with three protocol patients in the past week for consultation purposes, covering while another clinician was away. The compound persisted in the system. If the mirror-state effect was bilateral — if the patient, dosed enough, could induce the shared state even without the clinician being dosed — then proximity was enough.

She was still in range.

She turned off the tap. She went back to her office and started writing.

Chapter 20: Agatha's File

Fall from height, cardiac event.

Chapter 21: Pattern Recognition

She spread the documents across her kitchen table that evening, when she was certain she was alone and the door was locked. Calista's log. Agatha's incident reports. The photographs from the mirror room. The molecular data from Ashby. The 2003 paper and retraction. Her own observation notes.

She mapped the deaths first.

2018: two patients, enhanced empathic engagement protocol, pilot phase. Dosage elevated in forty-eight hours preceding death. Both deaths ruled accidental. Webb told Agatha not to escalate.

Present: Devin Chen. Protocol Phase 1. Dosage — she checked the medication records she'd photographed — was elevated on Monday, two days before he died on Wednesday. Nighttime window-seeking behavior logged by Agatha. Death ruled accidental pending investigation.

Pattern: all three deaths followed by forty-eight to seventy-two hours a dosage elevation in a patient with a phobia that created physical risk. In each case the phobia involved proximity to a specific environmental feature — height, or in one of the 2018 cases, cardiac stress from confined spaces. In each case the compound was lifting the cognitive override. Removing the protection.

She drew a timeline. She numbered the nodes.

Then she wrote a second track, parallel: clinician behavior. In 2018 she had not been at Harmon. But she found, in the internal records she'd photographed, a note from one of the other clinicians from that period — a clinician no longer at the institute — describing "unusual attunement experiences" during sessions, which had been filed under "protocol adjustment effects, transient."

Transient. Right.

Her own visions mapped exactly onto the timeline of Devin's escalated dosing. The second vision, the most specific, had come on day two of the elevated dose. Day three he was dead.

She wrote: the compound creates a shared neurological field. The more distressed the patient, the more intense the clinician's mirror response. The clinician's response amplifies the patient's distress. It is a feedback loop with no natural off-switch.

She wrote: Webb knows this. This is what he's measuring. The loop itself is the phenomenon he is harvesting.

She wrote: Veridian Cognitive Systems is buying data on human mirror-state co-regulation during crisis. The product is not therapy. The product is fear.

She sat back and looked at what she'd written. Then she looked at all the documents spread across her kitchen table.

She photographed the layout. She folded the originals back into the folder. She went to bed and did not sleep for a long time.

Chapter 22: Confronting Liam

She waited until the next morning. She found him at his desk before the first session of the day, which meant before eight-thirty. She closed his office door.

Chapter 23: The Data Buyer

On a Friday afternoon she went back to the mirror room.

She had been more careful this time about the timing — she knew the maintenance schedule, she knew when the clinical wing was emptiest. She was inside the room within four minutes and had the door behind her.

She did not touch the server. But she moved along the edge of the monitoring station and looked at what was visible. A clipboard on a hook beside the door contained a sheet of paper with a printed header: RTX-7 Session Data Transfer Protocol — Harmon Site. She photographed it.

Beneath the header were columns: session date, session ID, patient identifier, clinician identifier, data volume, transfer status.

The clinician identifiers included hers. Three sessions, marked as transferred. She had not known she was in these sessions as a measurable variable. She thought she had been simply a clinician doing clinical work. But her neurological mirror-state during those sessions had been recorded and transferred.

She photographed the sheet twice.

There was a manila envelope pinned to the corkboard above the station. She opened it. Inside were two pages: the front page of a contract, and a schedule of data-transfer fees.

The contract was between Harmon Institute LLC and Veridian Cognitive Systems. The stated purpose was clinical research data acquisition. The scope included "neurological state recordings, dual-subject mirror sessions, baseline and elevated protocol conditions."

The fee schedule was per session. The elevated protocol conditions — the sessions following dosage increases — were priced three times higher than baseline.

She photographed both pages and replaced everything exactly.

She left the room.

She stood in the utility cupboard for thirty seconds and breathed. Then she walked back through the corridor in the direction of her office as if she had been nowhere unusual, doing nothing unusual, and had simply passed through on her way to something else.

The fee schedule. The dual-subject sessions. The timing of the dosage elevations.

Webb was not just collecting data. He was collecting the most valuable data, the crisis data, at optimal yield. And he was doing it by engineering the crises.

The deaths were not accidents born of negligence. The deaths were the result of a system optimized for maximum fear response data, with no adequate mechanism to prevent a patient from crossing the line from enhanced fear to lethal action.

She went to her office and photographed her own face in the small mirror above the sink. She looked at herself for a long time. She looked like herself.

She wasn't certain that meant what it used to mean.

Chapter 24: Calista's History

Calista was in the reading room on a Tuesday morning, a room used by perhaps two or three patients regularly, which meant it was usually empty. She was reading, as she usually was, but she set the book down the moment Sonia came in.

"I need to know about Veridian," Sonia said.

Calista looked at her hands. "I worked there for three years. Research data management, initially. Then I was moved to what they called clinical outcomes analysis." A pause. "The outcomes were psychological state recordings. Neuro-imaging, EEG, galvanic response. The subjects were clinical patients at partner institutions."

"Did you know the subjects had consented to that use of their data?"

"I believed they had. The consent documentation crossed my desk. It was technically adequate." She paused. "But I was in data management. I didn't see the original consent forms. I saw processed versions. After my second year I began to ask questions about the consent chain and was told that it was handled at a different level."

"What made you leave?"

"I reviewed a data set that included audio and neurological recordings from sessions at a facility I recognized. I had a friend who had been a patient there." She looked at the window. "She had told me about her sessions in detail, over dinners, the way you do with a friend. What I saw in the data set was not what she had consented to. It was not what she thought she was part of."

She said this simply, without affect, which Sonia recognized as the tone of something told many times internally and very few times aloud.

"When you left, did you report it?"

"I tried. The regulatory contact I spoke to was also a Veridian consultant. The paper chain was — I realized it was circular. Anywhere I turned, the path led back into the structure." She looked at Sonia. "I had a breakdown afterward. That's the clinical truth. The dissociation began then. My referring psychiatrist found Harmon."

"Do you believe your referral to Harmon was intentional? That someone directed you here?"

"Yes." She said it without drama. "I believe I was placed here to be contained. The assumption was probably that I would become a patient and my testimony would be clinically compromised. A dissociating former employee who had a breakdown." A thin smile. "It was actually quite elegant."

"But you've been watching since you arrived."

"I have." She looked at the book she'd set down. "I'm not as compromised as they assumed. The dissociation is real but it doesn't affect my cognition. I can observe. I can document."

"I may need your testimony."

"I know. I've been preparing it." She reached into her cardigan pocket and produced a flash drive. "This is a copy of the documents I kept when I left Veridian. I kept them in a format that can't be attributed to me forensically — I learned something in data management." She held it out. "I've been waiting to give this to someone I trusted."

Sonia took the drive. It was small and very light. It felt heavier.

Chapter 25: Webb Moves

She was not surprised when Webb called her in. She had been expecting it for four days, and when his assistant came to find her between sessions on a Thursday, she went with the particular internal stillness that she had learned to produce when she needed it.

His office was arranged differently. The chairs were closer together, angled toward each other rather than toward the desk. He was standing when she came in, which was unusual — Webb typically used the desk as a piece of territorial staging.

"Sit down, Sonia."

She sat. He sat across from her.

"I've been hearing some things," he said. "Staff talk. Questions being asked in places they shouldn't need to be asked." He looked at her with frank concern — the concern of a man who is genuinely worried about a colleague's welfare, or who performs that concern so convincingly as to make the distinction immaterial. "How are you sleeping?"

"Well enough."

"You've been working a lot of evening hours. Night rounds."

"Once. I covered for a colleague."

"You've been in the records room several times in the past two weeks."

She met his eyes. "Case review. Devin Chen's death has naturally prompted me to review my own clinical records."

"Of course." He nodded slowly. "That makes sense. And it's appropriate. I want to make sure you're also taking care of yourself, Sonia. The protocol generates significant emotional intensity — even for clinicians not on the supportive track. I've been concerned that the secondhand exposure might be affecting you."

"I'm not experiencing any adverse effects."

"No intrusive thoughts? Any difficulty distinguishing your own emotional material from your patients'?"

She looked at him. He was watching her with such precise attention that she understood: he already knew. About the visions. About the resonance episodes. He had the session data. He had the recordings from the observation equipment.

"I'm functioning at full capacity," she said.

"I'm glad." He reached for a small folder on the end table beside him. "I'd like to add you to the clinician wellness track. Not the attunement dose — I know you've been hesitant about that. This is a monitoring protocol. Low-dose supportive pharmacology, nothing unusual, designed to stabilize the empathic load during high-intensity clinical periods." He held out the folder. "I'd like to start you on it this week."

She took the folder. She looked at it.

"What's the compound?" she said.

"A standard anxiolytic. Nothing novel."

She opened the folder. The prescription was for something labeled, again, as "Protocol Wellness Support, Compound B-2."

"I appreciate the offer," she said. "I'll review the literature."

"Of course." He watched her tuck the folder under her arm. His smile was benevolent and precise. "The first dose would simply be in the morning vitamin dispensary. Nothing complicated. You'll barely notice it."

She looked at him.

She smiled back.

Chapter 26: The Blurred Line

She did not take the supplement. She did not touch the vitamin dispensary. She bought her own vitamins from a pharmacy in town.

But the visions came anyway.

Not as frequently as before. Not with the same intensity. But on a Thursday afternoon, reviewing session notes in her office, she experienced a sudden, brief vivid sense of being in a small enclosed space — a broom closet, perhaps, with the door closed and the walls very near. It lasted six seconds and then it was gone, leaving only the residue of constriction, of air that wasn't quite enough.

She looked at the session calendar. The patient with claustrophobia — she had been brought to Sonia's attention in the past week, in a consultation role. Diana Taggart, forty-one, on the protocol.

She wrote it down: Thursday, 3:07 p.m. Enclosed space. Six seconds. Consistent with Taggart's phobia presentation. No prior compound exposure through a tracked vector in past seventy-two hours. Possible residual compound from earlier period. Possible direct mirror-state activation from close clinical contact.

She did not know which.

She began to wonder if this mattered less than she'd assumed. If the compound was doing what Ashby thought it was doing — activating mirror neurons, dissolving self-other boundaries — then the dissolution might not require continuous dosing. It might need only an initial activation and the right proximity. Like a resonance that, once established, could be maintained by the field itself.

She was having thoughts she could not identify as clearly her own.

This was, she understood, the specific terror of the situation. Not the visions themselves — those, she could document, analyze, hold at arm's length. What was harder was the growing uncertainty about whether the analysis was hers. Whether the careful, methodical investigation she had been conducting was driven by her own judgment, or whether she had been, in some sense, guided — the critical thoughts amplified, the concern for patients enhanced, the urgency calibrated — because it served someone's data-collection purposes for her to investigate.

She wrote this thought down. She labeled it: possible paranoid ideation secondary to compound exposure.

Then she labeled it: or accurate perception.

She could not tell which.

She began keeping two columns in her notebook: one for observations she trusted as factual, and one for observations she was uncertain about. The factual column was longer. But the uncertain column was growing.

Chapter 27: Agatha's Choice

She found Agatha in the medication room again — Agatha seemed to orient toward that room the way the protocol patients oriented toward their fears, as if it were the place where she kept something she couldn't otherwise hold.

"I need to tell you something," Agatha said, before Sonia could speak. She closed the medication cabinet. She looked at the door, then at Sonia. "Two years after the 2018 incidents, there was a man who came to see me. He said he was from a patient advocacy organization. He had some questions about clinical protocols." She paused. "I didn't tell him anything. I was careful. But he left a card. I found it last month in an old coat pocket." She reached into the pocket of her nursing uniform and produced it.

Sandra Luu. Health Policy Quarterly. New York.

"She's a journalist," Agatha said. "She was investigating Veridian three years ago. I looked her up."

Sonia took the card.

"I want to report," Agatha said. "I've been carrying those files for six years. I should have acted in 2018 and I didn't and Devin Chen is dead." She said it flatly, as a statement about herself. "I want to make the formal report. But I'm frightened."

"Tell me what frightened you."

"After the second 2018 death, I wrote a memo. A private one, not filed. I mentioned it to one person — a colleague, someone I thought I could trust. The next week Webb called me in for a performance review that had never been scheduled. It was polite. Professional. But it covered my documentation practices in detail. Very specific detail." She looked at the cabinet. "He knew about the memo. Someone had told him."

"The colleague you mentioned it to."

"I believe so."

"Who was that?"

Agatha was quiet for a moment. "Dr. Ferris," she said. "He was new then. I thought he was — I thought he was safe." A pause. "I was wrong about that."

Sonia looked at the card in her hand. "Liam told Webb you had a memo."

"I believe so. Yes."

She put the card in her pocket. "Make your formal report to me, in writing. Everything you have, every date, everything. I'll keep it with the other documentation." She paused. "If I ask you to be ready to speak to a journalist and a state oversight official in the same week, can you do it?"

Agatha looked at her directly. "Yes," she said. "I've been waiting to be asked."

Chapter 28: Liam's Confession

He came to her office on a Sunday morning when the institute was quiet. He knocked. He stood in the doorway looking like a man who had spent a week not sleeping and had decided that sleeping was less important than resolution.

"Come in," she said.

He sat down. He put a folder on her desk. "Everything I know. Everything I did. I've written it out. I want you to have it."

She looked at the folder but didn't open it yet. "Tell me out loud first."

He told her.

The full structure of the data collection scheme: Webb had been running a research program at Harmon for seven years, in three phases. Phase one had been the 2018 pilot, two patients, proof of concept. Both died. Webb had attributed this to dosage calibration errors and had refined the protocol. Phase two had been a smaller trial, two patients in 2021, no deaths, lower-quality data because the dosage was too cautious. Phase three was the current program.

The target data was specific: neurological recordings during what Webb called "peak fear engagement" — the moment of maximal phobic response, measured through the TMS monitoring system, recorded through the observation equipment, uploaded to Veridian. The clinician's mirror-state was recorded simultaneously. The combined recording — patient and clinician in synchronized fear states — was what Veridian wanted.

"Why?" Sonia said.

"Mapping," Liam said. "Veridian is building a model of human fear architecture. Patient data alone gives you the patient's neural map. But the clinician's mirror-state gives you the same fear mapped through a second nervous system, interpreted rather than experienced. You get the inside view and the outside view simultaneously. It's extraordinary data, from a pure neuroscience standpoint." He looked at his hands. "I told myself it was for science. I told myself no one would be hurt."

"But you knew about the 2018 deaths."

"I found out about them four months in. Webb told me then. He said the protocol had been refined. He said the risk was now negligible." He looked up. "I chose to believe him."

"Why are you telling me now?"

"Because you were right and I knew you were right and I couldn't sleep." He looked at the folder. "And because I think it's going to come out regardless. I'd rather be on the right side of it when it does."

She opened the folder. The first page was a signed declaration. At the bottom he had written, in his own handwriting: I participated knowingly in a research program that resulted in at least one patient's death and I did not report it. I am providing this account voluntarily.

She read it twice. She put it on top of the other documents.

"I need you to not discuss this with Webb," she said. "Can you do that?"

"Yes."

"If he comes to you — if he asks about your state of mind, your cooperation, anything — give him nothing."

"He already knows I've been struggling," Liam said. "He offered me the wellness protocol last week."

She looked at him. "Did you take it?"

He hesitated. "One dose," he said. "I didn't want to arouse suspicion by refusing."

She closed her eyes briefly. "That was a mistake."

"I know."

"It's probably a sedating preparation. Something to slow your critical faculty."

"I know." He looked defeated. "I'm sorry."

"Stop apologizing," she said. "Help me instead."

Chapter 29: The Third Patient

She got the call at six-forty on a Tuesday morning. Agatha's voice, quiet and precise: Diana Taggart was non-responsive in the supply closet on the first floor, east wing.

She got there before the medical team. Agatha was already there with a flashlight, kneeling beside Taggart in the narrow space between metal shelving and industrial cleaning supplies. Taggart was seated with her back against the wall, her knees drawn up, her eyes open and unfocused. She was breathing. She was not responding to her name or to touch.

Catatonic state. Sonia had seen it twice before in severe dissociative presentations.

She crouched beside Taggart and spoke her name steadily and then differently, at a different register, coming at her from a different angle. She watched Taggart's eyes. After approximately ninety seconds — which was long, but not forever — a focus began to return.

"Diana," she said. "You're in the building. You're safe. I'm Dr. Park."

Taggart's eyes moved to her face. Then to Agatha's. Then to the door of the closet, which Agatha had propped open with her foot.

"Is the door open?" Taggart said.

"Yes. You can see the corridor."

"Don't close it." She looked at the walls. "Don't close it."

"We won't," Sonia said.

The medical team arrived. Taggart was moved to the clinical observation ward — carefully, with the door problem managed, the gurney placed in the corridor first, then her walking to it rather than being carried through a narrowing space. She was calm once she was in the open ward.

In the corridor afterward Sonia spoke to Agatha quietly.

"How long was she in there?"

"I found her at six-forty. My last round was eleven. She wasn't there at eleven."

"So potentially up to seven hours."

"The supply closet is equipped with an internal latch. She could have gotten in and then couldn't get out in the dark." Agatha paused. "Or she got in and latched it herself."

"What was her dosage schedule last night?"

"Elevated," Agatha said. "Dr. Webb authorized an increase yesterday afternoon."

They looked at each other.

"I need to know who made the call to increase," Sonia said.

"Webb. Directly. He overrode Dr. Yuen's standing order."

She thought of the fee schedule in the mirror room: elevated protocol conditions, three times the baseline rate.

"Is she still on the protocol?" she said.

"Formally, yes."

"I'm removing her from the protocol," Sonia said. "Under my clinical authority, as consulting clinician on this case. I'll document it and I'll face whatever Webb wants to say about it."

"And Mr. Pereira?" Agatha said.

The third patient. She had almost lost track of him in the specific terror of the past hour.

"Where is he?"

"In his room. I checked at six-thirty. He was asleep." She paused. "His dosage hasn't been increased yet. I watched the schedule."

"Watch it very carefully," Sonia said. "Any change to his dosage, I want to know immediately."

She went to find Webb.

Chapter 30: The Recording

She didn't find Webb that morning — he had an offsite appointment, his assistant said, back after lunch. She spent the morning with Diana Taggart, then with the rest of her caseload, and she did not go to the mirror room until mid-afternoon.

This time she risked the server.

She had Calista's flash drive with Veridian documents. She did not use it. She simply connected her own drive to the server's front-facing port — the server's file system was accessible without authentication, a configuration detail that she suspected was intentional, because the people who reviewed this data trusted the physical security of the room rather than the digital security.

She navigated to the session folder. She found, as she expected, files organized by date and clinician code. She found her own code. She copied everything to the drive.

She disconnected and left the room.

In her car, in the parking lot, she listened to three minutes of the first audio file.

She heard herself, in session with Devin Chen, from a session she remembered clearly. Her questions, her voice, her precise clinical language. And then, underneath it, at a register she had not been aware of in the room — not a sound, exactly, but a physiological signal rendered as audio — a continuous monitoring of some kind of biological response.

Then she heard something she had not been aware of in the session.

At a moment when she had leaned forward to ask a question — she could hear the change in her own voice, the small shift of posture — there was a soft, almost subsonic pulse. Barely audible. Timed precisely to the moment she made the empathic bridge with Devin.

The TMS. The transcranial stimulation, timed to her own moment of peak empathic engagement.

They had been stimulating her as well as the patient. Without her knowledge. Without her consent.

She had been in the experiment from the first session.

She sat in the parking lot for twelve minutes. The engine was off. Outside the car the late afternoon was grey and cold and very normal. She looked at the windshield.

Then she started the car and drove to the pharmacy and bought a second locked case for the documents she was accumulating, and drove back, and returned to her office, and proceeded with the rest of the day's work in the way that a person proceeds when they have understood something that cannot be ununderstood.

Chapter 31: Trust Nobody

She trusted three people. She made herself be honest about this.

She trusted Calista Graves, who had come here to watch and had the data to prove what she'd seen. Calista was a patient, which meant her testimony was easily dismissed — which meant Sonia needed to protect and archive her account carefully.

She trusted Agatha Rourke, who had carried six years of guilt and documentation and had given both to Sonia without conditions. Agatha was a nurse, which meant she was credible in some contexts and invisible in others — a combination that could be useful.

She did not trust Liam Ferris. She had his written confession, which was valuable, but she did not trust that he would maintain his position if Webb applied pressure. One dose of the wellness compound was either negligence or capitulation, and she had not decided which.

She did not trust anyone at the institute she hadn't already spoken to about this. She did not know, with any certainty, who else had been dosed. Who else was a variable in the protocol she hadn't accounted for.

She did not entirely trust herself.

This was the hardest part. She was aware that her reasoning about the protocol was correct — the facts were the facts, the documents were the documents. But she was also aware that the compound had been in her system for an unknown duration, and that its effects on her perception included a documented susceptibility to other people's emotional states. She was seeing the situation clearly, she believed. But she was seeing it through a perceptual apparatus that had been chemically altered.

She made a rule: every conclusion she was confident in, she would test against the documents. If the documents supported it, she would act on it. If the documents didn't support it, she would hold it.

The conclusions the documents supported were clear enough: people were being experimented on without proper consent. People were being harmed. Someone needed to be told and evidence needed to be preserved.

She went to the library in Millhaven that evening and used a public computer to send a large encrypted file to her personal email account. The encryption she used was something Calista had helped her set up, using a program Calista had kept on her phone.

She sent the same file to Grant Ashby in New York, with a brief message: hold this. If you don't hear from me in ten days, open it and act on it.

She went home. She made a list. She was careful with the list.

Chapter 32: The Regulator

She called the New York State Office of Mental Health Oversight on a Monday morning from her car, with the engine running and the door locked. She had the name of a specific compliance officer from the oversight body's website — a woman named Carolyn Voss, listed as senior investigator for psychiatric facility compliance.

The call was transferred twice and then answered.

She identified herself. She said she had concerns about clinical practices at the Harmon Institute related to non-consented experimental pharmacological administration. She used the exact language the oversight regulations required to trigger a mandatory review.

The woman on the line — Carolyn Voss — was professional and measured. She asked clarifying questions. She asked for documentation. She said she would open a preliminary inquiry file.

Then she said: "Are you familiar with Dr. Marcus Webb's research history?"

Sonia paused. "Somewhat," she said.

"He's been a contributor to the oversight body's advisory panel," Voss said. "For about four years."

A silence.

"You should be aware of that as context," Voss said, and her voice was perfectly neutral in a way that was either professional or pointed.

Sonia said, "I appreciate you noting that."

"I'll be in touch."

She sat in the car for a moment after the call. Then she thought about what Grant Ashby had said: you need more than you have.

She did not know whether Carolyn Voss was compromised. She did not know whether the advisory panel connection meant anything in practice or whether it was simply an institutional fact that Voss was required to disclose. The disclosure itself might have been a warning, or it might have been standard.

She noted it as: regulator contact established. Response ambiguous. Advisory panel connection noted. Do not rely on this channel exclusively.

She had another line. She thought about Sandra Luu's card in her pocket.

She was not ready to use it yet. She needed to be further along before she involved media — once that move was made, she could not control the pace of what followed.

She drove back to the institute and went to work.

Chapter 33: Webb's Rationale

He found her. She had been expecting him to find her, and on a Wednesday at three he appeared in her office doorway with his hand on the doorframe and his reading glasses up on his forehead, which was the posture he used when he wanted to seem casual.

"Do you have a moment?"

"Yes."

He came in and left the door open, which surprised her. Either he was confident enough to have this conversation publicly or he was staging openness.

"I know you've been making inquiries," he said. "I'm not going to pretend otherwise."

She waited.

"I also know you've spoken to the oversight office." He sat down without being invited, which he sometimes did as a territorial gesture. "Carolyn Voss is not who you think she is."

"I don't think I know who she is."

"She's a competent regulator who will do a standard investigation and find a standard set of documentation and close the inquiry with a recommendation for protocol review. Nothing will happen quickly. And in the meantime—" He looked at her. "In the meantime, I'd rather you and I had an honest conversation."

"We're having one."

He leaned forward with his elbows on his knees in the posture of a man speaking frankly. She had seen this posture many times. "The protocol works, Sonia. Not in the way that was intended in 2003 — I've learned a great deal since then. But the data we're collecting will change how neurological treatment approaches phobia and trauma. We will be able to design precision interventions. We will save an enormous number of lives."

"You've killed people."

"There have been casualties. Two in 2018 that I should have prevented, and one this month. I take responsibility for that. I've made errors in dosage calibration."

"You made three people dead," she said. "And you've been recording their deaths for money."

His expression didn't change. "Veridian is funding critical research. The data has scientific value that—"

"The data has commercial value. That's different from scientific value."

"They're not exclusive."

"You recorded Devin Chen dying," she said. "Whatever else you want to say, that's what happened. His fear response during his fatal episode is in your server and has been transferred to a company that sells it."

A long silence.

"I need you to stop," he said. "Not permanently — I'm not asking you to ignore this forever. I'm asking you to wait. To let the research complete. The full protocol data will be published. It will be peer-reviewed. It will be—"

"No," she said.

He looked at her with something she had not seen on his face before. A recalibration.

"You should take care," he said. It was not a threat, exactly. It was precise enough that she couldn't prove it was a threat.

"I always take care," she said. "Thank you for the conversation."

Chapter 34: The Break

She tried to leave the campus on a Friday evening. She had been planning to drive to a friend's apartment in the city and spend the night, put distance between herself and the institute's walls. She had her keys. She had her coat. She had the locked case in her bag.

She was in the parking lot when Dr. Yuen came out of the side door and walked across to her.

"Dr. Park." He was pleasant, uninflected. "Are you heading out for the weekend?"

"Yes."

"Actually—" He reached into his coat pocket. "Webb left a note for you. He was hoping you'd be able to stay on campus this weekend. We have two patients on enhanced monitoring and he'd feel better with full clinical coverage."

She looked at the note. It was handwritten, on Webb's personalized notepad, requesting her voluntary extended presence for patient safety reasons. Voluntary. The word was doing a great deal of work.

"I wasn't aware there were any coverage concerns this weekend," she said.

"It was decided earlier today." Yuen looked at her with a pleasantness that had no texture beneath it. "The scheduling office should have sent you an alert."

She checked her phone. There was a schedule update sent forty minutes ago.

She looked at the parking lot. The exit gate was open — it was always open during daylight hours. No one was blocking her physically. Everything was procedural, professional, voluntary.

She could drive away. She could simply drive away.

She looked at Pereira's window on the fourth floor. The room was lit.

"Of course," she said. "I'll stay."

She went back inside. She went to her office and sat down and looked at the walls and thought about what it meant that she had chosen not to leave.

It meant she was not going to leave Pereira here alone under elevated monitoring.

It also meant she was now, as of today, someone who had voluntarily stayed when given the opportunity to go. If things went badly, that would matter.

She called Ashby. She kept the call short: "I'm going to be delayed on my end. Make sure you have what I sent." A pause. "I'm fine. I just need you to have it."

She disconnected. She went to check on Pereira.

Chapter 35: Calista's Escape

Calista had a family visit scheduled for a Sunday — her mother, a standing arrangement that the institute had approved in week two. Sonia had not engineered this. But she had been thinking about it.

On Saturday evening she went to Calista's room.

"You have a family visit tomorrow," she said.

Calista was reading. She looked up. "Yes."

"I need you to take something with you and not bring it back."

Calista set down the book. She looked at Sonia carefully. "What do you need me to take?"

"A copy of everything I have. The photographs, the documents, the audio files." She had already prepared the drive. She put it on the small table. "In case something happens to the copies I'm keeping here."

"I'm a patient," Calista said. "If I take confidential institutional documents out of a facility—"

"They're not institutional documents. They're photographs I took on my personal phone. And Veridian's contract documents that you originally documented yourself."

Calista looked at the drive. "What should I do with them?"

"Keep them. If you haven't heard from me in two weeks, open the sealed document in the folder labeled FOR EXTERNAL USE. It has a contact name and number. Follow the instructions."

"Who is the contact?"

"A journalist."

Calista picked up the drive. She turned it in her fingers. "You're going to stay," she said. It wasn't a question.

"Yes."

"Even though you could go."

"There are still patients here."

Calista looked at her for a long moment with the frank, observational quality that characterized her at all times. "You know they're using your presence. They're using your decision to stay as part of the data. You're the ideal subject — a clinician who suspects what's happening and stays anyway."

"Yes," Sonia said. "I know that."

"But you're staying."

"The patients are real," Sonia said. "The data is real. If I go, Pereira is here alone."

Calista put the drive in the pocket of her cardigan, beside the one where she'd originally kept the documents she'd given Sonia.

"All right," she said. "I'll go tomorrow. I'll keep everything safe."

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me." She picked up her book again. "Just be careful about what you eat and drink."

Chapter 36: Liam Turns

She found out on a Monday, from Agatha. Liam had been in Webb's office for two hours on Friday afternoon and had come out looking lighter in the particular way that indicates relief from a burden one has been carrying improperly.

By Monday morning a printed memo was in Sonia's inbox. A statement from Dr. Liam Ferris, clinical psychologist: following review of concerns raised by a colleague regarding the Harmon Protocol, I find no basis for clinical or ethical alarm. The protocol is being administered within standard parameters and with appropriate consent documentation. Any anomalous personal experiences reported by the clinical team are consistent with expected empathic attunement effects disclosed in the protocol briefing.

Any anomalous personal experiences reported by the clinical team. Meaning her experiences. Her visions. Meaning Liam had told Webb what she'd shared with him over lunch.

She read the memo twice. She put it in the folder.

She thought about Liam's one dose of the wellness compound. She thought about how quickly things had moved. She thought about what it was like to have a document in your desk drawer that said I participated knowingly in a research program that resulted in at least one patient's death and then to produce a memo that said I find no basis for ethical alarm.

She felt, very briefly, the specific grief of understanding that someone had chosen to protect themselves by destroying something they'd briefly managed to be.

Then she filed it and moved on.

She still had his signed declaration. He didn't know she still had it.

She made a note to verify the chain of custody on everything she had. She was going to need every piece.

Chapter 37: The Vision Crisis

It came on a Thursday afternoon between four and four-forty, while she was alone in her office reviewing notes.

There was no warning. The office dissolved and she was in the experience completely.

She was sitting on a ledge. Beneath her was nothing — a very long way down, grey and terminal. She knew, with a certainty that made cognitive override impossible to access, that she was about to jump. Not that she wanted to. Not that she intended to. But the body she was inhabiting was past the point of intending and wanting and was operating on something more fundamental: the presence of the ledge and the proximity of the air below it and the fact of the next moment.

She was aware she was in her office. She was aware she was sitting in a chair. She could feel the chair's arms beneath her hands, the pressure of the upholstered seat. These facts were available to her, but they were in a different register from the primary experience, like a voice calling from a room three doors away.

She used the chair. She pressed her palms down hard on the arms. She felt the texture of the fabric. She said, out loud, in the empty office: "I am in my office. I am sitting in a chair. My name is Sonia Park."

Then again.

Then she wrote it in her notebook, pressing hard enough that the pen made a dent in the page below.

It took twenty minutes to fully withdraw. At four-forty she was back in her office, entirely in her office, her hands still pressing on the chair arms.

She sat for five more minutes.

Then she opened her notebook to a new page. She wrote the full account, with timestamps. She noted the duration, the content, the recovery procedure. She noted that she had not been in session with any patient for two hours prior. She noted that the content was consistent with acrophobic imagery from Devin Chen's sessions.

Devin Chen was dead. She had not been in a session with him for three weeks.

She wrote: persistent compound effects or direct mirror-state from Devin Chen's final episodes, encoded in my neurological system and replaying. Then she wrote: I am all right.

She wrote it twice because the first time she wasn't fully convinced.

She sat with the notebook on her lap and breathed carefully until she was.

Chapter 38: Agatha Acts

She didn't tell Sonia until after the fact. Sonia found out on a Saturday morning when Agatha came to her office without knocking and said, "I called the journalist."

Sonia set down her coffee. "When?"

"Yesterday. After Mr. Pereira's dosage change."

Pereira's dosage had been elevated on Friday — she had been notified by Agatha as agreed, had immediately gone to Webb, and had been told it was within the protocol parameters. She had spent Friday evening watching Pereira's behavioral patterns and finding them consistent with what Calista's log had described in the days before Devin Chen's death.

"You used the card."

"I did." Agatha stood very straight. "Sandra Luu has been investigating Veridian for three years. She had the company name. She had some of the contract structure from a prior source. What she was missing was clinical documentation." A pause. "I told her I had documentation. She's coming here."

"When?"

"She's already en route. I told her to come as a visitor for another patient — there's a public visiting day this afternoon. She'll be here at two."

Sonia looked at the clock. Two hours.

"Agatha," she said. "This is irreversible. Once a journalist is in the building—"

"I know." Agatha's expression was as flat and certain as she had ever seen it. "Devin Chen is dead. I filed nothing in 2018. I am not filing nothing again."

They looked at each other.

"All right," Sonia said. "Here's what we're going to do."

She spent the next ninety minutes organizing everything. She created two identical evidence packages: one in a physical folder, one on a drive. One of each for Luu. One of each to be mailed today, from the town post office, to Grant Ashby. She printed Calista's log in its complete form. She printed Liam's signed declaration.

She included the recording. The audio file from her session with Devin, with the subsonic TMS pulse clearly audible.

At two-fifteen Sandra Luu sat in the institute's family visiting room under a visitor badge that said she was there to see a patient named Claire Morrison. She was thirty-eight, observant, and moved through the visiting room as if she had calculated every exit. Sonia sat across from her with the folder on the table between them.

"How long have you been building this?" Luu said.

"About three weeks of documentation. The foundation goes back to 2018 — that's Agatha Rourke's contribution."

Luu looked at the folder. "Walk me through it."

Sonia walked her through it. All of it. She kept her voice level and her language factual. She did not editorialize. She let the documents speak where the documents spoke clearly and she narrated the context where they needed context.

It took forty-five minutes.

At the end, Luu looked up from the last page. "This is enough," she said. "This is more than enough."

Chapter 39: Inside the Protocol

Webb called her on a Wednesday morning to invite her to observe the protocol session. He framed it as a professional development opportunity — she would observe one of the enhanced sessions from behind the observation glass, not in the room, purely to understand the protocol's mechanism at first hand.

She said yes.

She said yes for the same reason she had said yes to Webb's invitation to the Thursday staff meeting two months earlier: she would learn more from the inside than she would learn from declining.

The session was scheduled for three in the afternoon. The patient was Pereira — his dosage had been elevated again, this time substantially. She knew this from Agatha's notification. She also knew, from Agatha, that Webb had overridden Yuen's objections in writing this time.

She was in the monitoring room at two-fifty. She was alone in it. The TMS equipment was active.

She watched through the one-way glass.

Webb conducted the session himself. She had not seen him in a clinical session before — he was primarily administrative, and his few clinical hours were generally observed only by protocol staff. He was, she noted with a cold attention, very good. His manner with Pereira was warm and specifically attuned. He asked about the stairwells — Pereira's documented phobia was a fear of falling upward, a rare variant of acrophobia in which the fear was not of height per se but of the sensation of ascending. He spoke about the stairwells in a way that made Pereira's fear present without alarming him.

Then he nodded to someone off-camera.

The TMS pulse began.

She heard it. Barely. Under the audio feed, under the session sounds, the subsonic calibration of the pulse. And she felt — she did not want to feel it, but she did — the small, cold vertical certainty of Pereira's fear as it was amplified through his enhanced state and found the bridge to her own mirror system.

She took out her notepad. She wrote: pulse initiated 3:12 p.m. Protocol elevated dose plus TMS stimulation. Patient Pereira affect: initially calm, escalating to visible agitation by 3:18.

She wrote: I can feel his state. I am documenting that I can feel it and that I am not allowing it to affect my actions.

She kept writing. She wrote everything she saw and heard for forty minutes, and at the end of the session she had twelve pages of precise observation that described, in clinical language, a therapeutic session designed not to heal but to produce optimal data at the moment of maximal patient distress.

She had the notes. She had the audio from the recording system, which she had attached her drive to at two-fifty, before Webb arrived.

She had the final piece.

She went back to her office and added it to the folder and mailed a second copy to Ashby that evening.

Chapter 40: The Threshold

On a Friday night she sat in her office after the last session and did not move for a long time.

She had everything she needed. She had more than she needed. The case was documented to a degree that would survive professional scrutiny. The journalist had the evidence. The regulator had the initial report. Ashby had the backup. Calista was outside the institute.

What remained was the question she had been avoiding since week two: how much of what she believed was actually true.

She was trained in epistemology. She knew how to evaluate claims against evidence. She had done that throughout this investigation. But she was also a person who had been dosed with a compound that altered the boundary between self and other, who had experienced multiple vivid intrusive neurological states, who had spent weeks in a building where the line between what was happening to patients and what was happening to her had been systematically blurred.

She had a notebook with two columns. The factual column was long and specific and supported by documents and recordings and two independent sources. The uncertain column was also specific — her visions, her suspicions about being followed by her own thoughts, the moments when she couldn't be sure if what she was thinking was hers.

She read the uncertain column.

Every item in it was consistent with the documented effects of the compound. The visions were consistent with mirror-state activation. The difficulty distinguishing her own thoughts was consistent with boundary dissolution. The fear that her investigation was being guided externally — that was consistent with the paranoid ideation that Ashby had warned about as a possible compound effect.

But the investigation was also correct. The documents were real. The deaths were real. The equipment in the mirror room was real.

She wrote: I am acting on incomplete certainty. This is the condition of all action. The incomplete certainty on the side of acting is documented and supported. The incomplete certainty on the side of not acting consists of three deaths and an active experiment on at least one current patient.

She closed the notebook.

She had already made the decision. She was simply catching up to having made it.

---

Chapter 41: The Journalist

Sandra Luu published the first piece on a Tuesday morning under the headline: PRIVATE CLINIC TIED TO UNAPPROVED NEUROLOGICAL EXPERIMENT — PATIENTS' DEATHS UNREPORTED. Health Policy Quarterly, online edition, full documentation package linked.

The piece named Harmon Institute, Dr. Marcus Webb, and Veridian Cognitive Systems by name. It cited the contract documentation, the fee schedule, the compound's pharmacological mechanism, and the 2018 incident reports. It quoted a confidential source within the institute and a former Veridian employee.

It quoted Grant Ashby by name, as an independent pharmacologist, on the compound's neurological mechanism and the probable cause of the three deaths.

It did not yet name Sonia. Luu had agreed to that.

Sonia was in the institute when it published. She had known when it was going to publish. She had been at her desk at eight-fifteen, doing what she always did, when the notification came through on her phone from a news alert she had set.

She set the phone face-down on the desk.

Within two hours the institute's phone lines were active in a way she had not seen before. Webb's assistant was fielding calls. Webb was behind his closed door. Yuen came past her corridor twice with his face set in a way that communicated neither guilt nor innocence but pure institutional calculation.

Agatha came to her door at ten. "It's out," she said.

"Yes."

"How are you?"

Sonia looked at her. "I don't know yet." A pause. "How are you?"

"I filed thirty years ago for people being hurt and I've been carrying it since." She put her hand on the doorframe. "I feel lighter," she said. "I know that sounds wrong."

"It doesn't," Sonia said.

Chapter 42: Webb Closes In

He came to her office at eleven on the same morning the article published. His face was controlled with the same precision he brought to everything, but his hands were not entirely still.

"Your patient access is suspended, effective immediately," he said. "Pending clinical review."

She had been expecting this. "On what grounds?"

"Impaired clinical judgment. Documented anomalous behavioral patterns during sessions." He held out a formal notice. "This is per the institutional patient safety protocol. It's not punitive. It's a precautionary measure."

She looked at the notice. It cited two specific incidents: her "episode" during the Marcus Delgado session, and her "irregular" presence on night rounds. Both incidents that he had been watching. Both incidents she had documented herself.

"I'll review this with legal counsel," she said.

"Of course." He was still using the benevolent voice but something had changed in the precision of it — it was a calculation now, not a warmth. "I'm also recommending you begin the wellness protocol this morning. Given the level of stress you're under—"

"No."

"Sonia—"

"No."

He looked at her for a moment. Then he placed a second document on her desk: a prescription, formal, signed, for "supportive pharmacological intervention under clinical supervision." Below it, a schedule indicating she was to receive the medication through the institute's morning dispensary.

"This is a formal clinical order," he said. "If you decline, it creates a record of your non-compliance with safety protocols."

She looked at the documents. "I understand," she said. "I'll consult with outside counsel."

He left.

She sat at the desk for two minutes. Then she went to the medication room — not the dispensary, the medication room — and found Agatha.

"He's ordered a compulsory wellness protocol for me," she said. "If I don't take it, I'm non-compliant. If I do take it, I'm medicated."

Agatha's expression was flat and decisive. "I'll handle the morning dispensary entry. You won't receive anything you haven't verified."

"Can you do that?"

"I'm the senior ward nurse." She said it simply. "I've been administering medication at this institution for fifteen years. Trust me on this."

Chapter 43: The Medication

She did not take the medication. She sat at the morning dispensary at the standard time, with the other staff members who were on various supportive programs, and she took the paper cup from the dispensary technician and she turned away and emptied it into a tissue in her pocket and disposed of it in the bathroom.

She did this for three mornings.

On the third morning she documented it: dated entry, precise language, noting that she had been ordered to take an unnamed compound under clinical authority, that the compound was labeled "Compound B-2" with no independent pharmacological verification, and that she had declined to consume it while preserving the institutional record of apparent compliance.

She then documented it again in a second form: an affidavit, which she sent to her personal attorney — a friend from her residency who had specialized in medical law — with a note: in case anything happens to me in the next two weeks, make sure this is on record.

She sent the affidavit on a Thursday morning.

On Thursday afternoon, Sandra Luu's second article appeared. This one was longer and included a response from Veridian Cognitive Systems' legal department — a response that denied nothing specific and confirmed, by the precision of its denials, that everything specific was accurate.

Luu texted her: second one's out. How are you holding up?

She texted back: fine. Keep going.

On Friday she had a session with Diana Taggart — she had resumed limited clinical contact with Taggart, whose active crisis had resolved and who was being carefully monitored by Agatha. Taggart was lucid and alert and progressing. The claustrophobia was still present but the catatonic risk had passed.

After the session Taggart looked at her and said, "The article said things were done to patients here without our knowing."

"There's an investigation," Sonia said.

"I think I was one of those patients."

She looked at Taggart. "If you're willing to tell your story to investigators, that will help."

"Am I damaged?" she said. "Neurologically?"

"I don't know. There will be evaluations. Independent ones."

"Okay." She looked at the door. "Thank you for staying," she said. "I know you didn't have to."

Chapter 44: Liam's Return

He came on a Sunday evening. She was at her desk finishing a clinical note. The knock was tentative in the particular way of someone who has done something they need to undo and isn't sure of their reception.

"Come in," she said.

He stood in the doorway. He was thinner than the last time she'd looked at him closely. His eyes were clear in a way they hadn't been for weeks.

"I stopped taking the wellness medication," he said.

"When?"

"After the second article. I read what the compound was described as." He looked at his hands. "I could feel my thinking clearing."

"That's not a small thing to recognize."

"I knew the memo I wrote was wrong. I knew it when I wrote it." He sat down without being invited. She did not object. "I wrote it because I was afraid. And because the one dose of the compound made the fear feel very manageable and the problem feel very far away, and I chose to stay in that feeling instead of acting correctly."

She looked at him.

"I still have my original declaration," she said. "The one you signed."

"I know." He reached into his jacket and put a flash drive on the desk. "The session summaries I filed with Webb directly. Everything I did in the enhancement sessions. I had a backup copy." He looked at her. "I want to add it to what you have."

She picked up the drive. She put it in the folder.

"Sandra Luu is going to want to speak to you," she said. "On record. With your name."

"I know."

"You'll face a professional conduct board. Probably disciplinary proceedings."

"I know." He looked at the desk. "I came into this wanting to do good work. That's true, even if it doesn't mitigate anything." A pause. "I thought Webb was doing something remarkable. He is doing something remarkable. It's also illegal and it's cost three people their lives and I let it happen."

She looked at him for a moment.

"All right," she said.

"That's all? 'All right'?"

"What else do you want me to say?"

He was quiet. Then: "Nothing, I suppose."

"Contact Luu tomorrow," she said. "Tell her you're on record. Tell her you'll cooperate fully with the state investigation."

He stood up. At the door he paused. "Sonia. I'm sorry."

"I know," she said. "Go home, Liam."

Chapter 45: Sandra Luu Publishes

The third article appeared on a Wednesday at six in the morning. Sonia read it in her car before going in, sitting in the parking lot with the heater running.

It was the full account. It named Veridian Cognitive Systems' contract with Harmon. It named the fee schedule and its tiers. It identified the compound's mechanism by name, citing Ashby. It named the three deaths and their dates and the specific protocol circumstances surrounding each. It named Dr. Marcus Webb.

It quoted a former employee of Veridian Cognitive Systems, unnamed, describing the data acquisition program from the inside.

It quoted a current Harmon Institute clinician, named, with a photograph: Dr. Liam Ferris, who stated that he had participated in the protocol and was cooperating fully with the investigation and with regulatory authorities.

It included an independent statement from the New York State Office of Mental Health Oversight, which announced that it was opening a formal investigation into clinical practices at the Harmon Institute, effective immediately.

Carolyn Voss's name appeared in the OMHO statement. Whatever her connection to Webb's advisory panel had meant, she had opened the investigation.

Sonia read the article twice. She put her phone away. She went inside.

The institute was already different. Two people she didn't know were in the reception area with government credentials. Webb's office was closed, the light off. Yuen was in the hall with the look of a man trying to calculate the fastest way out of a building that was not, technically, on fire but smelled like it.

Agatha was at the nursing station. She looked up when Sonia came in.

"They're here," she said.

"I see that."

"Veridian's lawyers called the institute's administration last night, apparently. Trying to get ahead of the documentation request." She paused. "Too late."

"Yes," Sonia said. "Too late."

Chapter 46: Webb's Last Gambit

She heard about it from the state investigator who came to speak to her on Thursday afternoon — a woman named Torres who had the flat, efficient manner of someone who had seen a great many institutional crises and was not surprised by this one.

"Dr. Webb filed a clinical statement yesterday," Torres said, "alleging that the three deaths at the institute were the result of clinical errors in primary care management. He named two primary care clinicians. One is Dr. Ferris." She paused. "The other is you."

Sonia looked at her. "I see."

"He claims that anomalous behavior during sessions — visions, perceptual disturbances — documented in both your self-report and his administrative notes, indicate a compromised clinical state that may have contributed to poor therapeutic judgment with the affected patients."

She thought about this. It was, as a gambit, precise. He was using the evidence of the compound's effects as evidence of her incompetence. Circular. Destructive. And not entirely without the kind of surface plausibility that could cause problems.

"Dr. Ferris has provided a statement," Torres said, "describing the pharmacological mechanism that would produce those symptoms in any clinician in proximity to protocol patients. He notes that both he and Dr. Park were subject to the compound's effects without consent."

"That's accurate."

"I know." Torres made a note. "I'm telling you about Webb's filing so you can prepare. The statement he filed will go into the record. It won't be the only thing in the record."

"No," Sonia said. "It won't."

"There's one more item. The server in the secondary observation room has been secured. The data on it is now in state custody." She looked at Sonia steadily. "There was a file structure indicating that data recorded from your sessions was flagged as 'primary research quality.' Your mirror-state responses were apparently of particular interest."

"How many sessions?"

"Forty-three."

Sonia sat very still.

"You had no knowledge of this," Torres said.

"None."

"That's clear from the documentation. I'm sorry." She closed her notebook. "There will be a full neurological evaluation offered to all identified research subjects. Including you." She paused. "Calista Graves has already filed a formal statement from outside the institute. Her documentation is substantial."

"I know," Sonia said. "She's thorough."

Chapter 47: The Reckoning

Her formal statement took three hours. Torres ran the interview with a second investigator present and a recorder running. Sonia had prepared carefully: she spoke from notes, she stuck to documents, she was precise about what she knew as opposed to what she suspected, and she noted the uncertain column clearly: these are elements I cannot fully verify because my own perception during the relevant period was affected by the compound.

Torres listened. She asked questions. She went back to specific dates. She pressed on the pharmacology — wanted to understand, from a clinical standpoint, what the compound actually did. Sonia explained it in lay terms and then in clinical terms and then they went back to lay terms because that was what the record needed.

She described the visions. She described them without flinching and without drama: duration, content, physiological correlates, documentation procedures she had employed. She noted that the visions had eventually occurred even without recent compound exposure, consistent with Ashby's assessment of persistent neurological activation.

She described the audio recording she had found in the mirror room, with its subsonic TMS pulse timed to her moments of peak empathic engagement.

"You were being used as an instrument," Torres said.

"I was being measured as an instrument, yes."

"Without consent."

"Without consent. Without knowledge."

Torres wrote. The second investigator wrote.

"Were there moments," Torres said, "during your investigation, where you were uncertain whether your conclusions were your own or artifacts of the compound's effects?"

"Yes," Sonia said. "Many moments."

"How did you navigate that?"

"I checked every conclusion against the physical documents. If it was supported by documentation, I acted on it. If it was only supported by my own perception, I held it."

Torres looked up from her notepad. "That's a very careful way to conduct an investigation when your own cognition is a variable."

"It's the only methodologically sound way to do it," Sonia said. "And it was the most frightening part of everything."

Chapter 48: Aftermath

Webb was suspended from practice pending criminal investigation within a week of the third article. The charges were being prepared: unauthorized medical experimentation, falsification of clinical records, failure to report patient deaths. The Veridian Cognitive Systems contract was under federal review. Two Veridian executives had retained separate criminal defense attorneys.

Liam Ferris appeared before the professional conduct board in the second week. He had submitted a full voluntary disclosure. The board hearing was ongoing, the outcome not yet determined. His license was conditionally suspended pending the investigation's completion.

Agatha Rourke was interviewed three times by Torres's team. Her documentation — six years of it, precise and dated — was, as Torres told Sonia in a brief update, the single most useful evidential contribution to the investigation. The 2018 deaths were now being reviewed as potential wrongful deaths.

Calista Graves left the institute three days after the article published and entered an outpatient program closer to her family. She sent Sonia a brief note, handwritten, that said only: the pattern holds. I hope you're doing all right. Sonia kept it in her desk at home.

The institute itself was under external oversight, its clinical programs suspended except for emergency and transition care. The remaining patients were being supported by an oversight-appointed clinical team, with Sonia serving in an advisory capacity.

She went, twice more, to speak with Diana Taggart, who was preparing to transfer to an outpatient program. Taggart was alert, functional, frightened, and angry — the appropriate responses, in Sonia's assessment. She said so. Taggart said, "Good. I want to be appropriate."

She said, "I'm going to tell my story. To whoever needs to hear it."

"Yes," Sonia said. "Please do."

There was a message from Ashby, who had by then read all the documents she'd sent him and given his statement to investigators: you did the right thing, and that's separate from whether it was the safe thing.

She thought about that. She thought about it for several days.

Chapter 49: What Remains

The neurological evaluation took place in a hospital in Albany — not an institution with any affiliation to Harmon or Veridian — in the fourth week after the publication. An independent team: a neurologist, a psychiatrist, an EEG technician. Three hours of testing.

She sat in the clinical environment with the familiar quality of being both clinician and patient simultaneously — a doubling she had always found interesting in the abstract and found, in practice, acutely vertiginous. She told the neurologist this. He wrote it down.

The results came back eight days later.

Normal. Across all parameters. The EEG was normal, the neuropsychological testing was within standard range, the structural imaging was unremarkable.

She read the report and then read it again.

Normal.

She held this for a long time. She thought about what it meant and what it didn't mean.

It did not mean the visions had not happened. They had happened. She had documented them. The documentation was in her notebook, dated and timed and described with clinical precision, and it existed in physical form in a locked folder that she had given to Torres as part of the evidentiary record.

It did not mean the compound had not been in her system. It had been. The pharmacological mechanism was documented.

What it meant was that the effects, as measured at this point in time, by these instruments, were no longer present.

She was not sure if she trusted that.

She recognized this as paranoid ideation secondary to trauma. She wrote it in her journal.

She also recognized that some suspicions, formed correctly in conditions of genuine threat, do not fully dissolve when the threat is resolved. She wrote that too.

She went for a walk in the late afternoon, through a neighborhood she liked, where the streets were wide and the buildings were modest and there were trees that had just started losing their leaves. She walked for an hour and she did not think about the institute. She thought about dinner, and the sound of the leaves, and the specific color of the sky at that hour.

When she got home she sat at her kitchen table where the documents had once been spread in their detailed arrangement. The table was empty. It was just a table.

She thought: I am all right.

She thought: I am not completely certain of that.

She thought: that will have to be enough.

Chapter 50: The Other Side of the Glass

She started the outpatient practice six weeks later. Not with Harmon — never again with Harmon. A small office in a two-room suite above a bookstore on Millhaven's main street, shared with another clinician who specialized in family therapy. The commute was eight minutes. The parking was free.

On the first day she arranged the office carefully. Two chairs, positioned at an angle to each other rather than directly facing, because she had read the evidence on perpendicular positioning and found it supported. A low table. A lamp with warm light. No one-way mirror.

Behind her desk a window, single-paned, that looked out at the back of the building behind them and a stretch of sky.

She reviewed her first patient's file in the morning. A forty-year-old woman referred by her GP for generalized anxiety. No phobia, no specific trauma, a general and pervasive sense that she was always waiting for something to go wrong. A common presentation. Treatable.

The session began at nine.

The woman sat down and looked at her hands. Then she looked up.

"I've never done this before," she said. "I don't quite know what to do."

"Most people don't," Sonia said. "You can start wherever you like."

The woman looked at the window. She looked back. She began to talk.

Sonia listened. She was aware of the room: the light, the temperature, the woman's voice and its particular quality. She was aware of her own hands, resting on her notepad. She was aware of the small, careful attention that gathered in her when she was doing this work properly — not the false acuity of a chemically enhanced mirror-state, but the real thing: presence.

Forty minutes in, the woman said something precise about the feeling of waiting — a description so specific and accurate that Sonia felt the familiar resonance, the old empathic pull that she had spent years learning and years protecting and then spent several months being unable to trust.

She felt it. She noted it. She held it at the proper distance.

She looked at her patient with full attention. She wrote her note.

The session continued.

Behind them, on the wall above her desk, there was a small mirror — a practical object, hung there by the previous occupant, that she had not yet decided to remove. It reflected the room in its modest, simple way: two chairs, two people, an ordinary afternoon in a room with no secrets.

She did not look at it. She was looking at her patient.

On the other side of the glass, there was only the room.
