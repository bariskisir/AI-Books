Chapter 1: A Verse for the Drowned

The fisherman's name was Ormr, and he had drowned in shallow water. His family brought Sigrid that fact and nothing else, because a death in shallow water is a shameful thing and they wanted the verse to look past it. They came to her longhouse in the early dark of an October morning, the father and the eldest son, and sat on the benches she kept for visitors while she put a pot of broth over the fire and listened.

"He was a good man," the father said. His name was Björn and his hands shook.

"Tell me something specific," Sigrid said.

He looked at her. She had heard this hesitation before. Grief made people general. They loved the feeling of the dead more than the facts of them, and so they offered her nothing she could use.

"He mended nets on the beach," the son said. He was perhaps twenty. "He taught me to read the tide by how the seabirds flew."

"Good." Sigrid wrapped her hands around her cup. "What else?"

"He argued with Þóra Magnusdóttir for three years about where the boundary of their drying racks lay. He never won."

"Did he find that funny?"

The son paused. "He did. He called her the better negotiator."

They talked for an hour. By the end Sigrid knew that Ormr had been afraid of horses, that he preferred smoked fish to salt fish, that he had lost two fingers on his left hand from a rope accident and made dark jokes about it ever after. She knew he had once saved a boy from the surf at Kleifarvatn — not from heroism but because he happened to be there and could not watch the child drown. She knew he had been drunk when he went into the shallows that last night, following a voice he thought was his wife's, who had died two winters before.

When they left, Sigrid sat with the fire and composed. She did not write it down — the verse lived in her mouth and her memory, which was where verse belonged. She shaped it around the lost fingers and the drying rack dispute and the seabirds. She kept the shallow water. It was where he had gone, and to take it out would be to lie about him, and Sigrid did not compose lies. She composed the truth in a way that honored rather than diminished.

The funeral was held two days later, in the cold wind off the sea. Sigrid stood among the gathered settlement, the salt in the air, and spoke the verse. She watched the family's faces. The father wept. The son did not, but his jaw was tight with the effort of it. When she finished, the old woman standing beside Björn touched his hand.

Sigrid walked home through the dark thinking about what it took to drown in shallow water. You had to give up. Some part of you had to let the sea have you. She was not certain whether that made it a brave death or a sad one, or whether those were different things.

She reached her longhouse and put more peat on the fire and took out the small tablet of wax she used for notes. She wrote nothing. The verse was done. She sat in the warmth and thought about nothing in particular, which was how she spent most evenings, and found she was content enough.

The wind outside was sharp and northerly. Winter was very close now.

Chapter 2: The Huscarl's Summons

Gunnar the Bald came to her door on a day when the first ice had formed at the edges of the stream. He was a big man, slow-moving, with a face that had been broken and reset badly at some point and now sat a little crooked on his skull. He wore good wool and his sword was old but well-kept. He stood in her doorway and filled it.

"Sigrid Ulfsdóttir."

"That is my name," she said. She had been grinding grain and her hands were dusty with it. "Come in or stay out, but close the door either way."

He came in and did not sit, though she gestured at the bench. Men like Gunnar tended not to sit unless they were at ease, and he was not at ease.

"I come from Ragnar Blackhand."

Sigrid set down the grinding stone. Everyone in the settlement knew Ragnar Blackhand. He had been chieftain for forty years. He had a hall three days' ride to the east and a reputation that preceded him by considerable distance.

"He is ill," she said. This was also known. The word had come through traders three weeks ago: a sword wound to the belly, sustained in a dispute with a man from the eastern valleys. The wound had been treated but not healed well. At seventy-one, a belly wound was serious.

"He is dying," Gunnar said. There was no particular emotion in his voice, which told her that the emotion was there but had been packed down somewhere deep. "He has perhaps two weeks. Perhaps less."

"And he wants a skald."

"He wants you, specifically."

Sigrid looked at him. "There are better poets in Iceland."

"He did not ask for better. He asked for honest." Gunnar met her gaze steadily. "His exact words were: find me the skald who told the truth about Ormr the fisherman."

Sigrid was quiet for a moment. She crossed to the fire and added a small piece of wood to it. The ice was coming; she needed to be careful with her fuel stock.

"What does he offer?"

"Thirty ells of good cloth, a silver arm-ring, and passage back to Reykjavik on his horses when the work is done."

It was generous. More than she would have asked for. The generosity made her cautious.

"Who else will be at the hall?"

"His second wife, Astrid. His eldest son Eirik. A physician. Servants. A few of his huscarls."

"A household full of people who want the story told a certain way."

Gunnar's jaw moved. "You could say that."

"I am saying it." She turned to look at him. "Ragnar Blackhand has been chieftain for forty years. Whatever I compose, it will become part of the record. The family will know that."

"Yes."

"So the pressure to compose a flattering account will be considerable."

"It will," Gunnar said. "I will not tell you otherwise."

She considered him. He had not lied to her yet. That was something.

"I will need three days to put my household in order," she said. "And I want the thirty ells paid in advance."

Gunnar nodded.

"Tell Ragnar I will come."

Chapter 3: The Road East

The ride to Blackhand Farm took two and a half days on the horse Gunnar provided — a solid grey animal with good feet and no particular opinion about weather. Sigrid rode with Gunnar and two other huscarls who spoke little and kept to themselves. The landscape opened into lava fields and then contracted again into valley, frost on every stone.

On the first night they sheltered at a farmhouse whose owner was a man named Þorleifr, fat and cheerful, who fed them smoked lamb and did not ask why they were traveling in October. Gunnar drank two cups of ale and then stopped, which told Sigrid something about his discipline. The other two huscarls played a dice game in the corner. Sigrid sat near the fire and watched the embers.

On the second morning the cold sharpened. The horses moved carefully across ground that had frozen hard overnight. Sigrid wore two layers of wool and kept her hands inside her cloak. The landscape was extraordinary in the way Iceland always was in October — bleak and enormous and lit by a low sun that turned everything amber and orange and the color of old blood.

"You've worked for chieftains before," Gunnar said, riding beside her.

"I composed the memorial for Björn of Borgarfjörðr," she said. "And the account of the Althing dispute seven years ago."

"Not the same as composing for a man who is still alive to hear it."

"No."

He did not say anything else for a while. Then: "Ragnar is not easy to be with. He was never easy. The dying has made him more so in some ways and less in others."

"In what way more?"

"He is not pretending anymore." Gunnar looked at the road ahead. "For forty years he managed what people thought of him. Now he cannot be bothered. He says what he means. Some people find that alarming."

"I find it easier," Sigrid said.

They passed through a narrow valley where a frozen stream ran alongside the track. The sound of ice over stone was faint and high.

On the second night they stayed in a shepherd's hut, cold and drafty, and Sigrid slept poorly. She lay awake listening to the wind and thinking about what it meant to compose for a living man. The dead could not object. The living could. The dying were somewhere between — they could object but would soon lose the ability to do anything about it.

The question was whether Ragnar's version of his own life was worth preserving.

Every man believed his own story. That was how they continued. Ragnar had constructed a version of himself over forty years, and now he wanted her to set it in verse so it would survive him. The question she would need to answer was how much of the structure he had built was true, and how much was the kind of editing that every life involved — the convenient forgettings, the emphases placed where they could do the most good.

She composed nothing that night. She only lay in the cold and thought.

In the morning the farm appeared at the end of a long track through brown grass — a substantial hall, stone and timber, with outbuildings and a smokehouse and a well. The settlement's chieftain had done well for himself. The farm was large and prosperous, and you could see in it forty years of accumulation. Every stone placed deliberately. Every wall solid.

Gunnar rode ahead and she followed.

Chapter 4: The Dying Man

Ragnar Blackhand was sitting up when they brought Sigrid to him. She had expected a ruin. She found something closer to a fire that has burned down to coals — reduced, but still hot.

He was in a room off the main hall, in a bed of good timber with wool hangings. A lamp burned on the table beside him. The bandaging around his midsection was visible at the hem of his shirt. His face was the color of old oak, deeply lined, and his eyes were pale grey and sharp.

His left hand, resting on the blanket, was indeed black — not from dye but from old burning, the skin contracted and smooth, the fingers permanently curled. He did not hide it.

"Sigrid Ulfsdóttir," he said.

"Ragnar Blackhand," she said.

"Sit." He gestured at a stool near the bed. She sat. The room smelled of herbs and sickness and the underlying smell of a man who had been wealthy for a very long time.

He studied her. She let him.

"You're younger than I expected," he said.

"You're older," she said.

He made a sound that might have been a laugh. "I heard you told the truth about Ormr the fisherman. That he drowned in shallow water drunk."

"Yes."

"His family permitted that?"

"They did not have veto. They hired me to compose his memorial. I composed it."

Ragnar considered this. "Most poets would have found a way to frame it more heroically."

"Most poets are cowards."

He looked at her steadily. "And you are not?"

"I have not decided yet," she said. "It depends on what you want composed."

He shifted against his pillows, and she saw the pain cross his face and disappear, the practiced suppression of a man who had been managing pain for some time. "I want my memorial saga. The account of my years as chieftain. The battles, the decisions, the settlement — what I built and how I built it."

"That is a large request."

"It is why I'm offering a silver arm-ring."

Sigrid folded her hands in her lap. "Tell me the first thing you did when you came to Iceland."

He told her. She listened. She did not write anything down. She would begin by filling her memory with the material, and then she would start to arrange it. The arrangement was where the truth or the lie lived. Any skald knew that.

He talked for an hour, until the physician came and asked her to leave. As she rose, Ragnar said: "Come back tomorrow."

"I will," she said.

He watched her go with those pale grey eyes, and she had the feeling — that she would have again over the following days — that he was measuring her, trying to determine exactly what kind of person she was and whether she could be managed.

She walked back into the main hall and stood by the fire and thought that she was doing the same to him.

Chapter 5: Astrid's Warning

Astrid Thorvaldsdóttir met Sigrid in the yard the following morning, as Sigrid was walking back from the latrine in the cold. Astrid was already dressed and composed, her hair bound in a practical knot, her cloak good quality but not showy. She was a handsome woman, wide across the shoulders, with the look of someone who had been making careful decisions for a very long time.

"You slept well?" she said.

"Well enough."

"Good." Astrid fell in beside her, walking at the same pace, as if they were simply taking the morning air together. "I want to talk to you about why you are here."

"I know why I am here," Sigrid said.

"You know part of it." Astrid glanced toward the hall. "Ragnar asked for you. But Ragnar is dying, and when he is gone, this household will be managed by Eirik. What Eirik wants is a specific kind of memorial — one that establishes the Ragnarsson inheritance as unimpeachable. The eldest son of a great chieftain who built this settlement from nothing."

"That is not an unusual request."

"No. But there are things in Ragnar's history that do not fit that story." Astrid paused at the hall door. "I am telling you this not to warn you against honesty. I am telling you so that you understand what you are walking into."

Sigrid looked at her. "What things?"

Astrid met her gaze briefly, then looked away. "Talk to Gunnar. Talk to the old woman if she comes. Do not accept Eirik's money." She pushed the hall door open and stepped inside, leaving the last word hanging in the cold air behind her.

Sigrid stood in the yard for a moment. The sky was pale grey, the sun not yet clear of the ridge. Somewhere in the outbuildings a cow was lowing. An ordinary morning, ordinary sounds.

She went inside.

The hall was substantial — long and low, with a central fire and benches along both walls. Shields hung at the far end. Good weapons on the rack by the door. The floor had been swept clean. Servants moved at the far end, preparing the morning meal.

Leif Skjaldmær was sitting at one of the benches, working with a small stylus on a wax tablet. He did not look up when she entered. He was a lean young man with a pointed beard that was beginning to look pretentious, though he was not yet old enough to carry it with confidence. His hands were the hands of a craftsman — careful, economical.

He looked up after a moment, saw her, and smiled. "The famous Sigrid Ulfsdóttir."

"I don't know that I'm famous," she said.

"You composed the Ormr verse." He set down the stylus. "I heard it recited three months ago by a trader. It was excellent."

"Thank you." She sat down at the bench across from him and took the bread a servant offered. "You're composing something."

"Preliminary notes. Lord Eirik has asked me to begin work."

"On the memorial saga."

"Yes."

She looked at him steadily. He did not look away, which was interesting. Most people looked away when you caught them doing something they knew to be at least partially wrong.

"There will be two versions, then," she said.

"Or one," he said. "Depending on how things develop." He picked up the tablet again. "I mean no offense, Sigrid. I was hired before you arrived. My commission predates yours."

"I know that," she said. "I'm not offended."

She ate her bread and thought about what Astrid had said. Do not accept Eirik's money. As if she had been planning to.

Chapter 6: Eirik's Offer

Eirik found her that same afternoon, in the passage between the hall and the storehouse. He managed to arrange it so that they were alone, which told her he had been watching for the moment. He was a compact man, well-fed but not soft, with his father's grey eyes and a mouth that was permanently set in the expression of someone who has already decided what is going to happen.

"Sigrid." He spoke without preamble. "I wanted to speak with you before you became too deep into your work."

"I'm listening," she said.

"My father hired you for an honest account of his life. I understand that impulse. He is dying, and dying men sometimes want to make confessions." He said the last word with careful precision, giving it just enough weight to be meaningful. "But a chieftain's memorial is not a private document. It will be recited at the thing. It will be the account by which this settlement remembers his years of leadership, and by which the Althing judges the legitimacy of my inheritance."

"I understand what a memorial saga is," Sigrid said.

"Then you understand why accuracy is important. Not just factual accuracy, but the accuracy of emphasis. Of proportion." He looked at her with those grey eyes. "Certain events in my father's past were — complicated. The context is not simple. A skald who did not understand the full context might characterize them in ways that are technically accurate but functionally misleading."

"Give me an example," she said.

His expression shifted slightly. He had not expected her to ask that. "Land acquisitions," he said after a moment. "There were disputes, forty years ago, about certain properties. The disputes were settled. But without context, they could look —"

"Unlawful?"

A pause. "Contentious," he said.

Sigrid nodded slowly. "And what is it you're offering me, in exchange for this contextual accuracy?"

He reached into his cloak and produced a small cloth bag. He set it on the shelf between them. It clinked.

"Silver," she said.

"Enough to keep your household through two winters," he said. "It is a fair price."

She looked at the bag. She looked at him. "No," she said.

He did not move. "You haven't heard the full —"

"I've heard enough." She stepped past him. "I was hired by Ragnar. I work for Ragnar. When Ragnar is dead, the memorial will be composed and I will return to Reykjavik. If what I compose displeases you, that is your concern, not mine."

She walked away. She heard him pick up the bag behind her. He said nothing more.

That evening she sat by the central fire and felt the weight of what she had done settle into her body. Not regret. Something like clarity, which was usually the first step toward fear.

Chapter 7: The Young Poet's Measure

Leif found her at the fire and sat beside her without being invited, which was the kind of thing a young man with confidence did. He poured ale from the jug on the table and offered her a cup. She took it.

"Eirik spoke to you," he said.

"How do you know?"

"I know his timing. He would have approached you this afternoon, after you'd settled in enough to be considered a serious threat." He drank. "What did he offer?"

"Silver," she said. "Enough to keep my household through two winters."

Leif nodded. "He offered me the commission itself. All the cloth and the arm-ring, and a further sum besides, if my saga was the one performed at the thing." He said it without apparent shame.

"And you accepted."

"I accepted the commission. I haven't decided what kind of composition to deliver." He turned the cup in his hands. "I'm twenty-nine, Sigrid. I need the money more than I need to be remembered for composing a flattering lie. But I also need to be remembered as the man who composed the great memorial of Ragnar Blackhand, if it is in fact great. A hollow memorial won't serve that purpose."

She looked at him sideways. "You're more complicated than you look."

"Most people are." He stretched his legs toward the fire. "What do you know about Ragnar so far?"

"That he has managed his reputation carefully for forty years."

"And now he wants an honest account."

"He says he does." She set down her cup. "Dying men often want honesty in the abstract. Whether they still want it when they hear what the honest account contains — that is the test."

"You've done this before."

"Not with a man still breathing." She watched the fire for a moment. "What do you know about him? You've been here longer than I have."

Leif was quiet. He was thinking, which she found encouraging. Young poets who thought before speaking were rarer than they should be.

"There's a woman in the settlement," he said finally. "A völva, old, called Halla Ironteeth. People say she knew Ragnar's first wife. People avoid saying what they know about how the first wife died."

Sigrid went still.

"That's all I know," Leif said. "I haven't pursued it because Eirik told me not to mention the first wife." He picked up his cup. "Make of that what you will."

She made something of it. She sat with the fire and the information and composed nothing, but thought a great deal.

Chapter 8: The Farm's History

Gunnar took her around the farm the next morning. He did it properly — the granary, the smithy, the stables, the longboat pulled up on dry ground for winter — and he told her the stories that went with each building. He had a good memory and a methodical way of speaking. He had been with Ragnar since he was twenty-two years old.

"You've seen everything," she said, when they stood outside the stables in the pale morning.

"Most things," he said.

"The expansion of the farm — the land acquisitions. How did that happen?"

Gunnar's jaw moved. He had a habit of doing this when he was choosing words carefully. "Ragnar was skilled at negotiating. He knew when to press an advantage and when to offer fair terms."

"That sounds like something you rehearsed."

He looked at her. "It's not untrue."

"But it's not the whole of it, either."

He looked away, toward the lava field at the edge of the cleared ground. "There were disputes. As there always are. Land disputes. Boundary disputes. Ragnar resolved them in his favor. That is the nature of power."

"What happened to the people who lost those disputes?"

"Some left. Some stayed and found arrangement with Ragnar. Some —" He stopped.

"Some?" she said.

"Some died," he said. He said it flatly, without inflection. "This is Iceland. People die. The winters are hard. Accidents happen."

"Did they happen to Ragnar's opponents with particular frequency?"

Gunnar looked at her steadily. "You are going to ask these questions no matter what I say."

"Yes."

He nodded, once. "Then I will tell you that the farm came to be this size through forty years of Ragnar's will and intelligence, and that not all of it was clean, and that if you want the details you will need to find them somewhere I am not the source." He turned back toward the hall. "Come inside. It's too cold for this."

She followed him, thinking that he had told her more than he intended, and that he knew it, and that he had done it deliberately.

Chapter 9: The Gaps

She spent two days working through the material she had gathered. She did not compose verse yet — she was still in the phase of accumulation, of filling herself with the known shape of the life before she tried to render it in words.

Ragnar's story had a clear architecture. He had come from Norway at twenty-nine with a ship, a small crew, and enough silver to establish a claim. He had settled near the coast, then moved inland and east as the holding grew. He had fought in two raids as a younger man, returned with spoils. He had served as a mediator at several thing-meetings, earning the settlement's trust. He had been made chieftain by consensus when the previous chieftain died.

All of this was clean and legible.

What was not legible was the space between the events. The gaps. How he had acquired the eastern fields when their previous owner had died without heirs — or had there been heirs? How the boundary with the Eiríksson family to the north had been settled in Ragnar's favor when the arbitration had initially gone against him. How a neighboring farmstead, burned in Ragnar's third year on the island, had never been rebuilt, and its land had eventually become Ragnar's grazing.

Sigrid wrote these gaps on a wax tablet in shorthand only she could read. Not accusations. Observations. Things that required explanation.

She went back to Ragnar.

He was stronger that day, sitting up, taking broth. The physician Thorkel — a slight man from Dublin, of mixed Irish and Norse blood — was changing his bandages, and Sigrid waited outside the door until he emerged.

"He is stable," Thorkel said, meaning not improving.

Sigrid went in.

"You look like you have questions," Ragnar said.

"I do." She sat on the stool. "Tell me about the Bergjónsson farm. The one that burned."

His eyes showed something. Not guilt — not quite. Recognition. "That was forty years ago."

"Forty-two," she said. "Your third winter here. What happened?"

He looked at the ceiling for a moment. "There was a fire. These things happen. Thatch is dry in autumn. A fire got into it."

"Was anyone in the building when it burned?"

A pause. "Ósvald Bergjónsson was away. His family survived."

"They left Iceland the following spring."

"Many people left that first decade. Iceland was hard."

"They left," Sigrid said, "and their land became yours."

"Their land was unoccupied. It needed management. Someone had to take responsibility."

She watched him. He held her gaze without flinching, which was either the evidence of innocence or the practiced steadiness of a man who had been holding this particular gaze for forty years.

"Did you set the fire?" she said.

Ragnar was quiet for a long moment. The lamp burned between them.

"That is a very direct question," he said.

"Yes," she said. "It is."

---

Chapter 10: The Völva

Halla Ironteeth arrived at the farm on a horse that was slightly too old for the journey, leading a pack animal. She came without announcement, at midday, while the household was at its meal. She was a large woman, broad across the back, with white hair bound in a complex arrangement and eyes so dark they seemed to have no pupils in certain lights. Her teeth were her own remarkable feature — they had been filed to points at some point in her past and were an unsettling thing to encounter when she smiled. She did not smile often.

She had lived, people said, in the eastern valleys for thirty years. She had been there when the first settlers arrived. She did not attend things. She did not participate in the governance of the settlement. But she knew things, and the things she knew had a way of being accurate.

Sigrid was in the yard when Halla rode in. She watched the household's reaction. The servants moved carefully, without looking at the völva directly. Gunnar came out of the smithy and stopped, then went back in without speaking. Eirik, who had been standing at the hall door, stepped inside and pulled it closed behind him.

Only Astrid came out to meet her. The two women exchanged brief words in voices too low to carry.

Sigrid approached.

Halla turned to look at her. The dark eyes assessed her with visible efficiency. "You are the skald," she said. It was not a question.

"Sigrid Ulfsdóttir."

"I know your name." Halla dismounted with more ease than her age suggested. She handed the reins to the groom who had appeared, still without looking at him. "I came when I heard Ragnar was dying. I have something to say to him before he goes."

"He may not receive you," Sigrid said.

"He will," Halla said. "He owes me that."

Astrid put her hand briefly on the older woman's arm and led her inside. Sigrid followed, keeping enough distance to be unobtrusive.

At the door of Ragnar's room, Astrid knocked. She went in. Sigrid heard Ragnar's voice, low and even, and then a longer silence. When Astrid came back out, her face was composed into neutrality.

"He will see her," she said. She looked at Sigrid. "Not you. Not yet."

The door closed. Sigrid stood in the passage and heard nothing through the thick wood.

She went back to the main hall and sat at the bench and thought about what it meant that Ragnar would receive a woman he clearly feared, without the protection of witnesses.

---

Chapter 11: Halla's Hints

Halla found Sigrid after dinner, when the hall had emptied of most of its inhabitants. She pulled a bench near the fire and sat with the directness of a person who has never learned to approach anything indirectly.

"He told me about you," Halla said.

"What did he say?"

"That you were dangerous." She turned her hands toward the fire. They were large hands, work-worn, with amber rings on two fingers. "He means it as a compliment, in his way. Ragnar has always respected things that pose a threat to him. He thought it made him perceptive."

"Did it?"

"Sometimes." She looked at the fire. "He was perceptive enough to know when to keep Ragna quiet. When to move quickly."

Sigrid kept her voice neutral. "Ragna was his first wife."

"Yes. A Greenland woman. She came with him from Norway. She was not well-suited to Iceland — she found it too cold, too empty. But she was well-suited to Ragnar in the early years, because she was also hard and practical." Halla paused. "She changed her mind about him around his fifteenth year on the island."

"Why?"

"She began to understand what he had done to get the land." Halla looked at Sigrid directly. "Not just the Bergjónsson fire. The other things. The man who drowned in the river when there was no flood. The woman who fell from a cliff while gathering eggs."

Sigrid was very still. "How do you know this?"

"Ragna told me. We were close." Halla's jaw was steady. "She told me in the spring of Ragnar's seventeenth year. And that autumn she drowned in the fjord."

"An accident," Sigrid said carefully.

"That is what was said." Halla's filed teeth showed briefly. "Ragnar was with her when it happened. There were no other witnesses. He came back alone and reported she had slipped."

The fire shifted. A piece of peat collapsed and the light changed.

"Can you prove it?" Sigrid said.

"No," Halla said. "I cannot. I have only what Ragna told me, and Ragna is dead." She looked at the fire. "What I can do is speak at the thing if I am permitted. Say what she told me. Let the settlement weigh it."

"Eirik will not permit it."

"Eirik does not run the thing," Halla said. "Ragnar still does. For a few more days."

Sigrid thought about this. She thought about Ragna, a Greenland woman in Iceland, who had understood too much and said it to the wrong person.

"Why did she tell you?" she said. "If she was afraid?"

Halla was quiet for a moment. "Because she needed someone to know. Because silence felt worse than the risk." She looked at Sigrid. "You understand that."

Sigrid said nothing. She thought she probably did.

Chapter 12: The Direct Question

She went back to Ragnar the following morning.

He looked worse. The night had taken something from him — a small but visible reduction, the way a fire dims when the fuel is burning low. He was awake and his eyes were still sharp, but his color had worsened and his voice, when he spoke, required more effort.

"You spoke to Halla," he said.

"I did."

"She told you about Ragna."

"She told me her version," Sigrid said. She sat on the stool. "I want yours."

He looked at the ceiling. The lamp burned low — she reached past him and trimmed it, the small practical act that you learn when you spend time with the sick.

"Ragna and I argued," he said. "She had become — she had grown away from me, over the years. Iceland changed her, or perhaps Iceland showed her something about me that she hadn't seen in Norway. We argued at the fjord. She lost her footing. She fell."

"You did not try to save her."

A long pause. "The water was cold. The current was fast."

"You were younger then. A strong man."

"Yes," he said. He closed his eyes. "I did not try hard enough."

Sigrid listened to the quality of the silence after that. It was not the silence of a man confessing. It was the silence of a man who had found the exact form of words that would satisfy the question without answering it.

"Did you push her?" she said.

His eyes stayed closed. "No," he said.

"Did you help her fall?"

He was quiet for a very long time.

"I did not prevent her from falling," he said.

Sigrid sat with that. The fire in the small room was low, giving heat without much light. Outside she could hear wind against the timbers of the hall.

"That is not the same as innocence," she said.

"No," he said. "It is not."

He looked at her then. His pale grey eyes had the same quality she had noticed from the first day — measuring, trying to determine what kind of person she was and whether she could be managed. But there was something else in them now. Something that might have been relief.

"I told you the truth," he said. "That is a thing I have not done in a long time."

Sigrid stood. "I will come back tomorrow."

"Yes," he said. "You will."

---

Chapter 13: Eirik's Pressure

Eirik stopped her on the path between the hall and the well. He was with two of his men — not as a threat, exactly, but as a statement about who was present.

"You're spending a great deal of time with the völva," he said.

"She has information relevant to my commission," Sigrid said.

"Her information is unreliable. She is very old and has a history of grievance against my father's family."

"Based on what?"

He met her gaze without hesitation. "Based on the fact that she was Ragna's friend and blames my father for her death. Which was an accident."

"Your father told me it was not entirely an accident," Sigrid said.

Eirik's face went very still. "What do you mean?"

"I mean that the details of what happened at the fjord are complicated." She watched him. He was processing the information rapidly, she could see it behind his eyes — what she knew, what she might say, what damage it could do.

"The inheritance," he said, after a moment.

"Eirik —"

"The inheritance depends on my father being remembered as what he was: a great chieftain who built this settlement. Not as —" He stopped. "The thing will hear the memorial. The neighboring chieftains will attend. If there is a shadow over his name, the legitimacy of everything I inherit is questioned."

"Legitimacy of property built on murder might reasonably be questioned," Sigrid said.

"Do not say that word." His voice was very quiet now. "Do not say that word in this hall, or anywhere that anyone can hear you."

She looked at him. He was frightened — she could see it clearly, beneath the controlled surface. Not for his father, or not primarily. For himself.

"I was paid to compose an honest memorial," she said. "That is what I will compose."

"My father is dying," Eirik said. "Whatever he told you, whatever the völva told you — he is not thinking clearly. He is not the same man. What he built is real. What he created is real. This settlement exists because of him."

"Yes," she said. "And I will say so, in the memorial. The settlement is real. His accomplishments are real." She held his gaze. "So are his crimes."

She walked past him. He did not follow.

---

Chapter 14: Astrid's Suspicion

Astrid came to her room that evening. She knocked first, which was a courtesy that Sigrid noted.

She sat on the small stool by the window and looked out at the darkening sky. Sigrid sat on the bed, and they were arranged like two women having a practical conversation, which was what this was.

"Eirik told me you refused the money," Astrid said.

"Yes."

"He is afraid now. He will do something."

"I know." Sigrid looked at her hands. "What will you do, after?"

Astrid's expression did not change. "I have made arrangements. There is a man in the western settlement — Björgvin Sigurðarson. He is a widower with land and need of a practical wife. We have come to an understanding."

"You are already planning beyond Ragnar's death."

"I have been planning beyond Ragnar's death for five years," Astrid said. She said it without apology. "I married him because he offered protection and security. I have no more illusions about what that means." She looked at Sigrid. "I never believed Ragna's death was an accident. I want to tell you that plainly."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"To whom?" Astrid's voice was level. "To the thing? With what proof? I was not yet Ragnar's wife when it happened. I came to this farm three years afterward, and by then the story was settled." She shifted on the stool. "What I want from you — not as a payment, not as an exchange — is that when you compose the memorial, you keep my name separate from his crimes. I have my own account to maintain."

Sigrid thought about this. "Your name is not connected to them, as far as I know."

"Good." Astrid stood. "Then keep it that way."

She went to the door and paused. "Halla told me something the day she arrived. She said Ragna used to say that the worst thing about Ragnar was not what he did. It was that he could be charming. That he could be kind. That you could almost believe in him, most of the time." She looked back at Sigrid. "I found that to be accurate."

She left. Sigrid sat in the small room and heard the wind against the shutters and thought about the particular horror of men who are not entirely monstrous — who build real things and destroy real people and who, in their dying hour, tell you the truth as if it costs them nothing, or everything, depending on how you look at it.

---

Chapter 15: Gunnar Slips

Three nights into her stay, Gunnar got drunk.

Not badly drunk — he was too disciplined a man for that. But Sigrid found him at the far end of the hall after the evening meal, working through his second jug of ale with the methodical determination of a man trying to dull something.

She sat across from him and did not speak. He looked up at her.

"You're going to ask me questions," he said.

"Eventually."

He drank. Outside, sleet had started — she could hear it on the roof, the small ticking sound of ice on thatch.

"I've served him thirty years," Gunnar said. "Thirty years and I would do it again." He set down the cup. "Does that surprise you?"

"No," she said. "Men serve men like Ragnar for all kinds of reasons."

"He was good to me," Gunnar said. "When I was young and had nothing, he gave me work. Good work. He trusted me. He trusted me more than anyone else in the settlement." A pause. "There is a weight to that trust. When a man trusts you completely and you know what he has done — there is a weight to it."

"What did he do?" she said.

Gunnar looked at his cup for a long moment. "The Bergjónsson fire," he said. "You've asked about that. Forty-two years ago. Ósvald and his family were away at the thing. The fire —" He stopped.

"He set it himself?"

"He did not set it himself." Gunnar's voice was slow, careful. "He gave instruction. He gave instruction to a man named Ketil, who is long dead. And Ketil set the fire, and the house burned, and Ósvald came home to nothing."

"And then Ósvald left."

"He didn't want to leave. He had nowhere to go. Ragnar —" Gunnar stopped again. He was drunk enough that the words were coming before the editing. She could see him fighting it. "Ragnar made it clear that leaving was the better choice. That staying would be difficult."

"He threatened him."

"He made things uncomfortable," Gunnar said. "He is — he was — very good at making things uncomfortable. At making the path of resistance much harder than the path of compliance." He looked at her with eyes that were a little red at the edges. "Ósvald had a son. A young one — ten or twelve. They took the ship south and west and I don't know what became of them."

Sigrid filed this. "Why did Ragnar want the Bergjónsson land?"

"It was good farmland. The best drainage in the valley. And Ósvald had resisted an offer." Gunnar poured more ale. "He had refused a fair price, in Ragnar's view. And Ragnar was not accustomed to being refused."

She let the silence sit. He drank.

"Ragnar told me once," Gunnar said, "that every great thing requires the willingness to do what small men cannot stomach." He looked at her. "He meant it sincerely. He believed it. He believed that what he was doing — taking the land, building the farm, building the settlement — was worth the cost. That the cost was paid by people who were too small to understand the project."

"And you?" she said. "Did you believe it?"

Gunnar was quiet for a long time. "I believed in him," he said finally. "That is not the same thing, but it was enough, for thirty years."

---

Chapter 16: The Boundary Records

The farm kept records — not formal documents, but a series of notched sticks and knotted cords that Ragnar's steward, a meticulous man named Þorvald, maintained in a chest in the storage room. These recorded the boundaries of the various properties, the dates of acquisition, the terms of exchange where there had been exchange.

Sigrid asked to see them. Þorvald hesitated, looked at her with assessment, and then fetched the chest.

She spent a morning working through the records with the help of Þorvald's explanations. He was not part of whatever story was being concealed — he was too young, and too earnest about the methodology of record-keeping to be engaged in active concealment. He told her what the notches meant, which land parcels they referred to, what dates of transfer were marked.

The pattern was clear once she knew what to look for.

The Bergjónsson land — the large eastern field — had been transferred in Ragnar's third year. No price recorded.

The northern field — property of a man named Svanr — transferred in Ragnar's seventh year, after Svanr had died in the river flood. No heirs listed. No competing claim.

The pasture land to the south — originally belonging to a family called the Helgasons — transferred in Ragnar's twelfth year after an arbitration at the local thing. But Sigrid found a notation she could not fully interpret, and when she pressed Þorvald on it, he said only that it had been "a disputed arbitration" and went quiet.

Three transfers. Three acquisitions that did not have the clean quality of honest transactions.

There were other transfers too, later ones, that looked entirely ordinary. Purchases, inheritances, straightforward trades. Ragnar had, over time, become respectable enough that he no longer needed the other methods. The foundation, though, had been built in a particular way.

She rolled the cords up and thanked Þorvald and walked out into the cold air.

She stood in the yard and looked at the farm — the good stone walls, the substantial hall, the prosperous outbuildings — and tried to hold simultaneously the reality of what had been built and the reality of how it had been built. Both were true. That was the difficulty. The monument and the crime were made of the same materials.

This was what she would have to render in verse.

---

Chapter 17: Leif's Honesty

Leif found her in the stable, where she had gone to be away from the hall and its various people wanting things from her. She was sitting on a hay bale in the dim warmth, her wax tablet on her knee, composing nothing but thinking hard.

He came in and saw her and sat on the next bale over without apologizing for the intrusion. This was the thing about Leif that she found both irritating and interesting: he treated his own presence as a reasonable default.

"What are you composing?" he said.

"Nothing yet. Thinking."

"About what you've found."

She looked at him. "What do you know about what I've found?"

"I know that you've been to the boundary records, that you've spoken with Halla at length, and that Eirik is frightened." He picked up a piece of straw and turned it in his fingers. "I also know that Gunnar told you something the other night. He came to breakfast looking the way men look after they've said more than they intended."

She studied him. He was more perceptive than she'd initially credited.

"You've been watching," she said.

"I'm a poet," he said. "It's what we do."

"What are you composing?"

He was quiet for a moment. Then: "A eulogy for a man I've never met, based on stories his son told me. It is technically correct. It has good meter. It lacks any quality that would make it memorable." He set down the straw. "I find that I do not want my name on it."

"Then don't put your name on it."

"Eirik has paid me. That creates an obligation."

She looked at him. "No," she said. "It creates a debt. An obligation is a different thing."

He thought about this. "What's the difference?"

"A debt is paid with the thing agreed on. An obligation is paid with the thing actually owed." She turned back to her tablet. "You can give Eirik a eulogy and fulfill your debt. What you owe the craft is different."

He was quiet for a while. She wrote a few notes, shorthand observations about structure.

"If you compose the true account," he said finally, "Eirik will not simply be angry. He has men. He has resources."

"I know."

"Are you afraid?"

She thought about it honestly. "Yes," she said. "But not more afraid than I am of composing a lie." She looked up. "That is the terrible arithmetic of this work. The fear of the lie has to be larger than the fear of the consequence."

He nodded slowly. "Is it?"

"Ask me again when I'm finished," she said.

---

Chapter 18: Ragnar's Version

Ragnar was stronger the next day — not healing, but in one of the temporary surges that the dying sometimes experienced, as if the body were making a last effort at itself. He sat up properly and ate real food and spoke for two hours without stopping.

He told her his version of the settlement's founding. It was magnificent, in its way. He had a great memory and the storytelling habit of a man who had spent his life in halls where story was the primary entertainment. He described the crossing from Norway with vivid detail — the storm off the Faroes, the first sight of Iceland's coast, the volcanic smoke rising from the interior. He described the first winter, when three of his original crew had died of cold and one from a wound that went bad. He described the decisions he had made in those early years with what seemed like genuine clarity about his own logic.

"The land was empty," he said. "Much of it. A man could take what he could defend and manage. That was the law of the place."

"Some of it was not empty," Sigrid said.

"Some of it was disputed," he said. "I resolved disputes. That is what leaders do."

"How did you resolve the Bergjónsson dispute?"

He looked at her steadily. "I made Ósvald an offer."

"The boundary records show no payment."

"He refused the offer."

"And then his house burned."

Ragnar held her gaze. "Iceland in October. Thatch and fire. These things happen."

She did not argue with him. She was not here to prosecute. She was here to understand, and what she was understanding was the nature of his certainty — that he had told himself this story so many times, in so many forms, that the edited version had become more real to him than what had actually occurred.

"Tell me about Ragna," she said.

His face changed. Not much. But something moved in it that was different from the chieftain's control.

"She was a good woman," he said. "She was not suited to Iceland."

"She was afraid of you, toward the end."

He looked away. "She misunderstood certain things."

"What things?"

"What it costs to build something." His voice was lower now. "She had a — a softness. She did not understand that the building of a settlement requires decisions that are not pleasant. She could not separate the decisions from the man."

"Can they be separated?"

He looked back at her. His grey eyes had the quality of old ice — cold not from cruelty but from depth. "I don't know," he said. "I have been trying to answer that question for forty years."

Sigrid wrote nothing. She sat with the question, which she thought might be the most honest thing he had said to her.

---

Chapter 19: Halla's Danger

On her eighth day at the farm, Eirik's men found Halla performing a seiðr rite in the field below the hall.

She had built a small platform of poles and sat on it above the ground, wrapped in her dark cloak, with a staff across her knees and her eyes closed, chanting something low and rhythmic. The snow had fallen overnight and the field was white and the platform stood above it like a dark interruption. Three farmhands had gathered to watch at a respectful distance.

Eirik's two huscarls walked through the field and one of them, a young man with a red beard named Bárðr, reached up and took hold of Halla's staff.

She opened her eyes.

Sigrid was at the yard fence when she saw this. She stayed where she was.

Gunnar appeared from the direction of the stable. He walked across the field without hurrying and stood between Eirik's men and the platform. He said something she could not hear. Bárðr let go of the staff and took a step back, but he did not leave. The three of them stood for a moment in the white field while Halla sat above them with her dark eyes open and her face entirely still.

Then Gunnar turned and walked back toward the hall, and the two huscarls followed him, and Halla resumed the low chanting.

Sigrid found Gunnar in the passage.

"What did you say to them?" she asked.

"That interfering with a völva on ritual ground is bad fortune," Gunnar said. "Bárðr believes in that. It was the right argument for the man."

"Eirik sent them."

"Yes." Gunnar looked at the hall door. "He is afraid of what Halla knows and what she might say. He is trying to make her feel unwelcome." He looked at Sigrid. "The same goes for you. You should know that."

"I know," she said. "What will you do if he moves more directly?"

Gunnar was quiet for a moment. "My loyalty has always been to Ragnar," he said. "Ragnar gave you his protection. Eirik has not superseded his father yet."

"Yet," she said.

"Yet," he agreed.

---

Chapter 20: The Crime's Shape

That night Sigrid sat alone with a lamp and her tablet and assembled what she knew.

The shape of Ragnar's crimes had three components.

The first was the Bergjónsson fire. A farmstead destroyed deliberately, forcing a family off land Ragnar wanted. Ordered through an intermediary now dead. No surviving witness except Gunnar, who had confirmed it indirectly but would not speak at the thing.

The second was Svanr's death. A man who had owned the northern field, drowned in the river. Ragnar's direct involvement was unverifiable, but the timing and the acquisition that followed it created a pattern. Patterns were not proof. But they were something.

The third was Ragna. A woman who had understood what her husband was, told another woman, and then died at the fjord. Ragnar's own words: I did not prevent her from falling. Halla's testimony: she had been frightened of him. The drowning explained away for forty years as an accident.

These three things were connected by a single quality: Ragnar's belief that what he was building justified what he was destroying to build it. The settlement was real. The hall was real. The farmland, productive and well-managed, was real. The community that had grown around his leadership — imperfect, contentious, but functioning — was real.

And the crimes that had funded the foundation of all of it were also real.

Sigrid thought about how the poets she had known composed memorial verse. They chose the shape that honored the commission. They left out what would embarrass. They elevated what would impress. It was not dishonesty exactly — it was the compression and selection that any art required.

But there was a line between selection and erasure.

She thought about Ragna, who had needed someone to know and had told the wrong person. She thought about Ósvald's family, cold and displaced, taking a ship south with a child. She thought about Svanr, who might have simply drowned, or might not have.

She thought about what a skald was for.

The answer she kept coming back to was this: a skald was the record. Not the flattery. The record. The difference between a memorial and a myth was whether it held true events within it. A myth was useful for inspiring. A memorial was useful for understanding. And you could not understand a man by the version of him he had constructed for public view.

She would compose the true saga. She knew this now, had known it probably from the first conversation with Ragnar, when she had recognized in his careful management of what he told her the shape of a lie that had been forty years in the making.

The question was not what to compose. The question was how to survive the composing of it.

---

Chapter 21: What Gunnar Confirms

She found Gunnar the next morning before anyone else was awake. He was in the stable, seeing to the horses, which was something he did before breakfast even though it was a task for the grooms.

She told him what she had assembled. Not everything — not Ragnar's own admission — but the framework: the fire, Svanr's death, the boundary records, Halla's testimony about Ragna.

He listened without interrupting. When she finished, he continued brushing the horse's flank in long steady strokes.

"You have it mostly right," he said.

"Mostly."

"The Svanr drowning — I don't know. I was not there. I have wondered. But I cannot tell you what I don't know."

"And the rest?"

He set down the brush. He leaned against the stall wall with his arms crossed, looking at the horse rather than at her. "The fire — yes. As I told you." A pause. "Ragna — yes. Not pushed. But —" He stopped.

"He held her down," Sigrid said.

Gunnar's jaw moved. "He let the current take her," he said finally. "She had fallen, or slipped, or — there was a struggle of some kind. He told me afterward. He came back from the fjord and his clothes were wet and he told me she had slipped and fallen and he had not been able to reach her." He looked at Sigrid. "I knew, when he told me. I knew from his face. That it was not exactly that."

"And you said nothing."

"Who would I say it to?" He looked at the horse. "He was the chieftain. He was my lord. He had given me everything I had." A long pause. "I have told myself, for twenty years, that I did not know for certain. That what I believed could have been wrong." He looked at her. "But I do not believe I was wrong."

Sigrid was quiet. Outside the stable, the farm was beginning to stir — voices, the sound of the well being worked, a dog barking.

"If I include this in the memorial," she said, "Eirik will come for me."

"Yes."

"Will you protect me?"

He was quiet for a long moment. "Until Ragnar dies," he said, "I will protect you. After that — I am an old man, and I have protected things for long enough."

It was honest, at least. She appreciated honesty, even when it was unhelpful.

"One more thing," she said. "Ósvald's son. Gunnar said the family went south and west. Do you know what became of them?"

Gunnar looked at her with something complicated in his face. "His name is Þormóðr Ósvaldsson," he said. "He is fifty-two years old now. He has a farm in the eastern valleys." He paused. "He has been sending messages."

"What kind of messages?"

"To the neighboring chieftains. To the thing leaders." He looked at the ground. "He has been building a case. For forty years he has been building a case."

---

Chapter 22: The Physician's Word

The physician Thorkel came to Sigrid that evening and said that Ragnar had perhaps a week. Maybe less. The wound had begun to smell in a way that was not encouraging, and the fever, which had been manageable, was rising.

Thorkel was not a gentle man, but he was a direct one. He had the manner of someone who had spent years delivering bad news and had arrived at the understanding that directness was a mercy.

"He has more lucid hours than bad ones still," Thorkel said. "But that will change."

"I need three more days with him," Sigrid said. "Three more good conversations."

"You may have two." He looked at her with professional assessment. "What is it you are composing, exactly? The household is — divided about this commission."

"A memorial saga," she said. "An honest one."

He nodded slowly. "I have been a physician long enough to know that honesty is not always welcome." He picked up his bag. "But I have also noticed that people who receive an honest account of their illness manage their remaining time better than those who are told comfortable lies." He paused at the door. "For what that is worth."

She thought it was worth something.

---

Chapter 23: Leif's Hollow Work

Eirik asked Leif to perform a preliminary recitation of his composition for the household. This happened at the evening meal, with torches lit and the household gathered — Sigrid included, though she had not been told about it until an hour beforehand.

Leif stood at the end of the hall and recited.

The verse was technically accomplished. He had good prosody, a clean sense of meter, and the alliterative structures required by the form. The imagery was conventional but not incompetent: Ragnar as the firm-handed leader, the settlement builder, the defender of his people. There were kennings for ship and sword and land. The individual episodes were described in ways that implied heroism without specifying it.

It was, Sigrid thought, the kind of memorial that would be entirely forgotten within twenty years. It had the quality of all verse composed to specification: it fit the shape it had been asked to fit, and nothing else.

She watched the faces in the hall. Eirik nodded with satisfaction. His men responded appropriately. Astrid's face was closed. Gunnar listened without expression. The servants went on with their tasks in the background.

When Leif finished, there was applause of the courteous kind.

Later, in the passage, Leif caught her eye. She gave him a very small nod. He knew what she meant, and his expression told her he agreed.

It was hollow. It was competent and hollow. And some part of him knew it, because he was good enough to know the difference.

That was the tragedy of talent placed in service of a patron's convenience: it could feel exactly like this. The form was there. The fire was not.

---

Chapter 24: Astrid's Arrangement

Astrid came to Sigrid's room again, three nights before the end.

"I wanted to tell you something," she said. "About Björgvin."

"The widower in the western settlement."

"Yes." She sat. "He is not what you might think. He is a decent man, capable of decency. He lost his wife three years ago — fever, not violence. He manages his farm honestly and he has never taken anything that was not his." She looked at her hands. "I tell you this because I am aware of how it looks. A wife planning for life after her husband's death while he is still breathing."

"It looks practical," Sigrid said.

"Yes. It does." Astrid met her gaze. "I have survived this marriage by being practical. By not looking too closely at certain things, by keeping my own account separate from Ragnar's, by making myself useful enough that he treated me well." She paused. "I am not proud of every choice I have made. But I am alive. And Ragna is not."

"You knew," Sigrid said. "About Ragna."

"I suspected." Astrid's voice was flat. "When Ragnar first courted me, three years after Ragna's death, I knew enough to be cautious. But I also knew that caution without marriage meant a different kind of vulnerability." She looked at the window. "There are not many options for a woman of forty-two in this settlement, Sigrid. Perhaps in Reykjavik it is different. Here — you are either someone's wife, or you depend on the generosity of a household that can withdraw that generosity at any time."

Sigrid thought about her own life. Her longhouse in Reykjavik that she owned. Her craft, which paid for it. The choices she had made.

"What I want you to understand," Astrid said, "is that I am not a villain in this. I am a woman who made survivable choices."

"I know," Sigrid said. "I'm not going to put you in the saga."

Astrid nodded. Something in her face released.

"But Halla will speak," Sigrid said. "At the thing. About Ragna."

"I know." Astrid stood. "Let her. That is Halla's truth to tell, not mine."

---

Chapter 25: Eirik's Confinement

On the ninth day, Eirik confined Halla to the storage room.

He did it while Sigrid was with Ragnar and did not announce it. She found out from a servant who came to her afterward, wide-eyed and frightened.

"He put her in the store with the grain," the girl said. "He said she was disturbing the household."

Sigrid went to find Gunnar. He was already at the storage room door, arms crossed, facing down Bárðr the red-bearded huscarl.

"Let her out," Gunnar said.

"Lord Eirik's orders," Bárðr said.

"Lord Ragnar is not dead yet." Gunnar's voice had a quality that Sigrid had not heard from him before — a very quiet certainty that suggested he had been in worse situations than this and had not been moved by them. "Open the door."

Bárðr looked past Gunnar at the two other men behind him. He looked at Sigrid. Then he stepped aside and opened the door.

Halla walked out. Her dark eyes moved around the passage without particular alarm. She looked like a woman who had been briefly inconvenienced, not confined.

"Thank you, Gunnar," she said.

"You should leave," Gunnar said. "Go into the village for a few days."

"I will not," Halla said. "I came to see this through." She looked at Sigrid. "I came to be here when the memorial is spoken. I will not be moved."

Gunnar looked at the ceiling briefly. "Then stay close to where I can see you."

---

Chapter 26: The Full Account

That night, Halla and Sigrid sat together in Halla's room at the back of the hall, and Halla told her everything.

She had known Ragna for six years. They had met at a summer gathering in the third year of Ragnar's settlement, when Ragna had come with her husband. Ragna had been twenty-eight then, practical and clear-eyed and already, Halla said, beginning to understand that she had made a mistake.

"She did not think it had been a mistake at first," Halla said. "She thought he was a strong man. She thought strength was what Iceland required. And he was kind to her, in those early years. Genuinely kind. He valued her judgment. He listened to her."

"When did it change?"

"Gradually." Halla poured from a small flask — something herbal, medicinal-smelling. "She began to notice the gaps. The things that didn't quite fit. A neighbor who had been resistant suddenly compliant. A piece of land that appeared in their holdings without explanation. She asked about the fire. He told her it had been an accident. She believed him the first time." A pause. "The second time she asked — after Svanr died — he was angry in a way that frightened her. Not violent. Just cold. He looked at her and said: this is not something you ask about again."

"And she didn't," Sigrid said.

"She didn't. But she knew." Halla held her cup. "She told me in the spring of her seventeenth year. She said she did not know what to do. She had two children. She was bound to the settlement. She had no family in Iceland. She said she thought if she was careful — if she was not a threat — she would be all right."

"She was wrong."

"In the autumn she was at the fjord with him," Halla said. "They had argued the day before, about the same topic — I don't know the specifics. But she had said something that threatened him, some indication that she might speak publicly about what she knew." Halla's dark eyes were steady. "He came back alone. He said she had slipped."

The night outside was very quiet. No wind. The cold pressing against the walls.

"He told me himself," Sigrid said. "He said he did not prevent her from falling."

Halla nodded. "That is the kind of admission he would make. It satisfies his own sense of honesty without being a full confession. He is, in his way, a very careful man."

"Will you speak at the thing?"

Halla looked at her. "I have been intending to speak at the thing for twenty years," she said. "I have been waiting until Ragnar was dead, so the speaking would serve some purpose. The living chieftain can silence testimony. The dead chieftain cannot."

"What purpose do you think it serves?"

Halla thought. "Ragna is owed the truth told publicly. Ósvald's family is owed the truth told publicly. The settlement is owed an honest account of how it was built." She set down her cup. "The settlement will go on. Eirik will inherit. These things cannot be undone. But the record can be true."

Sigrid sat with that. It was, she thought, exactly the argument she had been making to herself for days.

---

Chapter 27: The Vigil

She could not sleep that night and went to the hall and sat by the low fire. The hall was dark and quiet. One of the servants had fallen asleep on a bench in the corner and was snoring softly.

She sat with the fire and thought about Ragnar Blackhand, dying in the room off the hall. She tried to hold him clearly — not the monster version, not the hero version, but the actual man. A man who had crossed a sea and built something. Who had been capable of kindness and capable of murder. Who had told himself that these two things could coexist if the balance was right — if the building was large enough to justify the cost.

How many men had told themselves this? How many settlements, cities, countries, had been built on the same arithmetic? The foundation a crime, the structure above it real and useful and eventually detached from what it rested on.

She thought about the skald's art. What it was for. She had become a skald at twenty because she had been good with words and because her father had valued words above most things and had passed that valuation on to her. She had composed verse for the dead because it was what she did, and she did it honestly because dishonest verse was a form of contempt for the dead.

She had never composed for a living man. She had never had to decide whether truth was worth surviving for.

She decided, sitting by the low fire, that she already knew the answer. She had known it when she told Eirik no and when she talked to Halla and when she went to the boundary records.

The question now was not whether to compose the truth but whether she had the material to render it fully, and whether she had the craft to render it in a way that honored the obligation of the form.

She began to compose, silently, testing lines in her memory. The meter of the old tongue. The kennings that compressed meaning into images. She worked for two hours by the fire, and by the time she was done she had the opening section solid in her memory.

It was, she thought, the best thing she had made.

---

Chapter 28: Ragnar's Lucidity

He woke from fever into clarity on the eleventh day, and Sigrid was sent for. She found him sitting up, his eyes clear and his voice stronger than it had been.

"I have been thinking," he said, without preamble. "About all of it."

She sat on the stool. The lamp was brighter than usual — someone had trimmed it and filled it more recently.

"About Ragna," he said.

"What have you been thinking?"

He was quiet for a moment. He looked at his blackened hand on the blanket. "That I am the first honest person I have spoken to in a very long time. In here." He touched his chest. "I have been trying to work out how I became this. It does not feel like a decision. It feels like a series of small decisions, each of which seemed reasonable at the time."

"That is how most things are built," Sigrid said.

"Yes." He looked at the ceiling. "Ragna was not like that. She made decisions whole. She decided what a thing was and did not revise." He paused. "I think she decided I was capable of what I was capable of, before I had done it. She saw it first. And then I proved her right."

"Why did you have her brought down to the fjord?" Sigrid said.

A long silence. The lamp burned.

"She had told me she was going to the thing," he said. "That she was going to speak to the chieftains there about the Bergjónsson fire. She had found Ósvald's family — there was a letter, or a message of some kind. She had been in contact with them." He closed his eyes. "I could not allow that. The settlement — everything — depended on the story remaining stable."

"So you killed her."

His eyes opened. He looked at her without flinching. "She fell," he said. "I did not push her. But I held her arm, and then I let go, and I watched the current take her, and I did not go in after her." He breathed. "In thirty years I have never found a form of words that does not mean the same thing."

"No," she said. "There isn't one."

He nodded. As if she had said something true and useful and that he had needed to hear.

"You are the first honest person I have spoken to," he said again. "That is what I mean."

---

Chapter 29: The True Question

She came back to him the next day. He was dimmer — the fever returning at the edges.

"I want to ask you something directly," she said.

"You have been asking me things directly for twelve days," he said.

"This one is different." She sat. "Do you want me to compose the true memorial? The one that includes what you have told me?"

He looked at her for a long time.

"That will destroy Eirik's inheritance claim," he said.

"Not destroy. Complicate."

He closed his eyes. "He will hate you for it."

"Yes."

"He may try to harm you."

"Yes."

"And you will compose it anyway."

"That is what I am asking you," she said. "Whether you want me to."

He was silent for a very long time. The fire in the small room was low. Outside, the wind had returned. She could hear it.

"I called you here," he said finally, "because I heard you had composed an honest verse about a drowned fisherman. A man who died in shallow water drunk, because he was following the voice of his dead wife. And you said: that is what he did, and I will not lie about it."

"Yes," she said.

"If you could do that for Ormr," he said, "can you do it for me? Is there a form of honesty that does not simply destroy everything?"

She thought about it. "Yes," she said. "There is. The honest account is not a condemnation. It is a description. It holds what you built and what you destroyed. Both. Neither erased."

He considered this for a long time.

"Then yes," he said. "Compose it."

---

Chapter 30: The Decision

She walked back from Ragnar's room through the hall and stood by the central fire for a while. The household was quiet in the early afternoon lull.

She had her commission. Not just Ragnar's original request, but this one — the harder one. The specific permission to be honest about what the memorial would contain.

Now she needed to survive long enough to deliver it.

She found Gunnar in the yard and told him what had happened. He listened without expression.

"He told you to compose the true account," Gunnar said.

"Yes."

"And you will."

"Yes."

He looked at the sky, which was the color of old pewter. "Eirik will discover what you're doing before you're finished. He has been watching."

"I know." She looked at him. "I need two things. First: I need Ragnar's explicit protection stated in front of witnesses. Second: I need someone to get word to Þormóðr Ósvaldsson."

Gunnar looked at her sharply. "How do you know about him?"

"You told me," she said. "That he had been building a case."

He was quiet for a moment. "Getting word to him means his claim comes to the thing."

"Yes. The thing should hear it." She met his gaze. "It cannot undo what was done. But the settlement should know what their chieftain's land is built on. They have the right to know."

Gunnar stood for a long time looking at the pewter sky.

"You are going to make enemies," he said.

"I have been making enemies for twelve days," she said. "One more will not change the arithmetic."

He nodded, finally. "I will arrange Ragnar's declaration tomorrow, in front of the household. And I will send a rider east." He looked at her. "There is one other thing."

"What?"

"Where you sleep tonight — change it. And bar the door."

She looked at him. He looked back, steadily.

She changed where she slept.

---

Chapter 31: The Draft

She composed in secret for three days.

She found a corner of the hay barn where the light was tolerable during the midday hours, wrapped in two cloaks, and worked with a small wax tablet to make notes in shorthand. The verse itself she held in memory. Memory was safer than writing. Eirik could take a tablet. He could not take what she had committed to her mind.

The structure took shape as she worked. She used the form of the old memorial sagas — the kviðuháttr meter, appropriate for a chieftain's verse — and within that form she built the account in sections. The crossing from Norway. The first years on the island, honest about the difficulty. The expansion of the holding, with the gaps named clearly. The first wife and her death.

She did not compose it as a condemnation. She was not a judge and this was not a verdict. She composed it as a description of a man who had wanted a great thing and had made terrible choices to get it. She held the chieftain's genuine accomplishments — the settlement, the governance, the years of reasonable administration — alongside the crimes. Both were given their proper weight.

The section on Ragna was the hardest. She worked on it for a full day, alone in the cold barn, until she had a passage that was accurate and not gratuitous. It named what had happened without celebrating it. It said: this woman lived, and knew the truth, and was silenced, and her name was Ragna.

She worked on the section about the Bergjónsson farm. She worked on the pattern of the acquisitions. She used the language of fact rather than accusation — these things happened, in this order, and this was the result — and trusted the listener to draw the inevitable conclusion.

When she had all fifty sections solid in memory, she tested it. She recited it to herself in the barn, standing upright, performing it as she would perform it publicly. Her breath made frost in the cold air.

It was honest. It was well-made. It held the full weight of Ragnar Blackhand's life.

She walked back to the hall with the verse in her memory and her heart beating hard.

---

Chapter 32: Leif's Offer

Leif found her the afternoon she finished.

He came to the hay barn — he must have been watching, because she had not told him she worked there — and stood in the doorway and said: "I know what you've done."

She looked at him. "Then you know as much as anyone."

"I know you've finished something." He came in and sat on a hay bale. "I can tell from the way you're standing."

She studied him. "What do you want?"

He was quiet for a moment. The barn was cold and smelled of animals and old hay. "Eirik asked me this morning if I knew what you were composing. He is worried. He doesn't know, but he suspects." He paused. "I told him I thought your composition was conventional."

Sigrid looked at him. "Why?"

"Because he was going to come to you directly if I hadn't. And I thought you needed another day." He picked up a piece of straw. "I need something from you."

"Go ahead."

"I have been composing for seven years," he said. "I know how to construct a verse. I know the forms, the meters, the structures. I know how to make something that is technically correct." He looked at her. "What I don't know — what I have not been able to figure out — is how to compose something that is actually true. That has the quality yours has."

She waited.

"Teach me," he said.

She considered him. He was twenty-nine and talented and had been willing to sell his talent for Eirik's silver, and he knew it, and it made him unhappy.

"You can't learn it from instruction," she said. "You can only learn it from composing honest things."

"Then show me what that looks like." He met her gaze. "I won't tell Eirik. I could, and I haven't, and I won't."

She thought about it. "Come back when this is finished," she said. "When I'm back in Reykjavik. We can talk then."

He nodded. He stood to go.

"Leif." She stopped him at the door. "You told him my composition was conventional."

"Yes."

"He'll find out you lied."

"Yes." He looked at her with those young, clear eyes. "But that's later. Right now, you needed a day."

---

Chapter 33: The Watching Men

Gunnar came to her before dawn.

He knocked softly at the door of the different room she had taken — a small space off the main hall, a servant's room — and she was awake immediately and let him in.

He spoke low. "Men have been watching the road for three days. Not from the settlement."

She waited.

"Þormóðr Ósvaldsson sent word," Gunnar said. "He received my message. He is coming."

"When?"

"Two days. He is traveling with witnesses — two men from the eastern valleys who know the history of the land dispute."

She breathed slowly. This was what she had wanted. It was also significantly more dangerous than it had been yesterday.

"Eirik has been told," she said. It was not a question.

"I don't know. Possibly. The men watching the road are not ours." Gunnar looked at the floor. "Þormóðr Ósvaldsson is fifty-two years old and has spent his adult life building a legal case at the thing for the return of his family's land. He is not a violent man — he has always chosen legal means." He paused. "But he is coming now because Ragnar is dying, and he knows this is the moment. The land case dies when it cannot be challenged."

"It doesn't die," Sigrid said. "It is decided."

"What do you mean?"

"If the thing hears the memorial and hears his testimony, the thing decides. The settlement decides." She looked at Gunnar. "I am not trying to give him back the land. I am trying to ensure the settlement knows what happened. What they do with that knowledge is the thing's business."

Gunnar looked at her with something she had not seen in his face before. "You are very certain about this."

"I know what a skald is for," she said. "I am less certain about everything else."

---

Chapter 34: Astrid Reads

Astrid found her in the passage that afternoon. "I need to see it," she said. "Part of it."

"Why?"

"Because I need to know that my name is not in it."

Sigrid considered. The verse was in her memory — she had no written version. But she could recite a passage.

She found a quiet corner and recited the section that covered Ragnar's years before his second marriage. She spoke the relevant lines carefully. Astrid's name appeared nowhere in them.

Astrid listened with her arms crossed and her face closed.

When Sigrid finished, Astrid said: "Ragna's death is in it."

"Yes."

"And the fire."

"Yes."

A long silence. Then: "Is it — is it well made? As a piece of work?"

Sigrid thought about this. "Yes," she said. "I believe it is."

Astrid nodded once. Something in her posture shifted — not relaxation exactly, but a kind of resolve. "Then Eirik cannot simply dismiss it," she said. "If it is well made, people will remember it. A badly made verse can be forgotten. A well-made one stays."

"That is the hope," Sigrid said.

Astrid looked at her directly for a moment. "You know this will follow you. People will know you composed it. Eirik has connections."

"I know."

"You're going ahead anyway."

"Yes."

Astrid studied her for a moment longer. Then: "All right," she said. She walked away, and that was all.

---

Chapter 35: The Confrontation

Eirik found the partial notes on a wax tablet she had left in the hay barn by mistake — not the verse itself, but the shorthand framework. Enough.

He came to the hall that evening while the household was at dinner. He walked to where Sigrid sat and stood over her with the tablet in his hand.

"What is this," he said. It was not a question.

The hall went quiet. Sigrid looked up at him. "Notes," she said.

"Notes." He read from the tablet in a tight voice: "'Bergjónsson — fire — ordered. Svanr — river — pattern. Ragna — fjord — not prevented.'" He set the tablet on the table in front of her. "Explain to me what this is."

"The outline of a memorial saga," she said.

"This is slander," he said. His voice was controlled but she could hear the edge beneath it. "This is an account designed to destroy my inheritance and my father's name."

"It is an honest account," she said. "Which is what your father hired me to compose."

"My father is feverish. He is not —"

"He told me to compose it," Sigrid said. "In the presence of witnesses. Gunnar was there." She looked at him steadily. "Would you like to speak to your father about it?"

Eirik's face went through several expressions very quickly. He put his hand on her arm — not violently, but with force. "If you perform this —"

Gunnar was beside him then. Where he had come from was unclear. He was simply there, between Eirik and Sigrid, and his hand was on Eirik's wrist.

"Your father's order stands," Gunnar said. "Until the morning after his death, her protection is his protection." He looked at Eirik with something beyond loyalty — something older and sadder, like a man who has come to the last limit of what he is willing to do.

Eirik released Sigrid's arm. He stepped back. He looked around the hall — at the household watching, at Gunnar, at Sigrid.

"This is not finished," he said.

"No," Sigrid agreed. "It's not."

---

Chapter 36: The Father's Word

The next morning, Ragnar summoned Eirik.

Sigrid heard about it from a servant who had been carrying broth through the passage. Ragnar had been lucid again — another surge, burning out the last of what he had — and had asked for his son. The conversation lasted an hour.

She went to Ragnar afterward.

He was exhausted. The hour with Eirik had cost him something.

"What did you tell him?" she said.

"I told him that I hired you and that your commission stands." He breathed carefully. "I told him that the memorial was mine to commission and mine to approve."

"How did he take it?"

"As Eirik takes things. With perfect control and fury underneath." He looked at the ceiling. "He is his father's son. That is not a compliment, as it turns out."

She sat on the stool. The room was warmer than usual — someone had put more fuel on the small fire.

"I told him something else," Ragnar said. "I told him that the land — the Bergjónsson land — I told him I had not acquired it cleanly." He paused. "He already knew. He has always known, I think, in the general way that sons know things about their fathers that they have decided not to examine too closely."

"What did he say?"

"He said: it was forty years ago. No court will hear it now." Ragnar's mouth made a shape that was not quite a smile. "He is probably right about that. The land will stay. The crime is past the time when it can be made good in the legal sense."

"But it can be made true in the record," Sigrid said.

"Yes." He looked at her. "I know. That is what you are doing." He breathed. "It is what I asked you to do, and I am not withdrawing that request. But I want you to understand that what will come from it is limited. The land stays. Eirik inherits. Ragna is still dead."

"I know," she said.

"Then why —"

"Because the record should be true," she said. "Not because it changes what happened. Because the lie is an insult to everyone it erases, and truth is the only repair available to the dead."

He was quiet for a long time.

"You are a better person than I am," he said finally.

She thought about disagreeing, and decided not to.

---

Chapter 37: Halla's Warning

Halla found her the following morning with an urgency she had not shown before.

"He is dying tonight," she said. "Or tomorrow. Thorkel told me this morning."

"I know," Sigrid said. "I have the verse ready."

"Then you need to perform it before the thing meets." Halla looked at her directly. "Once Ragnar is dead, the household's protection passes to Eirik. Gunnar will not protect you against Eirik once that happens — he said as much."

"He said until the morning after Ragnar's death."

"Then use that morning. The thing convenes three days after a chieftain's death. You have the morning after — use it." Halla's dark eyes were steady. "Eirik will not stop you at the thing itself. The assembled chieftains would never permit it. The thing is the one place in Iceland where even Eirik cannot silence testimony."

Sigrid looked at her. "You're telling me to survive until the thing."

"I am telling you to survive." Halla put her hand briefly on Sigrid's arm. "I have been telling myself for twenty years that the truth would be told eventually. I am old enough now to know that eventually does not arrive without help."

"What about you?" Sigrid said.

"I have been managing Eirik's type for sixty years," Halla said. "I will manage this."

She walked away, and Sigrid stood in the cold passage and thought about what was left to do: one night, one morning, three days, and then the thing.

---

Chapter 38: The Night Work

Ragnar died in the early hours of the following morning.

She knew it when Thorkel came through the hall at the third hour and woke the household with the flat announcement that the chieftain was dead. There was the expected response — grief, some of it genuine, some of it the performance of grief that a household offers when a lord dies. Eirik's face was closed and still. Astrid's was the same.

Gunnar sat for a long time by the central fire and did not move.

Sigrid was already awake. She had not slept. She had spent the night doing what Halla had told her to do: holding the verse in memory, testing it, revising two small passages that she had not been satisfied with. She had worked in the dark, silently, moving her lips very slightly with each line.

By the time Thorkel announced the death, the verse was complete in her memory and she was as certain of it as she had ever been of anything she had composed.

She went to Gunnar and sat beside him at the fire.

"He is gone," Gunnar said.

"Yes."

"I served him thirty years."

"I know."

He sat for a long time with his big hands on his knees. "Was it worth it?" he said. Not to her, not to anyone particular. To the fire, maybe.

She did not answer. It was not a question she could answer for him.

"I am going to leave," he said, finally. "After the thing, I will go north. There is a farm there that needs a huscarl. A smaller farm. A cleaner one."

"Good," she said.

He looked at her. "You should get out of this hall before Eirik wakes fully." He paused. "I will protect you until morning. After that —"

"After that I will protect myself," she said. "But I need you to do something first."

"What?"

"Take me to the thing leaders. Now, tonight. So I can register my intention to perform."

He looked at her for a long moment.

Then he stood and picked up his cloak.

---

Chapter 39: The Poet's Judgment

Leif found her as she came back from registering with the thing leaders, just before dawn.

He was sitting on the bench outside the hall door, in the cold, as if he had been waiting. When she came up the path, he stood.

"He's dead," he said.

"Yes."

"I heard the verse you've been composing," he said. "Not the whole of it. Parts. Through the wall." He looked at her. "It is extraordinary."

She stopped. "What parts did you hear?"

"The section about the crossing. And the section about Ragna." He was quiet for a moment. "It is the best thing I have heard composed in Iceland. And I have heard a great deal."

She did not know what to say to this.

"I am going to tell Eirik," Leif said.

She went still.

"I am going to tell him that the verse is extraordinary," he said. "And that if he prevents you from performing it, he will be the man who silenced the finest memorial composed in Iceland, and that will follow him far longer than anything in the verse itself." He looked at her steadily. "I know him. He cares about legacy. The idea of being remembered as the man who burned a great work of art —" He paused. "It will not stop him from trying to harm you. But it may give him reason to think twice about stopping the performance."

Sigrid looked at him. He was twenty-nine and talented and had spent two weeks at this hall compromising himself, and now, in the cold before dawn, he was offering her the one kind of help he had.

"Thank you," she said.

He nodded. He went inside. She stood for a moment in the dark before dawn, feeling the cold and the weight of what was in her memory, and was grateful that someone had thought to tell her it was good.

---

Chapter 40: The Reading

She asked to see Ragnar's body before the household performed the washing. Not out of ceremony — she would not compose anything further about him. But because she had spent two weeks with the man, asking him difficult questions and receiving answers of various kinds, and she felt that she owed him this: to look at what was left.

He lay on the bed where she had seen him every day for two weeks. His face in death had released something — the constant management of expression that he had maintained even in his worst moments was gone. He looked older than he had looked alive, and also, somehow, simpler.

She stood at the foot of the bed for a minute. Then she spoke the memorial saga quietly, for him alone, in the small room with the lamp.

She spoke it from the beginning. The crossing, the first winter, the decisions made and what they cost. The expansion of the farm and what lay beneath it. Ragna, who had seen him clearly, and what had happened to her. The settlement he had built over forty years, real and substantial and founded on something that was neither.

And at the end — the last section, which she had composed in the hay barn on the last day — she had written a passage about a dying man who had told the truth to a poet. Who had, in his last weeks, found a form of honesty that he had not been able to manage for forty years. It was not an absolution. It was a fact, and it had its own weight.

When she finished, the room was very quiet.

She stood there for another minute. Then she left the room and closed the door softly behind her.

---

Chapter 41: He Saw Clearly

Eirik was in the passage when she came out of the room.

He had been there. She did not know how long.

He looked at her, and she looked at him, and for a moment neither of them spoke.

"You spoke it to him," Eirik said.

"Yes."

His jaw was tight. He looked past her at the closed door for a moment. "What did he say, when you read it to him two days ago?"

She thought back. Ragnar, in the lamplight, listening to the full memorial without interrupting. His face as she reached the passages about Ragna and the fire. The quality of his listening — not angry, not defensive, but a very deep attention, as if he were hearing himself from outside for the first time.

When she had finished, he had been quiet for a long time.

Then he had said: "You saw me clearly."

She told Eirik this.

He was quiet. He looked at the floor. Something moved in his face — grief, she thought, or something very close to it. For his father, or for the kind of father his father had been, or for the fact that these two things were the same.

"He said that," Eirik said.

"Yes."

"He knew what was in it."

"Yes."

Another long silence. "I am still going to contest it," he said. "At the thing. I have the right."

"You do," she said.

He looked at her. The fury was still there, underneath the control, but there was something else now — something that recognized what had just been described to him as a moment of honesty between two people, and was not able to entirely condemn it.

He walked away.

---

Chapter 42: The Household Wakes

The hall filled with grief the way water fills a vessel — not all at once, but steadily.

The washing of the body was performed by Astrid and two older women from the settlement who had been sent for. Halla assisted, which Eirik permitted without comment, perhaps because he was conserving energy for larger battles.

The farmhands and servants gathered in small clusters. People spoke in low voices about Ragnar — stories, memories, the texture of daily life under his chieftainship. Some of the stories were good. He had been generous with his farmhands on the whole, fair in his distribution of work and reward. He had remembered names. He had a talent for making each person feel seen.

Sigrid moved through the hall and listened. She was not collecting material — the verse was finished. She was listening to understand something that she had not fully understood before: that a man could be genuinely good to those immediately around him and still have done terrible things. The two were not in contradiction. They were in parallel, and the people who had benefited from the kindness were not wrong to mourn it.

Gunnar sat by the fire for most of the morning, not speaking to anyone, occasionally accepting food from the servants. He had the quality of a man in the last stages of a long watch — still upright, still present, but coming to an end.

In the afternoon, a rider arrived from the east. Sigrid saw him come in and saw Gunnar go to meet him. She followed.

Þormóðr Ósvaldsson would arrive in two days. He was bringing three witnesses. He was prepared to speak at the thing.

Gunnar told her this quietly. She nodded.

"And Eirik's men?" she said.

"Watching the road." Gunnar paused. "But the thing leaders have already been informed that Þormóðr is coming. He has registered his intention to speak through an intermediary." He looked at her. "Eirik cannot stop him now without violating the thing's protections."

"Good," she said.

---

Chapter 43: The Burning

Eirik came for the manuscript that evening.

Two of his men, Bárðr and another, came to her room and knocked on the door and said that Lord Eirik required the written memorial.

She opened the door. She was holding a small bundle — the wax tablet with her shorthand notes and a single piece of vellum she had used to sketch a structural outline.

"He wants the written manuscript," Bárðr said.

"He can have it," Sigrid said.

She handed him the tablet and the vellum. She went to the small fire in the room and put one piece of straw to the lamp flame and then dropped the burning straw onto the vellum and the tablet. The wax softened and ran. The vellum caught.

Bárðr watched this happen with an expression of confusion.

"There is no written manuscript," Sigrid said. She tapped her temple. "It's here."

Bárðr looked at her. He looked at the burning vellum. He said something under his breath and left.

She stood in the room after they were gone and held completely still for a moment. The verse was in her head, complete and solid, the way a well-made thing lives in the body of the person who made it.

You could burn paper. You could not burn memory.

She stood at the window and looked out at the dark farm — the outbuildings, the well, the track that led toward the thing-field — and felt a clarity that was close to calm.

Whatever happened next, the verse was still there.

---

Chapter 44: Halla's Rites

The funerary rites were performed the day before the thing.

Halla led them, as was appropriate for a völva of her standing. Eirik had not invited her to do so — she had simply presented herself that morning dressed in the ceremonial garments and informed Astrid that she would lead the rites, and Astrid had said yes, because the alternative was to cause a scene that would reflect badly on the entire household.

The rites took two hours in the cold field behind the hall. Halla chanted the necessary formulas, made the necessary gestures and offerings. The household stood in a ring. Sigrid stood among them.

She watched Eirik's face during the rites. He stood at the front, as was appropriate for the eldest heir, and his face was closed and controlled. But when Halla's chanting reached its central invocation — a passage that named the dead and enumerated their qualities — she paused, and then said Ragna's name alongside Ragnar's.

She said it clearly: Ragna, wife of Ragnar, who went into the fjord.

A ripple went through the gathered household. Some people looked at each other. Most kept their eyes forward.

Eirik did not move. But the muscles in his jaw tightened visibly.

Halla finished the invocation and moved on as if she had said nothing unusual.

After, when the household dispersed, Sigrid fell in beside Halla.

"He will hear that as a provocation," she said.

"Good," Halla said. She pulled her cloak closer. "He should be provoked. Tomorrow at the thing he should arrive already thinking about Ragna, so that when the memorial is spoken, he cannot pretend it is unexpected."

"You are very deliberate," Sigrid said.

"I have had twenty years to plan this day," Halla said. She looked at the dark sky. "I have been careful with it."

---

Chapter 45: The Thing Convenes

People arrived from across the settlement on the third morning — farmers, freeholders, the neighboring chieftains from the western and southern valleys. They came on horses and on foot, in groups of two and three and larger, arriving at the thing-field where the flat stone that served as the speaking platform had been brushed clear of frost.

The thing was not a formal court — it was an assembly, a place where the settlement's collective memory was held and tested. The law speaker presided: an old man named Þórðr who had been doing this for thirty years and who had the reputation of scrupulous impartiality and an encyclopedic memory for precedent.

Sigrid arrived early and stood at the edge of the field. The cold was extreme — she could see people's breath in the grey morning light. The ground was hard. She wore her warmest cloak and kept her hands folded inside it.

Eirik arrived with his men. He took a position near the front, as heir. He looked at Sigrid once, across the field, and then looked away.

Halla stood at the far side with three women from the settlement, all older, all of whom had known Ragna. Their presence was not accidental.

Þormóðr Ósvaldsson arrived as the assembly was beginning to take shape. Sigrid had not seen him before. He was a compact man, grey-haired, with the unhurried manner of someone who had been waiting a very long time and had learned patience. He took a position near the law speaker. He had two men with him.

Gunnar stood somewhere behind Sigrid. She did not look for him. She knew he was there.

Þórðr the law speaker called the thing to order. His voice carried in the cold air. He announced the death of Ragnar Blackhand, the convening of the assembly to hear the chieftain's memorial and to address the question of the succession. He noted that there were other matters to be heard, as was customary when a chieftain died.

The wind was very still. The sky was low and grey.

Sigrid breathed.

---

Chapter 46: Leif's Eulogy

Leif was called to perform first, as Eirik had arranged.

He stood on the speaking stone and composed himself. He was younger and less imposing than the occasion probably called for, but he had a clear voice and a composed manner.

He performed the memorial he had composed for Eirik. Sigrid had heard most of it at the household recitation. It was still technically accomplished. The meter was correct, the imagery appropriate. It described Ragnar as the settlement builder, the firm-handed chieftain, the man who had brought order to the eastern valleys.

It was a professional piece of work. It was also, unmistakably, the kind of verse you composed when you were paid to compose something and you were in some internal conflict about it. The conflict did not show explicitly — Leif was too skilled for that — but Sigrid could hear it in the places where the verse reached for resonance and did not quite find it. A hesitation in the phrasing. A kenning that was technically correct but somehow flat.

The assembly listened. There was polite response when he finished. The older farmers near the speaking stone consulted each other quietly.

Leif stepped down from the stone and came to stand near Sigrid. He did not say anything. He looked at the stone, waiting.

"It was well made," she said quietly.

"It was what it was," he said. He did not look at her. "Yours will be different."

"Yes."

A pause. "Good," he said.

Þórðr the law speaker called a brief interval for people to move and warm themselves. People gathered in small groups, breathing steam, talking about the dead chieftain. Some were telling stories. Some were watching Sigrid, who was known to be the second poet.

Eirik was watching her from across the field. His face told her nothing.

---

Chapter 47: The Memorial

Þórðr called her to the speaking stone.

She walked to it through the parted crowd, climbed up, and stood above them. The cold air was still. She could see, from the slight elevation, the whole of the assembly — two hundred people or more, wrapped in wool and leather, their faces turned up to her. At the edges, the land ran off into the pale winter distance.

She began.

She spoke the opening section in the kviðuháttr meter, and her voice carried. She had trained her voice for this — to project across a hall, across a field, into cold air. It did not waver.

She spoke the crossing from Norway. The storm off the Faroes. The first sight of Iceland. She named the three men who had died in the first winter and gave each one a line that honored them. She spoke the early years, the building of the hall, the establishment of the holding.

Then she spoke the Bergjónsson fire.

She did not name it as arson. She said: a farmstead burned, and the family departed, and the land passed to the chieftain, and the manner of this acquisition was not the manner of fair exchange. She said: the man who built ordered it done, and the man who did it is long dead, and the family who lost it went south with a child.

A murmur went through the assembly. She held her place on the stone and let the murmur pass and continued.

She spoke the years of expansion. She named the pattern of the acquisitions without specifying each one. She said: great things were built, and the building of them was not entirely clean, and the settlement should know what it was built on, and should be neither ashamed of what is real in it nor ignorant of what it cost.

Then she spoke Ragna.

She spoke the name slowly and gave it its full weight. She said: a woman came with him from Norway, and she understood what he was, and she died at the fjord, and the manner of her dying was not an accident and was not a full murder but was something between these two, and her name was Ragna, and she should be remembered.

The assembly was very still. She could hear the wind, faint and far, in the lava field.

She spoke the final section: the settlement itself, the forty years of governance, the chieftain who had been capable of kindness and capable of terrible things and who had, at the end, told a skald the truth and asked her to set it in the record.

She said: the record is what it is. The land is what it is. The settlement goes on.

She finished.

The silence lasted perhaps ten seconds. Then one of the older farmers near the front — a woman, one of the ones Sigrid had seen standing with Halla — put her hands together, and the sound started, and spread, until most of the assembly was making the sound of acknowledgment, and the thing held what she had spoken in its collective memory, and it would not be lost.

---

Chapter 48: Þormóðr Speaks

Þormóðr Ósvaldsson spoke after her.

He was given the speaking stone by Þórðr, who had the authority to grant it and the integrity to do so over Eirik's visible objection.

Þormóðr stood on the stone and was quiet for a moment, looking at the assembly. He had the quality of a man who has rehearsed something in his mind so many times that the performance of it is now simply the natural expression of what he knows.

He told them the story of his family. He did not perform it as a memorial — he was not a poet. He told it as a man tells facts he has been holding for forty years. His father had owned the eastern field. The fire had not been an accident. He had been twelve years old when they left. His father had died in the south five years later, not from violence but from the accumulated cost of displacement, of having no land and no future and nothing to leave to a son.

He named the field. He named the year. He named Ketil, the man who had set the fire, and said he had learned of Ketil's role through a deathbed confession to Ketil's own son thirty years ago.

He did not ask for the land back. He said: the law cannot reach back forty years, and he knew that. He said: what he was asking for was acknowledgment. For the record to contain the truth of what had happened.

He stepped down from the stone.

Eirik's men were very still.

Þórðr the law speaker said that the assembly had heard, and that the hearing was part of the record, and that the proceedings would now move to the question of the succession.

---

Chapter 49: The Succession

The succession debate took an hour.

Eirik's claim was clear in legal terms — he was the eldest son, the inheritance was straightforward, and the crimes Sigrid had named were past the statute for legal remedy. Two of the neighboring chieftains argued briefly that the land transfers should be examined, but without a formal claim filed at the national Althing, the thing had no mechanism to act.

Sigrid stood at the edge of the field and watched. She was not surprised. She had not expected the thing to reverse forty years of accumulation. That was not what she had come to do.

What she had come to do was make the account true.

Eirik was declared chieftain by consensus, with conditions: that the matter of the Bergjónsson land be brought before the national Althing for formal consideration, and that Þormóðr Ósvaldsson be recognized as a claimant of record. These were legal formalities, and Sigrid was not certain how much practical weight they would carry. But they were in the record.

She watched Eirik accept the succession with the controlled expression of a man who had gotten what he wanted and knew that it had cost him something significant. Not the land, not the title — but the story. The clean story. The version of his father's life that would have made everything simple and descended without complication.

He would now be the son of a man the settlement knew truthfully. That was not the same as being the son of the myth Ragnar had constructed.

Halla came to stand beside her near the end.

"Well?" Sigrid said.

"It is what it is," Halla said. "It is more than I expected and less than Ragna deserved." She paused. "But her name is in the record now. That is what I came for."

---

Chapter 50: The Walk Home

She left the thing-field in the late afternoon.

Gunnar had arranged the horses. She would ride back to Reykjavik with two of the other huscarls who were heading that direction — Gunnar himself was going north, as he had said he would.

At the edge of the field, she stopped. Leif was there, at the periphery, watching the last of the assembly disperse.

"What will you do?" she said.

He thought about it. "Go back to the settlement. Find honest work." He looked at her. "I will come to Reykjavik in the spring. If the offer stands."

"It stands," she said.

She mounted and rode.

The track back toward the coast ran through the lava field and then along the valley, and the light was already failing, the pale winter afternoon compressing into dark. The horses moved carefully on the frozen ground. She rode with the two huscarls in comfortable silence.

She thought about what she had done and what it had cost and what it had been worth. The cost was real — Eirik would not forget, and she would need to be careful for a time. The worth was harder to assess. The settlement now held a truer account. Ragna's name was in the record. Ósvald's family had been acknowledged. These were not small things, but they were not the same as justice.

She thought about Ragnar, who had said you saw me clearly as if that were a gift. She thought about whether it was.

She rode through the dark and the cold, and in the back of her mind — the part that was always working, even when she was not trying to compose — she was already building something new. Not Ragnar's memorial. Something else.

The first line came to her as they came over the ridge and the distant coast appeared, the sea black and cold under the early stars.

She held the line in her memory and added a second, testing the meter. It was the beginning of something she was composing for herself — about a woman who had ridden east in October to tell the truth to a dying man, and what that had meant, and what it had cost, and whether it had been enough.

She did not have the answer to that last question. But the absence of the answer was not, she thought, a failure. Some things you composed into open questions. That was what they were, and to close them prematurely would be to lie.

She rode on into the dark.

The stars were very clear over Iceland in October, very cold and very far, and the horses moved steadily beneath them, and the verse grew in her memory one line at a time.
