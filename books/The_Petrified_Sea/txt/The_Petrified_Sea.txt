Chapter 1: The Tooth in the Schoolhouse Wall

The schoolhouse at Monument Junction was built of chalk blocks sawed out of the river bluffs, and on hot afternoons the walls gave back the smell of the quarry, dusty and faintly marine, like a cellar where the sea had once kept its things. Eleanor Granger had taught in it for three years and thought of the smell as the smell of arithmetic.

On the second Friday of September, Alvy Sorensen stopped reciting the eights and stood staring at the wall beside the window.

"Ma'am. There's a arrowhead in the wall."

"An arrowhead," Nell said. "Sit down, Alvy."

"It's shining," he said, which was not a thing Alvy Sorensen would invent. Imagination cost him too much effort.

She crossed the room. Low in the third course of blocks, where the afternoon light came raking in, a small triangle sat in the chalk like a nail head in pine. It was the brown of strong tea, glassy, with a fine sawtooth edge that caught the sun. She put her thumbnail against it. It was harder than the wall by a great deal.

"It is not an arrowhead," she said. "Pearl, fetch my penknife from the desk drawer."

The children gathered in defiance of every rule she kept, and she let them, because a rule that could not survive a marvel was not worth keeping. She worked the blade into the soft chalk around the triangle, turning the knife the way her husband had taught her to turn a screw, patiently, without temper. The chalk gave in small white crumbs. The thing came loose into her palm and lay there, three inches of tooth, root and crown, heavier than it looked.

"A shark," she said.

The room objected at once, all of it, even the Kohler twins who never spoke. There were no sharks. There was no water. Alvy's father had hauled water in barrels through the whole of August; everyone present had personal knowledge of the scarcity of water.

"This block was cut from the bluffs above the river," Nell said. "You have all seen the bluffs. They are chalk. Chalk is made on the floor of a sea. It is the dust of small shelled creatures, so small you would need a lens to know them, falling for a very long time through quiet water, the way the snow falls in January when there is no wind. Think of the deepest snow you have seen. Then think of snow falling, slow as that, for longer than men have had names."

"How long?" Pearl asked.

"Long enough to bury everything that died in that water. Fish. Shells. Sharks. Things we have no word for except in Latin." She held the tooth where the light could work on it. "This fellow swam over the spot where you are now sitting. The sea he swam in dried away. The floor of it came up into the weather, and the river cut it into bluffs, and Mr. Sorensen's uncle sawed it into blocks, and we built our school out of the bottom of an ocean. I would call that a more remarkable fact than anything in your reader."

"Is it worth money?" Alvy said.

"It is worth knowing," Nell said, and the room understood the subject was closed.

She kept them at the eights until four, dismissed them into the yellow afternoon, and sat at her desk while their noise went away down the section road. The tooth lay on her blotter beside the pen tray. Without deciding to, she took a sheet of foolscap and drew it: crown, root, the little chipped shoulder where the knife had touched it, the serrations rendered one by one, each its own small decision. Thomas had taught her that, too, on the survey: draw what is there, not what ought to be there. What ought to be there is the commonest lie in the West, he used to say. He had been dead two years that month, of the typhoid that came up the line in '74, and his transit and chains and drawing instruments were in a trunk at the foot of her bed at Mrs. Bly's, oiled, because oiling them was a thing she could do for him.

The light got long. She wrapped the tooth in a square of flannel and put it in her reticule with the drawing.

Outside, the bluffs stood up west of town in the late sun, white as washing, banded with the gray of old weather. She stood on the school steps and looked at them the way a woman looks at a respectable neighbor she suspects of an extraordinary secret. The grass ran toward them for two miles, dry as paper, hissing a little. Underneath all of it, she thought, the sea is still lying there with its dead in its arms.

She had a borrowed Owen at the boardinghouse, the volume on reptiles, eleven weeks overdue to a lending library in Topeka that had likely written her off. She meant to renew her apology and keep the book another month. There was a chapter on the teeth of fishes she wanted beside her lamp that night.

A wagon passed on the road, Sorensen's, raising chalk dust off the ruts. The dust drifted over her in the sunlight, very fine, and she stood in it a moment longer than she needed to, the dust of the old sea settling on her shoulders like the beginning of snow.

Chapter 2: What the Storm Uncovered

The hail came on a Tuesday night in late September, off a green sky, and stripped the gardens of Monument Junction in eleven minutes. It took shingles off the depot, killed a shoat in Teal's pen behind the mercantile, and beat the chalk bluffs along the Smoky Hill the way a laundress beats a rug.

Sunday after church, Nell saddled the mare and rode west. The mare was called Census, because Thomas had bought her with the pay from a county enumeration, and she had a government way of going, steady, thorough, indifferent to weather. The draws were cut fresh, their bottoms floored with new gravel, and everywhere the bluff faces showed bright white scars where slabs had spalled away in the storm.

She rode for the pleasure of looking and because the boardinghouse on Sundays filled up with the smell of boiled hen and the sound of Mrs. Bly's opinions. Where the river bent south around Dane Hollis's claim, the bluff stood sixty feet, capped with sod, fluted by old rains. The hail had brought down a slab the size of a granary door, and the fall had peeled the face back to clean chalk.

Nell saw the line from two hundred yards and knew it was a line, and made herself ride the whole way at a walk, because hurrying toward a thing changed what you saw in it.

At the base of the bluff she dismounted and stood holding the rein.

Out of the white face, in a band a little above her head and running level as a course of masonry, came a row of disks. Each was the size of a wagon hub. They were the gray-brown of scalded cream, and they stood a half inch proud of the chalk where the softer stone had washed from around them, and they marched into the unbroken face at either end of the scar and did not stop. She counted eleven. The twelfth showed only its edge, like a coin in a frozen pond.

Vertebrae. She had Owen well enough by heart to be sure of that much, and she had nothing at all to tell her what owned a backbone laid out longer than a hay barn.

"Well," she said to the mare, because some things had to be said aloud and there was no one else fit to hear them.

She tied Census to a chokecherry at the foot of the draw and went up the spall pile on hands and feet. Close to, the bone had a fine grain, like waterlogged oak gone to stone, and the chalk held each disk in a socket of slightly darker stone, a stain of old decay, the ghost of flesh as plain as a tea ring on a tablecloth. She laid her knotted cord along the row. Nine feet for eleven vertebrae, and no taper to speak of in either direction. Whatever it was, this was the thick of it, the loins of it, and the head and the tail were both still in the hill.

She sat back on her heels in the rubble with her heart going in a way she did not approve of.

A surveyor's widow knew how to think in acres. If the spine ran true into the bluff at the dip of the bedding, the animal lay with perhaps forty feet of itself entombed, lying as it had settled in the soft sea floor, undisturbed by anything except time and one fall of Kansas hail. She had read in the Topeka paper of the eastern professors and their sea serpents, of bones going east from the chalk by the boxcar, of the prices. She had read the names they gave things, the long Greek names like rolls of dark cloth unbolted all at once, and she had thought, reading, that the men who wrote seemed less interested in the animals than in getting their own names stitched to them.

The sun moved while she sat. The bluff face went from white to cream.

She took out her sketchbook and drew the scar, the row of disks, the bedding lines above and below, the chokecherry for scale, the bend of the river. She wrote the date. She did not write the place. On the facing page she drew the twelfth vertebra, the one showing only its rim, with its socket of stain around it, and beneath it she wrote: prox. 40 ft. concealed. Then she sat with the pencil against her lip and crossed out 40 and wrote nothing instead, because Thomas was right, and what ought to be there is the commonest lie in the West.

This was Hollis's deeded quarter, or near it; the line wanted checking. Dane Hollis was a held-together man, two years a widower, his wheat hailed flat twice in three seasons. He did not invite company. His wife was buried on the rise behind the soddy where you could see her stone from the kitchen door, if he kept the kitchen door, which Nell did not know.

The bones were nobody's secret. They lay in a public face of rock above a river anybody might ride. But a thing is only found when somebody knows what they are looking at, and within a week she had better than ordinary reason to believe that men were coming into the country who knew exactly what they were looking at.

She rode home the long way, north about, so as not to print a habit into the grass between the Junction and the bluff. The mare disapproved of the detour. At supper Mrs. Bly remarked that wind and rough country had put color into her face for once, and Nell agreed that they had, and asked for the gravy, and said nothing else all evening, in the contented way of a woman with one whole ocean in her keeping.

Chapter 3: Two Telegrams

Word came to Monument Junction the way all its weather came, off the wire.

Mr. Finch, the operator, was a narrow man with a green eyeshade and a memory like flypaper, and it was his custom to copy out anything of general interest and pin it to the depot board, the freight rates and the wheat prices and the war news from the Sioux country. On the first Monday of October he pinned up two messages, side by side, and stood back from them with the air of a man hanging pictures.

The first was from Aldermont College, Connecticut, addressed to the station agent: PROF H ORDWAY PARTY EIGHT ARRIVING OCT 9 REQUIRE TEAMS FORAGE QUARTERS FOR SEASON ADVISE.

The second had been filed from Philadelphia not four hours later: DR L PRATT PARTY FIVE ARRIVING OCT 11 REQUIRE SAME.

By noon the board had drawn more readers than the church door at a funeral. By two o'clock, Amos Teal at the mercantile had moved his pick handles to the front window, and the proprietor of the Idle Hour had wired Ellsworth for whiskey against expected demand.

"Bone men," said old Carbury at the land office, pronouncing it the way he pronounced grasshoppers. "Two crews of bone men in one town. There'll be money first and trouble after, like every other thing that ever come in on that railroad."

Nell read both telegrams twice and walked back to the boardinghouse with her sums undone in her head.

Mrs. Bly had the news before her, naturally; Mrs. Bly received intelligence as a spider receives the affairs of flies. She stood in her kitchen amending a notice in pencil, and the notice said BOARD $4.50 WEEK, and as Nell watched she rubbed out the figure and wrote $6, and considered, and added GENTLEMEN OF SCIENCE $7.

"Two parties," Mrs. Bly said. "Out of the same papers, I expect, and squabbling, I expect. Quality folk feud worse than kin. I'll want your room for the season, Mrs. Granger, if the school board ever fails you, but till then you're grandfathered at the old rate, being as you carry kindling without I ask."

"You're a just woman, Mrs. Bly."

"I am a widow with a roof," said Mrs. Bly, "which is the same thing wearing an apron."

Reverend Mott came to the gate while they stood talking, his hat in both hands, which with Mott was a sign of weather in the soul. He was past sixty, built like a fence post, and he had buried most of the town's dead since the war, including Thomas, whom he had sat up with three nights at the end.

"You've seen the board," he said. "They're coming for the bones, Mrs. Granger. The big lizards out of the bluffs. I have read of these men. They stand up in lyceums back east and they say"—he looked at his hat—"they say the world is older than Scripture allows, older by more than a man can hold in his head, and they offer the bluffs of this county as their evidence. Our bluffs."

"The bluffs were always going to say what they say, Reverend," Nell said gently. "These men only read louder than most."

"That is what frightens me," Mott said. "A thing read loud enough drowns the readings beside it." He set the hat on his head and squared it. "Mrs. Sorensen tells me you pulled a shark's tooth out of the schoolhouse wall and preached the ocean to her boy."

"I taught geology to her boy. Fifteen minutes of it, between the eights and the nines."

"From a wall I had thought safely Methodist," Mott said, and there was, far down in the dry of his voice, the smallest water of a joke. He touched his hat to Mrs. Bly and went off up the street, narrow and upright, a man carrying a full pail of trouble and spilling none.

Mrs. Bly watched him go. "There'll be lectures," she said with relish. "Lantern slides. Subscription lists. I'll bake for it." She went in to her stove.

Nell stayed at the gate a while. Down at the depot the wire was clattering again, Finch's key answering some far-off question, and the sound carried up the empty street the way the first dry locusts had carried in the grasshopper year, small, businesslike, of no account at all unless you knew what was coming behind it.

Eight men and five men. Teams, forage, quarters, for the season. She thought of the row of gray disks marching into the white face of Hollis's bluff, level as a course of masonry, patient as the dead.

"Keep still," she said, to the bones or to herself, and went in to help with supper.

Chapter 4: The Professor's Men

The nine o'clock freight on the appointed Tuesday put off at Monument Junction: fourteen mules, two wagons knocked down in crates, forty barrels of plaster of paris, picks, spades, crowbars, coils of rope, a crated cookstove, a ton of flour and bacon and airtights, a case of carbines, and eight men in clothing that had started east as good clothing.

Nell watched from the depot platform with half the town. School did not keep until ten on freight days; the pretense that this was the reason she stood there cost her nothing.

The man running the unloading was broad through the shoulders and economical everywhere else. He did not shout. He stood where every man could see him, said a name and a place, and the name went to the place carrying its burden. Sandy hair going gray, a hide man's squint, boots older than his coat. When the plaster came off he stood over the barrels and tried three bungs himself, and the relief on him when the powder ran dry through his fingers was the first feeling he had shown.

"Decker," the station agent said, in answer to somebody. "Silas Decker. Foreman for the professor. Hunted buffalo on this very grass, time was. Now he digs up whatever was here before the buffalo. Says it pays steadier and the game don't run."

The case of carbines went onto the wagon last, and Decker himself rode beside it.

He came back along the platform while the teams were hitching, checking the count on a paper against the pile, and stopped in front of Nell, because she was the only person on the platform looking at the freight instead of the men.

"Ma'am. You'd be the schoolteacher."

"Mrs. Granger."

"Silas Decker." He did not offer his hand to a woman, but he took off his hat, which from the look of the hat was the greater courtesy. "Folks tell me school keeps at ten on freight days. I've a question wants local knowledge. The Professor's wrote ahead and engaged us a camp ground on Cedar Draw, southwest. What I can't learn off a map is the water. Does the draw hold a spring through October, or is it a March creek and a lie the rest of the year?"

"It holds," Nell said. "There's a seep under the second bluff that waters stock through Christmas most years. It went dry in the grasshopper year. No year since."

"Grass?"

"Buffalo grass on the flats, cured standing. Your mules will do better than your men."

Something moved behind the squint, possibly amusement. "Men freight cheaper," he said. "I'm obliged, ma'am." He resettled the hat and then did not leave. "You looked at that plaster coming off like a woman pricing it."

"I was wondering at the quantity. Forty barrels is a deal of mending."

"It ain't for mending." He studied her a moment, deciding, she supposed, whether the explanation was worth the breath, and concluding, to her faint surprise, that it was. "Bone in this chalk comes out soft, ma'am. Cheese in a cellar. You jacket it where it lies—burlap and plaster, like a doctor sets an arm—and it rides to Connecticut hard as a church. Man that taught me is dead of a bluff that came down on him at Antelope Station, so I buy plaster by the ton and I try every bung my own self." He said it without weight, the way Thomas used to mention the men lost on the survey, a ledger fact, a grave you walked past daily and kept swept.

"Mrs. Granger," he said, by way of leaving, and went to his wagons.

They rolled out the section road in a noise of chains, trailing chalk dust, and the town watched them the whole way out of sight, two wagons and fourteen mules and a case of carbines, gone southwest toward Cedar Draw, which was eleven miles by the road from the bend of the river where a row of gray disks lay in a white bluff, saying nothing.

Thursday's train would bring the Philadelphians.

Nell rang the bell at ten and the school came in smelling of excitement and horse. Alvy Sorensen had the cigar band off something called La Flor de Aldermont, bestowed on him at the depot by one of the eight, and wished to know whether professors were rich.

"Some," Nell said. "Open your readers."

"Richer'n railroad men?"

"Differently rich," she said. "A railroad man buys what he wants. A professor wants what can't be bought, and then he buys everything around it. Page forty, Alvy."

She did not look at the window. The window faced southwest, and a person could get nothing from it anyhow but grass and sky and the white shoulder of the bluffs, which had kept their own counsel through drought, hail, fire, and the Cheyenne wars, and might be relied on, she told herself, to keep it a few weeks more in the face of mere Connecticut.

Chapter 5: Dr. Pratt at the Lectern

The Philadelphians came in on Thursday, five men and a mountain of baggage, and before his tents were fairly pitched Dr. Lucius Pratt had engaged the church hall, papered the town with handbills, and announced a free public lecture: THE GREAT EXTINCT MONSTERS OF THE KANSAS SEAS, with lantern illustrations, Friday at seven, all welcome.

"Free," said Mrs. Bly darkly, icing a cake for it. "Nothing a clever man does is free. It's seed corn."

The hall was full by a quarter to seven, farm families in from eight miles out, the Idle Hour empty, Finch the operator in a front pew with his eyeshade in his pocket. Reverend Mott sat at the end of the third row with his arms folded, attending in the spirit of a man inspecting a rival's livestock.

Pratt was younger than Nell had expected, dark-whiskered, quick on his feet, with a voice that filled the room without seeming to try and a way of looking at the crowd as if each person in it had been individually awaited. The lantern was worked by his foreman, a silent square man named Krug. The first slide went up on the bedsheet screen: a painted ocean, green and sunlit, and hanging in it a creature like a steamboat that had given itself over to wickedness, all jaw and paddle and tapering tail.

"Neighbors," Pratt said, "you have been told this country is poor. I am here to tell you that you farm the lid of a treasure house. Beneath your wheat"—he let it swing—"lies the bed of an ocean that rolled a thousand feet deep over this very hall, an ocean that swarmed with the most prodigious animals ever fashioned, and when I have shown you their portraits, you will never call Kansas a poor country again. You will call it the richest graveyard in creation."

He was good. Nell sat between Mrs. Bly and Pearl's mother and conceded it without reservation. He gave them the sea, the slow snow of the chalk, the fish big as skiffs, the turtles broad as wagon beds, the long-necked plesiosaurs rising under flocks of swimming birds; he gave them a mosasaur taking a shark the way a barn cat takes a sparrow, and the hall went oh in one voice, and a Kohler twin had to be removed, dreaming aloud. He never said evolution. He said wonders. He said record of the rocks. He swam in the same waters as Mott's objections and never once splashed him.

At the end he stood in the lantern's spill with the painted monster glowing behind him.

"Some will ask, why dig? Why not let the dead sleep? Friends, a country is known by what it knows of itself. France digs. England digs. Shall Kansas leave her giants in the ground for want of asking? My party is camped on the river. We pay cash for sound bone, we pay wages for honest work, we trespass on no man's land uninvited, and we will, before this season's out, raise out of your chalk a monster that will stand in Philadelphia with the name of this county on its label, forever."

Applause, sustained. Mott's arms stayed folded.

"Questions are a courtesy I'm glad of," Pratt said.

A farmer asked about pay. A boy asked about the biggest one ever. Mott unfolded his arms and asked, with terrible mildness, how old, in the doctor's estimation, this ocean was, and Pratt said that men of science differed, that the rocks recorded duration the way a ledger records debt, page upon page, and that he had never met the page yet that quarreled with its Author; and Mott sat down neither satisfied nor armed, which Nell judged was the precise effect intended.

She had not meant to speak. She heard herself anyway.

"Doctor. Your mosasaur slide showed teeth on the palate, behind the regular rows. A second set, set back, pointing down the throat. Were those drawn from specimens, or are they the artist's?"

The hall turned to look at her. Pratt's eyes came to her with a click, like a lens finding focus.

"From specimens, madam. Pterygoid teeth. I have prepared them with my own hands."

"Then the animal could not chew," Nell said. "Teeth set so, on the bones of the palate, are a ratchet. They are for holding, and walking the prey back. Whatever it took, it swallowed whole, or in halves. I ask because your painted fellow was shown biting pieces from the shark, and I wondered which to believe, the picture or the palate."

There was a silence of a particular weight, the town not knowing whether it had witnessed a discourtesy, and Pratt laughed, once, with what looked like honest delight, and the room eased.

"Believe the palate, madam. The picture is for selling tickets; the palate is true. The painter has my instructions to terrify, and anatomy has no mercy on painters." A ripple of laughter. "You did not get pterygoid off a handbill. May I know whom I've the honor of correcting me?"

"Mrs. Granger teaches our school," said Mrs. Bly, ringingly, before Nell could size the question.

"Then this county's children are better armed than Harvard's," Pratt said, and bowed, and took the next question, and did not look at her again. Which was itself a thing noticed, by Nell and by one other: at the back of the hall, leaning against the doorframe with his hat in his hand, Silas Decker had come in out of the dark sometime during the ocean, and gone again before the lamps were lit.

Chapter 6: A Position Offered

Amos Teal kept a cigar box behind his counter with ROCKS &c. penciled on the lid, and in it he sold, on Nell's behalf and at a commission of one part in five, such small fossils as she gathered on her Sunday rides: shark teeth, fish vertebrae like checkers, the pearly coils of ammonites no bigger than a watch. The trade had been worth perhaps a dollar a month, the buyers passengers off the noon train with curious children.

The Saturday after the lecture, Teal's window held her drawings as well, six of them, pinned to a length of calico. Teal had pressed for it. The bone men have made rocks a subject, he said; a subject in the window is money in the till.

Nell was at the counter pricing cambric when Silas Decker's shadow crossed the drawings, stopped, and came no farther.

He looked at them a long time. Townfolk moved around him the way water moves around a snag. Through the glass Nell watched him take in the shark's tooth rendered at twice scale with its serrations counted true; the ammonite in section, chambers stepped like a stair; the fish skull exploded into parts, each part lettered in her surveyor's print, a, b, c, keyed to a margin note.

He came in, removed his hat, and laid on the counter a fish vertebra from the cigar box.

"Who keys the drawings?" he said to Teal, and Teal, with a shopman's instinct for the destination of credit, nodded across at Nell.

Decker turned. If he was surprised, the squint absorbed it. "The teacher."

"The same."

"Pterygoid," he said, proving where he had spent his Friday evening, and pronouncing it carefully, like a man stepping on river ice. "Where'd a schoolhouse come by all this?"

"My husband was the county surveyor. He taught me drafting. The anatomy is out of a borrowed Owen, and the practice is out of that cigar box. Eleven weeks of evenings, Mr. Decker. It isn't sorcery; it's lamp oil."

He set his hat on the counter beside the vertebra, which she had learned already was his way of sitting down.

"I'll speak plain, ma'am, time being short. The Professor reaches camp Monday week. He writes me he wants the season's finds figured—drawn—as they come out of the ground, not next winter in Connecticut by a man who never saw the ground. His regular artist took a post at the college museum and won't rough it. I've eight men can swing a pick and not one can letter a label. What I am asking is whether you'd take field work. Three dollars the day, found, Sundays off, and you'd ride out from town; camp's no place I'd quarter a lady, and the Professor wouldn't allow it if I would."

The school year is begun, was the answer. School pays twenty-two dollars a month and is certain. The school board includes Mr. Sorensen, who voted against a woman teacher in the first instance.

What she said was: "My plates. The drawings I make. I'd want the right to copy my own work for my own keeping. Copies, not originals; the originals would be the Professor's, bought and paid."

"Take that up with the Professor," Decker said. "Past my reach. I can write it in my letter that you ask it."

"Write it in your letter. Why me, Mr. Decker? Wire Topeka. There are draftsmen in Topeka."

"Wired Thursday," he said, unembarrassed. "Topeka offers me a railroad man who draws bridges, at five a day, who'd want a tent, a horse, and explaining to. You'd cost less and you've half the Latin already." He recovered his hat. "And you was the only soul on that platform watched the plaster come off before the men, and the only soul in that hall Friday looked at the slide's mouth instead of its eyes. The work is mostly looking, ma'am. I been four seasons learning what to look at. You come to it natural."

It was, she would think later, the only flattery in the entire transaction, and it was aimed not at her bonnet but at her eye, which was perhaps why it worked.

"The term," she said. "I can't leave thirty children to the prairie."

"Term's yours to settle. We dig till the freeze."

The school board, applied to that evening in the person of its chairman, discovered that Miss Eudora Fitch, lately of Illinois and boarding with her Junction cousins through the winter, held a certificate and wanted occupation; and that the board could engage Miss Fitch at twenty dollars the month, effecting a saving of two dollars; and that what the bone men paid a careful widow was, as Mr. Sorensen put it while studying the stovepipe, between the widow and the bone men and the Almighty.

On the first of November, in a hard bright wind, Nell rode Census out the section road to Cedar Draw with her instruments in Thomas's oilcloth roll behind the saddle, and reported for work.

Chapter 7: First Quarry

The quarry was a notch in the second bluff above the seep, where a plesiosaur had come apart in the old sea and settled into the ooze like a dropped harness, and the first thing Nell learned in the Professor's service was that the work was nothing like treasure and everything like harvest.

The men worked on their knees in a white trench, in a glare that came off the chalk hard enough to redden the underside of a man's chin. Bone in the chalk was not white; it was the brown of old saddle leather, and it did not gleam; it lay in the matrix like sticks in dirty ice, and the trade consisted of knowing the next inch before the next inch existed. The tools were not picks. Picks lived at the far wall, for moving the worthless yards. Around bone the tools were awls, kitchen knives ground to nubs, shoemaker's brushes, and a patience Nell had not seen the like of since the survey.

"Rule one," Decker said, walking her down the trench that first morning. "Nothing leaves the ground till it's drawn in the ground. That's the Professor's religion and it's why you're hired. A bone out of place is a bone that lies. Where it lay is half of what it's worth."

"And rule two?"

"Don't fall on nothing." A pause. "There's a man at the end there, Lawler—if he offers you the seat on the north side of the dig, that's where the dead burro went in the spring flood. Take the south."

Lawler offered her the north side within the hour, gravely, hat in hand. She thanked him and took the south, and the trench absorbed this in silence except for the cook, Web Halloran, who laughed from the top of the cut like a crow, and after that they mostly let her be.

She drew. Plan view at one inch to the foot, the whole notch gridded with string at two-foot centers, every bone in its square; then each bone at scale on its own sheet, with the depth below datum in her husband's neat figures. Her hands knew the work before her mind caught up to it. A grid is a section line; a datum is a benchmark; a paddle bone is a quarter corner that has lain unvisited since the Cretaceous and must be tied in true or all the deeds fail. By noon of the second day she had stopped translating and was simply at work, in the glare and the chalk smell, the bluff at her back ticking as the sun came around.

Mid-afternoon of the third day she earned her wages and did not know it until evening.

The men were taking up the spoil from around the south paddle, screening it down a chute of canvas, and in the screenings Nell saw a knuckle of brown the size of a thimble and put her hand on the boy Eccles's wrist before the shovel moved again.

"That's a phalanx," she said. "Paddle bone. From the third row, by its waist." She held it up against her own sheet, against the drawn paddle with its one gap in the third file like a missing tooth. "Here is its empty chair, gentlemen."

Silence along the trench. Eccles went red. The bone had crept ten feet from its socket in some old slump of the sea floor, or in last week's barrow, no one would ever know, and that, said Decker, arriving down the cut and setting it in its socket with his own hand, was the entire sermon: it fit, the row ran complete, and a paddle drawn complete with its strays accounted was worth real money to a man in Connecticut to whom a gap meant doubt and doubt meant a rival's footnote.

"Mrs. Granger spotted her own gap off her own sheet," he told the trench, mild as ever. "The sheet knew. Draw first, dig second. School's out."

Web Halloran put molasses on her beans at supper, unasked, which she gathered was a decoration for valor.

She rode home at dusk, stiff in the knees, chalk in the seams of her hands, her roll of plates behind the saddle, and was surprised on the dark road by her own contentment. The work was the survey again: the patient fixing of true positions against all the lying that came after. Thomas would have taken to a fossil grid in an afternoon. The thought of him arrived without the usual undertow, like a neighbor calling, and she rode the last two miles in his easy company, under a sky coming out in cold stars, over the bed of the old sea.

The row of disks in Hollis's bluff she had visited once in October, before ever the wagons came. Level, patient, untroubled. Her sketchbook from that Sunday lived at the bottom of Thomas's trunk now, under the chains, where her room could burn down and not give it up.

Chapter 8: The Crooked Skull

Payday Saturdays, Decker stood the camp a supper, and Nell stayed for it once the cold made the dusk ride hungry work, sitting on a cracker box at the cook fire with the men ranged in their unvarying places like a parliament.

The third such Saturday, a newspaper came up the line in the supply wagon, a Philadelphia paper five weeks stale, with a column on the season's collecting, and the column mentioned Pratt, and the mention of Pratt brought on, as reliably as banking the fire brought sparks, the story of the crooked skull.

Web Halloran told it. He had been with Ordway's party the year it happened and had polished the telling since.

"Doctor Pratt, you understand, is a man in a hurry. Fastest man in America with a dead animal. Eighteen and sixty-nine, he gets a freight of bones up from a Kansas army post—not chalk bones, older—a sea lizard long as a string of freight cars. Long neck, says the army surgeon dug it. Longest neck in creation. Pratt sets it up for the Academy and writes the name in Greek, means something like ribbon-of-the-flood, and the neck on this animal, boys, the neck runs out past the lamplight. He has the skull stuck on the end of all that neck like a bead on a string, and he prints a picture, and the picture goes to every learned society from here to Russia."

Pause for coffee, ceremonial.

"Comes the annual meeting. Professor Ordway rises. Has the honor to thank Doctor Pratt for the most remarkable restoration in the annals of science: a serpent with its head"—Halloran let it hang—"on the wrong end. Them long-neck lizards, see, carry near as much tail as neck, and a tired man at midnight with a surgeon's bad notes had strung the head on the tail and called the tail the neck. And Ordway proves it from the joint faces, cold as a hangman, in front of the entire learned world, and then he prints it, and then he prints it again in three languages, and has the plates struck off and mailed, they say, to every man Pratt ever dined with."

"Once'd have done," said Lawler.

"Once does for a correction," Decker said from the dark side of the fire. "The mailing was for keeps."

"And Pratt?" Nell said.

"Pratt stands up at that same meeting," Halloran said, with the connoisseurship of a man arriving at the best part, "white as this chalk, and allows the error and thanks the Professor for the lesson in—what was it—in the courtesies of science. And the whole room laughed. They say it's the laugh he never got over. The error he'd have outlived; errors is human. The laugh was bought and paid for, and he knows the man that bought it."

The fire worked on its cottonwood. Out in the dark the picketed mules shifted.

"He's paid interest since," Decker said. "Year after, Pratt wires a description east while our party's still digging the animal—names it off a tooth and a newspaper account, beats the Professor into print by a week on the Professor's own find. Professor answers by buying up the freight agents, so Pratt's crates go astray. Pratt hires our quarry men out from under us at double wages in 'seventy-three. We salt nothing and we steal nothing"—this with a level look around the fire, a foreman's look—"but I don't speak for every man either of them has hired, and there's been salting, and there's been worse. You're working in a war, Mrs. Granger. It's politer than the Sioux country, but it keeps longer books."

"And the science?" Nell said. "Somewhere under the ledger, is anybody right?"

"Both, mostly," Decker said. "That's the hell of it, if the cook'll pardon me. Two of the best eyes in the world, and the only use either can think to put his own eye to is reading the other man's mistakes."

Nell rode home under a moon that put the bluffs up white as a washed sheet on a line. A man humiliated in proportion to his haste, she thought, and a man triumphant in proportion to his patience, and between them they had turned the floor of God's own ocean into a courtroom. She thought of eleven gray disks, and a twelfth showing only its rim.

Whoever she gave them to, she gave the other man's enemy a weapon. There was arithmetic in that worth doing slowly, and she had, she judged, until the freeze to do it.

Chapter 9: Spades at Night

The first frost came, and on its heels a discovery: spade cuts in the floor of Ordway's prospect ravine two miles south of the main quarry, fresh ones, made since the Thursday rain, in ground where no man of Decker's had orders to be.

Decker walked the cuts in the early light with his hands behind his back, like a deacon inspecting hail damage. Whoever had dug had known their business: neat test pits at the foot of the bone-bearing band, backfilled and tramped, screened tailings carried off rather than left to tell. But chalk holds boot heels like a ledger holds figures, and one heel had a horseshoe of hobnails missing two nails at the toe.

"Krug's," said Halloran, who knew every boot at both fires the way other men knew horses. "Or his twin brother's."

"You won't hang a man on a boot heel," Decker said. "Pratt's camp is on public land same as ours, and a man may walk where he pleases on the public domain, and dig it too, for all the law west of Topeka cares." He stood looking down the ravine, where the bone band ran like a brown sill under the white. "What he can't do is walk it at night and tramp his fill-dirt flat behind him. Daylight is prospecting. Night work is reconnaissance. They've found the band, and they know we've found it, and they came at night so we'd not know they know."

"Except now we know," said Eccles, who was young.

"Except now we know," Decker agreed, kindly. "Commerce of the trade, son."

He rode to Pratt's camp that same morning, alone except for Nell, whom he asked along with a deliberateness she understood only later: a woman's presence set the terms of a parley below powder. Pratt's camp was trim as a regiment, five wall tents in a line, and Pratt came out in his shirtsleeves, all welcome, and the two parties of the conversation lied to each other for ten minutes with great civility while Krug stood by a tent rope saying nothing and wearing, Nell observed, boots fresh from the Junction's cobbler, heels bright as coin.

"Trespass is an ugly word between camps, Mr. Decker," Pratt said at the end, smiling. "I'll say plainly: no man of mine has orders to enter your workings. What men do of a Sunday, hunting curiosities on the public lands, I can no more govern than you can. Shall we leave it at the public lands are public? I'd hate to read in the eastern papers that the Professor's people had taken to fencing Kansas."

"Public's public," Decker said. "We fence nothing. We watch our diggings, though, nights included, and a man coming into a quarry in the dark amongst the jacks and the undercuts could take harm by pure accident. I'd grieve it. The Professor would write a letter of condolence himself, I expect. He's wrote some fine ones."

"I'm sure of it," said Pratt, and the smile stayed on while something behind it went to half-cock, and they parted with mutual raised hats, two camps now formally at peace, which as Decker observed on the ride home was the same as formally at war but with the paperwork filed.

The night watches began that evening, two men till midnight, two after. Nell took her turn over Decker's objection, which she met by pointing out that she was the only soul in camp who could tell at lantern light whether a disturbed surface had given up bone or only looked robbed, and he gave in with the suddenness of a man who had hoped to be argued with.

Her watch was the early one, Tuesdays and Fridays, with Halloran for company, who respected her quiet after the first quarter hour and kept his own. The quarry by night was another country. The chalk gave back the starlight in a gray glow, like snow under cloud; the jacketed bones lay in their plaster on the trench floor, white loaves in white dust; and the bluff ticked and creaked as the day's heat went out of it, small dry reports, the old seafloor talking in its sleep.

Coyotes ran the river breaks toward morning. Nothing human came.

But twice in the dark of those weeks, far off on the north horizon where nobody's camp lay and nobody's road ran, Nell saw a light show and die, show and die, patient as a survey lamp: a rider passing along the high ground above the Smoky Hill, taking his bearings, working his way—she laid the line on the map in her head and could not make it come out anywhere else—toward the bend of the river at Hollis's, where eleven gray disks and their twelfth sat level in a bluff, growing no older.

She mentioned the light to no one. She was learning the commerce of the trade.

Chapter 10: The Color of Deep Time

On the last Sunday of November, Reverend Mott drove his buggy out to the schoolhouse where Nell was boxing the borrowed Owen and her own oddments for the winter—Miss Fitch was installed now, and entitled to the desk—and stood in the doorway with his hat in both hands.

"They tell me you keep the Sabbath digging for the Connecticut Pharaoh," he said.

"They tell you wrong, Reverend. Sundays I keep for my own rocks." She set the last book in the box. "You've ridden four miles past your dinner to say a hard thing. Say it sitting; the stove's still warm."

He sat. He turned the hat. Outside, the first dry snow of the year went by the window sideways, not falling, just passing through on its way to Missouri.

"I buried your Thomas," he said at last. "I sat with you the night after, and you asked me where he was, and I gave you the answers of my office, and you took them politely, the way a woman takes a calling card. I've watched you three years since, Mrs. Granger. You are the most honest soul in my district, and you are at work with men who say the world is older than the Word, and the trouble I have brought out here in this buggy is that I cannot tell anymore which of you I'm afraid for." The hat went around once. "Sorensen's boy stood up in my Sunday school and said the sixth day might be longer than we counted. He had it off you."

"He had it off a wall," Nell said. "The tooth was in the wall, Reverend. I didn't put it there. I only declined to lie about it being there."

She went to the box and took out, from a nest of flannel, an ammonite the size of a dinner plate, sawed and polished across the coil by a lapidary in Topeka in trade for two of its smaller kin. She set it on the desk in the gray window light, and the chambers showed in section, a stairway of little vaulted rooms spiraling inward, each sealed, each pearled, the small rooms at the heart of it fine as a wren's bones.

"This was alive," she said. "It made these rooms one at a time, the way we add to a house. It lived in the newest and kept the old ones for trim, for rising and sinking in the water, like ballast tanks. It died, and the sea snowed on it for longer than I know how to say to you, and the snow turned to stone with it inside, and the river opened the stone, and here it is on a school desk in Kansas with the snow going by the window." She turned it a quarter turn. "I have read the men who say how long that takes. Their figures may be out, Reverend. Figures often are. But whether the rooms of it were built in six thousand years or sixty million, I am looking at the rooms. I can count them. Counting isn't blasphemy. I refuse to be afraid of a number, and I cannot make myself believe in a God who'd want me to be."

Mott looked at the ammonite for a long while. The stove ticked. The snow went by.

"The men you work for count the same rooms," he said, "and one of them mails his counting to his enemy's dinner guests. I've made inquiries too, you see. It isn't the number that frightens me anymore, Mrs. Granger; you're right, the number is the Lord's arithmetic and He may carry what digits He pleases. It's the men. A thing that size"—he nodded at the coil—"a time that size—a man who looks at it daily either gets small and quiet, like you, like the better astronomers, or he decides he's the only thing in creation big enough to put his name on it. The bluffs won't suffer for it either way. The bluffs have weathered worse than professors. But this town will be asked to choose sides, and a town that chooses sides over the age of the earth ends up quarreling about everything down to the school readers, and I am one old man with one pulpit, trying to keep eleven hundred people in charity with each other through a Kansas winter."

He stood, and set the hat on, and at the door he turned.

"The sixth day may be longer than we counted," he said, trying the weight of it. "It wants saying better than the boy said it. But it'll preach, Mrs. Granger. God help me, it'll preach."

She watched the buggy go off into the passing snow, then wrapped the ammonite and set it in the box with the books, on top, where it would ride safe; and it came to her, latching the schoolhouse door, that of all the men in the county that winter who wanted what lay in the bluffs, the only two she trusted entirely were a preacher afraid of it and a foreman who tried the plaster bungs himself; and that the season coming would require her to do business with all the others.
Chapter 11: The Railhead Crates

The season's first shipment east went out the second week of December: nine crates, plaster-jacketed bone packed in prairie hay, each crate stenciled ALDERMONT COLL. MUS. and numbered, each number entered in Decker's book and in Nell's, with her plates rolled in tin tubes riding the same car.

The crates reached Connecticut. Eleven days later a letter reached Decker, and he read it at the cook fire with a stillness that emptied the talk out of the whole camp.

Crates four and seven had arrived with their lids drawn and reset, the hay disturbed, the jackets within shifted and one cracked. Nothing taken. That was the body of it, twice underlined in a clerk's hand: nothing taken. And the waybill showed the car had stood thirty hours on a siding at Kansas City for no reason any agent could give.

"Opened and read," Decker said. "Like mail."

"Read for what?" Eccles asked. "It's bone."

"It's the season's hand," Decker said. "A man that knows bone could lid those crates back up knowing what horizon we're working, how rich, how big, and what we judge worth freight. He'd know what to bid this county's homesteaders for digging rights before the Professor's agent steps off the train. He'd know"—he folded the letter into its envelope with care, as if it might be wanted in a court someday—"most of what I know, and I been four months learning it."

Suspicion in a small town is a wheel with few spokes. The car had been sealed at the Junction; the seal at Kansas City was whole; therefore what was known of the crates had traveled some way other than with them. The contents of the crates were listed in two books and one telegram—Decker's wire to the college, sent the day before shipment, itemized at the Professor's standing instruction.

Nell was at the depot the following noon, mailing her December letter to the Topeka lending library, when Decker came in and laid a folded paper on the counter and asked Mr. Finch to send it.

Finch read it, and his key hand came up slow.

The message said: PROFESSOR NINE CRATES SHIPPED CONTENTS AS PER LIST IN MY LETTER OF TUESDAY WHICH LETTER TRAVELS BY MAIL THIS DAY DECKER.

"That's all?" Finch said.

"That's all the wire, Mr. Finch. The wire's got a list-shaped hole in it, you might say. Letters now"—Decker counted out his coin—"letters is slow, but a letter don't loiter on a siding in Kansas City. Nor in a depot office neither."

Finch's face did not change, which was itself a kind of change; a man accused unjustly looks struck. He took the coin and bent to his key, and the message went off east in little dry clicks, and Decker touched his hat to Nell on the way out as though they had met over nothing but the weather.

She finished her own business and stood a moment in the cold sun on the platform. Through the office window Finch sat at his table with the green shade over his eyes, the soul of public service. A narrow man with a memory like flypaper, in a room where every fact in the county changed trains.

That evening she began a private practice she kept thereafter: her field journal went where her sketchbook of the Hollis bluff had gone, into Thomas's trunk, under the survey chains, and what rode to camp in her saddle wallet each day was the working book only, which began fresh each Monday and burned, page by page, in Web Halloran's cookstove each Saturday, after she had posted its substance into the true book at night in her room.

Decker noticed the new thinness of the book she drew from within the week, and approved it with one nod and no remark, the highest decoration the service gave.

Chapter 12: Pratt's Offer

The young man was waiting for her at Teal's counter on a Saturday, holding his hat and a parcel, and he was so plainly an emissary, and so plainly miserable in the office, that Nell felt for him before he said a word.

"Mrs. Granger. August Tilley. I collect for Dr. Pratt." He was perhaps twenty-six, sunburned to the ears, with a Maryland softness to his vowels and bone callus on every finger of his right hand, the mark of the awl, the same callus she was raising herself. "The doctor asks—the doctor wishes me to say—" He gave it up and held out the parcel. "He wrote it down, ma'am. He does that."

The letter inside was a single sheet in a racing hand.

Madam. The pterygoid question was the best of a poor evening. I have inquired and am told you now draw for Connecticut at three dollars. I offer five, the originals to remain your property on loan to the Academy, your name on every plate as delineator when published—a courtesy Connecticut has never extended to any hand it hired—and the first description of the season dedicated to the schools of your county. Science is poorly served by good eyes in mean employment. Your servant, L. PRATT.

It was a clever letter. Every clause of it was aimed at a sore place, and the aim was personal: someone had told him about the plates, and the someone could only be one of eight men or the Professor's clerks. Commerce of the trade.

"Do you know what's in it?" she asked Tilley.

"The substance, ma'am." He looked at his hat. "It's a fair offer. The doctor's fair with wages. Better than fair." He stopped, and then said, with the loyalty of the young, which spends itself in honesty rather than in silence: "You'd want paying in March, though. Before April. The doctor's money comes in freshets. It's the museums and the books between times, you see. He owes the lithographers from last winter yet."

"And Mr. Krug's new boots?"

"Cash," said Tilley, wretched.

Nell folded the letter. Through the window the bluffs stood white over the brown river breaks. Five dollars was five dollars; her name as delineator was a thing Connecticut would never print, and both of them knew it; and the letter had reached for her hand by way of her vanity with such skill that her vanity was offended on its own account.

"Tell the doctor," she said, "that I'm engaged for the season, and that a woman who left one employer on a better bid would be worth exactly that bid to the next, and we'd both of us know it. Tell him the pterygoid question is answered to my satisfaction and I hold his anatomy in respect." She gave back the letter. "The dedication to the county schools he may do freely, whoever draws for him. The schools can use it and the schools don't care whose ox is gored."

Tilley's mouth tugged, the first daylight in him. "He'll like that last, ma'am. He'll hate the rest and like that."

"And Mr. Tilley." He stopped in the door. "When the doctor's money fails in March, and you're holding wages due against a Philadelphia bank draft, Mrs. Bly sets a table for gentlemen of science at seven dollars the week, and will carry a civil man thirty days on his face. Not sixty. You'll want to know that."

He looked at her a moment, did the sum of what she had and hadn't said, and put his hat on. "I'm obliged, ma'am. I hope we don't meet professional." He meant across a disputed quarry, and went out, and she watched him ride south with his collar up into the wind and thought: that one digs because he loves the animals. There was one in every war, and the war always found a use for him, and it was never a kind one.

Chapter 13: Salted Ground

In January a chinook bared the south-facing draws, and Lawler, prospecting on a warm Wednesday, came back to camp at a high trot with his hat in his hand, which was how good news rode.

Forty rods up a side ravine, weathering from a low band, lay vertebrae, ribs, a paddle's worth of small bones in a scatter, and teeth, big conical teeth that meant mosasaur and money. The camp shifted to the new ground inside two days. Picks for the overburden, then the awls, then the grid; Nell strung her two-foot squares and began the plan.

It went wrong for her on the fourth day, on paper, before it went wrong for anyone in the ground.

The plan would not balance. A backbone is a sentence; it reads. These vertebrae sat in the band at the proper horizon, but they read like words shaken from a sack: a neck bone next to a mid-tail, facing wrong; ribs of two sizes that refused to pair; and the teeth, when she drew them at scale, would not agree among themselves about the jaw that had carried them.

She took it to Decker as a question, because an illustrator did not cry fraud in a camp that had wintered on hope.

"My sheet won't read," she said. "Either this animal died in a churn, or—" She let him take the sheet.

He looked a long time. Then he went down into the cut with a lantern, alone, against his own rule, and was there an hour, and came up with a rib in his hand and called the camp together in the cold.

"File marks," he said, and ran his thumb along a faint flat where stone had been dressed to fit a socket it was never born to. "And shellac in two colors, and a tooth from one beast set in a jaw from another, and the whole of it bedded in tamped spoil, gentlemen, tamped, with a crust rubbed over it from a slurry bucket. This quarry's a quilt. Somebody sewed it out of culls and buried it where a warm winter and an honest man would find it." He set the rib down gently, which was somehow worse than throwing it. "We've put nine days and a wagon road into a lie."

Eccles said, white: "Pratt?"

"A man that hated Pratt and wanted him blamed," Decker said, "or a man of Pratt's that hates us, or a man paid by neither that sells mischief to both. The bones is genuine, mind—genuine culls, bought or stole from somebody's tailings. Could've come off our own dump from the burro dig, some of it. I'll know the dealer's hand before spring." He looked around the fire, at the season in their faces, and added the only comfort in his stock: "It cost us nine days. It cost them the proof of what they are. Keep your tempers banked, and keep this off the wire."

It did not stay off the wire, of course. Within a fortnight a Denver paper had ORDWAY PARTY DUPED BY MANUFACTURED MONSTER, with details only a man at one of two fires could have furnished, and the laughter went east on the exchanges, and somewhere in Connecticut a cold man read it, and somewhere in Philadelphia a quick man read it too. Nell, posting her plates of the false quarry to the college at Decker's instruction—the Professor will want the lie documented better than most truths—found she could not name, of the two readers, which one the dispatch had been built to wound.

That was the winter she stopped thinking of the feud as a fire that might burn the camps, and began thinking of it as a climate, a thing the work was done inside of, like the glare and the wind.

Chapter 14: The Bone Room

The American Journal of Science reached Monument Junction six weeks stale, addressed to no one, because Professor Ordway's standing order included a copy for the camp the way it included tobacco. Halloran read the cookery out of it, he said, and the rest aloud on Sundays to whoever sat still.

In the February number, between a paper on Wyoming coal and a notice of the Transit of Venus reductions, stood eleven pages over Ordway's name: On New Pythonomorphs from the Cretaceous of Kansas, with Remarks on the Restoration of the Group. Halloran read the title twice, the second time with gravy, rolling the Greek.

"That's our autumn," Decker said. "That's the freight the man in Kansas City read."

It was. Nell took the number to the lamp after supper and went through it with a pencil, and her autumn came back to her dressed in Latin: the plesiosaur of the first quarry, the paddle she had proved complete, the mosasaur teeth of the true digs—measured, compared, christened. Two new species stood named in small capitals. Where her grid had fixed each bone, the paper said in place; where her sheets had keyed each element, the plates said—

She stopped pencilling.

The plates. Four lithographs, folded in at the back, redrawn in a Hartford shop from her field sheets, correct to her last serration—she had counted—and signed in the stone, lower left, in letters the size of broom straws: E. GRANGER DEL.

"Del.," she said. "Delineavit. He drew it."

"Who drew it?" Halloran asked, across the fire.

"Mr. E. Granger," Nell said. "It seems a Mr. E. Granger drew it."

The text thanked, in a final paragraph of acknowledgments set small, the energetic services of Mr. S. Decker and party, and of Mr. E. Granger, draughtsman, whose figures of the paddle of CIMOLIASAURUS were singled out by name as of unusual excellence. Mister. Not a lie anywhere in it, if you allowed that an initial is a fact and a courtesy is a judgment. Her sheets had gone east signed E. A. Granger, as Thomas had taught her to sign a plat: initials carry no petticoats, Nellie; let the work walk in bare.

So the work had walked in bare, and the world had dressed it to its own convenience, and the world's convenience wore trousers.

"It's good work, the paddle figure," she said at last, because it was, and someone in that bone room a thousand miles east had thought so too, had perhaps pinned it to a wall, believing well of a man who did not exist.

Decker took his pipe down. "I put 'Mrs.' in my letters," he said, to the fire rather than to her, a man laying a fact where she could take it up or leave it. "Every letter since November. The Professor reads my letters. Reads everything. Files everything." He drew, and let the smoke go, and his voice stayed level as a sill. "There's no man in Connecticut don't know what the E. stands for, Mrs. Granger. There's just no man in Connecticut paid to know it in print."

The fire worked. Out on the flats the wind went over the cured grass with a sound like slow water.

"Then the plates are mine twice," Nell said. "Once for the drawing and once for the christening." She rolled the journal and stood. "I'll keep my copies current, Mr. Decker. Paper outlasts politeness."

She did not know yet that she was quoting, near word for word, a man she had never met, seventy years old, in Philadelphia, who was at that hour reading the same paddle figure under a better lamp and asking the room at large who in God's abundant creation E. Granger of Kansas might be, because the foreshortening of the third digit was the first honest piece of drawing to come out of either war party in ten years.

Chapter 15: Hollis's Fence

She went back to the bluff at the river bend on the last Sunday of February, reasoning that a county that believed her a fossil woman no longer needed her rides explained, and found the row of disks as she had left it, and a new thing besides: boot prints in the frozen mud of the river road below, a week old or more, pacing back and forth along a forty-rod stretch with the patience of a man measuring, and the burnt ends of three cigars.

She was on the spall pile with her cord stretched when Dane Hollis came down the draw with a shotgun in the crook of his arm, not aimed, which on the frontier was a punctuation mark, not a threat.

"You'd be the bone woman," he said. "Off the Connecticut crew."

"I'd be Nell Granger. I taught school to the Dwyer children, two claims east of you. You hauled my husband's coffin in your wagon, Mr. Hollis, in 'seventy-four, when the livery wanted three dollars for the loan of theirs. I haven't forgot it."

The gun stayed in the crook, but the man behind it came down a notch. He was a long-boned, wind-dried forty-five, with the look they got in the hailed-out years, of a post standing after the fence wire's gone.

"Granger. The surveyor." He looked up at the bluff face, at the disks. "Thought them was wagon hubs first time I seen them. Washed out of some freighter's wreck." He spat, economically. "They ain't hubs."

"No."

"Man come by my place in the fall. Then again at Christmas. City coat, livery horse, calls himself a mineral agent. Offers me forty dollars for quarry rights on this quarter—lime burning, he says. Forty dollars." Hollis's eyes came around to her, pale as the chalk. "I burnt lime myself one season at Russell. There ain't forty cents of lime custom in this county. So either he's a fool, which his boots said otherwise, or there's something in my bluff worth lying about, and here's the Connecticut crew's drawing woman on my spall pile of a Sunday with a knotted string. You'd best come to the house, Mrs. Granger. It's a cold talk and I've a stove."

The soddy was clean-swept, bare as a barracks, with one chair and one stool and one cup and one tintype on the shelf: a square-built young woman in wedding black, holding sweet flag flowers. He saw her see it and said nothing, and put the kettle on.

She told him. It was his hill; the law of the thing was that simple and so was her conscience in the matter, whatever the commerce of the trade might say. She told him what the disks were, near as she could fix it; what such a thing might run to, whole, in eastern money, which was not forty dollars nor four hundred; and what was camped eleven miles southwest of him in two parties, and how those parties dealt, with the railhead crates and the salted ravine for evidence.

Hollis heard it all standing, his back to the stove, still as a man listens to weather coming.

"Why tell me?" he said at the end. "You're took on by one set of them. Your foreman'd want this brung to him quiet. There's likely a finder's wage in it for you."

"There likely is," Nell said. "But the bones are in your land, Mr. Hollis, and I've watched this war all winter, and what it does to a man who don't know what he's holding is decide for him what he's holding. I had a quarter section decided for me once, by men with soft hands and a county map. The figure they named was fair, they told me, and I was a woman fresh widowed and believed them. I've learned a few prices since." She set down the cup. "Don't sell anything yet. Not rights, not rock, not so much as a wagonload of spall. When the mineral agent comes back, tell him your brother in Salina holds a mortgage on the quarter and nothing signs without him. You haven't got a brother in Salina, but it'll travel up whatever wire he answers to, and buy you a season while they hunt the brother."

For the first time the dried-out face moved somewhere around the eyes. "And what is it buys you, Mrs. Granger?"

"Time," she said. "Same as you. Come spring, every man in that bluff's price is going to change, mine included. I'd as soon the man who owns the ground was standing up when the bidding starts." She rose and tied her bonnet. "I can write the Salina letter so it reads like a mortgage holder's lawyer. I write a fair hand."

"I know it," Hollis said. "It's on Martha's stone." And then, because that was further than he had meant to open the door: "Wind's backing north. You'll want the river road." Which was, from Dane Hollis, a benediction.

Chapter 16: Wages of Glory

Professor Hiram Ordway reached his own camp at last on the fourth of March, a season and a half behind his luggage, in a buffalo coat over town broadcloth, and Web Halloran, watching the buggy come up the draw, observed that the Professor had been arriving Monday week since October and it had only now caught up with him.

He was not what the campfire stories had sized. Nell had built a hawk from the testimony and got instead a gray, heavy-lidded man with beautiful small hands, who spoke just above a whisper so that listeners did the work of coming to him, and who walked his quarries for two hours before he addressed a word to anything living. He tried the plaster store first, like Decker; she marked that. He read the grid pegs like a man reading scales. At the false quarry, the salted one, kept open under canvas at his own wired instruction, he stood the longest, and what was on his face was not anger but appetite, the look of a collector before a forgery rare enough to want for the cabinet.

The plates he reviewed that evening in the cook tent, by two lamps, with Nell standing at his shoulder because he did not offer her the other chair.

He went through forty sheets without a word. At the thirty-first—the windpipe question would sit in that quarry, though neither knew it yet—he paused longest. At the end he squared the stack with those small hands, precisely, as a man squares money.

"You measure twice," he said.

"I measure three times, Professor. The third is against the wish."

"The wish?"

"The wish for what ought to be there. It's the third measurement that breaks it."

The heavy lids came up, and she met what the lecture-hall stories were made of: an attention with no warmth in it at all, but no laziness either, total, the way the glare off the chalk was total. "Decker writes that you found the Cimoliasaurus stray in the screenings against your own sheet. The Hartford men redrew your figures and improved nothing but the paper. The journal acknowledges an E. Granger." A pause, exact as a decimal. "Errors of the press are corrected in the next number, Mrs. Granger, when they are material. An initial is not material. The figure is the figure regardless of the figurer; that is its virtue; it is why your sex is no bar to the work where it is a bar to the platform. You are the best bone draughtsman west of Berlin and I will not insult you with the qualification I would otherwise attach, and I pay the compliment no oftener than the press corrects initials, so bank it."

It was, she came to understand, the most words he spent on any subordinate that season, and it contained, in the manner of his published papers, one alp of a compliment, one glacier of an insult, and no apology anywhere in the range; and the question she carried out of the tent into the cold was why a man so wintry mailed his corrections to his rival's dinner guests, and the answer stood plain once she stood outside: because the only heat in him was that hatred. The science was the man's spine, but the feud was his pulse. Pratt humiliated had become, in some bookkeeping deeper than the college's, the one account that paid.

He left for the East again in eight days, having named her wage for the coming season—three twenty-five, unasked—and having said, on the last morning, with his foot on the buggy step and no inflection whatever: "Decker reports a request regarding copies of your sheets. Copies for a lady's portfolio. Granted. The originals are the Museum's; the Museum is not sentimental; you will find I am scrupulous about property, Mrs. Granger, mine and yours alike. It is friendship I keep no books on."

The buggy went down the draw small and black on all that white, and Decker came to stand at her shoulder.

"You'll want to write that grant down," he said, "date and witness," and she looked at him, and he was already writing it.

Chapter 17: The Storm Cellar

The equinox came in over the high plains with a green stillness Nell had learned in eleven Kansas years to read like a posted bill. By four in the afternoon the southwest stood the color of a healing bruise, and the air went thick and electrical, and the stock on every claim within ten miles began to walk fence lines with their heads up.

The Junction's school let out into it. Nell was in town for plaster—the freight office held six barrels consigned to camp—and she saw Miss Fitch on the school steps with her thirty children and the cloud behind her organizing itself into an anvil, and made the decision the sky had already made. They parceled the children into the three nearest cellars by the time the first hail came through, stones like hen's eggs, then the world's light going a sick yellow-green, then the sound.

Mrs. Bly's cellar took eleven souls down its plank steps: Mrs. Bly, Nell, Miss Fitch, five children, the depot agent's wife—and then boots on the stairs out of the roar, and into the lamplight came Web Halloran, August Tilley, and Mr. Krug, who had been at the freight office on the same errand as everyone in the trade, weather having no politics.

The door went down. The roar walked over them.

It is a fact of cellars that they suspend the constitution. For forty minutes under that lid there were no parties to the war, only people in the dark counting the world's worst noise, Halloran with a Kohler twin on each knee telling them a soft endless lie about a cyclone in Texas that had carried a man's fiddle nine miles and set it down in a tree retuned, Tilley gone gray-white and breathing wrong until the depot agent's wife, sixty and knitting by feel, asked him to hold her wool taut and brought him back by way of his hands, and Krug—Krug the silent, Krug of the new boots and the night spades—sitting square under the swinging lamp with a child's dog inside his coat, having carried it down the steps under one arm like a parcel, because it had been standing in the street.

The thing passed. They came up into a town still standing, mostly: shingles in the gardens again, the church's north window gone, Teal's awning wrapped around a telegraph pole like a bandage, and the air rinsed and ringing and full of meadowlarks as though the sky had never meant a word of it.

Krug set down the dog. He looked at Halloran, and Halloran looked at him, two foremen's seconds in a war neither had declared, standing in wreckage neither had made, each holding skim milk and children's blankets they now handed back to Mrs. Bly with identical awkwardness.

"Bad year for roofs," Krug said. It was the first complete sentence Nell had heard out of him.

"Be worse for canvas," Halloran said. "Your tents'll be in Nebraska."

"Some," Krug agreed. He picked chalk-white hail slush off his sleeve, deliberate. "Your plaster's wet, I expect. The freight office took water at the north door. We got four barrels dry under the scale shed. Pratt's buying at Russell anyhow, end of the month." A pause the width of a wagon. "First two's yours if you send before Friday."

Halloran, to his lasting credit in Nell's books, did not look around to see who was listening. "We'll send Wednesday," he said. "Obliged."

They touched hats and went their ways, and the war resumed at sundown, as wars do; but Nell, helping Mrs. Bly hang soaked blankets in the rinsed evening light, put the exchange away in the place she was keeping things that winter, with Tilley's honesty and Decker's letters and the tintype on Hollis's shelf: the growing file of evidence that the men were better than the war, every one of them, and that this fact had never yet, in the history of any war she'd read of, been enough.

Chapter 18: The Composite Man

Decker ran the salting to ground in April, the way he did everything, by walking it slower than other men and writing more down.

The bones of the false quarry were culls, he had said in January, bought or stolen from somebody's tailings. Through the winter he had matched them: three vertebrae to the spoil dump of Ordway's own first-season dig at Antelope Station, two hundred miles east; a paddle bone to a lot sold openly at Hays by a hide man turned bone peddler; and the teeth, the mismatched teeth, to a dealer in Kansas City who supplied curiosity shops and, on the quiet, both warring parties, and who kept books because vanity is a bookkeeper—a ledger of buyers that a man could be induced to read aloud if approached as a fellow tradesman with a bottle.

The buyer of the lot that matched was entered as O. MEAD.

"Orville Mead," Decker said at the fire, "teamstered for us in 'seventy-five and was let go at Antelope Station for selling camp flour. Teamstered for Pratt's outfit last summer out of Russell and was let go in September—Tilley don't say why, which says why. Since October, Mead's been seen at Hays and at the Junction both, in funds, idle, and twice in the company of our friend the mineral agent of the forty-dollar lime quarry. And the funds, where they've been broke and looked at, run to Philadelphia banknotes."

Eccles, older now by a winter, said carefully: "Pratt's money, then."

"Philadelphia money," Decker said. "Which is Pratt's money like Kansas City money is mine. Mead's the kind a war breeds in its ditches, son. Let go by both armies, knowing both camps' grounds, hating both, useful to anybody and owned by nobody, selling each side the other's secrets and selling somebody—somebody with a lithographed look to him—a nine-day lie buried where we'd waste a wagon road on it. You can hire a Mead through three men and a post office box and never show your face. I know it certain because I priced it once myself, to my shame, in 'seventy-three, and the Professor wrote back one line: We do not buy what we cannot exhibit."

He let that stand in the firelight, his own confession laid level with the rest, which was why men twice his wage did what Decker said.

"So it's never proved," Nell said.

"It's proved to me," Decker said. "Proof for a court, no. Proof for the papers, neither—and mark this, all of you, for it's the one lesson the salt pit teaches worth its freight: the Denver dispatch that crowed us duped was filed from the Junction the same week Mead drank at the Idle Hour, and it knew the count of our wasted days to the day. Whoever set the lie also sold the laughing at it. That's not spite, that's craft. There's a mind in this war that fights both generals, and it ain't either general." He knocked his pipe. "Pratt wrote the Professor denying the salt on his honor. First letter between them in six years, Tilley tells me. The Professor read it twice and filed it under M."

"For Mead?" said Eccles.

"For mendacium," Nell said, before she could school herself.

"For Maybe," said Decker, getting up. "Which in this trade is the widest drawer in the cabinet."

Chapter 19: The Question of the Skull

The true quarry of that spring—the one the season would be remembered by, until the bluff at the river bend made all that year's other memory small—was a mosasaur in the gray shale band above the seep, thirty feet of it, lacking the tail, lying on its back with its ribs sprung like a foundered barn.

It was Nell's best work to that date and her slowest. The skull had come apart at the sutures in the old sea and lay opened across four of her grid squares like a watch taken down on a jeweler's cloth, and Ordway's standing instructions on skulls ran to a page and a half: every element figured in place, three views, before lifting.

On the ninth day she came to the throat.

Under the lower jaws, displaced a little down the bedding, lay a run of small arched bones, two dozen and more, light as wafers, no two quite alike but all kin: half-rings, open at one side, the largest the size of a napkin ring, stepping down small as a wedding band. They lay in a loose train, as a string of them would lie if the soft pipe that carried them had rotted and let them drift.

She knew what they were before she let herself say it, because she had spent a January evening with Owen on the anatomy of crocodiles, and because Mrs. Bly's Christmas goose had come to table that winter with its windpipe drawn, and Nell had, to the scandal of the parlor, asked for the pluck and boiled it clean and kept the tracheal rings in a pill box, for no reason on earth except that they were a thing she had read of and here they were in the hand.

Cartilage rings. A windpipe, turned to stone. The breathing of a sea lizard, recorded.

What stopped her brush was not the wonder of it. It was the memory of eleven lithographed pages in the February journal, On New Pythonomorphs, with Remarks on the Restoration of the Group, and the remark in those remarks that had run loudest through the lecture circuit since: the Professor's reconstruction of his new species with a fringe—a crest of bony half-hoops down the spine like a cock's comb, restored from elements found scattered along the backbone of the type specimen, and figured so, and defended already in two replies as the diagnostic glory of the genus. Pratt had mocked the fringe in print—called it the Professor's rooster—but mockingly only, having no specimen to argue from, and the mockery had been entered in the ledgers as one more interest payment on the crooked skull.

Nell looked at the train of half-rings lying where the throat had been, under the jaws, nowhere near the spine.

A fringe on the backbone, restored from scattered hoops. A windpipe lies along the neck; let a carcass roll belly-up as this one had, let the soft parts go and the bedding shift a hand's breadth, and hoops of throat cartilage would drift and settle along the vertebrae like washers spilled along a plank, and a man in a hurry, or a man in love with a crest for his new species, would find his rooster lying ready-made.

She drew what was there. Three views, every ring in place, the bedding lines true, the displacement measured in inches and entered. Then she sat back on the cracker box in the wind and faced the arithmetic: her sheet, published, unmade the fringe. Unmade it the way the joint faces had unmade Pratt's neck—publicly, mechanically, beyond appeal—except that this time the engine of the unmaking would be the Professor's own quarry, his own hired hand, his own scrupulous rule: draw first, dig second; the figure is the figure regardless of the figurer.

She could leave the rings unkeyed. Scattered ossicles, undetermined. Two words, and the sheet would pass any eye in Connecticut.

She keyed them. tracheal rings, displaced; cf. throat of recent Crocodilus; see note. And in the true journal that night, in her room, by lamp, she wrote the note, four pages, with her pill box of goose rings open on the table like a witness, and dated it, and signed the entry as Thomas had taught her to sign a survey she might someday have to stand to in court: full name, no initials. Eleanor Alice Granger.

Then she put the journal under the chains in the trunk, and sat a while with her hands still, understanding with great clarity that she had just written the first page of whatever the rest of her life was going to be about.

Chapter 20: What Nell Keeps

The freeze-up of that spring's last cold snap did what hail had begun. The wet March had soaked the bluff at the river bend; the hard nights of early April got their bar into every seam of it; and on a Sunday morning Nell rode out the north way under a sky like new tin and saw from a half mile that the face had calved again, a fall twice the size of the granary door, lying in fresh ruin at the foot with its dust still on the chokecherries.

She left Census at the draw mouth and walked up the spoil with her heart conducting itself badly, the way it had the first day, and stood still for the better part of a minute before her mind agreed to hold what her eyes brought it.

The fall had taken the face back nine feet and opened the band wide. The vertebrae ran on as they always had, level, patient—and to the south, where the new face stood clean as a sawn plank, the band rose gently with the bedding and delivered, at the height of her two raised hands, the skull.

It lay in profile in the chalk, complete, undisturbed, long as a church pew. The great low wedge of it, the ring of the eye, the back-swept teeth in their files—conical, recurved, and behind the visible row, just proud of the matrix where a flake had spalled, the pterygoids, the second teeth of the palate, the ratchet for walking prey down the throat. The jaws were not closed quite true. They were closed on something.

She climbed the spoil to it, and looked, and made herself name it bone by bone before she would let it be a marvel, because hurrying toward a thing changed what you saw in it.

Crosswise in the gape, held at the angle of a stick in a dog's mouth, lay a paddle. A plesiosaur's fore-paddle, the rowing limb of the long-neck kind, its five splayed files of little square bones fanned like a hand of cards: bitten off, or bitten and held, in the moment the world ended for them both. The sea had read it and snowed on it, and here was the sentence whole: the great lizard had taken the long-neck, and died of its dinner or with it, and gone down holding it, and the mud had kept the receipt sixty feet under the wheat of Dane Hollis.

"Oh," Nell said. Once, quietly, to the entire Cretaceous.

Predator and prey in one slab. There was no such mount in Philadelphia. There was no such mount anywhere; she had read every catalogue the lending library could summon; the museums of two continents would bid their roofs off for it; and either general in this ten-year war would burn the other's whole season's work, and possibly the other's camp, to put his Latin under it.

She did not draw it. For the first time since the schoolhouse wall, she sat on a spall in front of the finest fossil in America and did not draw, because a drawing was a fact that could travel, and travel was the one thing this must not yet do.

Instead she did surveyor's work. She walked the section line by Thomas's compass and her own paces and proved what she had feared since February: the calved face stood not on the deeded quarter Hollis had proved up, but on the quarter adjoining—the odd-numbered quarter, railroad land by the checkerboard of the grant, unpatented, unsold, lying open under the spring sky for any man with the fare to Topeka and the wit to ask for it.

The bones had been Hollis's by sixty rods. The fall had carried the prize across a line drawn in Washington by men who had never seen water on this ground, living or dead.

She rode home with her mouth set, by the long way, and the question riding pillion the whole eleven miles was no longer which general. It was how a schoolteacher, a foreman, and a hailed-out homesteader were to hold sixty feet of the floor of the vanished sea against a railroad, two colleges, and the United States land office, with nothing in hand but a knotted cord, a true journal, and the only secret in Kansas that mattered.
Chapter 21: The Season Ends

The summer of 'seventy-seven, Nell led two lives and kept both of them in order, which she would say afterward was the hardest work of the whole affair.

The open life dug the gray-band mosasaur to its finish, jacketed it in eleven loads, and shipped it east; prospected the breaks south and west; and earned her wage so plainly that Lawler took to calling her sheets the gospels, as in fetch the gospel for this square, somebody, before Eccles improves the truth with a shovel. The mosasaur went off in September. With it went her sheet thirty-one, the throat sheet, keyed tracheal rings, displaced; cf. throat of recent Crocodilus, and a fair copy of the same stayed behind in Thomas's trunk, which was no longer mistrust but method.

The secret life rode north on Sundays, by varied ways, and watched a bluff.

She had told Hollis about the skull the week after the freeze-up showed it, standing in his dooryard, because the thing lay sixty rods over his line and a man deserved to know what his fence was suddenly worth being a fence to. Hollis had walked up with her and looked at it a long time.

"Railroad quarter," was the first thing he said. He spat. "Martha used to say the lines out here was drawn by men playing checkers drunk. Red square, black square." And then, the practical poetry of the man: "Nothing shows from the river road but the spall. I'll run my young stock in this draw all summer. Stock in a draw means a man riding to it regular, and a man riding regular means nothing to nobody."

So Hollis's heifers kept the draw all season, and Hollis's silhouette on a ridgeline became the ordinary furniture of that country, and twice when livery horses with city saddles came up the river road of a Sunday they found a long-boned homesteader sitting his fence with a shotgun in the crook of his arm, watching his stock in the manner of a man with nothing in the world to do but watch it, and they rode on.

The mineral agent did not come back. The forty-dollar offer was not renewed. That worried her more than the livery horses.

The norther that ended the season came the last week of October, and Decker paid the crew off at the cook fire with the ceremony the service required: wages in gold, a written character for any man that wanted one, and a handshake apiece, Nell's included, as if her hand were any other hired hand's, which was the courtesy she had come to prize above most.

Her own arrangement came in a letter under the college's seal, in the Professor's small upright script, and it ran, complete: Mrs. Granger. The Museum employs preparators through the winter at fifty dollars the month. The work is acid, plaster, and patience, and the bench is cold. Your sheets of the gray-band skull require your hand at the proofs in any case. A respectable boardinghouse adjoins the campus. Reply by wire. H.O.

"He wants the proofs watched," Decker said, when she showed it. "And you with them, where he can do the watching. The man hires nothing he can't lock up at night." He handed it back. "Go, though. You've a winter coming either way, and there's a deal of this trade lives indoors, and you've only ever seen the half that sunburns. See what the bones come to, after us. See the bone room." He looked off down the empty draw, where the wind was taking the cook fire's smoke flat. "A man digs four seasons, he earns a look at the cathedral."

"You've never gone."

"They don't show the cathedral to foremen," Decker said, without heat, as a man reports the depth of a well. "Foremen get the cemetery wage and the cemetery thanks. You they'll show it to, on account of the sheets. Go and look for the both of us, and write me what they've done with my paddle."

She wired her acceptance from Finch's office on a Tuesday, watched him send it, and rode out to Hollis's once more before the line of her life bent east. They stood in the cold under the bluff, the heifers gone down to winter pasture, the skull sleeping in its white wall with the chokecherries bare before it.

"Five months," Hollis said. "Bluff's stood five thousand winters. It'll stand five months." He worked his jaw. "You'll be in the same town as him. The Connecticut one." It was not quite a question.

"Same building, like enough."

"Martha had a rule for horse traders," Hollis said. "Watch what he does with the animal he already owns. You're the animal he already owns, Mrs. Granger. No disrespect in it. Watch how you're stabled, and you'll know what to ask before you ever show him this." He looked up at the skull, the long jaws, the crosswise paddle. "Whatever it comes to," he said, "she'd have admired this. Martha. She kept a jar of them little stone curls off this very hill on the kitchen shelf. Called them the first folks." He turned and went down the spall, and that was as far as the door of the man ever opened, and farther than Nell had had any right to expect.

Chapter 22: East by Rail

She had crossed half the continent once before, westbound, a bride of three weeks reading aloud to a husband who checked the conductor's time against his own watch out of professional principle. Eastbound alone in November, she found the journey ran in reverse like a proof: grass to corn, corn to woodlot, woodlot to brick, the horizon stepping closer by the hour until at Albany it stood at the end of every street like a wall, and she understood she had been eleven years in a country where a person could see the weather coming a half day off, and that she would miss it the way you miss a faculty.

The college town was older than Kansas as a word. Elms over the streets, black and dripping. Houses with fanlights that had watched the Revolution. And on a rise at the campus edge, new as a coin, the Museum: three stories of brownstone with its name cut over the doors and its windows lit at five in the winter dusk, burning the Professor's inherited money in the cause of the dead.

The bone room was the cathedral Decker had named it, and it was also a warehouse, and the warehouse was winning.

Her escort that first morning was the chief preparator, a Bavarian named Anschutz with forearms like a smith and the manners of a duchess, and he walked her down the aisles of the great basement between racks that went up into lamplight and shadow: crates, crates, crates, stenciled with the field seasons of a decade, KANSAS 68, KANSAS 69, WYOMING 70, the lids gray with settled dust, the hay sticking out at the seams like the stuffing of burst chairs.

"Opened?" she asked, and Anschutz's eyes went down the rank and came back with the patience of a man who had buried this particular hope years since.

"Mrs. Granger. We are four men and the acid is slow. The Herr Professor ships faster than God can make winters. What is opened is what is wanted for the papers—a skull here, the paddle there, the type pieces, the figured pieces. The rest waits." He laid one hand flat on a crate of the '69 season as a man lays a hand on a horse. "Some I will not live to open. They came out of the ground, Mrs. Granger, and into the dark again, and between the two darks they ride a train. It is not what the boys in Kansas think, I believe."

"No," Nell said. "It isn't."

Her paddle—Decker's paddle, the Cimoliasaurus of the first autumn—she found on the second day, and the finding redeemed a great deal. It was mounted. It hung in a case in the long gallery on the main floor, every bone in its place against painted sea-green, the strays she had socketed home riding true in the third file, and a card under it in copperplate: FORE-PADDLE OF CIMOLIASAURUS, NIOBRARA CRETACEOUS, KANSAS. COLLECTED BY THE MUSEUM'S PARTY, 1876. Lower, in smaller letters: Figured in Amer. Journ. Sci., Feb. 1877.

Collected by the Museum's party. No Decker. No Granger, Mr. or Mrs. The Museum collected, the Museum figured, the Museum knew; the men and the woman it had hired to be its hands in Kansas had evaporated off the card the way water leaves the plains.

She wrote Decker that night, as promised. They have done your paddle handsome, in a case on the main floor, against green. It is the best mount in the gallery and the card says the Museum dug it. I begin to see the shape of the cathedral. The congregation is bones and the only name in the hymnal is the bishop's.

Chapter 23: The Preparator's Bench

The bench taught her the winter's first lesson inside a fortnight: that the field was the cheap half of the trade.

In Kansas a bone cost sweat and sunburn. Here it cost time, the dearer coin. A jacket that two men had plastered in an afternoon was a month at the bench—the plaster sawn, the chalk pared, the bone hardening under washes of glue size, every flake set back with a brush point, the matrix coming away crumb by crumb under needles, until what the sea had flattened and the bluff had cracked stood clean and whole-seeming on its bed of tow. Anschutz's four men worked under green-shaded lamps in a quiet like a sickroom's, and Nell, who had come to draw proofs and stayed to learn needles, found her hands schooled all over again, and was glad of it with a gladness that had no politics in it: this part, at least, was only the dead and the living, patience against time, nothing in the room but care.

The politics came to her anyway, by way of sheet thirty-one.

The proofs of the gray-band mosasaur came up from the Hartford lithographers in January, and the Professor's instruction came with them, in the small upright hand, clipped to her throat sheet: Plate IX as figured. Re-letter key, item f: for "tracheal rings, displaced; cf. throat of recent Crocodilus," read "dermal ossicles, position doubtful." The figure is unchanged. H.O.

The figure is unchanged. That was the whole of his ethics in five words, she thought, standing at the bench with the proof in her hand. He would not touch a line of the drawing—the drawing was evidence, the drawing was property, the drawing was the Museum's honor. But a key was words, and words were the platform, and the platform was his. Dermal ossicles, position doubtful walked her windpipe rings thirty feet aft and turned them into the makings of his fringe, and did it without one false line, the way a surveyor's plat may be true in every call and still hang the wrong man, if the title page lies about who ran the lines and why.

She took the stairs to the director's office with the proof in her hand and her year's wages in her mouth, and got, instead of the Professor, his secretary, and instead of an interview, an appointment for Thursday week; and by Thursday week she had done what the bench and the basement had been teaching her all winter to do, which was to think like an institution.

She kept the appointment. She did not argue anatomy. She said: "Professor, item f. If the rings are dermal ossicles, they are the only ossicles in the genus found at the throat, and Dr. Pratt will count their position off Plate Nine with dividers, and publish the count."

Silence, while the heavy lids considered her, and behind her, hung where the desk faced it, her own drawing of the throat in proof, pinned beside the fringe restoration like a debt beside its mortgage.

"Reword," he said at last. "Item f: 'series of cartilaginous half-rings, displaced; nature debated.'" A pause. "You will note, Mrs. Granger, that I do not ask how you would letter it."

"Tracheal rings, Professor."

"When you have a museum," the Professor said, without any heat at all, turning back to his correspondence, "you may letter your plates as you please. Mind the proofs of Ten and Eleven; the Hartford men crowd their stippling."

She went down the stairs neither winner nor loser, holding the one thing he had surrendered—nature debated, a tied flag on a fence line—and knowing two things she had not known an hour before. The first was that he understood perfectly well what lay at the throat of Plate Nine, had understood it from her first key, and meant to ride the fringe anyway, because the fringe was named and the name was his. The second was the sentence he had handed her like a discharged shell, worth more than he priced it: When you have a museum.

Well, she thought, crossing the cold quadrangle under the elms. Well.

Chapter 24: Pratt in the Doorway

Dr. Lucius Pratt came to the Museum on the last Friday of January, under a flag of truce woven of lawyers: some property of the Philadelphia Academy's, seized in a freight dispute in 'seventy-four, was to be examined by both parties prior to arbitration, and the appointment stood in the Museum's book for eleven sharp, PRATT, L., & COUNSEL, two hours, basement access, ACCOMPANIED THROUGHOUT, the last words twice underlined in the secretary's hand.

Nell was at her bench in the preparators' room, needles in a mosasaur palate, when the party came through on the way to the racks: the secretary, two lawyers built like umbrellas, Anschutz with the keys—and Pratt, hat in hand, taking the room in one sweep the way she had seen him take a lecture hall, and stopping dead at her bench as if he had walked into the end of a wire.

What stopped him was the palate, or so a stranger would have read it. He looked at the bone, and at the green-shaded lamp, and at her, and she watched him assemble it: the room, the bench, the needles, the gray dress; the schoolhouse hall in Kansas and the pterygoid question; the February journal, the paddle figured with unusual excellence, E. GRANGER DEL.

"Mr. Granger, I presume," Pratt said.

"Doctor."

"They letter you E. in Connecticut." He said it gently, not as a barb but as a diagnosis, and put his hat under his arm. "In Philadelphia we'd have lettered you Eleanor and been proud of the type expense. I made you an offer once, through young Tilley."

"You made it to my vanity, Doctor. It was very well aimed, which is why it failed."

The laugh came up out of him the way it had in the church hall, sudden and entire, and the lawyers stirred at the unscheduled sound. "And I am told—by the same lithographers, madam, who serve us both; Hartford is a sieve—that Plate Nine of the forthcoming Pythonomorph memoir carries, at the throat, a series of arched elements whose key has been"—he weighed it—"revised in proof. Twice."

The room got very quiet. Anschutz's keys had stopped. The secretary had the look of a man counting the seconds of a fuse.

"I letter what I draw, Doctor," Nell said. "And I draw what is there. The key is the Museum's. The plate will bear dividers, if a careful man owns a pair."

"A careful man," said Pratt, "owns little else." He bowed, the full Philadelphia article, to her and her needles both. "Madam, your servant. When the war is over—there will be an over, we are neither of us rich enough for perpetuity, whatever he tells his trustees—I hope you will do me the honor of disagreeing with me in print, where it counts." He went on after the umbrellas toward the racks and the dark of the basement, and his voice came back along the aisle, pitched for the secretary and meant for the building: "Good God, the crates. It's true, then. He's got the Cretaceous in here nailed shut. Tell him from me he may keep the seizure, gentlemen—a fossil's safer in his cellar than the bottom of the sea. Less current."

That evening Nell wrote two letters. One went to Decker, plates riding well, Pratt here today, counted our throat rings by rumor before ever the memoir's out—Hartford leaks. The other went, after she had sat over it the better part of an hour, to no one; she wrote it and burned it in the grate, because it had begun Dear Dr. Pratt and there was no sentence she could put after those three words that the war would not eventually find a use for.

Chapter 25: The Academy Hearing

The hearing was nobody's plan. It assembled itself out of the winter's grievances the way a prairie fire assembles out of section-line burnings, and by February it had a name and a room: a special committee of the national scientific brotherhood, five gray heads empowered to inquire into the conduct of collecting parties in the Western Territories, sitting in Philadelphia, papers to be submitted, witnesses heard, gentlemen of the press excluded—a clause which guaranteed, Anschutz observed, that the press would have it whole within the day, garnished.

Ordway submitted the salted quarry, documented to the pound and the penny. Pratt submitted the opened crates of the Kansas City siding, the bought freight agents of 'seventy-two, and a sheaf of telegrams that had reached Connecticut faster than their contents had reached anyone else. Each submission was a mirror of the other and each general called only God and his bookkeeper as witnesses—and then a committee clerk, working down the salted-quarry file, found at its bottom a page of field drawings keyed in a fine surveyor's print, item by item, file marks, shellac in two colors, tooth of beast A in jaw of beast B, signed in full: documented in place before removal, Eleanor Alice Granger, and the committee, weary to its five gray souls of testimony that wanted believing, issued a summons for the one signature in the file that read like a measurement.

The Professor's instruction, handed her with her eastbound ticket, was a masterpiece of its kind: You will answer what is asked, fully. You will volunteer nothing. The first sentence is for their oath, Mrs. Granger; the second is for mine.

The room held five gray heads at a green table, the two generals at two smaller tables with counsel, like duelists who had brought their own surgeons, and a hard chair for witnesses. She sat in it ninety minutes by the clock on the mantel.

What she learned in that chair, she thought after, was worth the chair. Asked could she swear who had salted the ravine, she said no, and watched both tables lean back content. Asked what she could swear to, she said: the marks, the materials, the dates, the tamped fill, and the documented fact that the Denver dispatch had known the count of the wasted days before the camp's own letters went out, and watched both tables lean forward again, displeased in stereo, because a fact that fit neither indictment was a fact wasted, and the room's whole appetite was for indictments.

One gray head only worked differently. He sat at the table's end, very old, with a face like a kindly fist, and he asked the few questions she would have asked herself: at what horizon had the salt been bedded; how deep the tamping; whether the shellac had been weathered artificially or by exposure; and at the end, peering at her file drawings through a lens: "Madam, these figures. You drew the fraud with the same care I should hope to see spent on a type specimen. Why?"

"Because the fraud was the specimen, sir," Nell said. "It was the only honest thing in the ravine. It was exactly what it was."

The old head looked at her over the lens for a long moment, and something in the kindly fist of his face undid one finger, and he made a note, and that was Nell's whole first meeting with Dr. Emil Brandt, emeritus of the anatomy chair, the last courteous man in American science.

The committee found, in April, in nineteen pages, that practices prejudicial to science had occurred in the Territories; that responsibility could not be individually assigned; and that gentlemen engaged in collecting were earnestly enjoined to comity. Decker, receiving Nell's account of it by mail, replied in one line: They surveyed the storm and enjoined the wind to comity. Buy plaster.

Chapter 26: An Evening of Honest Men

The summons to dinner came in a hand like a wren's track, on paper an inch thick: Dr. Emil Brandt requests the pleasure, Thursday, seven o'clock, quite informal. The honor of refusing was not, the wren-track managed to convey without saying so, on offer.

The house was a Philadelphia row house gone entirely to books and bones. A whale's ear bone propped the parlor door; the umbrella stand was the rolled-up leg of an elephant, on which subject the housekeeper had clearly retired from the field years since. The company was seven at a round table: Brandt; his sister, deaf and merry; two young naturalists Nell never untangled, being twins of method if not of blood; an ancient botanist; a lady who translated German monographs; and Mrs. Granger of Kansas, delineator, placed at the old man's right hand with a plainness of intent that embarrassed her more than grandeur would have.

The talk was bones, books, expeditions, the deficiencies of lithographers—shop talk, the first she had ever sat inside of as a member of the shop, and she drank it like the thirsty drink. No one at that table asked her whether she found the West very rough. They asked her the dip of the chalk at the gray band; they asked what glue size she favored under shellac; the translating lady asked, in a swift aside, what she was paid, nodded once at the figure as at a specimen confirming a suspected species, and said only: "Anschutz gets ninety."

At the walnut and madeira, Brandt turned the talk with the bluntness of his years.

"Mrs. Granger. The committee is adjourned and I may say what committees forbid. Your fraud drawings were the best documents laid before us. Your throat key—oh yes," to her look, "Hartford is a sieve and I am downstream of it like every soul in this trade—your throat key was right the first time. Tracheal rings. I have dissected the throat of every reptile that would hold still for me these fifty years, and the Professor's fringe is a windpipe lying along a backbone, and in five years or fifteen the literature will say so, with or without his consent. Or yours."

"My consent isn't asked, Doctor. I draw on wages. The key is the Museum's."

"The key, yes. The plate, yes. The journal"—he tapped the table—"the field journal, madam, the dated record in your own hand of what lay where in God's ground before any man's institution touched it: whose is that?"

"Mine," Nell said. "Bought paper, my ink, kept at night on my own time. Mr. Decker had a lawyer in Hays read the terms of my engagement, after the February number. Drawings made in service convey. The journal does not."

The old man sat back. Around the table the shop had gone quiet, the way a quarry crew goes quiet when the foreman walks the rim.

"Then I will tell you the only secret in this profession," Brandt said. "It is the one I told Pratt too late and Ordway too early, and they made of it the war you have wintered in. Priority is the whole law, and priority belongs to print. Not to wealth—print. Not to genius—print. A page, a date, a description that holds: there is the entire kingdom, and the kingdom's one gate stands open to anything that can hold a pen, and the gatekeeper is mortally blind, madam—blind to sex, blind to wages, blind to museums. The men know the gatekeeper is blind. It is the best-kept worst-kept secret in science, and every soul they hire is kept too poor or too grateful or too well supervised to test it." He filled her glass, steadily, an old man's careful pour. "Publish, Mrs. Granger. On your own ground, in your own name, the first time the ground is yours to stand on. Publish. Paper outlives politeness. It is the only thing I have ever seen outlive money."

In the cab afterward, and in the cars all the way north, and at the cold bench among the needles for weeks, the sentence worked in her the way frost works in chalk.

The first time the ground is yours to stand on.

There was ground. It stood sixty feet above a river in Kansas, white as washing, with a church-pew skull asleep in it and a paddle crosswise in the jaws, on a quarter-section belonging, for the moment, to a railroad's lapsed promise and a checkerboard drawn by drunk angels; and the whole question of her life had quietly become the question of the title.

Chapter 27: The Letter from Hollis

It came in March, addressed in a fist like fence wire, MRS GRANGER AT THE COLLEGE MUSEUM CONN, and the postmaster of the college town had written under it, in pencil, Try Museum basement, which Nell chose to take as the place finding its level.

Mrs Granger. The man with the lime quarry lie come back. Not to me. He has been to the land office at Hays. Carbury's boy seen the tract books out for our township and the agent's writing in his notebook the while. Quarter is still rail road on the book. But here is the thing and I set it down plain. There is talk the rail road grant on this stretch is forfeit or like to be. Some bill or suit back east on account the line was never built past the date fixed. If the grant falls in, the quarter goes back to the Government and opens to entry like any land. Filing day at Hays there would be a line at that door. The agent knows it. I judge he is not buying for no body now but waiting on the falling in, same as a man waits under a tree with his apron out.

Also a survey crew run the river road in Feb. Calls itself the rail road's. Krug the Philadelphia forman rode with them one day. I counted him through Martha's glass. They chained the bends and set two hubs below our bluff and I pulled the hubs after and burnt the stakes, which is a crime I expect, so now you know a felon.

Bluff stands good. Stone curls come out of the spall all winter. I put a jar of the best on the shelf alongside hers.

Your servant D. HOLLIS

She read it twice at the bench, and the second reading was worse than the first, because the second reading did the arithmetic. The agent was not a fool buying lime; he had never been; he was a man with early intelligence of a land-grant forfeiture, waiting to enter the quarter the hour it opened—and now Krug had ridden a survey to the foot of the bluff, which meant Philadelphia money knew which quarter mattered, which meant Pratt had a thread of it; and a thread was all that man had ever needed of anything.

And she was a thousand miles east, paring chalk in a cellar, with the season's terms not yet set and her secret aging toward common knowledge at the rate of other men's telegrams.

She went up the stairs that evening and put her name in the Professor's appointment book for the Thursday, in the line under a trustee and over a lamp-oil contractor, and stood a moment looking at her own writing in the institutional column—Granger, E. A., re: season's terms—and understood that she had just opened the bidding on the only property she owned in the world, and that the property was knowledge, and that every rule of this trade she had learned in eighteen months said the same thing about knowledge a plainsman's rules said about water: whoever states their need first, pays.

She would have to state hers first. There was no help for it. But she spent the days until Thursday at the bench, deciding, needle stroke by needle stroke, exactly how much thirst to show.

Chapter 28: Terms

The Professor received her standing, which his appointment book taught was the length of audience set for contractors; she had allowed for it, and stated her business inside the first minute.

"I know the position, in place, of an articulated pythonomorph exceeding any specimen now mounted in this country," Nell said. "Skull in connection, jaws closed upon the fore-paddle of a plesiosaur. Predator and prey in a single horizon. I have seen it. No one else of consequence has."

She had rehearsed the sentence at the bench for four days, and it did what she had built it to do: it stopped the standing audience. The heavy lids came up; the small hands set down the letter they held; and Hiram Ordway sat, and indicated the other chair, and the meeting became the kind his book had no column for.

"Where."

"That is the property, Professor. I'll not spend it on the first question."

A pause, with appetite in it, controlled. "What do you ask?"

She laid it out as she and the bench had drafted it. The Museum to fund the excavation, fully, Decker to lead it. The landowner—"the title is particular, and part of what I bring"—to be paid honestly, by negotiated contract, not by agent's trick: a named sum and a royalty on exhibition. Announcement withheld until title and contract stood perfected, the camp's discipline on the point to be absolute. And the description, when published, to carry her name—"as illustrator on every plate, and as discoverer in the text, in words of the Museum's choosing. Not an initial, Professor. The name."

He heard it through without motion. Then, in the voice that made listeners do the work of coming to him:

"The funding is nothing; money is the cheapest thing the Museum contains. Decker, certainly; I had as soon trust the season to any other man as trust a chronometer to a blacksmith. The landowner shall have his contract; I am scrupulous about property, as I told you once, and you will find the Museum's contracts are the only honest instruments in this trade. Withholding announcement—" the ghost of something crossed the gray face, the appetite showing its rim, "—agreeable, and easier preached than practiced with Philadelphia's survey crews chaining river roads, yes, I read my foreman's letters too, Mrs. Granger; we are neither of us spending our secret on the first question." The lids came down a fraction. "The name. Illustrator on the plates: granted; it costs a line of type. Discoverer in the text—" He turned one of the small hands over, examined it, turned it back. "Discovery, madam, is an institutional act. A find is made by a party; the party is the Museum's; the text will say the Museum's party, as every text of mine has said since 'sixty-six. I make no exception for you that I have not made for Decker, who has found me forty things finer than ever I found myself, and is content."

"Mr. Decker is not content, Professor. He is paid."

"The two are not distinguished in my bookkeeping." He rose; the audience was returning to its scheduled length. "Take the rest, and the wage of three-fifty, and my word that no other hand will touch the excavation. The name in the text I do not sell. It is not yours to buy, Mrs. Granger; it is mine to bestow, and the day I bestow what is mine because a subordinate has cornered me is the day this museum has two directors." At the door, with the latch in his hand, the only smile she ever saw on him, brief as frost on a window in sun: "You bargain better than my trustees. Wire your homesteader. We dig in May."

She went down the marble stairs with most of what she had come for and the one thing refused that she had known he would refuse, had built the whole approach expecting him to refuse—and stood at the bottom in the gallery, by the paddle in its case, COLLECTED BY THE MUSEUM'S PARTY, reading the card she had read a hundred times.

She had her answer recorded. That was what Thursday had been for. Now there was a letter to write to Philadelphia after all, and this time she knew every sentence that could safely follow the salutation, because Gus Tilley had written her at Christmas, as the young write, news and homesickness and loyalty all in one envelope, and at the bottom of it, meant kindly, meant idly, the thread she had not pulled till now: The doctor says if ever Connecticut wears on you, his offer stands, and he never withdraws an offer, only the funds behind it. He says co-author. He says it like other men say checkmate.

Chapter 29: Counter-Terms

They met in the public parlor of her boardinghouse, by her stipulation, with the landlady's ferns for witnesses: Dr. Lucius Pratt, come north on the cars on eleven hours' notice, which was itself a term of the negotiation stated plainly, and August Tilley, brought, Nell judged, half as secretary and half as character reference.

"Madam," Pratt said, hat off, before his coat was. "You have something in the Niobrara that closes its jaws on something else, and Connecticut will not print your name in the text. Tilley, sit down, this is history; the lady has dividers and we are about to be measured."

"Your survey crew chained the river road in February," Nell said. "Mr. Krug rode with it. You have a thread of this already, Doctor, and a man who waits on a forfeited land grant waits in your interest or near it. I'll not sell you the thread you own. I'm here to learn your terms for the cloth."

"Co-authorship," Pratt said instantly. "Granger and Pratt—Granger first, madam, the field name leads, I have published forty papers on that principle and it has cost me forty quarrels with men who outrank me and not one with the truth. Joint description, your plates, your field journal cited in extenso, my anatomy where you want it and my silence where you don't. The Academy to fund the digging at whatever figure Connecticut named, plus a fifth. And the mount to stand in Philadelphia with both names on the label and the county of Kansas it came from in letters as big as mine."

It was everything the gray man had refused, offered before her chair was warm, and the offering of it confirmed everything Decker and the boardinghouse ledger and Tilley's Christmas honesty had taught her to weigh against it. She let the silence sit a moment, out of respect for the size of the thing, and then did the weighing aloud, because this man, of the two generals, had earned plain dealing by practicing it at least in patches.

"Your lithographers are owed from two winters back, Doctor. Your wages run in freshets; I have watched a man of yours hold your paper against April. The funding you offer is a fifth above a figure you cannot match, let alone exceed. If I take your terms, I take them knowing the dig may stop for money in August with sixty feet of bone half-jacketed under a Kansas sun and every idle pick in the county knowing where to look. That is not a risk I am free to price, because the risk falls on the bones first." She said it without cruelty, and watched him take it without flinching, which was the best of him; the worst of him arrived in the same breath.

"Money mends," Pratt said. "Priority doesn't. Madam, I will write the preliminary notice the week you wire me a sketch—the week, on whatever I have—and your name and mine will hold the species against all the museums in Christendom while the funding finds itself. It is how I have held every field I hold. First in print, then solvent."

"And there it is," Nell said. "The week you have a sketch, Doctor, you are capable of publishing—on a sketch. Sight unseen. You did it to the Professor in 'seventy-one off a tooth and a newspaper. The animal in that bluff has lain whole sixty million years, by Brandt's count of the chalk. It will not be named off a sketch in its first week above the mud. Not by him for spite, and not by you for speed, and not by me for vanity."

Pratt stood at the window a while, hands behind him, watching the elm shadows on the snow, and when he turned around the lecture-hall surfaces were all put away and what stood in the parlor was a man of two-and-forty with debts, genius, and a wound, taking honest measure of a refusal.

"He will eat the name, madam," he said quietly. "Whatever he has promised. The Museum's party will discover it, the Museum's memoir will describe it, and in forty years a clerk dusting his cases will know the word Granger only as a misprint for an initial. I am vain, hasty, and mortgaged; you have counted all that correctly and aloud, and I am obliged to you for the count. But I would have printed your name, and he never will, and you know it." He took up his hat. "My offer doesn't lapse. My funds do. Tilley—" at the door, with the old performing flourish coming back over him like a coat, "—mark the date. We have both just been declined by the best eye in the Niobrara, and the Niobrara will be the judge of which of us deserved it."

Chapter 30: West Again

She gave the Museum notice at the bench on the first of April, worked her fortnight, and took the cars west with her trunk riding ahead of her and the whole proposition of her life packed as carefully as the goose-ring pill box in her reticule.

She had declined Philadelphia. She had taken Connecticut's terms—all but the name, and the name refused was a debt entered, not forgiven; Thursday's appointment-book audience had been recorded in the true journal, verbatim, dated, against whatever court the future kept. The dig was funded, Decker would lead it, Hollis would have an honest contract or she would stop the wagons herself. And the description, when it came—

The cars ran out of the woodlots into the long light, and Brandt's sentence rode with her past every depot on the line. The first time the ground is yours to stand on.

The ground was not hers. The ground was a railroad's lapsed promise, a checkerboard square sliding toward forfeiture and a filing-day scramble at the Hays land office, with a city agent camped under the tree and Philadelphia's chainmen measuring the road below it. Connecticut's money could dig the bones, but the bones would go where the title went; and the title, for perhaps one more season, would go to whoever stood first in a line at a government door—not the richest man in the line, nor the fastest into print; the first with a lawful claim and the fee in hand.

She had written three letters from the boardinghouse parlor the night after Pratt left, and mailed them from three different boxes, an eastern habit she had decided to keep.

One went to Decker at Hays: terms agreed, dig in May, buy plaster, say nothing to the wire.

One went to Hollis: a single page, plain as his own. Watch for filing notices on the forfeiture. If the quarter opens, a citizen may enter it who has not used his homestead right, or a preemption may lie. Ask Carbury quietly which, and what fees, and say it is for your brother in Salina.

And one went to Philadelphia—not to Pratt. To Dr. Emil Brandt, emeritus, in the house of books and bones, and it asked him two questions a woman could not get answered in a land office or a museum, and trusted him with the asking, which was the largest wager of the three. First: whether a scientific description, lawfully published, of a specimen still in the ground conveyed any right in law or custom against the land it lay in. And second—the question under all the others, the one the bench and the basement and the gallery card had been forging all winter—whether the learned journals of the United States had ever yet refused a sound paper because the author signed it Eleanor.

The grass began at Junction City, thin at first, like the first gray in chalk. By Ellsworth it owned the world again, running west under the wind in long silver passes, and Nell sat by the window with her hands still in her lap and watched her country come back, the biggest room in America, floored with the floor of a sea.

Somewhere ahead, eleven miles by the section road from a depot with a flypaper memory, a white bluff stood over a brown river with its dead asleep inside it, sixty million winters patient.

One more season, she told it. Hold one more season.
Chapter 31: The Race Renewed

The camps came back to the chalk country in the first week of May like two armies remembering a border, and Monument Junction, which had wintered lean, woke up fat.

Ordway's party was twelve men this season, with four wagons, a second cook, and a pay chest that rode from the depot under Decker's eye and two carbines. Pratt's was eight, in new canvas, with Krug promoted to something that required him to ride a better horse and smile at storekeepers, neither of which suited him. Mrs. Bly's table ran full at both sittings, gentlemen of science seven dollars; Teal put in a second counter and a boy to mind it; the Idle Hour engaged a fiddler; and the school, Miss Fitch reported to Nell with a new teacher's mixture of pride and despair, had lost every boy over twelve to the wage of water-hauling, and would get them back, gray-headed, in November.

Nell came back to her old room, her old rate, and a parcel of accumulated mail, and found the country talking land.

It talked land at Teal's counter and at the depot board and across the aisle in church: the Smoky Hill & Denver grant, the dead branch line whose checkerboard quarters had sat for a decade pricing every farm in the county, was before Congress at last. The road had never laid an iron rail past the date fixed in its charter; a forfeiture bill had passed the House, the paper said, and lay in the Senate's committee on public lands; and every claim shack and law office between Hays and the Colorado line was running the same arithmetic on the same townships.

And at Dane Hollis's gate, the buggies had begun to call. He stood them off one by one, long-boned in the doorway: land agents with railroad letterheads offering to buy his deeded quarter—the quarter, men said in town, that controlled the only water on that stretch of the river road; an attorney from Hays who represented, he said, eastern parties interested in scientific leases; and once, on a Sunday, a smiling man in a city coat whom Hollis recognized from two winters back, the mineral agent of the forty-dollar lime quarry, now calling himself a locator, who sat his livery horse and observed to Hollis, pleasantly, that a man with hay in two stacks ought to insure against lightning, and that his principals insured most things, and Hollis replied that lightning had been tried on him twice by experts and the stacks stood, and the smiling man rode away smiling, and Hollis went in and wrote his second letter of the year.

She is back at Blys, he wrote. The Connecticut crew is back on Cedar Draw. The Philadelphia crew is camped at the forks not five miles from our bend, which is two miles closer than last year, and their forman rides the river road with a glass. The Senate has our quarter by the tail. Come Sunday. Bring your figures.

Nell rode out the north way, by habit now grooved deep, and they sat in the bare swept soddy with Martha's tintype and the two jars of stone curls on the shelf, and counted what they held against what was coming: one secret, one season, one foreman they trusted, one museum's money with a cold man behind it, one government door that might swing open any week between June and never, and one smiling man under the tree with his apron out.

"Figures first," said Hollis, and put the coffee on.

Chapter 32: The Patent Question

Old Carbury at the land office had survived thirty years of Kansas by answering questions in the order asked and volunteering nothing, a discipline Nell had come to recognize as the local form of a museum key. She went to him openly, on a Tuesday, with the county plat book under her arm and the question framed as a schoolteacher's: she was writing, she said, a primer lesson on the public lands, the children being asked daily by their parents what forfeiture meant, and would Mr. Carbury check her sums.

Carbury looked at her over his spectacles for a moment that said he had watched her ride north of a Sunday for two years and had been bribed for the direction of her rides twice that spring, once in cigars and once in cash, and had given both buyers the same answer, which was that schoolteachers rode for their health.

"Recite your lesson," he said.

She recited. The grant lands of the Smoky Hill & Denver: odd-numbered sections in a belt twenty miles wide, patented to the company only as track was built and accepted; no track built past Monument Junction; therefore the odd sections west of the Junction—she did not say including the southeast quarter of section thirteen, township fourteen, range twenty-six, but it lay under her finger on the plat—had never passed to patent, and stood withdrawn from entry, in trust, pending the road's performance or the grant's death.

"Sums correct so far," Carbury said.

"If the Senate passes the forfeiture and the President signs, the withdrawal dies with the grant. The lands restore to the public domain."

"After the Commissioner gives notice," Carbury said. "Restoration's not the bill; it's the notice. The General Land Office publishes the date the lands open to entry at the district office. That's the gun, Mrs. Granger. Nothing files a minute before it; everything in creation files a minute after."

"And the preference, when the gun fires?"

"Settlement," Carbury said, and took his spectacles off, which was his manner of italics. "Actual settlement holds the preference, same as it's held it on this frontier since before either of us. A qualified entryman in actual residence on the tract at the date of restoration, with improvements showing good faith—house, breaking, well begun—files his declaratory statement first day and relates back. Them that waits under trees with their aprons out"—the spectacles went back on—"stand in line behind the man living on the ground. Or the woman. Widows qualify, Mrs. Granger. Always have. Head of her own family in law, sole and entire. Preemption, one hundred sixty acres; inside a railroad belt the price is the double minimum, two-fifty the acre, four hundred dollars cash at proof. That's the whole primer. Mind, a person settling on withdrawn land before restoration is a trespasser against the railroad till the hour the grant falls in, at which hour"—he closed the plat book gently—"the trespass converts to the oldest right in Kansas. It's been done on every forfeited grant from here to Oregon. It'll be done on this one, on the quarters worth doing it on. Whichever ones them might be."

He charged her nothing, observed that the primer ought to be in every school in the district, and bid her good morning; and at the door he added, to the plat book rather than to her, in the voice of a man recalling weather: "Survey crew chained the river road in February. Philadelphia man rode with it. Tract books was out for township fourteen twice this spring, once for the smiling fellow, once for a Hays attorney. If a person had improvements to make, Mrs. Granger, a person wouldn't make them of a Sunday with the whole county watching the road. A person'd make them weekdays, off her wage hours, of an evening. Evenings is long out here in June."

Chapter 33: Nell Shows Decker

She told Decker the whole of it on the first Saturday of the season, because the dig could not be planned blind, and because two years had taught her exactly how far the man could be trusted, which was all the way to the wall and one step past it.

She did not tell him at camp. She asked him to ride with her after the pay table closed, on a stated errand of inspecting the seep at the river—and led him instead down the long draw in the last light, past Hollis's heifers, around the shoulder of the bluff, and stood Census aside at the foot of the spall so that the new face opened to him all at once, the way the freeze-up had opened it to her.

The skull lay in the evening sun the color of honey in tea. The light came raking, the way it had come for the shark's tooth in the schoolhouse wall, and every tooth in the long jaw threw its small shadow, and the plesiosaur paddle lay crosswise in the gape with its five files of little square bones fanned, dealt, and folded, sixty million years called.

Silas Decker sat his horse without a word for the better part of two minutes. Then he got down, and walked up the spall pile, and took off his hat—not to see better; the brim threw no shadow where he stood. He stood bareheaded in front of it the way men stood at the rail of a grave, and Nell, watching the buffalo hunter's squint go up the jaws an inch at a time, doing its own arithmetic, found she had been wrong about one thing: she had expected to have to explain what it was worth.

"Lord," Decker said at last. Just that, quietly, to the bluff.

And then the foreman came back on duty all at once, like a lamp turned up, and he was walking the face, and the questions came in working order: dip of the band, run of the spine into the hill, weight of overburden, water, road, season. He paced the foot of the bluff twice. He knelt and crumbled spall between his fingers and smelled it like a farmer. He looked once, long, at the river road below and the line of the survey hubs Hollis had pulled.

"Forty foot of animal if it's whole behind the skull," he said. "Two hundred ton of overburden off the top before we dare undercut, and the face is rotten with frost seams—that's two falls in two years and it'll calve again the first wet winter, on its own schedule, onto whatever's standing under it. This ain't a summer's politeness like the gray-band dig. This is quarry work, powder work up top, then surgeon's work below, eight men, ten weeks, and a road built for the out-haul. It can be done. It can be done this season, if the money's what you say." He put his hat back on, which closed the grave-rail portion of the proceedings. "And it sits on the disputed checkerboard, with Philadelphia's glass on the road, and the announcement embargoed till a government gun fires on a date no man knows. Mrs. Granger, I've dug under the Professor's orders nine seasons. I've never once dug under sealed orders against the calendar and the United States Senate both." He looked at the skull a last time, and the rarest thing in Nell's whole inventory of the man happened: Silas Decker grinned. "I'd not miss it for the wage of bishops. Who else knows the ground?"

"Hollis. You. Me."

"Keep it three till the wagons must move. After that it's a town secret, which is a bell with the rope already swinging." He mounted. "I'll want the powder man from Hays, the one that don't drink. And Mrs. Granger—your evening improvements, on the quarter. Chalk-block, I think, not sod. There's three of us now can lay chalk block of an evening, and one of us has built in it." He gathered the reins, sober as a sill again. "House first, surveyor's widow. The law likes a roof. The bones have got one already, sixty foot thick. Ours wants raising by August."

Chapter 34: Three-Way Bargain

The contracts were drawn in the second week of May, in three rooms, and Nell was the only soul who sat in all three.

The first room was Hollis's soddy. The Museum's printed form of agreement—Decker carried a pad of them, the Professor being scrupulous about property—was amended at the kitchen table in Nell's surveyor print until the printed parts were a fence around handwritten land: access across the deeded quarter of D. Hollis by the stated road and no other; camp, water, and spoil ground at stated rents; all surface damage made good; his team and his labor hired at the county's top wage when wanted; and a royalty—Nell had held out on the word against the form's silence until Decker, who could not grant it, wrote it in anyway for the Professor to strike—of one half of one percent of paid admissions to any exhibition of material removed via the Hollis road, in perpetuity, payable to D. Hollis, his heirs and assigns. The Professor, reading it in Connecticut, wired back one line: APPROVE ALL. ROYALTY APPROVED AS WRITTEN. SCRUPLE IS CHEAP AT THE PRICE TELL GRANGER. Decker read the wire twice and observed that the man had even costed his own scruples, and filed it.

The second room was Carbury's office, where Eleanor Alice Granger, widow, citizen, head of her own family in law, sole and entire, swore and signed a preemption declaratory statement in blank—dated, by Carbury's gray-headed advice, the day of her settlement, to be filed the hour the restoration notice posted. The smiling man was in Hays that week. Carbury mentioned it to his plat book.

The third room was no room at all but the open evening on the southeast quarter of section thirteen, where through the long light of late May a house went up, chalk-block, twelve feet by fourteen, one door, one window, set back on the bench above flood line with its window facing the bluff. They worked evenings and Saturdays, the three of them and no hired man: Hollis cutting block in the old quarry seam at the draw mouth, where sawed faces would surprise nobody; Decker laying courses with a hide man's economy; Nell striking the joints and keeping, in the true journal, the record of every evening's work—dates, courses laid, the well begun, the breaking plow hired for one day of turned sod—the diary of improvements that was itself a legal instrument, good faith made of paper, born of the same pen that had documented file marks and tracheal rings.

Pratt's camp watched the road. The road stayed empty. The house rose off the road, behind the shoulder of the draw; the wagonloads of block moved at dusk, on Hollis's own ground, by the cut Decker had graded for the dig's out-haul and graded first, with foresight, on the blind side of the hill. Twice Krug rode the river road with his glass and saw heifers, a homesteader mending fence, and the schoolteacher's mare grazing loose by the seep, her saddle on the fence rail; the third week he did not come, which Decker liked less.

On the first of June, Nell slept the first night under her own roof on the floor on a tick of prairie hay, with the window gray-blue before dawn and the bluff standing in it. Her trunk came out by wagon the next evening, chains, journal, pill box and all.

She was, by the law's lights, a trespasser against a dead railroad, in actual residence, improvements showing, waiting on a gun in Washington.

She was also, she found, standing in her own door at sunup with coffee, eleven years a boarder in other women's houses and one night a settler in hers, unreasonably, unprofessionally, entirely happy.

Chapter 35: The Dig

The powder man from Hays came the second week of June, drilled the cap rock in a line Decker chalked for him, and brought the top of the bluff down in three shots so tidy that Hollis, watching from his fence, allowed that it was the first useful thing he'd ever seen done in this county by a man from Hays.

After the powder, the barrows; after the barrows, the picks; and the quarry settled into its long middle, the part Nell had learned at the gray band and learned now at scale: two hundred tons of overburden going down the spoil chute cart by cart, the bench above the bone band stepping back foot by foot, the August undercut still a rumor. The crew was eight, then ten when the Professor read Decker's progress letters and wired more wage. They camped on Hollis's ground at Hollis's rent, watered at the seep, and were forbidden the river road, the Junction's saloons on weeknights, and the word skull anywhere a stranger might stand, on Decker's standing order, enforced once with a discharge that taught the rest.

The town knew, of course, by July. A town always knows. What it knew was shaped, though, because Decker understood the wire and the human ear alike: the Connecticut crew was on a big lizard up the river—true; bigger than the gray-band one—true; in the bluffs on the rough stretch where the road ran from nothing to nowhere—true to the letter. Nobody's hired ear got the one word that priced it, the paddle in the jaws, because the five men who had seen the gape kept it the way quarrymen keep a pay seam, and the slab face itself stayed under canvas on Decker's order, even at noon, even in drought.

Gus Tilley was caught on the spoil chute side on the last Sunday of July, flat in the bunch grass with a notebook, having crawled the better part of a mile from where his horse stood hid in a plum thicket, and was brought to the cook fire between Lawler and Eccles with grass in his hair and his chin set for whatever came.

What came was Decker, who looked at the notebook—a page of honest sketch, the trench, the canvas, the chute, no skull, for the canvas had held—and handed it back, and said: "You'll stay to supper, Mr. Tilley. Then you'll ride home by the road, like a Christian."

"I'll not say I wasn't sent," Gus said, white but level. "I'll not say who sent me neither."

"Nobody asked you the first and everybody knows the second," Decker said. "Sit. Web, the man's crawled a mile in August; feed him." And to the crew's twelve faces, in the same voice he priced plaster with: "This is Mr. Tilley of the Philadelphia party. He come to see what we've got. What we've got, Mr. Tilley, is a deal of overburden and the best-watered camp in the county, and any man of Dr. Pratt's that wants to see it may ride in by daylight and welcome, for there's nothing under that canvas but bone in the ground, same as his own diggings, and nothing in this craft was ever kept secret past its season anyhow. Tell the doctor we'll trade him water for plaster if his Russell freight's late again. Tell him also"—mild as milk—"the river road washes in a wet August, and a man under that bluff of a night could take harm by pure accident, and I'd grieve it, same as I told him once about another quarry. He'll remember."

Gus ate, and rode home by the road; and the next morning, at the foot of the canvas, the night watch found a tin of Philadelphia tobacco and a paper twist of needles, shoemaker's best, with a note unsigned: For the water. Your seep is the only honest thing in this county. Decker smoked the tobacco and issued the needles, and the war went on, but Nell kept the note in the pill box with the goose rings, in the growing museum of evidence that the men were better than the war.

Chapter 36: Flood

The wet August came. It came the way the dry country does its weeping, all at once, upstream and out of sight: a black-green range of cloud standing over the Colorado line at supper, lightning in it like wires in a wall, not a drop falling on the quarry; and at midnight, under stars, the river began to talk.

Decker had the camp up at the first change in its voice. By one o'clock the Smoky Hill was over its low bars and walking up the spall at the bluff's foot, and the spoil-bank—two hundred tons of chalk rubble graded into a bench that carried the out-haul road—stood between the cut and the current like a dike, which until that night was all it had ever been asked to be.

"If the spoil goes, the river works the cut," Decker said, level, lantern up. "If the river works the cut, it works the undercut line, and the face comes down in the water and we lose the season and maybe the whole of it. Bags, shovels, every man. We hold the bank."

They held the bank. It was work with no craft in it, the first crude labor of the whole dig: chalk rubble and grain sacks and wheelbarrows in the dark, the lanterns hissing in the spray, the river arriving in long pushes that climbed a hand's breadth and fell back a finger's, Hollis appearing out of the night with his team and a wagonload of sandbags from his own seed wheat, sacked that very evening because he had heard the storm to the west and done the arithmetic of his neighbors' weather all his life.

And at half past two, when the bank had begun to lose by inches, lights came down the blind-side cut: eight men of the Philadelphia party with Krug at the front of them, shovels on their shoulders like rifles, Gus Tilley among them soaked to the ribs from the ford.

Krug stood in the lantern light with the water loud behind him and addressed Decker across the whole history of the war, in his sentence-a-wagon way: "Doctor's compliments. Says bones don't have politics. Where do you want us."

"East end," Decker said, no second's pause, "the bank's bleeding at the culvert," and that was the entire negotiation, the truce of weather signed and ratified.

They worked till gray light, two crews braided into one line, passing sacks hand to hand with the rain arriving at last on top of them as if the storm had come to inspect the precaution. Nell worked the line with the rest—Decker had stopped arguing with her about such things a year since—between Eccles and a Philadelphia man whose name she never learned, whose whole conversation across four hours was once, at the worst of it, when the culvert gave and was stopped with three sacks and Lawler's borrowed coat: "She holds."

She held. The river crested at four, stood an hour licking at the patched places like a thing deciding, and went down sulking in the rain. Full day showed the bank gnawed, the road axle-deep in slurry, the canvas on the face untouched, the cut dry. It showed also, ranged along the spoil bank in the gray drizzle, sixteen men of two armies sitting in one row, too far gone to remember which uniform was which, drinking Web Halloran's coffee out of a common pail; and Reverend Mott, who had ridden out at dawn on the news as he rode to fires and confinements, stood on the bank looking down at the row of them a long time.

"I have prayed eleven years for this congregation," he said to Nell, "and it took the flood. The Almighty's methods are scriptural, Mrs. Granger, I'll say that for them. Rain on the just and the unjust, and the unjust holding the just's sandbags." He accepted coffee. "Mind, it won't outlast the mud."

It outlasted the mud by one day exactly. But Nell wrote the row of men into the true journal that night all the same, dated, because the record was the record, and some facts deserved to outlive the war whether the war liked it or not.

Chapter 37: The Collapse

It was the flood that did it, the engineers agreed afterward, the way it is always the flood, or the frost, or the haste—always weather and never the man who reads it; but Decker never once said so, then or after, and Nell knew why: he had walked the face that morning, seen the seep line darken along the old frost seam above the undercut, and given the order that saved every life under it—clear the cut, bar the east end, nobody below the band till she dries—and then gone down himself with Gus Tilley, on loan a week now with Pratt's blessing while the Philadelphia camp waited out the mud, to fetch the recording barometer and the tray of small bones lying nearest the lip.

The face spoke once—a tick, Nell said in the journal, like the bluff cooling at night, but at ten in the morning—and Decker had Gus by the collar and was two strides into the run when the seam opened.

It was not the whole face. The whole face would have been a burial. It was the slab over the east end of the undercut, forty tons of it, and it came down not as a wall but as a shrug, breaking on the barred cribbing Decker himself had set after the flood, and the cribbing turned the worst of it outward into the cut, and the rest took the two men off their feet and sealed them under a tent of leaning slab and timber in a space the size of a wagon bed.

The crew came off the bench like a hatch knocked open. And into the white dust and the shouting walked Nell Granger with her sheets in her hand, because she had drawn that face stone by stone for three months, and she was the only soul on the ground who knew the architecture of what had fallen as a fact rather than a fear.

"Quiet," she said, and got it. "Decker. Knock if you hear."

Three knocks, slow, from under the east tent of slab. Then five quick, which was Decker for two alive.

"The big slab's bedded on the crib and the chute timbers," she said to Lawler, sheets down on the rubble, finger moving. "It can't slide east; the crib's took it. It wants to rotate west, here, where the seam runs. Nobody digs at the west toe. We come in from the chute side, through the spoil, under the sound timber—here. It's nine feet of loose rubble and then the void. Single file of buckets, no bars, no jacks till we're under the sound piece, and no man on the slab top for any reason, I don't care what he weighs."

It took four hours. She called the line of the bore foot by foot off the sheets, and the men passed buckets like a fire brigade, and at the third hour Web Halloran began very quietly to sing at the rubble face, not for music but so the men inside would have the direction of them, and at the fourth a bucket went back full and the next came back empty with Decker's voice behind it, dust-choked, conversational: "Mind the chute timber overhead. She's earning her wage."

Gus Tilley came out first, dragged flat on a plank, his left leg broken below the knee and splinted underground in the dark by Decker with a barrow stave and Decker's belt. Decker came out last, on his own feet because he had decided to, gray to the lips, three ribs gone and a gash above the ear, and stood swaying in the white sunlight counting his crew like a man counting change, and finding the count good, sat down on the rubble with great dignity and fainted.

The doctor came out from the Junction by buggy. Mott came with him. Pratt came at sundown, alone, having ridden at a gallop on the news, and stood over Gus's cot a long while, and then went out to the rubble where Nell's sheets still lay weighted with chalk, and looked at the drawn face and the bore line penciled through the spoil, and said nothing at all in any direction, which from Lucius Pratt was the profoundest tribute in his power.

The skull, under its canvas at the west end, had not lost so much as a flake.

Chapter 38: The Slab Rises

They came out of the August like survivors of a small war, which Nell supposed was the literal case, and the dig changed character: no more secrecy worth keeping—the collapse had been in the Hays paper, and the Hays paper in the eastern exchanges—and a deadline now visible to every man on the bench, for the frost that had calved the bluff twice would be back making its third survey by November.

So Decker, rising off his cot against the doctor's program and lashed into a corset of plaster bandage by Anschutz's own mail-order instructions, did what the trade had never yet seen done. He jacketed the whole death scene as one piece.

Not the skull alone; the skull, the jaws, the paddle in the jaws, and the first nine feet of neck behind, one slab, the sea floor's own sentence kept in its own grammar. The undercutting alone took three weeks, tunnel by tunnel, each tunnel timbered and plastered before the next was opened, until the great block stood on eleven chalk pillars like a table on its legs; then the strapping, oak and iron, bent and bolted by the Junction's blacksmith, who came out an atheist toward bones and went home a tithing man; then the cribbing and the screw jacks, and the pillars cut away one by one on a schedule Nell drew and posted like a hymn board, until forty-one days after the collapse, the largest plaster jacket in the history of the science—nine tons with its cradle, Decker's books said—hung free on the jacks with daylight under it for the first time in sixty million years.

The hauling took four days. Hollis's road, Decker's grades, sixteen oxen hired from the freight contractor at Hays, and a wagon built double under it like a gun carriage; and on the third day, where the road crossed the open bench above the river, the town came out to see it move.

They came in wagons and on foot, the Junction and the claims both, two hundred souls or near it standing along the grade in the September light; and there was no holiday in it, Nell marked, no fiddler, none of the railroad-celebration noise the town put on for ordinary wonders. They stood the way people stood at the big funerals and the church raisings, hats in hands, children quiet, while nine tons of sea floor came up the grade behind sixteen oxen at the pace of a tide, white as a sail, broad as a granary door, with SE 1/4 SEC 13 stenciled on the cradle timbers in Decker's hand, because the foreman recorded provenance even on the artillery.

Mott stood at the crest with Nell. He had buried two men that summer, married three couples, and held the county's hand through the forfeiture rumors, and he watched the slab breast the rise with an expression she had stopped trying to name.

"I preached on Job last Sunday," he said. "Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook? I had thought it was a question expecting the answer no." The oxen leaned; the slab came on. "I am going to have to write a different sermon, Mrs. Granger. Not the one the lyceum men want, mind. The beast is real and the Lord's question stands; it only seems the answer is: not with an hook, no. With a neighbor's road, and a surveyor's widow, and four hundred dollars of plaster, and every soul in the county pulling the same direction once in its life. I can preach that. I can preach that to a full house."

The slab came to rest that evening in the one building in the county with a floor to bear it: Hollis's stone barn, swept bare, its doors widened by the blacksmith in an afternoon, a stove set in it for the watch. The skull faced the door. Hollis stood in the doorway a long time that night, lantern up, looking at what his hailed-out, twice-struck, wife-emptied quarter-section had been keeping for him underneath itself all the years he had cursed it.

"First folks," he said finally, and hung the lantern on its nail like a man closing up a church.

Chapter 39: Pratt's Move

The preliminary notice was datelined Philadelphia, October the ninth, and it ran two and a quarter pages in the Proceedings, and it reached Monument Junction in the same mail sack as a letter from Brandt that had crossed it on the way west, like a warning crossing a shot.

ON A COLOSSAL PYTHONOMORPH FROM THE NIOBRARA OF KANSAS, WITH EVIDENCE OF A NEW TYPE OF HIND LIMB. By Lucius Pratt. The text was Pratt entire: brilliant, breathless, generous in every direction the truth permitted and several it didn't. A skull surpassing nine feet; a new genus, instituted with apologies for haste upon materials of observation but not yet of possession—he had named it, the Latin meaning lord of the chalk sea—and the diagnosis, the heart of the thing, the character that justified a new genus on two pages of hearsay: a hind paddle of plesiosaurian construction, five-fingered, broad as a door, preserved at the anterior end of the specimen, dislocated forward by the death-throes—proof, Pratt argued in a paragraph already audibly out of breath, that the great mosasaurs retained paired swimming limbs of the ancient pattern, that the pythonomorphs were no serpents of the sea but the plesiosaurs' own cousins armored in a new age—

Nell read it standing at the depot, and sat down on the baggage truck, and read it again.

The spy sketches had been good. Krug's glass, the teamster gossip, Gus's honest page—whichever combination had reached Philadelphia, it had carried the shape of the gape truly: the great skull, and lying at it, fanned, five-fingered, unmistakably plesiosaurian, a paddle. And Pratt, nine hundred miles from the canvas, mortgaged to his lithographers, hearing Connecticut's oxen in his sleep, had done at forty-two what he had done at thirty-four: assembled the animal in the dark by the lamp of his own brilliance, and put the wrong bones on it, and signed.

The paddle of his new genus, the diagnosis of lord of the chalk sea, the evidence of a new type of hind limb, was the dinner.

"He's named the lizard for the leg of the lamb," said Decker, when she brought it to camp—wincing as he laughed, the ribs only half-knit. Then, sobering fast, the foreman's ledger opening: "Which means he's named nothing—the name's hung on a character the beast ain't got—but no man east of the river knows that, and the name's loose in the world with a date on it, and the Professor—" He stopped. He folded the Proceedings with the care he gave evidence. "The Professor will be on the next cars west, or his lawyers will. Pratt's forced the season, Mrs. Granger. Title or no title, gun or no gun, Connecticut has to answer in print now or watch ten years of Kansas wear Philadelphia's collar. He'll want the answer published yesterday and he'll want it built out of your sheets."

Brandt's crossed letter, opened that night at her own table in the chalk-block house, was four lines.

Madam. P. has heard oxen and lost his head, as I feared when the Hays dispatches ran. Whatever he prints, print nothing in reply, sign nothing of O.'s reply, and above all convey no plate, no tracing, no measurement to any hand until the ground question is settled. The man who owns the figures owns the argument. The woman who owns the ground owns the figures. Stand on the ground. E.B.

Chapter 40: The Telegraph War

The Professor did not come west. What came west, in the ten days after Pratt's notice, was paper: and Nell, standing in the eye of it, learned more of the trade's true machinery in those ten days than in the three years prior.

Ordway's answer was in type within a week—a communication to the rival journal, cold as a coroner's docket, deposing that the Philadelphia notice rested on no examination of the specimen; that the specimen was excavated by the Museum's party under legal contract; that the so-called hind limb was, upon the testimony of the only scientific records made in place, the fore-paddle of a plesiosaur held in the jaws—prey, not anatomy; and that the true description, with figures, would issue from the Museum in due season, the priority of the empty Philadelphia name being a question for the societies, which have dealt with the gentleman's restorations before. Every clause of it was true, and the truest part was the engine under it: upon the testimony of the only scientific records made in place. He had deposed her journal and her sheets into the argument—sight unseen, unconveyed, still locked in a trunk in a chalk-block house he did not know existed—because he knew they existed, and knew the war was unwinnable without them, and reckoned them his the way the card in the gallery reckoned the paddle: collected by the Museum's party.

The wires ran hot. Finch's key clattered through the days like weather. Reporters' queries came addressed to the Connecticut camp, to the Philadelphia camp, to the postmaster, to the famous skull itself—one envelope ran, in earnest, TO THE GREAT SEA SERPENT, MONUMENT JUNCTION, KANSAS, and Web Halloran proposed it be answered in character and was overruled. The smiling man Selby appeared on the river road twice with new companions in city coats. And Decker, reading the whole board the way he read ground, made his one move of the ten days: he walked into the depot, laid a message on Finch's counter, and paid for it in front of witnesses—a wire to the Museum stating that the specimen would be crated for eastern shipment the moment the out-haul road dried from the October rains, say three weeks.

The road was dry as a bone. Decker had hauled freight over it that morning.

"There's a kind of fish you take with a lantern," he said to Nell on the ride out, serene. "Whatever wire Finch sells, and whoever buys it, every man in this war now believes he's got three weeks, and the one thing nobody'll do for three weeks is anything sudden. We'll know the buyers by the watchers on the road by Friday." By Friday the road had three watchers, one of them an attorney's clerk from Hays who had never sat a horse before that week, and Decker counted them off Hollis's ridge through Martha's glass like a man counting his own poultry.

And on the Monday following, the gun fired.

GENERAL LAND OFFICE — NOTICE OF RESTORATION, the posted bill at Carbury's door began. The forfeiture had passed the Senate in the summer recess's last hour; the President had signed in September; the Commissioner's notice, grinding west through the machinery at the speed of government, fixed the date the lands of the late Smoky Hill & Denver grant within the Hays district should be restored to the public domain and opened to entry: November the fourth, at nine o'clock in the forenoon.

Eleven days. Nell read the bill in the cold sunlight with the town gathering at her back, rode home to the quarter, and took the true journal out of the trunk; and that night, by lamp, at her own table, under sixty feet of chalk and one window's worth of stars, she wrote out in her surveyor's print, slow and fair, the document she had been three years becoming the only person alive able to write: the history of one specimen, in place, dated, measured, witnessed — discovery to slab — and signed it, full name, no initials, the way you sign what you will stand to in court.

Whoever held the ground on the fourth of November would hold the rest. She blew out the lamp and slept like the chalk.
Chapter 41: What the Journal Holds

She was at the land office door at half past four in the morning of the fourth of November, in the dark and a thin freezing mist, and she was not first.

First was a man she had never seen, asleep sitting up against the door jamb with a carpetbag, who proved when the line woke to be a locator's stand-in, paid to hold a place; and behind Nell by ten minutes came Selby, the smiling man, smiling, with the Hays attorney and a clerk carrying a japanned deed box like a pall bearer. By seven the line ran down the steps and along the front of the building, thirty entries' worth of cold breath, claim by claim across two townships of restored checkerboard, and Hollis arrived from the wagon yard with coffee in a syrup pail and stood beside Nell against the wall, shotgun left home, jaw set for court.

At nine, Carbury opened the door, and government began.

The stand-in offered his declaratory statement on the southeast quarter of section thirteen at six minutes past nine. Carbury read it, laid it flat, and asked the man the questions the law gave him: whether he was settled upon the tract, and the nature of his improvements. The stand-in recited a tent and intended breaking. Carbury wrote it down. Then Selby's attorney came forward, not for the stand-in but over him, and offered papers of his own — a contest, prepared in advance, alleging that the tract's known mineral and scientific character excepted it from agricultural entry, with affidavits, and the room got the particular stillness of money showing itself.

"Filed and noted," Carbury said, unexcited, as he had been unexcited since Buchanan. "Next."

Nell stepped to the counter and laid down the declaratory statement sworn in May, the diary of improvements copied fair, and the certificate of the county surveyor — Thomas's successor, a young man who owed his chains and his transit to a widow's auction and had measured her house, well, and breaking on the first of November for a fee of one dollar, entered in his book. House of stone, twelve by fourteen, shingle roof, glazed window. Well, sixteen feet, cased. Four acres broke. In actual residence since the first of June.

Carbury read it through, every line, as if he had never ridden past the house himself on a gray afternoon and noted the smoke. Then he stamped it, and the stamp going down sounded no different than any of the morning's other stamps, which was, Nell thought, the whole dignity of the thing: the gun had fired the same for everyone, and the race had been run in June evenings, in courses of sawed chalk, a season before the others knew there was a race.

"Settlement holds the preference," Carbury said to the room at large, to the attorney without looking at him. "Contest's noted and will take its course. The course"—he squared the papers—"runs uphill. Next."

The course ran uphill eleven weeks and died in January, when the Commissioner's office ruled what every land lawyer west of Topeka had told Selby's principals it would rule: that bones in the ground were neither mineral within the meaning of the statute nor any bar known to law against a widow's preemption; science, the ruling observed in the one sentence Brandt later had framed, is not an encumbrance upon title.

She made cash proof inside the time fixed for restored lands, in February: four hundred dollars in gold and certificates at the receiver's window, two seasons' wages and most of a third, counted twice. The receipt came over the counter and the southeast quarter of section thirteen, township fourteen, range twenty-six — one hundred sixty acres of buffalo grass, one chalk-block house, one cased well, and the grave-scar of the grandest quarry in the Niobrara — passed to Eleanor Alice Granger, her heirs and assigns, forever.

Brandt's letter, when she wrote him the news, crossed the prairies in eight days and said: Now the ground is yours to stand on, madam. Stand. They will both come to you within the month, and they will both come for the same thing, whatever name they give it. It is no longer the slab; the slab is freight. It is the journal. The journal is the specimen now. Guard it as you would have guarded the bluff, and price it in the only coin you have ever asked: the name.

Chapter 42: Ordway's Price

The Professor's letter requested, in nine lines, the pleasure of an interview at the Junction's hotel parlor, the Museum having business of consequence to lay before Mrs. Granger; and the date proposed was four days out, which meant he was already on the cars when it was mailed.

He came alone. No lawyer, no secretary; gray broadcloth, the buffalo coat over his arm like a pelt he had personally subdued; and he stood at the parlor window while the proposition was made, his back half-turned, addressing the street in his just-above-a-whisper, so that even now, even here, the listener did the work of coming to him.

The proposition was, as Brandt had wired, everything.

"The memoir will issue over two names," Ordway said. "Mine and yours. Granger and Ordway — yours leading, madam; the field name leads; Philadelphia preaches that doctrine and never practices it, and I shall enjoy practicing it once for the pure discomfort it will cause him. The plates carry your name as delineator and discoverer both. The text states the circumstances of the discovery in your words, over your initials — your name." A small pause, exactly the width of the thing he was about to ask. "In return, the Museum's archive must be complete. The field journal — the true journal, Mrs. Granger; my foreman's reports have made me acquainted with your methods for some years — accessions to the Museum, with your plates, the fair copies, the whole record. Property of the institution. Available to scholars in perpetuity. Bound in buckram, if you like, with your name on the spine. The Museum is not sentimental, but it is permanent. Nothing of yours would ever again depend on the courtesy of any living man, mine included. That is the offer entire. It is the best offer I have made any person in my life."

And the terrible thing, Nell thought, standing with her hands folded, watching the gray profile against the window light, was that it was. He was not haggling. He had come a thousand miles to pay full price, and the price he understood himself to be paying was immortality, institutional, brownstone, fireproofed — the only heaven he believed in, offered with both small hands.

"And the slab, Professor?"

"Freight," he said, as Brandt had said. "The slab goes east, of course. The hall is building for it now; I have the elevation in my bag. It will stand under skylights in the finest gallery in America with your county's name cut in the architrave, and forty thousand souls a year will walk past it. Kansas may have casts." The lids came around at last, the whole attention arriving on her like the glare off the chalk. "You are about to speak to me of the schoolchildren of this district, Mrs. Granger. Spare us both. Children require a future; this state cannot yet floor a building to bear nine tons; and a specimen of this consequence belongs where the science is, and the science, madam, is where the money keeps it. You know this. You have wintered in my basement. You have seen what becomes of bones that stay where the money isn't."

"I have," Nell said. "I spent a winter cataloguing it. Kansas sixty-eight. Kansas sixty-nine. Wyoming seventy. Nailed shut, Professor, the lids gray, the hay coming out at the seams. I have seen the finest gallery in America, and I have seen the cellar it stands on, and I have watched four men and a Bavarian fight the arithmetic and lose. Your museum is not a heaven, sir. It is a harbor with one pier and a thousand ships standing off, and you are out here this morning bidding for one more cargo you will not live to unload."

The silence had a different grain than his usual silences. She had surprised him, she saw; not the refusal — he had discounted refusal into the price before he boarded the cars — but the ground of it.

"The journal is not for sale," she said. "Nor for accessioning, nor binding, nor any permanence but mine. The description will publish in the spring, with the plates, over the name Eleanor Alice Granger — alone, Professor. Not leading. Alone. The Museum's costs will be repaid to the dollar; the subscription is half-raised now. The Museum will have the first casts made in America, of the whole slab, at Kansas's expense, and my plates' published figures free to its scholars like any other man's, forever. That is what is for sale, and it is the best offer I have made any person in my life."

He looked at her a long moment, and then he did the thing she told Decker of afterward and Decker refused two days to believe: he laughed. Once; a short, rusty, astonished sound, like a hinge asked to work after thirty years.

"You learned the trade," he said. He took up the buffalo coat. At the door, hat in hand, the frost-on-glass smile, second sighting in a lifetime: "My lawyers will write you, madam. It will come to nothing; I knew the title question was lost when I read the Commissioner's ruling; I came to buy before the suit because suing first spoils a market. Note the sequence in your journal. It is the only apology I have ever paid anyone, and it is, of course"—he settled the hat—"off the record."

Chapter 43: Pratt's Confession

Pratt came the following week, and he came, being Pratt, in the worst weather available, through the first hard snow of the winter, on a livery horse with his collar up, so that the knock at her own door — the chalk-block house, the lamp lit, the stove ticking — opened on a snow-caked apparition stamping like a cab horse, and the first words of the interview were hers: "Doctor, the fire's there, and the coffee's there, and the speech you've prepared will keep until you can feel your hands."

He stood at her stove and dripped, and looked about him while he thawed — at the one room, the trunk, the shelf with Owen and the pill box and Martha's second jar of stone curls, Hollis's house-warming; at the table where the true journal lay closed beside her working sheets; and the lecture-hall surfaces, she watched them, went down one by one like lamps being put out along a street, until what stood at her stove was what had stood in the boardinghouse parlor in Connecticut: a man of three-and-forty now, mortgaged past his eyes, with the wound showing.

"I haven't come for the animal," he said at last. "Brandt will have told you. He tells everyone everything; it's how he launders the profession's linen; God preserve him." He turned the coffee cup in his hands. "The notice of October ninth. The hind limb. You hold the proofs of what it is — what it was. The dinner, madam. I named a genus on the contents of the brute's mouth."

"You named it on a spy's sketch of the contents of his mouth," Nell said, "which is a stage worse."

"Yes," Pratt said simply. "It is the 'sixty-nine business again, and this time without even a surgeon's bad notes for cover — haste, debt, and the sound of another man's oxen; those were my materials of observation." He set the cup down. "Here is what I rode through snow to say. When your description publishes — oh, the whole trade knows it's yours now; the societies talk of nothing else; the bets in Philadelphia run three to one you'll be refused by the journals and five to two that he buys you first — when it publishes, my name of October falls. It must. I ask one thing of the manner of its falling." He took a breath like a man stepping off a ledge. "Don't spare me. Cite the notice. Figure the paddle in the jaws so no eye on earth can mistake it, state plainly what the so-called limb is, and retire my genus with the full rites — page, date, and demonstration. No charity, madam. No silence. Silence is what colleagues do for each other's blunders, and the silence becomes a debt, and the debt becomes a leash. I have been on his leash since 'sixty-nine, not for the error — errors are human — but because the world was let laugh at it and I was let stand there while it laughed, owing. I'll not owe you. Correct me in print, clean, with the bones on the table, and I am a free man. You'll have done me the first kindness this trade has shown me in ten years, and it will look to the whole world like a blow."

The stove ticked. The snow went at the window, fine and dry, the sound of sand.

"You rode out in this," Nell said slowly, "to ask to be corrected."

"I rode out in this," Pratt said, "because it is the only request I have ever made in my professional life that I am not ashamed of, and I wanted to make it standing up, in person, before my nerve went. Also"—a sudden ordinary grin, the church-hall article, entirely real—"because Krug refuses to carry messages to you anymore. He says you and Decker and the homesteader won the war and the rest is funerals. Krug is the best judge of terrain I ever employed."

She gave him supper before she sent him back — biscuit and side meat and the last of the dried-apple pie, eaten with the appetite of a man whose debts had not lately left room for dinner; and at the door, muffled to the eyes, he turned once more, and the snow blew in around him.

"Marthae," he said. "The species. Tilley let it slip; the boy keeps nothing from anyone he loves, which is everyone. For a homesteader's wife." He shook his head slowly. "He'd have named it for a trustee, and I'd have named it for myself, madam. We were neither of us ever going to deserve that animal." And he went up into the snow and was gone, and Nell stood in the cold doorway a moment, adding the visit to the museum of evidence, the file three years thick now, that the men were better than the war; and thinking that this one, at least, had found his way clear of it at last by the only gate that had ever stood open: the truth, told plain, signed.

Chapter 44: The Third Way

The Kansas proposition had begun, like most large things in that country, at a kitchen table — Brandt's letters, the chancellor of the young state university, a Topeka senator who had seen the slab cross the bench behind sixteen oxen and never quite got over it — and by February it stood on paper, and the paper said:

A hall. Stone, one story, floored to bear twenty tons, built at Monument Junction — not at the university, and that clause had cost the chancellor a night's sleep and won him, Brandt wrote, the only fight worth winning: the specimen stays in the county that bore it; the students can ride the cars; the farmers cannot. A state appropriation, thin as March ice, for the floor and the roof. A county subscription for the rest — Teal's counter, Mrs. Bly's parlor, the church socials, the Idle Hour's fiddler playing benefits, a dollar at a time, a county buying its own dead back from the deep. The Museum of the West, the bill called it; the town called it the Bone House and would forever; and its first keeper, at a salary that made the Connecticut bench look princely — Curator and Preparator, E. A. Granger.

"It's thin," Nell told Decker, the two of them walking the staked foundation lines in a thaw, mud to the ankles. "The salary's thin, the appropriation's thin, the legislature could starve it any session it pleases. Ordway's right about the money; the science lives where the money keeps it. I'd be trading the best bench in America, twice offered, for a stone shed at a railroad junction and a subscription list."

"You would," Decker agreed, pacing off the west wall. "And the shed'd have the slab, the journal, the title, and you, all four under one roof of your own choosing, answerable to a board of Kansans you can out-sit and out-wait and out-document till Judgment." He stopped at the corner stake. "I hauled hides eight seasons, Mrs. Granger. I know what it is to strip a country and ship it east and leave nothing standing on the grass but the smell. Then I dug bones nine seasons for a man who done the same thing politer. The whole West goes out of the West in crates; it's the one crop this country's never once failed. The first thing in my lifetime that ever stayed"—he nodded across the mud, at the bluff line white on the horizon—"is forty foot of dead lizard, and it stayed because one schoolteacher held her tongue for two years and built a house of an evening. You'll take the shed."

"I'll take the shed," Nell said. "I took it three weeks ago. I'm only rehearsing the regret so it'll be wore out before the work starts. You've quit him, then. It's sure?"

"Wrote it at the pay table, New Year's. One line. He wrote back one line: my character, and the sentence 'You were the only thing in Kansas I never overpaid for.'" Decker turned the corner stake with his boot toe, thoughtful. "From him that's a wreath. I'm hired to this county now — mason first, till the hall stands. After, the board wants a collector kept in the field, seems a museum's a hole you shovel at from both ends forever." He squinted at the staked rectangle, the mud, the thin March sun. "Cathedral," he said, trying it; and then, in the tone of a man correcting a key: "Grain house. For the kind of grain this ground grows." He walked on down the wall line, calling footings, and Nell came behind him with the level, and the regret, rehearsed twice more that afternoon, wore out on schedule by the time the frost was off the stakes.

Chapter 45: Breaking with Ordway

The lawyers' letter came in March, as promised, on paper so heavy it stood up in the wind: the Museum's claim to the specimen by virtue of contract and expenditure, replevin threatened, the courtesy of a reply within thirty days.

The reply went back in fourteen, from a Topeka firm engaged by the university's board, and it was four pages of the quiet kind of law: the excavation contract, drawn on the Museum's own printed form, conveying access, rents, and royalty, and silent as the chalk on title to specimens; the bones severed from land which at the hour of severance belonged to no contracting party but lay in the trust of the United States; the only continuous record of the specimen's identity, position, and chain of custody being the private journal of a private citizen, available to the court entire — and the closing paragraph, which Nell drafted herself and the Topeka men polished but could not improve: The Museum's expenditures, with interest, stand tendered in full as of this date, in certified funds, declined twice by the claimant. The defendant invites the suit. The defendant's records are in order, and the defendant's witnesses include every adult resident of a Kansas county. A jury may be had at Hays in the spring term.

No suit was filed. What was filed, in the April number of the Museum's own Bulletin, was the footnote war's opening gun — but that was April's trouble; March's trouble came in person, the second week, when the pay agent arrived at Cedar Draw to close out the camp's accounts and collect the Museum's movable property, with an inventory, and at the foot of the inventory, in the small upright hand itself: Item. Plaster, in store, est. 30 bbl. Item. Field plates, sheets 1 through 214, the season of 1878, property of the Museum per terms of engagement. All sheets. All seasons.

It was lawful. It had always been lawful; drawings made in service convey; she had said it herself at Brandt's table. The agent was apologetic and thorough, and Nell stood in the cook tent while three years of her hand went into a japanned case — the gray-band skull, the throat sheet's fair twin, the paddle that had been her apprenticeship — every original she had drawn on the Museum's dollar, leaving her the copies her own lamp-oil had bought, leaving her the journal, leaving her, as the case latched, with the exact shape of the cage Ordway had once offered to gild, palpable at last in the empty portfolio.

"He empties the West politer," she said to Decker, who had come to stand the inventory with her, and her voice came out level, which she was glad of.

"He does," Decker said. "And he's just made the same mistake with you he made with the buffalo men. He thinks the hide's the animal." He picked up the empty portfolio and handed it to her like a hat after a wind. "The figures for your memoir, now. They'll want drawing fresh, off the slab itself, at full scale, in a warm room, by a woman with title to her own evenings. I've seen your field sheets, Mrs. Granger, three years of them, drawn on cracker boxes in the glare with the grit blowing. I'm bound to say"—and the grin came, the second sighting, like spring—"the Museum's case just bought itself the worst of that bargain."

Chapter 46: The Description

She drew all spring and wrote all summer, in the north light of the half-built hall, on a trestle table Decker knocked together from cribbing timber, with the slab before her like a text and the journal open beside her like a concordance.

The drawing came first and the drawing was the joy: the great skull at one-quarter scale and its every suture at full; the teeth in their files, marginal and pterygoid both, the ratchet of the palate figured so plainly that the church-hall question of 'seventy-six answered itself off the plate; the paddle in the jaws — the plate of plates, the one the bets in Philadelphia had been about — drawn bone by bone, the five fanned files of the long-neck's rowing hand foreshortened in the grip of the jaws that had taken it, with the bite-scored humerus lettered and the score marks matched, tooth to groove, to the very files of the marginal row, predation proved out of the stone like a sum. And the throat. Sheet thirty-one reborn at full scale off the slab's own evidence: the train of cartilage half-rings lying displaced below the jaws, keyed in her own letters at last and forever — tracheal rings; cf. the throat of recent Crocodilus — with a quiet supplementary figure beside it, drawn from a pill box: the windpipe of the domestic goose, for comparison, del. E. A. Granger.

The writing came harder. She was schooled in plats and field notes, not in the architecture of a memoir, and the first drafts read, she told Brandt by mail, like a surveyor deposing against the Cretaceous. Brandt's answers came every eight days, a correspondence course in the courtesies of the craft conducted from a house full of bones: State the locality first and your debt to every hand, named, that worked it. Diagnosis before description; description before discussion; the reader forgives any dullness but disorder. Where you correct P., cite the notice, figure the proof, and append not one adjective; the figure is the blow, and adjectives only bruise your own knuckles. Where the fringe falls — and it falls, madam, your Plate IX fells it — name no one. Print the rings, cite the prior restorations 'of authors,' and let the genus of authors die of natural causes. The trade will know. The trade always knows. That is what the trade is.

The name she had carried two years, and the naming paragraph she wrote in one sitting, in September, with the door of the hall standing open on the cured grass and the masons' trowels ringing on the last courses of the west wall, and read aloud once to the empty room and the slab before she let it stand:

The species is referred to the genus Tylosaurus, and is named Tylosaurus marthae, for Martha Hollis, late of this county, who gathered the shells of the Niobrara chalk from her dooryard and called them the first folks of Kansas; upon whose husband's land the approaches to the quarry lay; and in whose memory the specimen is to stand, by covenant of the trustees, in the county where it lived and died.

Hollis, shown the paragraph at his own kitchen table because she would not have him read it first in a journal, sat over it a long while in the lamplight, his thumb moving once along the printed proof of the name as along a sawed joint, testing.

"She'd have laughed," he said at last. "A lizard. Forty foot of lizard wearing her Sunday name." He folded the proof with the exactness he gave all paper. "Then she'd have cried. Then she'd have asked you in to dinner and showed you the jar." He stood, and went to the shelf, and took down the older jar, Martha's own, the stone curls dim with kitchen years, and set it in Nell's hands like a christening. "For the case in the hall," he said. "By the big one. First folks alongside first folks. She'd want her things where her name's at."

Chapter 47: The Reading

The memoir was read before the Academy in Philadelphia on the eleventh of December, by Dr. Emil Brandt, emeritus, the author being detained in Kansas by the duties of her museum — which was the trade's polite fiction, agreed among all parties; the plainer truth, which the trade also knew, being that the author was a woman, and the Academy's platform had never yet borne one, and Brandt had judged the memoir's first night no occasion to fight that war and Nell had judged the same, having a war's worth of fights at home and the only platform she wanted already floored to bear twenty tons.

So she had the night of the eleventh at the Bone House instead, with the lamps lit.

It was Decker's notion, put up to the board as economy: the hall wanted a trial of its stove and lamps before any public opening, and the trustees, the subscribers, the crews — both crews, the wire to Philadelphia had said, any man of either party in the county that week — might as well sit in the warm for it. They came through a light snow, wagons and saddle horses, forty souls: Hollis in his wedding-and-funeral coat; Mott; Mrs. Bly with two hampers; Carbury; Miss Fitch and the big Sorensen boy, who was reading law at Hays now and had ridden down; Lawler, Eccles, Halloran; Krug, in the county that week by arrangement nobody examined; Gus Tilley on his cane. They sat on subscription chairs between the slab and the stove, and at eight o'clock by the depot's time — nine in Philadelphia; Brandt would be rising to the desk — Nell stood up before the slab with the page proofs in her hand and read her memoir to Monument Junction, Kansas, while a thousand miles east an old man read it to the learned world.

She read them the locality and the debts, every hand named, and the room sat listening to its own three years told back to it in measured prose, the storm, the freeze-up, the road, the flood, the collapse, the slab; and when she came to the diagnosis the farmers took out, unembarrassed, the spectacles of people who mean to follow the argument.

What happened in Philadelphia she had from three letters, which agreed in substance and differed only as witnesses honestly differ about lightning.

Brandt read the description entire, the plates standing on easels — her plates, lithographed in Boston, the Hartford sieve being judged unsuitable for this particular cargo. At Plate VI, the paddle in the jaws, the hall's murmur was general and sustained. At Plate IX, the throat, it stopped altogether; the fringe restorations of authors stood corrected in fourteen lines of text and one small supplementary goose, and in the third row a gray, heavy-lidded man sat without motion through the falling of his rooster, and was watched doing it by two hundred colleagues pretending to watch the easel.

And at the discussion of the pythonomorph affinities — at the retirement, page and date cited, figure demonstrated, of the genus of October ninth, with not one adjective appended — Lucius Pratt stood up out of the fourth row before the chairman could speak, was recognized, and said (this part the three letters had word for word, having all written it down on their programs in the moment): "Mr. Chairman, I rise to second a funeral. The genus dies of evidence supplied by Mrs. Granger's plates, and I tender the corpse my compliments. Gentlemen, I have twice in my career assembled an animal in the dark and been corrected by daylight. The first correction was made in the manner this body remembers, and it cost me ten years and the corrector more. The second stands tonight in figures so honest that error has nowhere to sit down in their presence. That is what the craft is for. I move the memoir be received with the thanks of the Academy, and I will tell any man my odds were always on the lady."

The motion, Brandt wrote, carried with applause; the Professor's hands were observed to move three times, deliberately, which witnesses unacquainted with the climate took for faint praise and the rest knew for thaw at the pole; and the chairman, closing, misspoke the author's name as "E. A. Granger, Esquire," and corrected himself — "Mrs. Eleanor Granger, of Kansas" — and the second version went into the minutes, where it stands.

In the Bone House the stove ticked and the snow brushed the dark windows, and Nell finished the naming paragraph and looked up from the proofs at her town, and her town looked back; and Dane Hollis, in the front row in his terrible coat, nodded once, the way you nod at a true corner staked.

Chapter 48: Ordway's Footnote

The rebuttal came in the spring number of the Museum's Bulletin, eleven pages, On the Alleged Tracheal Apparatus of the Pythonomorpha, with Remarks on Certain Recent Determinations, and it was the Professor at his coldest and most exact: seven points of dissent, four of them trivial, two of them arguable, and one — a matter of the count of cervical vertebrae, where crushing made the suture genuinely doubtful — flatly correct, as Nell acknowledged in a four-line note in the autumn journals with a courtesy so plain it could not be told from mockery and was neither.

The trade read the eleven pages as the trade reads everything, for position; and the position, the exchanges agreed, was a garrison firing from a fort already bypassed. The fringe was not reasserted. The fringe was not mentioned. The genus of half-hoops had died of natural causes, as Brandt had promised, mourned only by a footnote observing that restorations are provisional in the nature of the science — which sentence, Pratt wrote to Nell with relish, is the nearest thing to a white flag in thirty years of the man's collected works; frame it.

What the spring also brought, in the eastern papers first and the Kansas exchanges a week behind, was the cellar.

A trustees' committee, appointed after a donor's estate inquired too precisely into the disposition of a funded expedition's results, had gone down into the brownstone basement with lanterns and a clerk, and the clerk's inventory had walked out the door within the month, as inventories do: upward of nineteen hundred crates unopened, some fourteen years in store; specimens of record cited in published papers and never since located; the type of one famous name found at last under three seasons of Wyoming in a stairwell. THE BURIED MUSEUM, the New York papers ran it, and worse; the gift of the dead sea swallowed twice, ran the unkindest, once by Kansas and once by Connecticut. The Professor met it as he met everything, in print, with figures — accessions outrunning preparation being the common arithmetic of every great collection in Europe, which was true, and known to every museum man alive, and no help at all once the lanterns had been down the stairs.

Nell read the long dispatches at her trestle table in the Bone House and found, examining herself, no satisfaction anywhere in reach. She had wintered at that bench. She knew the four men and the Bavarian by name, knew the arithmetic was real and the war had made it — every crate nailed in haste against a rival's season, every dollar that might have hired preparators spent on telegrams and teamsters and the buying of glory at the railhead. Two of the best eyes in the world, Decker had said at the cook fire, years back, and the only use either could think to put his own eye to was reading the other man's mistakes; and here at last was the sum of that ledger, drawn by clerks, published by inches, and the whole trade poorer for the proving.

"It bought one thing," Decker said, when she said as much to him on the hall steps in the spring evening. He nodded in at the lamplit door, the slab, the cases, the jar of stone curls on its shelf by the big one. "Every museum man in America's reading them dispatches tonight and thinking of his own cellar. And forty year from now, when they've all built their grain houses small and near the ground and open the crates the same season they nail them, nobody'll remember it was a schoolteacher and a hide man and a hailed-out homesteader showed them how. That's the footnote we get, Mrs. Granger." He settled his hat, entirely content. "Unsigned. Like the best work in this country always was."

Chapter 49: The Hall Opens

The Museum of the West opened to the public on the seventh of June, at ten in the forenoon, with the Junction band playing in front of the stone porch and Mrs. Bly's tables under the cottonwoods doing a business that settled the institution's coal bill for the coming winter by two o'clock.

The county came. They came as they had come to watch the slab cross the bench, but in their good clothes now, paying their dime — children free, the board had voted, over the treasurer's body — and they filed out of the white June light into the cool stone room, and stopped, each of them, at the same six feet of floor, the way water checks at a sill.

The slab stood as it had lain. That had been Nell's one unbending specification, carried against two mounting men from Topeka and a helpful letter from a New York firm that did dramatic poses in iron: no rearing, no glass eyes, no iron theatrics. The sea floor itself, raised on its edge like a page stood upright, the great skull in profile at a child's eye height, the jaws closed on the fanned paddle, the long spine running away down the stone, the train of throat rings lying where the currents of sixty million years ago had left them; and on the wall beside it, framed, the field sheets — her own fair copies, lamp-oil copies, the gray band and the grid and the fraud documented in place — so that a farmer could stand and read the whole craft of it, what was found and how it was known, evidence first, wonder after, which was, the label said, the order the house was built in.

The label itself was four inches by six, white card, her surveyor's print fired into porcelain enamel by a Topeka sign man against the meddling of future trustees:

TYLOSAURUS MARTHAE Granger, 1879.
Niobrara Chalk; SE 1/4 Sec. 13, T. 14, R. 26, this county.
Discovered 1877 by E. A. Granger. Excavated 1878 by S. Decker and party.
The paddle in the jaws is the fore-limb of a plesiosaur, its last prey.

Gus Tilley stood his first day of paid duty beside it, on his cane, in a new collar, Assistant to the Curator, answering the questions of children with the patient delight of a man who had crawled a mile through bunch grass for this animal and now owned the right to introduce it; Philadelphia had released him with Pratt's blessing and a letter Nell kept in the files, He is the best of me; mind he stays that way. Decker walked the room slow with his hands behind him, foreman of nothing today, checking his joints and his skylight glazing, and found occasion four times to step out front where the band was and look at the building entire. Carbury came, and stood before the framed preemption receipt in its small case by the door — the board had wanted it; the title was, the chancellor said, half the specimen — and read his own stamp on the wall of a museum, and took off his spectacles, and put them on again, and said to nobody: "Sums correct."

And at noon Reverend Mott, asked by the trustees for an invocation and given the porch steps and the crowd and the open door at his back, took out no text, and looked out over the county's bared heads at the bluff line standing white in the south, and gave them the sermon he had been three years rewriting:

"Canst thou draw out leviathan with an hook?" he said. "The Lord asked it of Job out of the whirlwind, and Job had the sense to keep silence, which has never yet been Kansas's failing." Laughter, easy, his own people. "Friends, I have seen the answer to that question. It is no. Not with an hook. With patience, and with neighbors, and with a road built first on the blind side of the hill; with a widow's true ledger and a quarryman's plumb line; with the gleanings of a good woman's dooryard kept in a jar. The beast in this house lived and died ages before our father Adam, in a sea that stood over this very porch, and I will tell you plainly what I have settled with my own soul about it, standing where you stand now: a God who keeps sixty million years of His doings on file in a chalk bank, and then raises up a schoolteacher with the patience to read the hand He wrote it in — that is not a smaller God than the one I was ordained to, neighbors. That is a vastly larger one, and His sixth day is longer than we counted, and this stone room is the first new page of it I ever watched my own county turn. Enter His gates with thanksgiving. The admission is ten cents. The children of this district go in free, now and, by covenant of the trustees, forever."

Chapter 50: The Petrified Sea

In the cool of the evening, when the band had packed its horns and the last wagons stood away north and east with their lamps swinging, Nell locked the hall — one key; she had refused a janitor in the budget's third draft — and rode out the river road to the bluff with Dane Hollis, by standing arrangement neither of them had ever put into words.

The quarry scar was healing the way chalk heals, whitening, weathering its tool marks soft, the spoil bench greening over with the volunteer grass that forgave everything in that country sooner or later. The swallows had found the new face the first spring and built under the overhang of the band, and their coming and going stitched the air in front of sixty feet of emptiness shaped, if you knew how to look, like the going-away of something vast.

They sat their horses at the foot of the draw, where she had stood holding the rein on a Sunday in 'seventy-six with her heart conducting itself badly.

"Heifers done well off this draw again," Hollis said. "Three year running. Whatever's in that grass, I'm letting the lease money stand toward the hall's coal." He had announced this to the trustees, who had minuted it; he announced it to Nell now, evening repetition, the way the settled facts of a country get themselves said aloud at intervals so they will stay settled. "Martha's stone wants repointing, fall. Decker says he'll come."

"He'll come," Nell said. "He likes work he can finish."

The sun went down the long way it goes down in that country, with the whole western sky working through the colors of the inside of a shell, and the bluff face took the last of it and held it, the way it held everything, and gave it back rose, then gray, then white. A nighthawk began over the river. The first star came on over the Colorado line like a lamp set in a far window.

She thought, sitting there, of the things the day's crowd had said in the cool stone room, and of the one thing nobody had said, because it took the evening and the bluff to say it. The sea was not gone. That was the fact under all the other facts, the one she had been circling since a tooth came out of a schoolhouse wall into her palm. The sea had only stopped moving. It lay under the whole of that country, white and patient, with its dead in its arms, keeping its own true journal — every fall of snow through its quiet water entered in order, every life that ended in it filed where it lay — and once in a great while, by frost or flood or a widow with a knotted cord, the country opened a page of itself, and what was written there could be read by anyone honest, in any age, in the one hand that never lied.

The label in the hall was four inches by six. Her name on it was set in letters smaller than the animal's and larger than the Museum's, which was, she had decided at the proof stage, the correct order of the three, and the only sentence of her own she had ever needed printed anywhere.

"Getting on dark," Hollis said.

"It is."

They turned the horses. Behind them the bluff stood up against the first stars, white as washing, sixty feet of the floor of the old sea standing in the Kansas night with the swallows asleep in its seams; and inside it, level and patient, going away into the hill where the lamplight of no museum would ever reach them, the rest of the first folks lay as they had lain through everything, in the long keeping of the chalk, waiting for nothing, complete.

She had the hall's one key in her reticule, and her supper banked in the stove at the claim house, and tomorrow's work was labels. Nell Granger rode home across her own quarter-section in the dark, under the whole slow wheel of the sky, over the bed of the petrified sea.
