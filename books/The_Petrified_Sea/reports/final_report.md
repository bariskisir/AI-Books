## Narrative Structure

The novel spans 50 chapters in five parts, following Eleanor "Nell" Granger from the schoolhouse wall to the curatorship of her own museum:

- **Part I: The Chalk (1–10)** — Nell's discovery of the vertebrae in Hollis's bluff; the arrival of the two rival fossil expeditions; her hiring as field illustrator.
- **Part II: Claims (11–20)** — The dirty war (intercepted crates, salted quarries, the "Mr. E. Granger" erasure); the tracheal-rings discovery; the storm exposes the complete skull with prey in its jaws.
- **Part III: Winters East (21–30)** — The museum interlude; the unopened crates; the Academy hearing; Brandt's "publish" doctrine; both generals' offers weighed and declined.
- **Part IV: The Bluff (31–40)** — The land-title race (railroad grant forfeiture, Nell's quiet preemption settlement); the excavation; flood, collapse, and the slab rising; Pratt's premature naming off spy sketches.
- **Part V: Names in Stone (41–50)** — Filing day; the refusals of Ordway's gilded cage and acceptance of the thin Kansas third way; the description published as *Tylosaurus marthae* Granger; the hall opens; coda at the healed bluff.

The central historical engine is a fictionalized Bone Wars (Cope–Marsh) rivalry, with original institutions and principals. The science is period-accurate for 1876–1879: Niobrara chalk, mosasaurs and plesiosaurs of the Western Interior Seaway, plaster-jacket field technique, priority-of-naming culture, the railroad land-grant checkerboard, and preemption law (widows qualified as entrywomen).

## Thematic Elements

- **Who gets to write the label** — credit, erasure, and the politics of scientific naming; "Mr. E. Granger" vs. the four-by-six porcelain label.
- **Deep time and small honesty** — the field journal as moral instrument; measurement against "the wish for what ought to be there."
- **Institutions vs. neighbors** — the brownstone museum's unopened crates against the county-built Bone House; what the West loses in crates going east.
- **The men are better than the war** — every antagonist (Ordway excepted, deliberately) is given a redemptive human floor: Krug's dog, Pratt's confession, the flood truce.
- **Faith and the fossil record** — Reverend Mott's three-year journey from fear to the "sixth day longer than we counted" sermon.

## Writing Approach

- Spare, concrete frontier prose; short sentences; period texture (chalk, plaster of paris, telegraph keys, oxen, preemption filings) without dialect spelling.
- Dialogue-driven: each major character has a distinct register (Decker's ledger dryness, Pratt's performance, Ordway's whisper, Hollis's wire-fence terseness, Carbury's bureaucratic italics).
- No incantatory refrains; the repetition-recursion pitfall avoided throughout. Chapters average ~730 words (range roughly 600–1,100), lean and purposeful.
- Violence limited to weather and accident, handled with restraint; no deaths in the collapse, adult cast throughout.
