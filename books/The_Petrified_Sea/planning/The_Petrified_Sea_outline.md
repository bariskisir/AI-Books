# The Petrified Sea — Outline

**Genre:** Paleontological Historical Fiction / Frontier Science Drama
**Setting:** Western Kansas chalk country (the Smoky Hill River breaks), 1876–1878, with winter interludes in the museum halls of the East.
**Length:** 50 chapters, ~700–1,100 words each.

## Premise

In 1876, the chalk bluffs of western Kansas are the floor of a vanished ocean, and two famous men are at war over its dead. Professor Hiram Ordway of Aldermont College and Dr. Lucius Pratt of the Philadelphia Academy race to strip the badlands of sea-reptile bones, bribing telegraph operators, salting each other's quarries, and naming species by wire. Eleanor "Nell" Granger — a widowed schoolteacher who has taught herself anatomy from a borrowed book and draws fossils with a surveyor's precision — is hired into Ordway's field crew as an illustrator, and discovers what neither man has: a complete giant mosasaur lying in a homesteader's bluff, its jaws closed on the paddle of its last meal. The find could end the feud or feed it. Nell's field journal and plates become the only honest record in a war of liars, and she must decide whether the bones will carry a rich man's name, a desperate man's, or — against every custom of the age — her own. A novel about deep time, small honesty, and who gets to write the label.

## Core Cast (all adults)

- **Eleanor "Nell" Granger, 34** — Widowed schoolteacher at Monument Junction, Kansas. Self-taught anatomist, exacting illustrator. Quiet, stubborn, allergic to flattery.
- **Professor Hiram Ordway, 55** — Aldermont College, Connecticut. Rich by inheritance, cold by practice, obsessed with priority of naming. Buys what he cannot win.
- **Dr. Lucius Pratt, 42** — Philadelphia naturalist. Brilliant, fast, vain, in debt; once mounted a sea reptile's skull on the end of its tail, and Ordway has never let the world forget it.
- **Silas Decker, 38** — Ordway's field foreman, former buffalo hunter. Practical, dry, loyal to wages until something better than wages turns up.
- **August "Gus" Tilley, 26** — Pratt's young collector. Decent, eager, badly used by the feud.
- **Dane Hollis, 45** — Homesteader whose claim holds the bluff. Broke, grieving his wife Martha, suspicious of every man with soft hands.
- **Reverend Jacob Mott, 60** — Town preacher, shaken by what the bones imply about the age of the earth. A conscience, not a villain.
- **Cora Bly, 56** — Boardinghouse keeper. Sees everything, prices accordingly.
- **Dr. Emil Brandt, 70** — Old anatomist in Philadelphia, semi-retired, the last courteous man in American science. Nell's ally by letter.
- **Mr. Finch, 40s** — Telegraph operator at the Junction. Sells what passes through his key.

## Structure — Five Parts

### Part I: The Chalk (Chapters 1–10)
1. **The Tooth in the Schoolhouse Wall** — Nell pries a fossil shark tooth from the chalk-block wall of her schoolhouse and tells her pupils what it means; the sea is introduced.
2. **What the Storm Uncovered** — Riding the draws after a hailstorm, Nell finds a line of vertebrae weathering out of a bluff on Dane Hollis's claim, and keeps it to herself.
3. **Two Telegrams** — Town learns that two rival fossil expeditions are coming on the same railroad; Cora Bly raises her rates.
4. **The Professor's Men** — Ordway's crew unloads at the depot under Silas Decker; Nell watches picks, plaster, and rifles come off the car.
5. **Dr. Pratt at the Lectern** — Pratt lectures on the ancient sea in the church hall; Mott is shaken; Nell asks one anatomy question that Pratt cannot answer smoothly.
6. **A Position Offered** — Decker sees Nell's fossil drawings at the mercantile and hires her into Ordway's crew as field illustrator.
7. **First Quarry** — Nell learns the work: white glare, plaster jackets, the patience of uncovering; she proves herself with a brush.
8. **The Crooked Skull** — The crew's campfire story: Pratt's old reversed-skull blunder and Ordway's public destruction of him; Nell understands the feud's wound.
9. **Spades at Night** — Fresh spade-cuts appear in Ordway's ravine by dark; the first accusations of claim-jumping fly between camps.
10. **The Color of Deep Time** — Nell shows Reverend Mott an ammonite and answers his fear of deep time plainly; an uneasy respect begins.

### Part II: Claims (Chapters 11–20)
11. **The Railhead Crates** — A shipment of Ordway's fossils is misrouted and opened; suspicion falls on Finch, who wires more than weather east.
12. **Pratt's Offer** — Gus Tilley brings Nell a better offer from Pratt; she declines but likes the boy more than the letter.
13. **Salted Ground** — Ordway's crew wastes a week on a planted composite skeleton; Decker finds file marks on the bone.
14. **The Bone Room** — Nell's plates travel east and are praised in a journal as the work of "Mr. E. Granger"; the museum's crates stack unopened.
15. **Hollis's Fence** — Hollis catches Nell at the bluff; she trades silence for help with his land-patent papers and learns about Martha.
16. **Wages of Glory** — Ordway visits camp, inspects Nell's plates, corrects her grip on nothing, and does not apologize for "Mr. Granger."
17. **The Storm Cellar** — A tornado crams both crews and half the town into the same cellars; for one night the feud is suspended by weather.
18. **The Composite Man** — Decker traces the salting to a dismissed teamster paid in Philadelphia notes; Pratt denies it by letter; nobody is believed.
19. **The Question of the Skull** — Drawing a partial mosasaur, Nell records evidence bearing on the very dispute that ruined Pratt; she writes a private note and says nothing.
20. **What Nell Keeps** — The spring cut at Hollis's bluff now shows a complete skull, jaws closed on a plesiosaur paddle — predator and prey in one slab. Nell tells no one.

### Part III: Winters East (Chapters 21–30)
21. **The Season Ends** — The chalk season closes; Ordway offers Nell winter work as a preparator at half wages, and she takes it for the bone halls.
22. **East by Rail** — Nell crosses from grass to brick; first sight of the museum, its galleries, and its locked rooms.
23. **The Preparator's Bench** — Winter work with acid and brush; Nell sees crates from three summers still nailed shut while Ordway races names into print.
24. **Pratt in the Doorway** — Pratt visits under a flag of truce, recognizes the hand behind the journal plates, and meets "Mr. Granger" in person.
25. **The Academy Hearing** — A priority dispute becomes a public airing of theft, salting, and libel; Nell testifies narrowly about a crate label and hates every minute.
26. **An Evening of Honest Men** — At a dinner, old Dr. Brandt tells Nell the only rule that outlives politeness: publish.
27. **The Letter from Hollis** — Hollis writes that strangers are measuring his bluff; the railroad's land men are circling too.
28. **Terms** — Nell offers Ordway a specimen of consequence in exchange for funding, fair pay to Hollis, and her name on the work; he agrees to the money and smiles at the name.
29. **Counter-Terms** — Pratt, tipped by Gus, offers co-authorship outright; Nell sees the debt behind the generosity and boards her train uncommitted.
30. **West Again** — Spring on the long line home; Nell decides the bones themselves will choose their keeper.

### Part IV: The Bluff (Chapters 31–40)
31. **The Race Renewed** — Both camps re-form; the town grows fat; land agents knock on Hollis's door with papers he cannot read.
32. **The Patent Question** — The bluff lies on a clouded quarter-section of railroad checkerboard; whoever perfects the title owns the dead.
33. **Nell Shows Decker** — At dusk she walks Decker down the draw and shows him forty feet of articulated sea lizard; the hard man takes off his hat.
34. **Three-Way Bargain** — Nell brokers it: Hollis sells excavation rights for cash and a share, Ordway funds, and the announcement waits on the title.
35. **The Dig** — Trenching, undercutting, jacketing; heat, rattlesnakes, and Gus Tilley caught spying and put to work carrying water.
36. **Flood** — A Smoky Hill flash flood comes for the quarry by night; Pratt's men cross the river to hold the spoil-bank levee with their rivals.
37. **The Collapse** — The undercut bluff calves off, burying Decker and Gus; Nell's knowledge of the bedding planes directs the dig-out; broken bones, no graves.
38. **The Slab Rises** — Block and tackle, a doubled-axle wagon, and the whole town on the bluff to watch the sea lizard swing into the light.
39. **Pratt's Move** — With the title still clouded, Pratt wires a description east from spy sketches and names the animal sight unseen — mistaking the prey's paddle for its own limb, a second crooked skull.
40. **The Telegraph War** — Competing wires, Finch playing both keys, and one fact emerging: only Nell's journal and plates record the truth in place.

### Part V: Names in Stone (Chapters 41–50)
41. **What the Journal Holds** — Brandt advises by letter: dated field notes and plates decide validity; the war will be settled by whoever kept honest books.
42. **Ordway's Price** — Ordway offers co-authorship at last — in exchange for the journal as museum property and her plates as his copyright; Nell sees the cage inside the gift.
43. **Pratt's Confession** — Pratt comes to the boardinghouse parlor and asks not for the fossil but for the correction of his error in print, whoever signs it.
44. **The Third Way** — Brandt and the young state university propose: the slab stays in Kansas, a hall is built, and the description published by E. A. Granger.
45. **Breaking with Ordway** — Ordway threatens suits and blacklists; Decker quits him on the quarry floor and hires on to build the hall instead.
46. **The Description** — A winter of writing: measurements, plates, and the naming — *Tylosaurus marthae*, for a homesteader's wife who loved the bluff.
47. **The Reading** — Brandt reads the paper in Philadelphia while Nell opens the hall in Kansas; Pratt rises from the audience and corrects his own old error, citing her plates.
48. **Ordway's Footnote** — Ordway's sour rebuttal lands hollow as the unopened-crates scandal breaks; the feud has spent two fortunes and bought a footnote.
49. **The Hall Opens** — The slab is mounted as it lay; schoolchildren file past; Mott blesses the deep water without surrendering his God; Gus signs on as first assistant.
50. **The Petrified Sea** — Coda: Nell and Hollis at the healed bluff scar at evening; the label in the hall reads small and correct; the sea keeps its bones and its name.

## Style Notes

- Spare, concrete frontier prose: chalk dust, plaster, telegraph keys, wagon timber. Short sentences. Real dialogue with period texture but no dialect spelling.
- No incantatory refrains. No padding. Each chapter advances either the dig, the feud, or Nell's standing.
- Science handled accurately for 1876: Niobrara chalk, mosasaurs, plesiosaurs, Cretaceous inland sea, priority-of-naming culture; the institutions and principals are fictional.
- Content: adult cast, clean subject matter; violence limited to accident and weather, handled with restraint.
