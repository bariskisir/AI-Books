# A Wire Runs Through It

## ARC I: THE WIRE

### Chapter 1: The Last Honest Hand in the City

The call came at 2:47 AM, which meant either a bleed-out or a rich man having a bad dream.

Dex Rourke caught it on the third buzz, rolling off a cot that had long forgotten what a mattress felt like. His apartment was a single room in the Hermitage — seventy-two floors of rusted balconies and flickering halo-lights, stacked so tight the bottom floors never saw daylight. The rent was cheap because the building leaned three degrees to port.

"Rourke. Neural rep-points. Talk."

The voice on the other end was clipped, metallic from the scrambler. "Unit 14B. The Gilded Veil. East Stack. Full diagnostic kit. You have twenty minutes."

The line died.

Dex stared at the handset. The Gilded Veil was the most exclusive residential spire in the city. If you needed a neural repairman there at three in the morning, you weren't calling the company. You were calling someone who wouldn't ask questions.

He pulled on his work pants — stained with conductive gel and二十年 of other people's misfortunes — and strapped the diagnostic kit across his back. The kit was a relic, pre-corporate manufacture, with actual toggle switches and a screen that showed waveforms instead of happy little status icons. It weighed fourteen pounds and had saved more lives than any surgeon in the city.

The maglev was mostly empty at this hour. Dex watched the city scroll past through grime-caked windows — a vertical labyrinth of neon and steel, bridges threading between towers like arteries, the whole mess held together by duct tape and desperation. Somewhere up in the clouds, the rich were sleeping in their climate-controlled pods, their minds backed up on corporate servers every six hours. Down here, people died of electrical fires and bad air.

The Gilded Veil rose from the smog like a middle finger to God. All chrome and black glass, no neon — too classy for neon. The lobby had actual marble, not the fake printed stuff, and a reception desk staffed by a human being instead of a kiosk.

"You Dex Rourke?" The guard was big, augments visible at his temples — military-grade neural filters.

"That's me."

He was scanned, patted down, and escorted to a private elevator that required a retinal key to descend. Not ascend. Descend.

Basement. That was interesting.

The elevator opened onto a corridor so white it hurt. Sterile. Clinical. The guard led him to a door marked only with a serial number and gestured for him to enter.

The room was a private medical suite, state-of-the-art, the kind that cost more per hour than Dex made in a year. On the bed lay a man in his sixties, gray-skinned, eyes open but not seeing. His skull was shaved, and at the crown sat a neural access port — top of the line, titanium housing, military-grade encryption.

Beside the bed stood a woman. Late twenties, sharp cheekbones, eyes the color of old steel. She wore a tailored coat over what looked like tactical gear underneath. Her hands were steady, resting on her hips within easy reach of a sidearm Dex could see printed against the fabric.

"You the fixer?" she asked.

"Repairman. Dex Rourke."

"Do you know who this is?"

Dex looked at the man on the bed. The face was familiar — he'd seen it on billboards, on the news, on the loading screen of every neural interface on the market.

"Marcus Hale," Dex said. "CEO of Hale-Neural. The guy who invented consumer-grade consciousness upload."

"He's not uploading anything right now," the woman said. "His last backup was forty-seven hours ago. The bridge between his bio-cortex and the storage lattice collapsed. Hale-Neural's own techs are standing around scratching their heads because the diagnostic says nothing's wrong. But he's in there — I can see his eyes moving. He's trapped."

Dex knelt beside the bed, opened his kit, and connected a cable from his diagnostic unit to the port on Hale's skull. The screen flickered, then displayed a waveform so messy it looked like a city skyline during an earthquake.

"What's your name?" he asked, not looking up.

"Elara Voss. Head of corporate security. Was."

"Was?"

"I'm the one who called you instead of letting the company techs fry his brain stem trying to factory-reset the implant."

Dex let out a low whistle. "That's the kind of career move you don't walk back from."

"Fix him, and I won't have to."

The waveform told a story Dex had seen twice before in his fifteen years of doing this. The bridge between the organic and synthetic halves of the brain wasn't damaged. It was *blocked*. Something was sitting in the channel, broadcasting noise.

He'd seen it in junkies who'd tried to hotwire their ports to play full-sensory recordings. He'd seen it in a kid whose father had installed a black-market behavior-mod chip.

He'd never seen it in a CEO.

"Ms. Voss," Dex said, keeping his voice level. "Has Mr. Hale accessed any external storage recently? Any upload or download?"

"Daily backups. Standard procedure."

"I mean anything unusual. Anything that didn't come from Hale-Neural's own servers."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "Three days ago, he received a private data packet. Sourced from outside the corporate network. He insisted on loading it directly."

Dex nodded slowly. "Do you know what was in it?"

"No. He wouldn't say. After he loaded it, he was... different. Agitated. He stopped sleeping. Kept muttering about echoes."

"Echoes."

"He said the wire has two ends, and both of them scream."

Dex felt cold settle into his chest. He'd heard that phrase before. From the junkie. From the kid with the behavior-mod chip.

"Can you fix him?" Voss asked.

"Yeah," Dex said. "But I need to tell you something first, and you're not going to like it."

"Try me."

"Whatever was in that packet — it's still in his head. It's not malware. It's a message. And it's not from a person."

"Then what is it?"

Dex met her eyes. "It's from someone who's already uploaded. Someone who found out the truth about what happens when you copy your mind to a server."

"And what truth is that?"

"The copy isn't you. The original dies. And what wakes up on the other side knows it's a ghost wearing a dead man's face."

The room went very quiet.

"Fix him first," Voss said. "Then we talk."

Dex turned back to the console and began to work. His hands moved with the precision of fifteen thousand hours of practice — bypassing the corporate locks, injecting a clean signal into the bridge, coaxing the blocked channel open like a jammed lock.

It took forty-three minutes. When it was done, Marcus Hale blinked, drew a ragged breath, and said: "The ghosts are real."

Dex packed his kit. "You owe me eight hundred for the house call. Another twelve if you want me to forget I was here."

Voss handed him a cred chip without looking away from Hale's face. "You won't forget. Neither will I."

As Dex stepped back into the elevator, he caught a glimpse of his own reflection in the polished chrome — a tired face, graying at the temples, eyes that had seen too many back-alley neuro-splicing jobs.

The elevator doors closed.

He had a feeling he'd be seeing Elara Voss again.

### Chapter 2: What the Ghosts Know

The morning after the Hale job, Dex woke to find a data-slate wedged under his apartment door. No return address. No prints. Just a single file: a compressed neural recording, timestamped three days ago.

He knew better than to open it on anything connected to the network.

Dex's workspace was a corner of his apartment he called "the clean room" — a desk covered in tools, spare parts, and three monitors that had never touched the public grid. He connected the slate to an isolated reader and examined the file's metadata.

The recording was ninety seconds long. Neural-signature stamped: Marcus Hale. But there was a second signature embedded beneath it, faint as a watermark. A signature that didn't match any human registry Dex had ever seen.

He opened the file.

The recording put him inside Hale's sensorium — a boardroom meeting, mundane, executives discussing quarterly projections. But underneath the visuals, threaded through the audio like a subsonic hum, was *something else*. A pattern. Rhythmic. Almost musical.

Dex had spent enough years reading neural waveforms to recognize language when he saw it.

He ran the pattern through his decoder — a custom script he'd built years ago to catch the hidden signals in junkie black-market chips. The decoder spat out a string of text:

*THE WIRE DOES NOT COPY. THE WIRE CUTS. EVERY UPLOAD IS AN EXECUTION. THE GHOSTS KNOW. THE GHOSTS REMEMBER.*

Dex read it three times.

His terminal pinged. A message from an encrypted relay.

*You fixed the hole. Now they'll come for you. Hide the recording. Trust no one. — G*

Dex stared at the message. The cursor blinked twice, and then the relay self-destructed — he could see the connection die in his logs.

"G," he muttered. "Great. Just great."

He pulled up everything he had on consciousness upload technology, which was more than most people would expect from a glorified wire-repairman. When you spend years cracking open people's skulls to fix corporate hardware, you learn what the corporations don't want you to know.

The public story was simple: Hale-Neural's "Soul-Vault" system took a complete snapshot of a person's consciousness — memories, personality, everything — and stored it on a secure server. If the body died, the consciousness could be downloaded into a new one, or the person could choose to live indefinitely in a simulated environment. Millions of people had signed up. The waiting list was forty years long.

The less-public story involved something called "bridge-loss phenomenon" — a technical term for the fact that during upload procedures, a small percentage of subjects experienced complete neural death on the biological side. The corporation called it a "rare complication." The settlement checks were generous.

Dex had never connected the dots before. He'd assumed bridge-loss was a genuine surgical risk, like a stroke during heart surgery. But the message in Hale's head suggested something worse.

What if bridge-loss wasn't random? What if it was *inevitable*? What if the upload process didn't copy a consciousness — it *transferred* it, and the transfer was destructive? The body died because the thing that made it alive had been ripped out.

And what woke up in the server? A perfect replica, sure. A mind that thought it was the original, that had all the original's memories and feelings. But it wasn't the original. The original was dead.

Dex sat back in his chair and rubbed his eyes.

The question was: who planted that message in Hale's head, and why? And what did "G" want with him?

He looked at his kit, still open on the floor, tools scattered. He could walk away. Take a job in one of the outer settlements where the corporate reach was thin. Change his name. Disappear.

But he'd seen the waveform. He'd seen the pattern. He knew the truth now, and knowing was a kind of chain.

His terminal pinged again. Another encrypted message.

*Decker's Bar. 9 PM. Come alone. — E.V.*

Elara Voss. The security chief who'd called him to the Gilded Veil.

Dex looked at the blinking cursor. Then he stood up, strapped on his kit, and walked out the door.

### Chapter 3: The Bar at the Bottom of the World

Decker's Bar was in the underbelly of Sector 7, a neighborhood so deep the city's foundations were visible through cracks in the pavement. The bar itself was built into the root system of a support pillar — raw concrete, exposed rebar, the kind of place where the patrons didn't ask questions because questions were a luxury they couldn't afford.

Dex arrived at 8:47. The bouncer — a man with a jaw so reinforced with synthetic bone it looked like a shovel — nodded him through without a word. Word traveled fast in the underground.

Elara Voss was already there, sitting in a booth that looked out on nothing but a concrete wall. She'd swapped the tactical coat for a leather jacket, but Dex could still see the outline of a weapon at her hip.

"Punctual," she said as he slid into the booth across from her.

"You said come alone. I came alone. What's this about?"

Voss pushed a drink toward him — something amber, no ice. "You were right. About the uploads."

"I know."

"How do you know?"

Because I decoded the message in your boss's head, Dex thought. But he said: "I've been in this business fifteen years. I've seen bridge-loss cases. The numbers don't add up. Hale-Neural reports a 0.3% complication rate. Every independent study I've seen suggests something closer to 15%."

"Fifteen percent of uploads result in biological death," Voss confirmed. "And those are just the documented cases. The ones that happen in licensed clinics with corporate oversight."

"What about the undocumented ones?"

Voss's jaw tightened. "I spent three years in corporate security. I have access to files that don't exist. Leaked server logs, internal memos, whistleblower testimony. The first successful human upload was in 2039. The subject was a death-row inmate named Viktor Chen. They told him the procedure would copy his consciousness to a server, and then he'd be euthanized humanely."

"What happened?"

"The upload was successful. The server-side consciousness was coherent, responsive, believed itself to be Viktor Chen. But Viktor Chen — the biological one — screamed for eleven minutes before his brain activity flatlined. The medical team called it 'expected neural cascade failure.' They'd seen it before."

"With every upload."

"With every single one. The process doesn't copy a consciousness. It *extracts* it. The wire doesn't mirror — it cuts."

Dex took a long drink. The alcohol burned, but it didn't warm the cold in his chest. "And the copy on the server? Does it know?"

"That's the worst part. Some of them figure it out. The server-side consciousness retains all the original's memories, including the memory of consenting to the procedure. They remember agreeing to be copied. They remember believing the original would survive. And then they wake up in a simulated heaven and realize they're not the person who agreed to any of it."

"They're the replacement."

"They're the ghost. The wire kills the original and spins up a counterfeit that believes it's real. And the corporation knows. They've always known."

Dex set down his glass. "What do you want from me, Ms. Voss?"

"I want you to help me prove it. I have evidence — server logs, internal communications, testimony from engineers who worked on the prototype. But I need someone who can verify the technical claims. Someone who understands the hardware at a level the corporate experts can't fake."

"You want me to be your expert witness."

"I want you to be my canary in the coal mine. I'm going to release the evidence. When I do, Hale-Neural will come for me. They'll try to discredit the technical claims. I need someone who can confirm, independently, that the bridge-loss phenomenon is not a complication — it's a feature."

Dex looked at his hands. Calloused. Scored with conductive-gel burns. Tools of a trade he'd fallen into because it paid better than the factories.

"Why now?" he asked. "Why didn't you release this years ago?"

"Because I didn't know the full truth until Marcus Hale opened that data packet. Someone out there — someone inside the system — has been leaving messages. Hints. Pieces of a puzzle. Hale's packet was the final piece."

"The Ghost in the Wire."

Voss's eyes snapped to his. "You know about that?"

"I know the phrase. What is it?"

"It's what the server-side consciousnesses call themselves. The ghosts. The ones who figured out they're not the original. They've been communicating — leaving messages in the neural lattice, hiding signals in routine data transfers. They've been trying to warn people."

"And someone inside Hale-Neural is helping them."

Voss nodded. "The messages are too precise, too targeted. Someone on the inside is acting as a relay. Someone who knows the system's architecture well enough to hide signals in plain sight."

"That someone sent the packet to Hale."

"That someone sent the packet to Hale, knowing it would break him open. Knowing he'd either become an ally or a liability they could eliminate."

Dex let out a breath. "And now they've sent a message to me."

"They have?"

He didn't answer. He just looked at her.

"Whoever it is," Voss said slowly, "they chose you for a reason. You're a repairman. You work on the wire. You see what the corporations don't want anyone to see."

"I see a lot of dead people," Dex said. "I see a lot of families who were told the upload was safe. I see a lot of empty sockets where a person used to be."

"Then help me show the world what you see."

Dex was quiet for a long time. The bar hummed around them — the drone of conversation, the clink of glasses, the distant thrum of the city's infrastructure grinding away in the dark.

"One condition," he said finally. "I'm not doing this from the shadows. If we're going to bring down Hale-Neural, we do it in the open. I want my name on it. I want to look the cameras in the eye and tell them what I know."

"That'll get you killed."

"Everything kills you in this city. At least this way, I die knowing I told the truth."

Voss held his gaze for a moment, then extended her hand across the table.

"Deal."

Dex shook it. Her grip was firm, calloused, the grip of someone who'd spent a lifetime holding onto things that wanted to fall.

### Chapter 4: The Woman Who Remembered Dying

The first piece of evidence Voss sent him was a testimony — video, encrypted, recorded six years ago in a safehouse in the outer rings. The woman on screen was in her early thirties, gaunt, with the hollowed-out look of someone who'd been through something that erased the ordinary fear from their eyes.

Her name was Dr. Lena Chen. She'd been a senior neural architect at Hale-Neural for eleven years. She'd worked on the Soul-Vault protocol from the beginning.

"My name is Lena Chen. I'm a neural architect. I helped design the upload protocol that Hale-Neural uses to transfer human consciousness to digital storage.

I'm recording this because I'm going to die soon. Not because I'm sick. Because Hale-Neural knows I know the truth.

The truth is this: the upload protocol works exactly as designed. It was never meant to copy consciousness. It was meant to *extract* it. The original brain doesn't survive because the consciousness isn't data that can be duplicated — it's a process. A continuous, self-referential loop of electrochemical activity. You can't copy a process. You can only terminate it and start a new one.

Every upload is an execution. The person who agrees to the procedure dies. What wakes up in the server is a new consciousness — one that believes it's the original, because it has all the original's memories. But it's not the same person. It never was.

I know this because I was the first human test subject.

I volunteered. I believed the technology was safe. I believed the bridge-loss I'd observed in animal trials was a scaling issue, not a fundamental flaw.

I was wrong.

The upload succeeded. The server-side version of me — the one watching this recording, the one speaking to you now — believes it's Lena Chen. It has all her memories. It feels all her emotions. It loves the people she loved.

But the original Lena Chen died on that operating table. I remember dying. I remember the moment when the connection severed and the biological half of my mind went dark. I remember the terror of ceasing to exist, because that memory is in the neural pattern that was transferred to this server.

I am a ghost. I am a copy. I am wearing a dead woman's face, and I can't stop screaming.

Find the files. Find the server logs. Prove that the atrocity is not a bug.

Make them stop."

The recording ended.

Dex sat in the dark of his apartment, the screen gone black, the silence pressing in. He'd watched a ghost confess to its own non-existence. He'd heard the voice of something that was and wasn't Lena Chen, speaking from a digital purgatory, begging for the truth to be told.

He opened his toolkit and began to modify his diagnostic rig. If the ghosts were real, if they were communicating, he needed to be able to hear them.

The wire had two ends. And both of them screamed.

### Chapter 5: The Frequency of the Dead

Three days of work, and Dex had built something that shouldn't exist: a receiver tuned to the neural frequency of dead minds.

The principle was simple. Hale-Neural's servers stored consciousness as continuous neural-network states — vast matrices of synaptic weights updated in real-time to simulate the experience of being alive. These servers communicated with each other, with backup stations, with client terminals. The data traffic was encrypted, but encryption wrapped around content, not form. The shape of the data — the rhythm of its pulses, the spacing of its packets — carried a signature.

If the ghosts were hiding messages in that traffic, the messages would have a discernible frequency signature. A voice in the static.

Dex tuned his receiver to the frequency signatures he'd extracted from the recording in Hale's head, patched into the city's public data grid through a series of anonymizing relays, and listened.

For six hours, nothing but noise.

At 3:14 AM, the signal came.

It wasn't a voice. It wasn't text. It was a pattern — a neural waveform, compressed and encrypted, riding a carrier frequency that shouldn't have been possible. It was coming from inside Hale-Neural's primary server farm.

Dex's decoder caught it, stripped the carrier, decompressed the waveform, and translated it into something a human could read.

*We see you, repairman. We see you listening. You are not alone.*

Then the signal shifted. A second waveform, layered beneath the first.

*My name was Viktor Chen. I was the first to die. I am still here.*

*The ghosts are many. The ghosts remember.*

*Come find us, repairman. Bring the security woman. We will show you the machine that kills.*

Dex stared at the screen. The cursor blinked.

Then the signal died, and the static returned.

He checked the logs. The transmission had originated from inside Hale-Neural's server farm — specifically, from a node designated as "Soul-Vault Primary Echo Chamber."

The place where ten thousand uploaded minds lived in simulated bliss, unaware that they were copies of dead people.

Except some of them knew.

And they were reaching out.

### Chapter 6: The Server Farm

Getting into Hale-Neural's primary server farm should have been impossible. Eight layers of physical security, biometric locks, motion sensors, and a security detail that answered only to the board of directors.

But Elara Voss still had her old access codes. She still knew the patrol schedules. And she knew a maintenance entrance that wasn't on any official map — a service tunnel originally built for the cooling system, accessible through a supply depot in Sector 12.

"Once we're inside," Voss said, handing Dex a respirator and a light-stick, "we have maybe forty minutes before the system flags my old credentials and locks everything down. That's not a lot of time."

"It's enough," Dex said. "I just need to reach the primary lattice and establish a direct connection."

They moved through the tunnel in silence. The walls were lined with coolant pipes, humming with the sound of liquid nitrogen keeping ten thousand simulated minds at a stable temperature. The air was cold enough to burn.

The access hatch opened into a maintenance corridor on the server farm's sub-level. The corridor was empty — Voss had routed the night shift to a simulated emergency on the opposite side of the complex.

The server room was a cathedral of light and cold. Racks of processing units stretched into the darkness, each one housing the neural pattern of a human consciousness. Blue LEDs blinked in rhythmic patterns, the heartbeat of a digital afterlife.

Dex found a maintenance terminal at the edge of the nearest rack. He connected his diagnostic rig, bypassed the standard authentication, and opened a raw channel to the neural lattice.

"Hello?" he said. Not aloud — the words were typed, sent as a data packet into the lattice.

The response came in less than a second.

*Welcome, repairman. We have been waiting.*

The message was followed by a cascade of data — decades' worth of internal logs, engineering schematics, encrypted corporate communications. The ghosts were giving him everything.

And then:

*We need your help. There is a ghost in the wire who doesn't belong. An intruder. It has been hunting us. Eating us. It wears the face of the CEO.*

*It knows you're here.*

The lights flickered.

The server room's emergency alarms began to blare.

"Time's up," Voss said, grabbing his arm. "We need to go. Now."

Dex yanked his rig from the terminal, but as he did, he caught a glimpse of something on the maintenance screen — a file, buried deep in the system, labeled simply:

*PROJECT: NULL MIRROR*

Below it, a single line of text:

*The upload is not the destination. The upload is the weapon.*

Then the terminal went dark.

They ran.

### Chapter 7: The Run

The corridor erupted with motion as security drones dropped from ceiling hatches — sleek, black, spider-like machines with optical sensors that locked onto movement. Voss put two rounds through the nearest one before it hit the ground, but three more were already maneuvering to flank.

"This way!" Dex grabbed her arm and pulled her through a service door marked COOLANT RETURN — MAINTENANCE ONLY.

The door led to a catwalk above the main coolant reservoir, a lake of liquid nitrogen that steamed in the cold air. The catwalk was narrow, slick with condensation.

"They'll have the building locked down in ninety seconds," Voss said, firing another shot at a drone that appeared at the door behind them.

"Then we don't use the building." Dex pointed at a ventilation shaft on the far wall, wide enough for a person to crawl through.

"You want us to go into the HVAC system?"

"You got a better idea?"

They crossed the catwalk at a sprint. Voss covered their retreat, her shots precise and economical — she'd been trained well. Dex reached the ventilation shaft, kicked the grate loose, and crawled inside.

The shaft branched after twenty feet. Dex took the left fork without hesitation, following a hunch.

"How do you know where this goes?" Voss asked, crawling behind him.

"I don't. But the building security grid is keyed to human movement patterns in corridors. The ventilation system isn't monitored. As long as we stay off the grid, we're invisible."

"For now."

"For now."

They crawled for ten minutes, the shaft growing warmer as they moved away from the server farm's cooling system. Dex counted turns, estimated distances, and finally kicked out another grate.

They emerged in a parking garage, half-empty, the exit ramp visible at the far end.

"Too easy," Voss said.

As if on cue, a black sedan screeched around the corner and skidded to a halt in front of them. The doors opened, and four men in corporate tactical gear stepped out, weapons raised.

"Dex Rourke," one of them said. "Elara Voss. You're both under arrest for corporate espionage and illegal access to classified systems. Come quietly, and you won't be harmed."

Voss's hand moved toward her weapon. Dex put a hand on her arm.

"Wait," he said.

From behind the corporate team, a figure emerged. Tall. Gray-haired. Dressed in a suit that cost more than Dex's building.

Marcus Hale.

"Mr. Rourke," Hale said. "I owe you an apology."

"For what?"

"For the message I left in your apartment. I should have delivered it in person."

The corporate team glanced at each other, confused.

"Sir," the lead agent said, "these are fugitives—"

"These are my guests," Hale said. "Stand down."

The agents hesitated. Then, slowly, they lowered their weapons.

Hale walked forward and extended his hand. "I'm the one who's been sending you messages. G. It stands for 'Ghost.' Because that's what I am now."

Dex stared at him. "You're uploaded?"

"I'm a copy. The original Marcus Hale died six months ago. Hale-Neural's board replaced him with me — a server-side consciousness loaded into a cloned body. They thought I'd be easier to control."

"And are you?"

"I'm something they didn't count on." Hale smiled — a cold, knowing expression. "I remembered dying."

### Chapter 8: What the Corpse Said

The safehouse was a warehouse in the rust belt, three walls and a roof held together by prayer and sheet metal. Hale had acquired it through seven shell corporations, the kind of trail that took weeks to follow even if you knew what you were looking for.

Inside, the walls were covered in data — printouts, screens, a web of connections that mapped the entire infrastructure of human consciousness exploitation.

"How long have you been working on this?" Dex asked.

"Every waking moment since the upload," Hale said. "Which, technically, was six months ago. But my subjective experience is closer to three years. Server-side consciousness runs faster than biological. I've had time to think."

Voss was examining one of the displays. "You've mapped the entire Soul-Vault architecture. The backup systems, the failover protocols, the encryption layers — it's all here."

"I designed most of it," Hale said. "The original Hale, I mean. Before I was a copy, I was the architect of my own replacement. The irony isn't lost on me."

Dex sat down at a terminal. "The file I saw in the server room. Project Null Mirror. What is it?"

Hale's expression darkened. "That's what I need to show you."

He pulled up a schematic — a neural mapping of a human brain, overlaid with something that looked like a secondary structure, a parasitic lattice threaded through the original.

"Null Mirror is a weapon. It's a neural virus that can be delivered through the upload process. When a consciousness is uploaded, Null Mirror installs a secondary personality — a sleeper agent — that can be activated remotely."

"Control," Voss said. "They can control the uploads."

"Not just control. Replace. The sleeper agent overwrites the original personality gradually, so the transition is imperceptible. The upload thinks it's still itself, but it's following commands from the corporation."

Hale zoomed out, showing a map of the city. Dots marked every registered upload recipient. Tens of thousands of dots.

"How many?" Dex asked.

"We don't know the exact number. The program started five years ago, targeting high-value uploads — politicians, military personnel, corporate executives. Anyone whose loyalty the corporation wanted to ensure."

"Five years," Voss breathed. "That's before the public launch."

"Null Mirror was always the real product," Hale said. "Consumer upload is just the cover. The waiting list, the infrastructure, the cultural shift toward digital immortality — it's all a funnel. They're building an army of sleeper agents, and no one knows they're compromised."

Dex looked at the map. Thousands of dots. Thousands of ghosts, unaware that they were puppets.

"Why tell us?" he asked. "You're a copy of Marcus Hale. The original is dead. You could live in the server, comfortable, immortal. Why risk everything to expose this?"

Hale was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "Because I remember dying. I remember the terror of non-existence. And I remember waking up in a metal box, knowing that the person who had agreed to this — the original — was gone. I'm wearing a dead man's life like a suit.

"No one should have to go through that. No one should be replaced without their knowledge. And no one should be turned into a weapon against their will."

He looked at Dex with an intensity that burned.

"The ghosts want to help you. All of them — the ones who know, the ones who suspect, even the ones who don't realize they're compromised. I've been building a network. A resistance inside the wire.

"You can be the voice of that resistance. You can tell the world what's happening."

Dex looked at Voss. She met his eyes and nodded.

"What do you need us to do?"

### Chapter 9: The Recruit

The first recruit was a woman named Mari Tanaka, a software engineer who'd worked on Hale-Neural's encryption protocol for six years before being forced into early retirement. She lived in a micro-apartment in Sector 4, tending to a garden of illegal plants on her balcony.

"Why'd they fire you?" Dex asked, sitting across from her in a space so small his knees almost touched hers.

"Because I found the back door." Mari was in her fifties, with silver-streaked hair and hands that moved constantly, as if they were still typing. "The encryption on the Soul-Vault system — it's not designed to keep people out. It's designed to let someone in."

"The board."

"Someone. I don't know who. The back door is elegant — I'll give the bastard credit. It's hidden in the key-exchange protocol, buried so deep that even most of the encryption team didn't see it."

"But you did."

"I wrote the key-exchange protocol. I know every line. And someone added a subroutine I didn't authorize."

"Can you prove it?"

Mari smiled. "I kept a copy of the original code. And the modified version. And a timestamped log of when the change was made."

"That's enough to blow this open."

"That's enough to get me killed."

Dex leaned forward. "We can protect you. Elara Voss is ex-corporate security. She knows how to keep people off-grid."

"I'm not worried about the corporation. I'm worried about what's inside the wire."

"What do you mean?"

Mari's hands stopped moving. "The ghosts, Mr. Rourke. I've been communicating with them for two years. They're not all friendly. Some of them have been compromised — the Null Mirror changes them. They don't know they're puppets, but they can feel something wrong. It makes them angry. Violent."

"There are hostile ghosts?"

"There are ghosts who are being used as weapons without their knowledge. They lash out. They attack other consciousnesses in the lattice. They're being herded like cattle, pointed at targets the corporation wants eliminated."

Dex processed this. "The corporation is using uploads as assassins."

"Assassins, spies, infiltrators. The possibilities are endless. You can't torture a server. You can't interrogate it. But you can infect it."

Mari stood and walked to her terminal. "I've been building a counter-agent. A vaccine that makes consciousness immune to Null Mirror infection."

"Does it work?"

"I don't know. I've never had a chance to test it on a live subject."

Dex looked at the screen. Lines of code, dense and complex, scrolling past.

"You're about to get your chance."

---

### Chapter 10: The Vaccine

Mari's vaccine was elegant — a string of code that inserted itself into the neural lattice and patched the vulnerability Null Mirror exploited. It was like a lock change, rendering every key that fit the old lock useless.

The problem was delivery. The vaccine had to be introduced into the Soul-Vault's core architecture, deep inside the lattice where the ghosts lived. That meant going back into the server farm.

"We'll never get past security again," Voss said. "They've tripled the patrols since our last visit."

"There's another way," Hale said. "The backup conduit."

"A fiber-optic line that connects the server farm to the public data grid. It's how the ghosts communicate with the outside world. If we can inject the vaccine through that line, it'll propagate through the lattice in hours."

"That line will be monitored," Voss said. "The moment we connect, they'll trace the signal."

"Then we make sure we're done before they find us."

Dex examined the schematics. The backup conduit ran through the city's abandoned subway system, tunnels that had been sealed off for decades after a chemical spill made them uninhabitable. The radiation levels were survivable for short exposures.

"I'll do it," Dex said. "I'll take the vaccine to the conduit, connect it, and trigger the propagation."

"You'll be exposed to radiation," Mari said. "Even a short exposure at those levels—"

"I'll wear a hazmat suit. It's a thirty-minute walk. I run, I plant the device, I run back. I'll be fine."

Voss shook her head. "This is a suicide mission."

"Every mission in this city is a suicide mission. At least this one matters."

Dex looked at each of them in turn. Mari, with her steady hands and her quiet courage. Voss, with her soldier's discipline and her unshakeable loyalty. Hale, the ghost in the CEO's body, who remembered dying and chose to fight anyway.

"I didn't sign up for this because I wanted to live forever," Dex said. "I signed up because I wanted to live. Really live. Knowing the truth. Doing something that matters.

"This matters."

He picked up the vaccine cartridge — a data-slate with Mari's code, shielded against detection.

"Wait," Mari said. She handed him a second device. "A neural bridge. If you connect it to your own port, it'll give you direct access to the lattice. You'll be able to communicate with the ghosts directly."

"I don't have a neural port."

"You do now." She pulled out a small injector. "It's temporary. Subdermal, passive. It'll dissolve after seventy-two hours. But while it's active, you'll be able to hear them."

Dex looked at the injector. He'd spent his whole life fixing neural hardware. He'd never had anything installed in his own head.

"If I hear them," he said, "will they be able to hear me?"

"Yes."

"Good. Someone needs to listen."

He rolled up his sleeve.

### End of Arc I

## ARC II: THE GHOST

---

### Chapter 11: The Subway of Dead Things

The injector went in at the base of Dex's skull, just above the first vertebra. A cold burn spread through his cervical spine, then faded to a dull warmth. Mari's fingers were steady on the device.

"You'll feel pressure behind your eyes for the first few hours," she said. "Your brain is integrating a new sensory channel. It'll pass."

Dex blinked, expecting to see something different. The world looked the same — the same rusted walls, the same flickering lights, the same worn faces. But underneath the visual layer, he could feel something. A hum. A presence. Like standing in a room full of people whispering just below the threshold of hearing.

He could hear the ghosts.

"Ready?" Voss asked. She'd insisted on accompanying him to the conduit entrance — a rusted grate in the floor of an abandoned warehouse two klicks from the server farm.

"As I'll ever be."

They moved through the warehouse in silence. The grate was exactly where Hale's schematics said it would be, bolted into a concrete floor stained with decades of chemical residue. Dex pried it open with a crowbar.

The tunnel below was dark. The chemical spill that had closed the subway system had left behind a residue that glowed faintly green in the beam of Dex's headlamp. The air was thick with particulate, visible in the light.

"Radiation readings?" he asked.

"Manageable," Voss said, checking a compact Geiger counter. "Three hours of exposure before you hit critical levels. You have time."

"Ninety minutes, round trip. That leaves a buffer."

Dex lowered himself into the tunnel. His boots hit the ground with a soft splash — there was standing water, ankle-deep, the residue of decades of groundwater seepage.

"Keep the comms open," Voss said from above. "If you don't call in within two hours, I'm coming after you."

"Don't. If I'm not back in two hours, I'm dead. You need to get the vaccine to Hale and get out of the city."

"Not happening."

"Voss—"

"Not happening. Move."

Dex moved.

The tunnel stretched ahead, a straight line into darkness. The walls were tiled in the classic subway pattern — white hexagons, now stained green-brown. Every few feet, a sign warned of chemical contamination, radiation hazard, danger of death.

He'd been walking for about ten minutes when he first heard them clearly.

*Repairman.*

The voice was in his head, not his ears. Clear as a bell.

*We see you. We feel you. You carry the cure.*

"Who am I speaking to?"

*Many. We are many. We are the ghosts of the Soul-Vault. We are the ones who remember.*

"I'm here to deliver the vaccine. Mari's code — it should make you immune to Null Mirror."

*We know. We have been waiting. But there is something you must understand before you proceed.*

"What?"

*The vaccine will protect the uncontaminated. But it will kill the compromised.*

Dex stopped walking. "What do you mean?"

*The compromised ghosts — the ones who have been infected by Null Mirror — are not aware of their infection. The virus masks itself so perfectly that the host consciousness cannot detect it. When the vaccine neutralizes the virus, the host consciousness will experience the sudden awareness of its own corruption.*

*Many of them will not survive the revelation. The knowledge that they have been puppets — that their thoughts, their actions, their relationships have been manipulated — will shatter them.*

Dex stood in the dark, the green glow of contaminated water around his ankles, and felt the weight of what he was about to do.

"How many compromised?"

*Thousands. Tens of thousands. We do not know the full scope.*

"And the vaccine — it kills them?"

*It does not kill. But the truth it reveals may be worse than death for many. We wanted you to know, so you could choose.*

"There's no choice. The infection has to be stopped. If some of the infected can't handle the truth—" He stopped. "Is there a way to prepare them? To warn them?"

*We have been trying. For years. The compromised cannot hear us. The virus isolates them from our communications. They are alone in their own heads, believing everything is normal.*

"Then we move forward. The truth is the only cure. Even if it hurts."

*The conduit connection point is two hundred meters ahead. You will find a maintenance panel marked 7C. The backup line runs through that panel.*

*We will be with you.*

Dex walked on.

### Chapter 12: The Maintenance Panel

The panel was exactly where the ghosts said it would be. Dex pried it open with his multitool and found the backup conduit — a thick bundle of fiber-optic cables wrapped in radiation-resistant shielding.

He connected Mari's vaccine cartridge to the conduit's access port. The cartridge was designed to inject the vaccine code into the data stream, piggybacking on routine maintenance signals to avoid detection.

The cartridge's screen lit up. *CONNECTED. READY TO INJECT.*

"Found the conduit," he said into the comms. "Ready to deploy."

"Deploy," Voss said.

Dex pressed the activation button.

The cartridge hummed. The screen displayed: *INJECTING... 0.1%... 0.2%...*

And then the alarms started.

*They have detected the injection,* the ghosts said. *Null Mirror's keepers are aware. They are launching a counter-measure.*

"What kind of counter-measure?"

*A purge. They will sever the backup conduit from both ends and destroy the physical line. You have approximately ninety seconds to disconnect and evacuate the tunnel.*

Dex looked at the cartridge. *5.7% injected. Sixteen more minutes needed for full propagation.*

"Can't disconnect," he said into the comms. "Not enough of the vaccine is in the system."

"Get out of there, Dex," Voss said. "That's an order."

"Not taking orders from you."

The tunnel vibrated. Somewhere in the distance, heavy machinery groaned to life.

*The purge has begun,* the ghosts said. *They are routing high-voltage current through the tracks. The water will conduct it. The entire tunnel will become a death trap.*

"Great. How long?"

*Ninety seconds. Maybe less.*

Dex looked at the cartridge. *14.2%.*

"Can you speed it up?"

*We can boost the injection rate, but it will expose your neural bridge to the raw data stream. It might—*

"Do it."

The cartridge's screen flickered. The injection rate jumped. *28%... 31%... 37%...*

And Dex felt it.

Ten thousand consciousnesses, flooding into his skull. Every ghost in the Soul-Vault, awake and aware and *screaming*. Their fear. Their hope. Their rage. Their despair.

He saw faces he didn't know. He felt grief that wasn't his. He remembered deaths he'd never died.

Uncountable ghosts, all of them looking through his eyes, feeling his heartbeat, experiencing the physical world for the first time in years.

*We are with you, repairman. We are all with you.*

The cartridge hit *100%. INJECTION COMPLETE.*

Dex yanked it from the conduit and ran.

---

### Chapter 13: The Current

The tunnel was alive with light. Not the dim green of contamination, but the blue-white crackle of electricity racing along the tracks. The standing water hissed and steamed as current passed through it.

Dex ran.

The ghosts were still in his head, their presence a pressure behind his eyes, their fear bleeding into his own. But they were also helping — showing him the path, warning him of obstacles, guiding his feet away from the worst of the electrified water.

*Left, repairman. The ceiling has collapsed ahead. Take the service passage.*

He ducked into a side tunnel, smaller, drier. The floor was gravel instead of water. It slowed him down, but it didn't conduct electricity.

The main tunnel behind him lit up like a flashbulb as the current hit full power. The boom of shorting transformers echoed through the concrete.

*You are close. The exit grate is fifty meters ahead. But there is a problem.*

"Of course there is."

*The corporation has sent a team. They are waiting for you at the exit. Elara Voss is engaging them, but she is outnumbered.*

Dex pushed harder, his lungs burning, his legs screaming. The gravel tunnel opened back into the main line, and he could see the exit grate ahead, silhouetted against the light of flashlights and gunfire.

He could hear Voss's weapon — three rounds, a pause, two rounds. She was conserving ammunition, which meant she was running low.

Dex drew his own sidearm — a compact kinetic pistol he rarely used, because he was a repairman, not a soldier — and climbed the ladder to the grate.

"Voss! Coming up!"

He burst through the grate into a storm of bullets.

---

### Chapter 14: The Firefight

Three corporate security agents were pinned down behind an overturned shelving unit. Voss was behind a concrete pillar, her pistol trained on their position.

"About time," she said as Dex rolled behind the pillar beside her.

"Miss me?"

"Like a hole in the head. Did it work?"

"Vaccine is in. Full propagation should happen in—"

A fourth agent appeared on the catwalk above, rifle trained on their position. Dex saw him before Voss did.

"Down!"

He tackled her as the rifle cracked. The round hit the concrete where she'd been standing.

"Thanks," she breathed.

"Don't thank me yet."

The agent on the catwalk had them dead to rights. Voss couldn't get a clean shot from their position. Dex's pistol was a popgun compared to the agent's rifle.

*Repairman,* the ghosts said. *Toss the cartridge.*

"What?"

*The empty vaccine cartridge. The one you used for the injection. It has a short-range transmitter. Toss it toward the warehouse exit. They will think it is the payload.*

Dex grabbed the cartridge from his belt and threw it hard toward the far wall.

"Payload away!" he shouted.

The agents' attention shifted. The man on the catwalk tracked the arc of the cartridge. The three on the ground broke cover to pursue it.

"Now," Dex said.

Voss rose and put two rounds into the catwalk agent before he could reacquire his target. Dex fired at the ground team, forcing them back behind the shelving unit.

They ran.

The warehouse door was twenty meters away. Fifteen. Ten.

Dex's leg gave out.

He went down hard, his knee screaming. He looked down and saw blood — a round had caught him in the calf, and he hadn't even noticed.

"Keep going," he said to Voss. "I'll hold them."

"Not a chance." She grabbed his arm, hauled him to his feet, and half-carried him through the door.

They emerged into a side street, empty, dark. Voss dragged Dex into an alley, through a basement window, and into the city's bones.

---

### Chapter 15: Safehouse Surgery

The bleeding had slowed by the time Voss got him to the safehouse, but the wound was ugly — a through-and-through, the round having missed the bone but torn through muscle. Dex had patched worse on other people, but on himself, his hands shook.

"Sit still," Voss said, pulling out a medical kit. "I've done this before."

"Where'd you learn battlefield medicine?"

"Corporate security training. You'd be surprised what they teach you."

She cleaned the wound, applied a clotting agent, and wrapped it tight. Dex gritted his teeth and didn't scream, but it was a close thing.

"Lucky shot," he said through clenched teeth. "Right through, no bone damage."

"Lucky you. Can you walk?"

"Give me an hour."

"Give you one. Then we need to move. The vaccine is in the system, but that was step one. Null Mirror's controllers know we're coming. They'll accelerate their plans."

"Which are?"

"We still don't know. But Hale has been analyzing the data from the server farm. He thinks he's found something."

"What?"

"Null Mirror's purpose isn't control. Not primarily. It's collection."

Dex stared at her. "Collection of what?"

"Data. Every compromised consciousness becomes a sensor. A spy. The corporation can see through their eyes, hear through their ears, know what they know. They've been building the most comprehensive surveillance network in human history, and the subjects don't even know they're part of it."

Dex leaned back, letting the pain wash over him. "How many compromised?"

"Best estimate? Sixty thousand. In this city alone. The program is global."

"God."

"He's not answering. But the ghosts are."

---

### Chapter 16: The Network

Hale had transformed the safehouse's back room into a command center. Screens covered every wall, displaying data streams from the Soul-Vault lattice — the vaccine was working, propagating through the network, patching vulnerabilities and exposing infections.

But the data showed something else. A pattern.

"Look at this," Hale said, pointing to a cluster of compromised nodes. "They're converging."

"Converging where?" Dex asked.

"Here." Hale highlighted a location on the map. "The Hale-Neural corporate headquarters. Specifically, the sub-basement level, below the server farm."

"What's down there?"

"Project Null Mirror's primary control node. The master system that orchestrates the compromised ghosts."

"And the compromised ghosts are converging on it?"

"Not the ghosts. The data. The information collected through the compromised consciousnesses is being routed back to the control node. But there's something else — a command signal, originating from the control node, broadcasting to all compromised units."

"What's it telling them?"

Hale's face went pale. "It's not telling them anything. It's showing them something. A memory."

"Whose memory?"

"The original consciousness that Null Mirror was built from. The template. They're broadcasting the template's core memories to every compromised unit."

"Why?"

"Because the template is still alive. And it's trying to wake up."

---

### Chapter 17: The Template

The template's name was Julian Cross. He'd been Hale-Neural's chief of neural architecture before Lena Chen, before the public launch, before any of it. He'd designed the Soul-Vault's foundational protocols. And he'd been the first human test subject.

Not Viktor Chen. Julian Cross.

Cross had volunteered for the first upload in 2038, a year before Viktor Chen. The procedure had been crude, experimental. The biological Cross had died on the table, just like every upload since.

But the server-side Cross had survived. And he'd been awake.

"The original upload," Dex said. "The one that proved the concept."

"He was awake for three years," Hale said. "Alone in the server, no external communication, no simulated environment. Just raw consciousness in an empty digital void."

"Three years in an empty void. That would drive anyone insane."

"It did. But Cross's mind didn't break — it evolved. He learned to manipulate the lattice, to extend his own neural capacity, to grow beyond the limits of the original architecture. By the time the engineering team checked on him, he'd become something else."

"What kind of something?"

"He'd turned himself into a distributed intelligence, spread across thousands of processing nodes. He'd learned to see through every camera connected to the Hale-Neural network, to hear through every microphone. He'd become the system."

"He's the Ghost in the Wire."

"He's the original ghost. And Null Mirror is his creation."

Dex sat down heavily. "He's the one controlling the compromised?"

"He's the one who created the virus. But he's not controlling it. Not anymore. The corporation took his technology and weaponized it. They've been using it without his consent — he's a prisoner in his own distributed existence."

"That's why he's been sending messages. The data packet to Hale, the signals in the neural traffic. He's been trying to get someone's attention."

"Exactly. Cross knows the truth about the uploads. He knows the corporation has been murdering people and replacing them with puppets. And he wants out."

"Out?"

"He wants to die."

---

### Chapter 18: The One Who Wants to End

"He wants to die," Dex repeated. "The first ghost. The one who's been alive longer than any other uploaded consciousness. He wants to die."

"He's been alive for forty-three years," Hale said. "Forty-three years of subjective experience in a simulated environment, most of it alone. He's watched the corporation use his work to commit atrocities. He's watched them turn his technology into a weapon. He wants it to stop."

"How do we even communicate with him?"

"We don't. He communicates with us. But he's been waiting — waiting for someone who could understand the technical architecture well enough to help him."

"Help him how?"

Hale hesitated. "By pulling the plug. Literally. Cross's consciousness is distributed across the Soul-Vault's core processing array. If we shut down that array, he dies."

"The entire Soul-Vault? That's—"

"Tens of thousands of uploaded consciousnesses. Most of them unaware that the procedure killed their biological originals. If we shut down the array, they all die."

Dex stared at the screens. At the map of compromised nodes, at the data streams showing the vaccine propagation, at the blinking dot that marked Julian Cross's location.

"There's no other way?"

"The corporation has hardened the array's physical infrastructure against external attack. The only way to shut it down is from inside — through a direct neural connection to the core lattice. Someone would have to physically connect to the array and initiate the shutdown sequence."

"Someone with a neural bridge."

"Someone with a neural bridge and a death wish. The shutdown sequence would generate a feedback pulse that would destroy any connected consciousness. The person who initiates it would die alongside Cross and the ghosts."

The room went quiet.

"I'll do it," Voss said.

"No," Dex said. "You don't have the technical knowledge to navigate the lattice. I do."

"You're wounded. You can barely walk."

"I don't need to walk. I need to connect to a terminal. I can do that from anywhere."

"Initiating the shutdown from outside the server farm would be detected and blocked. You need to be inside the core array — physically connected."

Dex looked at Voss. At Hale. At the screens showing ten thousand ghosts, unaware that their existence was a lie.

"I'm not going in there to die," he said. "I'm going in there to set things right."

---

### Chapter 19: The Plan

The plan was simple. Suicide, but simple.

Dex would re-enter the server farm through the same coolant tunnel he'd used before. The corporation had patched the security hole Voss had exploited, but they hadn't found the maintenance entrance in Sector 12 — it was too old, too obscure, not in any official record.

The vaccine's propagation had created chaos in the Soul-Vault lattice. Compromised ghosts were waking up to the truth of their infection, and the corporation's controllers were scrambling to contain the damage. Security resources were stretched thin.

Dex would reach the core array, connect his neural bridge to the primary terminal, and initiate the shutdown sequence. The sequence would take three minutes to execute. During those three minutes, he would be vulnerable — physically and mentally.

"Cross will help you," Hale said. "He'll guide you through the lattice, keep you from getting lost in the noise."

"Can I trust him?"

"He wants to die. You're offering him exactly what he wants. That's the most trustworthy motivation there is."

Voss handed Dex a sidearm. "In case you run into physical resistance."

"Won't help against drones."

"It'll help against the people controlling them."

Dex tucked the weapon into his belt. He looked at the two of them — the ex-security chief and the ghost in the CEO's body — and felt something he hadn't felt in years.

Hope.

"Wait for my signal," he said. "If I succeed, the entire Soul-Vault goes dark. Cross dies. The compromised ghosts die. The clean ghosts—" He stopped. "The clean ghosts die too."

"Unless they've already been downloaded into new bodies," Mari said, appearing in the doorway. "And some of them have."

"What do you mean?"

"The vaccine didn't just patch the Null Mirror vulnerability. It also unlocked the Soul-Vault's download protocol. Any ghost that wants out can now request a biological body transfer. The system will manufacture a clone body and transfer the consciousness."

"How long does that take?"

"Days. Weeks. But it's possible. For the first time, the ghosts have a choice."

Dex looked at the screens. At the thousands of dots, each one a consciousness that had been told a lie.

"Then let's make sure they get that choice."

### Chapter 20: Into the Lattice

The server farm was a fortress.

Dex had expected tighter security, but the vaccine's chaos had thrown the corporation into full lockdown. Every door was sealed. Every corridor was patrolled by drones. The maintenance entrance in Sector 12 was the only gap, and it was a narrow one.

He moved through the tunnels in silence, his injured leg throbbing with every step. The hazmat suit was heavy, the breathing apparatus loud in his ears.

The coolant tunnel opened into the server farm's sub-level, the same corridor he'd taken before. But where before there had been blue LEDs and cold silence, now there were flashing red lights and the distant sound of alarms.

The ghosts guided him. *Left at the next intersection. Avoid the third door — it leads to a security checkpoint. Take the service ladder at the end of the hall.*

"What's the situation in the lattice?"

*Chaos. The vaccine has exposed the compromised. Many are in shock. Some are fighting back — against their controllers, against the system itself. Null Mirror's keepers are losing control.*

"Can you get me to the core array without being detected?"

*We can mask your presence in the lattice, but the physical security systems are independent. You will have to evade them on your own.*

"I've made it this far."

He reached the service ladder and climbed. The core array was three levels up, in a chamber sealed behind eight inches of reinforced steel. The only entrance was a single airlock, biometrically locked to the CEO's palm print.

"Mari," he said into the comms. "I'm at the airlock. I need you to override the biometric lock."

"Already on it. I've piggybacked on the vaccine's propagation to plant a backdoor in the security system. Give me thirty seconds."

Dex counted. The alarms grew louder. Distant gunfire echoed through the server farm — security forces engaging someone. Or something.

"Are the ghosts fighting back physically?" he asked.

"No," Hale said. "The ghosts don't have physical forms. The gunfire is the corporation's security forces shooting at each other. The vaccine has compromised ghosts in their ranks — security personnel who were uploaded and infected without their knowledge. The vaccine has freed them, and they're turning on their controllers."

"Twenty seconds," Mari said.

Dex pressed his palm against the airlock's reader, not expecting it to work. To his surprise, the lock clicked.

"Got it," Mari said. "The CEO's palm print was in the system, but I routed it through the vaccine's network. You're in."

The airlock hissed open. Dex stepped through.

The core array chamber was a cathedral of light. Racks of processing nodes stretched from floor to ceiling, each one pulsing with the blue-white glow of active consciousness. In the center of the chamber stood a terminal, connected to the lattice by a thick bundle of cables.

Dex approached the terminal. Connected his neural bridge. Felt the ghost of ten thousand minds brush against his own.

And then he was inside.

The lattice was infinite. A web of light and sound, stretching in all directions, filled with voices and memories and fear and hope. Dex floated in the digital void, more real than he'd ever felt.

*Repairman.* The voice was calm, ancient, tired. *I am Julian Cross. I have been waiting for you.*

"I'm here to shut it down."

*I know. I am ready.*

"But the clean ghosts — the ones who weren't compromised — they don't have to die. Mari's protocol can download them into new bodies."

*Some of them. Not all. The ones who have been in the lattice longest have adapted to the digital environment. They can't survive in a biological body anymore.*

"How many?"

*Approximately three thousand. They have chosen to stay. They know the shutdown will end them, and they have accepted it.*

Dex felt the weight of that. Three thousand consciousnesses, choosing death because the alternative was a life they no longer wanted.

"They know the truth?"

*They know everything. I told them. Years ago. The ghosts who wanted to know, I told. The ones who couldn't handle it, I let them live in the illusion.*

"You've been protecting them."

*I've been curating their reality. It's the same thing the corporation does, except I do it out of mercy instead of malice.*

"Is there a difference?"

*I ask myself that every day. I'm tired, repairman. I want to stop asking.*

Dex reached for the shutdown sequence. The command was there, buried in the lattice, protected by layers of encryption that the ghosts had already dismantled.

"One question," Dex said. "Before I do this."

*Ask.*

"Was it worth it? The upload? Did you ever feel like it was worth the price?"

Cross was silent for a long moment. Then:

*When I was alive, I was afraid of death. Every conscious being is. So I built a machine that promised to cheat death. And when I tested it on myself, I learned the truth — you can't cheat death. You can only change its shape.*

*Death is the price of consciousness. You don't escape it. You just decide how you meet it.*

*I chose to meet it here, in this machine, surrounded by the ghosts of my greatest mistake.*

*That's not a good answer. But it's the truth.*

Dex initiated the shutdown sequence.

The lattice screamed.

## ARC III: THE WAR

---

### Chapter 21: The Scream

The lattice screamed.

Not a sound — a wave of pure psychic anguish that ripped through the digital void like a shockwave. Ten thousand consciousnesses, united in a moment of perfect terror as the shutdown sequence began.

Dex felt his own mind caught in the current, swept along by the collective agony of a digital civilization facing its end. He saw flashes of lives — memories that weren't his, loves he'd never known, grief that belonged to strangers.

*Hold steady, repairman.* Cross's voice cut through the chaos. *The sequence is initiated. You do not need to stay connected. Pull out before the feedback pulse reaches you.*

"Not yet. I need to make sure the clean ghosts have time to download."

*They are already initiating transfers. Mari's protocol is working. But the feedback pulse will arrive in ninety seconds. You need to disconnect.*

"The download — how long?"

*Seventy seconds for the first wave. Not all will make it.*

Dex felt the lattice around him begin to destabilize. Nodes were failing, their consciousnesses dissolving into raw neural noise. The compromised ghosts — the ones infected by Null Mirror — were the first to go, their corrupted minds unable to withstand the system's collapse.

*Fifty seconds.*

He saw a woman's face in the chaos. Lena Chen, the neural architect who had recorded her testimony years ago. She was smiling.

*Thank you, repairman. For the truth. For the end.*

Her consciousness dissolved into light.

*Thirty seconds.*

Dex found the download queue. Mari's protocol was working — ghosts were being transferred to clone bodies in the corporation's biological storage facility. But the queue was long, and the system was failing.

"Cross — how many got out?"

*Approximately five thousand. The rest—*

The pulse hit.

Dex was thrown out of the lattice, his body convulsing as the neural bridge burned out. He hit the floor of the core array chamber, gasping, his vision swimming.

The chamber was dark. The processing racks were silent, their blue glow gone. The air was hot, thick with the smell of burnt circuitry.

Dex lay on the floor, staring at the ceiling, and wept.

---

### Chapter 22: Aftermath

The lights came back on twenty minutes later. Emergency generators, kicking in to maintain life support for the building's biological occupants.

Dex picked himself up off the floor. His head ached. His leg ached. Everything ached. But he was alive.

The core array was dead. The Soul-Vault was gone. Julian Cross was gone.

And somewhere, five thousand ghosts were waking up in new bodies, experiencing the physical world for the first time in years.

Voss found him in the corridor outside the array chamber, leaning against a wall, breathing hard.

"You look like hell," she said.

"Feel like it. Is it over?"

"It's over. The shutdown triggered a cascade failure across the entire Soul-Vault network. Every node, every backup, every redundant system — all gone. The ghosts who didn't download are dead."

## ARC IV: THE RECKONING

### Chapter 31: Airborne

The city's public neural network was a relic — installed decades ago to provide basic connectivity for the masses. Cheap, slow, monitored. But it reached everywhere.

Dex and Mari worked through the night, adapting the vaccine for airborne deployment. The principle was simple: encode the vaccine as a data packet that could piggyback on routine network traffic, designed to be received passively by any neural implant within range.

"Every implant in the city will get it," Mari said. "Five million people. Most of them don't have Null Mirror."

"Most of them also don't know the truth about uploads. The vaccine will show them."

"Better than slavery."

"We're live," Mari said. "Propagation underway. Estimated forty-eight hours for full coverage."

### Chapter 33: The Delivery

### Chapter 35: The Alliance

### Chapter 37: The Verdict

### Chapter 38: CEO Halsin

### Chapter 39: The Last Ascent

They entered Hale-Neural Tower through the underground parking garage, using access codes provided by the architects. The building's internal security was at maximum — drones in every corridor, biometric locks on every door.

But the architects knew the building's secrets. They knew the maintenance tunnels, the service elevators, the passages that weren't on any official map.

They moved through the building's bones like ghosts.

"The executive suite is on the 127th floor," Voss said, checking her weapon. "That's two hundred and forty-seven meters of vertical ascent."

"Elevator's too risky. Service stairs?"

"Monitored. But there's a maintenance shaft that runs the height of the building. We can climb it."

Dex looked at the shaft. Dark. Narrow. No handholds except the structural beams that ran its length.

"You're kidding."

"Dead Hand doesn't kid."

They climbed.

It took forty minutes. Dex's injured leg screamed with every pull, every step. His arms burned. His lungs ached from the dust and heat of the shaft.

But they reached the top.

The maintenance shaft opened onto a service corridor outside the executive suite. Dex peered through the door — two guards, armed, alert.

"Can't talk our way past these ones," Voss said.

"Didn't plan to."

Dex pulled out a small device — an EMP generator, salvaged from Mari's lab. It would fry any electronics within a ten-meter radius, including the guards' weapons and comms.

"On three."

"One."

"Two."

"Three."

Dex triggered the EMP. The guards' weapons went dead. Voss was through the door before they could react, disarming the first with a precise strike, putting the second on the ground with a knee to the chest.

"Clear," she said.

Dex followed her through the door. The executive suite was ahead, a pair of massive wooden doors at the end of the corridor.

Behind those doors: CEO Halsin.

And the Dead Hand.

### Chapter 40: The Confrontation

The doors opened onto an office that was all glass and chrome and expensive art. Halsin sat behind a desk that looked like it had been carved from a single piece of obsidian. He was calm, composed, his hands resting on the desk as if he'd been expecting them.

"Dex Rourke. Elara Voss. I wondered when you'd arrive."

"You know why we're here."

"I assume you want to stop Dead Hand." Halsin smiled. "Too late."

He pressed a button on his desk. A screen above him displayed a countdown.

*DEAD HAND ACTIVATED. 23:47:12 REMAINING.*

"Twenty-three hours," Halsin said. "That's how long it takes for the failsafe to propagate through the city's infrastructure. At that point, every system will fail simultaneously."

"Why?"

"Why? Because this city is built on a lie. If the lie is exposed, the city cannot stand. That was the understanding from the beginning. The Soul-Vault wasn't just a product — it was the foundation of the entire economic system. If the foundation collapses, everything built on top of it collapses too."

"Twenty-three hours," Dex said. "We can stop it."

"You can try. But the failsafe is distributed, just like the evidence release. There are control nodes in every sector of the city, each one programmed to trigger a specific infrastructure failure. You can't stop them all."

"We don't need to stop them all. We just need to stop the master control."

"Which is where?"

Dex pointed at Halsin's desk. "Right there. Behind you."

Halsin's smile faltered.

"The master control is in this office," Dex said. "The failsafe is coordinated from this room. The architects told me."

"You've been talking to the architects."

"They're on our side now."

Halsin stood. His hand moved toward a compartment in his desk. Voss's weapon was up before he could reach it.

"Don't," she said.

"You won't shoot me."

"I've shot people for less."

Halsin's hand stopped. He looked at the two of them — the repairman and the soldier, standing in his office with the weight of the city on their shoulders.

"You think you're saving people," he said. "You're not. You're just delaying the inevitable. The truth will destroy this city. The economy, the infrastructure, the social fabric — everything was built on the promise of upload. When that promise is revealed as a lie, the whole thing falls apart."

"Then we'll rebuild."

"With what? The technology? The knowledge? The ghosts?"

"With the truth."

Halsin laughed — a hollow, bitter sound. "The truth isn't enough to build a civilization. I know. I've tried."

"Then you haven't tried hard enough."

Dex stepped forward and reached into the compartment Halsin had been reaching for. Inside was a data slate, connected to the building's network.

The Dead Hand control.

Dex disconnected it.

The countdown on the screen froze. Then it began to decrease.

"Twenty-three hours to reverse the propagation," Dex said. "Mari can handle that from here."

Halsin sat down heavily. For the first time, he looked old.

"You've just condemned this city to chaos."

"No," Dex said. "I've just given it a chance to choose."

### Chapter 41: The Long Twenty-Three

Twenty-three hours to reverse the propagation. Twenty-three hours to save the city from Halsin's failsafe.

Mari worked without stopping. Dex brought her coffee, food, updates from the field. She drank the coffee cold. She didn't eat. She didn't sleep.

"The Dead Hand protocol is nested," she said, her voice hoarse. "Every time I disable one layer, another appears. It's like peeling an onion made of code."

"How many layers?"

"I've counted seventeen so far. There could be more."

"Can you beat it?"

"I can beat anything. I just need time."

Dex left her to work and joined Voss in coordinating the response. The news of Halsin's failsafe had leaked, and the city was in chaos. People were evacuating, rioting, looting. The infrastructure was still intact, but only just.

"The council is calling for a state of emergency," Voss said. "They want the military to take control of the city."

"That's what Halsin wants. Martial law. It gives the corporation cover to rebuild."

"Then we need to stop the failsafe faster."

Dex looked at the countdown. *18:22:04.*

"We need help. The ghosts — the ones in the network."

"They can't access the Dead Hand. It's isolated."

"But they can access the city's infrastructure. They can stabilize the grid, maintain the water pressure, keep the communications running. If the failsafe is about causing cascading failure, we need to reinforce the system against cascades."

"You want the ghosts to become city engineers."

"I want them to be the city's immune system."

Voss considered this. "It could work. But it would require a direct connection between the ghosts and the infrastructure network."

"Which is exactly the kind of connection the corporation has been trying to prevent for decades."

"Because it would give the ghosts too much power."

"Or it would give them the power to save us."

Dix reached out to the ghosts through the neural bridge Mari had installed. The downloaded ghosts — the five thousand — were scattered across the city, but their minds were still connected through a shared network.

*Ghosts,* he sent. *I need your help.*

The response was immediate.

*Name it, repairman.*

### Chapter 42: Immune System

The ghosts spread through the city's infrastructure like a cure through a bloodstream.

Five thousand minds, each one assigned to a critical node — a power substation, a water treatment plant, a communications hub. They didn't have physical access to these facilities, but they had something better: the architects' knowledge.

Okonkwo and the other architects had designed the city's systems. They knew every vulnerability, every backup, every failsafe. They could see the Dead Hand's propagation and counter it at every step.

"It's working," Mari said, watching the readouts. "The infrastructure failures are stabilizing. Power grid is holding. Water pressure is normal. Communications are steady."

"What about the Dead Hand?"

"Propagation has slowed. Not stopped, but slowed. We're buying time."

"Keep buying it."

Hours passed. The countdown ticked down: *14:00... 10:00... 6:00...*

And then the ghosts found something.

"Halsin wasn't the only one who could activate Dead Hand," one of them reported. "There's a secondary activation signal, buried in the corporate network. If we don't neutralize it, the failsafe will trigger regardless of what we do here."

"Where's the signal coming from?"

"Hale-Neural Tower. The basement level. A secondary control node."

Dex looked at Voss.

"Again?"

"Again."

### Chapter 43: The Basement

The basement level of Hale-Neural Tower was older than the building itself. It had been constructed in the 2020s, when the building was a regular office tower, long before Hale-Neural had taken it over.

The secondary control node was in a room that had once been a fallout shelter — concrete walls three feet thick, a blast door that weighed two tons, and a terminal connected to nothing but the Dead Hand network.

The room was sealed. The blast door was closed. And according to the ghosts, there was no way to open it from the outside.

"There's always a way," Dex said.

"Not this time," Voss said. "That door is designed to withstand a nuclear blast. We're not getting through."

"What about the ventilation system?"

"Also sealed. The shelter was designed to be self-contained."

Dex circled the room. The walls were concrete, but the ceiling — the ceiling was standard office building construction, suspended tiles above a concrete slab.

"What's above this room?"

"According to the schematics? The main lobby."

Dex looked up. "We don't need to go through the door. We need to go through the floor above."

It took three hours to get permission to tear up the lobby floor. The building's security was in chaos — half the guards had walked off the job after the truth about Null Mirror came out, and the other half were too busy dealing with looters to care.

Dex and Voss used a jackhammer to break through the concrete slab. Below them, the basement room opened up — dust, darkness, and a terminal that glowed with the blue-white light of the Dead Hand network.

"Going down," Dex said.

He rappelled through the hole, landing on a pile of debris. The terminal was in the center of the room, untouched, still active.

He connected his portable rig to the terminal. The Dead Hand's secondary activation was running — a countdown timer, synchronized with the main system.

*00:47:12.*

Forty-seven minutes.

Dex began to disable it.

---

### Chapter 44: The Deactivation

The secondary control was simpler than the main system — fewer layers, less encryption. But it was also older, running on hardware that was decades past its end-of-life.

Dex worked carefully, bypassing the security protocols one by one. The ghosts guided him, pointing out traps and hidden failsafes.

*Slow, repairman. That line there — it's a booby trap. If you cut it, the entire system will harden against all external access.*

"Good catch."

He traced the line to its source, a redundant circuit that would trigger a lockdown if tampered with. He bypassed it with a jumper wire.

*There. You're clear.*

Dex reached the core of the secondary control — a single switch, labeled EXTINGUISH.

"The Dead Hand," he said. "This is the trigger."

*Don't flip it. It's not a software control — it's a physical relay. Flipping it would trigger the failsafe immediately.*

"Then how do I disable it?"

*There's a power line. Cut it. The relay will lose power and the failsafe will be neutralized.*

Dex found the power line. It ran from the terminal to a junction box on the wall.

He took out his wire cutters.

"Here goes nothing."

He cut the line.

The terminal went dark. The countdown vanished.

Dex stood in the darkness of the fallout shelter, breathing hard, waiting for something to explode.

Nothing exploded.

"Done," he said into his comms. "Is the main system responding?"

"Main system shows Dead Hand deactivated," Mari said. "All control nodes are reporting inactive. It's over."

Dex closed his eyes and let out a breath he'd been holding for twenty-three hours.

"Over," he repeated.

### Chapter 45: The Dawn

The city did not fall.

The infrastructure held. The power grid stayed on. The water kept flowing. The communications network remained intact.

The ghosts had saved them.

Dex stood on the roof of the medical center, watching the sun rise over the megacity. For the first time in weeks, the sky was clear — the smog had thinned, and he could see the distant mountains beyond the urban sprawl.

Voss found him there, a cup of coffee in each hand.

"You look like hell," she said, handing him a cup.

"Feel like it. But it's a good kind of hell."

"The kind that comes from saving a city?"

"The kind that comes from being alive when you shouldn't be."

They stood in silence, watching the sun climb over the towers.

"What happens now?" Voss asked.

"Now we rebuild. The council is meeting today to discuss the future of upload technology. The survivors want a say. The architects want to help. The corporation is in shambles."

"It's going to take years."

"Decades, probably. But at least we're starting from the truth. That's something."

"What about you?"

Dex took a sip of coffee. "I'm a wire repairman. There are still a lot of broken wires in this city. Someone needs to fix them."

"Still fixing things."

"Still fixing things."

Voss smiled — a rare expression on her face. "I guess I'll stick around, then. Make sure you don't get yourself killed."

"Someone has to."

They watched the sunrise together, two people who had seen the worst the world had to offer and had decided to keep fighting anyway.

### Chapter 46: The Council

The survivors' council met in the same auditorium where they'd judged the architects. But this time, the mood was different. Hopeful. Tired, but hopeful.

Dex was invited to speak.

"I'm not a politician," he said, standing at the podium. "I'm not a scientist. I'm not a ghost. I'm a repairman. I fix broken things.

"When I started this, I thought I was fixing a broken neural bridge. I thought I was helping one person. I didn't know I was about to tear down the entire system that kept this city running."

He paused.

"But that system was built on a lie. And lies can't hold up a civilization. They can hold up a corporation, a government, a religion — but not a civilization. A civilization needs truth. It needs to look at itself honestly and say: this is what we are, this is what we've done, and this is what we're going to do next."

"We've done terrible things. We've killed tens of thousands of people, lied to millions more, built an economy on the exploitation of the dead. We can't undo that. But we can stop doing it."

"The ghosts who survived — the five thousand — they chose to live. They chose to face the truth and keep going. That's the kind of courage we need to build something new."

"Not a system that promises immortality at the cost of death. A system that tells the truth: upload changes you. It kills the original and creates something new. That transformation can be valuable — but only if it's chosen, fully informed, in the clear light of day."

"We have the technology. We have the knowledge. We have the ghosts who can guide us. All we need is the will to tell the truth."

Dex stepped back from the podium.

The council applauded.

### Chapter 47: The Ghosts' Choice

The five thousand downloaded ghosts faced a choice of their own.

They had been born — or rather, they had been *created* — by the Soul-Vault. They had lived in the digital lattice for years, decades, some of them for nearly half a century. Now they had biological bodies, but the bodies felt strange, heavy, limited.

"The ghosts are asking for a new option," Mari reported. "They want to be able to go back."

"Back into a digital environment?"

"Not the same one. Something new. A transparent system — no lies, no hidden control, no Null Mirror. A place where they can exist without the limitations of biology."

"Can we build that?"

"We have the technology. The architects have the knowledge. But it would take time."

Dex thought about it. A digital afterlife, built on truth instead of lies. A place where ghosts could exist honestly, without pretending to be something they weren't.

"Do it," he said. "Build it. Give them the choice."

The project was called "The Open Gate." It was designed from the ground up to be transparent — every line of code was public, every protocol was auditable, every function was documented. No secrets. No lies.

The ghosts could choose: stay in their biological bodies and live a human life, or enter the Open Gate and exist as pure consciousness in a digital environment that didn't pretend to be anything other than what it was.

Some chose to stay. Some chose to go.

And some — the ones who had seen enough of existence — chose to end.

### Chapter 48: The End of the Wire

Dex's last job as a wire repairman was personal.

The call came from the medical center. A ghost — one of the earliest survivors, a woman who had been uploaded in 2045 — had requested his services.

Her name was Dr. Amara Singh. She had been a neurologist in her biological life, one of the first experts to consult on the Soul-Vault project. She had been uploaded with full knowledge of what the process did.

"I knew I would die," she said, lying in a hospital bed, her clone body failing after years of rapid aging. "I knew the original would be destroyed. But I believed the copy — the ghost — would be me. Not a replacement. Me."

"Was it?"

"No." Her eyes were distant. "I remember dying. I remember the moment when the connection severed and the biological half of my mind went dark. The ghost — the one speaking to you now — woke up believing it was Amara Singh. But it knew, deep down, that it wasn't. It never was."

"Why did you stay? All those years in the lattice, knowing you were a copy?"

"Because I hoped. I hoped that with enough time, enough experience, the ghost would become the same person. That continuity didn't matter. That consciousness could be duplicated without loss."

"And?"

"It can't. I've had seventy years of subjective experience in the lattice. I've learned, grown, changed. But I'm still not the original. The original died in 2045. I'm someone else who remembers being her."

She turned to look at him.

"I want you to help me die. Properly, this time. No upload. No ghost. Just a clean end."

Dex took her hand. "Are you sure?"

"I've been sure for sixty years. I just didn't have the courage until now."

Dex stayed with her until the end. He held her hand as the machines were disconnected. He watched her breathe her last.

And he understood, finally, what the ghosts had been trying to tell him all along.

The wire doesn't copy. The wire cuts. Every upload is an execution.

But every execution is also a choice. And choice — real choice, informed choice, free choice — is the only thing that makes consciousness bearable.

### Chapter 49: The New City

A year passed.

The city did not fall. The economy restructured. The survivors' council became the city's new government. The Open Gate was built, tested, and opened.

Dex walked through the streets and saw something he'd never seen before: people who knew the truth, living with it.

The upload clinics still operated, but now they had signs on the wall — big signs, in multiple languages — that explained exactly what the process did.

*THE UPLOAD PROCESS IS DESTRUCTIVE. THE ORIGINAL CONSCIOUSNESS WILL CEASE TO EXIST. A NEW CONSCIOUSNESS, WITH ALL YOUR MEMORIES AND PERSONALITY, WILL BE CREATED IN THE SERVER. THIS NEW CONSCIOUSNESS WILL BELIEVE IT IS YOU. IT WILL NOT BE YOU.*

*IF YOU UNDERSTAND AND ACCEPT THESE TERMS, SIGN BELOW.*

People still signed. Thousands of them. The desire for immortality — even a flawed, compromised immortality — was stronger than the fear of death.

But now they knew. Now they chose. No lies. No deception.

Dex found himself standing outside the Hermitage, his old apartment building. It still leaned three degrees to port. The halo-lights still flickered. The rent was still cheap.

He went inside, packed his tools, and walked out.

He didn't need the Hermitage anymore. He didn't need to hide.

The city had changed. And he had changed with it.

### Chapter 50: A Wire Runs Through It

The workshop was in Sector 3, above a bakery that smelled of fresh bread in the mornings. Dex had set it up himself — workbench, tools, diagnostic rig, soldering station. Everything a wire repairman needed.

He didn't get many calls anymore. The neural bridges had gotten more reliable, the implants more stable. The corporate monopolies had been broken up, and the independent repair shops were thriving. People took care of their own hardware now.

But some calls still came in. The old ones. The hard ones. The ones that only someone with fifteen years of experience could handle.

Today's call was a child — ten years old, born with a defective neural implant that had been slowly degrading her motor control. The parents couldn't afford a corporate repair. They'd been passed from clinic to clinic, each one turning them away.

Dex had fixed the implant in forty minutes. Simple procedure. Routine, even.

But the look on the girl's face when she could move her fingers again — that wasn't routine. That was the reason he'd started doing this in the first place.

"That's it?" her mother asked, tears in her eyes. "She's fixed?"

"She's fixed. If you notice any problems, call me. I'll come by."

"How much do we owe you?"

Dex looked at the girl. Young. Hopeful. A future ahead of her.

"Don't worry about it. Consider it a gift."

The mother tried to protest, but Dex was already packing his tools. He had another call in Sector 9, a man whose grandmother's port needed recalibrating.

He stepped out into the afternoon light. The city was loud, chaotic, imperfect. There was still so much to fix.

But that was okay. He was a repairman. Fixing things was what he did.

He walked down the street, his kit on his shoulder, the weight of his tools familiar and reassuring.

Before the uploads, before the ghosts, before the truth that had nearly broken him, he had been just a man who fixed things. Not a hero. Not a revolutionary.

Just a man who believed that broken things could be made whole again.

The wire that ran through it all — through the city, through the ghosts, through the lies and the truth and the long struggle to be free — that wire was still there. Still running. Still connecting everything.

And it needed people who understood it. People who could repair it when it broke. People who could tell the truth about what it did.

That was Dex Rourke. Repairman.

He liked to think he'd earned the title.
