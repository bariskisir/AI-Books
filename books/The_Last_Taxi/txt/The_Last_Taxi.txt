Chapter 1: The Night Shift

The taxi idled at the rank in Taksim Square. Kemal watched the meter tick. 3.50 lira. Standing still.

He had been driving for fourteen years. Before that, his father drove the same cab — a 2004 Fiat with a cracked rearview and upholstery that smelled of stale tea and cigarettes. The company had repainted it twice, replaced the engine once, but the smell never changed.

At forty-seven, Kemal knew the city the way a doctor knows a patient's body. Every pulse point, every blockage, every street that swelled with traffic at the wrong hour. Istanbul was a living thing. It breathed through its ferries and bled through its broken sewers and coughed through the exhaust of a million cars.

Tonight the air was thick with late summer heat. The tourists had gone home. The square was quieter than it should have been.

Kemal lit a cigarette, cracked the window, and waited.

The other drivers in the rank were playing backgammon on a cardboard box. Mehmet from stall seven had won forty lira and was bragging about it. Kemal did not play. He watched the fountain in the center of the square, the way the light caught the water and scattered it.

A girl appeared at his window. He had not seen her approach.

She was twelve, maybe younger. School uniform. White blouse, navy skirt, knees scraped. Dark hair tied back. She looked at him with eyes that did not belong to a child.

"Are you free?" she asked.

Kemal stubbed out his cigarette. "Where are you going?"

"Üsküdar," she said. "Çınar Sokak."

The street name did not ring a bell. He knew Üsküdar. He did not know Çınar Sokak.

"Get in," he said.

She climbed into the back seat, closed the door, and said nothing more. Kemal pulled out of the rank, the meter resetting with a click. 4.00 lira. The night had begun.


Chapter 2: The Girl

The Girl sat in the center of the back seat, knees pressed together, hands folded in her lap. She held a small object wrapped in tissue paper.

Kemal checked his mirrors. Her face was half in shadow, half in the green glow of the dashboard lights. She watched the buildings slide past with a familiarity that unsettled him.

"You're out late," he said.

"So are you."

"Fair point."

They crossed through Beyoğlu, past the neon signs and the empty tram tracks. The streetlights threw orange pools onto the asphalt. A cat darted across the road. Kemal braked. The Girl did not flinch.

"I don't have enough money," she said.

"I figured."

"Will you take me anyway?"

Kemal considered this. He had done worse favors for strangers. Once he had driven a woman to the hospital in the middle of a coup scare. Once he had transported a corpse — a heart attack in the back seat — to the morgue because the ambulance was stuck in traffic.

"Yes," he said.

The Girl nodded as if she had expected this.

"Everyone helps me," she said. "I don't know why."

"You look like you need it."

She smiled. It was a small, precise thing, like she had practiced it.

"I need to go home," she said. "But I don't know where home is anymore."

Kemal said nothing. He drove toward the bridge.


Chapter 3: The Ferry That Stopped Running

"I took the ferry this morning," The Girl said.

Kemal glanced in the mirror. "Which one?"

"Kadıköy to Karaköy. The one that goes under the bridge."

"That line stopped running in 2019."

The Girl shook her head. "No. It was running today. I sat on the upper deck. There was a man selling simit and a woman with a baby who would not stop crying. The water was very green."

Kemal said nothing.

"I sat next to an old man who told me he had been riding that ferry for fifty years. He said the city used to smell different. He said he could tell the season by the color of the Bosphorus."

"What color was it today?"

"Green," she said. "Spring green. He said that meant a hard winter was coming."

Kemal merged onto the bridge. The Bosphorus spread out below them, black and silver under the half-moon. Ships sat at anchor like sleeping animals. The lights of the Asian shore flickered on the water.

"You know the ferry stopped running," he said again.

"I know."

"Then how did you ride it?"

She did not answer. She unwrapped the tissue paper in her lap and revealed a small wooden boat, hand-carved, the paint chipped. The word "Levent" was written on the hull in careful block letters.

"My father made this," she said. "Before I was born."


Chapter 4: The Toy Boat

Kemal looked at the boat in the rearview mirror. It was small enough to fit in a child's palm. The wood was dark, maybe walnut. The carving was crude but careful — the work of someone who had time and wanted to do it right.

"Levent," Kemal read. "That your father's name?"

"No," The Girl said. "It's mine."

Kemal waited.

"My father carved it for me," she said. "He started it when his mother was pregnant with me. He finished it the day I was born."

"That's a nice story."

"It's true."

"I'm not saying it isn't."

She held the boat up to the window, letting the streetlights catch it. "He said the wood came from a tree in our garden. A walnut tree. He said the tree was planted the year he was born, so the wood was exactly his age when I was born."

Kemal tried to do the math. He gave up.

"The tree is still there," The Girl said. "But the house is gone."

"What happened to it?"

She put the boat back in her lap. "There was an earthquake."

Kemal felt a chill that had nothing to do with the air conditioning. "When?"

"Next year," she said.

They drove in silence for a long time after that. Kemal wanted to ask more questions, but the words would not form. The Girl seemed content to let the silence sit.

She directed him off the main road onto a narrowing street, then another, then a cobblestone lane that he had never seen before.


Chapter 5: Çınar Sokak

The street was narrow, barely wide enough for the taxi. Old Ottoman houses leaned toward each other over the cobblestones, their wooden upper stories almost touching. A single streetlamp cast a weak yellow circle at the corner.

"Here," The Girl said.

Kemal stopped the car. The street was empty. No cars. No people. The houses were dark.

"Are you sure this is it?"

She opened the door. "Thank you for the ride."

"The address you gave me — this number doesn't exist on this street."

"It will," she said. "After the earthquake."

She stepped out of the taxi. The night air rushed in, cool and smelling of jasmine.

"Wait," Kemal said.

She turned.

"Who are you?"

She considered this. "I'm someone who is going to live a long time," she said. "And I'm someone who already died. It depends on the night."

She walked to a house that had no number, no light, no sign of life. The door opened before she touched it. She stepped inside. The door closed.

Kemal sat in the taxi for five minutes, engine running, staring at the door. It did not open again.

He checked the meter. It read 3:17 AM.

He had been driving for less than an hour.


Chapter 6: The Soldier

Kemal drove back toward the bridge, his mind circling the girl, the boat, the street that did not exist. He lit another cigarette. His hands were steady but his chest felt tight.

At the Galata Tower, a figure stepped into the road and raised an arm.

Kemal slowed. The figure wore a military uniform — old style, maybe ceremonial, maybe not. A young man, mid-twenties, with hollow eyes and a duffel bag slung over one shoulder.

"Where to?" Kemal asked.

The Soldier opened the back door and got in. "Selimiye Barracks," he said. "In Kadıköy."

Kemal turned in his seat. "That building has been closed for thirty years."

"Take me there anyway."

"You won't get in."

"That's not your problem."

The Soldier's voice was flat, worn down. He had not shaved in days. His uniform was rumpled, one button missing from the collar. There was a dark stain on his sleeve that could have been oil or could have been blood.

Kemal drove.

The Soldier did not look out the window. He stared at the back of Kemal's seat as if he could see through it, through the metal, through the road, to something else entirely.

"I know that girl you picked up," the Soldier said.

Kemal's hands tightened on the wheel.

"She's my daughter," the Soldier said. "I haven't met her yet."


Chapter 7: The Photograph

The Soldier reached into his duffel bag and pulled out a photograph. He held it up so Kemal could see it in the rearview mirror.

It was a woman in her early thirties. Dark hair. The same eyes as The Girl. The same precise, practiced smile.

"That's my wife," the Soldier said. "She doesn't know I'm coming."

"She looks like the girl."

"She is the girl. Twenty years older. Give or take."

Kemal's mind rejected this, but his gut accepted it. He had seen enough strange things in this city to know that Istanbul did not obey the usual rules. His father had told him once: time in this city is like water in a leaky pipe. It goes where it wants.

"Where are you coming from?" Kemal asked.

"A war."

"Which one?"

The Soldier was quiet for a moment. "I don't know what they call it. It hasn't started yet. But I was there. I saw it."

"How can you be in a war that hasn't started?"

The Soldier laughed. It was not a pleasant sound. "You picked up a girl who rode a ferry that stopped running. You took her to a street that doesn't exist. And you're worried about the timeline?"

Kemal had no answer.

They crossed the Bosphorus Bridge in silence. The Soldier put the photograph back in his bag and closed the zipper carefully, as if the bag contained something fragile.

"I shouldn't have come back," the Soldier said. "But I wanted to see her one more time."


Chapter 8: The Scar

Kemal merged onto the coastal road. The Sea of Marmara was dark and flat. A single cargo ship sat on the horizon, its lights steady.

"Can I ask you something?" Kemal said.

"You're going to anyway."

"The girl I picked up — she had a scar. Behind her left ear. Looked like a burn."

The Soldier said nothing.

"Do you have that scar?"

The Soldier did not answer. He shifted in his seat. The duffel bag rustled.

Kemal checked his mirror. The Soldier had turned his head to the window, giving Kemal a clear view of the left side of his neck, the skin behind his ear.

The scar was there. Same shape. Same size. A crescent moon, pale and puckered.

Kemal's throat went dry.

"What happened to you?" he asked.

"I was a child," the Soldier said. "I touched something I shouldn't have."

"What?"

"A stove. In a house in Üsküdar. The house with the walnut tree."

Kemal merged onto the Kadıköy approach, his mind spinning. The walnut tree. The girl had mentioned a walnut tree. The boat was made from it.

"What year were you born?" Kemal asked.

The Soldier smiled grimly. "Which time?"


Chapter 9: The Letter

The barracks appeared on the left — a sprawling Ottoman-era building, its stone walls crumbling, its windows boarded. The Soldier did not tell Kemal to stop. He simply watched it grow larger in the window.

"It looks different," he said.

"It looks abandoned."

"No. It looks peaceful. That's what's different."

Kemal pulled over across the street. The engine idled. The meter read 3:17 AM.

The Soldier noticed. "Your clock is broken."

"It was 3:17 an hour ago."

"It's 3:17 now."

They sat in silence. The Soldier did not get out.

"Will you read something for me?" the Soldier asked.

"I don't read well."

"You don't have to read well. You just have to listen."

The Soldier reached into his bag and pulled out an envelope, creased and stained, the paper soft with handling. He did not open it. He held it in both hands.

"It's from my daughter," he said. "She wrote it before I left for the war."

"How can she write to you if you haven't met her yet?"

"Because I've already lived this part. I know what the letter says."

"Then why do you need me to hear it?"

The Soldier opened the envelope. His hands were shaking. "Because she writes about her mother. About the night her mother met me in a taxi. She writes about you."

Kemal turned in his seat. "Me?"

"She writes: 'The driver's name was Kemal. He was kind. He took us both where we needed to go.'"


Chapter 10: The Burning City

The Soldier did not read the letter aloud. He folded it, put it back in the envelope, and returned it to his bag.

"I can't," he said. "Not tonight."

"Why not?"

"Because if I read it, it becomes real. And if it becomes real, I have to go back."

"Go back where?"

The Soldier opened the door. The night air rushed in, hot and dry. "To the war."

He stepped out. Kemal watched him stand on the sidewalk, looking at the barracks. The building seemed to waver in Kemal's vision — solid one moment, transparent the next. For a split second he saw it whole, windows lit, flag flying. Then it was ruined again.

The Soldier walked toward the gate. Before he passed through, he turned.

"There's one more thing," he said. "The city burns. I saw it. Not in the war. Later. The whole Golden Horn on fire."

"When?"

The Soldier did not answer. He stepped through the gate and vanished into the darkness of the barracks courtyard. The gate swung shut behind him.

Kemal sat in the taxi. The meter read 3:17 AM.

He checked his phone. The screen showed 3:17 AM.

He started the engine and drove.


Chapter 11: The Tea House

Kemal needed to think. He drove to a seaside çay bahçesi in Moda, one of the few open at this hour. The old man who ran it, Cemal, was sweeping the terrace. Three night fishermen sat at a corner table, silent, nursing glasses of tea.

Cemal looked up as Kemal approached. "You look like you've seen a ghost."

"Something like that."

Kemal ordered tea. He sat at a table facing the water. The Bosphorus was calm. A tanker moved slowly up the strait, its lights reflected in long streaks on the surface.

He tried to piece it together. The girl. The soldier. The toy boat. The scar. The street that did not exist. The clock that would not move.

His father had told him stories about this city. About the night the ferries ran backwards. About the man who hailed a cab in 1953 and gave an address in 2020. Kemal had never believed them. He had thought his father was telling tall tales, the way old drivers always do.

Now he was not so sure.

The tea arrived. Kemal drank it too fast and burned his tongue. The pain was real. That was something.

He sat for forty minutes, watching the water, waiting for the clock to change. It did not.

When he returned to the taxi, an old man in a wool coat was sitting in the front passenger seat.


Chapter 12: The Old Man

Kemal stopped at the driver's door. The old man did not look at him. He stared straight ahead through the windshield, his hands resting on a wooden cane.

It was summer. The old man wore a heavy wool coat, buttoned to the collar. A scarf was wrapped around his throat. His face was a map of wrinkles, crosshatched by decades of weather and worry.

"You left the door unlocked," the old man said. "Not smart in this neighborhood."

Kemal got in. He did not start the engine.

"Do I know you?"

The old man turned. His eyes were pale blue, the color of winter sky. They were the same eyes Kemal had seen in the rearview mirror twice tonight.

"Not yet," the old man said. "But you will."

"You're Levent."

The old man smiled. It was the same precise, practiced smile The Girl had worn.

"Not for a long time," he said. "But yes. I was Levent. I am Levent. Depending on the night."

Kemal gripped the steering wheel. "What is happening to me?"

The old man — Levent — reached over and patted Kemal's hand. His fingers were cold.

"You're driving the last taxi," he said. "The one that runs between the hours that don't exist. My father drove it. His father before him. And now you."


Chapter 13: The Inheritance

"My father drove this taxi?" Kemal asked.

Levent nodded. "1978 to 1995. Same plates. Same cracked rearview. He never told you?"

"He said he drove a cab. He never said it was special."

"They never do. The drivers, I mean. They don't know until the night comes."

Kemal stared at the dashboard. The meter still read 3:17. "What night?"

"The night the city opens," Levent said. "The night when the layers of time get thin and the ghosts come out. It happens once every generation. Someone has to drive the taxi. Someone has to carry the passengers between their lives."

"Why me?"

"Because your father drove it. And his father before him. The taxi chooses a bloodline. It's always been this way."

Kemal thought of his father, dead six years now. Heart attack at the wheel. The taxi had rolled into a ditch on the E-5 highway. No one else was hurt.

"Did my father know?" Kemal asked.

"He knew," Levent said. "He just never found the words to tell you. It's hard to explain. People think you're crazy."

"And you? Are you real?"

Levent laughed. The sound was thin, reedy. "Less than I was. More than I will be. I'm the version that knows how it ends."


Chapter 14: The Streets of Fatih

Levent directed Kemal through the back streets of Fatih. They passed through neighborhoods Kemal had never seen — narrow alleys where the buildings leaned so close they blocked the sky, courtyards with fountains that had not run in centuries, mosques whose minarets seemed to bend in the moonlight.

"Turn here," Levent said.

"There's no road."

"There is. You just can't see it yet."

Kemal turned. The taxi passed through a wall of shadow and emerged on a cobblestone street lined with gas lamps. The lamps were lit. There were no cars, no people, no sign of the twenty-first century.

"This is the old city," Levent said. "The real one. The one beneath the one tourists see."

They drove in silence. Kemal's hands were steady now. The fear had burned off, replaced by a strange calm.

"How many times have you done this?" Kemal asked.

"Done what?"

"This. The loop. Riding in this taxi."

Levent counted on his fingers. "Let me think. The first time was 1947. I was a boy. I didn't understand what was happening. The last time was... well. This time."

"And the girl? She's you?"

"She's my daughter. And she's me. And she's the woman you'll pick up next. We're the same person at different bends in the road. The city folds us together on nights like this."


Chapter 15: The Walnut Tree

"The first time I carved that boat," Levent said, "I was twenty-three years old. My wife was pregnant. I wanted to make something for the child. I didn't know if it would be a boy or a girl."

He paused. The taxi had stopped at a dead end. Beyond the windshield, a garden stretched into darkness. In the center of the garden stood a walnut tree, massive and old, its branches spread like a canopy.

"I carved it from the wood of that tree," Levent said. "The tree my grandfather planted the day I was born. I finished it the morning my daughter came into the world."

Kemal looked at the tree. It was huge, easily a hundred years old. Its leaves rustled in a wind he could not feel.

"I gave the boat to my daughter on her fifth birthday," Levent said. "She kept it her whole life. She gave it to her daughter on her fifth birthday. The boat is older than both of us now."

"Where is your daughter now?"

Levent was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was barely a whisper.

"She's waiting for me. At the end of the line."


Chapter 16: The Date in the Stone

"Drive forward," Levent said.

"There's no road. Just the garden."

"Drive."

Kemal pressed the accelerator. The taxi rolled forward, passed through the garden wall as if it were made of smoke, and emerged in a mosque courtyard. The stones were wet with dew. The minaret rose sharp against the dark sky.

"Stop here."

Kemal stopped. Levent opened his door and stepped out. His joints creaked. He leaned heavily on his cane.

"Come," he said.

Kemal followed him across the courtyard to a stone wall near the entrance. Levent pointed to an inscription carved in the marble.

"Read it."

Kemal bent closer. The inscription was a date in the Islamic calendar, followed by the Gregorian equivalent. He read the numbers.

"2027."

"Look closer."

Kemal looked again. The date was carved deep, the edges worn. It did not look new. It looked like it had been there for decades.

"That stone was laid in 1874," Levent said. "But the date 2027 has been on it since the day the mosque was built. Some things are written before they happen. The city knows its own future."

"What happens in 2027?"

Levent did not answer. He walked back to the taxi, moving slowly, his breath clouding in the warm air. "Take me to the water."


Chapter 17: The Loop

They drove to the shore. Levent asked Kemal to stop at a small park overlooking the Bosphorus. The water was black glass. A lone ferry sat at the dock, dark and silent.

"Every generation," Levent said, "the loop opens. A night when time stops running straight. The driver carries the passengers. The passengers carry the pieces of a single life. And at the end of the night, someone has to make a choice."

"What choice?"

"Whether to close the loop or keep it open."

Kemal turned off the engine. The silence was complete. No traffic. No wind. No birds.

"What happens if the loop stays open?"

Levent's eyes were distant. "The city forgets itself. The layers keep separating. Time becomes a knot that cannot be untied. Istanbul stops being one city and becomes a thousand, each layer unaware of the others."

"And if it closes?"

"Then the night ends. The clock moves. You go home. You wake up tomorrow and you remember nothing."

"Nothing?"

"Nothing. That's the price. The driver always forgets. It's a kindness."

Kemal looked at his hands. They were the hands of a forty-seven-year-old man who drove a taxi for a living. Callused. Clean. Ordinary.

"I don't want to forget," he said.

"You will anyway."


Chapter 18: The Vanishing

Levert turned to face Kemal. In the dim light, his face seemed to shift, younger one moment, ancient the next. The wool coat hung loose on his shoulders.

"Thank you," he said. "For the ride."

"I didn't do anything."

"You listened. That's more than most."

Kemal watched as Levent opened the door and stepped out. The old man walked toward the water, toward the dark ferry at the dock. His steps were slow but certain.

"Levent," Kemal called.

The old man stopped but did not turn.

"How does the loop close?"

"Someone has to give the boat back," Levent said. "Someone has to return it to the place where it began. The tree. The garden. The girl who hasn't been born yet."

He walked on. When he reached the edge of the dock, he did not stop. He kept walking. The darkness swallowed him.

Kemal blinked. The dock was empty. The ferry was gone.

The meter read 3:17 AM.


Chapter 19: The Woman Under the Light

Kemal drove without destination. He crossed the bridge again, heading back toward the European side. The city was empty. The streets were wet, as if it had rained, but the sky was clear.

On İstiklal Avenue, past the shuttered shops and the silent tram line, a woman stood under a streetlamp. She wore a dark coat. Her hair was pulled back. She raised her hand, and it trembled.

Kemal pulled over.

She opened the back door, then stopped. Something passed across her face — recognition, or something like it.

"Front seat," Kemal said. "If you want."

She closed the back door and opened the front. She sat down, pulled the seatbelt across her body, and clicked it into place. She smelled of jasmine and cigarette smoke.

"Where to?" Kemal asked.

"Beşiktaş," she said. "Akaretler. Number 47."

The address was his own apartment building.

Kemal did not react. He pulled away from the curb and drove.

"You know, you were a lot taller in my dreams," the woman said.

Kemal's knuckles went white on the steering wheel.

"In your dreams?"

"In the memory my father gave me. Before he left. He told me about the taxi driver who carried him across the night. He said you were a giant."


Chapter 20: The Daughter

"You're Levent's daughter," Kemal said.

"I'm his daughter, yes. And I'm also the girl you picked up in Taksim. The one with the boat."

Kemal looked at her. She was in her late thirties. The dark hair was streaked with grey. The eyes were the same — pale blue, winter sky — but they were older, sadder, weighed down by things she had seen.

"You're her. Grown up."

"I'm one of her. There are others. The loop creates copies. Every time the night opens, a new version splits off. I'm the one who stayed."

"Stayed where?"

Istanbul. Everyone else left. My father left. My daughter left. I stayed in the city, in the same house, on the same street. I watched the buildings change and the people change and the language change. But I never left."

Kemal drove slowly through the empty streets. The woman — Levent's daughter — stared through the windshield, her reflection ghostly in the glass.

"He talked about you," she said. "My father. Before he went into the hospital the last time. He said: find the taxi driver. He'll know what to do."

"Know what to do about what?"

"About the boat. About the loop. About the night that never ends."


Chapter 21: The Apartment Building

They arrived at Kemal's building. He did not park. He stopped in the middle of the street, engine running.

"You live here," she said. "Third floor. The window with the yellow curtain."

"How do you know that?"

"I told you. My father described everything. The taxi. Your face. The crack in the rearview mirror. The photograph of your daughter on the dashboard."

Kemal's breath caught. He had a photograph of his daughter, Aylin, wedged into a crack in the dashboard. He had put it there the day she was born. He had never told anyone about it.

"So you believe me now?" she asked.

"I believed you before. I just didn't want to."

She laughed. It was a sound that broke apart halfway through, becoming something else. A sob, maybe.

"I'm tired," she said. "I've been in this loop for thirty years. Every time I fall asleep, I wake up on the same night. The same taxi. The same conversation."

"Why?"

"Because I haven't finished. The loop won't close until someone gives the boat back."

"And you can't do it?"

"I tried. Many times. But the boat has to be given by someone outside the loop. Someone who isn't Levent and isn't connected to Levent. Someone like you."


Chapter 22: The Grief

Kemal turned off the engine. The silence settled around them like dust.

"Why me?" he asked.

"Because you're the driver. The taxi chose you. The same way it chose your father and his father before him."

"I don't want this."

"No one ever does."

She reached into her coat and pulled out the toy boat. The same boat. The one The Girl had carried. The one Levent had carved. It sat in her palm, small and worn, the paint almost gone.

"I've been carrying this for years," she said. "I found it in my mother's things after she died. She had kept it all her life. She said it was the only thing my father ever made."

Kemal took the boat. It was lighter than he expected. The word "Levent" was still visible on the hull, barely.

"Where do I take it?"

"The garden," she said. "The walnut tree. You have to bury it at the roots. That's where the loop started. That's where it ends."

"And the girl?"

"She'll be there. The young version. The one who hasn't lived through any of this yet. Give the boat to her. Tell her to plant it."

"What happens to her after that?"

The woman smiled. "She grows up. She lives a normal life. She forgets this night ever happened. The loop closes."


Chapter 23: The Instructions

Kemal held the boat. It felt significant in his hands, heavier than wood, heavier than it had any right to be.

"How do I find the garden?"

"It finds you. Drive. The city will take you where you need to go."

"And you? What happens to you?"

She opened the door. The night air rushed in, smelling of salt and diesel. "I go home. I go to bed. I wake up in the morning and the date has changed for the first time in thirty years."

She stepped out of the taxi. She stood on the empty street, looking up at his building, at the yellow curtain in the third-floor window.

"He never forgot," she said. "My father said the driver always forgets. But he was wrong. My father remembered everything. He just pretended not to."

"How do you know?"

"Because he told me. On his deathbed. He said the driver remembers in his dreams. The dreams are the price. You lose the memory when you're awake, but you live it every night when you sleep."

Kemal had been having strange dreams for years. He had never told anyone about them.

"Goodbye, Kemal," she said. "Thank you for carrying us."

She walked away. He watched her until she disappeared into the darkness between streetlights.

The boat sat on the passenger seat. The meter read 3:17 AM.


Chapter 24: The Boy

Kemal drove. He did not choose a direction. The streets seemed to choose themselves, turning left or right without his input. He passed through neighborhoods he knew and neighborhoods he had never seen.

In Karaköy, near the old fish market, a boy sat on the curb. He was small, maybe eight years old. He wore a sweater that was too big for him and shoes that were held together with tape.

Kemal pulled over.

"Are you lost?" he asked.

The boy looked up. His face was smudged with dirt. His eyes were pale blue.

"I ran away," the boy said.

"From where?"

"Home."

"We're going to need a better address than that."

The boy shrugged. He was Levent. Young Levent. The version of the old man before the soldier, before the daughter, before the boat.

"You're driving the taxi," the boy said.

Kemal's heart stopped. "How do you know about the taxi?"

"Everyone knows about the taxi. My father drove it. His father before him. I'm going to drive it someday."

Kemal stared at the boy. This was before everything. Before the war. Before the daughter. Before the wool coat and the cane and the vanishing at the dock. This was the beginning.


Chapter 25: The Simit

Kemal bought the boy a simit from an all-night bakery near the market. They sat on a bench overlooking the Golden Horn. The water was silver under the moon. A few fishing boats bobbed at their moorings.

The boy ate the simit in small, careful bites. He did not talk. Kemal watched him and thought about the old man he had met, the soldier, the girl, the woman. All this from one small boy eating bread in the dark.

"Why did you run away?" Kemal asked.

"I wanted to see the water."

"At three in the morning?"

"The water is best at three in the morning. No one else is looking at it."

Kemal had no argument for that.

"My father says I think too much," the boy said. "He says it will get me in trouble."

"Your father might be right."

"You sound like him."

Kemal did not know what to say to that. He watched the water. The boy watched the water. For a moment, they were just two people on a bench, sharing bread, looking at the sea.

"I have something that belongs to you," Kemal said.

The boy looked at him. "What?"

Kemal pulled out the toy boat. The boy's eyes went wide.

"You found it."

"Yes."

The boy took the boat carefully, reverently, as if it were made of glass. He turned it over in his hands.

"Where did you get it?" the boy asked.

"A friend. She said you lost it."


Chapter 26: The Scar

The boy held the boat and traced the word "Levent" with his fingertip.

"I carved this," he said. "Last week. My father helped me with the lettering."

"It's very good."

"It's not finished. I want to paint it. Blue and white. Like the old ferries."

Kemal looked at the boat. The wood was smooth, the carving clean. It did not look like the work of an eight-year-old.

The boy shifted on the bench, and his sweater pulled up, revealing a scar on his forearm. A crescent moon, pale and puckered.

Kemal's blood ran cold.

"What's that?" he asked.

The boy touched the scar. "Stove. When I was smaller. My mother was so angry."

The same scar. The girl had it. The soldier had it. The old man had it. Now the boy had it.

Every version of this person carried the same mark.

"Do you know what it means?" Kemal asked.

"It means I touched something hot and I shouldn't have."

"No. I mean the shape. The moon."

The boy looked at the scar. "It's a crescent. Like on the flag."

"Your flag."

The boy looked at him strangely. "Our flag."

Kemal sat back. An eight-year-old boy with a crescent moon scar. Istanbul beneath them. The Bosphorus silver in the darkness.

"Will you take me home?" the boy asked.

"Where is home?"

"Üsküdar. Çınar Sokak."


Chapter 27: The Address

They drove back across the bridge. The boy sat in the front seat, the boat in his lap. He hummed a tune Kemal did not recognize.

"What's that song?" Kemal asked.

"I don't know. My mother sings it."

"Sing the words."

The boy sang. The words were old Turkish, a lullaby about a fisherman who waited for his love to return from the sea. Kemal had heard it before. His own mother had sung it to him.

"Heading home," he said.

The boy directed him through Üsküdar, past the new apartment blocks, past the old wooden houses, down streets that narrowed until they became alleys. At the end of one alley, the garden appeared. The walnut tree rose against the sky.

The garden was real. The tree was real.

But there was no house. Just the garden, the tree, and a single patch of bare earth where a foundation might have been.

"Here," the boy said.

"This is where you live?"

"It's where I lived. Before the house fell down."

"When did it fall?"

"Next year."

Kemal's hands were shaking. He gripped the wheel to steady them.

"Levent," he said. "I need you to do something."

"What?"

"I need you to bury the boat. Here. Under the tree."

The boy looked at him. "Why?"

"Because that's where it belongs. That's where it started. If you bury it here, everything will be okay."

The boy considered this. He was eight years old, but his eyes held a gravity that did not belong to a child.

"Will I still get to carve another one?" he asked.

"Yes. But not this one. This one has to stay."


Chapter 28: The Burying

The boy climbed out of the taxi. He walked to the walnut tree, the boat in his hands. His small figure was silhouetted against the dark trunk of the tree.

Kemal watched.

The boy knelt at the base of the tree. He found a loose stone and used it to dig. The earth was soft. He worked quickly, like a child who had buried things before.

He placed the boat in the hole. He covered it with soil. He patted the earth flat.

Then he stood and looked back at the taxi.

Kemal got out. He walked to the tree and stood beside the boy.

"Did I do it right?" the boy asked.

"You did it right."

"Will I see you again?"

"No. You won't remember me."

The boy nodded, accepting this in the way that children accept strange things from adults.

"My father told me about you," the boy said. "Before he died. He said there was a man who would come and take me for a ride. He said I should listen to him."

"When did your father die?"

"Last week."

Kemal's chest tightened. "I'm sorry."

"It's okay. He was old. He was tired."

They stood together under the walnut tree as the first light of dawn touched the horizon. The sky turned grey, then pink, then gold.

"What happens now?" the boy asked.

"Now you go home. You grow up. You live your life."

"And you?"

Kemal looked at the taxi. At the meter that still read 3:17. At the city that was waking around him.

"I think I go home too," he said.


Chapter 29: The Dawn

The boy walked away through the garden. He did not look back. He passed through the wall at the edge of the garden — the wall that was solid, then not solid, then solid again — and disappeared.

Kemal stood alone under the walnut tree.

The sun was rising over the Asian shore. The Bosphorus caught the light, turned turquoise and gold. A ferry horn sounded in the distance. A real ferry, running on a real schedule.

He walked back to the taxi. He got in. He checked the meter.

It read 4:32 AM.

The clock had moved.

He sat for a moment, letting the reality of it settle. The night had ended. The loop had closed.

He started the engine and drove.

The streets were filling with morning traffic. Delivery trucks. Buses. Commuters in suits and school uniforms. The city was waking up, and none of them knew what had happened in the hours before dawn.

Kemal drove toward Taksim. He needed to return the taxi to the rank. He needed to go home. He needed to sleep.

But first, he needed to see the water one more time.


Chapter 30: The Morning Ferry

Kemal parked the taxi near the Karaköy ferry terminal. He bought a token and boarded the first ferry of the day.

He sat on the upper deck. The air was cool and clean. Seagulls circled overhead, crying their sharp cries.

The ferry pulled away from the dock. The city spread out on both sides — the minarets of the old city, the towers of the new, the bridges spanning the water between continents.

He had driven this city for fourteen years. He had never seen it like this.

The water was spring green, just as The Girl had described. A man walked past selling simit from a basket. A woman sat nearby with a baby who would not stop crying.

Kemal bought a simit. He ate it slowly, watching the shore.

He thought about the girl who rode a ferry that stopped running. The soldier who came back from a war that had not started. The old man who vanished into the darkness. The woman who knew his address. The boy who buried a boat under a tree.

He knew he would forget. That was the price. But for now, while the morning light was still new, he let himself remember.

The ferry reached the Kadıköy shore. Kemal stayed on board for the return trip. He rode the ferry all the way back, then again, then once more.

He was the last passenger when the ferry docked at Karaköy. The crew was already tying the lines.

"End of the line," the deckhand said.

Kemal nodded. He stepped off the ferry. The sun was fully up now. The city was loud and bright and alive.

He walked back to the taxi. The photograph of his daughter was still wedged in the dashboard. He touched it gently.

Then he drove home.


Chapter 31: The Dream

Kemal slept for fourteen hours.

He dreamed of water. Of a boat carved from walnut wood. Of a girl standing on a dock, holding something out to him. He could not see her face. He could not reach her.

He woke in the dark. The room was unfamiliar for a moment, then it resolved into his bedroom. The yellow curtains were drawn. The ceiling fan turned slowly.

He lay still, listening to the sounds of the city through the window. Traffic. Distant voices. The call to evening prayer from a mosque somewhere nearby.

He felt different. Hollowed out. Like something had been taken from him and he could not remember what it was.

He checked his phone. The date was the same as yesterday. No — it was the day after. He had lost a day somewhere.

He put the phone down. He closed his eyes.

The dream was already fading. By the time he got out of bed, he could not remember it at all.

That night, he went back to work.

The taxi was waiting in its usual spot in the rank. The meter was reset. The tank was full. The cracked rearview mirror showed him his own face, tired and ordinary.

He started the engine. He waited for the night to begin.


Chapter 32: The Routine

Weeks passed. The summer heat gave way to autumn. The tourists left. The streets filled with rain.

Kemal drove his shifts. He picked up passengers. He dropped them off. He did not think about the night of the loop.

But sometimes, in the quiet hours between fares, he would catch himself looking at the passenger seat. Expecting someone to be there. An old man. A woman. A boy.

No one was ever there.

The photograph of his daughter remained on the dashboard. He had started carrying it years ago, after the divorce, when he only saw Aylin on weekends. She was sixteen now, almost grown, with her mother's smile and his silence.

He called her one night during a break. She did not answer. He left a message.

"Just checking in. I love you. Call me when you can."

He hung up and stared at the phone. He had the strange sense that he had said goodbye to her already, somewhere, in a conversation he could not remember.

The meter ticked. 4.50 lira. Standing still.

He pulled out of the rank and drove into the night.


Chapter 33: The Passenger

It was November. The rain had been falling for three days. The Bosphorus was grey and choppy. The ferries ran late.

Kemal picked up a fare near the Spice Bazaar — a young woman with a suitcase, heading to the airport. She sat in the back seat. She did not talk.

Halfway across the bridge, she started crying.

Kemal said nothing. He handed her a box of tissues from the glove compartment.

"I'm sorry," she said. "I'm fine. I'm sorry."

"You don't have to be sorry."

"It's just — I'm leaving. I don't know when I'm coming back."

"Istanbul?"

"Yes."

Kemal nodded. He had heard this before. The city was full of people leaving, people staying, people not knowing which was worse.

"I stayed once," he said. "I don't know why."

The woman laughed through her tears. "That's the thing about this city. You can't explain it. You can only feel it."

Kemal drove on. The wipers beat a steady rhythm. The city lights blurred through the rain-streaked windows.

When they reached the airport, the woman paid and tipped generously. She paused at the door.

"Thank you," she said. "For the ride. And the tissues."

"Safe travels."

She walked into the terminal. Kemal watched her go. He had the strange feeling that he knew her, that he had known her for a long time, but the memory would not surface.

He drove back toward the city. The rain continued. The night was empty.


Chapter 34: The Call

Kemal's daughter called him at midnight.

"Dad?"

"Aylin. Everything okay?"

"Everything's fine. I just wanted to hear your voice."

Kemal pulled over. Something in her tone made him stop.

"What's wrong?"

"Nothing's wrong. I just had a weird dream about you. You were in a taxi, driving through a garden. There was a big tree. You were talking to a boy."

Kemal's skin prickled. "A boy?"

"Small. Dark hair. He had a toy boat."

The silence stretched between them. Kemal's heart was beating too fast.

"What happened in the dream?" he asked.

"You gave the boy something. I don't know what. And then you drove away. I woke up and I felt like I needed to call you."

Kemal closed his eyes. He had forgotten. The dream was gone. But his daughter had dreamed it for him.

"That's strange," he said.

"Yeah. I know. Anyway. I love you. I'll see you on the weekend."

"I love you too."

She hung up. Kemal sat in the taxi, the phone in his hand, the city glowing around him.

The loop was closed. He had paid the price. But somewhere, in the dreams of his daughter, the night lived on.


Chapter 35: The Winter

Winter came. The city turned grey and cold. Snow fell on the minarets, on the domes, on the Bosphorus that never froze.

Kemal drove through the snow, picking up passengers who shivered in the back seat. He kept the heat on high. He drank tea from the thermos his ex-wife had given him years ago and never asked for back.

He stopped dreaming. Or if he dreamed, he did not remember.

But he found himself driving through Üsküdar more often than necessary. He would take the long way home, passing through the back streets, looking for a garden with a walnut tree.

He never found it. The streets were different in daylight. The landmarks did not match.

One night in December, he took a fare to a house on Çınar Sokak. The street existed. He had checked. It was a small lane in Üsküdar, lined with new apartment buildings. No old houses. No gardens. No walnut tree.

The passenger was a young man in a military uniform, home for the holidays. He was cheerful, talkative, full of plans.

Kemal dropped him at his parents' house. The young man paid and wished him a happy new year.

Kemal watched him walk up the path. A woman opened the door. She had dark hair and pale blue eyes.

For a moment, the world tilted.

"Happy new year," the woman called.

Kemal nodded. He drove away.

He did not know why his hands were shaking.


Chapter 36: The New Year

On New Year's Eve, Kemal worked. The city was loud and bright. Fireworks exploded over the Bosphorus. The streets were clogged with celebrants.

He picked up a couple in Nişantaşı, a group of students in Beşiktaş, an old woman in Fatih who gave him a piece of baklava and told him he looked tired.

At midnight, he was stuck in traffic on the bridge. The fireworks were going off all around him. Red and green and gold reflections in the water.

He called Aylin.

"Happy new year, Dad."

"Happy new year, sweetheart."

"What are you doing?"

"Driving. What else?"

"Come home. It's midnight."

"I will. After this fare."

She laughed. "You always say that."

"When I'm right, I'm right."

They talked for a few more minutes. The traffic started moving. Kemal hung up and drove on.

The city stretched before him, ancient and new, full of ghosts and living people, full of loops and straight lines, full of stories he would never know.

He thought about his father. About the taxi. About the night he could not remember.

Maybe the loop was still open. Maybe it had never closed. Maybe he was still driving through it, night after night, carrying passengers between their lives.

He turned up the radio. A song from the 1980s was playing. A love song. He hummed along.

The meter ticked. 7.50 lira. Moving forward.


Chapter 37: The Photograph

Spring came early. The city softened. The trees along İstiklal Avenue budded green. The Bosphorus turned turquoise again.

Kemal was cleaning the taxi one afternoon, wiping down the seats, when he found something under the passenger seat.

A wooden boat.

It was small, hand-carved, the paint worn. The word "Levent" was barely visible on the hull.

He sat in the driver's seat, the boat in his hands, and stared at it.

He had no memory of this boat. He had no idea where it came from.

But his hands knew it. His hands held it the way they held the steering wheel — with familiarity, with care.

He turned it over. There was a date carved into the bottom: 2026.

The current year.

He put the boat on the dashboard, next to the photograph of his daughter. It fit perfectly there, as if it had always belonged.

That night, a girl in a school uniform climbed into the back seat.

"Are you free?" she asked.

Kemal looked in the rearview mirror. Dark hair. Pale blue eyes. A scar behind her left ear.

"Where are you going?" he asked.

"Üsküdar," she said. "Çınar Sokak."

The boat sat on the dashboard. Kemal looked at it. Then he looked at the girl.

"Get in," he said.

The loop began again.


Chapter 38: The Echo

Kemal drove. The girl talked. She told him about a ferry that stopped running. About a wooden boat her father carved. About an earthquake that had not happened yet.

He listened. He did not interrupt.

The words felt familiar, like a song he had heard many times but could not name.

"Everyone helps me," the girl said. "I don't know why."

Kemal did not answer. He was watching the road, but he was also watching the dashboard, where the boat sat next to his daughter's photograph.

The boat he had found under the seat. The boat he did not remember.

"I need to go home," the girl said. "But I don't know where home is anymore."

He crossed the bridge. The water was dark. The stars were out.

When they reached Üsküdar, she directed him through streets he had never seen but somehow knew. Every turn felt right. Every corner felt expected.

They stopped at a cobblestone lane. The houses were old, leaning toward each other. Jasmine grew over a garden wall.

"Thank you," the girl said. She got out. She did not pay.

Kemal watched her walk to a house with a walnut tree in the garden. The door opened before she reached it. She stepped inside.

He sat in the taxi, engine running, the boat on the dashboard.

He wondered if he had done this before.

He was certain he had.


Chapter 39: The Soldier Returns

Kemal did not go back to the rank. He drove.

Near the Galata Tower, a young man in uniform stepped into the road.

Kemal stopped. The soldier got in.

"Selimiye Barracks," he said.

"Closed."

"Take me anyway."

The soldier's eyes were hollow. His uniform was rumpled. His duffel bag was stained.

Kemal drove toward the bridge.

"Did you pick up a girl tonight?" the soldier asked.

"Yes."

"She's my daughter."

Kemal nodded. He did not argue. He did not question.

"You know," the soldier said, "I've done this before."

"How many times?"

"I don't know. Many. The loop keeps opening. Every time, I come back. Every time, I try to stay. Every time, I fail."

Kemal drove in silence. The boat sat on the dashboard. The soldier saw it.

"You found it."

"Yes."

"I lost it a long time ago. Or I will lose it. Time doesn't work the way I think it does anymore."

Kemal picked up the boat and handed it over his shoulder. The soldier took it.

"I'll give it to her," the soldier said. "The girl. My daughter. She needs to carry it for a while."

"What happens after that?"

The soldier smiled. "You know what happens. You've done this before. You just don't remember."


Chapter 40: The Recognition

The soldier did not get out at the barracks. He asked Kemal to keep driving.

They crossed the bridge again, heading back to the European side. The soldier held the boat in his hands, turning it over and over.

"I knew your father," the soldier said.

"My father died six years ago."

"I knew him before that."

Kemal said nothing.

"He drove this same taxi," the soldier said. "Same plates. Same cracked mirror. Same photograph on the dashboard. Different photograph. His daughter — your sister. She died when she was young."

Kemal's hands tightened on the wheel. "I don't have a sister."

"You did. You don't remember her. Your parents never talked about her. But she existed. She was real. My daughter — my first daughter — was her friend."

Kemal's vision blurred. He pulled over. The engine idled.

"What are you saying?"

"I'm saying the loop is older than you think. It goes back further than this night. Your father drove it. His father drove it. The taxi carries the weight of every driver who ever sat in this seat."

Kemal looked at the photograph on his dashboard. His daughter. Aylin. Alive. Real.

"You have a daughter," the soldier said. "She's the reason you're in the loop. She's the one who closes it."

"I don't understand."

"Neither do I. But that's the thing about the loop. Understanding comes after."


Chapter 41: The Old Man's Return

They drove through the night. The soldier directed them through Fatih, through streets Kemal knew and streets he did not.

At a mosque courtyard, the old man was waiting.

He stood by the fountain, leaning on his cane, his wool coat buttoned against the warm night air.

Kemal stopped. The soldier got out. The two of them — young and old versions of the same person — stood facing each other.

They did not speak. They did not need to.

The old man walked to the taxi and got in the front seat. The soldier got in the back.

"Three in one night," the old man said. "That's a record."

"You're not surprised to see me," Kemal said.

"I'm never surprised. I've been waiting for this night for sixty years."

"The girl is waiting too," the old man said. "She's at the garden. She has the boat. She needs you to take it from her."

"Why me?"

"Because you're the driver. You're the only one who can carry it between them."

The old man's voice was thin, worn. He looked at Kemal with eyes that had seen too much.

"My daughter — the grown one — she told you to bury the boat," the old man said. "But you can't bury it. You have to give it back. To the beginning."

The old man pointed a trembling finger at the dashboard. At the photograph.

"To your daughter."


Chapter 42: The Understanding

Kemal sat in the taxi, the boat in his hands, the night stretching around him like a living thing.

"Aylin," he said. "My daughter."

"Your daughter," the old man said. "She's the one who breaks the loop. She's the one who remembers when you forget."

"Why?"

"Because she's not part of the bloodline. The taxi chose your father and you. But Aylin is outside the choice. She can hold the memory. She can carry the boat forward."

Kemal thought of his daughter's phone call. The dream she had. The garden. The boy with the boat.

"She already knows," he said. "She dreamed it."

"The loop reaches her. It always has. She was born into the orbit of the taxi. Every night you drive, she dreams."

Kemal put the boat on the dashboard. He started the engine.

"Where do I find her?"

"She's waiting at your apartment. She came home from university for the weekend."

Kemal looked at the clock. 4:32 AM. The night was almost over.

He drove.


Chapter 43: The Apartment

Kemal parked the taxi outside his building. The yellow curtain in the third-floor window was lit.

He took the boat from the dashboard. He climbed the stairs. His legs were heavy. His heart was beating too fast.

He unlocked the door. The apartment was quiet. Aylin was sitting on the couch, a book open in her lap.

She looked up when he came in. "Dad. You're home early."

"I need to give you something."

He crossed the room and held out the boat. Aylin looked at it. Her eyes widened.

"Where did you get this?" she asked.

"It's a long story."

"It's the boat from my dream."

"Yes."

She took it carefully. She held it the way the girl had held it, the way the woman had held it, the way the boy had held it. With recognition. With reverence.

"There's a garden," she said. "A walnut tree. I have to bury it there."

"No," Kemal said. "You have to keep it. You have to remember."

She looked at him. "Remember what?"

"Everything. The girl. The soldier. The old man. The loop. You have to remember so that when the night comes again, you'll know what to do."

Aylin looked at the boat. She turned it over in her hands.

"I've been dreaming about this boat my whole life," she said. "I thought it was just a dream."

"It's not just a dream. It's memory. My memory. The memory I can't keep."

Kemal sat down next to her. He was tired. He was forty-seven years old and he was tired in a way that went deeper than sleep.

"I love you," he said.

"I love you too, Dad."

They sat together in the quiet apartment, the boat between them, as the sun rose over Istanbul.


Chapter 44: The Daylight

Kemal did not sleep. He sat at the kitchen table, drinking tea, watching the light change over the city.

Aylin had gone to bed, the boat on her nightstand. She had promised to keep it safe.

He thought about his father. About the sister he never knew. About the weight the taxi carried.

The phone rang. He answered.

"Kemal?"

It was the dispatcher. "You're late for your shift."

Kemal looked at the clock. 6:00 PM. He had been sitting at the table for twelve hours.

"I'll be there."

He hung up. He washed his face. He put on his jacket.

On his way out, he stopped at Aylin's door. She was asleep, the boat clutched to her chest.

He touched the doorframe. Then he left.

The taxi was waiting. He got in. He started the engine.

He was not surprised to see a toy boat on the passenger seat.

It was the same boat. Or a different one. He could not tell.

He picked it up. He put it on the dashboard.

Then he drove into the city, into the night, into the loop that never really ended.

He did not know if he was remembering the past or living the future.

He did not think it mattered.


Chapter 45: The Repetition

Kemal drove. The hours passed. The city pulsed around him.

He picked up a businessman, a tourist couple, a woman in labor. Each one ordinary. Each one real.

At 2:00 AM, he found himself on Çınar Sokak.

He did not know how he got there. The street was empty. The houses were dark.

He stopped the taxi. He got out. He walked to the garden wall.

The walnut tree was visible over the wall, its branches black against the sky.

He stood there for a long time.

He did not know what he was waiting for.

A figure emerged from the shadows. A woman. Middle-aged. Dark hair streaked with grey.

"You came back," she said.

He knew her voice. He did not know why.

"I don't remember," he said.

"I know."

"Who are you?"

She smiled. It was a precise, practiced smile, worn smooth by years of use.

"I'm the one who stayed."

She stepped closer. Her face was familiar and strange. He knew her eyes. He knew the curve of her jaw. He knew the way she held herself, the way she tilted her head, the way she looked at him as if she had been looking at him for a very long time.

"Give me your hand," she said.

He held out his hand. She placed something in his palm. Small. Wooden.

A toy boat.

"You'll know what to do with it," she said.

She turned and walked away. He watched her go.

She did not look back.


Chapter 46: The Inheritance

Kemal stood in the garden, the boat in his hand, the walnut tree above him.

He did not remember. But his hands knew.

He knelt at the base of the tree. The earth was soft. He dug with his fingers.

Beneath the soil, he found something hard. He pulled it out.

Another boat. Older. More worn. The paint was almost gone. The word "Levent" was a ghost on the hull.

He held both boats. The old one. The new one. The same boat, separated by time.

He understood, suddenly, what he had to do.

He placed the new boat in the hole. He covered it with soil. He kept the old boat in his hand.

He walked back to the taxi.

The girl was waiting in the back seat.

"Did you find it?" she asked.

He showed her the old boat.

"I think this belongs to you."

She took it. She held it the way you hold something precious. Something lost.

"Thank you," she said.

"Where to?"

"Home."

"Where is home?"

She pointed through the windshield. At the garden. At the tree. At the house that had not fallen yet.

"I live there," she said. "With my father. He's waiting for me."

Kemal did not drive. He opened his door. He walked around and opened her door.

"Then go home," he said.

She got out. She looked up at him. Her eyes were pale blue. Her face was the face of every passenger he had carried tonight.

"You'll forget," she said.

"Yes."

She nodded. "That's okay. I'll remember for you."

She walked toward the house. The door opened. A man stood in the doorway. Young. Dark hair. The same pale blue eyes.

Levent. The boy grown up. Before the war. Before the old age. Before the loop began.

He looked at Kemal. He nodded.

Kemal nodded back.

The door closed.

The night ended.


Chapter 47: The Empty Hour

Kemal drove through the empty city. The streets were his. The stars were his.

He crossed the bridge one more time. The water was black and silver. The ships were sleeping.

He had no passengers. He drove alone.

The boat — the old one, the one he had dug up — sat on the dashboard next to Aylin's photograph.

He did not know where it came from. He did not know why it was there.

But he left it.

He drove until the sky turned grey, then pink, then gold.

At a tea house in Moda, he stopped. Cemal was sweeping the terrace.

"You look tired," Cemal said.

"I am."

"Same dream?"

Kemal looked at him. "What dream?"

Cemal shrugged. "The one every driver has. The one about the water."

Kemal bought tea. He sat facing the Bosphorus.

The city was waking. The first ferry of the day was crossing the strait. The minarets caught the morning light.

He finished his tea. He returned to the taxi.

The boat was still on the dashboard.

He touched it. The wood was warm.

Then he drove home.


Chapter 48: The Memory

Aylin was waiting for him. She stood in the doorway of the apartment, a cup of tea in her hand.

"Did you sleep?" she asked.

"No."

"Me neither."

She held something out. The boat. The one he had given her.

"I've been thinking about it all night," she said. "Your face when you gave it to me. You looked like you were giving me a secret."

"I don't remember where I got it."

"That's the thing about secrets. Sometimes you carry them without knowing you're carrying them."

Kemal took the boat. He held it in both hands.

"I think it's been in our family for a long time," he said.

"I think so too."

He put the boat in his pocket. It fit perfectly.

"Come," he said. "I'll make you breakfast."

They ate together at the kitchen table. Eggs and bread and olives. The tea was strong. The morning light was yellow.

Aylin talked about university. About a boy she liked. About the future.

Kemal listened.

He did not tell her about the garden. About the girl. About the old man and the soldier. About the loop.

He did not need to.

She already knew.

And someday, when he forgot completely, she would remember for him.


Chapter 49: The Return

That night, Kemal returned to the rank.

The other drivers were playing backgammon. Mehmet had won forty lira. He was still bragging about it.

Kemal parked in his usual spot. He lit a cigarette. He waited.

The city hummed around him. The fountain in Taksim Square caught the light and scattered it.

He did not know what he was waiting for.

A girl appeared at his window. School uniform. Dark hair. Pale blue eyes.

"Are you free?" she asked.

Kemal looked at her. Looked at the face he had seen a thousand times. Looked at the face he had never seen before.

"Where are you going?"

"Üsküdar," she said. "Çınar Sokak."

The name meant nothing to him.

He reached for the ignition. His hand brushed against something in his pocket.

Small. Wooden.

A toy boat.

He pulled it out. Looked at it. Looked at the girl.

She smiled.

"Get in," he said.

She climbed into the back seat. He started the engine.

The meter ticked. 4.00 lira.

He drove into the night.

He did not know where he was going.

But the city knew.

It always knew.


Chapter 50: The Last Taxi

The taxi hummed through the streets of Istanbul. Kemal drove. The girl sat in the back.

Neither of them spoke.

The boat was in his pocket. The photograph was on the dashboard. The city was spread out before them like a living map.

They crossed the bridge. The water was dark. The stars were out.

Kemal thought about his father. About the stories he had never believed. About the night that kept repeating.

He thought about the girl in the back seat, who was everyone and no one, who carried the weight of a life she had not yet lived.

He thought about Aylin, asleep in her bed, the memory of a loop she had never entered.

He thought about the boat in his pocket, which had been carved and buried and dug up and given away so many times that the wood remembered every hand that had held it.

"Your father," Kemal said. "What was his name?"

"Levent," the girl said.

"Levent," Kemal repeated.

It meant nothing to him.

It meant everything.

He looked in the rearview mirror. The girl was watching him with eyes that were older than the city.

"Will you remember me?" she asked.

"No."

"Good."

She smiled. It was the saddest smile he had ever seen.

They drove through the night, the old taxi, the girl who carried time in her pocket, the driver who carried nothing but a photograph and a wooden boat.

Istanbul surrounded them. Ancient. Eternal. Full of ghosts.

The meter ticked forward.

3:17 AM. 3:18 AM. 3:19 AM.

The clock was moving.

The loop was holding. The loop was breaking. The loop was both, always, for as long as someone drove the last taxi through the streets of the city that never stopped becoming itself.

The girl leaned back in her seat.

"Tell me a story," she said.

Kemal thought for a moment. Then he told her about a ferry that stopped running. About a boy who carved a boat from a walnut tree. About a city that folded time around itself like a prayer.

He drove. He talked.

The girl listened.

The night went on.

The last taxi kept moving.
