The Reversal
Barış Kısır

Chapter 1: The Intake

Cassia Morrigan took the file from the tray and read the name twice. Henry Voss. Referred by Dr. Philip Lyle, general practitioner, with a one-line note: Anxiety. Possible dissociative episodes. Please assess.

She did not recognize the name. The intake clerk had slotted him in at four o'clock, between Mrs. Tennant and the new Tuesday patient. Cassia preferred four o'clock cases. They were the last of the day and required less energy. The clinic was quieter then. The waiting room had thinned to a single person.

Henry Voss arrived three minutes early. He was tall, soft-shouldered, with a narrow face and gray hair combed straight back. He wore a gray suit, no tie. His shoes were polished. He shook her hand with a careful pressure.

"Dr. Morrigan," he said. "Thank you for seeing me."

"Sit, Mr. Voss."

He sat in the chair across from her. He did not look around the office the way new patients often did. He did not look at the diplomas on the wall. He looked at her.

"How are you settling in?" Cassia asked. The standard question.

"Settling in where?"

"Here. The clinic. The city. Whatever brought you to this office."

"I was not aware I had moved." He said it without humor.

Cassia wrote nothing. She studied him. No tremor. No rapid blinking. No signs of stimulant use. His pupils were even.

"What brings you to therapy, Mr. Voss?"

He folded his hands in his lap. "I have been told I am difficult to know."

"By whom?"

"My wife. My colleagues. The people closest to me."

"And what do they say is the difficulty?"

"They say I am always the one asking questions. Even when I am meant to be answering them." He paused. "They say I have been doing it for so long I have forgotten which side of the chair I should sit on."

Cassia felt the small professional lift of an interesting case. She wrote: Borderline dissociative features. Possible narcissistic overlay. Begin process.

She did not notice that Henry Voss had been watching her write, and that his eyes followed the pen with a familiar, almost clinical attention.

---

Chapter 2: The Question

The second session came on a Tuesday. Henry sat in the same chair. Same gray suit. Same hands folded in his lap. Cassia had spent the weekend thinking about him. He had not appeared in her dreams, which surprised her. He had the shape of a man who belonged in dreams.

She opened his file. "Last week you mentioned you ask too many questions."

"Yes."

"Do you remember what you were asking about?"

He thought for a moment. "I was asking about your dog."

Cassia kept her face still. She had not, to her knowledge, mentioned a dog. "What dog would that be?"

"The one you had at nine. A brown terrier. You called it Bean."

The clinic was quiet. The radiator clicked once. Cassia heard the blood in her ears.

"I don't recall mentioning a dog," she said.

"You did not. That is the point. I knew before you mentioned it."

Cassia wrote in his file: Confabulation? Pseudo-reminiscence? She underlined the question mark. "Where did you hear this name, Mr. Voss?"

"Nowhere. I have simply always known it."

"People do not simply know things, Mr. Voss."

"No," Henry said. "But they forget that they know them. That is the more common problem."

Cassia ended the session early. She told him she would see him Thursday. He nodded, rose, and closed the door softly behind him. She sat for a long time at her desk. The brown terrier had been named Bean. She had not spoken that name aloud in twenty years.

---

Chapter 3: The File

Cassia spent the following morning on the phone. She wanted to reach Dr. Philip Lyle, the referring physician. The clinic switchboard did not recognize the name. The number on the referral slip rang to a recorded message: the line had been disconnected. Cassia searched the state medical registry. There was no Philip Lyle licensed in the city. There was no Philip Lyle licensed in the state.

She opened Henry Voss's file. The referral slip was typed on plain paper, no letterhead. The signature at the bottom was a single, fluid P. It could have been a P. It could have been almost any letter.

The rest of the file was thinner than she remembered. An intake form with the basics: name, age, address. The address was a post office box. The emergency contact was a woman named Mara Voss, listed as wife, with a number Cassia dialed twice and reached no one.

She sat with the file open on her desk. The radiator clicked. The afternoon light was beginning to fade. She thought about the brown terrier. About the name Bean. About the man who had known it before she said it.

She considered discharging Henry. The referral could not be verified. There was no history. The patient might be dangerous. She reached for the phone to call Adam Wilkes, her supervisor, and then she put it down. She told herself she wanted one more session. She told herself it was good clinical practice.

She did not write down the real reason, which was that Henry Voss was the most interesting case she had seen in ten years, and she did not want to give it up.

---

Chapter 4: The Office

Cassia gave Henry a tour of the clinic on his third visit. She did this only for new patients who had expressed anxiety about the space. Henry had not expressed anxiety. Henry had asked, politely, to see the office where he would be sitting.

The clinic occupied the second floor of a converted townhouse. There was a waiting room at the front with three leather chairs, a tall window, a small table stacked with magazines no one read. Down a short hallway were three consulting rooms. Cassia's was the middle one.

She opened the door. The room was small, square, with a single window facing the street. There was her desk, her chair, the patient chair. On the desk: a notepad, a pen, a small ceramic bowl she had bought in Lisbon. On the wall: her medical school diploma, her licensing certificate, a print of a Magritte she had bought at auction.

Henry stood in the doorway for a long moment. He looked at the patient chair. He did not look at the desk. He did not look at the diplomas. He looked at the chair the way a person looks at a chair they have seen in a dream.

"Whose chair is that?" he asked.

"Yours," Cassia said. "For the sessions."

"And that one?"

"Mine."

He nodded slowly. "Of course."

Cassia gestured to the locked drawer at the side of her desk. "I keep personal notes in there. Not part of your file. Just reminders to myself."

Henry looked at the drawer. He did not ask what was in it. He walked to the patient chair and sat down. He placed his hands on the arms. He closed his eyes for a moment.

"Comfortable?" Cassia asked.

"Very," he said. "Thank you for showing me."

He opened his eyes and looked at her. "You have a beautiful office, Dr. Morrigan. I am glad to be back in it."

She did not ask him what he meant.

---

Chapter 5: The Other Chair

In the next session, Henry did not speak for the first ten minutes. Cassia was accustomed to silence. She let the room hold it. Outside, a delivery truck passed. The radiator clicked. The light in the window shifted.

When Henry finally spoke, he said: "I have been in that chair before."

"This chair?"

"Yes."

"When?"

"A long time ago." He paused. "It was the other chair, then. The one you are sitting in."

Cassia did not move. "I have been the only psychiatrist in this office for eleven years, Mr. Voss."

"I am not disputing that. I am saying I have sat in your chair. In another office. Years ago. The chair feels different from that side. The light falls differently. The patient looks different."

Cassia wrote nothing. She watched him.

"Patients often believe they have met their therapist before," she said, carefully. "It is a common transference."

"Yes," he said. "I imagine it is."

He was quiet for a moment. Then he said: "I remember the first thing you said to me, in the other office. You said, 'What brings you here today?' And I said, 'A long walk.' And you laughed, even though you tried not to. I could see the corner of your mouth move."

Cassia's chest tightened. She had said those words, years ago, to a different patient in a different city. The patient had said, "A long walk." She had laughed. The memory was small, private, untouched.

"I don't recall that conversation," she said.

"Of course you do," Henry said. "You simply have not found the file for it yet."

---

Chapter 6: The Childhood

Cassia did not sleep that night. She sat at her kitchen table with a cup of tea that went cold. She thought about the brown terrier. About the office in another city. About the patient who had said, "A long walk."

She had been twenty-eight. She had worked for six months at a community clinic in a town she had since left. The patient had been a young man with a soft voice. She had laughed at his answer. She had not thought of him in fifteen years.

In the morning, she drove to the clinic earlier than usual. She unlocked her office. She opened the bottom drawer of the filing cabinet where she kept old notes. She found a manila folder with a name she had not thought about: David Reiss. Intake date eleven years ago. Diagnosis: generalized anxiety. Treatment duration: four sessions. Discharged: transferred to another city.

She opened the file. The intake photo was a small black-and-white of a young man with a soft face. Henry Voss was not David Reiss. They were not the same age, not the same height, not the same coloring. But the photo had something in the eyes, something Cassia could not place, that reminded her of the man in her gray suit.

She put the file back. She closed the drawer. She sat at her desk. The morning light came through the window. The patient chair was empty. She thought: I am imagining this. She did not write down that she had thought it.

At four o'clock, Henry Voss arrived for his session. He sat in his chair. He folded his hands. He said, "You went looking this morning."

Cassia's throat went dry. "I don't know what you mean."

"You went to the filing cabinet. You opened the bottom drawer. You found a folder with a name in it. You put it back."

She did not answer.

"You were always good at putting things back," Henry said.

---

Chapter 7: The Sessions Resume

Cassia forced herself back into her routine. She had seven other patients. They were her practice. They were not Henry Voss.

Mrs. Eloise Tennant came on Tuesday morning. She was seventy-eight, widowed three years, and absolutely certain that her husband Bernard was still in the house. "I hear him in the kitchen in the morning," she said. "He opens the cupboards the same way he used to."

Cassia had been seeing Eloise for two years. She knew the shape of the grief. She knew how to sit with it.

"Do you talk to him?" Cassia asked.

"I don't have to. He knows I'm there."

Eloise smiled, and Cassia felt the small clinical sorrow she always felt in these sessions. She wrote: Bereavement, complicated. Continue supportive therapy.

Jonah Park came on Tuesday afternoon. He was seventeen, brought in by his mother. He was a quiet boy with a habit of watching Cassia the way one watches a window.

"How have you been, Jonah?"

"Fine."

"School?"

"Fine."

"Your mother says you've been having trouble sleeping."

"I'm thinking about things."

"What kinds of things?"

"Things I should have done differently."

Cassia wrote: Adolescent anxiety. Possible rumination. Refer for CBT.

The new Tuesday patient was a man in his fifties named Frank Doolan. He had been sent by his wife. He believed someone had replaced him. Not his wife. Him. The man sitting in the chair was not the man his wife had married.

"Explain to me what you mean," Cassia said.

"I mean I look in the mirror and there is someone there. He has my face. He wears my clothes. But he is not me."

Cassia wrote: Capgras variant. Possible prodromal psychotic features.

She did not notice that the man across from her was describing a feeling she had begun to recognize in her own office window.

---

Chapter 8: The Ringing

It was a small thing. She was in her office on Wednesday evening, late, finishing notes. The clinic was empty. The hallway lights were off. She heard a phone ring.

She looked at the phone on her desk. It was not ringing. She listened. The ring came again, soft, from somewhere down the hall. It could have been the front desk. It could have been a phone in one of the other consulting rooms.

She stood. She walked into the hallway. The rings stopped. The hallway was dark. The doors to the other offices were closed. She went back into her room. The phone on her desk was silent. Her own mobile, in her bag, was silent.

She sat down. She picked up the desk phone to dial her answering service. There was a dial tone. There was a number on the small screen where the call had been logged. The number was hers. Her own home number.

She put the phone down.

She did not call her answering service. She did not call Adam. She finished her notes by the light of the desk lamp. She left the building at eight o'clock. The stairwell was quiet. The street was quiet. The city was doing what cities do at night, which is pretend to be empty.

At home, she checked her answering machine. There were no missed calls. There was no message. She stood in her kitchen and listened to the silence and thought, briefly, that her own home number had called her at work, and that the line had been disconnected for two years.

She did not write any of this down.

---

Chapter 9: The Reflection

On Thursday morning, Cassia walked to the clinic in a fine rain. The clinic was on a quiet street of brownstones. The windows of her office faced the street. From the street, if one looked up, one could see into the room. She had never minded this. Most patients preferred a window.

She let herself in the front door. She climbed the stairs. She passed the waiting room. She unlocked her office and turned on the lights. The patient chair was empty. The desk lamp was in its place. The Magritte print on the wall showed a man with an apple in front of his face.

She sat at her desk. She opened her bag. She took out her compact to check her face before the day began. She had a habit of doing this. It was a small vanity she allowed herself in the morning.

She opened the compact. The face in the small mirror was hers. Brown hair, gray eyes, a small scar at the corner of her mouth from a fall when she was eleven. She looked for a long time. She watched the face watch her.

The face did not move when she moved.

She blinked. The face blinked, half a second late.

She closed the compact. She put it in her bag. She sat very still for a long time. Outside, a car passed. The rain picked up. The window was dark with water.

She thought, briefly, that the face in the compact had been someone else. She did not write that thought down. She did not tell Adam. She told herself that reflections are always a half-second late, that the brain is easily tricked, that the morning was gray and she had not eaten.

She opened the compact again. The face was hers. The face moved when she moved.

She closed it and put it away.

---

Chapter 10: The Wrong Note

Henry Voss was due at four. Cassia spent the day seeing patients. She saw a man with a hand-washing compulsion. She saw a woman who could not stop apologizing. She saw a teenage girl who had not spoken to her mother in three months. She wrote her notes carefully. She used the clinical language she had been trained in. She was, by all external measures, a competent and careful psychiatrist.

At three-thirty, she cleared her desk for Henry. She opened the top drawer to put away her notepad. She saw, in the back of the drawer, a piece of paper she did not recognize.

It was a small square of lined paper, folded once. Her own handwriting. She did not remember writing it. She unfolded it.

The note said, in her careful script: He is right.

She stared at it. The ink was hers. The paper was from the notepad she kept in the drawer. She had written it at some point, in some session, in some week she could not bring back.

She thought about throwing it away. She thought about putting it back. She put it in her bag, inside the compact, and closed the bag.

At four o'clock, Henry arrived. He sat in his chair. He folded his hands. He said: "Did you find the note?"

Cassia did not answer. She wrote the date at the top of a fresh page. She wrote: Patient continuing to demonstrate possible referential thinking. Recommend further assessment.

Henry watched her write. He did not ask what she had written. He said, very quietly: "The note is correct, Dr. Morrigan. That is the only thing you have written today that is correct."

Cassia ended the session. Henry left. She sat at her desk and thought: I am his patient. She did not write that down.

She thought, also: I have been his patient for a long time.

She did not write that down either.

---

Chapter 11: The Tuesday Patient

Frank Doolan came back on Tuesday. He was wearing a different shirt. He sat in the chair and did not speak for a long time. Cassia waited. She had learned to wait.

"They replaced you too," he said finally.

"Replaced me?"

"Not the body. The person inside it. The person who was here last week. The person who is here this week. They are not the same."

Cassia wrote: Capgras delusion, persisting. Possible worsening.

"Mr. Doolan, I want to make sure I understand. You believe the woman who was your therapist last week and the woman who is your therapist this week are different people."

"Yes."

"What is different about them?"

"The first one listened. The second one takes notes."

Cassia kept her face neutral. She thought, briefly, that he was right. She had been listening less. She had been writing more. She had been writing things she did not always remember writing.

"Have you noticed anything similar in yourself?" she asked.

Frank looked at her for a long time. "Yes," he said. "I have been replaced too. The man in the mirror this morning is not the man I went to bed as."

Cassia wrote: Patient describes experience consistent with depersonalization. Recommend supportive psychotherapy. She paused, pen on the page. She did not write the next thing, which was that she had brushed her teeth this morning with a toothbrush that was not hers. She had not bought a new toothbrush. The old one had been on the left side of the sink. This morning it had been on the right.

She did not write any of this down.

---

Chapter 12: The Locked Drawer

After Frank left, Cassia sat alone in her office. The clinic was quiet. The afternoon light was long and thin through the window. She thought about the note. She thought about the toothbrush.

She unlocked the drawer at the side of her desk. Inside were folders, journals, three boxes of old letters from her mother. She had not looked through the drawer in months. She had not needed to.

She lifted the stack of folders. Underneath, she found a small leather notebook she did not recognize. It was soft, dark blue, the size of her palm. It was not hers. She was certain it was not hers. She had never owned a blue leather notebook.

She opened it. The first page said, in handwriting that was hers but was not hers: Session notes. C. Morrigan. Day 1.

She read the first entry. It was a session note. It described a patient who had come to see her for anxiety. It described the patient's appearance, the patient's voice. It described Cassia herself, sitting across from the patient, asking questions.

She turned the page. The next entry was dated a week later. It described the same patient. It described Cassia's reaction to the patient. It described Cassia's hands, in her lap, twisting a ring she no longer wore.

She closed the notebook. She held it for a long time. She thought about the note in the drawer that morning. He is right.

She put the notebook back. She locked the drawer. She did not open it again that day.

She sat at her desk and wrote, in her own clinical language: Personal notes. Recommend review at next supervision.

She did not know which Cassia was writing the note.

---

Chapter 13: Mrs. Tennant

Eloise Tennant arrived with a small suitcase. She set it down by her chair. It was leather, the color of tobacco, with a brass clasp.

"Whose is that?" Cassia asked.

"Bernard's," Eloise said. "I brought it because I thought he might want to come with me."

Cassia nodded. She did not argue. In two years of sessions, she had learned that the path with Eloise was not to correct, but to accompany.

"Tell me about Bernard," she said.

"He was a quiet man. He liked to read in the evenings. He made tea in the morning, even in summer. He had a habit of clearing his throat before he spoke, even when he had nothing to say."

"What else?"

"He wore gray suits. He was soft-shouldered. He had a narrow face."

Cassia felt the air in the room change. She kept her face still.

"He sounds like a careful man," she said.

"He was very careful," Eloise said. "He was always the one asking questions."

"What kinds of questions?"

"He would ask me about my childhood. He would ask me about the dog I had when I was young. He would ask me questions I had not been asked in a long time."

Cassia's hands, in her lap, had gone cold. She did not move them.

"Was he a good man, Eloise?"

"He was a very good man." Eloise paused. "He was the only person who ever knew me completely. He knew things about me I had forgotten."

Eloise opened the suitcase. Inside were folded shirts, a small book, a pair of reading glasses, a worn photograph of a man Cassia did not recognize. The photograph could have been anyone. It could not have been Henry Voss. The man in the photograph had a different face, a different build, a different coloring.

And yet.

Cassia did not take the photograph. She sat with her hands in her lap. She thought about the brown terrier. She thought about the man in the gray suit. She thought about the suitcase of a man who had been dead for three years and was sitting, this morning, in a chair across from her.

She did not write any of this down.

---

Chapter 14: The Question Marked

Henry arrived at four. He sat in his chair. He folded his hands. He did not speak.

Cassia waited. The room was quiet. The light through the window was starting to fade. The radiator clicked once.

"How was your week, Mr. Voss?"

"Quiet," he said. "I spent most of it reading. There is a textbook I have been meaning to finish. The one on your shelf, second from the left."

Cassia did not turn to look at the shelf. She knew which book he meant. The spine was dark green, the title small, the author a name she had studied under in medical school. The book had been on her shelf for eleven years. She had not opened it in nine.

"Which chapter?" she asked.

"The one on transference. The one that begins with the case of the patient who is certain his therapist is treating him for a different condition than the one he was sent for."

Cassia wrote nothing. She had a pen in her hand. She did not move it.

"That is an unusual chapter to read," she said.

"It is an unusual condition." He paused. "Have you had a patient like that, Dr. Morrigan? A patient who knew your secrets before you told them?"

The question was small and precise. It sat in the room like a held breath.

"Patients often believe they know their therapists," Cassia said. "It is a common projection. They are describing themselves."

"Yes," Henry said. "That is what the textbook says. But the textbook is a defense. The patient is not describing himself. He is describing the person sitting across from him."

Cassia wrote, in his file: Patient continuing to demonstrate referential thinking. Possibly elaborate. Risk to therapeutic frame.

She did not write what she had started to think, which was that the man across from her had been sitting in that chair for a long time, and that she had not noticed him until four weeks ago.

---

Chapter 15: The Drift

The drive home took twenty-two minutes. Cassia knew this because she had driven it a thousand times. She had the radio on. She had a small bag of groceries in the passenger seat. She had the window cracked to let in the cool of the evening.

She parked in the driveway. She turned off the engine. The engine was warm. She did not remember driving.

She sat in the car for a long time. The street was quiet. The porch light was on, which she had not turned on. The house was dark, which she had not left it.

She took the groceries inside. She put them away. She made a cup of tea. She sat at the kitchen table. The cup was in her hand. She did not remember making it.

She called Adam.

He picked up on the second ring. "Cassia. It's late."

"I know. I wanted to hear your voice."

A pause. "Are you all right?"

"Yes. I think so. I'm tired."

"You've been working too hard. The Henry Voss case. I saw the notes."

"He's an interesting case."

"He's a strange case. Cassia, the referral was a fraud. The name was made up. The number is disconnected. You know this."

"I know."

"Why are you still seeing him?"

Cassia looked at the kitchen window. Her own face looked back at her from the dark glass. The face did not move when she moved.

"Because I think he knows something I don't," she said.

Adam was quiet for a long time. "Come in tomorrow," he said. "Let's talk."

She agreed. She hung up. She sat at the table. The tea was cold. She did not remember drinking it.

---

Chapter 16: The Tape

On Saturday, Cassia went to the clinic. She had a key. She sometimes worked weekends, when her caseload was heavy. Today her caseload was not heavy. Today she wanted to listen to the recordings of her sessions with Henry.

She had three recorded sessions. She sat at her desk with her headphones on. She pressed play on the first session.

Her own voice came through, warm and professional. "What brings you to therapy, Mr. Voss?"

Henry's voice, low and precise: "A long walk."

Cassia listened. The recording felt different from her memory. She rewound and played again. The same words. The same pause. She did not hear a laugh. She did not hear the corner of her mouth move.

She played the second session. She played the third.

In the third session, after Henry had mentioned the dog, her voice on the tape said: "I had a brown terrier. I called it Bean."

Cassia had never said those words aloud. She had not told Henry about the dog. She had not told anyone. She had not written it in any file.

She sat in the empty clinic with the headphones around her neck. The office was quiet. The patient chair was empty. The Magritte print on the wall watched her with its apple.

She thought about the note in the drawer. He is right. She thought about the blue notebook in the locked drawer. She thought about the drive home she did not remember.

She thought, very clearly, that she had been answering Henry's questions in sessions she could not remember having. She thought that her own voice, on a recording she had made herself, was confessing things she had not confessed.

She did not call Adam. She did not call anyone. She sat in the office and listened to the tape again, and again, until the light outside the window was gone.

---

Chapter 17: Jonah

Jonah Park came on Tuesday. He sat in the patient chair. He did not look at Cassia. He looked at the window, at the rain, at the small ceramic bowl on her desk.

"How are you, Jonah?"

"Fine."

"Have you been sleeping?"

"Sometimes."

"Your mother says you have been getting up at night."

He nodded.

"What do you do when you get up?"

"I go to the window. I look out."

"What do you see?"

"The street. The houses. The windows across the way."

Cassia waited. The rain streaked the glass. The radiator clicked.

"I see your apartment," Jonah said.

Cassia did not move. "My apartment?"

"The one on Crescent Lane. Third floor. The one with the green door."

Cassia lived on Crescent Lane. Third floor. The door had been green for eleven years.

"How do you know where I live, Jonah?"

"I have been there."

"When?"

"Last week. I watched you come home. I watched you make dinner. I watched you sit at the kitchen table and not move for a long time."

Cassia's mouth was dry. She wrote nothing. She kept her eyes on the boy.

"I also watched you not being there," he said. "The kitchen light was on. The chair was pulled out. But no one was in the room."

Cassia ended the session. She told Jonah she would see him next week. He nodded and left. The waiting room was empty. The clinic was quiet.

She sat at her desk. She did not call Adam. She did not call the police. She sat, and she thought about the kitchen light on a Tuesday night, and the chair pulled out, and the room empty, and the boy across the street who had been watching.

She did not remember pulling the chair out.

She did not remember sitting in it.

---

Chapter 18: The Wrong Question

Henry came on Thursday. He sat in his chair. He folded his hands. He looked at Cassia for a long time before he spoke.

"Have you ever been asked the wrong question in a session?" he said.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean the question that belongs to the other chair. The question the patient should be asking the therapist. The one that has been waiting in the room for a long time."

Cassia wrote the date. She wrote: Session 6. She did not write anything else.

"Have you?" he asked again.

"I don't think so," she said.

"I think you have." He paused. "I think you have been asked it many times. I think you have been answering it for years. I think you are still answering it now."

"What question, Mr. Voss?"

He leaned forward slightly. He looked at her with the same gray eyes she had been seeing for six weeks, and she thought, briefly, that they were also the eyes of the man in the photograph in Eloise's suitcase, and also the eyes of a boy named David Reiss, and also her own eyes in the compact that morning, half a second late.

"The question," he said, "is whether you are the doctor, or the patient."

Cassia's pen stopped on the page. She did not write anything. She watched him. He watched her. The radiator clicked. The light in the window shifted.

"Who is asking that question?" she said.

"You are," he said. "You have been asking it for a long time. I am simply the one who is finally letting you hear it."

---

Chapter 19: The Coffee

After Henry left, Cassia went to the small kitchenette at the back of the clinic. She had not been there in days. She filled the kettle. She opened the cupboard. There were two mugs in the sink.

One was hers. The other was smaller, white, with a faint blue rim. It was not hers. It was not from the clinic. She did not recognize it. It was still warm.

She stood in the kitchenette holding the kettle. The water was dripping from the tap. The fridge hummed. She set the kettle down. She picked up the mug. The rim was damp. There was a faint smell of coffee.

She thought about the kitchen at home. She had a mug like that. A small white one with a blue rim. She had bought it at a flea market in another city, the year she had started at the community clinic. She had not brought it to the clinic. She had not used it in months.

She put the mug down. She did not wash it. She did not throw it away. She went back to her office. She sat at her desk. She thought about Henry. She thought about the boy. She thought about the man who believed he had been replaced. She thought about the old woman and the suitcase.

She thought about a mug in her sink at the clinic, warm, that did not belong to the clinic.

She thought: I have been here before.

She did not know what she meant.

---

Chapter 20: The Break

Cassia canceled her afternoon appointments. She told the receptionist she was not feeling well. The receptionist asked if she needed anything. Cassia said no. She left the clinic at noon.

She drove home. She did not remember the drive. She parked in the driveway. The porch light was off, as she had left it. The house was dark. She went inside. She did not turn on any lights.

She sat in the living room. She did not move for two hours. The light through the windows shifted. The room went from gray to amber to gray again. The phone did not ring. The fridge hummed. The cat, which she had not thought of in days, came and sat in her lap and she stroked it without thinking.

She thought about Henry. She thought about the brown terrier. She thought about Bean, who had died when she was twelve, who had been put down at the vet while she waited in the waiting room with her mother. She thought about the photograph in Eloise's suitcase. She thought about the boy at the window.

She thought about the patient who had said, "A long walk," and the laugh she had not let out, and the man in the photograph who had gray eyes, and the patient who was certain his therapist was treating him for a different condition.

She thought about the drive home she had not remembered. The mug in the sink. The toothbrush on the wrong side. The note in the drawer.

She thought: I am losing this.

She did not know what this was.

At some point in the afternoon, she stood. She went to the bathroom. She looked at her face in the mirror. It was her face. It moved when she moved. She did not blink late. She brushed her teeth. She put the toothbrush back on the left side of the sink, where it belonged.

She sat in the living room again. The cat came back. She stroked the cat. She thought: I will go back tomorrow. I will see Adam. I will tell him everything.

She did not know what everything was. She did not know where to begin.

Chapter 21: The Catalog

On Monday, Cassia went back to the clinic early. She sat at her desk with the caseload list in front of her. Seven patients. She had been seeing most of them for years. She had written each of them carefully. She had known each of them.

She read the list slowly. She read the diagnoses. Generalized anxiety. Complicated grief. Adolescent rumination. Capgras variant. Obsessive-compulsive features. Adjustment disorder with depressed mood. Marital conflict.

She read the names. Mrs. Eloise Tennant. Jonah Park. Frank Doolan. Margaret Sykes. Linnea Holm. Aaron Pratt. Daniel Voss.

She stopped at the last name. Daniel Voss. She did not have a patient named Daniel Voss. She had a patient named Henry Voss. She looked at the name on the page. It was typed. It was in the clinic's font. It was in the caseload list she had printed herself.

She pulled the file. The file was thin. It had an intake form dated eleven years ago. The patient was a man named Daniel Voss, age forty-eight at intake. Referred for anxiety. Treated for four sessions. Discharged to another city.

The intake photo was a small black-and-white. The man in the photo had a narrow face, gray hair combed back, soft shoulders. He was not Henry Voss. He was older. Heavier. But he had the same eyes, the same careful hands, the same way of holding his mouth.

Cassia closed the file. She sat at her desk. She thought: this is the second patient I have found in this cabinet that I do not remember treating. She thought: this is the third, if she counted David Reiss.

She did not know if she was counting correctly. She did not know if she was the same Cassia who had counted the others.

She thought: my patients are not describing themselves. They are describing me. They are describing my life. The dog. The childhood. The kitchen. The chair.

She thought: I am in their files.

She did not write any of this down.

Chapter 22: Eloise's Husband

Eloise came on Tuesday. She brought the suitcase again. She set it by her chair. She wore a dark blue dress. She had combed her hair.

"He is in the waiting room," she said.

Cassia looked at the closed door. "Bernard?"

"He came with me today. He wanted to see where I go on Tuesdays."

Cassia nodded. "Tell me about him."

"He has been quieter lately. He is reading more. He is interested in old cases. He asks me questions about the people I see in this room."

"What kinds of questions?"

"He asks me whether the doctor ever talks about herself. He asks me whether the doctor has ever mentioned a dog."

Cassia felt the small, familiar tightening. She had heard this pattern before. She wrote nothing.

"He also asks me," Eloise said, "whether the doctor has ever had a chair that didn't feel like hers."

Cassia looked at the patient chair. It was the same chair it had always been. It was the chair her patients sat in. It was a chair.

"I don't know what you mean, Eloise."

"He says the chair knows her. He says it has been waiting for her to sit in it."

Cassia ended the session early. She told Eloise she would see her next week. Eloise nodded. She picked up the suitcase. She left the room.

Cassia sat alone. She did not move. The chair across from her was empty. The window was open an inch. The street noise was far away.

She thought, briefly, that Bernard was not in the waiting room. She thought, briefly, that Bernard had never been in any waiting room. She thought, briefly, that Bernard was the man who had been sitting in the chair across from her for the last six weeks, and that Eloise had been telling her this for two years, and that she had not been listening.

She did not write this down.

Chapter 23: The Replaced Man

Frank Doolan came on Tuesday afternoon. He sat in the chair. He did not look at Cassia. He looked at his own hands.

"Have you noticed anything different about me?" he asked.

Cassia studied him. "You seem more settled than last week."

"I am more settled. The replacement is more like me now. He has learned my voice. He has learned how I sit. He does not stand out the way he used to."

Cassia wrote: Patient reports decreased distress. Possible accommodation to delusional belief.

"That must be a relief," she said.

"It is. But it is also strange. Because if he is now exactly like me, then I am not needed. The man I was can leave."

"What would you do if you left?"

"I would sit in the other chair. I would ask the questions."

Cassia looked at him. He looked back at her. His eyes were clear. He was not agitated. He was not afraid. He was, in some way Cassia could not name, calm.

"Whose chair would you be sitting in?" she asked.

"Yours," he said.

Cassia ended the session. She walked Frank to the door. She went back to her office. She sat at her desk. She thought about the man in the chair. She thought about the man in the chair across from her, in the gray suit, who had been there for six weeks.

She thought: I am not in the right chair.

She did not write it down.

Chapter 24: Henry's Reading

Henry came on Thursday. He sat in his chair. He took a small book from his bag. Cassia saw the spine. It was the dark green textbook from her shelf. He set it on his knee, open to a page near the back.

"Do you mind if I read a passage?" he said.

"Please."

He read, in his soft, careful voice: "The patient, having spent several sessions describing the therapist's life in greater detail than his own, was asked why he continued. He said, 'Because the therapist has forgotten more about herself than the patient has ever known. The patient is simply the keeper of the memories.'"

Cassia knew the passage. She had underlined it once, in her own copy of the book, years ago. She had not known the copy in Henry's hands was her copy. She had not known the copy in Henry's hands had ever been opened to that page.

"Where did you get that book?" she said.

"It was on your shelf. I took it this morning."

"I did not see you take it."

"You were in the kitchenette. You were looking at the mug."

Cassia did not answer. She looked at the book in his hands. The spine was cracked at the same place hers was. The page was the same. The underline was hers. She could see her own pencil mark, faint, at the side of the paragraph.

"That book is mine," she said.

"Yes," Henry said. "It has been yours for a long time."

He closed the book. He set it on his knee. He looked at her with the eyes she had been seeing for six weeks, the eyes that were not quite the eyes of the man in the photograph in Eloise's suitcase, and not quite the eyes of David Reiss, and not quite her own eyes in the compact, and yet were all of them.

"I think the book is yours too," Cassia said.

Henry nodded. "I think it is."

He did not return the book. She did not ask for it back. He put it in his bag and left the office. The door closed softly behind him. The book stayed on her shelf, in his bag, in a place she could not reach.

Chapter 25: The Mirror Patient

On Friday morning, a new patient arrived. The name on the intake form was Irene Maeda. Referred by her own internist for "stress and confusion." The intake note was brief: "Patient believes her therapist is treating her own reflection."

Cassia read the note twice. She went to the waiting room. A woman in her sixties stood by the window. She was small, gray-haired, carefully dressed. She looked at Cassia with the kind of directness that is not rudeness, only clarity.

"Dr. Morrigan?"

"Yes."

"I am Irene Maeda. I am here about my therapist."

Cassia led her to the office. They sat in their chairs. Irene did not look around. She looked at Cassia.

"Tell me about your therapist," Cassia said.

"She is treating her own reflection. She has been doing it for years. She sits in her chair, and across from her is a mirror, and in the mirror is the person she believes she is. The real patients are an interruption."

"How long has this been going on?"

"Eleven years. I have been her patient for six. I am one of the interruptions."

Cassia wrote: Patient presents with referential thinking. Possible projection of own dissociative experiences.

"What do you see, in the mirror?"

"I see the woman my therapist thinks she is. She is not the woman who is sitting in the chair. The woman in the chair is tired. The woman in the mirror is not tired. The woman in the mirror is the one my therapist prefers to be."

Cassia did not move. She did not write anything.

"What is the woman's name, in the mirror?" Cassia asked.

Irene smiled, the way one smiles at a question one has been waiting for.

"She doesn't have a name," Irene said. "She is the part of my therapist that forgot her name."

Cassia ended the session. She walked Irene to the door. She went back to her office. She sat at her desk. She opened the compact in her bag. The face in the small mirror was hers. She watched the face watch her.

The face did not blink late this time. The face moved when she moved. The face was hers.

She closed the compact. She put it away.

She did not call Adam. She did not call anyone. She sat at her desk and waited for the next session, and the next, and the next.

Chapter 26: Jonah's File

After the morning sessions, Cassia opened Jonah Park's file. She had not read it in weeks. She had been relying on her memory, which had been unreliable.

The intake form was complete. Name, age, school, address. Reason for visit: depression, sleep disturbance, possible social withdrawal. There was a paragraph from his mother. There was a paragraph from his school counselor. There was a page for Jonah to fill out himself, with the prompt: "Reason for visit."

The space was blank.

Cassia stared at it. She had filled in this space herself. She always did. It was a clinical habit. She wrote a brief summary of her first impression, the patient's stated reason, the working diagnosis. She did it in the third person. She did it in a careful, professional hand.

The handwriting on Jonah's "Reason for visit" page was hers.

She did not remember writing it. The ink was the same ink she used. The paper was the clinic's standard intake form. The page was dated two months ago. She had been seeing Jonah for two months.

She sat with the file in her lap. She thought about the boy at the window. She thought about the green door. She thought about the kitchen light, the chair pulled out, the empty room.

She thought: I have been writing things I do not remember writing. She thought: I have been filling in my patients' lives. She thought: my patients' lives are filling in mine.

She put the file back. She did not call Adam. She did not call anyone. She sat at her desk and waited for the rest of the day, and the rest of the week, and the rest of whatever it was she was waiting for.

Chapter 27: The Sleep

Cassia did not sleep on Sunday. She lay in bed and watched the light move across the ceiling. The cat slept at her feet. The house was quiet. The street was quiet. The city, on a Sunday night, was holding its breath.

At four in the morning, she sat up. She went to the kitchen. She made tea. She sat at the table.

She thought about the patient chair in her office. She thought about her own chair, behind the desk. She thought about the locked drawer. She thought about the blue notebook. She thought about the note that said, He is right.

She went back to bed. She closed her eyes. She did not sleep.

She dreamed.

She was in her office. It was night. The desk lamp was on. The window was dark. The patient chair was empty. She was sitting in the patient chair.

Across from her, in her own chair, was a woman. The woman had brown hair, gray eyes, a small scar at the corner of her mouth. She was wearing a gray suit. She had no tie. She was holding a pen. She was writing on a notepad.

The woman looked up. The woman said, "What brings you here today?"

Cassia opened her mouth. She could not speak. The woman in her chair was waiting. The woman was patient. The woman was, in the way dreams are sometimes, exactly the right person to be sitting there.

Cassia said, in the dream, in a voice that was hers and not hers, "A long walk."

The woman in her chair did not laugh. The woman said, "Tell me about the dog."

Cassia woke. The light through the curtains was gray. The cat was gone from the foot of the bed. The house was quiet. She lay still for a long time, looking at the ceiling.

She thought: I am the patient. She thought: I have been the patient for a long time. She thought: the man in the gray suit has been treating me since before I knew his name.

She did not go back to sleep. She got up. She showered. She drove to the clinic. She did not remember the drive.

Chapter 28: The Office Chair

It was six in the morning. The clinic was dark. Cassia let herself in. She did not turn on the lights. She walked down the hallway in the gray light. She went into her office.

She sat in the patient chair.

It was the first time she had ever sat there. The chair was lower than her own chair. The angle was different. The desk was farther away. The diplomas on the wall were smaller. The Magritte print was the same man with the apple, but the apple was closer.

She sat for a long time. She thought about Henry. She thought about the patient chair. She thought about the way the light fell on the desk from this side, the way the patient saw the diplomas, the way the patient saw the bowl from Lisbon, the way the patient saw the notepad, the pen, the doctor's hands.

She thought about the man in the gray suit, who had sat in this chair for six weeks, who had seen her office from this side, who had known things about her from this side.

She thought: he was always going to be in this chair. I was always going to end up in it. We were always going to swap.

She stood. She crossed the room. She sat in her own chair. The desk was in front of her. The patient chair was empty. The light was gray. The clinic was quiet.

She sat in her own chair and did not know if it was hers.

Chapter 29: The Voice in the Hall

Cassia was late for her first appointment. She was hurrying down the hallway with a folder in her hand. The clinic was just opening. The receptionist had not yet arrived. The waiting room was empty.

She heard her own voice.

It was coming from down the hall, from behind one of the closed office doors. The words were clear, low, professional. They were the words she used to open a session.

"What brings you here today?"

Cassia stopped. She stood in the hallway. The voice spoke again.

"Tell me about the dog."

Cassia did not move. The voice was hers. The cadence was hers. The careful, soft, professional warmth was hers. She had said those exact words, in that exact tone, in a thousand sessions.

The voice continued, from behind the closed door.

"And the chair. Tell me about the chair. Have you ever sat in the other one?"

Cassia walked to the door. It was her own office. She had left it ten minutes ago. The door was closed. She had closed it herself.

She opened the door.

The room was empty. Her desk was empty. The patient chair was empty. The Magritte print was on the wall. The diplomas were on the wall. The bowl from Lisbon was on the desk.

The room was empty, and the voice was gone, and the hallway behind her was quiet.

She closed the door. She went to her desk. She sat down. She picked up a pen. She wrote, on a clean sheet of paper, in her own hand: I am losing this.

She looked at the words. She did not recognize the handwriting. The handwriting was hers.

She put the paper in the locked drawer. She locked the drawer. She did not open it again that day.

Chapter 30: The Tuesday Return

Henry arrived at four. He sat in his chair. He did not speak. He did not move. He folded his hands. He looked at Cassia, and he waited.

Cassia looked at him. The clinic was quiet. The light through the window was the late afternoon light, the long, thin, gold light. The radiator clicked once. The Magritte print watched from the wall.

Henry waited. He did not prompt. He did not ask. He sat in the patient chair and he waited for the woman in the other chair to begin.

The minutes passed. The light moved. Cassia did not speak. She did not write. She did not pick up the pen. She sat in her chair and she felt the weight of the room, and the weight of the chair, and the weight of the man across from her who had been waiting, she now understood, for a long time.

At four-twelve, Henry said, very softly, "It is all right. Take your time."

Cassia nodded. She did not trust her voice. She wrote nothing.

At four-fifteen, Henry said, "We can end here. We can begin again Thursday."

Cassia nodded again.

Henry stood. He smoothed his gray suit. He picked up his bag. He walked to the door. He paused with his hand on the frame.

"Dr. Morrigan," he said.

"Yes."

"You sat in the other chair this morning."

Cassia did not answer.

"I am glad you did," he said. "It is a long time coming."

He closed the door softly behind him. Cassia sat in her chair and watched the door. She did not move for a long time. The light faded. The clinic went dark around her, room by room, as the building settled into evening.

She thought: I sat in the other chair. She thought: he knew. She thought: he has been waiting.

She did not write any of this down. She did not call Adam. She did not call anyone. She sat in the dark, in the chair that was not hers, and waited for the morning, and the next session, and the next.

Chapter 31: The Supervising Voice

Adam Wilkes called on Wednesday morning. Cassia was at her desk, finishing a note from a morning session. She picked up the phone.

"Cassia. How are you?"

"I am well."

"How is the caseload?"

"Heavy. The Henry Voss case is ongoing."

"I want to talk about that case. I have been hearing things. From the receptionist. From other patients. They say you have been canceling appointments. They say you have been running late. They say you have been looking at the door during sessions."

Cassia did not answer.

"I am not trying to alarm you," Adam said. "I am trying to help. Will you come in? Tomorrow, before the first session. Just to talk."

"I will come in."

"Cassia. One more thing. On the phone just now, your voice sounded different."

"Different how?"

"Older. Tired. As if you have been working a long shift."

"I have been tired."

"Get some rest. We will talk tomorrow."

He hung up. Cassia set the phone down. She sat at her desk. The morning light was coming through the window. The patient chair was empty. The desk was neat. The compact in her bag was closed.

She thought: he is right. I am tired. I have been tired for a long time. She thought: I have been the patient for longer than I have been the doctor. She thought: I have been answering his questions since before I knew they were his.

She did not call Adam back. She did not call anyone. She sat at her desk and waited for the day to begin.

Chapter 32: The Refusal

Cassia did not go in on Thursday. She did not call Adam. She did not call the clinic. She sat in her living room and looked at the phone and did not pick it up.

At noon, Adam called. She did not answer. He called again at one. She did not answer. He called at three. She did not answer.

At four, her phone rang again. She picked up.

"Cassia. It is Adam. I have been calling."

"I know."

"Why are you not at the clinic?"

"I cannot come in."

"Cannot, or will not?"

She thought about this. "I do not know."

"Cassia. I am worried. The Henry Voss case. The notes. The canceled appointments. The voice on the phone. I am asking you, as your supervisor, to take a leave."

"No."

"No?"

"I cannot take a leave. I have patients."

"You have seven patients. They can be transferred. I will transfer them myself."

"No."

Adam was quiet for a long time. Then he said, very gently, "Cassia. Listen to me. Your voice. On the phone, just now. It does not sound like you."

"I know."

"It sounds like someone else. It sounds like a patient."

Cassia closed her eyes. The living room was quiet. The cat was on the sofa. The afternoon light was long and thin.

"It is not a patient," she said. "It is me. It is me, Adam, on a different chair."

"Cassia—"

"Take a leave yourself, Adam. Take a leave and sit in the other chair and see how it feels."

She hung up. She sat on the sofa. The phone did not ring again. The afternoon went by. The light faded. The room went gray.

She thought: I have refused. She thought: I have refused because I cannot leave the chair. She thought: I cannot leave the chair because the chair is the only place where I am still me.

She did not write any of this down.

Chapter 33: Henry's Wife

On Friday morning, Cassia was in her office. The receptionist knocked. There was a phone call. A woman named Mara Voss. She was Henry's wife. She wanted to speak with Dr. Morrigan about her husband.

Cassia picked up the phone. "Mrs. Voss. This is Dr. Morrigan."

"Dr. Morrigan. Thank you for taking my call. I want to ask you a question about my husband."

"Of course."

"Is he a patient of yours?"

"Yes. He has been coming to see me for six weeks."

A pause on the line. "Dr. Morrigan. My husband is a psychiatrist. He has been a psychiatrist for thirty years. He retired four years ago. He has never been a patient of any clinic."

Cassia set the pen down. She did not write anything.

"He is not a patient?" she said.

"No. He may have been coming to your office. He may have been sitting in the chair across from you. But he is not, and has never been, a patient."

"Then what is he doing in my office?"

"I don't know. I have been asking him for weeks. He says he is doing important work. He says he has been doing it for a long time. He says the woman he is treating has finally started to listen."

Cassia did not answer.

"Dr. Morrigan. Are you all right?"

"I am well, Mrs. Voss. Thank you for calling."

She hung up. She sat at her desk. The morning light was bright through the window. The patient chair was empty.

She thought: he is not a patient. He is a psychiatrist. He has been treating me. He has been treating me since before I knew his name.

She thought: I am the patient.

She did not write this down. She did not call Adam. She did not call Mara Voss back. She sat at her desk and waited for the next session, and the next, and the next.

Chapter 34: The Confession

Henry arrived at four. He sat in his chair. He folded his hands. He looked at Cassia, and he said, "My wife called you."

"Yes."

"I thought she might. I am sorry."

"Why are you sorry?"

"Because the truth is harder to give to a patient than to a friend. I have been trying to give you the truth in small pieces. I did not want to give it to you all at once."

Cassia wrote nothing. She had a pen in her hand. She did not move it.

"What is the truth, Mr. Voss?"

"I am a psychiatrist. I have been a psychiatrist for thirty years. I retired four years ago. I have been treating you for eleven."

"Eleven years."

"Yes. Since before you opened this clinic. Since before you had this office. Since before you had a license."

Cassia did not move. The room was quiet. The light was long. The radiator clicked.

"I have notes," Henry said. He reached into his bag. He took out the small blue leather notebook. The one from her locked drawer. He set it on his knee.

"This is yours," she said.

"It is the record of your treatment. I have been writing in it for eleven years. I have written in your handwriting, in your voice, in your chair. I have written the sessions we have had that you do not remember."

"Why in my handwriting?"

"Because it is your treatment. Because you have been writing it, in some part of yourself, since the beginning. I have simply been the one to hold the pen."

Cassia looked at the notebook. She thought: this is what I have been missing. She thought: this is what has been locked in the drawer. She thought: this is what I have been trying to remember.

"What is the diagnosis, Mr. Voss?"

He did not look away. He said, "Dissociative identity disorder, with onset in late adolescence, secondary to early childhood trauma. The patient has, for the last thirty years, organized her life around being the doctor. The patient has used the role of the psychiatrist to keep the patient hidden. The patient is now emerging."

Cassia did not answer. She sat in her chair. She thought about the brown terrier. She thought about Bean. She thought about the boy at the window, and the old woman with the suitcase, and the man who believed he had been replaced.

She thought: they are all me.

Chapter 35: The Notebook

Henry set the blue notebook on the desk between them. Cassia looked at it. The leather was soft and dark. The cover was unmarked. She did not reach for it.

"Read the first page," Henry said.

She reached. She opened the notebook. The first page said, in her own hand: Session 1. C. Morrigan, age thirty-three. Referred by self. Reason for visit: not given.

She read the entry. It was a session note. It described a woman in her early thirties, a psychiatrist, who had come to see Henry because she had begun to suspect she was not, in some fundamental way, herself. The woman had been having gaps in her memory. The woman had been finding notes in her own handwriting that she did not remember writing. The woman had been hearing her own voice in empty rooms.

Cassia closed the notebook. She opened it again. She read the second entry. A week later. The woman had come back. The woman was more certain of the gaps. The woman was beginning to suspect her patients were describing her own life.

She turned the pages. The entries went on for years. The woman grew older. The woman opened a clinic. The woman got a cat. The woman had a patient named Henry Voss, who had been treating her, in some form or another, for as long as she had been treating him.

She read the last entry. It was dated a week ago. The woman had finally heard the man's voice in the hallway. The woman had finally sat in the other chair. The woman had finally read the notebook.

Cassia closed the notebook. She set it on the desk. She looked at Henry.

"How long have I been you?" she said.

"You have always been you," Henry said. "I have been the one helping you remember."

She nodded. She did not cry. She was past crying. She sat in the chair, in the office, in the clinic, and she let the man across from her be the man across from her, and she did not try to be anywhere else.

She did not write any of this down.

Chapter 36: The Reversal

Henry did not move from his chair. Cassia did not move from hers. The light in the window had gone from gold to gray.

"I would like to ask you to do something," Henry said.

"What is it?"

"I would like you to sit in the other chair."

Cassia looked at the patient chair. It was the same chair. It was just the other side of the desk.

"Why?"

"Because you have been sitting in this chair for a long time. Because it is time to sit in the other one. Because I have been sitting in it for eleven years, and it is time for me to sit in this one."

Cassia did not move. The room was quiet. The light was gone. The radiator was off. The building was quiet.

"What if I cannot?"

"You can. You did it once. The other morning. You sat in the patient chair and you saw the room from that side."

She thought about that morning. She thought about the angle, the light, the small Magritte print, the diplomas smaller from the other side.

She stood. She walked around the desk. She sat in the patient chair.

Henry stood. He walked around the desk. He sat in her chair.

For a long time, neither of them spoke. They sat in their new chairs, in the gray office, in the quiet clinic, and they looked at each other.

Then Henry said, very softly, "What brings you here today?"

Cassia opened her mouth. She could not speak. The room was too large. The chair was too low. The desk was too far. The man across from her was too certain.

She said, finally, in a voice that was hers and not hers, "A long walk."

Henry did not laugh. He said, "Tell me about the dog."

Cassia began to talk. She talked about Bean. She talked about the brown terrier. She talked about the morning at the vet, the waiting room, the way her mother had held her hand, the way the dog had not come out.

She talked for a long time. Henry listened. He did not take notes. He did not write anything down. He sat in the chair, and he listened, and he let the woman in the other chair be the patient she had been for a long time.

Chapter 37: The Symptoms

Henry did not let her leave. He asked her to stay in the patient chair. He sat in her chair. He took out a small pad of paper. He began, in his own hand this time, to take notes.

"Memory gaps," he said. "Auditory hallucinations of self-generated content. Misidentification of reflection. Confabulation in writing. Transient depersonalization. The patient reports her patients describe her own life. The patient reports her own handwriting appears in places she does not remember writing. The patient reports hearing her own voice in empty rooms."

Cassia sat in the patient chair and listened to her own symptoms read back to her in the voice of the man who had been listening to them for eleven years.

"The patient is forty-four years old," Henry said. "She has been a psychiatrist for fifteen years. She has had these symptoms for at least as long. She has organized her life around the role of the doctor. The role of the doctor has allowed her to keep the patient at a distance. The patient has, in the last six weeks, emerged."

He set the pad down. He looked at her.

"I am going to ask you a question," he said. "I want you to answer honestly."

"Yes."

"When did you first know you were the patient?"

Cassia thought about this. She thought about the first session, when Henry had asked about the dog. She thought about the moment in the kitchenette, when she had seen the mug in the sink. She thought about the drive home she had not remembered, the toothbrush on the wrong side, the note in the drawer that said, He is right.

"I think I always knew," she said.

"Yes," Henry said. "I think you did."

---

Chapter 38: The Acceptance

Cassia cried.

She did not know she was going to cry. She had not cried in years. The tears came quietly, the way water comes from a tap that has been left slightly open. She did not sob. She did not make a sound. She sat in the patient chair and let the tears go where they wanted to go.

Henry handed her a tissue. He did not speak. He let her cry.

After a long time, she said, "I am sorry."

"You have nothing to be sorry for."

"I have been treating patients I was not ready to treat."

"You have been treating them the best you could. You have been treating them for years. You have helped a great many people."

"I have been treating myself through them."

"Yes," Henry said. "You have. And that is not a sin. It is what patients do. We treat ourselves through the people who come to us. We are no different from the people we are trying to help."

Cassia looked at the tissue in her hand. She looked at the man across from her. He was gentle. He was patient. He had been gentle and patient for a long time, she now understood. He had been gentle and patient with her, in the chair, in the office, in the clinic, in the life she had built around not knowing.

"What happens now?" she said.

"That depends on you."

"I do not know what I want."

"I know," Henry said. "I have known for a long time."

She nodded. She did not trust her voice.

"You want to be the patient," Henry said. "You have wanted it for a long time. You have wanted to sit in this chair and not be in charge. You have wanted to be the one who is being asked the questions. You have wanted to be the one who does not have to know."

"Yes," Cassia said. "I think so."

"Then sit in the chair. I will ask the questions. You will answer them. We will do this for as long as it takes."

She nodded again. She sat in the chair. She did not move. The office was quiet. The light was gone. The clinic was quiet. The building was quiet. The city was quiet. The night, which had come without her noticing, was quiet around her, and the chair was warm, and the man across from her was patient, and she sat in the chair and let herself, finally, be the patient.

---

Chapter 39: The Question of Identity

Henry asked her a series of questions. He asked her name. He asked her age. He asked her where she lived. He asked her about her mother, her father, the dog, the move to the city, the medical school, the first patient, the man who had said, "A long walk."

Cassia answered. She answered the way she had never answered in her own office. She answered in the first person. She answered in the present tense. She answered without taking notes.

Some of the answers she did not know. Some of the answers were new. Some of the answers she had been carrying for a long time, the way a person carries a stone in a pocket, smoothing it with the thumb without thinking.

At some point, Henry asked, "If the doctor is the patient, then who is the doctor?"

Cassia thought about this. She thought about the man in the gray suit. She thought about the man in the photograph in Eloise's suitcase. She thought about David Reiss, who had said, "A long walk," fifteen years ago, in another city, in another office.

"I don't know," she said.

"That is the right answer," Henry said.

"Why is that the right answer?"

"Because the question is not meant to be answered. The question is meant to be lived. The doctor and the patient are the same person. The chair is the same chair. The room is the same room. The only thing that changes is which side of it you are sitting on."

Cassia nodded. She sat in the chair. She did not know if she was the doctor or the patient. She did not know if she had ever been the doctor. She did not know if the man across from her was the doctor, or a different doctor, or the part of her that had been the doctor for a long time.

She did not try to know. She sat in the chair and let the question live in the room, and she did not try to make it smaller.

---

Chapter 40: The Bargain

Henry set his pad down. He folded his hands. He looked at Cassia, and he said, "I want to make a suggestion."

"Yes."

"I think you should stop seeing your other patients."

Cassia felt the small, sharp pain of a clinical instinct. "I have seven patients."

"I know. I have read your notes. You have done good work with them. But you are not well enough to keep doing it. You have not been well enough for some time. The work you have been doing has been keeping the patient at a distance. The patient is here now. The patient is not going to leave."

Cassia thought about the seven patients. Eloise with the suitcase. Frank with the reflection. Margaret with the apology. Linnea with the grief. Aaron with the obsession. Daniel Voss, in the cabinet, the one she had never remembered. Jonah at the window.

"They need me," she said.

"They need you to be a doctor. You are not well enough to be a doctor. You need to be a patient for a while. I want to help you be a patient."

"What about them?"

"I will help you find them other doctors. I will help you with the referrals. I will help you tell them. I will be here, in this chair, for as long as you need me to be here."

Cassia thought about the clinic. The waiting room. The hallway. The diplomas on the wall. The bowl from Lisbon. The locked drawer.

"If I do this," she said, "I am not sure I can come back."

"You may not need to. The clinic has been a holding room. It has been a place for the doctor to live while the patient has been hiding. The patient is not hiding anymore. The clinic may not be needed."

Cassia sat in the chair for a long time. The room was quiet. The night was quiet. The city was quiet.

"Will you see me, if I do this?"

"Yes," Henry said. "Three times a week. As long as it takes."

Cassia nodded. "I will do it."

She did not feel relief. She did not feel grief. She felt something in between, the way one feels at the end of a long drive, when the road has been everything, and the destination is just a place to stop.

She stood. She walked to the door. She paused.

"Henry."

"Yes."

"Thank you. For the chair. For the dog. For Bean."

He nodded. "I will see you Monday."

She left the office. She walked down the hallway. She went to the receptionist's desk. She told her she was taking a leave. She told her the patients would be reassigned. The receptionist nodded. The receptionist did not ask why.

Cassia walked out of the clinic. She did not look back. She drove home. She did not remember the drive. She did not need to remember it. She had been remembering enough for a long time.

---

Chapter 41: The Empty Waiting Room

The clinic was closed. The waiting room was empty. The chairs were vacant. The magazines were stacked. The tall window let in the afternoon light.

Cassia had been the one to cancel the appointments. She had called each patient personally. She had explained, as Henry had helped her to explain, that she was taking a leave. She had given each patient the name of another doctor. She had not given them the name of the doctor she had seen, the one who was sitting in the other chair.

Eloise had cried. Frank had said he understood. Margaret had apologized for the inconvenience. Linnea had been quiet. Aaron had asked if she would be all right. Jonah had said nothing. Daniel Voss had no appointment to cancel. He had been a file in a drawer, and she had not had to call him.

She had stood in the waiting room on the day after the calls, and she had watched the light move across the floor. The chairs had been empty. The light had moved. The room had been quiet.

She had thought, briefly, that she had failed the patients. She had thought, briefly, that the patients were a part of her, the way the patients in a long practice are always a part of the doctor. She had thought: I have left part of myself in this room.

She had not thought this for long. She had walked out of the waiting room. She had gone home. She had sat in her living room and waited for Monday, and for Henry, and for the chair in his office, which was also her office, which was also the room in which she had been a patient for a long time.

---

Chapter 42: The Hall

Cassia went back to the clinic on Saturday, alone, to pick up a few things. The building was quiet. The clinic was dark. The waiting room was empty. The doors to the offices were closed.

She walked down the hallway. The lights were off. The floor was dark. She passed her office. The door was closed. She passed the kitchenette. The kettle was unplugged.

She heard voices.

They were coming from the closed offices. They were soft, conversational, the kind of voice one hears in a session. They were her patients' voices, and they were her own voice, and they were coming from offices that had been closed for days.

She stopped. She listened.

Eloise was saying, "He is in the waiting room. He has been there for hours."

Frank was saying, "The replacement is more like me now. He has learned my voice."

Jonah was saying, "I see your apartment. I see the chair pulled out. I see the empty kitchen."

Margaret was saying, "I am sorry. I am sorry. I am sorry."

Cassia did not open the doors. She stood in the hallway and listened to the voices of her patients, talking to her, in the empty clinic, in the dark, as if she had never left. The voices were not accusatory. They were not angry. They were the voices of patients who had come to a place where they had been heard, and who were still talking, even after the doctor had gone.

She stood in the hallway for a long time. She did not interrupt. She did not go in. She stood in the dark and listened to her patients be her patients, and she let them be, and she walked, eventually, back down the hallway and out of the clinic.

The voices followed her for a block. She did not turn around.

---

Chapter 43: Adam's Visit

Adam came to the house on Sunday. He knocked. Cassia did not answer. He knocked again. He called her name. She did not answer. He sat on the porch for a while. He left a note in the door.

The note said: Cassia. I am worried. I have heard you have taken a leave. I do not know what is happening. If you want to talk, I am here. If you do not, I am still here. The note is yours, and the door is yours, and the choice is yours.

The handwriting was hers.

She read the note through the window. She did not open the door. She sat on the sofa in the living room and watched Adam drive away, and she read the note again, and she thought: the handwriting is mine. Adam's handwriting is mine. The note is mine.

She thought: this is what the patient does. This is what the patient has always done. The patient takes the voices of the people around her, and she writes in their handwriting, and she answers her own questions in their voices.

She put the note in a drawer in the kitchen. She did not call Adam. She did not call Henry. She sat in the living room and waited for Monday, and for the chair, and for the man who had been waiting, patiently, in the other chair, for a long time.

---

Chapter 44: The Notebook, Continued

Henry came to the house on Monday. He brought the blue notebook. He set it on the kitchen table between them. He did not open it.

"This is yours," he said. "It has always been yours. I have been the keeper of it. I want you to be the keeper of it."

Cassia looked at the notebook. The leather was soft. The cover was blank. The pages were full of a handwriting she had thought was hers.

"I am not sure I can read it," she said.

"You can. You have been reading it, in some part of yourself, for a long time. I am simply asking you to read it with the rest of yourself."

She reached for the notebook. She opened it. She read the first page. She read the second page. She read the third. She read for a long time. Henry sat with her at the table. He did not speak. He let her read.

The first entry was dated eleven years ago. She had been thirty-three. She had been a resident. She had not yet opened the clinic. She had not yet known Henry Voss. She had, however, already been writing in the notebook, in her own hand, about a man who would sit in the chair across from her and ask her questions she had been trying to answer for a long time.

She read the entries. The woman in the entries was her, and was not her. The woman was the patient. The woman had been writing in the notebook since before the doctor had started practicing. The woman had been waiting, in the locked drawer, for a long time.

She closed the notebook. She looked at Henry.

"I don't remember writing these," she said.

"You do," he said. "You just don't remember remembering."

She nodded. She held the notebook for a long time. The kitchen was quiet. The cat was on the table. The afternoon light was coming through the window.

"What do I do with it?"

"You keep it. You read it. You add to it. It is the record of your treatment. It is yours."

She nodded again. She put the notebook in her bag. She would keep it. She would read it. She would add to it, in her own time, in her own hand.

She did not write any of this down. She did not need to. She had the notebook.

---

Chapter 45: The Wrong Name

On Tuesday, Cassia went to the clinic one last time, to return her keys. The building was quiet. The waiting room was empty. The chairs were stacked against the wall. The receptionist had gone home for the day.

Cassia went into her office. The room was bare. The diplomas had been taken down. The Magritte print was gone. The bowl from Lisbon was wrapped in newspaper in a box by the door. The desk was empty. The patient chair was empty.

In the bottom drawer of the desk, she found an old clinic directory. It was from eleven years ago, the year the clinic had opened. The cover was soft. The pages were yellowed.

She opened the directory. The first page listed the staff. There was a name she did not recognize: Dr. C. M. Voss. Psychiatrist. License number. Address. Phone number.

She did not recognize the name. She had never heard of a C. M. Voss. She had never been C. M. Voss. She had been Dr. C. Morrigan, then Dr. Cassia Morrigan, then Dr. Morrigan.

She turned the page. The next name was a woman she did not know. Dr. C. M. Voss. The photo in the directory was a small black-and-white of a woman with brown hair, gray eyes, a small scar at the corner of her mouth.

It was her photo. It was not her name.

She sat with the directory in her lap. She thought: this is who I was. She thought: this is who I was before I was the doctor. She thought: this is the patient, in the directory, waiting in the back of the file.

She took the directory. She put it in her bag, with the notebook. She left the office. She locked the door. She put the key in an envelope and slid it under the receptionist's door.

She walked out of the clinic. The building was quiet. The street was quiet. The city was quiet, in the way cities are quiet at the end of a day.

She did not go back.

---

Chapter 46: The Mirror Patient Returns

On Thursday, Cassia was at home. The doorbell rang. She opened the door. The woman on the porch was the one who had come to the clinic as a patient, weeks ago. Irene Maeda. The woman who had said her therapist was treating her own reflection.

"Dr. Morrigan," Irene said. "I am here to see you."

"I am not practicing, Mrs. Maeda. I am on a leave."

"I know. I am not here as a patient. I am here as a therapist."

Cassia looked at the woman. The woman was small, gray-haired, carefully dressed. She had the same directness she had had in the office.

"I think I have been treating you for a long time," Irene said. "I think I have been the one sitting in the other chair. I think the man you have been seeing, the one in the gray suit, is the part of me that you have been seeing, the part of you that has been treating you, the part of you that has been the patient for a long time."

Cassia did not answer. She looked at the woman on the porch.

"I am not here to confuse you," Irene said. "I am here to tell you that the chairs are the same chair. The doctors are the same doctor. The patients are the same patient. I am here to tell you that you do not have to choose which one you are."

Cassia nodded slowly. She did not know if the woman was right. She did not know if the woman was a patient, or a doctor, or a part of herself, or a stranger.

"Thank you, Mrs. Maeda," she said.

"You are welcome, Dr. Morrigan."

The woman turned. She walked down the porch steps. She walked to the street. Cassia watched her go. The street was quiet. The afternoon was quiet. The house was quiet.

Cassia closed the door. She sat in the living room. She did not turn on the light. She sat in the gray afternoon, in the quiet house, and she let the doorbell's echo fade, and she did not try to know who the woman had been.

She had been enough things for a long time. She did not need to be one more.

---

Chapter 47: The Final Session

Henry came to the house on Friday. He did not bring the notebook. He did not bring his pad. He sat across from her in the living room, in the chair that was the patient chair, and she sat in the chair that was the doctor chair, and they sat in the gray light of the afternoon, and he asked her one question.

"What did you lose?"

Cassia thought about this. She thought about Bean. She thought about her mother. She thought about the boy at the window. She thought about the man in the photograph. She thought about the man in the gray suit. She thought about the patient chair and the doctor chair and the clinic and the locked drawer.

She thought about the woman in the directory, with the brown hair and the gray eyes and the small scar, who had been someone else before she had been her.

"I lost myself," she said.

Henry nodded. He did not speak for a long time. The room was quiet. The cat was on the sofa. The light was gray.

"You did not lose yourself," he said. "You put yourself somewhere. You put yourself in the patient chair, in the patient role, in the patient file, and you left the doctor chair empty. The doctor chair has been empty for a long time. The doctor chair is not empty anymore. The doctor is here. The patient is here. They are in the same room. They are in the same chair. They are in the same person."

Cassia nodded. She did not trust her voice.

"You are not lost," Henry said. "You are found. You are, finally, in the same room with yourself."

She nodded again. She did not cry. She was past crying. She sat in the chair and let the man across from her finish what he had come to finish, and she did not try to be anywhere else.

---

Chapter 48: The Admission

On Monday, Henry drove Cassia to a small psychiatric hospital on the edge of the city. The hospital was quiet. The hallways were painted a soft green. The staff were gentle.

Cassia signed her own intake form. She wrote her name in the space provided. She recognized the woman at the desk. The woman had the same brown hair, the same gray eyes, the same small scar. The woman was, Cassia knew, the part of herself she had been carrying for a long time.

The woman at the desk smiled. "Welcome, Dr. Morrigan."

"Thank you," Cassia said.

She was given a small room. The room had a bed, a desk, a window, and a chair. The chair was across from the bed. Cassia sat in the chair. She did not sit on the bed. She sat in the chair, and she waited.

Henry stood in the doorway. He said, "I will see you Wednesday."

"Thank you, Henry."

"You are welcome, Cassia."

He left. The door closed softly. The room was quiet. The window was small. The light was gray.

She sat in the chair for a long time. She did not know who was going to sit in the other chair. She did not know if anyone was going to sit in the other chair. She sat in the chair that had been the doctor chair, in the room that had been the patient room, and she waited for the patient who would never come, or for the patient who would come tomorrow, or for the part of herself that had been waiting, in the back of the file, in the back of the chair, in the back of the room, for a long time.

She did not try to know. She sat in the chair, and she let the room be quiet, and she let the light be gray, and she let the day go by.

---

Chapter 49: The Other Chair

In the hospital, the days were small and the same. Cassia ate breakfast. She walked the hallway. She sat in the chair in her room. She did not see Henry every day. She saw him three times a week, on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday, in a small office down the hall.

He asked her questions. She answered. She did not always know the answers. Some of the answers were new. Some of the answers were old, the kind of old that lives in the body, in the way a person holds a cup, in the way a person sits in a chair, in the way a person looks out a window.

She did not see the other patients. She stayed in her room. She read the blue notebook. She added to it, in her own hand, in her own time. She did not write a great deal. She wrote the small things. The dog. The mother. The boy at the window. The man in the gray suit. The chair.

In the afternoons, she sat in the chair across from her bed. The chair was empty. The bed was empty. The window was gray. The room was quiet.

She thought, sometimes, that the chair was waiting for someone. She thought, sometimes, that the chair was her chair, the patient chair, the chair that had been in her office for eleven years, the chair that had been in the hospital for a long time before that. She thought, sometimes, that the chair had been following her, the way a person follows a person, the way a patient follows a doctor, the way a doctor follows a patient, the way a chair follows a person from one room to another.

She did not mind. She sat in the chair, and she waited, and she did not try to know.

---

Chapter 50: The Reversal

Months later, Cassia was well enough to leave the hospital. She moved into a small apartment in another part of the city. The apartment had a window. The apartment had a chair. The chair was across from a small sofa.

She did not return to the clinic. She did not return to practice. She did not return to the chair behind the desk, or the locked drawer, or the diplomas on the wall, or the bowl from Lisbon. She left those things where they were. She let them be in the clinic, in the past, in the file.

She sat in the chair in her apartment. She waited.

A new therapist came to the apartment on Tuesdays and Thursdays. The therapist was a woman with a soft voice and a careful manner. The therapist sat in the chair across from Cassia, in the chair that was the doctor chair, and asked the questions.

Cassia sat in the chair that was the patient chair, and she answered. She did not always know the answers. She did not always remember the answers. She answered, sometimes, in the first person, and sometimes in the third, and sometimes in a voice that was not hers, and the therapist let her answer however she answered, and did not try to make the answers smaller.

One afternoon, the therapist asked, "What brings you here today?"

Cassia thought about this. She thought about the dog. She thought about Bean. She thought about the boy at the window. She thought about the man in the gray suit. She thought about the man in the photograph. She thought about the locked drawer, and the blue notebook, and the woman in the directory, and the small scar at the corner of her mouth.

She thought about the chair. The chair she was sitting in. The chair she had been sitting in for a long time.

She said, finally, in a voice that was hers, and was also not hers, and was, she had come to understand, the only voice she had:

"A long walk."

The therapist nodded. The therapist did not laugh. The therapist said, "Tell me about it."

And Cassia, in the chair, in the room, in the quiet apartment, in the small life she had made for herself, in the long quiet of a winter afternoon, began, at last, to speak.
