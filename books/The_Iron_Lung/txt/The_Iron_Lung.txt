Chapter 1: The Breathing Room

The room was long and white. Six iron lungs lined the walls like silver coffins. Each one hissed and sighed in its own rhythm. Elinor Chase walked between them in soft-soled shoes. She carried a chart and a stethoscope.

At bed one, a man named Harris blinked at her. She checked the seal on his machine. The pressure gauge held steady. She adjusted the mirror above his head so he could see the window. He blinked twice in thanks.

Bed two was empty. A girl named Maria was coming in today. Elinor had prepared the lung herself. She tested the bellows, checked the hoses, listened for leaks. The machine was ready.

The shift was quiet. Elinor preferred it that way. Quiet meant no crises. Quiet meant everyone was breathing.

She sat at the nurse's station and wrote her report. The only sounds were the rhythmic push and pull of the iron lungs. She had learned to sleep to that rhythm. It was the sound of people still alive.

Dr. Chen appeared at the door. He looked tired. His white coat was wrinkled.

"The new patient is downstairs," he said. "Polio. Respiratory involvement. She is eight years old."

Elinor stood up. "I know the bed."

"Her name is Maria," he said. "She does not understand what is happening to her."

Elinor nodded. She walked to the supply closet and pulled out a fresh gown. She thought about what she would say to the girl. There was no good way to tell a child she would live inside a machine.

Chapter 2: Rounds

Dr. Chen made his rounds at eight. Elinor walked beside him with the charts. They stopped at each bed.

Harris was stable. His blood pressure was good. The lung pressure was correct. A man in bed three had developed a cough. Chen ordered a chest X-ray.

"He will need to come out for the film," Elinor said.

"I know. Schedule it for the afternoon. Have respiratory therapy ready."

The next patient was a woman named Dorothy. She had been in the lung for three weeks. Her husband visited every evening. He read the newspaper aloud to her. Dorothy blinked to tell him when to turn the page.

They reached the empty bed. Elinor had covered it with a clean sheet.

"Maria arrives at ten," Chen said. "The mother will be with her. I need you to handle the admission."

"Of course."

"Be gentle, Elinor. She is scared."

Elinor looked at the iron lung. It was polished and ready. The bellows were new. The portholes were clean.

"I am always gentle," she said.

Chen smiled. It did not reach his eyes. "I know you are. That is why you are here."

He moved on to the next bed. Elinor followed with the charts. The rhythm of the lungs filled the silence between them.

Chapter 3: Tommy

Tommy Beldon was at the far end of the ward. He had been in the iron lung for eight weeks. Polio had taken his legs and his diaphragm. He could move his eyes, his fingers, and his lips.

Elinor sat beside him. She held up the letter board.

"Good morning, Tommy."

He blinked once. Yes.

"Did you sleep?"

Two blinks. No.

"I am sorry. We can adjust the pressure tonight. It might help."

She positioned the board. He moved his eyes to spell: W-I-N-D-O-W.

"You want to see outside."

One blink. Yes.

Elinor looked at his bed position. He faced the wall. The window was behind him. She called for an orderly. They turned the bed around.

Tommy's eyes found the glass. Outside, a tree swayed in the morning breeze. He watched the leaves move.

"Better?"

One blink. Yes.

Elinor sat with him for a while. She watched him watch the tree. He did not blink toward the board again. He did not need words right now.

She touched his forehead. His skin was warm.

"I will bring you a newspaper later," she said. "You can tell me what to read."

He blinked once. She wrote it down in his chart. Small victories.

Chapter 4: The Man in the Suit

A stranger appeared on the ward at midday. He was tall and thin. He wore a dark suit that did not belong in a hospital. The nurses noticed him before he reached the station.

"Can I help you?" Elinor asked.

Dr. Henry Keller pulled a letter from his jacket. It was stamped with the state health board seal.

"I am here to observe," he said. "I have been authorized."

"By whom?"

He smiled. "The administration. You can call them if you like."

Elinor read the letter. It was addressed to the hospital director. It asked for cooperation with Dr. Keller's research. The language was vague.

She handed it back. "You will need to speak with Sister Margaret. She runs this floor."

"Naturally. Where can I find her?"

Elinor pointed down the hall. Keller nodded and walked away. She watched him go. There was something careful about the way he moved. Like a man who had been in hospitals before.

She went back to her patients. But she kept one ear on the hallway.

Keller did not return to the ward that day. But Elinor knew he would.

Chapter 5: Permission

Sister Margaret Burke was fifty-eight years old. She had run a field hospital at Anzio. She did not frighten easily.

She spread Keller's papers across her desk. His credentials were from a Boston hospital she had never heard of. His license was current but recently issued.

"It is a research license," she said. "Not a clinical one. He cannot treat patients."

"He said he was here to observe," Elinor said.

"Observation I can allow. But he will not touch anyone."

Keller stood in the doorway. He had been listening.

"I do not need to touch them," he said. "I need to watch. I need to understand."

"Understand what?"

"How they survive. How long they last. The iron lung is not a cure. It is a cage."

Sister Margaret studied him. Her eyes did not blink.

"What is your interest, Dr. Keller?"

"I am developing a treatment for polio paralysis. I need a baseline. This ward is my baseline."

Sister Margaret folded her hands. "You may observe. You may take notes. You will not speak to the patients without a nurse present. Do you understand?"

Keller nodded. "Thank you."

He left. Elinor stayed.

"I do not trust him," she said.

"Neither do I. But the letter came from the board. We have to let him look."

She put the papers in a drawer. The lock clicked.

Chapter 6: The Old Ways

Maria arrived at ten. She was small for eight. Her mother walked beside the stretcher, holding her hand.

Elinor met them at the door. She knelt beside the girl.

"Hello, Maria. My name is Elinor. I am going to take care of you."

Maria could not speak. The polio had taken her throat first. Her eyes were wide and wet.

"Your mother will stay. You can see her through the porthole. I promise."

The transfer was delicate. Maria's lungs were weak. Elinor worked quickly. She positioned the girl's body, sealed the collar, and started the bellows.

The machine took over. Maria's chest rose and fell.

Her mother pressed her hand to the glass.

"It will take some time," Elinor said. "She will be tired today. Tomorrow we can start working with the letter board."

"Will she ever come out?"

Elinor hesitated. "Some patients recover enough to leave the lung. For others, it takes time. We do not know yet."

The mother nodded. She did not ask again.

Elinor stayed with Maria through the afternoon. She adjusted the pressure. She cleaned the portholes. She talked to her in a low voice.

The girl's eyes followed her around the room.

Chapter 7: Keller's Pitch

Dr. Keller cornered Chen in the cafeteria. He sat down without asking.

"Vagus nerve stimulation combined with a polypeptide serum," Keller said. "It regenerates the neural pathways damaged by the virus."

Chen studied the images. "How many patients?"

"Twelve."

"And how many survived?"

Keller paused. "Nine."

"Three died."

"Cardiac arrest. But the dosage was too high. I have adjusted the formula since then."

Chen shook his head. "I cannot authorize experiments on my ward."

"I am not asking you to. The board has already approved a limited trial. I am telling you as a courtesy."

He stood up and left the folder on the table.

Chen stared at it for a long time. He did not throw it away. He picked it up. He took it back to his office.
