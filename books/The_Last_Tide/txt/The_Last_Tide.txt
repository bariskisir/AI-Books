Noora traveled to the estuary by riverboat, a three-hour journey through channels that shifted with each tide. The boatman was a young man who had heard about the sandbar but had not visited it. "They say it is just mud and grass," he said. "Nothing grows there. But people are building houses."

It might not last. The river that had created the sandbar could take it back in a single monsoon season. The government could arrive with bulldozers and eviction notices. The rising sea could claim it before the mangrove roots had time to anchor the soil.

Noora filmed the sunset on her last evening, the sky turning orange and pink and purple above the makeshift shelters, the cooking fires glowing like scattered stars. She packed her camera and boarded the boat back to Khulna, carrying the footage of a place that was not yet on any map, a place that existed only because a group of displaced people had decided to stop fleeing and start building.

On her last morning, Noora woke before dawn and walked to the eastern edge of the sandbar. The sun was rising over the estuary, the light spreading across the water in shades of gold and rose. Chapter 49: The Captain''s New Watch

The civilian coastwatch was called the Bay of Bengal Maritime Monitoring Initiative, a name that was too long for its budget and too formal for its operations.
