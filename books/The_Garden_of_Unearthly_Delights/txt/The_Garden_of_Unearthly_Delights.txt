Chapter 1: A New Bloom

The greenhouse smelled of wet soil and something like cinnamon. Iris Vasquez knelt on the concrete floor, her bare knees taking the weight, and pressed her palm flat against the glass tank. Inside, the seedling stirred.

It had taken three months to coax the chimeric cells into differentiation. The base was orchid — a hardy epiphyte with aggressive root structures — but she had spliced in firefly luciferase and a synthetic operon that produced a slow, persistent glow. The result sat in its agar bed, little more than a green nub with translucent tendrils.

"Come on," she whispered.

The tendril twitched. A pulse of light traveled from the base to the tip, weak but real. Iris smiled and checked the nutrient drip. The mix was exactly right: magnesium, calcium, a proprietary blend of plant hormones she synthesized herself in the back room. Everything the law said she could not possess.

She had named this piece Nocturne. It would grow into a cascade of glowing blooms that hung from the ceiling like a frozen waterfall. If it survived the night.

The power had flickered twice already. The city's grid was failing, and rationing meant the greenhouse heaters might cut out before dawn. Iris pulled a thermal blanket from the shelf and draped it over the tank. She had lost pieces before. Each death took a part of her with it.

She checked the other tanks. The helix-vine coiled in its cylinder, healthy. The mirror-moss spread across its slate, fine. But the new one, Nocturne — the new one was the first of a new generation. The first since the crackdowns had begun in earnest.

Iris sat back on her heels and listened to the hum of the pumps. Outside, the city's sodium lights cast orange rectangles through the frosted windows. Somewhere out there, regulators were hunting people like her. But in here, in the warm wet dark, she was growing something the world had never seen.

She touched the glass again. The tendril pulsed brighter.

Chapter 2: A Tip

The message arrived on a secured line that routed through three proxy servers and left no trace. Agent Elara Cross read it on her personal tablet while standing in the Bureau parking garage, her breath fogging in the cold morning air.

"Genetic art," the message read. "Sector Seven. Abandoned textile mill. See for yourself."

No signature. No return protocol. The attached coordinates led to a building she knew: the old Merrimack mill, shuttered for a decade, a known haunt for squatters and people who wanted the city not to find them.

Cross got into her standard-issue sedan and sat with the engine off. Sector Seven was a gamble. She had chased a dozen tips from anonymous sources, and nine of them had led to empty rooms or booby-trapped labs designed to waste her time. But the tenth had been real — a basement full of modified zebrafish that glowed in ultraviolet light, each one a felony.

She keyed the ignition and pulled out of the garage.

The drive took twenty minutes through worsening traffic. The city was waking up, its citizens unaware that a woman in a gray coat was on her way to destroy someone's life work. Cross had stopped thinking about it that way. The work was illegal. That was the end of it.

The mill rose against the overcast sky, its brick facade stained with decades of soot. Cross parked half a block away and approached on foot. She carried a standard enforcement kit: sampling vials, a field sequencer, a sidearm loaded with sterilizing rounds. The rounds did not kill people. They killed cells.

She found the entrance unlocked. The door swung inward on rusted hinges, revealing a cavernous space filled with the smell of mildew and rot.

And something else. Something sweet and organic, like flowering jasmine.

Cross stepped inside and drew her sidearm. The door closed behind her, and the light from outside narrowed to a single dusty beam.

Chapter 3: The Collector's Appetite

The Collector did not touch the wine. He sat in a leather armchair in a penthouse that overlooked the river, his hands folded in his lap, watching a vine uncurl from a brass pot on the coffee table. The vine moved slowly, its tendrils searching for purchase, and where they touched the air, they left faint trails of phosphorescent blue.

"It's called a memory vine," said the dealer, a nervous man named Park who wore a suit two sizes too large. "It responds to electromagnetic fields. Every time someone passes near it, the vine remembers the shape of their body and adjusts its growth."

The Collector said nothing. He watched the vine find the edge of the pot and hesitate, then curl back on itself.

"The applications are extraordinary," Park continued. "It can be trained to grow around specific objects. To mark things. To signal — "

"Where did you get it?"

Park swallowed. "A source. A woman. She operates out of Sector Seven, I think. She doesn't use intermediaries."

The Collector picked up the pot and examined the vine's base. The stem was thick and fibrous, with a glossy sheen that suggested careful feeding. The phosphorescence was even, not blotchy. Quality work.

"How much?"

"Two hundred thousand."

The Collector set the pot down. "I want to meet her."

Park shook his head. "She doesn't work that way. She sells through drops. You leave payment, she leaves the piece. No contact."

"Then make contact." The Collector stood and walked to the window. The river below was gray and slow. "Tell her I have a commission. Tell her I will pay ten times her usual rate. And tell her that if she refuses, I will find her anyway."

He did not turn around. He did not need to. Park was already on his phone, stammering through a message to an encrypted address.

The vine pulsed once, a slow wave of blue that traveled from root to tip. The Collector watched its reflection in the glass.

Chapter 4: Rationing

The power cut out at 2:47 AM.

Iris woke to silence. The hum of the pumps had stopped. The backup battery should have kicked in, but the green indicator light on the panel remained dark. She swung her legs out of the cot and crossed the cold floor to the main breaker.

The battery was dead. The rationing schedule had shifted without warning, and the grid operators had cut her block an hour early. She had not charged the backup.

The temperature in the greenhouse was already dropping. Iris could feel it on her skin, the way the air went still and cool. She looked at the tanks. The helix-vine had gone limp. The mirror-moss had lost its luster.

Nocturne sat in its tank, the tendril dark.

"No," she said.

She found candles in the supply drawer and lit three of them. The heat was pathetic, barely measurable. She pulled the thermal blankets from the shelves and wrapped each tank as best she could. Then she gathered her own body heat by sitting in the center of the room, her arms around her knees, and let the candles do what they could.

At 3:30, she made a decision. She went to the small refrigerator where she kept the nutrient solutions and pulled out a bottle of sterile glucose. She drank half of it herself — she needed the energy — and poured the rest into a heating pan. She set the pan over a candle flame and waited for it to warm.

When it was tepid, she carried it to Nocturne's tank and poured it into the drip port. The liquid soaked into the agar. Nothing happened for a long minute.

Then the tendril pulsed. Faint. But alive.

Iris sat with it until dawn, feeding the candle flame, talking to the seedling in a low voice.

Chapter 5: The Raid

The mill was empty.

Cross swept the ground floor with her flashlight, finding nothing but collapsed machinery and pigeon droppings. The sweet smell had grown stronger as she moved deeper in, and she followed it to a door at the back of the building that led to a basement staircase.

She descended slowly, her boots careful on the crumbling concrete steps.

The basement was a single room, maybe forty feet square. In the center stood a row of glass tanks on metal shelving. Cross approached with her sidearm raised.

The tanks were dry. Each one had been drained and scrubbed clean. But the smell was here, concentrated, trapped in the close air. Jasmine and something sharp, like ozone.

She ran her gloved finger along the rim of a tank. Sticky residue. Fresh.

The artist had known she was coming. Someone had warned them.

Cross holstered her weapon and took out the field sequencer. She swabbed the residue and fed the sample into the device. The sequencer whirred for thirty seconds and returned a partial gene signature: orchid base, firefly luciferase, and two synthetic operons she did not recognize.

She recorded the data and attached the sealed sample to her belt. Then she walked the perimeter of the room, looking for anything the artist might have left behind. A fiber. A fingerprint. A mistake.

She found nothing.

Whoever had made this art was careful. Professional. And someone was tipping them off.

Cross climbed back up the stairs into the gray morning light, her jaw tight. This was not a dead end. It was a beginning.

Chapter 6: The Offer

The message appeared on Iris's encrypted terminal at 6 AM, just as she was drifting off to sleep.

"Client wants a commission. Ten times standard rate. He wants to meet."

She read it three times. The sender was her drop intermediary, a woman who called herself Juno and whose real name Iris had never asked. Juno handled the sales, took her cut, and never asked questions. That was the arrangement.

Ten times standard rate was half a million.

Iris typed back: "No meetings. Drop only."

The reply came within seconds. "He says he will find you if you refuse."

Iris stared at the screen. The words sat there, plain and threatening. She looked around the greenhouse. At the helix-vine, pulsing gently in the dawn light. At Nocturne, its tendril now stretching upward, seeking the warmth of the day.

She typed: "Find someone else."

She closed the terminal and went to check the nutrient levels. Her hands were steady, but her heart was not. She had dealt with aggressive buyers before. They made threats, then they moved on. The art was illegal, but the buyers were criminals too. They had as much to lose as she did.

This felt different. The message had come through Juno, which meant the buyer knew about Juno. That meant the buyer knew about the network.

Iris checked the lock on the door. She checked the false wall where she had hidden the youngest pieces. Everything was in order.

But she could not shake the feeling that someone was watching.

Chapter 7: The Oldest Piece

Iris's first successful sculpture sat in the corner of the greenhouse, in a tank twice the size of the others. She called it Lumen. It was a tree.

Not a real tree — it was too small for that, barely three feet tall. But it had a trunk and branches and a canopy of leaves that shone with a soft, steady light, like a lamp that never needed electricity. Iris had grown it from a modified bonsai ficus, splicing in genes from a bioluminescent jellyfish and a deep-sea bacteria that produced light without heat.

Lumen was eight years old. In human terms, it was ancient. In bio-art terms, it was a miracle.

This morning, the leaves were drooping.

Iris noticed it immediately. The light was dimmer than usual, and the leaves at the tips had begun to curl inward. She checked the soil. Dry. She checked the nutrient feed. Blocked.

She cleaned the feed line and refreshed the nutrient solution. She trimmed the dead leaves and sprayed the canopy with a fine mist of mineral water. She did all the things she knew how to do.

The leaves did not uncurl.

Iris sat on the floor beside Lumen's tank and pressed her forehead against the glass. The tree had been with her through everything. Through the expulsion from the university. Through the move to Sector Seven. Through every narrow escape and every sleepless night.

If Lumen died, it would be a sign. A sign that the city had finally become too hostile for living things.

She adjusted the light cycle and added a supplement to the nutrient mix. Then she waited.

Chapter 8: The Signature

Cross sat in her cubicle at the Bureau, staring at the gene signature on her monitor. She had run the sample from the mill through the central database twice. The first search returned no matches. The second returned a partial.

The partial linked back to a research project at the University of the Northern Basin, twelve years ago. The project was called "Synthetic Operon Expression in Ornamental Flora." The principal investigator was Dr. Helena Vasquez.

Cross pulled up Dr. Vasquez's file. Tenured professor. Published extensively. Retired five years ago and moved to a coastal town. Clean record.

But the grant documents listed a graduate research assistant: Iris Vasquez. The professor's daughter.

Cross expanded the search. Iris Vasquez had been expelled from the program in her third year for ethics violations. Specifically, for conducting unauthorized gene-editing experiments on living tissue without approval. The violations were serious enough that the university had considered pressing charges.

The file was sealed. But Cross had clearance.

She opened the seal and read the internal report. It described a secret laboratory in the basement of the biology building, where Iris Vasquez had grown a series of modified organisms — plants and animals — that she called "living sculptures." She had been creating them since her first year.

The university had destroyed everything. Iris had been banned from campus and threatened with legal action if she ever attempted similar work within state lines.

Cross printed the file and pinned the photo of Iris Vasquez to her board. A young woman with dark eyes and a defiant expression, staring at the camera like she had already won.

Chapter 9: Stem Cells

Iris needed a new source of pluripotent cells. Her supplier had gone silent after the threat from the Collector, and she could not afford to wait.

She prepared the harvest kit herself: a sterile syringe, a vial of anticoagulant, a cold pack. She tied a tourniquet around her own arm and found a vein at the crook of her elbow. The needle slid in cleanly. She drew ten milliliters of blood.

The process was crude. A centrifuge would have been better, but she had sold hers months ago to pay for nutrient medium. She separated the blood components by hand, using a manual centrifuge she had built from a salad spinner and some fishing line. It worked. It always worked.

She extracted the white blood cells and treated them with a reprogramming cocktail that flipped their development backward, turning them into induced pluripotent stem cells. The process took six hours. When it was done, she had a dish of cells that could become anything.

She divided them into three batches. One for Nocturne, if the seedling needed a boost. One for Lumen, if the old tree failed to recover. And one for something new, something she had been designing in her notebooks for months: a moth.

The moth would be her masterpiece. It would have a wingspan of twelve inches, iridescent scales that shifted color with the angle of light, and a nervous system sophisticated enough to fly. It would be the first animal she had attempted since the crackdowns.

She sketched the gene sequences in her notebook. The orchid base gave way to insectoid patterns. The luciferase would go into the wing veins. The synthetic operons would control the wingbeat frequency.

It would fly. She would make sure of it.

Chapter 10: Watching

The Collector sat in a van parked on the corner of Argyle and Seventh, watching the entrance to Iris Vasquez's building through a pair of binoculars. He had been there for three hours.

The building was unremarkable: six stories of brown brick, fire escapes zigzagging down the front, a convenience store on the ground floor. The address Juno had given him led to a door between the store and a laundromat. He had seen Iris enter it twice.

The first time, she had been carrying a bag of groceries. The second time, she had been carrying a cardboard box marked with biohazard symbols. Careless. Or confident.

The Collector lowered the binoculars and picked up his phone. He had a man inside the Bureau now, a clerk in the records division who fed him information for a monthly retainer. The clerk had sent him a file an hour ago: Agent Elara Cross had found the gene signature from the mill. She was closing in.

This was good. When Cross moved, Iris would panic. When Iris panicked, she would make mistakes. And when she made mistakes, the Collector would be there to catch her.

He did not want to destroy the artist's work. He wanted to own it. He wanted the woman in a room of his choosing, with her equipment and her nutrients and her cells, growing pieces for him and only him. The garden would be his, and the world would never see it.

He smiled and raised the binoculars again. The door between the store and the laundromat remained closed.

But he was patient. He had always been patient.

Chapter 11: Close

Cross stood in front of 1472 Argyle Street and checked the address against her notes. This was the building where the partial gene signature had led her. The building where Iris Vasquez's mail was routed. The building where, according to utility records, the electricity usage was three times the residential average — consistent with running a significant amount of laboratory equipment.

She walked into the convenience store and bought a bottle of water. She asked the clerk about the door next door.

"Storage," the clerk said without looking up. "Closed."

Cross thanked him and left. She walked past the door, examining it. Heavy steel. Three locks. A keypad. No visible camera, but there would be one somewhere.

She could force entry. She had the authority. But if she was wrong, if the tip was a dead end, she would waste the warrant and alert the real target.

She decided to wait. She found a café across the street with a window table and ordered coffee. She sat there for two hours, watching the door.

No one entered. No one left.

But at 4:17 PM, the light in the window above the convenience store flickered. Not a reflection. Not a passing cloud. A light turning on inside a room on the second floor.

Cross smiled and finished her coffee. The building was occupied. The game was on.

She stood and left a tip on the table. On her way out, she glanced up at the second-floor window. The light was still on.

Chapter 12: Flight

Iris paced the length of the greenhouse, her phone in her hand. The call from Juno had come ten minutes ago.

"The regulator was at your building today. A woman. She sat in the café across the street for two hours."

Iris's heart knocked against her ribs. "How do you know?"

"I have eyes on Argyle. You need to move."

The greenhouse suddenly felt like a cage. Iris looked at the tanks, at the glowing pieces, at the years of work that filled every shelf and corner. She could not take them all. She could not take most of them.

But she could take the small ones. The portable ones. The ones that mattered most.

She grabbed the carrying cases from under the cot. They were insulated polyethylene, designed to maintain temperature and humidity for up to twelve hours. She had built them for exactly this scenario.

She packed Nocturne first — the seedling was the future. Then the mirror-moss, which could survive a week without care. Then three smaller pieces, cuttings she had started from the helix-vine.

She left Lumen. The old tree was too big to move, and too sick to survive the journey. She put her hand on its tank and whispered an apology.

Then she carried the cases to the false wall at the back of the greenhouse. She slid the panel aside and revealed a narrow passage that led to the building next door — a decoy address she had rented years ago under a false name.

She stepped through and pulled the panel closed behind her. The greenhouse went dark.

Chapter 13: The Raid Next Door

Cross arrived at the building with a team of four agents at 6 AM the next morning. The warrant had come through at 5:47. She had the papers in her hand when she kicked the door between the store and the laundromat.

The door gave way on the first impact. The team poured through, flashlights cutting through the dark.

They found a stairwell. They climbed.

The second-floor landing led to a single door. Cross tried the handle. Unlocked.

She pushed it open and stepped into a small apartment. A cot. A sink. A refrigerator. A terminal.

No greenhouse. No tanks. No plants.

The apartment was clean. Spotless. As if no one had ever lived there.

Cross walked to the window and looked down at the street. The café was directly across. She had watched the wrong window.

"Clear," one of the agents called from the back room. "Nothing here."

Cross closed her eyes. She had been close. Close enough that Iris had run. Close enough that the artist knew she was coming.

Someone was tipping her off. Someone inside the Bureau.

She turned away from the window and saw something on the floor near the baseboard. A single leaf. Glowing faintly in the morning light.

She picked it up. It was still alive.

Chapter 14: The Mentor

Cross drove three hours to reach Dr. Helena Vasquez. The retired professor lived in a white house on a cliff overlooking the ocean, a quiet town where the only crime was tourists stealing towels from the beach. Cross found her in the garden, wearing a wide-brimmed hat and gloves, pruning roses.

"I wondered when someone would come," Helena said without looking up. "You're Bureau."

"Yes."

"Then you're here about Iris."

Cross sat on a stone bench and watched the old woman work. The roses were ordinary. No modifications. No glow.

"Iris hasn't contacted me in years," Helena said. "I don't know where she lives. I don't know what she's making."

"But you know she's making something."

Helena stopped pruning and turned to face Cross. Her eyes were sharp and tired. "My daughter is the most gifted genetic artist I have ever seen. The university threw her out because they were afraid. She was too good, too fast, and she didn't care about their rules. So yes, I know she's making something. She never stopped."

"Help me find her before someone else does."

Helena looked at the glowing leaf Cross had placed on the bench. "Who else?"

"There's a buyer. Wealthy. Connected. He wants her work enough to hurt her."

Helena picked up the leaf and held it to the light. The glow was weak but steady. "She was always best with the living things. I taught her the science, but the art — that was all her." She handed the leaf back. "I don't know where she is. But if she's in trouble, she'll run to the water. She always did."

Cross stood and thanked her. As she walked back to the car, Helena called after her.

"Don't destroy her work, Agent. The world needs more people who make beautiful things."

Chapter 15: Threats

The supplier's name was Duvall, and he ran a black-market gene-sequencing operation out of a laundromat in Sector Nine. Iris had been buying from him for two years. He was reliable, discrete, and afraid of nothing.

Until today.

The Collector's men found him at closing time. Two of them, big men in suits that did not fit the neighborhood. They cornered Duvall in the back room, where he kept the sequencers and the freezer full of plasmids.

"We want to know about the woman," one of them said. "The bio-artist. You supply her."

Duvall shook his head. "I don't know what you're talking about."

The second man picked up a sequencer and dropped it on the floor. The casing cracked. The device sparked and died.

"That was five thousand dollars," Duvall said.

"Tell us about the woman, or we'll break everything in this room. Then we'll break you."

Duvall looked at the ruined sequencer. He looked at the door. He looked at the men.

"Sector Seven. She has a drop point in an old mill. I don't know more than that. She never tells me where she works. It's safer for both of us."

The first man nodded. "The mill is already burned. The Bureau raided it yesterday."

"Then I don't know how to help you."

The men looked at each other. The second man picked up another sequencer and held it over the floor. "Think harder."

Duvall thought. "She mentioned a building on Argyle once. Just once. I don't know the number."

The man set the sequencer down. "That's all we needed."

They left Duvall standing in his ruined back room, surrounded by broken glass and leaking plasmids. He waited until their footsteps faded, then he pulled out his phone and sent a message to Juno:

"They know about Argyle."

Chapter 16: The False Wall

Iris was in the decoy apartment when the message came through. She read it, deleted it, and stood very still.

They knew about Argyle. The question was which part of Argyle they knew about. The apartment? Or the passage?

She crossed to the false wall and pressed her ear against it. Silence. She slid the panel open a crack and peered into the greenhouse. Dark. Empty. The tanks sat in their rows, their inhabitants glowing faintly. Lumen's light was dimmer than ever.

She stepped through and closed the panel behind her. The air in the greenhouse was warm and damp. She checked each tank quickly. The pieces she had left behind were still alive, but the power rationing had weakened them. The helix-vine had lost its tension. The mirror-moss was brown at the edges.

And Lumen — Lumen was dying. The leaves were dark, the trunk soft.

Iris knelt beside the old tree and touched the glass. "I'm sorry," she whispered. "I'm so sorry."

She could not save it. Not here, not now. She could only save the cuttings she had already taken.

She went to the workbench and gathered her tools. The scalpel. The nutrient vials. The notebook with the moth sequences. She packed them into a canvas bag and slung it over her shoulder.

Then she turned off the main power to the greenhouse. The pumps stopped. The lights went dark. The glow of the dying pieces faded slowly, like embers cooling.

She did not look back.

Chapter 17: The Patents

Cross sat in her cubicle, surrounded by printouts of patent applications filed by Iris Vasquez during her time at the university. There were fourteen of them, each one a detailed description of a genetic construct that the patent office had rejected on ethical grounds.

But the descriptions were enough. Cross read each one carefully, taking notes on the techniques Iris had developed. The orchid base vectors. The luciferase integration protocol. The synthetic operons for controlled expression.

The patents were a map of Iris's mind. They showed what she had been thinking about, what problems she had been trying to solve. And they showed the gaps — the things she had not patented because she had not wanted anyone to know.

Cross spread the patents across her desk. She looked for patterns. For connections. For the thing that Iris was working on now.

She found it in the last patent, filed just before the expulsion. It described a method for creating chimeric nervous systems — synthetic neural tissue that could be grown outside a host organism and integrated into modified organisms.

The patent had been rejected, but the method was sound. Cross had enough biology training to understand that. Iris had been working on something with a nervous system. Something that could move. Something that could think.

Cross sat back in her chair and looked at the photo of Iris pinned to her board. "What are you building?" she asked the photograph.

The photograph did not answer.

Chapter 18: The Mutagen

The package arrived at the decoy apartment addressed to "J. Smith." It was small, wrapped in brown paper, with no return address. Iris found it in the mail slot and brought it upstairs.

She opened it on the worktable. Inside was a glass vial filled with a iridescent liquid that shifted from green to blue as she tilted it. A note was taped to the side:

"A gift. For the next piece. No strings. — An admirer."

Iris knew who it was. The Collector. The buyer who had threatened her through Juno. He had found her decoy address. He knew where she was.

She should throw the vial away. She should pack her bags and leave the city tonight.

But she held the vial up to the light and watched the liquid shift. She had never seen a mutagen like this. The iridescence suggested a complex protein structure, something designed to interact with chimeric cells in ways that standard mutagens could not.

She uncapped the vial and sniffed. Odorless. She touched a drop to her fingertip and tasted it. Bitter.

She knew she should throw it away. But her curiosity was stronger than her fear.

She carried the vial to the dying helix-vine cutting and added a single drop to the nutrient medium. The liquid dispersed in swirling colors. The medium turned milky, then clear.

The vine did nothing for thirty seconds. Then it twitched. Then it began to grow.

Iris watched as new tendrils emerged from the cutting, thicker and brighter than the originals. They reached upward, searching for something, and when they found the lamp, they wrapped around it and began to pulse with a deep blue light.

The mutagen worked. It worked better than anything Iris had ever made.

She stared at the vial in her hand. The Collector had sent her a weapon. And she had used it.

Chapter 19: Revival

The helix-vine cutting grew six inches in the first hour. By the second hour, it had doubled in thickness. By the third, it had produced a flower.

Iris had never seen the vine flower before. The bloom was the size of her fist, deep purple, with petals that shimmered like oil on water. When she touched it, the petals closed and then slowly opened again, as if the flower were aware of her.

The mutagen had done something irreversible. The vine was no longer a cutting. It was a new organism, more vigorous and more intelligent than the original. It had learned, in three hours, how to respond to touch.

Iris sat on the floor and watched it grow. She should have been afraid. The mutagen was a trap, a way for the Collector to make her dependent on his supply. But she could not stop watching.

The vine's tendrils found the edge of the pot and began to descend, searching for more surface to colonize. Iris moved her bag out of their path, and the tendrils followed her hand, stopping where it had been, curling as if puzzled.

"You're beautiful," she said.

The vine pulsed in response.

Iris looked at the mutagen vial. There was enough left for one more dose. She thought about the moth. About the flying chimeric creature she had designed in her notebooks. With this mutagen, the moth could be more than she had imagined. It could be the most advanced bio-art piece ever created.

She capped the vial and put it in her pocket. She would use it. But she would use it on her terms.

Chapter 20: The Mentor's Warning

Cross called Dr. Helena Vasquez from the car on the drive back from the coast.

"I found something in her patents," Cross said. "A method for synthetic neural tissue. She was working on something with a nervous system."

The line was quiet for a moment. Then Helena spoke. "She told me about that once. She wanted to make something that could see her. Something that could recognize her and respond."

"That's dangerous."

"That's art, Agent. Art is supposed to be dangerous."

Cross gripped the steering wheel. "Do you know if she succeeded?"

"I don't know. But if she did, then there's something in the world that loves her. And she will do anything to protect it."

Cross ended the call and pulled over to the side of the road. She sat in the silence of the car, the ocean visible through the trees, and thought about what Helena had said.

A living thing that could see. That could recognize. That could love.

She had been thinking of Iris as a criminal. A lawbreaker. A threat to public safety. But Helena was describing something else — an artist who grew companions because she was alone.

Cross started the engine and pulled back onto the road. She was still going to find Iris. But she was no longer sure what she would do when she did.

Chapter 21: The Break-In

They came at midnight.

Iris was asleep in the decoy apartment when she heard the crash. She sat up, her heart pounding, and listened. Footsteps. Heavy. Multiple people.

She grabbed her bag and slid through the false wall into the greenhouse. The footsteps followed. They had found the passage.

Iris ran through the dark greenhouse, past the tanks, past the dying Lumen, to the fire escape on the far side. She heard the panel behind her slide open. She heard a voice call out.

"She's in here!"

She threw the fire escape window open and climbed out onto the metal platform. The ladder was rusted, but it held. She descended into the alley below and ran.

Behind her, the footsteps entered the greenhouse. She heard glass breaking. She heard shouting. She did not look back.

She ran through the alley, through the streets, until her lungs burned and her legs shook. She found a bus station and sat on a bench, her bag clutched to her chest, her breath coming in ragged gasps.

She had lost everything. The greenhouse. The pieces. The vine. The only thing she had left was the notebook in her bag and the vial of mutagen in her pocket.

And the moth. The moth was still inside her.

She closed her eyes and waited for the bus.

Chapter 22: Aftermath

Iris returned to Argyle at dawn. She knew it was stupid. She knew the Collector's men might still be there. But she had to see.

The false wall was still open. The greenhouse was destroyed. Every tank had been smashed. The nutrient solutions had pooled on the floor and dried into a sticky film. The pieces — her years of work — lay dead and withered among the shards of glass.

Lumen was gone. Not dead — gone. The tank had been moved. The tree had been taken.

Iris stood in the center of the wreckage and felt something break inside her. The Collector had not just destroyed her work. He had stolen her oldest piece. The first thing she had ever made that was truly alive.

She knelt down and picked up a shard of glass. Her reflection stared back at her, fractured and distorted.

She stayed there for a long time. Then she stood up and walked out.

She had nothing left. But that meant she had nothing left to lose.

Chapter 23: Too Late

Cross arrived at Argyle Street at 7 AM, called by a neighbor who had heard breaking glass in the night. She climbed the stairs and found the greenhouse.

She stood in the doorway, taking in the destruction. The smashed tanks. The dead organisms. The smell of rot and nutrient medium.

She stepped carefully through the wreckage. The pieces were beyond saving. Whatever the artist had been growing here was gone.

But there were signs of a struggle. The false wall had been forced open. A trail of boot prints led through the room to the fire escape. There had been two groups here: the intruders and the artist.

Cross followed the boot prints to the fire escape. The ladder was down. She climbed down to the alley and found more prints, overlapping, heading toward the street.

She called the Bureau and requested a forensic team. Then she stood in the alley, looking up at the broken window, and felt a cold anger settle in her chest.

Someone had gotten here first. Someone had destroyed the evidence and taken something valuable enough to carry away.

She pulled out her phone and called the records clerk she suspected was the leak. The call went to voicemail.

The Collector was ahead of her. But the game was not over.

Chapter 24: The Supplier

Iris found Duvall in the back of his laundromat, sweeping up glass. The sequencers were gone. The freezer was empty.

"Regulators?" Iris asked.

"Collector's men." Duvall leaned on the broom. "They broke everything. They made me tell them about Argyle."

"I know. They found me."

Duvall looked at her, really looked. She was dirty and exhausted, her clothes torn. "You look like you've been through hell."

"Close enough. I need a favor. I need a secure place to work. Somewhere they won't find me."

Duvall set the broom aside. "There's an old water-treatment plant in Sector Twelve. Abandoned. The power still works. I know someone who used to run it."

"Will you take me there?"

Duvall nodded. "For old times' sake. But you owe me."

"I know."

She followed him out the back door and into a waiting truck. As they drove through the city, Iris pressed her hand against the vial in her pocket. The moth was waiting. And now she had nothing to lose but the work itself.

Chapter 25: The Private Showing

The Collector stood in his penthouse, surrounded by guests in evening wear. In the center of the room, on a pedestal of black marble, stood Lumen.

The tree glowed with a soft, steady light. Its leaves had been cleaned and polished. Its trunk had been stabilized with a clear resin. It looked healthy — healthier than it had looked in months.

The guests circled the pedestal, murmuring. They were wealthy people, powerful people, the kind of people who owned art that no one else could see. They had paid a hundred thousand dollars each for the privilege of this evening.

"What is it?" a woman asked.

"A living tree," the Collector said. "Genetically modified. The first of its kind. It has been growing for eight years."

"Can it feel?"

The Collector smiled. "That is the question, isn't it? But I'll let you decide for yourself."

The woman reached out to touch a leaf. The leaf curled away from her finger.

The guests gasped. The Collector smiled wider.

"Art that knows it is being watched," he said. "That is what I offer you tonight."

The guests pressed closer, reaching for the leaves, watching them retreat. Lumen shone steady and silent, the oldest living sculpture in the world, now a trophy in a room full of people who wanted to own the impossible.

Chapter 26: Pressure

Cross sat across from her supervisor, a man named Dettmer who had never approved of her methods. He was reading her report with a frown.

"You raided the wrong building."

"Intelligence led me there. The artist had already fled."

"And now the scene has been compromised by a third party."

"Yes."

Dettmer set the report down. "I'm pulling you off this case, Cross."

"Sir — "

"You're too close. The report says you visited the artist's mother. That's not standard procedure."

Cross kept her voice level. "The mother is a former leading researcher in the field. She had information."

"And now you're compromised. Internal Affairs will review your actions. You'll be reassigned to desk duty until the review is complete."

Cross stood. She wanted to argue, but she knew it would not help. She nodded once and walked out of the office.

She went to her cubicle and began packing her personal items. The photo of Iris. The glowing leaf. The patent applications.

She would not stop investigating. She would just do it without the Bureau's approval.

If they wanted to call that a resignation, she would let them.

Chapter 27: The Water Plant

The abandoned water-treatment plant in Sector Twelve was a cathedral of concrete and rust. Iris stood in the main chamber, a vast hall filled with empty filtration tanks and dead pipes. The ceiling rose thirty feet above her, and the only light came from the windows near the roof.

It was perfect.

Duvall had helped her move the essentials: a generator, a worktable, a set of glass tanks salvaged from a closed laboratory supply store. She had set up in the corner of the hall, where a maintenance office provided a roof and four walls.

She had been there for three days. The generator hummed constantly, powering the heaters and the pumps. The first tank was already seeded with the base culture — orchid cells, reprogrammed and ready.

The moth would grow here. In the quiet dark of the dead plant, surrounded by the ghosts of the city's clean water, Iris would create her masterpiece.

She checked the culture every hour. The cells were dividing steadily, forming the first rudimentary structures of the wings. The mutagen sat on the shelf, untouched. She was saving it for the nervous system.

She ate canned food and slept on a camp mattress. She worked until her hands shook. And every night, she dreamed of Lumen, glowing in a stranger's house.

Chapter 28: The Invitation

Cross received the invitation on her personal tablet, three days after she was suspended. It came from an encrypted address with no sender name. The message was simple:

"Dinner. Tomorrow. 8 PM. A car will pick you up. Come alone."

She should have deleted it. She should have reported it as a potential threat. Instead, she typed back: "Where?"

The reply came instantly: "You'll see."

The next evening, a black sedan pulled up to her apartment building. Cross got in. The driver said nothing. They drove through the city, across the bridge, into a neighborhood of high-rises and private security.

The car stopped at a building with a lobby of marble and gold. A doorman escorted her to the elevator, which required a keycard for the top floor.

The doors opened onto a penthouse. And standing in the center of the room, under a glowing tree, was the Collector.

"Agent Cross," he said. "Thank you for coming."

Cross looked at the tree. At Lumen. She recognized it from the Argyle greenhouse. "You're the buyer."

"I prefer the term collector. It has a more generous spirit." He gestured to a chair. "Please. Sit. We have much to discuss."

Cross did not sit. "You destroyed her studio."

"I took what I wanted. I'm not responsible for the destruction." He walked to the tree and touched a leaf. It curled. "She is very talented. But she is also very stubborn. I offered her a partnership. She refused."

"So you stole her work."

"I preserved it. If I hadn't taken it, the Bureau would have destroyed it." He turned to face her. "You know I'm right. You've seen what they do."

Cross said nothing. But she did not leave.

Chapter 29: The Menagerie

The Collector led Cross through his penthouse, room after room, each one filled with living art. A tank of glowing fish that changed color when she approached. A vine that grew along the ceiling, its leaves opening and closing in patterns that seemed almost like language. A wall of moss that shifted through a slow gradient of green to blue.

"This is a fraction of what I own," the Collector said. "I have warehouses full of pieces that would take your breath away. But this one — " he stopped in front of a small tank on a pedestal, " — this one is my favorite."

Inside the tank was a creature the size of a cat. It had the body of a rodent and the wings of a bat, but its face was almost human in its expressiveness. It watched Cross with dark, intelligent eyes.

"What is it?"

"An experiment. The artist who made it called it a cherub. It's about intelligence — whether we can grow it, not build it." He tapped the glass. "It recognizes me. It knows my voice."

The creature pressed its nose against the glass, then chittered and retreated to the corner of the tank.

"He's shy," the Collector said. "But he's learning. Every day, he learns something new."

Cross stared at the creature. It was a felony — every cell in its body was a crime. But it was also alive. Aware. Capable of learning.

"You want me to look away," she said.

"I want you to understand what's at stake." The Collector turned to her. "The Bureau would kill this creature. They would incinerate every piece in this room and call it justice. Is that what you want?"

Cross did not answer. She did not know the answer.

Chapter 30: Stabilization

The moth's wings were growing unevenly.

Iris noticed the problem on the fourth day. The left wing was developing faster than the right, the cells multiplying at different rates. If she did not correct it, the moth would be lopsided, unable to fly.

She adjusted the nutrient concentrations. She increased the temperature on the left side of the tank. She monitored the cell division rates through the microscope, making tiny adjustments to the chemical balance.

The work was delicate and exhausting. She slept in two-hour increments, waking to check the cultures, to adjust the feeds, to record the growth patterns in her notebook.

The mutagen sat on the shelf. She had not touched it. She wanted the moth to grow naturally, to be her work, not the Collector's. But the temptation was always there.

On the fifth day, the wings began to color. The iridescence was exactly as she had designed it — shifting from blue to green to purple as the angle of light changed. The veins were beginning to form, thin channels that would carry nutrients and, eventually, the signals from the nervous system.

The moth was becoming real.

Iris stood over the tank and watched the wings move. A slow flutter, barely visible. The first sign of life beyond simple growth.

"Hello," she whispered.

The wings fluttered again, stronger this time. The moth was trying to fly before it was ready.

Iris smiled. It was a perfectionist's tic. The moth had inherited her impatience.

Chapter 31: The Offer

The Collector poured two glasses of wine and handed one to Cross. She took it but did not drink.

"I want you to work for me," he said. "Not as an agent. As a protector. Someone who understands the value of what I'm preserving."

"I'm a regulator."

"You were a regulator. Now you're a woman who has seen the truth." He sat down and crossed his legs. "The Bureau destroys art. I preserve it. Which side do you want to be on?"

Cross set the wine down. "You're asking me to betray my oath."

"I'm asking you to betray an institution that destroys living things. That's not a betrayal. That's a moral choice."

She looked around the room. At the glowing tree. At the fish that changed color. At the vine that traced patterns on the ceiling. She thought about the Bureau's incinerators, the sterilizing rounds, the sealed evidence lockers where living things starved to death.

"I won't work for you," she said. "But I won't report you. Not yet."

The Collector smiled. "That's more than I expected."

"Don't thank me. I'm going to find her first. And when I do, I'm going to make sure she's safe."

She turned and walked to the elevator. The doors closed, and she stood alone in the descending car, her heart pounding, her mind spinning.

She had just made a deal with a criminal. And she was not sure if she was the villain or the hero.

Chapter 32: The Warrant

Cross filed the warrant at 6 AM the next morning, before the Bureau's legal team had arrived to stop her. She used her remaining clearance to push it through the system, citing the destroyed greenhouse and the stolen piece as evidence of a criminal conspiracy.

The warrant was for the Collector's penthouse. For Lumen. For every living thing in that building.

She knew it was reckless. She knew she would be fired when Dettmer found out. But the warrant was legal, and the evidence was real, and if she could seize the Collector's collection, she could use it as leverage.

She drove to the Collector's building with a team of five agents, all of whom thought they were serving a routine warrant. The elevator required a keycard, so they took the stairs.

Eight floors up, Cross's phone rang. It was Dettmer.

"Stand down, Cross. The warrant has been vacated."

"What?"

"The Collector's lawyers called the judge. The warrant was based on evidence you obtained without authorization. It's been thrown out."

Cross stopped on the stairwell landing. The agents behind her stopped too.

"Sir — "

"Come back to the Bureau. We'll discuss your future here."

The call ended. Cross stared at her phone. The Collector had known she would come. He had been ready.

She dismissed the agents and walked back down the stairs alone. The Collector was always one step ahead. And she was running out of moves.

Chapter 33: The Delay

The Collector's lawyers buried the warrant in procedural delays. By the time Cross's request for a new hearing was scheduled, the Collector had already moved his collection to a secondary location. The penthouse was empty. The living tree was gone.

Cross stood in the bare room, looking at the space where Lumen had stood. The floor was clean. The air was still. No trace remained.

She called Helena Vasquez.

"He moved everything," Cross said. "I don't know where."

"I told you. He's always one step ahead."

"I need to find Iris. She's the only one who can testify against him."

Helena was quiet for a moment. "I don't know where she is. But if she's still working, she'll need supplies. Black-market gene sequences. Unregistered plasmids. There's only one person in the city who can provide those without leaving a trail."

"Who?"

"A man named Duvall. He runs a laundromat in Sector Nine. But he won't talk to you. He doesn't trust regulators."

Cross hung up and pulled up the map on her phone. Sector Nine. Laundromats. She searched and found one: Duvall's Laundry and Dry Cleaning.

She got in her car and drove.

Chapter 34: The Wings

The moth's wings reached full span on the eighth day.

Iris measured them with a ruler: twelve inches exactly. The iridescent scales caught the generator light and scattered it across the walls in rainbows. The pattern was perfect — a gradient from deep blue at the base to silver at the edges, with flecks of gold where the light hit at the right angle.

The body was developing too. A thorax covered in fine white hairs. Six legs, jointed and strong. Compound eyes that reflected the room in a thousand tiny facets.

The nervous system was the last piece. Iris had designed it to be the most sophisticated bio-art nervous system ever created. The moth would not just fly. It would navigate. It would avoid obstacles. It would seek light and warmth.

And if she used the mutagen, it would do more. It might learn.

Iris held the vial in her hand. The iridescent liquid caught the light, promising transformation. She could add it to the tank and watch the moth become something beyond her design. Something unpredictable.

Or she could trust her own skill. She could let the moth be her work, pure and unassisted.

She put the vial back on the shelf. The moth would be hers. All hers.

Chapter 35: The Trail

Cross found Duvall in the back of his laundromat, repairing a washing machine. He looked up when she entered and did not bother to hide his hostility.

"You're Bureau."

"Not anymore. I'm looking for Iris Vasquez."

Duvall turned back to the washing machine. "I don't know her."

"She's a bio-artist. You supplied her with plasmids and sequences. I'm not here to arrest her. I'm here to help her."

Duvall laughed. "Help her? The Bureau doesn't help people like her."

"I resigned this morning. I'm not Bureau anymore."

Duvall studied her for a long moment. Then he set down his tools. "Why should I trust you?"

"Because the Collector is going to find her. And when he does, he's going to take everything she has and lock her in a room where she'll grow art for him until she dies. If you care about her at all, you'll tell me where she is."

Duvall wiped his hands on a rag. "Sector Twelve. The old water-treatment plant. She's been there for a week."

Cross nodded and turned to leave.

"Agent," Duvall called after her. "She's working on something big. Something she's never tried before. Don't get in her way."

Cross did not answer. She walked out into the street and got into her car. The water-treatment plant was twenty minutes away. She drove fast.

Chapter 36: Bring Her To Me

The Collector received the news in his study. His man on the street had called with an update: the former regulator, Cross, had been spotted heading toward Sector Twelve.

"She's found the artist," the man said.

The Collector set down his pen. "How?"

"She talked to Duvall."

The Collector closed his eyes. He had been careless. He should have dealt with Duvall when he had the chance.

"I want the artist brought to me. Alive. Unharmed. I don't care what it takes."

"And the regulator?"

The Collector considered. Cross was a problem. She was smart and determined and no longer constrained by Bureau rules. But she was also useful — she had shown that she could be reasoned with.

"Bring her too. But she's secondary. The artist is the priority."

The call ended. The Collector stood and walked to the window. The river was dark below, the city lights reflecting on its surface.

Iris Vasquez was out there, creating something beautiful. And soon, she would be in his home, creating for him alone.

The thought made him smile.

Chapter 37: The Water Plant

Cross arrived at the water-treatment plant as the sun was setting. The building was a massive concrete structure, hulking against the orange sky. She parked a block away and approached on foot, her hand resting on the sidearm at her hip.

The main door was unlocked. She pushed it open and stepped into the vast hall.

The first thing she noticed was the light. A warm glow came from the far corner of the building, illuminating the empty filtration tanks and rusted pipes. The second thing she noticed was the hum — a generator, running somewhere nearby.

She walked toward the light. Her footsteps echoed in the empty space.

Iris was standing at a worktable, her back to Cross, examining something in a glass tank. The tank glowed with a shifting, iridescent light.

"Iris Vasquez."

Iris turned. Her face was thin and tired, her eyes sharp. She looked at Cross with recognition. "You're the regulator. The one who raided Argyle."

"I'm not a regulator anymore."

"Then what are you doing here?"

Cross held up her hands. "The Collector knows where you are. He's coming. I came to warn you."

Iris stared at her. Then she laughed. "You came to warn me? You've been hunting me for weeks."

"I was doing my job. I'm not doing it anymore." Cross stepped closer. "I've seen his collection. I've seen what he does. He took your tree, Iris. He's going to take you too, if you don't move."

Iris looked at the tank behind her. The moth inside stretched its wings, a slow, deliberate movement.

"I'm not leaving," Iris said. "I'm almost done."

Chapter 38: The Standoff

The Collector's men arrived twenty minutes later. Cross heard their vehicles first — two cars, pulling up to the plant's entrance. She drew her sidearm and positioned herself behind a concrete pillar.

Iris stayed at the worktable. She was trying to finish something, her hands moving quickly over the tank.

"They're here," Cross said.

"I know."

"You need to hide."

"I need to finish."

The main door burst open. Four men entered, spreading out across the hall. They were armed, but not with sterilizing rounds. These were real bullets.

Cross stepped out from behind the pillar, her weapon raised. "Stop. Federal agent."

The men paused. One of them laughed. "You're suspended, Cross. We know."

"Still armed."

The man shrugged. "So are we."

Cross looked at Iris. The artist was still working, her back to the intruders, her hands steady.

"Just give us the woman," the man said. "And we'll let you walk out of here."

Cross looked at the tank. At the glowing wings inside. At the woman who had created something the world had never seen.

She lowered her weapon.

"No," she said.

The man's smile faded. "Then we do this the hard way."

The standoff held for three heartbeats. Then Iris turned from the tank and said, "I'm done."

The tank opened. And the moth emerged.

Chapter 39: The Garden Burns

The moth rose from the tank on wings of iridescent light. It was the size of a bird, twelve inches of shifting blue and silver, and it moved through the air with a grace that seemed impossible.

The Collector's men stared. One of them raised his weapon, but another knocked it down.

"Don't shoot it," he said. "The Collector wants it alive."

The moth circled the hall once, testing the air. Then it flew to Iris and landed on her shoulder.

Iris touched its wing gently. "Go," she whispered.

The moth lifted off and flew toward the worktable. It hovered over the generator, and Iris understood what it was doing. She had programmed it to seek warmth. The generator was the warmest thing in the room.

The moth landed on the generator's exhaust vent. Its wings folded. Its body began to glow brighter, then brighter still.

"Get back," Iris said.

She grabbed Cross's arm and pulled her toward the exit. They ran through the dark hall, past the filtration tanks, toward the main door.

Behind them, the moth's body reached critical temperature. And the generator exploded.

The fire caught quickly. The old pipes, the dry concrete, the residual chemicals in the tanks — everything burned. The hall filled with orange light and black smoke.

Iris and Cross burst through the main door and into the night. Behind them, the water-treatment plant was a pyre, the fire consuming every trace of the garden.

Iris fell to her knees and watched it burn. The moth. The work. Everything.

Chapter 40: The Tracker

The Collector's men retreated from the burning building, their mission failed. But one of them had done his job.

As Iris had run past, he had pressed a small tracker to the inside of her coat collar. It was no larger than a button, magnetic, virtually undetectable. It would transmit her location to the Collector's system as long as she was within the city limits.

The Collector watched the tracker's signal on his phone. It was moving east, away from the fire. Iris Vasquez was alive. She was running.

He smiled and called his driver.

"Stay on the signal. Don't engage. Just tell me where she stops."

He would let her run. He would let her think she had escaped. And when she stopped, when she found a new place to work, he would be there.

The moth was lost. But the artist was still alive. And as long as she was alive, she could make more.

Chapter 41: Protective Custody

Cross drove them out of Sector Twelve with her foot on the accelerator. Iris sat in the passenger seat, staring through the windshield, her hands empty.

"You have nowhere to go," Cross said.

"I know."

"I can take you to a Bureau safe house. Protective custody."

Iris laughed bitterly. "Protective custody. You mean prison."

"No. I mean a room with a lock and a bed. No charges. No trial. Just safety."

"And my work?"

Cross was quiet. "Your work is gone. You saw it burn."

Iris closed her eyes. "I had notebooks. Designs. The sequences for the moth were in my head, but the details — "

"They're in your head. You can re-create them."

"It's not that simple."

"I know." Cross gripped the wheel. "But it's better than dying."

Iris said nothing. The city scrolled past the window, a blur of lights and shadows.

They drove for an hour. Cross took them to a Bureau building she knew, a low concrete structure in an industrial district. She parked in the underground garage and led Iris through a side entrance to a room on the third floor.

The room had a bed, a desk, a bathroom. No windows. The door locked from the outside.

"Get some sleep," Cross said. "Tomorrow, we figure out what comes next."

Iris sat on the bed and looked at the bare walls. She had nothing left. Not her garden. Not her tools. Not even her notebook, lost in the fire.

Just her hands. And her memory.

Chapter 42: Refusal

In the morning, Cross brought coffee and a change of clothes. Iris took them without thanks.

"There's someone who wants to talk to you," Cross said. "A prosecutor. If you testify against the Collector, they can seize his collection and prosecute him for bio-trafficking."

Iris set the coffee down. "No."

"Your work would be returned to you. Lumen would be returned."

"You don't know that. The Bureau would destroy everything. They always do."

Cross sat across from her at the small desk. "I can negotiate. I can make sure your pieces are preserved."

"You can't promise that. You're not even Bureau anymore."

Cross had no answer. Iris was right.

"Then what do you want to do?"

Iris looked at her hands. "I want to disappear. I want to find a place where no one knows my name and start over."

"I can help with that."

"No. You've done enough." Iris stood. "You hunted me. You trapped me. You put me in a room without windows and called it safety. I don't trust you."

Cross nodded slowly. "Fair enough."

"Let me go. Or arrest me. But don't pretend this is anything other than what it is."

Cross looked at the door. She had the keys. She had the authority, even without the Bureau. She could keep Iris here, safe and captive, and no one would question her.

But she had seen the moth rise from its tank. She had seen what Iris could create when she was free.

Cross unlocked the door.

"Go."

Chapter 43: Handover

Cross's supervisor did not take the news well.

"She walked out of protective custody? You let her walk out?"

"She wasn't going to testify. Keeping her was pointless."

Dettmer slammed his hand on the desk. "She was our only link to the Collector. Our only chance to shut down his operation."

"She was an artist who wanted to be free. I wasn't going to be her jailer."

Dettmer stared at her. "You're finished, Cross. You're suspended without pay. Internal Affairs will open an investigation. If I had my way, you'd be arrested."

Cross stood. "I've done more for this Bureau in the last month than you've done in your entire career. I found the artist. I found the Collector. I found the connection between them. And I made a choice about which side to be on." She walked to the door. "I chose the side that doesn't destroy living things."

She walked out of the office and out of the building. In the parking lot, she sat in her car and stared at the gray sky.

She had no job. No case. No plan.

She had a name and a tracker and a deep certainty that the Collector was not done.

Chapter 44: The Fugitives

Cross found Iris at the bus station, sitting on a bench with a cardboard cup of coffee. She sat down next to her.

"I'm fired."

"Good."

"So I have nothing to do today. Where are you going?"

Iris looked at her. "You're asking to come with me?"

"I'm asking if you want company."

Iris stared at her coffee. "I don't trust you."

"I know."

"And I don't need protection."

"I know."

"Then why?"

Cross looked at the departures board. "Because I've spent my whole life enforcing rules I didn't believe in. And the one time I found something worth protecting, I almost let it burn." She turned to face Iris. "I want to help you start again. No Bureau. No agenda. Just help."

Iris was quiet for a long time. Then she stood and walked to the ticket counter. She bought two tickets to a city on the coast, three hundred miles away.

She handed one to Cross.

"First sign of betrayal, I disappear."

Cross took the ticket. "Fair enough."

The bus arrived. They got on and sat in the back, watching the city shrink through the rear window.

Chapter 45: The Transit Hub

The Collector's tracker gave Iris's location as the central transit hub. He arrived with two men, expecting to find her alone.

Instead, he found her with Cross.

They were standing at the ticket counter, buying tickets. The Collector watched from the mezzanine, his hands in his pockets, his expression calm.

"Do you have a shot?" one of his men asked.

"Not from here. Too many witnesses."

"Then what?"

The Collector thought. He could wait. He could follow them to wherever they were going. But patience had its limits.

"There's a tunnel exit on the east side. They'll have to walk through it to reach the bus platforms. We'll take them there."

They moved through the terminal, keeping their distance. The woman and the regulator bought their tickets and walked toward the tunnel.

The Collector signaled his men.

But as they reached the tunnel entrance, Iris stopped. She turned and looked up at the mezzanine. Directly at him.

She knew.

She grabbed Cross's arm and pulled her into a crowd of passengers. The Collector pushed through the crowd, but they were gone.

He searched the terminal for twenty minutes. Nothing.

The tracker still showed her signal moving east. But when he checked the bus schedule, the eastbound bus had already left.

Iris Vasquez had escaped again.

Chapter 46: The Moth's Revenge

The moth did not die in the fire.

It had been programmed for survival. When the generator exploded, the moth had launched itself upward, seeking the roof. It found a broken window and escaped into the night, its wings charred but functional.

It flew east.

For two days, it flew. It followed the highway, its compound eyes tracking the movement of vehicles below. It did not know where it was going. It only knew that the warmth was behind it and the darkness was ahead.

On the third day, it found a city on the coast. It circled the rooftops, searching for something familiar. A scent. A signal. A trace of the woman who had made it.

It found her in a small apartment near the harbor. She was standing at a window, looking out at the water. The moth landed on the sill, and Iris turned.

She stared at the moth. Its wings were damaged, the iridescence dulled by soot. But it was alive.

She opened the window. The moth crawled onto her hand. Its legs gripped her skin, and its antennae twitched, tasting the air.

"Hello again," Iris whispered.

The moth's wings fluttered once. Then it climbed onto her shoulder and settled there, its damaged wings folded, its body warm against her neck.

Cross watched from the doorway. "I thought it died in the fire."

"So did I."

Iris touched the moth's wing. The creature pulsed with a faint light, weak but steady.

It had found her. It had crossed three hundred miles to find her.

She had not programmed it for that. It had learned.

Chapter 47: The Wound

The Collector did not react well to losing Iris at the transit hub. He stood in the middle of the terminal, his hands shaking, and watched the tracker signal fade as the bus carried her east.

"She knew," he said. "She knew I was following her."

One of his men stepped forward. "We can catch the next bus. — "

"No. She'll be off at the first stop. We've lost her."

The Collector's composure cracked. He turned and slammed his fist into a concrete pillar. The skin split on his knuckles, and blood dripped onto the floor.

His men watched in silence. They had never seen him lose control.

"She's just one woman," the Collector said. "One woman and a dead garden. And she's made me look like a fool."

He pulled out his phone and called his lawyer.

"I need a new strategy. Something that doesn't involve chasing her across the country."

The lawyer's voice was calm. "There's always the legal route. If she's no longer in the city, the Bureau loses jurisdiction. We could — "

"I don't want legal. I want her found."

The lawyer was quiet. Then: "I'll see what I can do."

The Collector ended the call and looked at his bleeding hand. He had collected art for twenty years. He had never lost a piece he truly wanted.

He would not lose Iris.

Chapter 48: The Border

The city on the coast was called Port Mercy. It was small and quiet, the kind of place where people went to disappear. Iris had chosen it because it was three hundred miles from the city, across a state line, and beyond the Collector's reach.

They arrived on a Tuesday morning. The bus station was a single building with a diner attached. Iris and Cross walked out into the salt air and looked at the ocean.

"Different laws here," Cross said. "Less strict about genetic work."

"How do you know?"

"I checked before we left."

Iris looked at her. "You really are done with the Bureau."

"Completely."

They found a room in a boarding house near the harbor. It had two beds, a kitchenette, and a window that faced the water. Iris opened the window, and the moth crawled onto the sill and spread its damaged wings in the sun.

"I'm going to need supplies," Iris said. "Equipment. A place to work."

"Tomorrow. Today, we rest."

Iris sat on the bed and watched the moth preen its wings. It was alive. Against all odds, it was alive.

She had lost the garden. But she had kept the future.

Chapter 49: Beginning Again

Iris found a warehouse near the docks, abandoned, with a concrete floor and a roof that leaked in three places. It was perfect.

She cleaned it with a broom and a bucket. She patched the roof with tarpaulin. She scavenged equipment from a closed university laboratory that was selling off its old stock. A centrifuge. A microscope. A set of glass tanks.

The moth watched from a beam overhead. It had grown stronger in the salt air, its damaged wings beginning to regenerate. Each day, new scales appeared, iridescent and bright.

Cross helped where she could. She ran errands. She carried equipment. She stood guard at the door while Iris worked.

On the fifth day, Iris seeded the first tank with a new culture. Orchid base cells, unmodified. She would build from scratch.

She had lost everything in the fire. But the fire had also cleaned the slate. She was no longer running. She was no longer hiding.

She was making something new.

"Any idea what it will be?" Cross asked.

Iris watched the cells divide under the microscope. "Something that's never been seen before."

She smiled. It was the first time Cross had seen her smile.

Chapter 50: The First Bloom

The new piece was smaller than the others. Iris had designed it to grow in a pot, on a windowsill, anywhere a person might want a small miracle. It was a flower, but a flower that remembered.

The base was a night-blooming jasmine, common and hardy. But Iris had spliced in genes from a species of sea creature that produced light when touched. The result was a bloom that glowed in response to contact, learning to recognize the pattern of a specific person's hand.

She named it Remembrance.

On the morning of the first bloom, Iris and Cross stood together at the window of the warehouse. The flower opened slowly, its petals unfurling in the gray dawn light. When Iris touched it, the bloom flared with a warm, steady light.

"Well?" Cross said.

Iris looked at the flower. At the light in her palm. At the future she had chosen.

"It's alive," she said.

She looked out at the ocean, at the horizon where the sun was beginning to rise. The moth sat on her shoulder, its wings restored, its body warm.

Port Mercy was not the end. It was the beginning.

And the garden, once lost, was growing again.
