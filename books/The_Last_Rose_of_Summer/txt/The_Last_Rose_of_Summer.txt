Chapter 1: The Widow's Garden

Margot Delacroix walked the rows before the sun cleared the hills. Dew clung to her boots. She touched each bloom as she passed — not counting, but greeting. The roses knew her hand.

There were thirty-seven bushes. Each had a name Étienne had given it. The pink one by the gate was "Morning Promise." The red one near the wall was "Heart's Fire." The yellow one that never bloomed right was "Stubborn Hope."

She stopped at the last row. A new shoot had appeared overnight, green and thin, reaching toward the first light. She knelt. The soil was cool. She pressed her fingers into it, feeling for moisture.

Étienne had taught her that. "The soil tells you everything," he would say. "You just have to listen."

She listened. The garden was quiet. A bee began its work in the lavender. The sun broke over the roofline of the stone farmhouse.

She had been alone for three years. The garden had kept her alive.

A neighbor once told her she should sell the nursery. Too much work for one woman. Margot had smiled and said nothing. That night she had pruned every bush by lantern light, her hands bleeding through her gloves.

The roses were not plants. They were prayers.

She stood and wiped her hands on her apron. The new shoot would need support. She would find a stake after breakfast.

The house was cool and dark inside. She lit the stove. She boiled water for coffee. Through the window, she could see the garden waiting.

She had not planned to enter a competition. She had not planned anything since Étienne died. She simply woke, and tended, and slept, and woke again.

But the letter would come today. She did not know it yet.

The coffee was bitter. She drank it standing at the window. The new shoot bent in the breeze. She smiled.

It was going to be a hot summer. The roses would need her.

Chapter 2: The Letter

The postman arrived at nine. Margot heard his motorbike from the end of the lane. She was on her knees weeding the border. She did not look up.

"Madame Delacroix!" His voice carried across the gravel.

She stood. Her knees complained. She wiped her hands on her apron and walked to the gate.

He held out a thick envelope. Good paper. Official seal. "Looks important," he said.

She took it. The return address read: Grand Prize of Provence — Aix-en-Provence Horticultural Society.

She thanked him. He tipped his hat and was gone.

She did not open it immediately. She carried it to the kitchen table and placed it next to the salt cellar. She made more coffee. She sat down.

The envelope sat between her hands. White. Cream. The seal was embossed with a rose.

She opened it slowly. Inside was a letter on heavy stock, a brochure, and a registration form.

"Dear Gardener," it began. "The Horticultural Society of Provence is pleased to announce the thirty-eighth annual Grand Prize of Provence. All amateur and professional growers are invited to submit their finest bloom. Judging will take place at the Harvest Festival in September. First prize: five thousand euros and the Silver Rose Trophy."

She read it twice.

Étienne had always wanted to enter. "Next year," he would say. "When I perfect the pink one." But next year never came.

She folded the letter and put it back in the envelope. She tucked the envelope into her apron pocket.

Outside, the garden was golden in the morning light. The new shoot had grown taller. She touched it.

"Should I do it?" she whispered.

The rose did not answer. But the wind moved through its leaves, and Margot felt something she had not felt in three years.

Possibility.

It hurt. It felt like hope.

She went back to weeding.

Chapter 3: The Rival's Estate

Laurent Vasseur took his coffee black on the terrace. Below him, the gardens stretched in perfect geometry. Thirty rows of hybrid teas. Twenty rows of floribundas. Ten rows of climbers trained on iron arches.

Every leaf was clean. Every stem was straight. Every bloom faced the sun.

His head gardener, Philippe, approached with the morning report. "Good news," he said. "The Crimson Queen has produced eleven buds. The color is deep. The form is excellent."

Laurent nodded. He did not smile.

"There is also this." Philippe handed him a letter. Same thick envelope. Same embossed seal.

Laurent read it without expression. He had won the Grand Prize three times. The Silver Rose Trophy sat on his mantelpiece, gathering dust.

"Register me," he said.

"Of course, sir."

"And find out who else is entering."

Philippe hesitated. "Is that necessary?"

"I want to know what I am competing against."

Philippe nodded and left.

Laurent finished his coffee. The gardens were beautiful. He had designed every inch of them. The water feature. The stone pathways. The greenhouse with its climate control system.

It was a masterpiece. It was empty.

He thought of his daughter. He pushed the thought away.

He walked down to the greenhouse. Inside, the air was warm and moist. His experimental roses grew in numbered pots. He checked each one. The measurements. The color saturation. The petal count.

Everything was perfect.

He wished he felt something about that.

He worked until the light failed. Then he went inside and ate alone. The house had seven rooms. He used two.

The letter sat on the counter. He looked at it while he ate.

He would win again. It was the only thing he knew how to do.

Chapter 4: The Last Rose

Three days after the letter arrived, Margot found it.

She was pruning the back row, the one that ran along the stone wall. These were Étienne's experimental bushes. He had been trying to create a new variety — a rose the color of dawn, with a scent like honey and lavender.

He had never finished.

She had kept them alive. That was all. She watered them. She fed them. She did not expect them to bloom.

But one had.

It was at the end of the row, half-hidden behind a wild lavender bush. A single stem had risen from the tangled branches. At its tip was a bud the size of her thumbnail, tight and green, with the first hint of pink at the edges.

She knelt. She parted the branches carefully. The bud was perfect.

"Étienne," she whispered.

She did not cry. Not yet. She simply looked at it, this small green thing that had come from nothing, from soil and water and the memory of a man who had loved roses.

She named it then. The words came without thought.

"The Last Rose of Summer."

She tended it every day. She gave it water when the drought came. She shielded it from the mistral wind. She talked to it.

"You are going to be beautiful," she told it. "You are going to be worth everything."

The bud grew. The pink deepened. The scent began to emerge — honey and lavender, just as Étienne had dreamed.

She did not tell anyone. Not even Celeste.

Some things were too precious for words.

Chapter 5: The Decision

Celeste came on Saturday afternoon. She brought bread and cheese and a bottle of rosé. She always brought food. "You don't eat enough," she said. "A woman cannot live on coffee and regret."

They sat on the terrace. The garden was full of bees. The air smelled of roses and thyme.

Margot told her about the letter.

Celeste put down her wine glass. "You have to enter."

"No."

"Margot."

"I am not a competitor. I am a gardener."

"That is exactly what a competitor is." Celeste leaned forward. "Étienne believed in you. He told me once that you had the best instincts he had ever seen."

"He was my husband. He was biased."

"He was a terrible liar. He said what he meant."

Margot looked at the garden. The new bud was hidden from view. She had not told Celeste about it.

"I am afraid," she said quietly.

"Of what?"

"Of wanting something. Of being disappointed."

Celeste reached across the table and took her hand. "You are already disappointed. You are already sad. The difference is whether you try or not."

Margot was silent.

"Étienne would want you to," Celeste said.

Margot closed her eyes. The sun was warm on her face. She could hear the bees. She could smell the lavender.

"All right," she said. "I will enter."

Celeste smiled and refilled their glasses.

The decision was made. Margot felt lighter. She also felt terrified.

That night, she went to the back row. The bud had grown. It was almost ready.

"Your father would be proud," she told the rose. "Whoever your father is."

She laughed at her own joke. Étienne would have laughed too.

She slept better than she had in months.

Chapter 6: The Registration

Aix-en-Provence was golden in the afternoon. The stone buildings glowed. The fountains splashed. The plane trees cast cool shadows on the Cours Mirabeau.

Margot had not been to the city since Étienne's funeral. She had forgotten how beautiful it was.

The Horticultural Society was housed in a seventeenth-century mansion near the cathedral. She climbed the stone steps and pushed through the heavy oak doors.

Inside, the hall was crowded. Dozens of growers. Some she recognized from the village market. Most she did not. They stood in clusters, talking in low voices. The air smelled of soil and cologne.

She registered at a long table. A woman with spectacles took her name and gave her a packet of documents.

"Your garden will be visited," the woman said. "The judge will come unannounced. Prepare your best bloom."

Margot nodded. She took the packet and stepped aside.

Then she saw him.

He was tall and lean, dressed in cream linen. His hair was silver at the temples. His face was severe, like a hawk's. He stood apart from the others, holding his registration packet as if it were beneath him.

Laurent Vasseur.

She knew the name. Everyone knew the name. Three-time winner. The king of Provencal roses.

He did not look at her. He looked through her, as if she were a window.

She felt small. She felt like a fraud.

But then she remembered the bud. The pink at the edges. The scent of honey and lavender.

She straightened her shoulders and walked past him.

He did not notice.

That was fine. He would notice later.

Chapter 7: The Rules

Madame Fontaine stood at the podium. She was old — perhaps eighty — but her voice was clear and sharp. She wore a navy dress and a string of pearls. Her hands were knotted with arthritis, but steady.

"The rules are simple," she said. "Each entrant will present one rose. The bloom must be cut no more than twelve hours before judging. It must be presented in a plain vase. No enhancements. No preservatives beyond clean water."

She paused. Her eyes swept the room.

"Judging will consider four criteria. Form. Color. Fragrance. And something beyond."

A murmur went through the crowd.

"Something beyond?" a man called out.

Madame Fontaine smiled. It was not a warm smile. "You will know it when you see it. Or you will not."

The growers exchanged glances.

Margot wrote the words in her notebook. Something beyond.

She thought of Étienne. Of the way he touched the soil. Of the way he spoke to the roses. Of the way he looked at her when she brought him coffee in the greenhouse.

Maybe that was it. Maybe the something beyond was love.

Or maybe it was grief.

She closed her notebook. The meeting ended. The growers filed out.

At the door, she passed Laurent Vasseur. He was talking to a man in an expensive suit. He did not look at her.

She walked back to her car. The sun was lower now. The plane trees cast longer shadows.

Something beyond.

She would find it. Or she would not.

Either way, she would try.

Chapter 8: What Lies Beyond

Margot could not sleep. She lay in bed, watching the moonlight move across the ceiling. The window was open. She could smell the night garden — jasmine, lavender, the faint sweetness of roses.

Something beyond.

What did that mean?

She got up and walked outside. The garden was silver and blue. The roses were closed for the night, their petals folded tight.

She went to the back row. The bud was there, catching the moonlight. It had grown again. Tomorrow it would begin to open.

She sat on the ground. The stones were cool through her nightgown.

"Tell me," she said to the garden. "Tell me what it means."

The garden did not answer.

---

Thirty kilometers away, Laurent stood at the window of his study. He was also awake. He was always awake.

He held a glass of brandy. He did not drink it.

Something beyond.

He had been a grower for twenty years. He understood form. He understood color. He understood fragrance. Those were technical matters. Measurable. Objective.

But the something beyond — that was different. That was the thing he could not buy, could not engineer, could not force into bloom with fertilizer and light.

He thought of Camille. He thought of the way she had looked at him the last time he saw her. She was seven. She did not understand why he was leaving.

He had not explained.

He put down the brandy. He picked up a pen.

He wrote a letter to his ex-wife. He wrote: "I would like to see Camille."

He read it. He tore it up.

He wrote it again.

This time, he did not tear it.

He sealed the envelope and placed it on the desk. He would mail it in the morning.

Or he would not.

He did not know yet.

Chapter 9: The First Encounter

The nursery supply shop was on the road to Saint-Rémy. Margot needed fertilizer, new gloves, and a proper pruning knife. Her old one had finally broken.

The shop was a cavern of burlap and metal. Shelves of pots. Bins of soil. Racks of tools. The air smelled of peat moss and copper.

She was reaching for a bag of rose fertilizer when another hand reached for it at the same time.

Their fingers almost touched. She pulled back. He pulled back.

Laurent Vasseur stood on the other side of the bag. He looked annoyed.

"Excuse me," he said.

"I was here first."

He looked at her. Really looked. For the first time, his eyes focused on her face.

"You are the woman from registration."

"Yes."

"I did not catch your name."

"Margot Delacroix."

He nodded. She could not read his expression.

"I need this fertilizer," he said.

"So do I."

They stood there, two people, one bag of fertilizer. The shopkeeper watched from behind the counter.

"There is more in the back," the shopkeeper said.

Neither of them moved.

"Take it," Laurent said finally. His voice was cold. "I have no desire to argue over fertilizer."

He turned and walked away. He did not say goodbye.

Margot picked up the bag. Her hands were trembling. She was not sure if it was anger or something else.

She bought her supplies and drove home. The bag of fertilizer sat on the passenger seat.

She had won. It did not feel like winning.

Chapter 10: The Daughter

The letter arrived on Tuesday. Laurent saw it on the hall table and recognized the handwriting. He did not open it immediately.

He made coffee. He went to the greenhouse. He checked the humidity levels. He inspected the Crimson Queen for aphids. He did everything except read the letter.

Philippe noticed. "Is something wrong, sir?"

"No."

"You have been staring at that rose for ten minutes."

Laurent straightened. "I am fine."

He went inside. He opened the letter.

His ex-wife had written:

"Laurent — Camille has been asking about you. She is twelve now. She wants to know who you are. If you want to see her, you may write to her directly. I will not stand in the way. But if you write to her, do not disappear again. She has been through enough."

He read it three times.

He thought of the last time he had seen Camille. She was seven. She was holding a teddy bear. She was crying.

He had been crying too. He had hidden it.

He put the letter in a drawer. He closed the drawer.

He went back to the greenhouse. The roses were perfect. They demanded nothing. They gave nothing.

It was easier that way.

But that night, he dreamed of his daughter. She was running through a garden. She was laughing. He tried to call out to her, but no sound came.

He woke at dawn. The pillow was wet.

He did not know if it was sweat or tears.

Chapter 11: The Visit

Celeste arrived with pruning shears and a bottle of wine. The shears were expensive — German steel, ergonomic handles. Margot protested.

"You cannot afford this."

"I can afford it. You cannot afford bad tools." Celeste pressed them into her hands. "Consider it an investment in victory."

They drank wine on the terrace. The sun was setting. The garden was pink and gold.

"When are you going to tell me about the rose?" Celeste asked.

"There is nothing to tell."

"There is everything to tell. You have that look."

"What look?"

"The look you used to have when Étienne was alive. The look of someone holding a secret."

Margot was quiet. She watched the sun sink behind the hills.

"There is a rose," she said finally. "Étienne's rose. The one he was working on."

"It bloomed?"

"Yes. One bloom. Perfect."

Celeste put down her glass. "Why did you not tell me?"

"Because if I tell you, it becomes real. And if it becomes real, I have to face what happens if it fails."

Celeste nodded. She understood. She had known Margot for twenty years.

"Show me," she said.

Margot led her to the back row. The bud had opened halfway. The pink was deepening. The scent was stronger.

Celeste looked at it. She was silent for a long time.

"That is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen," she said.

Margot felt tears prick her eyes. "It is him," she said. "It is Étienne."

"Then enter it. Win for him."

"I am not sure I can."

"Then enter it for yourself."

Margot looked at the rose. The petals were unfolding. Each one was perfect.

Maybe Celeste was right.

Chapter 12: The First Bloom

It happened on a Tuesday morning.

Margot went out at dawn, as she always did. The air was cool. The sky was clear. The garden was waking.

She reached the back row and stopped.

The Last Rose of Summer had opened fully for the first time.

It was the exact shade of pink Étienne used to bring her from the market. Deep at the center, fading to palest blush at the edges. The petals were arranged in perfect concentric circles. The scent rose in waves — honey first, then lavender, then something green and fresh.

Margot knelt. She touched a petal. It was soft as silk.

She cried.

She had not cried like this since the funeral. Great, heaving sobs that came from somewhere deep and hidden. She cried for Étienne. She cried for the rose. She cried for herself, for the woman she had become, for the woman she was still trying to be.

When she finished, she wiped her face with her sleeve. The garden was brighter now. The sun had cleared the hills.

She looked at the rose. It looked back at her, perfect and calm.

"Thank you," she whispered.

She did not know who she was thanking. Étienne. The rose. God. The garden.

It did not matter.

She stood up. She had work to do.

Chapter 13: The Greenhouse

Laurent's greenhouse was his sanctuary. Thirty meters long. Climate controlled. The glass roof opened automatically when the temperature rose too high.

He spent more time here than in the house.

Today, he was checking the Crimson Queen. It would be his entry for the competition. The color was perfect — deep red with hints of black at the edges. The form was classic. The fragrance was strong, with notes of tea and cloves.

"Perfect," Philippe said.

"Yes."

But Laurent was not satisfied. He did not know why. The rose was technically perfect. It would win. It always won.

But there was a hollowness in his chest that no amount of winning could fill.

"Have you heard anything about the other entrants?" he asked.

"There is one of interest. A widow from Saint-Rémy. Margot Delacroix."

"I have met her."

"She is growing something unusual. Her husband's experimental variety. Word is it is remarkable."

Laurent said nothing. He had seen her. A small woman in a worn apron. She did not look like a threat.

But there had been something in her eyes. A depth. A stillness.

"Do you want me to investigate?" Philippe asked.

"No. Let her grow her rose. I will beat it fairly."

Philippe nodded and left.

Laurent stayed in the greenhouse. He looked at the Crimson Queen. It was perfect.

He wondered why that did not feel like enough.

Chapter 14: The Watering

The drought came in July. The sky turned white. The sun was a hammer. The earth cracked.

The village imposed water restrictions. No hoses. No sprinklers. Hand watering only between dawn and dusk.

Margot woke at four each morning. She filled buckets from the well. She carried them to the roses, one by one.

Her back ached. Her hands blistered. She did not stop.

The Last Rose of Summer needed extra care. It was still young, its roots not yet deep. She gave it water every three hours. She shielded it with a parasol during the hottest part of the day.

Neighbors offered help. She refused. This was her garden. Her battle.

Celeste brought her ice water and made her sit down. "You will kill yourself."

"I will not let them die."

"The roses will survive. You might not."

Margot drank the water. She sat for five minutes. Then she went back to work.

The well was running low. She could hear it when the bucket hit the bottom. A hollow sound. A warning.

She prayed for rain. The sky stayed white.

At night, she lay in bed, too tired to sleep. She listened for thunder. There was none.

Her hands hurt. Her heart hurt.

But the rose was alive. That was what mattered.

Chapter 15: The Offer

He came on a Thursday afternoon. Margot was watering the back row. She heard a car engine, then footsteps on the gravel.

Laurent Vasseur stood at her gate.

She straightened. Her hands were muddy. Her hair was falling out of its knot. She looked exactly like what she was — a woman who had been working all day in the sun.

"Madame Delacroix."

"Monsieur Vasseur."

He did not open the gate. He stood on the other side, as if waiting for permission.

"I have heard about your rose," he said.

"Word travels fast."

"I would like to see it."

"No."

He blinked. He was not used to being refused.

"I am prepared to make you an offer."

"What kind of offer?"

"I want to buy it. The rose. Whatever you want for it, I will pay."

Margot stared at him. The audacity of it. The arrogance.

"It is not for sale."

"Everything is for sale, Madame Delacroix."

"This is not."

She picked up her bucket and walked away. She did not look back.

Laurent stood at the gate for a full minute. Then he got back in his car and drove away.

Margot's hands were shaking. She set down the bucket. She touched the rose.

"Do not worry," she whispered. "I will not sell you."

The rose swayed in the breeze.

She went back to work.

Chapter 16: The Story of Étienne

The village cemetery was on a hill overlooking the valley. Margot went on Sunday morning. She brought a single lavender sprig.

Étienne's grave was under an olive tree. The stone was simple. His name. His dates. The words: "He loved the earth."

She sat on the grass. The view was beautiful. Vineyards. Olive groves. In the distance, the mountains.

"I am entering the competition," she said.

The grave was silent. She imagined Étienne listening. He had always been a good listener.

"Your rose bloomed. It is perfect. It is the color of the market flowers you used to bring me."

She paused. A bee buzzed past.

"I do not know if I can win. I do not know if I even want to win. But I want to show them. I want them to see what you created."

She touched the stone.

"I miss you," she whispered. "Every day. Every single day."

A ladybug crawled across the stone. She let it crawl onto her finger. She placed it gently in the grass.

"I have to go now. The roses need watering."

She stood. She brushed the grass from her dress.

"I will come back next week. I will tell you how it goes."

She walked down the hill. The sun was warm on her shoulders.

She felt, for the first time in years, that Étienne could hear her.

Chapter 17: The Sabotage

She found it on Monday morning.

The back row. Three bushes uprooted. The roots exposed to the air. Soil scattered across the stones.

Someone had been in her garden.

She stood very still. She looked at the damage. The bushes were young — not the prize rose, but close to it. Whoever had done this had been looking for something.

They had not found it. The Last Rose of Summer was untouched.

But they had tried.

She felt rage. Hot and bright. Then fear. Cold and deep.

She checked the gate. The lock was intact. The fence was high. Whoever had done this had climbed.

She called Celeste.

Celeste came within the hour. She looked at the damage. Her face was hard.

"You need to report this."

"To whom?"

"The police. The Horticultural Society. Someone."

"I have no proof."

"You have three dead bushes."

Margot shook her head. "It will not bring them back."

She replanted the bushes. She watered them deeply. She did not know if they would survive.

That night, she took a lantern and a pair of shears and sat in the garden. She stayed until dawn.

No one came.

She stayed the next night too.

And the next.

Chapter 18: The Suspect

She found him in the village square. He was having coffee at the café, reading a newspaper.

She walked up to his table. She did not ask permission. She sat down.

He lowered his newspaper. His expression was unreadable.

"Did you do it?" she asked.

"Do what?"

"Someone came into my garden. They uprooted three of my bushes."

His eyes narrowed. "And you think it was me."

"I do not know who else would want to hurt my garden."

"Madame Delacroix, I compete with roses, not with violence. If I want to beat you, I will do it in the judging hall."

He said it with such quiet certainty that she believed him.

"Then who?"

"I do not know. But I will find out."

"Why would you help me?"

He looked at her. For a moment, his mask slipped. She saw something underneath. Something tired. Something almost kind.

"Because no one should have to protect their garden alone."

He stood. He placed money on the table for his coffee.

"I will send Philippe to check your fence."

He walked away.

Margot sat at the table, her coffee untouched. She did not know what to make of him.

A rival who offered to fix her fence.

She finished her coffee. It was cold.

Chapter 19: The Investigation

Philippe came that afternoon. He was a small, wiry man with quick hands and quiet eyes. He examined the fence. He examined the gate. He walked the perimeter of the garden.

"Fox," he said finally.

"Excuse me?"

"A fox. They dig. They push through weak spots. The damage to your bushes is consistent with an animal, not a person."

"Three bushes?"

"A fox den. There is one under your neighbor's shed. I saw it on the way in."

Margot felt foolish. And relieved.

"Thank you," she said.

Philippe nodded. He reinforced the weak spot in the fence. He refused payment.

"Monsieur Vasseur's instruction," he said.

After he left, Margot sat on the terrace. She looked at the garden. The damaged bushes were already recovering.

She thought of Laurent. He had sent his man to help her. He had believed her. He had offered to find out.

It confused her. He was supposed to be the enemy.

She went to the back row. The Last Rose of Summer was blooming. The scent filled the air.

"Maybe he is not so bad," she told the rose.

The rose did not answer.

But the wind carried its scent toward the house, and Margot felt something shift in her chest.

Something she was not ready to name.

Chapter 20: The Secret

Laurent drove to Aix on Saturday. He parked outside the school and waited.

He had written to his ex-wife. He had asked for details. The school. The daily schedule. The best time to see Camille without disrupting her day.

She had replied. She had been practical, as always. Camille was at the Lycée Saint-Charles. The school day ended at four. She walked home with friends.

He was early. He sat in his car and watched the gates.

At four, the students poured out. Laughing. Talking. Carrying backpacks.

He saw her immediately. She had his eyes. His hair. But his ex-wife's smile. She was walking with two other girls, her head thrown back, laughing at something.

She looked happy.

He did not get out of the car.

He watched her walk past. She did not look his way. She did not know he was there.

When she was gone, he started the engine. He drove home.

The letter was still on his desk. The one he had written to her. He had not sent it.

He was a coward.

He knew it.

But knowing did not make him braver.

He went to the greenhouse. He worked until his hands hurt. The Crimson Queen was almost ready.

Almost. But not quite.

Like him.

Chapter 21: The Blight

It started on the Crimson Queen.

A gray fuzz on the leaves. The edges curling. The buds withering before they could open.

Powdery mildew.

Philippe spotted it first. He called Laurent to the greenhouse immediately.

"How bad?" Laurent asked.

"The Queen is affected. Three others show early signs."

Laurent examined the leaves. His hands were steady. Inside, he was not.

"Cut out the infected material. Increase air circulation. I will prepare the spray."

He worked through the night. He mixed the solution himself. Sulfur. Copper. Water. He sprayed each bush individually, his movements precise and careful.

He did not sleep.

By morning, the infection was contained. The Crimson Queen had lost two buds. The others were intact.

Laurent stood in the greenhouse, covered in spray residue, exhausted.

He had saved his rose. He had done what needed to be done.

But there was a knock at the greenhouse door.

Margot Delacroix stood outside. She was holding a jar.

"I heard," she said. "About the blight."

"Word travels fast."

"I have something. A tincture. Étienne swore by it."

He looked at the jar. He did not want to accept it. Accepting help meant admitting weakness.

But the blight could return. He knew it.

He took the jar.

"Thank you," he said. The words were difficult.

She nodded. She left.

He put the jar on his workbench.

He did not open it. But he did not throw it away either.

Chapter 22: The Offer Returned

The tincture sat on Laurent's workbench for three days.

He looked at it every time he entered the greenhouse. A mason jar filled with brown liquid. A handwritten label: "Garlic and copper sulfate. One part to twenty parts water. Apply at dusk."

He did not use it.

On the fourth day, the mildew returned. A new patch on the Crimson Queen's lower leaves.

Philippe was distraught. "We need to try something else. The commercial spray is not working."

Laurent looked at the jar.

He opened it.

The smell was strong. Garlic. Copper. Something sharp and green.

He mixed a batch. He applied it at dusk, as the label instructed.

In the morning, the mildew had stopped spreading.

By the third day, it was gone.

Laurent stood in the greenhouse, staring at the jar. It had worked. A simple remedy from a widow's kitchen had done what his expensive supplies could not.

He did not thank her. Not yet.

But he kept the jar on his workbench. A reminder.

That evening, he wrote her a note. Short. Formal.

"The tincture was effective. I am grateful."

He mailed it before he could change his mind.

Chapter 23: The Recovery

Margot received the note on Saturday.

She read it three times. Short and formal. But it was something.

She put it on the windowsill above the kitchen sink. She looked at it while she washed the dishes.

She did not know why it mattered. But it did.

In the following days, Laurent's roses recovered fully. The Crimson Queen produced new buds. The color was deep. The form was strong.

Word spread through the village. The widow's remedy had saved the champion's garden.

People looked at Margot differently now. With respect. With curiosity.

She did not like it. She preferred to be invisible.

Celeste was delighted. "You are a local hero."

"I made a garlic spray. It is not heroic."

"You beat the blight. That is heroic enough."

Margot shook her head. But she smiled.

That afternoon, she found a bag of premium rose fertilizer on her doorstep. No note. No return address.

She knew who it was from.

She used it on the back row.

The Last Rose of Summer grew stronger. The petals deepened to a shade of pink that made her catch her breath.

Something was happening between them. She did not know what to call it.

She did not know if she was ready for it.

But the rose was blooming. And so, slowly, was she.

Chapter 24: The Village Festival

The summer festival was held on the last Saturday of July. The village square was strung with lights. Tables were laden with food. A band played under the plane trees.

Margot went with Celeste. She wore a white dress she had not worn since Étienne died. It was loose on her now. She had lost weight without noticing.

They ate. They drank. They danced.

Celeste danced with the baker. Margot danced with the postman, with the old man who sold honey, with a young woman who had just moved to the village.

She was laughing. She was alive.

She looked across the square and saw him.

Laurent stood at the edge of the crowd. He was not dancing. He was watching. He held a glass of wine he was not drinking.

Their eyes met.

She did not look away. Neither did he.

For a long moment, the festival noise faded. There was only him, and her, and the space between them.

Then he turned and walked away.

She watched him disappear into the darkness.

"Who was that?" Celeste asked.

"Laurent Vasseur."

"The rival?"

"Yes."

"He is handsome."

"He is complicated."

Celeste looked at her. "So are you."

Margot did not dance after that. She went home early.

She lay in bed, thinking of his eyes.

Chapter 25: The Conversation

He came the next morning.

She was in the garden, deadheading the front row. She heard his footsteps on the gravel. She did not turn around.

"Good morning, Monsieur Vasseur."

"Good morning."

He stood at the edge of the path. He was holding the jar. Empty now. Clean.

"I am returning this."

"Thank you."

She took it. Their fingers touched. She felt it like a spark.

"I wanted to say something," he said.

She waited.

"I am not good at this. At talking. At... people."

"I noticed."

He almost smiled. The corner of his mouth twitched.

"But I wanted to thank you. Properly. The tincture saved my garden."

"Your garden saved itself. I just gave it a push."

He nodded. He looked at the roses. They were beautiful in the morning light.

"Your garden is different from mine," he said.

"Is that bad?"

"No. It is better."

She did not know what to say.

"I should go," he said.

"You should stay."

The words came out before she could stop them.

He looked at her.

"All right," he said.

She made coffee. They sat on the terrace. They talked about roses. About the weather. About nothing important.

It was the first conversation she had enjoyed with a man since Étienne died.

When he left, she felt lighter.

Chapter 26: The Inspection

Madame Fontaine came on a Thursday.

Margot was in the garden, pruning. She heard a car. She looked up.

An old blue Citroën was parked at her gate. Madame Fontaine stepped out. She was small and elegant, dressed in gray linen.

"Madame Delacroix."

"Madame Fontaine."

"I am here for the inspection."

"I understand."

Madame Fontaine walked into the garden. She did not speak. She walked the rows slowly, pausing at each bush. She touched the leaves. She smelled the blooms.

Margot followed at a distance. Her heart was beating fast.

Madame Fontaine reached the back row. She saw The Last Rose of Summer.

She stopped.

She knelt. She studied the bloom. She leaned close and inhaled.

Silence.

She stood. She turned to Margot.

"Tell me about this rose."

"It was my husband's. He was working on it when he died. This is the first bloom."

"How long ago did he die?"

"Three years."

Madame Fontaine nodded. She looked at the rose again.

"And what does this rose mean to you?"

Margot thought about it. The truth rose in her chest.

"It means he is still here. It means love does not end when someone dies. It means I am not alone."

Madame Fontaine was silent for a long moment.

Then she placed her hand on Margot's shoulder.

"Thank you," she said. "That is what I needed to hear."

She left without saying more.

Margot stood in the garden, shaking.

Chapter 27: The Question

Madame Fontaine arrived at Laurent's estate at noon.

He was waiting at the gate. He had dressed carefully. He did not know why. She would see the garden, not him.

She walked the rows. She inspected the Crimson Queen. She checked the leaves, the stems, the soil.

"Your technique is flawless," she said.

"Thank you."

"The color is exceptional."

"Thank you."

She looked at him directly. "But I asked you a question before. At registration. What does your rose mean to you?"

Laurent was silent.

"You talked about technique. About form. That is not what I asked."

He looked at the Crimson Queen. It was perfect. He had made it perfect because he could not make anything else perfect. His marriage. His relationship with his daughter. His life.

"It means I am not a failure," he said quietly.

Madame Fontaine said nothing.

"I have won three times. I have a greenhouse full of trophies. But I am alone. My daughter does not know me. My wife left me. And I wake up every morning in an empty house."

He stopped. He had said too much.

Madame Fontaine looked at him. Her eyes were kind.

"Thank you," she said. "That is what I needed to hear."

She left.

Laurent stood in the greenhouse, his hands shaking.

He had told a stranger the truth. He felt raw. He felt exposed.

He also felt, strangely, relieved.

Chapter 28: The Crack

After Madame Fontaine left, Laurent could not work.

He walked through the house. Room after empty room. The study where he drank alone. The dining room where he ate alone. The bedroom where he slept alone.

He had built this life. He had chosen it.

But standing in the greenhouse, telling the truth to an old woman, had cracked something open.

He drove to the village. He parked outside the school in Aix. He waited.

When Camille came out, he got out of the car.

She saw him. She stopped. Her friends kept walking. She stood alone on the pavement.

"Camille."

She did not run away. She did not come closer.

"Who are you?" she asked.

"I am your father."

She looked at him. Her eyes were his.

"I know," she said. "Maman told me you might come."

"I should have come sooner."

"Yes. You should have."

She was twelve. She was wise. She was his daughter.

"Can we talk?" he asked.

She considered. Then she nodded.

They sat on a bench outside the school. He told her he was sorry. He told her he had been a coward. He told her he wanted to be better.

She listened. She did not forgive him. But she did not leave.

It was a start.

Chapter 29: The Crack Widens

He told Margot the next day.

He came to her gate in the afternoon. She was in the garden. She saw his face and put down her tools.

"What happened?"

"I saw my daughter."

He said it like a confession. Like a wound.

Margot led him to the terrace. She poured him water. She waited.

He told her everything. The divorce. The years of silence. The letters he wrote and tore up. The school visits where he did not get out of the car.

"I have been afraid," he said. "For five years."

Margot listened. She did not interrupt.

"Yesterday, I got out of the car."

"How did it feel?"

"Terrifying."

"And now?"

He considered. "Less terrifying."

She smiled. "That is how it works. You do the thing, and it becomes less terrifying."

"How do you know?"

She looked at the garden. "Every morning, I wake up and face a world without Étienne. It was terrifying at first. It still is, sometimes. But I do it anyway."

He looked at her. "You are strong."

"I am not strong. I am just too stubborn to give up."

He laughed. It was a small sound, rusty from disuse.

It was the first time she had heard him laugh.

She decided she liked it.

Chapter 30: The Invitation

"Would you like to see the garden?"

She asked it without thinking. They were standing at her gate. He had been there for an hour, talking. The conversation had flowed easily.

"Properly," she added. "Not just from the path."

He hesitated. "I do not want to intrude."

"You are not intruding. I am inviting."

He followed her through the garden. She showed him each rose. She told him their names. She told him the stories.

"This one is Morning Promise. Étienne planted it the week we moved in. It never blooms before ten."

"This one is Heart's Fire. It blooms only in June. The rest of the year, it sulks."

He listened. He asked questions. He was not cold. He was curious.

They reached the back row.

The Last Rose of Summer was in full bloom. The pink was luminous. The scent hung in the air like a song.

Laurent stopped. He did not speak.

He knelt. He looked at the rose. He brought his face close to the petals and inhaled.

When he looked up, his eyes were wet.

"It is beautiful," he said.

His voice cracked on the last word.

Margot did not say anything. She stood beside him and looked at the rose.

They stayed there, two people, one flower, silent in the afternoon sun.

Chapter 31: The Confession

They sat on the terrace as the sun went down. She opened a bottle of wine. He accepted.

"I have not talked to anyone in years," he said. "Not like this."

"Why not?"

"Because talking means trusting. And trusting means getting hurt."

"I have been hurt too."

"I know. That is why I am talking to you."

They drank. The sky turned pink, then purple.

He told her about Camille. About the day he left. About the years of silence.

"I told myself I was protecting her," he said. "That she was better off without me. But that was a lie. I was protecting myself."

"From what?"

"From failing her. From being the father my father was."

"Were you like him?"

"I did not know. I was afraid I might be."

She reached across the table and touched his hand. Just her fingers on his wrist.

"You are here now. That is not failure."

He looked at her hand on his skin. He did not pull away.

"Tell me about Étienne," he said.

So she did.

She told him about the day they met. About the way he laughed. About the way he died — suddenly, in the greenhouse, among the roses.

"I found him," she said. "He was holding a pruning knife. He was smiling."

"How did you survive?"

"The roses. They needed me. So I stayed."

He took her hand. He held it.

They sat in the dark, holding hands, not speaking.

It was enough.

Chapter 32: The Photograph

She brought out the album on his third visit.

"It helps," she said. "Looking at the pictures. It reminds me he was real."

They sat on the terrace. She opened the album on the table.

There was Étienne. Tall, dark-haired, laughing. Étienne in the garden. Étienne covered in soil. Étienne holding a rose he had just bred.

"He looks happy," Laurent said.

"He was."

"They are good pictures."

"They are all I have."

Laurent turned the pages. He stopped at the wedding photo. Margot in white. Étienne in a suit that did not quite fit. Both of them laughing.

"He looks like he knew how to be happy," Laurent said.

"He chose to be. Every day. He chose it."

Laurent was quiet.

"I do not know how to do that," he said.

"Neither did I. Not at first. But I am learning."

"How?"

"I start with the garden. Then I see if I can extend it to the rest of the day."

He looked at her. "You make it sound simple."

"It is not simple. But it is possible."

He closed the album. He looked at the garden. The roses were closing for the night.

"Do you think I can learn?" he asked.

"I think you are already learning."

He did not argue.

Chapter 33: The Letter Written

That night, Laurent wrote the letter.

He sat at his desk until midnight. He wrote three drafts. He threw away two.

The third one was simple.

"Dear Camille,

I am sorry I have been absent. I was afraid. I was wrong.

I would like to know you. I would like to be part of your life. I understand if you are not ready. I will wait as long as it takes.

I am your father. I want to be worthy of that.

With love,
Laurent"

He read it three times. His handwriting was shaky. He left a smudge on the corner.

He sealed the envelope before he could change his mind.

He drove to Aix at dawn. He posted it at the main post office. He watched the envelope disappear into the slot.

Then he went home and waited.

The waiting was worse than the writing.

He went to the greenhouse. He pruned. He watered. He checked the Crimson Queen.

The rose was perfect.

But it did not matter as much as it used to.

Chapter 34: The Waiting

Two weeks passed. No reply.

Laurent checked the mail every day. Nothing.

He called his ex-wife. No answer.

He drove past the school. Camille did not come out. He waited an hour. She never appeared.

He began to spiral. She had rejected him. She had read the letter and thrown it away. She hated him.

He stopped eating. He stopped sleeping.

Philippe noticed. "Sir, you need rest."

"I am fine."

"You are not fine. You look like a ghost."

Laurent walked away.

He went to Margot's garden. It was evening. She was watering. She took one look at his face and turned off the hose.

"Sit down."

He sat.

She poured him wine. She did not ask questions. She sat beside him and waited.

"What if she does not want me?" he said finally.

"Then you will survive. People do."

"I do not know if I can."

"You can. You are stronger than you think."

He looked at her. "How do you know?"

"Because you are here. Because you tried. Because you wrote the letter. That took courage."

He put his head in his hands.

She touched his back. Just her hand. Just there.

He did not cry. But he came close.

Chapter 35: The Answer

The letter arrived on a Tuesday.

Laurent saw it on the hall table. A small envelope. A girl's handwriting.

He picked it up. He carried it to the study. He sat down.

He opened it with a knife.

"Dear Papa,

Thank you for your letter. Maman said I should write back.

I remember you a little. You used to carry me on your shoulders. I liked that.

I would like to meet you. Maybe for ice cream? There is a good place near the school.

I am glad you wrote.

Love,
Camille"

He read it six times.

Then he cried.

He cried the way he had not cried in five years. Great, heaving sobs that came from somewhere deep. He cried for the years he had lost. He cried for the letter he had almost not written. He cried because his daughter had called him Papa.

He drove to Margot's garden. He showed her the letter.

She read it. She smiled.

"You did it," she said.

"We did it."

"No. You did it. I just kept you company."

He shook his head. "You kept me from giving up."

She did not argue.

"Can we celebrate?" he asked.

"I think we should."

They opened a bottle of champagne. They drank it in the garden. The roses watched.

It was the best day he had had in five years.

Chapter 36: The Preparation

The visit was set for Saturday. Camille would come to his house. His ex-wife would drop her off at noon and pick her up at six.

Six hours. He had not spent six hours with his daughter since she was seven.

He cleaned the house. He bought groceries. He bought sheets for the guest room — floral, because he thought a twelve-year-old girl might like flowers.

He bought art supplies. He bought books. He bought a board game.

Philippe helped him set up the guest room. "You have gone overboard."

"I want her to feel welcome."

"She will feel welcome if you are kind. The rest is decoration."

Laurent stopped. Philippe was right.

He put away half the things he had bought.

On Saturday morning, he stood at the window. He watched the road.

At eleven forty-five, a car appeared.

Chapter 37: The Visit

Camille got out of the car. She was wearing jeans and a pink sweater. She was carrying a small backpack.

His ex-wife looked at him through the windshield. She did not get out. She nodded. He nodded back.

Then Camille was standing in front of him.

"Hello, Papa."

"Hello, Camille."

They stood there. Neither knew what to do.

"Would you like to see the garden?" he asked.

"Okay."

He showed her the roses. She asked questions. He answered. She did not seem afraid of him. She seemed curious.

She stopped at the Crimson Queen. "This one is pretty."

"It is called the Crimson Queen."

"Can I smell it?"

"Yes."

She leaned in. She inhaled. "It smells like tea."

"It does. You have a good nose."

She smiled. It was his smile. He had forgotten.

They walked through the rest of the garden. They had lunch on the terrace. She told him about school. About her friends. About a book she was reading.

He listened. He did not interrupt.

At six, the car returned.

Camille hugged him before she left.

It was quick. A squeeze. But it was real.

"Can I come again?" she asked.

"Whenever you want."

She smiled. She got in the car. She waved.

He waved back.

When the car was gone, he went inside and sat down. He was exhausted. He was happy.

Chapter 38: The Weekend

The second visit was a full weekend.

Camille arrived on Saturday morning with a suitcase. She was staying until Sunday evening.

Laurent had planned activities. The garden. A walk in the hills. A trip to the market.

"Can we visit your friend?" Camille asked.

"My friend?"

"The lady with the garden. The one you told me about."

He hesitated. Then he called Margot.

They went to her garden on Saturday afternoon. Margot was waiting at the gate. She had made lemonade.

Camille ran into the garden. She touched the roses. She asked Margot their names.

"This one is Morning Promise. This one is Stubborn Hope."

"Stubborn Hope? That is a funny name."

"Your father named it that."

Camille looked at him. "You named a rose?"

"It was a long time ago."

"Before you were sad?"

The question hung in the air.

"Yes," he said. "Before I was sad."

Camille nodded. She went back to the roses.

Margot looked at him. She smiled.

"She is wonderful," Margot said.

"Yes. She is."

They sat on the terrace. They watched Camille run through the garden.

"We could do this more often," Margot said. "The three of us."

He looked at her. "I would like that."

It was the first time he had looked forward to the future in years.

Chapter 39: The Final Week

The competition was seven days away.

Margot woke each morning with a knot in her stomach. The Last Rose of Summer was at its peak. Every petal was perfect. The color was deep. The scent was strong.

But each day it stayed on the bush was a risk. Roses did not wait. They bloomed, and then they faded.

She checked it every hour. She watered it carefully. She talked to it.

"Just one more week," she whispered. "Stay beautiful for one more week."

It listened. The petals held.

Laurent's Crimson Queen was also at its peak. He had timed it perfectly. He had been doing this for twenty years.

But he found himself thinking less about winning and more about Margot. About Camille. About the weekend they had spent together.

He called Margot every evening.

"Is your rose holding?"

"Yes. Yours?"

"Perfect."

"See you at the judging."

"See you there."

They hung up. They both smiled.

Something had changed. Neither of them knew exactly what. But the competition felt different now. Less like a battle. More like a dance.

Chapter 40: The Offering

The morning of the judging arrived.

Margot woke at four. She dressed. She went to the garden.

The Last Rose of Summer was waiting. The petals were open. The scent filled the air.

She knelt. She took out her pruning knife.

"I am sorry," she whispered.

She cut the stem. Clean. Precise.

She carried the rose to the house. She placed it in a plain glass vase. She had practiced this. The vase was the right proportion. The water was room temperature. The stem was cut at the right angle.

She looked at the rose. It was perfect.

She dressed carefully. A cream dress. Simple earrings. Her mother's bracelet.

She carried the rose to the car. She placed it in the passenger seat. She drove slowly, avoiding bumps.

Aix was crowded. The Horticultural Society hall was full. Growers from all over Provence. Cameras. Officials.

She carried her rose inside.

She found her table. She placed the vase.

Then she saw Laurent.

He was across the hall. He was placing the Crimson Queen on its table. Their eyes met.

He smiled.

She smiled back.

Whatever happened now, they had already won something.

Chapter 41: The Exhibition

The hall was a garden of roses.

White, red, yellow, pink. Hybrid teas, floribundas, climbers, shrubs. Each one perfect. Each one a testament to patience and care.

The public filed through. They admired. They whispered. They pointed.

Margot's rose drew a crowd. The color was unusual. The scent carried across the hall.

"Where is that pink one from?"

"A widow in Saint-Rémy."

"Remarkable."

Laurent's rose drew its own crowd. The deep red. The classic form.

"The champion. He has won three times."

"Will he make it four?"

The growers stood by their roses. They answered questions. They pretended not to be nervous.

Margot was nervous. She could not stop looking at her rose. Was it wilting? Was the color fading?

She reminded herself to breathe.

At noon, Madame Fontaine entered the hall. She walked slowly down the center aisle.

The crowd parted.

The judging had begun.

Chapter 42: The First Cut

Madame Fontaine examined each rose.

She looked at the form. The symmetry of the petals. The way the bloom sat on the stem.

She looked at the color. The depth. The saturation. The way the light moved through the petals.

She smelled each one. She closed her eyes. She breathed deeply.

She made notes.

The growers watched. The crowd watched. The only sound was her footsteps and the scratch of her pen.

At the end of the first round, she eliminated half the entries.

The growers whose roses were cut gathered their vases and left. Some were gracious. Some were not.

Margot remained. Laurent remained.

They were among the final twelve.

Margot's heart was pounding. She looked at Laurent. He was calm. He had done this before.

But she saw his hand tremble slightly.

He was not as calm as he looked.

The second round began.

Chapter 43: The Final Judgment

The second round reduced twelve to six.

The third round reduced six to three.

Margot and Laurent were among the three. So was an old man from Arles with a white rose that smelled like honey.

The crowd pressed closer. The air was thick with tension.

Madame Fontaine examined the final three.

She looked at the white rose from Arles. She nodded. She moved on.

She looked at the Crimson Queen. She touched a petal. She closed her eyes. She nodded.

She looked at The Last Rose of Summer.

She stood very still.

She leaned close. She inhaled. She stayed there, eyes closed, for a long time.

The hall was silent.

She opened her eyes. She looked at Margot. She looked at Laurent.

"I will announce my decision in one hour," she said.

She left the hall.

The crowd erupted in speculation.

Margot stood by her rose. She was shaking.

Laurent walked over to her.

"Whatever happens," he said, "you should be proud."

"Should I?"

"Yes. That rose is extraordinary."

"Thank you."

They stood together, waiting.

Chapter 44: The Verdict

The hour passed slowly.

Margot could not sit. She walked around the hall. She drank water. She looked at her rose.

Laurent sat on a bench. He was calm on the outside. Inside, he was not.

At exactly one o'clock, Madame Fontaine returned.

The hall fell silent.

She stood at the podium. She looked at the crowd.

"Thank you all for your participation. The quality of entries this year was exceptional."

She paused.

"After careful consideration, I have reached my decision."

She looked at Margot. She looked at Laurent.

"I cannot choose."

The crowd murmured.

"I cannot choose between a rose grown from love and a rose grown from longing. They are two sides of the same grief."

She took a breath.

"The winner of the thirty-eighth Grand Prize of Provence is a tie. Margot Delacroix and Laurent Vasseur. Joint winners."

The crowd erupted.

Margot could not move. Laurent came to her. He took her hand.

"We did it," he said.

"We did it."

They stood together, hands clasped, as the crowd applauded.

Chapter 45: The Explanation

After the ceremony, Madame Fontaine found them.

They were standing in the garden behind the hall. The noise of the crowd was distant.

"You have questions," she said.

"Yes," Margot said. "Why a tie?"

Madame Fontaine sat on a stone bench. She looked tired.

"I have been judging for forty years. I have seen perfect roses. I have seen extraordinary technique. But I have rarely seen what you two brought today."

She looked at Margot. "Your rose is grown from love. I could smell it. I could feel it. It is Étienne's legacy."

She looked at Laurent. "Your rose is grown from longing. The desire to prove yourself. The hunger for something you have lost."

She smiled.

"The something beyond. That is what I was looking for. You both had it."

"And the man from Arles?" Laurent asked.

"His rose was beautiful. But it was just a rose. Yours were something more."

She stood.

"Wear the title well. Both of you."

She walked away.

Margot and Laurent stood in the garden, alone.

"So," Laurent said. "We are champions."

"Joint champions."

"That is better than being alone."

She looked at him. "Yes. It is."

Chapter 46: The Celebration

They celebrated in Margot's garden.

Camille came. Celeste came. Philippe came. Even Madame Fontaine came, driving her old blue Citroën.

They set up tables on the terrace. They brought food and wine. The roses bloomed in the twilight.

Camille ran through the garden. Celeste talked to Philippe about pruning techniques. Madame Fontaine sat in a chair and watched the sunset.

Margot and Laurent stood apart from the others.

"Thank you," he said.

"For what?"

"For making me write the letter. For not giving up on me."

"You did the hard part."

"I did not do it alone."

She looked at him. The light was golden. The air smelled of roses.

"Are we still rivals?" she asked.

"I do not think we ever were."

"Then what are we?"

He considered. "I do not know. But I would like to find out."

She smiled.

"So would I."

They stood together as the sun set. The garden was full of people and laughter.

It was the first time in three years that Margot had felt truly alive.

Chapter 47: The Acceptance

The next morning, Laurent came to the garden alone.

Margot was watering. She saw him at the gate and waved him in.

"I have been thinking," he said.

"That sounds dangerous."

He almost smiled. "I have been thinking about what I want."

"And?"

"I do not need to win anymore."

She put down the watering can.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean, the competition — I have been chasing it for years. The next prize. The next victory. But I do not need it."

"What changed?"

"You. Camille. The garden. I have enough."

She nodded. She understood.

"I do not need the Silver Rose," he said. "I have something better."

"What?"

"You. If you will have me."

She looked at him. The morning light was soft. The roses were blooming.

"Yes," she said.

He took her hand.

They stood in the garden. No crowd. No cameras. Just two people who had found each other through grief.

It was not a fairy tale. It was better.

It was real.

Chapter 48: The New Beginning

"Can I come back tomorrow?" Laurent asked.

"Yes."

"Can Camille come too?"

"Every day."

And so it began.

Laurent came every morning. He brought coffee. They sat on the terrace and planned the day. The garden. The meals. The small rituals of shared life.

Camille came on weekends. She had her own pair of gloves now. She helped Margot deadhead the roses. She was learning the names.

"They are not just plants," Margot told her. "They are stories."

"Tell me a story about a rose."

And Margot did. She told her about Morning Promise. About Heart's Fire. About Stubborn Hope.

Camille listened. She remembered.

Laurent watched them together. His daughter and the woman he was falling in love with. The garden was full of laughter.

He had not known this could exist. A life like this.

But here it was. Growing. Blooming.

Like the roses.

Chapter 49: The Last Bloom

In September, the weather turned cool.

Margot was in the garden, preparing the roses for autumn. She was pruning. She was mulching. She was saying goodbye to the summer blooms.

She reached the back row.

And stopped.

The Last Rose of Summer had produced a second bloom.

It was even more beautiful than the first. The pink was deeper. The scent was stronger. The petals were arranged with impossible precision.

She knelt. She touched it.

"How?" she whispered.

The rose did not answer. But she knew.

It was not a last rose after all. It was a first rose. Of a new season. Of a new life.

She cut it carefully. She carried it to the cemetery.

She placed it on Étienne's grave.

"I am all right," she told him. "I am going to be all right."

She stood there for a long time.

Then she walked home.

Laurent was waiting at the gate.

"You were gone a while."

"I had to say goodbye."

He did not ask to whom. He knew.

He took her hand.

Together, they walked into the garden.

Chapter 50: The Eternal Summer

One year later, the garden was full of roses.

New ones. Old ones. And one that had never existed before.

It grew in the center of the back row. A hybrid — half Margot's pink, half Laurent's crimson. The color was somewhere between, a deep blush that caught the light. The scent was honey and lavender and tea.

They had not named it yet.

"Any ideas?" Laurent asked.

They were standing in the garden. Camille was running between the rows. Madame Fontaine was coming for tea. Celeste was bringing a cake.

Margot looked at the new rose. It was beautiful.

"Eternal Summer," she said.

Laurent repeated it. "Eternal Summer."

He looked at her.

"It suits it."

"It suits us."

Camille ran over. "What are you naming it?"

"Eternal Summer."

"That is a good name." She looked at the rose. "It is pretty. Like you."

Margot laughed. "Like me?"

"Prettier than Papa, anyway."

"Hey."

Camille giggled. She ran back into the garden.

Margot took Laurent's hand.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For coming to my gate that day. For being my rival. For staying."

He squeezed her hand.

"Thank you for the garlic tincture."

She laughed. "That was the first thing you said to me."

"No. The first thing I said was a complaint."

"Close enough."

Madame Fontaine arrived. Celeste arrived with the cake. They sat on the terrace. The roses bloomed.

The last rose of summer had become the first rose of spring.

And the garden was full of life.
