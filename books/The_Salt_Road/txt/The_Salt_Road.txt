# The Salt Road

Chapter 1: The Bus

The bus from Ankara arrived at noon, forty minutes late, trailing a plume of dust that hung in the still air long after the engine had died. Derya stepped off and stood on the shoulder of the road, her bag at her feet, looking at a village that seemed to be holding its breath.

Yassıhöyük was smaller than she remembered. The main road was paved now — it had been gravel the last time she came, six years ago — but the pavement ended at the edge of the village and gave way to dirt. The houses were stone and whitewashed plaster, their roofs flat, their windows dark against the afternoon sun. A dog slept in the shade of a mulberry tree. There was no one on the street.

She had written to tell her grandfather she was coming. She had not received a reply. There was no one to meet her.

She picked up her bag and walked. The tea house was still at the corner of the main square. Hatice was sweeping the doorstep. She looked up, and her face broke into a smile.

"Look at you," she said. "A city girl."

"I told him I was coming."

"He knows." Hatice leaned the broom against the wall. "He's at the house. He's been sitting on the porch since sunrise."

Derya looked toward the house — the green door, the tin roof, the fig tree in the yard. It looked exactly as it had in her childhood.

"He didn't come to meet me."

"No," Hatice said. "He wouldn't."

Derya picked up her bag and walked toward the green door. The dust rose around her feet. The air smelled of hay and earth and something else — something dry and ancient that she had forgotten until this moment.

The smell of home.

Chapter 2: The House

Her grandfather was sitting on the porch when she arrived. He did not stand. He looked at her from under the brim of a cap that had once been brown and was now the color of the dust on the road.

"You came," he said.

"Yes."

He nodded. "I made coffee."

He stood slowly, his hand finding the doorframe for support. He was smaller than she remembered. Age had folded him into himself, narrowing his shoulders, slowing his steps. But his hands were the same — broad, stained white at the creases, the hands of a man who had spent a lifetime handling salt.

She followed him inside. The house was unchanged. The same wooden divan against the wall. The same copper coffee pot on the stove. The same photograph of her grandmother Emine on the wall, young and dark-haired, her face serious, as if she knew she would not stay long.

"You still have it," Derya said, meaning the photograph.

"I still have everything." He set the coffee pot on the stove. "I don't throw things away."

She sat on the divan. The cushions were the same ones she remembered — faded floral print, the stuffing lumpy from years of use. The room smelled of coffee and kerosene and her grandfather's tobacco.

"You got my letter?"

"My neighbor read it to me." He did not turn around. "I don't see as well as I used to."

"I'm sorry. I should have written sooner."

He did not answer. The coffee began to boil. He poured it into two small cups, the foam rising to the top, and handed one to her.

"Drink," he said. "Then we'll talk."

She drank. The coffee was thick and sweet, the way he had always made it. She sat in her grandmother's house and listened to the clock tick on the wall, and she waited.

Chapter 3: The Salt Shed

The salt shed was behind the house, a small stone building with a wooden door held shut by a leather strap. Derya had not thought about it in years. But on her second morning, she woke early and walked around the back, and she found the door hanging open.

Inside was empty.

She stood in the doorway, looking at the bare shelves, the swept floor, the hook where the scales had hung. The salt was gone. The shed had held salt for as long as she could remember — sacks of it, coarse and fine, white and pink and gray, salt from the mine at Tuzköy that her grandfather had carried across the valley for half a century.

She found him at the breakfast table, dipping bread into a bowl of olive oil.

"The shed is empty," she said.

"I know."

"The mine closed?"

"In the spring." He dipped his bread. "They said it was no longer profitable. The salt was too deep. The cost of extraction was too high. They sealed the entrance."

"And your salt? The salt you were going to deliver?"

He set down the bread. "There is no more salt to deliver. The last of it went to Gülseren in Kayalıkdere in March. I have been sitting here since."

She sat across from him. "What will you do?"

He looked at her. His eyes were the color of the earth after rain — dark, patient, holding more than they showed.

"I don't know," he said. "I've never known anything but the road."

Chapter 4: The Offer

The tea house was a single room with four tables, a wood stove, and a window that looked onto the square. Hatice ran it alone, serving çay and coffee and the same soggy toast she had served for twenty years. The place was empty when Derya walked in.

"The men are at the dam," Hatice said. "They're not coming back."

"The dam?"

"They offered them work. Construction. Good money." She wiped a glass with a cloth. "They'll be gone by winter. All of them. Then the water comes."

Derya sat at the window. "My grandfather doesn't know what to do."

"He knows." Hatice set a glass of tea in front of her. "He's been knowing for two years. He just hasn't said it."

"What does he know?"

Hatice sat across from her. In the light from the window, her face was lined and kind, her hair threaded with gray. She had been in love with Mükerrem for forty years. Everyone in the village knew it. Everyone except Mükerrem.

"He knows the road is ending," Hatice said. "The mine closing was just the last nail. He's been waiting for something to walk toward. And now you're here."

"I'm not a reason to walk."

Hatice looked at her. "Aren't you?"

She stood and went back to the counter. Derya sat with her tea, watching the empty square, feeling the weight of something she did not yet understand.

Chapter 5: The Map

Mükerrem unfolded the map on the kitchen table.

It was hand-drawn on yellow paper, the ink faded to brown, the edges soft from decades of folding and unfolding. The route ran south from Yassıhöyük, following the valley, crossing the Kızılırmak at the old stone bridge, passing through seven villages, ending at the salt market in Kayseri. Nearly a hundred miles.

"My father drew this in 1962," Mükerrem said. "His father drew it before him. The villages change — some appear, some disappear — but the road stays the same."

Derya leaned over the map. The names were written in careful block letters, the script old-fashioned, the spelling different from the modern maps she knew.

"You walked this every year?"

"Twice a year." He traced the route with his finger. "Spring and autumn. The salt was heavy, so we traveled slow. Eight days one way. Two days in Kayseri. Eight days back."

"Alone?"

"Sometimes with my father. Sometimes with Yusuf — the beekeeper at Kayalıkdere. Sometimes alone." He folded the map. "It takes ten days now. I'm slower."

"What will you do with it?" She meant the map.

He looked at her. His eyes were the same color as the river in winter.

"I thought you might want it," he said. "After I'm gone."

She did not know what to say. She looked at the map, at the faded ink, at the names of villages that would soon be underwater.

"I'm not gone yet," he said. "Will you walk it with me? One last time?"

He said it flatly, as if asking her to pass the bread.

Chapter 6: The Invitation

"I don't know how to walk that far," Derya said.

"Neither do I." Mükerrem poured himself another glass of tea. "But I've done it before, and I can do it again."

"Why now? Why this time?"

He was quiet for a moment. The clock ticked. A fly buzzed against the window.

"Because if I don't go now," he said, "I will never go. And if I never go, then the road ends with me sitting in this kitchen, waiting for water that will never come."

He said it simply, without self-pity, and Derya understood that he was not asking her to walk a hundred miles. He was asking her to witness something.

"Let me think about it," she said.

He nodded. "The bus to Ankara leaves at six in the evening. If you decide not to come, you can be home by midnight."

She left the house and walked to the edge of the village. The valley spread before her, golden and hazy, the river a silver thread in the distance. She could see the dam — a white scar across the horizon. She could see the minarets of villages that would soon be islands.

She thought about Istanbul. Her small apartment. The job search. The endless noise.

She thought about her grandfather, sitting alone in a house that no longer had salt.

She did not go to the bus station.

Chapter 7: The Excavation

The archaeological dig was on the eastern edge of the valley, a grid of trenches cut into the earth, shaded by tarps that snapped in the dry wind. Zeynep was kneeling in a trench, brushing dirt from a pottery shard with the careful attention of a surgeon.

Derya had met her at breakfast in the tea house. Zeynep was thirty, her face sunburned, her hands calloused. She wore a wide-brimmed hat and a vest with too many pockets.

"Welcome to the last season of the Yassıhöyük dig," she said, standing and stretching her back. "Next year, this will all be a lake."

"How much time do you have?"

"Until November. Then the dam gates close. After that, the valley fills." She held up the shard. "This is Hittite. Bronze Age. We found a seal yesterday — a bird and a sun. Four thousand years old."

She led Derya to a table where the seal lay in a plastic box. It was small, smaller than a thumbprint, carved from dark stone. The bird was simple — a few lines that suggested wings, a beak, flight.

"Someone wore this on a cord around their neck," Zeynep said. "Four thousand years ago. In this valley."

Derya touched it, gently, with the tip of her finger. She felt something — not heat, not cold, but a kind of pressure, as if the object was still full of the time it had waited through.

"What will happen to it?"

"It will go to the museum in Ankara. The valley will be underwater, but the things we find will live on." She looked at the seal. "That's the best we can do. Save what we can."

Derya walked back to the village. The seal stayed in her mind — the bird, the sun, the hand that had carved it four thousand years ago.

Chapter 8: The Decision

"I'll go," Derya said.

They were sitting on the porch. The sun was low, the shadows long. Mükerrem was smoking a cigarette, the smoke rising thin and blue in the still air.

He did not look at her. "You're sure?"

"I'm sure."

"Do you know how to walk?"

"I know how to walk."

"Walking on a road and walking across a valley are different things. The road will forgive your mistakes. The valley will not."

"I understand."

He nodded slowly. He stubbed out his cigarette and stood, his knees cracking.

"Then we leave at dawn," he said. "Get some sleep."

He went inside. Derya stayed on the porch, watching the sun set over the valley. The sky turned orange, then pink, then a deep purple that seemed to come from inside the earth. The minarets in the distance caught the last light and held it, glowing like candles.

She thought of the seal. The bird. The hand that had carved it.

She thought of her grandfather, folding the map for the last time.

She did not sleep well. But she did not change her mind.

Chapter 9: The Preparations

They packed at first light.

Mükerrem laid everything on the kitchen table: dried bread, dried apricots, a block of cheese wrapped in cloth, a bag of coarse salt, a copper teapot, two tin cups, blankets, matches, a knife. He tied the bundle with rope, the same way his father had tied it, the same way his grandfather had taught his father.

Derya packed her own bag: a change of clothes, a water bottle, a notebook, her camera. Mükerrem looked at her bag and said: "Too much."

"I need my camera."

"You need water. Put the camera in the middle of the bundle. It will break less."

She repacked her bag. He watched her without comment.

"Your shoes," he said.

"What about them?"

He pointed at her feet. "Those will not last three days. You need proper walking shoes."

He brought her a pair of leather sandals, the soles worn smooth, the straps soft from years of use.

"These were your grandmother's," he said.

She took them. The leather was warm in her hands. She put them on. They fit.

"Good," he said. "Now we are ready."

Chapter 10: The Leaving

They left before the sun cleared the horizon.

The village was still asleep. The tea house was dark. The dog under the mulberry tree opened one eye and closed it again. The air was cool and smelled of earth and thyme.

Derya walked beside her grandfather. He moved slowly, his walking stick finding the ground ahead of him, his bundle on his back. She matched his pace. The road was not a road — it was a path worn into the earth by centuries of feet and hooves, barely visible in places, marked by stones that had been placed by someone long dead.

They did not speak. The sun rose behind them, throwing their shadows across the path. The valley opened before them, golden and hazy, the river a silver thread in the distance.

Derya looked back once. Yassıhöyük was already small, a cluster of white shapes against the brown earth. She could see the fig tree in her grandfather's yard. She could see the green door.

She turned and walked on, into the valley, into the morning, into the road that was waiting for them.

Chapter 11: The First Miles

The road followed the valley south, keeping to high ground above the river. The earth was dry and cracked, the grass yellow, the stones worn smooth by the feet of those who had passed before. Mükerrem walked with his eyes on the ground, his stick tapping a steady rhythm.

Derya walked behind him. She had imagined conversation, stories, the slow unfolding of a lifetime of silence. But the road demanded something else. It demanded attention. The path was uneven, the stones loose, the dry earth crumbling underfoot. She had to watch where she placed her feet.

After an hour, her calves began to ache. After two hours, her shoulders. She did not complain.

At midday, Mükerrem stopped in the shade of a rock outcropping. He unrolled the bundle and handed her a piece of bread and a handful of dried apricots.

"Eat," he said. "You will need the strength."

She ate. The bread was dry, the apricots sweet. She drank from her water bottle. He drank from his, a sip, no more.

"How far have we walked?" she asked.

"Eight miles. Maybe nine."

"Nine miles. In four hours."

"On a good day." He wrapped the bread. "We will go slower in the afternoon. The heat is worse."

She looked at the path ahead. It stretched across the valley, faint and wavering, disappearing into the haze.

"Who made this road?" she asked.

He looked at her. "No one made it. It made itself. People walked from one place to another, and after enough people walked, there was a road. That is all a road is — a place that has been walked on."

He stood and picked up his bundle.

"Come. We have far to go."

Chapter 12: The Mine

They reached Tuzköy in the late afternoon.

The village was smaller than Yassıhöyük, a cluster of houses at the base of a low hill. The salt mine was on the hillside — a dark opening in the white rock, boarded up with fresh lumber, a sign in Turkish and English: DANGER. NO ENTRY. MINE CLOSED.

Mükerrem stopped at the entrance. He stood before the boards, his hand resting on the wood, his head bowed.

Derya stood beside him. She did not speak.

"The first time I came here," he said, "I was seven years old. My father brought me. We walked three days from Yassıhöyük. I remember the darkness of the mine. I remember the salt — white and pink and gray, piled in sacks, glittering in the lantern light. I thought it was the most beautiful thing I had ever seen."

His hand moved across the wood.

"Now it is closed. The salt is too deep. They say it costs more to extract than the salt is worth." He turned away. "I do not know what a thing is worth anymore."

He started walking. Derya followed.

She looked back at the boarded entrance. She tried to imagine it full of light and salt and her grandfather as a boy, seeing beauty for the first time. She could not. The mine was just a hole in a hill, sealed with fresh lumber.

But the salt was still inside. She could taste it in the air.

Chapter 13: The Beekeeper

They reached Kayalıkdere on the third morning.

The village was built into the side of a hill, the houses stepping down toward the river. A man was working among white boxes in a terraced garden, smoke rising from a metal smoker. He straightened when he saw them and waved.

"Mükerrem!" he called. "You came."

Yusuf was fifty-five, with a gray beard and the sunburned face of a man who worked outdoors. His hands were swollen from bee stings, the knuckles thick. He shook Mükerrem's hand and then Derya's.

"Your granddaughter?"

"Yes."

"I see your son's eyes." He smiled. "Welcome."

He led them to the garden. The hives were stacked in rows, their entrances busy with bees. Yusuf lifted the lid of one and showed them the frames — golden with honey, the cells capped and sealed.

"There are twenty hives in this valley. But they will be under water next year. I am moving them, one by one, to higher ground." He closed the lid. "The bees do not understand. They keep returning to the old places. I have to catch them and bring them to the new hives."

"How many have you moved?" Derya asked.

"Sixteen. Four left." He smiled. "Every day, I argue with bees. Every day, they win."

Derya helped him seal a hive for transport. The work was delicate — smoke to calm the bees, tape to secure the frames, a canvas strap to hold the box together. She was stung twice. She did not complain.

Yusuf gave her a jar of honey before they left. "For the road," he said.

She tucked it into her bundle.

Chapter 14: The Village

Kayalıkdere was half-empty.

The school was closed, the windows boarded. The mosque was locked, the padlock rusted. The tea house was a room in someone's house, with a plastic table and mismatched chairs. An old woman named Gülseren served them tea.

Mükerrem opened his bundle and took out a small cloth sack. He placed it on the table.

"The last salt," he said.

Gülseren opened the sack and looked at the white crystals inside. Her hands were thin, the veins visible through the skin. She closed the sack and held it against her chest.

"Thank you," she said.

"I have no one else to give it to."

She nodded. She went to the kitchen and came back with a jar of honey — darker than Yusuf's, almost amber.

"For the road," she said.

They drank their tea. The room was quiet. Derya watched her grandfather and the old woman sit in a silence that seemed to contain everything they had ever said to each other.

As they were leaving, Gülseren took Derya's hand.

"Your grandmother," she said. "Did anyone tell you how she died?"

"No," Derya said. "My mother never talks about it."

"She fell ill. A fever. Nothing the doctor could name. Your grandfather carried her on his back for twenty miles to reach the hospital. He walked through the night. She died in his arms in the waiting room." She paused. "He never told you?"

"No."

"He would not. He carries everything."

She let go of Derya's hand and went back into the house.

Chapter 15: The Story

Derya did not ask her grandfather about it. Not then. She walked beside him in silence, the honey jar heavy in her bundle, the story pressing against her chest.

She thought about her grandmother Emine. She had only vague memories — a warm hand, a voice singing, the smell of bread. She had been six when Emine died. She had not been told the details.

Now she knew. Twenty miles. Through the night. In her grandfather's arms.

She looked at him as he walked. The same hands that had carried her grandmother. The same back that had bent under the weight of salt and grief.

She wanted to say something. But she did not know what.

They walked until sunset. They made camp under a rock overhang. Mükerrem built a small fire, the smoke rising thin and straight in the still air. He made tea.

Derya sat across the fire, watching her grandfather's hands in the firelight.

"The hospital at Kayseri," she said. "The night Grandmother died."

He did not look up. His hands stopped moving.

"Gülseren told you."

"Yes."

He was quiet for a long time. The fire crackled. The stars were coming out, one by one.

"I could have taken a cart," he said. "But the road was bad that year. The rains had washed it out. A cart would have been slower than walking." He stirred the fire. "I walked as fast as I could. It was not fast enough."

"It wasn't your fault."

"I know." He looked at her across the fire. "But I have never forgiven myself anyway."

The fire crackled. The night deepened. They sat together in the dark, two people learning each other through the stories they had never told.

Chapter 16: The Crossing

Day three. The Kızılırmak.

They reached the river at midday. The old stone bridge was still standing — six arches of cut stone, worn smooth by centuries of water and wind. The river below was low, the water brown and sluggish. The dam upstream had already begun to slow it.

Mükerrem stopped in the middle of the bridge. He leaned on the stone parapet and looked down at the water.

"When I was a boy," he said, "this river was deep enough to swim in. We used to jump from this bridge. The water was clear. You could see the bottom."

"And now?"

"Now it is dying." He touched the stone. "The dam is killing it. They say the river will still flow, but it will be a different river. A controlled river. A river that has forgotten how to flood."

Derya stood beside him. She looked down at the brown water, the exposed banks, the trash caught in the branches of a half-submerged tree.

"How do you say goodbye to a river?"

"I don't know," he said. "I have never said goodbye to anything."

He pushed off from the parapet and continued across the bridge. Derya followed. The stone was warm under her feet.

Chapter 17: The Photograph

She asked him to stop on the bridge.

"Let me take your picture," she said.

He looked at her as if she had suggested something absurd. "Why?"

"Because I want to remember this."

He did not smile. But he did not refuse. He stood where he was, leaning on his stick, the river behind him, the white stone of the bridge under his feet. He looked at her with the same expression he wore for everything — watchful, patient, expecting nothing.

She raised the camera and pressed the shutter. The sound was small and final.

"One more," she said.

He did not change his expression. She pressed the shutter again.

"Enough," he said.

"One more. Smile."

"I do not smile for photographs."

"Please."

He did not smile. But something in his face shifted — a softening around the eyes, the barest suggestion of warmth.

She took the photograph. It was not a smile. But it was the closest thing she had ever seen.

She lowered the camera. They continued across the bridge, and she did not take another photograph of him for the rest of the journey.

Chapter 18: The Cave

The cave was hidden behind a fold in the rock face, invisible from the path. Mükerrem pushed aside a curtain of dry brush and revealed the entrance.

"I have been using this cave for forty years," he said.

Inside, the air was cool. A fire ring of blackened stones stood in the center, ash piled in the middle. A stack of dry wood was arranged against the wall. A tin of matches, wrapped in oilcloth, sat on a ledge.

Derya looked at the walls. Marks covered the stone — names, dates, symbols scratched into the surface. A bird. A sun. A hand.

"Who made these?"

"Everyone who came before." He knelt and began to arrange the fire. "Every man who traveled this road left his mark. So the next man would know he was not alone."

She walked along the wall, reading the names. Some were in Ottoman script, old and flowing. Some were in modern Turkish. Some were in languages she did not recognize.

"There's no name here I know," she said.

"They were here before me. They will be here after." He struck a match. "When I am gone, someone else will add their name. And the cave will keep them all."

The flame caught. The light filled the cave. The marks on the wall seemed to move in the firelight.

Derya sat across from her grandfather. The cave held them in its silence.

Chapter 19: The Storm

The storm came on their fifth day.

It began as a change in the light — the sky turning from white to gray to the color of a bruise. The wind picked up, carrying the smell of rain. Mükerrem looked at the sky and said: "We need to find shelter."

They found a rock overhang, a shallow depression in the cliff face. They sat with their backs against the stone as the rain began — first a few heavy drops, then a curtain of water that obscured the valley.

The thunder was close, cracking directly overhead. Derya flinched. Mükerrem did not.

"In 1973," he said, "the rain did not come."

She looked at him. "What?"

"The rains failed. The crops died. The river dropped so low you could walk across it without getting your ankles wet." He stared at the rain. "The salt road was the only thing that kept the valley alive that year. Men carried salt to the markets and brought back wheat. They walked day and night. No one rested."

"How do you know?"

"I was twelve years old. I walked with my father. I carried salt on my back."

The rain continued. The water pooled around them, running in rivulets down the rock face.

"Seventy-two years," he said. "I have seen this valley in flood and in drought. I have seen it green and brown and covered in snow. I have never seen it empty. And now I will."

He said it without self-pity. Just a fact, delivered to the rain.

The storm passed. The sun came out. The valley steamed.

They continued walking.

Chapter 20: The Silence

They walked without speaking for an afternoon.

It was not an uncomfortable silence. It was the silence of walking, of breathing, of placing one foot after another. The path required attention, and the attention made words feel unnecessary.

Derya found herself noticing things she would not have noticed if they had been talking. The way the light changed as the clouds moved. The call of a bird she could not name. The pattern of cracks in the dry earth. The white dust on her grandfather's hands.

She realized, with a kind of surprise, that she had never been silent with anyone before. There had always been something — music, conversation, the hum of the city. Even in her apartment, alone, there was the noise of traffic, neighbors, the refrigerator.

Here there was nothing. Just the wind, the stones, the sound of their feet on the path.

She thought about her mother. They had not spoken in two years. The silence between them was not like this. That silence was full of anger and grief and things unsaid.

This silence was different. It was not empty. It was full of something she could not name.

Her grandfather stopped. He pointed at a bird circling above the valley.

"Kartal," he said. "Eagle."

They watched it circle. Then they walked on.

Chapter 21: The Market

They reached a town on the sixth morning. It was not on the map — or rather, it was, but under a different name, a name that Derya could not find in the faded ink. The market was held in the square, a cluster of tables and tarps, men drinking tea, women selling vegetables and eggs and cloth.

Mükerrem found a place near the well. He opened his bundle and took out the bag of salt. He poured it into small piles on a clean cloth.

Derya watched as people came to him. An old man bought salt for his bread. A woman bought salt for preserving. A butcher bought a handful for his meat. Mükerrem did not bargain. He took what they offered — coins, eggs, a handful of dried mint.

"Why don't you bargain?" Derya asked.

"It is salt. Everyone needs it. You do not bargain for something everyone needs."

A young woman approached. She held out her hand, coins in her palm. Mükerrem gave her salt. She gave him the coins. He looked at her.

"Your grandmother used to buy salt from my father," he said.

The young woman nodded. "She died last winter."

"I am sorry."

"She always said your father had the best salt."

Mükerrem said nothing. He gave her more salt — twice what she had paid for.

She thanked him and walked away. Derya watched her go.

"You gave her extra," she said.

"Her grandmother was kind to my father." He folded the cloth. "The road remembers."

Chapter 22: The Mosque

The minaret appeared on the horizon at midday.

It was the only thing visible — a slender white tower rising from what looked like an empty field. As they drew closer, Derya saw the outline of walls, foundations, the rectangular imprint of a building that was no longer there.

"The village of Emirler," Mükerrem said. "It was here for six hundred years. Now it is gone."

They stopped at the edge of the empty site. The houses had been demolished, the debris cleared. The land was bare, marked only by the stone foundations and the single minaret.

"They moved the people to a new village on higher ground," he said. "They gave them new houses. Concrete. Running water. Everything modern."

"But the old village was destroyed."

"They said it was necessary. The reservoir will cover this whole valley. They could not leave buildings for the water to collapse on."

Derya walked to the minaret. The door was bricked up. She touched the stone. It was warm from the sun.

"What will happen to the minaret?"

"They will demolish it when the water comes. They do not want it falling into the reservoir."

They stood in silence, looking at the empty village, the single tower, the water that would soon cover everything.

Mükerrem said: "A man I knew lived here. His name was İsmail. He grew the best tomatoes in the valley. He used to say that a man who grows tomatoes does not need to travel. Everything he needs is in the soil."

"What happened to him?"

"He went to the new village. He planted tomatoes in a concrete planter on his balcony." He turned away. "They did not grow."

Chapter 23: The Archaeologist

Zeynep caught up with them at the next village.

She arrived on a motorcycle, dust rising behind her, a pack strapped to the back. She pulled up beside them and cut the engine.

"I found something," she said. "You need to see it."

She opened her pack and took out a tablet. It was clay, baked, the surface covered in cuneiform marks. She held it carefully, as if it might crumble in her hands.

"This is from the Old Assyrian period. About four thousand years ago. It's a trade record." She pointed at a section of the writing. "It mentions a salt merchant. From this valley."

Mükerrem looked at the tablet. He did not touch it.

"A salt merchant," he said. "From here."

"From this valley. The name is different — we can't read it fully — but the route is the same. The road you walk today is the road he walked four thousand years ago."

She held it out to him. He hesitated. Then he reached out and took it.

His hands — stained white at the creases, cracked from years of salt and sun — held the clay tablet. The marks on the surface had been pressed into the clay by a man who lived four thousand years ago. A man who carried salt, like him.

He held it for a long time. Then he gave it back.

"Thank you," he said.

Zeynep looked at him. She looked at Derya. She put the tablet back in her pack.

"I'll write a paper about it," she said. "I'll mention the valley. I'll mention the road."

She started her motorcycle. "The next village has a well that still works. You should fill your bottles there."

She drove away. The dust settled.

Mükerrem stood looking at the road ahead. His hands were empty now. But they still remembered the weight of the tablet.

Chapter 24: The Teacher

Adil was sitting by the well when they arrived.

He was forty-five, with a thin face and glasses held together with tape. He wore a jacket that was too warm for the weather and carried a notebook that was falling apart.

"Mükerrem," he said, standing. "I thought I would find you on the road."

"Adil." Mükerrem shook his hand. "You should not have walked so far."

"I wanted to see you before the water comes." He turned to Derya. "You must be Derya. I taught your mother. She was the best student I ever had."

"My mother?"

"Mathematics. She understood it the way other people understand music." He smiled. "She never thanked me."

"She doesn't talk about this place."

"No. She left." He opened his notebook. "I am compiling a dictionary. The local dialect. Not many people speak it anymore. In twenty years, no one will."

He showed them the pages — words and definitions, written in his careful hand. Some he had marked with a star.

"These are the words that will disappear first," he said. "Words for things that no longer exist. The old names for the wind. The word for the sound a well makes when it is dry. The word for the light at a certain time of day, when the valley turns gold and you can almost see the Hittite roads."

He closed the notebook. "I have collected eight hundred words so far. I will collect more until the water comes."

"Then what?" Derya asked.

He looked at her. "Then I will keep the words. That is all I can do."

Chapter 25: The Gift

Mükerrem gave her the map in the evening.

They had stopped for the night in the ruins of an old caravanserai. The roof was gone, but the walls were still standing, and the stone floor was clean. Mükerrem built a fire in the central hearth, where travelers had built fires for a thousand years.

He unfolded the map. The paper was soft in his hands, the folds nearly worn through.

"This is yours now," he said.

She looked at him. "I can't take this."

"It is meant for you. I have carried it for sixty years. Now it is your turn."

"But I won't use it. I don't walk this road."

"Not now. But someday you will need to remember. When you want to find your way back, this map will show you."

She took it. The paper was warm from his hands. She folded it carefully and slipped it into her bag.

"Thank you," she said.

He nodded. He stared into the fire.

"I never knew how to be anything other than a salt merchant," he said. "I did not know how to be a father. I did not know how to be a husband after your grandmother died. I only knew the road."

Derya did not say anything. She sat beside him and watched the fire.

"I want you to know," he said, "that I am proud of you. I do not say it enough. I do not say it at all. But I am."

The fire crackled. The stars came out.

She took his hand. They sat together in the ruins of the caravanserai, saying nothing.

Chapter 26: The Funeral

They came upon the funeral on the seventh day.

The village was called Akçakaya. Half of it was already underwater. The graveyard on the hill was being relocated — families digging up the graves of their ancestors to move them to higher ground before the water reached them.

A family was gathered around an open grave. Two men stood in the hole, carefully lifting a wooden coffin that had rotted through. A woman was crying silently, a handkerchief pressed to her mouth.

Mükerrem stopped at the edge of the graveyard.

"We should help," he said.

He walked over to the grave. He spoke to the men in a low voice. They handed him a shovel.

Derya watched from the edge. She did not know what to do. Her grandfather was in the grave now, helping lift the remains of a stranger.

She walked over and stood beside the crying woman. She did not speak. She simply stood there.

After an hour, the bones had been transferred to a new coffin. The family would take them to the new cemetery, on high ground, where the water would not reach.

Mükerrem climbed out of the grave. He was covered in earth. He did not brush it off.

The woman took his hand. "Thank you," she said.

"It is what we do," he said.

They walked away. Derya looked back at the graveyard. Half of it was already empty. The other half would be moved before the water came.

She did not know what she felt. But she knew she would not forget the sight of her grandfather in the grave, helping a stranger carry her dead to safety.

Chapter 27: The Night

They slept under the stars that night.

The sky was clear, the stars so bright they seemed close enough to touch. Derya lay on her back, her bundle under her head, looking up at the Milky Way. She had never seen it like this — a river of light across the black.

Mükerrem sat beside her, smoking.

"Did you sleep under the stars when you walked the road?" she asked.

"Always. In the summer, at least. In the winter, we slept in caves or in the villages."

"Were you ever afraid?"

He was quiet for a moment. "I was afraid of getting lost. Not of the dark. The dark is just the absence of light. Getting lost is something else. It is the absence of knowing where you are."

"How did you keep from getting lost?"

"I followed the road. And when I could not see the road, I followed the stars. And when I could not see the stars, I sat down and waited until I could."

He tapped his cigarette. "I lived by three rules. Rule one: the road will take you where you need to go. Rule two: if you cannot see the road, wait for the stars. Rule three: if you cannot see the stars, sit down and wait."

"What if the road is gone?"

He looked at her. The firelight played across his face.

"Then you make a new one."

Chapter 28: The Letter

Derya pulled out the notebook she had brought, the pages still blank except for the first one, where she had started a letter to her mother.

She had not mailed it. She was not sure she wanted to.

She read what she had written:

*Anne —*

*I am walking across the valley with Dede. The dam is coming. The village will be underwater by this time next year. He is saying goodbye to everything, and he asked me to come with him.*

*I did not know how to say no. But I also did not want to say yes. I said yes because I saw his hands.*

*I am learning things about him that I never knew. Did you know he carried Grandmother twenty miles to the hospital? Did you know he has been sleeping in caves and under the stars for sixty years? Did you know that he remembers the names of the villages that are already gone?*

*I don't know why I am writing this. I don't know if I will send it.*

*I just wanted someone to know that he is more than the silence we remember.*

She folded the letter and put it back in her notebook. She did not show it to her grandfather.

But that night, she dreamed of her mother, young and laughing, walking beside a river that no longer existed.

Chapter 29: The Spring

They found the spring on the eighth day.

It was marked on the map — a blue dot, the word *çeşme* written in fading ink. Mükerrem had been talking about it since they left Yassıhöyük. The clearest water in the valley, he said. The coldest. The sweetest.

They found a dry basin, cracked earth, a trickle of brown water that barely wet the stones.

He knelt beside it. He touched the cracked earth. His hand rested there, palm down, as if he could feel the water that used to flow.

"It was here," he said. "I drank from this spring when I was a boy. The water was so cold it hurt my teeth."

He stayed kneeling for a long time.

Derya knelt beside him. She did not know what to say.

"The dam," she said.

"The dam." He stood. His knees cracked. "The water table drops. The springs dry. The wells go brackish. Then the water comes and covers everything, and no one remembers what was beneath."

He picked up his bundle. He did not look back at the spring.

But as they walked away, Derya looked. The dry basin, the trickle of brown water, the stones that had known the feet of everyone who had ever traveled this road.

She thought: *This is what it means to lose something. Not to forget it. To watch it dry up while you are still alive.*

Chapter 30: The Halfway Point

Day six. The town of İncesu.

They had walked fifty miles. Derya's feet were blistered. Her legs ached. Her shoulders were raw where the straps of her bundle had rubbed. She sat on a rock beside the road and tried not to cry.

Mükerrem sat beside her. He did not ask if she was all right. He opened his bundle and took out bread and dried apricots and a piece of cheese.

"Eat," he said. "We still have far to go."

She took the bread. She chewed. She swallowed.

"How do you do this?" she asked. "At your age. How do you keep walking?"

He looked at her. "You do not think about the whole distance. You think about the next step. And the step after that. And after a while, you have walked a hundred miles without noticing."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only answer there is." He bit into his bread. "You put one foot in front of the other, and you do not stop, and after enough steps, you arrive."

She finished her bread. She stood up. Her legs protested.

"Let's go," she said.

He looked up at her. Something passed across his face — surprise, maybe, or respect.

"All right," he said. "Let's go."

They walked.

Chapter 31: The River People

They met the herders on the ninth morning.

A family was moving their goats across the valley, following a route that was older than any road. The old woman was walking ahead, a stick in her hand, her back straight despite the weight of the pack she carried. Her daughters followed with the goats. The children ran ahead.

Mükerrem spoke to the old woman. Their conversation was slow, halting — she spoke a dialect that he could barely follow, the words eroded by time and distance.

He introduced Derya. The old woman looked at her with sharp black eyes.

"She has your blood," she said to Mükerrem. "But she walks like a city girl."

"She is a city girl."

"Then she walks better than most."

The old woman reached into her pack and gave Derya a piece of goat cheese wrapped in a cloth.

"For the road," she said.

Derya took it. "Thank you."

"Where are you going?" the old woman asked.

"Kayseri. The salt market."

"The salt market is gone," the old woman said. "It is a shopping center now."

"I know."

"Then why are you walking?"

Derya looked at her grandfather. "Because he asked me to."

The old woman nodded. "That is a good reason."

She called to her goats and continued walking. The family moved across the valley, slow and steady, following a route that no map would ever show.

Chapter 32: The Trade

They stopped in a village where a sandal maker still worked.

His name was Mehmet. He was blind. His workshop was a single room with a workbench, a shelf of tools, and piles of leather in various stages of cutting and stitching. He sat on a low stool, his hands moving over the leather, measuring by touch.

Mükerrem's sandals had worn thin. He needed new ones.

Mehmet felt the old sandals. He ran his fingers along the sole, the straps, the stitching.

"I can make you a new pair," he said. "But it will take three hours."

"I can wait."

Mehmet chose a piece of goat leather, cut it by feel, and began to stitch. His hands worked without hesitation.

Derya watched him.

"After the water comes," she said, "what will you do?"

Mehmet did not stop working. "I will go to the new village. They have a house for me. A concrete house. They say I can make sandals there."

"But you will not have customers."

"No." He continued stitching. "But a man does not make sandals only for customers. He makes them because he has always made them."

He finished the first sandal and held it up. "It is the same for your grandfather. He does not carry salt because there is a market for it. He carries it because he is a salt carrier. You cannot stop being what you are."

Mehmet gave Mükerrem the new sandals. Mükerrem gave him salt — the last he had.

"A fair trade," Mehmet said.

"Always."

Chapter 33: The Well

The well was dry.

They had been told it was the last working well on this section of the road. But when they lifted the bucket, it came up empty. The rope was wet for the first three feet. Then dry.

Mükerrem lowered the bucket again. He let the rope run through his hands, feeling for the weight of water. Nothing.

"The water table has dropped," he said. "The dam is pulling the water down."

"What do we do?"

He stood. "There is a spring three miles east. We have to walk."

Three miles. East. Away from the road. Across open ground with no path.

They walked. The sun was high. The heat was intense. Derya's mouth was dry, her lips cracked. She had rationed her water, but it was almost gone.

She thought about giving up. Sitting down and waiting for the sun to pass.

But her grandfather kept walking. So she kept walking.

The spring was where he said it would be. A trickle of water from a crack in the rock, pooling in a basin of stone. They filled their bottles. They drank. They poured water over their heads.

"Thank you," she said.

He did not answer. But he nodded.

Chapter 34: The Argument

It happened on the tenth day.

They were walking through a landscape of dry grass and scattered stones, the road barely visible, the heat shimmering on the horizon. Derya was tired. Her feet hurt. The silence had become unbearable.

"Why did you never leave?" she asked.

He did not answer.

"Why did you stay in a village that was dying? Why did you keep walking a road that was disappearing? Why did you let the salt take everything?"

He stopped walking. He turned to face her.

"The salt," he said, "did not take anything. It gave. It gave me a life. It gave me purpose. It gave me a reason to wake up every morning."

"It gave you nothing else. You had a wife. You had a son. You had a granddaughter. And you chose the salt."

His face hardened. "You do not know what you are talking about."

"Then tell me."

He did not speak. He turned and walked ahead.

She followed. The silence between them was not the comfortable silence of the road. It was the silence of a door closing.

Chapter 35: The Apology

That night, he spoke first.

They were sitting by a small fire at the edge of a dried riverbed. The stars were out. The wind had died.

"I did not know how to be anything else," he said.

She looked at him.

"I know I was not a good father. I was not there. I was on the road. But I did not know how to be a father and a salt carrier at the same time. The road demanded everything."

She said nothing.

"I do not expect you to forgive me," he said. "I do not expect you to understand. But I want you to know that I tried."

She moved closer to him. She took his hand.

"I don't know how to forgive you," she said. "But I know how to walk beside you."

He looked at her. In the firelight, his eyes were wet.

"That is enough," he said.

They sat together, holding hands, the fire burning low. The road was still ahead of them. But for the first time, it felt like they were walking together.

Chapter 36: The Salt Pan

On the morning of the eleventh day, they reached the salt pan.

It was a white expanse that stretched to the horizon, cracked and glittering, the salt crusted on the surface like frozen waves. The heat shimmered above it, creating mirages of water that disappeared as they approached.

Mükerrem walked onto the salt pan without hesitation. His bare feet — he had taken off his sandals — pressed into the white surface. He walked to the center and stopped.

Derya watched from the edge. He looked small against the white. A figure in a vast emptiness.

She wanted to call out. She wanted to ask if he was all right.

But she did not. She understood that he was saying goodbye.

He stood in the center of the salt pan for a long time. The sun rose higher. The heat intensified. He did not move.

Then he turned and walked back. His feet were white with salt.

"Now I can go," he said.

He put his sandals on. They continued walking.

Chapter 37: The Memory

"The first time I walked the road, I was seven years old," he said.

They were walking side by side, the salt pan behind them, the road ahead.

"My grandfather brought me. He was old even then. He walked slowly. But he did not stop."

"What do you remember?"

"It was autumn. The grapes were being harvested. Every village we passed through, someone gave us grapes. I ate so many I got sick."

He smiled. A real smile. Derya had never seen him smile.

"I was afraid of the dark," he continued. "My grandfather gave me a piece of salt to hold. He said: 'As long as you hold this, you will find your way home.' I believed him. I held it all night."

"What happened to the salt?"

He touched his chest. "I still have it."

"You kept it?"

"For sixty-five years." He looked at her. "I am going to give it to you. When the journey is over."

Chapter 38: The Dam

They saw the dam on the eleventh day.

It was visible from miles away — a white wall rising across the valley, cranes and trucks moving around its base, men in hard hats working on the scaffolding. The river had already been diverted. The valley below the dam was dry.

Derya stopped when she saw it.

"It's so big," she said.

"It is."

"How long did they take to build it?"

"Seven years." He leaned on his stick. "Seven years to build. One year to fill. And a thousand years of history gone."

They stood looking at the dam. The sound of construction carried across the valley — the beep of trucks, the clang of metal, the distant hum of generators.

"Do you hate it?" Derya asked.

He was quiet for a moment.

"I hate what it will do. But I do not hate the dam. The dam is just concrete. It does not know what it is destroying."

"Then who do you hate?"

"Nobody. Everyone." He shook his head. "The world changes. The valley will become a lake. The villages will be underwater. The people will move to new houses. In a generation, no one will remember what was here."

"You will remember."

"Yes. But I am the last."

Chapter 39: The Village Below

They walked to the edge of the reservoir.

The water had already risen. A minaret stood in the middle of the lake, the water lapping at its base. Below the surface, Derya could see the shapes of houses — roofs, walls, the outline of a street.

She counted the houses she could see. Fifteen. Twenty. More than she could count.

"Which village is this?"

"Eskiköy. It was abandoned five years ago. The people were moved. The buildings were supposed to be demolished, but the water rose faster than expected."

She stared at the submerged houses. The water was clear enough to see the details — a door frame, a window, the shape of a minaret against the blue.

"It looks like it's sleeping," she said.

"It is not sleeping. It is dead."

He turned away. He walked back toward the road.

Derya stayed a moment longer, looking at the village below. She thought of the lives that had been lived there. The births. The deaths. The weddings. The arguments. The meals. The ordinary, invisible weight of a place that no longer existed.

She turned and followed her grandfather.

Chapter 40: The Offer

An official from the dam project found them at the next village.

He was young, clean-shaven, wearing a hard hat and a vest with reflective strips. He introduced himself as Mr. Kaya.

"Mükerrem Karadağ," he said. "I have been trying to reach you."

Mükerrem did not stop walking. "I have been on the road."

"I understand. But I need to discuss the compensation for your property in Yassıhöyük."

He opened a folder and took out papers.

"The government is offering compensation for all properties that will be affected by the reservoir. Your house. Your land. The outbuilding."

"I do not want compensation."

"Everyone is entitled to compensation. It is the law."

"The law." Mükerrem stopped. "The law says I have to leave my home. The law says the valley where I was born will be underwater. And now the law wants to give me money."

"It is not enough," Mr. Kaya said. "I know. But it is something."

Mükerrem looked at the papers. He did not take them.

"I will leave," he said, "when the water reaches my door. Not before."

He walked on.

Mr. Kaya looked at Derya. "Please. Talk to him."

Derya looked at the papers. Then she looked at her grandfather, walking away.

"I'll talk to him," she said. She did not take the papers.

She followed her grandfather down the road.

Chapter 41: The Last Village

The last village before Kayseri was called Çataltepe.

It was empty.

The houses were intact, but the doors were open. A dog lay on a porch, waiting for a family that had already left. A wind chime hung from a tree, ringing in the breeze.

Mükerrem walked through the empty streets. He looked at the open doors, the abandoned houses, the dog that waited.

"They left quickly," he said. "Some of them."

"How do you know?"

"The doors are open. If they had time, they would have closed them."

He stopped at a house with blue shutters. He looked at the garden, the fig tree, the child's bicycle abandoned in the yard.

"I knew the man who lived here. His name was Mustafa. He grew figs. Every year, he gave me a basket to take on the road."

"What happened to him?"

"He went to Kayseri. He lives in an apartment now. He told me the figs in Kayseri taste like paper."

He walked on.

They passed through the empty village in silence. The wind chime followed them, a thin music that seemed to come from everywhere at once.

Chapter 42: The Feast

An old friend of Mükerrem's insisted on hosting them for dinner.

His name was Hakkı. He lived in the last inhabited village before Kayseri, in a stone house with a garden full of tomato plants. He was eighty years old, with a mustache that yellowed from tobacco, and he laughed at everything.

"Look at you," he said when he saw Mükerrem. "You look like a ghost. Sit down. Eat."

The table was full: lamb, rice, yogurt, fresh bread, tomatoes from the garden, cucumbers, olives, cheese. Derya had not seen so much food in months.

They ate. Hakkı talked. He told stories about Mükerrem when they were young — the girls they had chased, the fights they had fought, the time they had gotten drunk on raki and fallen into the river.

"Your grandfather was a fool," Hakkı said, laughing. "A handsome fool."

"He's not handsome now," Derya said.

"He's old. We are all old." He refilled her glass. "But once, he was the handsomest man in the valley. Your grandmother thought so."

Mükerrem said nothing. But he was almost smiling.

Derya watched him. She had never seen him like this — relaxed, at ease, surrounded by stories that made him young again.

"This is the first real meal I've had in nine days," she said.

Hakkı laughed. "Your grandfather never could cook. I don't know how you survived."

"Salt," Mükerrem said. "Salt and bread. It is enough."

"Salt and bread is not enough." Hakkı pushed another piece of lamb toward Derya. "Eat. Tomorrow you will be back on the road, and then you will remember this meal."

Chapter 43: The Telling

After dinner, Hakkı brought out raki.

They sat in the garden, the stars overhead, the valley dark around them. Hakkı poured three glasses.

"To the road," he said.

"To the road," Mükerrem said.

Derya drank. The raki was strong, burning her throat. She coughed. Hakkı laughed.

"City girl," he said.

"City girl," she agreed.

Mükerrem told stories. The year the river froze solid. The year the wolves came down from the mountain and killed a man's sheep. The time he met an English archaeologist in 1968, a woman who told him the road was older than the Romans.

"She was the first person who told me that the road mattered," he said. "Before that, I thought it was just a road. A way to get from one place to another. But she said it was history. It was a record of everyone who had ever walked it."

"Where is she now?" Derya asked.

"I don't know. England, probably. Dead, maybe. But I have never forgotten what she said."

"What did she say?"

He looked at the stars. "She said that a road is not just a road. It is a story that people write with their feet."

Chapter 44: The Waking

Derya woke before dawn.

She slipped out of Hakkı's house and walked to the edge of the village. The sky was gray, lightening in the east. The valley lay below her, still and silent.

She stood at the edge and watched the sun rise.

The light came slowly, filling the valley like water. The minarets caught the first rays. The dry riverbed turned gold. The empty villages, the abandoned houses, the fig trees — all of it was illuminated, moment by moment, as the sun climbed over the horizon.

She realized she was crying.

She did not know why. She was not sad. She was not happy. She was something else — full, the way the valley was full of light, holding something she could not name.

She thought about Istanbul. Her apartment. The noise. The job applications. The feeling that she was waiting for something that would never come.

She did not want to go back.

She did not know what she wanted.

But standing at the edge of the valley, watching the sun rise, she felt something shift inside her. A door opening. A road appearing.

She wiped her face and went back inside.

Chapter 45: The Arrival

Day twelve. Kayseri.

They arrived in the afternoon. The city rose around them — buildings, traffic, noise, the smell of exhaust and cooking oil. Derya had been away from it for less than two weeks, but it felt foreign. The crowds seemed too dense. The noise seemed too loud.

The salt market was gone.

In its place stood a shopping center. Glass and steel, air conditioning, escalators. A food court. A cinema. A parking garage.

Mükerrem stood in the parking lot, holding his empty sack.

He stood there for a long time.

Derya stood beside him. She did not speak.

"It was here," he said finally. "There were wooden tables. Men selling salt from every mine in Anatolia. White salt, pink salt, gray salt. You could taste the difference. Every mine had its own flavor."

"Where did they go?"

"Nowhere. They died. The salt market did not close. It just stopped being a market."

He folded the empty sack and tucked it into his bundle.

"Let's go," he said.

He walked toward the bus station.

Chapter 46: The End

They waited for the bus in a café near the station.

Mükerrem ordered tea. Derya ordered coffee. They sat at a plastic table and watched the buses come and go.

"Was it enough?" Derya asked. "The journey?"

He looked at her. "It was the best journey I have ever taken."

"I thought you said every journey was the same."

"I lied." He stirred his tea. "Every journey is different. The road changes. The villages change. The people change. The only thing that stays the same is the walking."

"What will you do now?"

He was quiet. "I don't know. I have never been a man who knew what to do next. I have always just done what was in front of me."

"Maybe that's enough."

"Is it?"

She reached into her bag and pulled out the map. She unfolded it and laid it on the table.

"It's mine now," she said. "You gave it to me."

"I did."

"So I can give it back to you. When you need it."

He looked at the map. The faded ink. The names of villages that would soon be under water.

"You might need it before I do," he said.

"Maybe. But for now, you keep it."

She folded the map and pressed it into his hands.

He held it. His hands — stained white at the creases, cracked from a lifetime of salt — closed around the paper.

"Thank you," he said.

"Thank you for walking."

Chapter 47: The Return

The bus ride back took four hours.

Mükerrem sat by the window, watching the valley pass. The road they had walked ran parallel to the highway, visible in places as a faint line in the earth. Derya watched him watch it.

He fell asleep with his head against the window. His hands were folded in his lap. The map was pressed against his chest.

Derya looked at him. In sleep, his face was younger. The lines smoothed out. He looked like the grandfather she remembered from childhood — the man who had lifted her onto his shoulders and carried her through the fig trees.

She looked out the window at the valley. The bus passed a village she did not recognize. On a hill above it, a flock of sheep moved across a field, led by a woman in a white scarf.

She thought about what her grandfather had said: *A road is a story that people write with their feet.*

She wondered what story she was writing.

Chapter 48: The Question

The tea house was empty when they returned.

Hatice was wiping the counter. She looked up when they walked in. Her face did not change.

"You made it," she said.

"We made it."

"Sit. I'll make tea."

They sat at the window. The square was empty. The dog was still sleeping under the mulberry tree.

Hatice brought tea. She sat with them.

"Was it what you remembered?" she asked Mükerrem.

"It was different."

"It always is."

She looked at Derya. "And you? What did you learn?"

Derya looked at her grandfather. "I learned how to walk."

Hatice smiled. "That's a good thing to learn."

Derya looked at Hatice. The question had been forming in her mind since the journey began.

"Do you love him?" she asked.

Hatice's hands stopped moving. She looked at Derya. Then she looked at Mükerrem, who was staring out the window, not listening.

"Ask him," Hatice said. "He is the only one who does not know."

Chapter 49: The Evening

That evening, Mükerrem sat on his porch.

The sun was setting. The valley was golden. The minarets in the distance caught the last light. The fig tree was still.

Derya sat beside him.

They did not speak.

The light faded. The sky turned orange, then pink, then deep purple. The stars began to appear.

"You could stay," he said. "If you wanted."

"I know."

"But you won't."

"I don't know yet." She looked at the valley. "I don't know what I want to do."

"That is fine. You are young. You have time."

"Did you know? At my age?"

"No. I was lost. I walked the road without knowing why. It took me forty years to understand."

"Understand what?"

He was quiet.

"That the road was not about the salt. It was about the walking. The walking kept me alive."

They sat in the dark.

"I will come back," she said.

"When?"

"I don't know. But I will."

He nodded. He did not say anything.

But she saw his hands, resting on his knees. They were still.

Chapter 50: The Salt

The bus came at dawn.

Derya stood at the stop. Her bag was packed. Her grandmother's sandals were on her feet. The camera was in her bag. The map was in her hands — he had given it back to her that morning, pressing it into her palms without a word.

Mükerrem stood beside her.

"I have something for you," he said.

He reached into his pocket and pulled out a small cloth sack. He opened it and took out a piece of salt.

It was small. Irregular. The surface was smooth, worn by years of handling.

"This was my grandfather's," he said. "He gave it to me when I was seven. I have carried it every day since."

He pressed it into her hand.

"Now it is yours. As long as you hold it, you will find your way home."

She closed her hand around the salt. It was warm.

She looked at her grandfather. The old man. The salt merchant. The last of the road.

"I don't know what to say," she said.

"Then don't say anything."

The bus arrived. The door opened.

She hugged him. He stood still for a moment. Then his arms went around her, and he held her, and she felt how small he had become, how fragile, how much of him was still the seven-year-old boy who had been afraid of the dark.

She let go. She boarded the bus. She sat by the window.

He stood at the stop, his hands at his sides, watching.

The bus pulled away. The village shrank in the window. The green door. The fig tree. The empty salt shed. The valley opening behind it, golden and hazy, the river a silver thread in the distance.

She opened her hand.

The salt was there. Small. White. Warm.

The road was behind her.

But the salt was in her hand.

And that was enough.
