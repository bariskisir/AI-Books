# The Piano Tuner — Outline

**Genre:** Literary Gothic
**Setting:** Victorian Edinburgh, winter 1887
**Tone:** Quietly unsettling, atmospheric, literary restraint

---

## Premise

Mr. Croft, a blind piano tuner in Victorian Edinburgh, has built his reputation on an exquisite sensitivity to sound — a gift sharpened by his blindness. When Lady Ashworth summons him to her home on Royal Crescent, he finds a grand piano that plays on without a pianist. The music never stops. The player, her son William, is dead — three months buried — yet the instrument produces his compositions each night. As Croft returns week after week, he begins to understand that the piano is not haunted. It is listening. And it has something to tell him about the night William Ashworth died.

---

## Core Cast

- **Mr. Silas Croft** — A blind piano tuner, middle-aged, precise, solitary. His blindness has given him an almost supernatural ability to hear what others cannot. He lives in a small flat above a music shop on Candlemaker Row.

- **Lady Eleanor Ashworth** — A widow in mourning, late forties. She is composed, brittle, and watches her words carefully. She summoned Croft not because the piano needs tuning, but because she needs someone to hear it and tell her what it means.

- **William Ashworth (The Musician)** — Lady Ashworth's son, dead at twenty-seven by his own hand. A gifted composer who grew increasingly reclusive and erratic in his final year. His music, still playing, reveals the story he could not tell in life.

- **Dr. Marcus Finch** — The family physician, skeptical of the supernatural, rational to a fault. He tries to explain the piano through natural causes.

- **Mrs. Poole** — The Ashworth housekeeper, who hears the music every night and refuses to enter the music room after dark.

- **Inspector Jameson** — A retired policeman who investigated William's death and harbors doubts about the official verdict of suicide.

---

## 50 Chapter Plan

### ACT I: THE SUMMONS (Chapters 1–10)

1. **The Call** — Mr. Croft receives a letter from Lady Ashworth requesting his services for a piano that "will not cease its music."

2. **Through Candlemaker Row** — Croft walks the familiar streets of Edinburgh in winter fog, guided by sounds and memory.

3. **Royal Crescent** — Croft arrives at the Ashworth house. Mrs. Poole admits him. He hears the piano playing from an upstairs room.

4. **The Instrument** — Croft enters the music room. He touches the piano — a Broadwood grand — and feels its vibration. The keys move beneath his fingers.

5. **Lady Ashworth** — Lady Ashworth explains that her son William died three months ago. The piano began playing three weeks after his burial. It has not stopped since.

6. **The Melody** — Croft listens. The piece is melancholic, unfinished, repeating with subtle variations. Something in the harmony troubles him.

7. **William's Story** — Lady Ashworth tells Croft about her son: his genius, his isolation, the strange moods that overtook him in his final year.

8. **The First Tuning** — Croft begins his work. He finds the piano perfectly in tune — impossibly so, given the constant playing.

9. **A Wrong Note** — In the middle register, one note rings false. Not out of tune — wrong. As if the instrument remembers a different key.

10. **The House at Night** — Croft leaves as dusk falls. He pauses on the street. Through the window, he hears the piano still playing. The same phrase. Over and over.

### ACT II: THE INVESTIGATION (Chapters 11–20)

11. **A Return Visit** — Lady Ashworth summons Croft again. The piano has grown louder, more insistent.

12. **Mrs. Poole's Confession** — The housekeeper tells Croft that she saw William's shadow in the music room three nights ago. She is not the first to claim this.

13. **Dr. Finch's Skepticism** — The family physician examines the piano. He proposes a theory: the humidity, the warped soundboard, a mechanical memory in the action.

14. **The Second Tuning** — Croft works methodically. He discovers that the piano responds to his touch — the notes he tunes stay true, but the untouched ones drift sharp.

15. **A Sheet of Music** — Lady Ashworth gives Croft a page of William's handwriting. The notes are frantic, scattered. He has it read to him.

16. **The Inspector's Visit** — Inspector Jameson calls on Croft. He reveals that William's death was ruled suicide, but the window was locked from the inside and no note was found.

17. **The Hidden Correspondence** — Mrs. Poole reveals that William received letters at odd hours in the months before his death. She does not know who sent them.

18. **Listening at Midnight** — Croft returns to Royal Crescent at night, uninvited. He stands in the garden and listens. The music is different in the dark — darker, more complex.

19. **The Servant's Tale** — A former maid, now working elsewhere, tells Croft that William argued with someone the night he died. It was not his mother.

20. **A Name Whispered** — Croft hears, or believes he hears, a name in the music. A woman's name. "Catherine."

### ACT III: THE REVELATION (Chapters 21–30)

21. **Who Is Catherine?** — Croft asks Lady Ashworth about Catherine. Her face goes pale. She says there is no Catherine.

22. **The Attic Room** — Mrs. Poole leads Croft to a locked attic room. Inside: a portrait of a young woman, a writing desk, and letters tied with ribbon.

23. **The Letters** — Croft has the letters read. They are love letters — from William to a woman named Catherine. She never wrote back.

24. **Catherine's Identity** — Lady Ashworth confesses: Catherine was a maid in the house. William fell in love with her. Lady Ashworth dismissed her without references.

25. **The Piano Speaks** — Croft returns to the music room. He sits at the piano and plays a simple chord. The keys respond under his hands, playing a phrase he recognizes from the letters.

26. **A Threat Made** — Inspector Jameson has found a witness: a footman who heard William threaten to leave Edinburgh the night he died. He was angry, desperate.

27. **The Final Letter** — In Catherine's belongings, still in the attic, they find a letter she never sent. She was carrying William's child. She was leaving to protect him.

28. **The Night of Death** — Croft reconstructs the night: William learned of Catherine's dismissal and the pregnancy. He confronted his mother. Something broke in him.

29. **The Truth of the Piano** — Croft understands now: the piano is not haunted by William's ghost. It is playing the music William could not finish — the confession he owed Catherine.

30. **Lady Ashworth's Confession** — Lady Ashworth admits she sent Catherine away. She did not know about the child. She has lived with the music as her punishment.

### ACT IV: THE RESOLUTION (Chapters 31–40)

31. **A Change in the Music** — The piano's playing shifts. The repetition stops. A new melody emerges — softer, slower.

32. **Finding Catherine** — Croft and Inspector Jameson trace Catherine to a village outside Edinburgh. She is alive. The child did not survive the birth.

33. **Catherine's Story** — Catherine tells them her side: she loved William, but Lady Ashworth's cruelty drove her away. She never blamed William.

34. **The Last Movement** — Croft visits the piano one final time. As he plays, the instrument seems to play with him, completing the unfinished piece.

35. **Forgiveness** — Lady Ashworth writes to Catherine, asking for forgiveness. She receives no reply.

36. **The Piano Falls Silent** — For the first time in months, the piano stops playing. The house is quiet.

37. **A Final Tuning** — Croft tunes the piano one last time. It is just an instrument now. Beautiful, silent, ordinary.

38. **The Finished Piece** — Croft writes down the completed composition from memory, note for note.

39. **Departure** — Lady Ashworth decides to leave Royal Crescent. She cannot live in the silence.

40. **The Gift** — Lady Ashworth gives Croft the piano. He cannot accept it, but he takes the composition.

### ACT V: THE AFTERMATH (Chapters 41–50)

41. **A New Purpose** — Croft has the composition published as a private memorial under William Ashworth's name.

42. **Catherine's Return** — Catherine returns to Edinburgh. She visits the grave for the first time.

43. **Lady Ashworth's Farewell** — Lady Ashworth leaves Edinburgh. Before she goes, she gives Croft William's journal.

44. **William's Journal** — Croft has the journal read. It reveals William's slow unraveling — his obsession with a piece of music he could not finish, his love for Catherine, his despair at his mother's interference.

45. **The Unfinished Theme** — In the journal, William writes of a melody he heard in a dream. He could never capture it on paper. Croft realizes the piano was playing that melody.

46. **A Meeting in the Graveyard** — Croft and Catherine meet at William's grave. She tells him she has forgiven everyone. She has to, to survive.

47. **The Broadwood Sold** — Croft arranges the sale of the piano to a music college. It will be played by students who know nothing of its history.

48. **One Last Evening** — Croft sits in his flat on Candlemaker Row. He plays the finished composition on his own modest instrument. It feels complete.

49. **The Music Never Ends** — Croft realizes that the piece has lodged itself in his memory. He hears it in the rain, in the wind, in the clatter of wheels on cobblestones.

50. **Silence and Sound** — Months later. Croft receives a letter from Catherine. She is well. She has started a new life. And the piece — William's piece — has been performed at a small recital. Croft sits in the dark, listening to the world, and understands that some music never really ends. It just waits to be heard.
