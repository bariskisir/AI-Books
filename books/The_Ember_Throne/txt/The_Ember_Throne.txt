Chapter 1: The Comet Rises

Prince Kael stood on the northern watchtower and watched the sky burn. The Fire-Crown had come back, just as the old stories said it would. A red star with tails of orange light, dragging across the darkness like a wound that would not close. The wind cut through his wool cloak and bit at his cheeks. He had been twelve when his father died and Malachar took the throne. Fourteen years in the cold north, learning to swing a sword and keep his mouth shut. The men who raised him called him a foundling. They did not know about the iron ring hidden beneath his tunic, stamped with the ash tree of the royal line. His father had pressed it into his palm the night of the coup, blood on the metal, whispers in the dark. The garrison slept below. He could hear the snoring of the watch commander through the thin walls. He touched the ring and felt the warmth of the oath he had never spoken aloud. The comet did not lie. Something was ending. Something was beginning. He turned from the rail and descended the narrow stone stairs, his boots loud in the darkness. The time for hiding was finished.

Chapter 2: The Burning Dream

Serin woke coughing. Smoke filled the room, but the hearth held only cold ash. She sat up, hand pressed to her chest, and felt the pulse of the deep fire beating under the world. The dream had been vivid: five kingdoms burning in a circle, and at the center a throne made of ember and bone. A man stood before it, but she had not seen his face. The Fire-Crown had risen in the east above his shoulder, and the flames had reached for him like hungry hands. She crossed to the window. The comet hung there, smaller than in her dream but unmistakable, a smear of red light against the fading stars. She had told the Conclave it would come. They had called her dramatic. She had told them the deep fires were stirring. They had said she was imagining things. Alta, the eldest, had patted her hand and told her to get more sleep. Serin pulled on her leather tunic and packed her kit: a knife, a waterskin, dried herbs, a map of the old roads. If the Conclave would not act, she would act alone. She had no army and no title, but the flames had shown her what was coming.

Chapter 3: The Throne Uneasy

Lord Malachar stood at the high window of the Ashfall palace and watched the comet with a face of carved stone. Behind him, his councilors argued about grain taxes and border patrols. Their voices rose and fell like meaningless music. He let them talk. The comet was not in any of their reports. His spymaster, a thin man named Corvus, stood apart from the others, waiting with the patience of a man who had learned that silence was a weapon. When Malachar dismissed the council with a wave, the door closed and silence returned. Corvus stepped forward. "Speak," Malachar said without turning. "The northern watchers report a man traveling south. Young. Carries a sword. Travels alone." "Many men travel south." "This one has the ash-tree mark on his belongings. A ring, we believe. The old seal." Malachar's jaw tightened. He had killed the father in front of the son, fourteen years ago on this very floor, and watched the boy sent north with a single horse and a curse from the palace steps. He should have killed the boy too. The authority of Ashfall rested on fear and gold, and the gold was running thin. Malachar needed the boy dead before the northern lords saw an opportunity. Corvus would find him, or Varn would, and the matter would end.

Chapter 4: The Sword Unburied

Kael walked into the forest before dawn. The path was overgrown, the oaks thick with moss. Birds called overhead, their voices sharp in the gray light. He had not visited this place since the night they brought him north, a boy wrapped in a dead man's cloak, crying into the horse's mane. The tree stood where he remembered it, split by lightning, its trunk hollowed by age. The bark was rough under his fingers. He knelt and began to dig. The earth was cold and damp. His fingers found the oilcloth first, then the shape beneath it. He pulled the bundle free and unwrapped the layers of waxed fabric. His father's sword. The blade was plain, the steel dark with age. The hilt was wrapped in leather dark with old sweat and older blood. No jewels. No inscriptions. A working sword for a king who believed a king should work. Kael stood and held it. The weight felt right, heavier than the training swords of the north. He swung it once, testing the balance. It moved like an extension of his arm. He slid the blade into his scabbard and walked back toward the garrison. He wrapped the blade in oilcloth again, then thought better of it and left it bare. A sword hidden was a sword useless. If he was going south, he would go armed and ready for whatever waited on the road.

Serin stood before the Ember Conclave in their hall beneath the mountain. Nine women and three men, robed in red and black, their faces lit by a central fire that burned in a pit of black stone.
