Chapter 1: The Fire Before the Fire

The dream always burned the same way. Cassandra stood on the highest tower of the citadel and watched the sea turn red. Ships burned in the harbor. Men ran through streets that no longer existed, their bodies made of smoke. She opened her mouth to scream and no sound came out — the fire swallowed everything, even her voice.

She woke with her throat raw and her palms bleeding from where her nails had dug in. The room was dark. The oil lamp beside her bed had died sometime in the night, and the wick smoked in its pool of cold grease. She lay still and listened to her own breathing slow.

Outside her window, Troy slept. The walls rose against a sky full of stars. Nothing burned. Nothing moved. She pressed her forehead against the stone sill and watched the watchfires on the plain below. Sentries walked the battlements with torches that cast small, lonely pools of light.

Three years, she thought. Three years before the first sail.

She had seen the exact shape of the Greek hulls. She had seen the crest of Agamemnon, the lion and the boar, painted on the largest ship. She had seen the man himself — his thick chest, his close-set eyes, the way he would stand on the prow and point at her city.

She had told her mother at breakfast. Hecuba had touched her daughter's cheek and said, "Eat your bread, child. The dreams will pass."

They never passed. They accumulated. Each night another layer of fire pressed down on the city she loved, and each morning she woke with the weight of a future she could not change. She dressed in the dark and walked to the door. There was no one to tell. There was never anyone to tell.

Chapter 2: The Walls of Troy

Dawn came gray and cold. Cassandra walked the circuit of the walls the way other women walked to the well — a daily ritual, a gathering of what she needed to survive. The stones were wet with dew. Below her, the lower city stirred to life. Bakers opened their ovens. Goats bleated in their pens. A fishmonger shouted the night's catch.

She stopped at the eastern parapet and looked out across the plain toward the sea. No ships. Just the blue line of the horizon and the white caps of waves. She had seen it so clearly in the dream — the beach black with hulls, the tents spreading inland like a stain.

A sentry passed behind her. "Princess. Up early."

"Every day," she said.

He nodded and moved on. He did not ask why she stared at the empty sea. No one ever asked. They thought she was strange, touched by something they could not name. They were right, but not in the way they believed.

She counted the years on her fingers. She would be twenty when the Greeks came. Old enough to be married, old enough to bear children, old enough to watch everything burn. Apollo had given her the gift when she was sixteen, kneeling in his temple, the air thick with incense. She had said no. He had smiled and breathed the prophecy into her mouth anyway. And then he had leaned close and whispered the curse.

She could still feel his breath on her neck.

She walked on, past the Scaean Gate, past the tower where Hector trained with his spear, past the palace windows where her father slept. The sun cleared the ridge and the city turned gold. Beautiful. So beautiful it hurt to look at it.

She would remember this light. She would carry it with her through every dark thing that was coming.

Chapter 3: The Return

The messenger arrived at midday. Cassandra was in the courtyard helping the servant girls sort wool when she heard the horn from the gate. Three long blasts — a royal return.

She dropped the wool and stood. The girls looked at her.

"Princess?"

She did not answer. She was already walking.

The gates swung open and Paris rode through on a white horse, his cloak mud-spattered, his face bright with sun and pride. Behind him came the chariots, the captured treasure, the slaves. And behind them, veiled and golden, a woman.

Cassandra knew her before anyone spoke. Helen. She was taller than the stories said, and her eyes were the color of the sea after a storm. She rode in a litter carried by eight slaves, and she did not look at Troy. She looked at Paris, and Paris looked at her, and the whole city cheered.

Cassandra stood in the crowd and watched the woman who would burn them all.

Paris saw her. He dismounted and came to her with his arms out. "Sister. I have brought us glory."

"You have brought us war," she said.

His smile did not waver. Men nearby laughed and clapped him on the shoulder. "The prophet speaks," one said. "Run, brother. Hide your ships."

Paris kissed her cheek. "Always the same song, Cass. Come. Meet Helen. She is everything the poets say."

"I know what she is," Cassandra said. "I have seen her standing on these walls, watching the Greeks break through."

Paris laughed and walked away. The crowd followed him, leaving Cassandra alone in the sudden silence of the gate. The dust from the horses hung in the air like smoke.

Chapter 4: The Council

Priam convened the council that evening. The great hall filled with princes, generals, and elders. Torches blazed in their brackets and the air grew thick with smoke and sweat. Cassandra stood at the edge of the room, where the women were allowed to watch.

Paris stood in the center and told his story. The hospitality of Sparta. The beauty of Helen. The treachery of Menelaus — a bad host, a worse husband. The goddess Aphrodite had promised him the most beautiful woman in the world, and he had claimed his prize.

The elders nodded. The generals grinned. Antenor alone sat with his arms crossed, his face unreadable.

Priam spoke from his throne. "We are Trojans. We do not fear Greeks. If Menelaus comes with his brother, we will meet them on the plain and send them home weeping."

The hall erupted in cheers. Men stamped their feet and beat their spears against the stone floor.

Cassandra stepped forward. "Father."

The noise died. Heads turned. She felt their eyes on her like arrows.

"I have seen this war," she said. "I have seen our brothers fall. I have seen Hector dragged behind a chariot. I have seen our city burn. Send Helen back. Open the gates to Menelaus. Beg for peace. It is the only way."

Priam's face hardened. "You are dismissed, daughter."

"Father — "

"You are dismissed."

The guards took her arms. She did not resist. As they led her out, she heard the laughter begin again behind her, warm and careless and full of certainty. She closed her eyes and saw the fire. It was closer now.

Chapter 5: The King's Daughter

Priam came to her chamber after midnight. Cassandra sat by the window, still dressed, her hands folded in her lap. She had known he would come. She had seen it in the same vision that showed her the fire — her father's face, older than it was now, the lines deeper, the hope gone.

He did not sit. He stood by the door with his arms at his sides and looked at her the way he had looked at her since she was a child — with love and fear and something close to resentment.

"You cannot speak that way in council," he said.

"I cannot lie in council."

"You shame me. You shame your brother. You shame your city."

"I am trying to save my city."

He crossed to the window and stood beside her. The harbor lights flickered below. Somewhere a dog barked and another answered. The night was warm and smelled of jasmine.

"Apollo did this to you," he said quietly.

"He gave me sight. The curse is his parting gift."

"And you are sure of what you see?"

"I have never been wrong, Father. Not once."

Priam was silent for a long time. When he spoke again, his voice was tired. "Then what am I supposed to do? Tell my son to give back his bride? Tell my people we should kneel to the Greeks before a single ship has sailed? I am the king. A king cannot rule by fear of the future."

"You can choose to listen."

"I cannot. You must understand this, Cassandra. A king who heeds doomsayers invites chaos. Even if you are right. Especially if you are right."

He left her there, alone in the dark. She watched the harbor lights until they blurred and became the fires of her dream again. They looked the same. They always looked the same.

Chapter 6: The Scaean Gate

Andromache found her at dawn. Cassandra had not moved from the window. Her dress was wrinkled, her hair uncombed, her eyes dry and burning.

"Hector's wife should not be seen with me," Cassandra said without turning.

"I am not here as Hector's wife." Andromache sat on the edge of the bed. She was heavy with child, her belly round under her tunic, her face flushed with the effort of climbing the stairs. "I am here as a woman who cannot sleep."

"Your husband does not believe me."

"My husband believes in his spear and his shield and his brothers. He has never needed a prophet."

"Today he does."

Andromache studied her. The other women of the palace avoided Cassandra's eyes. They crossed the street when they saw her coming. But Andromache looked at her directly, the way a soldier looks at an enemy he respects.

"Tell me what you see," Andromache said.

Cassandra turned from the window. "I see your son."

Andromache's hand went to her belly. "What about him?"

"I see him thrown from the walls of Troy. I see the fall break his body on the stones below."

Andromache's face went white but she did not look away. "When?"

"At the end. When the Greeks are inside."

"Then we will make sure the Greeks never get inside."

"If you can."

Andromache stood. Her hands trembled but her voice was steady. "I will speak to Hector. I will make him listen."

"He will not."

"Then I will make him remember your words when the time comes."

She left. Cassandra listened to her footsteps fade down the corridor and wondered if Andromache had the strength to carry what she now knew. She doubted it. No one ever did.

Chapter 7: The Wooden Beast

The vision came in the middle of a sentence. Cassandra was talking to a servant about dinner, and then she was not in the room anymore. She was on the plain before the walls, and the Greeks were gone. The beach was empty. The tents were struck. The ships had vanished over the horizon.

Victory, the voices said. The Greeks have fled.

But she could still feel them. Their presence pressed against her like heat from a fire. She turned, and there it was. A horse. A wooden horse, fifty feet high, standing on the plain where the Greek camp had been. Its sides were smooth and its hollow belly held two dozen armed men. She could see them through the wood as if it were glass.

She opened her mouth to scream and the vision released her.

The servant was staring at her. "Princess? Are you unwell?"

Cassandra grabbed the girl's arm. "Fetch Andromache. Now."

She sat down heavily and pressed her hands to her face. The horse. The horse would be the end of them. Not the war, not the ten years of killing, but the horse. The Greeks would pretend to leave and Troy would open its gates to the gift and the gift would kill them all.

Andromache arrived breathless. "What happened?"

"I saw how they will do it," Cassandra said. "The Greeks will build a horse. A great wooden horse. They will hide soldiers inside and sail away. And Troy will pull it through the gates and celebrate, and in the night the soldiers will open the city from within."

Andromache sat down slowly. "That is a strange strategy."

"It is a trap. And every Trojan will walk into it smiling."

"How do you stop a trap no one believes exists?"

Cassandra had no answer.

Chapter 8: The War Council

Hector called a council of war in the seventh month after Paris returned. The scouts had reported movement in the Greek cities. Ships being dragged from sheds. Stores of grain and bronze being loaded. Menelaus was visiting Mycenae, and Agamemnon was summoning kings.

Cassandra was not invited. She came anyway.

The generals sat around a long table in the armory. Maps were spread across the surface, weighted at the corners with daggers. Hector stood at the head, his armor polished, his face calm.

"Speak," she said from the doorway.

Hector looked up. "This is a war council, sister."

"I know. I am here to tell you what will happen."

The generals exchanged glances. Aeneas sighed and leaned back in his chair. "Let her speak," he said. "It will be faster than sending guards."

Hector nodded once.

Cassandra stepped forward. "The war will last ten years. You will kill many Greeks. You will win every battle that matters until the last one. The last one you will lose. You will die outside these walls, and your body will be dragged around the city by Achilles. Troy will fall."

The room was silent. Then Pandarus laughed. "Ten years? The Greeks will be gone by winter. They have no stomach for a long war."

"Remember my words when winter comes," Cassandra said.

"Your words are wind," Pandarus said.

Hector raised his hand. "Enough. Cassandra, I respect your visions. But we cannot plan a war around prophecies. We plan around what we can see and touch and measure. The Greeks will come. We will fight. Troy will stand."

"Troy will fall."

"Then let me die believing otherwise."

He turned back to the map. The generals leaned in. Cassandra stood in the doorway for a long moment, then walked out into the empty hall. No one called her back.

Chapter 9: The Altar

Theano was waiting for her at the temple of Athena. The high priestess stood by the altar with her hands clasped, her white robes immaculate, her gray hair pinned tight against her skull. She was the only person in Troy who did not avoid Cassandra's eyes.

"Come," Theano said. "I have prepared a sacrifice."

Cassandra followed her into the inner sanctum. The air was cool and heavy with incense. A goat stood tethered to a ring in the floor, its eyes white with fear.

"Apollo speaks to you," Theano said. "Athena speaks to me. We serve the same Olympians, even if they do not agree with each other."

"Athena sides with the Greeks."

Theano's knife flashed. The goat fell without a sound. She pulled the entrails out and spread them on the stone slab, her fingers moving through the warm loops with practiced precision.

"The liver is clean," she said. "The intestines show no obstruction."

"What do you see?"

"A city that survives."

Cassandra stepped closer and looked at the entrails. They were pink and smooth. Healthy. She bent down and inhaled. The smell of blood filled her nose, and underneath it, something else. Rot. The same rot she had smelled in her dreams.

"The liver is clean, but I smell decay," Cassandra said. "Your goddess is lying to you."

Theano's face tightened. "That is a dangerous thing to say in a temple."

"Truth is always dangerous. You of all people should know that."

Theano washed her hands in the basin. "I believe you see what you see, Cassandra. But I also believe the gods protect Troy. We have stood for a thousand years."

"You will not stand for one more decade."

Theano dried her hands and turned away. "Go home, princess. Rest. Your mind is tired."

Cassandra left the temple and walked through the market. The merchants were packing their stalls. The sun was setting. She watched the shadows lengthen across the stones and thought about rot that hides beneath a clean surface.

Chapter 10: The Lovers

Paris found her in the garden behind the palace. He was alone, which was rare. Since Helen's arrival, he was never without her. But tonight he sat on the stone bench under the olive tree, his head in his hands.

"Does she know you are here?" Cassandra asked.

"She thinks I am with the horses."

Cassandra sat beside him. The cicadas buzzed in the dark. The moon was a thin crescent over the eastern wall.

"I love her," Paris said.

"I know you do."

"She is not a curse, Cass. She is a woman. She left her husband and her daughter and her country to be with me."

"She left because Aphrodite made her. She is a prize, not a partner."

Paris looked up. His eyes were red. "You have never loved anyone. You cannot understand."

"I have seen her death, Paris. She outlives you."

He stared at her. "What?"

"You die at the Scaean Gate. Philoctetes' arrow. The one Heracles gave him. It finds you in the last year of the war. Helen is taken back to Sparta. She lives a long life. She dies old and gray in the bed she shared with Menelaus."

Paris stood up. His hands were shaking. "Why do you tell me these things?"

"Because they are true."

"I do not want your truth."

"Then who will give it to you?"

He walked away without answering. Cassandra stayed on the bench and listened to the cicadas. The moon climbed higher. She thought about love and how it made people stupid, how it made them deaf and blind and eager for destruction. She had never loved anyone that way. She wondered if that was a mercy or another curse.

Chapter 11: The First Sail

The watchman saw it first. A speck on the horizon that grew into a sail, then two, then a dozen. He blew his horn and the city erupted.

Cassandra was in the temple when the horn sounded. She had been praying to no god in particular, asking for nothing. The sound brought her to her feet. She ran to the walls and climbed to the highest tower and looked out at the sea.

The ships were there. Hundreds of them. The Greek fleet had come.

Menelaus. Agamemnon. Achilles. Odysseus. All of them, packed into hulls that cut the water like blades. The black ships fanned out across the bay, their oars rising and falling in unison, their sails painted with the devices of a hundred kings.

Cassandra gripped the parapet. Three years. She had given them three years, and here they were, right on schedule.

Beside her, a soldier laughed. "Look at them. Thousands of men packed into wooden boxes. One good storm and they are fish food."

"You will not be laughing when they land," Cassandra said.

The soldier looked at her. Recognition dawned. "Ah. The prophet. Tell me, princess, do you see me dying today?"

"I see you dying in the third year. A spear through the throat. You fall face-down in the mud."

The soldier's smile vanished. He turned away and spat over the wall.

Cassandra watched the ships grow larger. The fire dream pressed against her mind, demanding attention. Not yet, she told it. Not yet.

Chapter 12: The Market

The lower city emptied as men took up arms. The market, normally bustling, was a skeleton of itself. A few merchants sat behind empty stalls. Women queued at the well with anxious faces. Children ran through the streets playing at war with wooden swords.

Cassandra walked among them. She needed to see their faces, to remember that behind her visions of fire there were people who laughed and cried and loved. She needed the weight of their reality to anchor her.

A fishmonger saw her and crossed his arms. "Come to tell us we are all dead, princess?"

"I come to buy fish."

"You come to curse us."

"I have never cursed anyone. I only speak what I see."

He laughed without humor. "Convenient, that. You say we lose, and when we win, you say you were wrong. When we lose, you say you told us so. Either way, you are the wise woman and we are fools."

"That is not — "

"Leave my stall." He turned his back on her.

She moved on. An old woman selling herbs caught her arm. "Ignore him. He is scared. We are all scared."

"You should be," Cassandra said. Then she regretted it. The old woman's face fell.

"Can you see nothing good?" the woman asked.

Cassandra thought about it. "I see Troy burned to ash. But I see new walls rising on the same ground. Not our walls. Not our hands. But the city does not die. It changes."

"That is something."

"Is it?"

The old woman pressed a bundle of sage into her hands. "For your dreams. Burn it and pray to Hestia. She protects the hearth."

Cassandra took the sage. She walked back toward the palace through streets that would soon run with blood. The sage smelled clean and green in her hands. She held onto it.

Chapter 13: The Temple of Apollo

She had avoided the temple for four years. Not since the day he cursed her. But the Greek fleet was anchored off Tenedos and she needed to understand, needed to ask the god why he had done this to her.

The temple was empty. The priest had fled to the lower city with his family. The altar was cold. Dust covered the floor.

Cassandra knelt at the foot of Apollo's statue and pressed her forehead to the marble base. The stone was cold. The air was still.

"Speak to me," she said. "You gave me this sight. The least you can do is tell me what to do with it."

Silence.

"I was a girl. Sixteen years old. I did not know what I was refusing. You did not tell me the price."

Silence.

"Are you listening? Or do you only speak when you want something?"

The air changed. The temperature dropped. The torch flames guttered and went out. Darkness fell like a blanket.

And she felt him. Not a voice, not a presence she could name, but a pressure in her chest, a heat behind her eyes. He was there. He had always been there, watching her suffer, and he did not care.

"Why?" she whispered.

No answer. The pressure lifted. The torches flared back to life. She was alone in the empty temple with a god who would not speak to her.

She stood up and walked out. She did not look back at the statue. She was done asking.

Chapter 14: The Believer

Andromache came to her rooms that night with Astyanax in her arms. The baby was two months old, fat and pink, his eyes the same deep brown as Hector's.

"I told him," Andromache said.

"And?"

"He said you are troubled. That your visions come from Apollo's anger, not his gift."

"Half-true."

"He said I should not fill my head with omens and portents. That I should trust the army."

Cassandra looked at the baby. He was sucking his fist, his eyes half-closed, peaceful in a way she had never been. She reached out and touched his cheek. He turned toward her finger.

"Hector is a good man," Cassandra said. "He is also a blind one."

Andromache sat down. The baby fussed and she shifted him to her shoulder. "I believe you, Cassandra. I believe every word you have said. But believing is not enough. What can I do with belief?"

"Prepare."

"For what? Ten years of war? The death of my husband? The murder of my son in front of my eyes? How do you prepare for that?"

"I do not know."

They sat in silence. The baby fell asleep on Andromache's shoulder, his small body rising and falling with each breath. Cassandra watched him and felt a grief so vast it had no edges.

"If you could change what you see," Andromache said, "would you?"

"Every day."

"Then perhaps the curse is not that no one believes you. Perhaps the curse is that you believe yourself."

Cassandra had no answer for that.

Chapter 15: The Landing

The Greeks came ashore at dawn. The beach at Troy was wide and shallow, and the black ships beached in perfect formation, their prows lined up like teeth. The first wave of soldiers jumped into the surf and waded through the foam with their shields raised.

The Trojans met them at the waterline.

Cassandra watched from the walls. She saw the two lines crash into each other, the flash of bronze, the spray of blood. She heard the screams, the war cries, the crash of shields. She watched men she had known since childhood fall into the surf and turn the water red.

Achilles was easy to spot. He moved through the Trojan line like a wolf through sheep, his spear never still, his victims falling before they could scream. She watched him kill three men in the time it took to draw a breath. The fourth was a boy she had danced with at the spring festival. His name was Corythus. She watched Achilles' spear punch through his throat.

Beside her, a young sentry vomited over the wall.

Cassandra did not look away. She made herself watch. If she was going to carry these visions, she owed the dead the dignity of witnessing their actual deaths. It was the only thing she could give them.

By midday, the Greeks held the beach. The Trojans fell back to the walls. The plain was covered with bodies. The surf washed over them, pulling them out and pushing them back, and the gulls gathered in the shallows and fought over the scraps.

Chapter 16: The Wounded

The palace became a hospital. Women tore their finest linens into bandages. The kitchens boiled water day and night. The wounded came in carts, on stretchers, in the arms of their comrades. The air inside the citadel turned thick with the smell of blood and rot and desperation.

Cassandra worked alongside the other women. She held men down while the surgeons cut. She packed wounds with honey and herbs. She sat with the dying and held their hands while they passed.

They called her witch under their breath. They made signs against evil when she passed. But when the pain was too much and the surgeon was needed elsewhere, they let her hold the basin and mop the sweat from their foreheads.

A young soldier grabbed her wrist as she passed. His eyes were wild. "You said I would die in the third year."

She looked down at him. His leg was gone below the knee. The stump was wrapped in bloody linen. She did not recognize his face.

"I am not dead," he said.

"Not yet."

He laughed, then coughed, then cried. "You are a curse on this city."

"Perhaps."

"But you were right about the beach. You said we would lose the beach. And we did."

"And what good did my being right do you?"

He had no answer. She pulled her hand free and moved to the next man. The surgeon was calling for more boiling water. The sun was setting outside, throwing long shadows across the room. She worked until her arms ached and her back screamed, and when she finally stopped, the dead were laid out in the courtyard like fish on a slab.

Chapter 17: The Witch

A delegation of women came to see her the next morning. They stood in her doorway, six of them, their faces hard, their arms crossed. The leader was a baker's wife named Xanthe, a broad woman with flour still dusting her forearms.

"You must leave the healing to the healers," Xanthe said.

"I am helping."

"You are bringing bad luck. The men die faster when you are near."

"That is not true."

"It is what we see. Every time you touch a man, he dies."

Cassandra looked at them. She saw their fear, their need for an explanation, their desperation to make sense of the senseless. She was their explanation. The witch. The cursed one. The reason bad things happened.

"If I leave the infirmary, the men will still die."

"Then they will die with clean souls, not cursed by your presence."

Cassandra nodded slowly. "You have made up your minds."

"We have."

She stepped past them and walked down the corridor. Behind her, she heard one of the women spit on the floor. She kept walking. There was no point in arguing. There never was.

She went to the roof and stood alone and watched the Greek camp spread across the plain like a stain. Tents, cookfires, picket lines. The sound of their drills carried across the distance. They were preparing for the next assault. She could see it in her mind already — the waves of men, the scaling ladders, the slaughter.

She wondered if Xanthe would blame her for that too.

Chapter 18: The Secret

Paris came to her at night, cloaked and hooded, moving through the shadows like a thief. He knocked twice on her door and slipped inside before she could speak.

"I need your help," he said.

"My help?"

"I am going to fight tomorrow. Hector has assigned me to the left flank."

"Good. You should fight."

Paris pulled back his hood. His face was thinner than it had been a year ago. Lines had formed around his mouth. "If I die, Helen will be alone. If I die, there is no one to protect her."

"Then do not die."

"Tell me how."

Cassandra studied her brother. She had never seen him afraid before. Arrogant, yes. Foolish, always. But never afraid. Fear had reshaped his bones.

"I cannot tell you how to survive a war," she said. "I can only tell you what I see."

"Then tell me. Do I die tomorrow?"

She closed her eyes and searched for his thread in the tapestry. It was frayed but unbroken. "Not tomorrow. You live another seven years. You die at the Scaean Gate."

"When?"

"The last year. The arrow comes from Philoctetes. You will see it in the air and have time to step aside. You will not."

Paris stood very still. "Why would I not step aside?"

"I do not know. That part of the vision is unclear. Perhaps you want to die. Perhaps you will be protecting someone. Perhaps you will simply be too tired to move."

He sat down on the edge of her bed. "Seven years."

"Seven years."

"Then I have time."

"We all have time. Until we do not."

He looked at her with an expression she could not read. "You carry so much, Cass. How do you bear it?"

"I do not bear it. It carries me."

Chapter 19: The Victory

Hector led a charge in the second year that pushed the Greeks back to their ships. It was the first real victory Troy had seen. The army returned covered in glory and blood, dragging captured weapons and enemy standards. The city celebrated.

Cassandra stood on the wall and watched Hector walk through the gates. His armor was dented. His shield was split in three places. But he was smiling, and the crowd adored him. They threw flowers at his feet. They called him the savior of Troy.

She saw the funeral pyre behind his head. She saw his body, naked and desecrated, dragged through the dust. She saw Andromache's face when she received his armor back from the Greeks.

She did not cheer.

Aeneas found her on the wall. He was the only Trojan leader who did not avoid her. "You do not share our joy."

"My joy does not change what is coming."

"Nothing changes what is coming. That does not mean we cannot celebrate what is here."

"I would rather be ready."

He leaned on the parapet beside her. "Can I tell you something, princess? I half-believe you. I think most of us do. But belief does not change strategy. We fight because we must. We celebrate because we are alive. The future will come whether we ignore it or not."

"That is the most sensible thing any Trojan has said to me."

Aeneas smiled. "Do not tell Hector. He will think I have gone soft."

He left her on the wall. Below, the celebration continued. Wine flowed. Songs were sung. Hector danced with Andromache in the torchlight. Cassandra watched her brother and his wife spin in circles, and she tried to memorize the joy on their faces. It would have to last a long time.

Chapter 20: The Plague

The plague came in the third year. It started in the Greek camp — Apollo's arrows, the priests said. Agamemnon had refused to return Chryseis to her father, and the god's wrath fell on the Achaeans like a hammer.

But plague does not respect sieges. It crossed the lines. It crept through the water and the air and the shared misery of war.

The first Trojan died in his sleep. The second was a child in the lower city. Within a week, the streets were thick with the sick and the dying.

Cassandra walked through the quarantine district with a cloth over her face. Bodies lay in doorways. The living had no strength to bury the dead. The smell was indescribable.

She found a girl of perhaps twelve years, sitting beside her mother's body, too weak to move. She knelt and pressed water to the girl's lips.

"You will live," Cassandra said.

The girl's eyes focused. "How do you know?"

"Because I see you old. I see you with grandchildren. You survive this."

"Will my mother?"

"No."

The girl did not cry. She was too dehydrated for tears. She simply nodded and drank the water and stared at the wall.

Cassandra moved on. She distributed herbs. She carried water. She held hands and closed eyes. Theano had taught her some medicine in the temple years ago, and she used every scrap of it. By the time the plague burned out, she had saved twelve people and lost count of those she could not save.

The city called her a witch. But they also called her when their children fell ill.

Chapter 21: The Herald

A truce was called in the fourth year. Both sides needed to bury their dead. Cassandra walked onto the plain with the other women, gathering corpses in the gray light of dawn.

A Greek herald approached the Trojan line. He was old, with a scar across his forehead and the bearing of a man who had seen too much. He stopped when he saw Cassandra.

"You are the prophet," he said.

"I am Cassandra."

He nodded. "I have heard of you. The Trojans say you are cursed."

"The Greeks say I am mad."

"We say many things." He looked out across the plain. "I have been at sieges before. I have seen what war does to cities. I have seen what cities do to themselves."

"What is your name?"

"Talthybius. Herald to Agamemnon."

"If you serve him, you will die at his side. A homecoming that turns to blood."

Talthybius did not flinch. He had the calm of a man who had accepted his fate long ago. "Then I will serve him until that day."

"You could leave. Desert. Go home to your wife."

"My wife is dead. My sons are grown. I have nowhere else to be."

They stood together in the gray light, Greek and Trojan, herald and prophet. The bodies lay between them. The truce would end at sunset.

"I will remember you," Talthybius said.

"I will not remember you," Cassandra said. "I have seen too many faces to keep them all."

She walked back to the city. Behind her, the Greek herald watched her go.

Chapter 22: The Child

Astyanax learned to walk in the fifth year of the war. Andromache brought him to Cassandra's rooms and set him on the floor. He took three steps and fell. He laughed and tried again.

"Is he not perfect?" Andromache said.

"He is."

"He has Hector's chin and my temper."

"He has a short life."

Andromache's smile vanished. "You do not need to say that every time you see him."

"I am not saying it to hurt you. I am saying it because it is true."

"I know it is true. I carry it with me every moment. I wake up and think, this might be the day. I put him to bed and think, this might be the night. I do not need you to remind me."

Cassandra picked Astyanax up. He grabbed her finger and put it in his mouth. His eyes were clear and curious. He had no idea what waited for him.

"I am sorry," Cassandra said.

Andromache took her son back. "Your sorry does not change anything."

"No. It does not."

They sat in silence. Astyanax babbled and pulled his mother's hair. The war continued outside, a distant rumble of thunder on a clear day. Cassandra watched the child and felt the familiar weight of knowledge settle deeper into her bones.

She wondered if it would be easier to be ignorant. To live each day without knowing how many were left. She thought it probably would be. But she had not been given that choice.

Chapter 23: The Gate

Hector refused to retreat. The Greeks had pushed the Trojans back to the walls, and the city gates were open, and the army was pouring through, and Hector stood in the gateway and turned back.

"Close the gates," his men begged.

"Fight," he said.

Cassandra heard it from the courtyard. She ran to the wall and climbed to the tower above the gate. Below her, Hector stood with his spear, holding the line, killing any Greek who came within reach. His men rallied around him. The Greeks faltered.

She saw it. The moment of decision. The space between his victory and his death.

"Hector!" she screamed from the wall. "Come inside! Let them have the field! You have done enough!"

He did not look up. He did not hear her. Or if he heard, he chose not to listen.

She watched him push forward. She watched the Greeks fall back. She watched Troy hold that day, and she knew that every victory brought him closer to the final defeat. He could not see it. He could only see the man in front of him, the spear in his hand, the next heartbeat of the fight.

He came inside at dusk, covered in blood that was not his own. The city cheered him. He raised his spear and the cheering grew louder.

Cassandra stood alone in the tower and watched the stars come out. She had screamed until her throat was raw. It had changed nothing.

Chapter 24: The Rage

Patroclus died in the ninth year. Cassandra felt it before the messenger arrived. A shift in the fabric, a loosening of threads. She was in her room when the knowledge came to her — Patroclus, son of Menoetius, cousin of Achilles, dead by Hector's hand.

She sat down slowly.

"Now it begins," she said to the empty room.

The Greeks would rage. Achilles would return to battle. The balance would tip. The war would enter its final phase.

She found Hector in the armory, being fitted for new greaves. He looked content, satisfied with the day's work.

"You killed Patroclus," she said.

"I killed many men today. Which one was Patroclus?"

"The one who wore Achilles' armor. The one you thought was Achilles."

Hector's smile faded. "I did not know."

"Now you do. And now Achilles knows you have killed the man he loved best."

Hector was silent. The armorer continued working, his hands steady, his face carefully blank.

"Achilles will come for you," Cassandra said. "He will hunt you across the plain. He will kill you in front of the walls. He will drag your body behind his chariot until there is nothing left."

Hector lifted his chin. "Then I will face him. I am not afraid."

"You should be."

"I am a Trojan prince. I do not run."

"You do not think. You only fight."

He turned away from her. The armorer finished the greaves and withdrew. Hector stood alone in the torchlight, his new armor gleaming, and Cassandra saw the hero and the corpse superimposed on each other. They were the same shape.

Chapter 25: The Return

Achilles returned to battle the next day. His chariot thundered across the plain, his armor blazing in the sun. The Greeks, who had been crumbling for days, reformed behind him like iron filings drawn to a magnet.

Cassandra watched from the wall. She had stopped screaming. What was the point? The thing she had seen was happening exactly as she had seen it. Every detail aligned. The curve of Achilles' spear. The way the Trojans fell back. The path he cut through the army, straight toward the city.

"The walls," she whispered. "Stay behind the walls. Do not meet him in the open."

Hector was already moving. She saw him push through the Scaean Gate, his shield up, his spear ready. He had heard Achilles was coming and he could not help himself. He had to meet the challenge.

"Hector!"

He did not look back.

The two armies parted. The space between Hector and Achilles cleared. Men stopped fighting to watch. The whole plain held its breath.

They clashed. Bronze on bronze. Spear on shield. The sound carried across the distance like a bell.

Cassandra watched her brother fight the greatest warrior of the age. She watched him hold his ground. She watched him give ground. She watched the spear find the gap between his shield and his neck.

The world went silent.

Hector fell. The dust rose around him. The Greeks roared. And Cassandra, from the wall of the city she had tried to save, watched Achilles strip her brother's armor and tie his body to the back of a chariot.

The chariot began to move. The body began to drag.

She did not look away.

Chapter 26: The Body

Achilles dragged Hector's body around the walls of Troy for twelve days. Every morning he rose, harnessed his horses, and made the circuit. The body that had once been the greatest warrior in Asia was ground against stone, caked with dust, torn by gravel. The Trojans watched from the walls. No one could stop it.

Priam did not leave his chambers. The court was silent. The army was broken.

Andromache did not weep. She sat by the window with Astyanax on her lap and stared at the plain. When she spoke, her voice was flat.

"You said this would happen."

"Yes," Cassandra said.

"You said it would be like this."

"Yes."

"And you could not stop it."

"I have never stopped anything."

Andromache turned to look at her. Her eyes were dry. "Then what is the point of your sight? If you see everything and change nothing, what are you?"

"I have asked myself that question every day for ten years."

"I hope you find an answer before we are all dead."

Cassandra left her. She walked through the silent palace. The servants moved like ghosts. The guards stood at their posts with empty eyes. Troy was a city in mourning, and she was the one who had buried it a thousand times in her mind.

She went to the temple of Apollo and stood before the god's statue.

"You did this," she said. "You gave me the sight so I could watch it all happen."

The stone face stared back at her. No answer. There was never an answer.

Chapter 27: The Ransom

Priam decided to go to Achilles. He would cross the Greek lines alone, at night, and beg for his son's body. The council tried to stop him. His daughters tried to stop him. The generals tried to stop him.

Cassandra did not try.

"Go," she said. "He will receive you. He will give you the body."

Her father looked at her. He was smaller than he had been ten years ago. The war had eaten his flesh and left the bones. "You see this?"

"I see you return with Hector. I see the funeral."

"The funeral. Not the rescue."

"You will succeed. But you will not succeed in saving him. He is already gone."

Priam nodded slowly. "Somehow, daughter, you have found a way to make good news sound like a curse."

"I am telling you the truth. It is the only thing I have ever given you."

He embraced her. It was the first time he had touched her in years. His arms were thin but his grip was strong.

"I am sorry," he said. "For not listening."

"It would not have changed anything."

"Perhaps. But you deserved to be heard."

He let her go and walked out into the night. Cassandra watched him go and felt something crack inside her chest. Not forgiveness. Not hope. Something quieter. The loosening of a knot she had carried for so long she had forgotten it was there.

Chapter 28: The Pyre

Hector burned on the twelfth day. The pyre was built on the plain, outside the walls, because the Trojans no longer had the strength to carry him through the gates. Priam had brought the body back. The funeral was the greatest Troy had ever seen.

Andromache stood beside the pyre with Astyanax in her arms. She did not speak. She did not cry. She watched the flames consume her husband and her face was the face of a woman who had already died.

Cassandra stood at the edge of the crowd. She had attended so many funerals in her mind that the actual event felt like a memory. The heat of the fire. The smell of burning meat. The wailing of the women. It was all exactly as she had seen it.

She looked at the faces around her. Priam, hollow. Paris, guilty. Helen, weeping quietly behind her veil. The Trojans, broken.

They had not believed her. She had told them this would happen. They had laughed. They had called her mad. And now they stood in the smoke of a funeral she had predicted before the war began.

She did not feel satisfaction. She felt nothing.

The fire burned all night. Cassandra stayed until the last ember died. When she finally turned to walk back to the city, she realized she had not eaten in three days. She did not feel hunger either.

She wondered what else she had stopped feeling.

Chapter 29: The Amazons

Penthesilea arrived in the tenth year. She came with twelve warrior women mounted on horses, their armor polished, their spears sharp. They rode through the Scaean Gate and the Trojans stared.

Cassandra met them in the courtyard. The Amazon queen dismounted and stood head and shoulders above the Trojan men. Her arms were muscular. Her face was scarred. Her eyes were the color of iron.

"You are the prophet," Penthesilea said.

"I am Cassandra."

"Can you fight?"

"I can warn. I cannot change."

Penthesilea laughed. "Then you are useless in a battle. But useful before one. Tell me what you see for me."

"You die at Achilles' hand. Tomorrow."

The Amazon's smile did not waver. "A good death?"

"A clean one. He will respect you. He will mourn you after."

"Then that is all I can ask for." She turned to her women. "Prepare. We ride at dawn."

Cassandra watched her go. The Amazons moved through the courtyard with the easy confidence of women who had never been told they were weak. She envied them. Not their strength. Their certainty.

She found Penthesilea that night, sharpening her axe by the fire.

"Why did you come?" Cassandra asked. "You know you will die."

"Troy asked for help. I gave it."

"Even knowing the cost?"

"Cost is just cost. Honor is honor. I do not count the one against the other."

The Amazon queen looked up from her work. "You have spent your whole life watching death, prophet. I have spent mine choosing it. One of us is free."

"Which one?"

"I will die tomorrow and not know another day of grief. You will live and carry every death with you."

Penthesilea returned to her axe. Cassandra walked away. The Amazon's words followed her like wolves.

Chapter 30: The Fall of the Queen

The battle was brief. Penthesilea rode at the head of her company, her axe spinning, her war cry cutting through the noise of the fighting. She killed twelve Greeks before Achilles noticed her.

When he did, the fight was over.

He approached her with respect. They circled each other. Their weapons met. The Amazon queen was fast and strong, but Achilles was faster, stronger. His spear found her chest. She fell.

Cassandra watched from the wall. She had not warned Penthesilea to avoid the fight. There was no point. The queen had come to die and she had died well.

Achilles stood over the body. He removed his helmet. He looked down at the Amazon queen with something that might have been regret. Then he bent and closed her eyes.

Troy had lost another ally. The pattern continued. Every force that came to help the city was ground down by the Greek war machine. Cassandra saw it all. She could do nothing.

She went to the well and drew water. She washed her face. She looked at her reflection and saw a woman who had aged twenty years in ten. Gray in her hair. Lines around her mouth. The eyes of someone who had watched too much.

"There is more to come," she told her reflection.

The reflection stared back. It did not disagree.

Chapter 31: The Ethiopian

Memnon came next. He arrived with a thousand black warriors from the east, their skin the color of night, their swords curved and strange. He was beautiful, Memnon, tall and proud, the son of the dawn goddess Eos.

He came to Cassandra before the battle. "Tell me my fate."

"You die at Achilles' hand."

"When?"

"Tomorrow."

He nodded. "My mother will weep."

"All mothers weep."

"Will Troy remember me?"

"Troy will not survive to remember anyone."

He smiled at that, a sad smile that made him look young. "Then I will have to be satisfied with having been here."

He left her. The next day he fought Achilles on the plain. The fight lasted longer than most. Memnon wounded the Greek hero. Drew blood. For a moment, it seemed possible that the Ethiopian king might win.

But Achilles did not lose. Memnon fell. His body was carried back to his men, who burned him on a pyre that lit the sky. Cassandra watched the flames and thought about the morning, when Eos would rise and find her son dead.

She wondered what it was like to be a goddess and unable to save the ones you loved.

She already knew. It was exactly like being a prophet.

Chapter 32: The Arrow

Paris killed Achilles in the tenth year. The shot came from the walls, a single arrow guided by Apollo's hand, finding the one spot where the hero was vulnerable. His heel.

Cassandra saw the arrow leave the bow. She saw its trajectory. She saw it strike and she saw Achilles fall. She had known it was coming. She had seen it a hundred times in her dreams.

But when it happened, she felt nothing. No satisfaction. No relief. The man who had killed her brother was dead, and the city she had tried to save was still doomed.

Paris came to her that night, flushed with victory. "I did it. I killed him. The war is over."

"The war is not over."

"Without Achilles, the Greeks will break. They will sail home."

"They will not. They will build a horse."

Paris stared at her. "A horse?"

"You will see. The Greeks will pretend to leave. They will leave a gift. And Troy will pull it inside and die."

"You and your visions. Can you not celebrate for one day?"

"I cannot celebrate what I know is coming."

Paris cursed and walked away. Cassandra stayed in her room. Through the window, she could see the Greek camp. They were burning Achilles' body. The fire rose high and orange, and the Greeks gathered around it, and their mourning would turn to rage, and the rage would turn to strategy, and the strategy would destroy everything.

She lay down on her bed and stared at the ceiling. She did not sleep.

Chapter 33: The Retreat

The Greeks began to burn their camp in the eleventh year. Tents went up in flames. Supplies were loaded onto ships. The fleet prepared to sail.

The Trojans watched from the walls, barely daring to believe. Scouts reported that the Greeks were boarding. The beach was chaos. Ships were being dragged into the water. The siege was over.

Priam declared a celebration. Wine was distributed. The temples were crowded with grateful worshippers. The city breathed for the first time in a decade.

Cassandra stood on the wall and watched the Greek ships pull away from the shore. They looked small against the horizon, black specks on the blue. She waited for the vision to change. It did not.

"They are not gone," she said.

The sentry beside her laughed. "My eyes tell me otherwise, princess."

"Your eyes are lying."

"Then where are they?"

"Behind Tenedos. They will return tonight."

The sentry shook his head. "You are the only person in Troy who sees defeat in victory."

"I am the only person in Troy who sees."

She left him on the wall and walked through the celebrating city. People danced in the streets. They kissed and drank and embraced. They had survived. They had won.

She walked to the Scaean Gate and looked at the plain. It was empty except for one thing. A structure, large and wooden, standing where the Greek camp had been.

The horse.

Chapter 34: The Horse

It was beautiful. That was the worst part. The horse was a masterpiece of craftsmanship, its flanks smooth, its mane carved in flowing lines, its eyes painted with such skill that they seemed to follow you. It stood fifty feet high on a platform of stone, gleaming in the afternoon light.

Trojans gathered around it. They touched its legs. They marveled at its size. They argued about what to do with it.

"Burn it," Cassandra said.

The crowd turned. They knew her. They knew her reputation. The mood shifted.

"Burn it," she said again. "It is a trap. The Greeks built it to destroy us."

A man stepped forward. He was a carpenter, his hands calloused, his eyes skeptical. "Burn a gift to the gods? That is bad luck."

"It is not a gift. It is a siege engine."

"It has no wheels. No ram. No weapons. It is a statue."

"It is hollow. It holds soldiers."

The crowd murmured. Some laughed. Others looked uncertain. The carpenter shook his head.

"Your madness is famous, princess. But we are tired of listening to doom."

He turned his back on her. The crowd followed his lead. They gathered around the horse, marveling at its beauty, discussing how to bring it through the gates.

Cassandra stood alone. The horse loomed above her. She could hear the men inside. Not with her ears. With her sight. They were there, waiting, packed into the darkness like seeds in a pod.

She laughed. It was the only sound she could make.

Chapter 35: The Spy

Sinon was brought before Priam in chains. He was a small man with a weak chin and desperate eyes. The Greeks had left him behind, they said. A deserter. A man they had abandoned.

Priam questioned him in the great hall. The court listened. Sinon wept and begged and told a story of a Greek who had been wronged by Odysseus, a man who had been left as a sacrifice, a victim of his own people's cruelty.

"The horse," he said. "It is an offering to Athena. If you destroy it, the goddess will turn against you. If you bring it into your city, she will bless you."

Cassandra stepped forward. "He lies."

Sinon looked at her. His eyes flickered.

"The Greeks left him here to tell this story," she said. "He is a soldier. A spy. Look at his hands. Those are not the hands of a deserter. Those are the hands of a man who has held a spear."

Sinon hid his hands behind his back. "I do not know this woman."

"She is my daughter," Priam said. "A prophet."

"A curse," Sinon said quickly. "I have heard of her. The Trojans say she brings bad luck."

"Bad luck does not make her wrong."

Priam looked between them. The court watched. The torches flickered.

"I will decide in the morning," Priam said. "Take the Greek to the cells."

Sinon was dragged away. He looked back at Cassandra and his eyes held no fear. She had seen that look before. It was the look of a man who knew his story had been believed.

Chapter 36: The Serpents

Laocoon acted where Cassandra could not. The priest of Poseidon ran from the temple with a spear in his hand. He pushed through the crowd and thrust the weapon into the horse's flank.

The bronze tip scraped wood. The horse did not burn. It did not bleed. It stood hollow and silent.

"Fools!" Laocoon shouted. "Do not trust this gift! I fear Greeks even when they bring gifts!"

The crowd fell silent. For a moment, Laocoon had them. For a moment, they doubted.

Then the serpents came.

They rose from the sea, two of them, enormous, their scales gleaming wet in the sunlight. They moved across the plain with terrifying speed. The crowd screamed and scattered. Laocoon's sons were on the beach, playing in the sand, and the serpents took them first.

Laocoon ran to save his sons. The serpents wrapped around the boys. They wrapped around Laocoon. They squeezed until the priest's ribs cracked, until his sons stopped moving, until the three of them lay dead on the sand.

The serpents slithered back into the sea.

The Trojans watched in horror. Then, slowly, they turned back to the horse.

"The goddess," someone whispered. "She killed him for striking her offering."

"The horse must be sacred."

"It must enter the city."

Cassandra watched it happen. She had seen it before. The serpents. The deaths. The way fear turned into certainty. She had seen it all and she could not stop it.

The crowd began to pull the horse toward the gates.

Chapter 37: The Gate

The Trojans brought the horse to the Scaean Gate. It did not fit. The gate was too narrow, the horse too wide. They discussed dismantling part of the wall.

Cassandra threw herself before the wheels.

"You will not bring it through," she said. "You will die. We will all die. This is the end of Troy."

The crowd stopped. They looked at her with pity. The prophet, raving again. The madwoman who could only see the worst.

"Move," the carpenter said.

"I will not."

"Then we will move you."

He grabbed her arm. She pulled free. Two more men took her by the shoulders and dragged her to the side of the road. She fought them. She screamed. She clawed and bit and kicked.

They held her down until she stopped struggling.

"Your visions are poison," the carpenter said. "Tonight we celebrate. Tomorrow we rebuild. You can watch from your room."

He released her. She lay in the dust as the crowd resumed their work. The horse scraped through the gate. Stones fell from the arch. The horse entered Troy.

Cassandra lay in the road and tasted dust and defeat. She had done everything she could. She had screamed until her voice broke. She had fought until her strength failed. And still the horse came through.

She closed her eyes. The fire had never been closer.

Chapter 38: The Night

The city celebrated. Torches blazed on every corner. Wine flowed in the streets. The Trojans sang and danced and embraced. They had endured ten years of war and they had won.

Cassandra sat in her room in the dark. She had not lit her lamp. She did not need light to see what was coming.

She listened to the celebration. The laughter. The music. The shouts of joy. It drifted up from the streets like smoke, and she breathed it in, and it tasted like ash.

She paced. She sat. She stood by the window and watched the horse's silhouette against the stars. It stood in the middle of the courtyard, huge and dark, patient as death.

She could hear them. The men inside. They were silent, but she could feel their breathing, their waiting, their hands on their weapons. When the city fell asleep, they would wake.

She went to the door. She considered going to the palace, to her father, to make one final plea. But what would she say? She had already said everything. There were no words left.

She went back to the window. The celebration was winding down. The streets were emptying. Troy was going to sleep.

"Hector," she whispered into the darkness. "Andromache. Father. I am sorry."

No one answered. There was no one left to answer.

Chapter 39: The Beacon

Cassandra made a decision. If she could not stop the destruction, she could at least warn the people. She could give them a chance to run.

She took a torch from the wall and climbed to the highest tower. The city spread below her, dark and quiet. The horse stood in the courtyard, a black shape against the gray stones. The sea was invisible in the darkness, but she knew the Greek fleet was out there, waiting for the signal.

She lit the beacon.

The flame rose into the night. A signal. A warning. She stood beside it, hoping someone would see, hoping someone would understand.

But the signal she lit was the one the Greeks were waiting for.

From the sea, a light answered. Then another. Then a dozen.

The Greek fleet was returning.

Cassandra stared at the answering lights and understood what she had done. The beacon she had meant as a warning had been seen by the wrong eyes. She had told the Greeks that the city was asleep.

She dropped the torch. It fell, sparking, into the darkness below.

"No," she whispered.

But it was done. The ships were coming. The horse was opening.

She had lit the fire herself.

Chapter 40: The Belly

The horse opened. A panel in its flank swung outward, and armed men spilled into the courtyard. They moved fast, practiced, going straight for the gates. The first Trojans to see them died without a sound.

Cassandra watched from the tower. She saw the Greeks fan out through the streets. She saw them cut down the guards at the gates. She saw the gates swing open.

And then she saw the Greek army pour through.

Thousands of them. Torches. Swords. Battle cries. They flooded into Troy like water through a broken dam. The city woke to fire and steel.

Cassandra did not run. She stood on the tower and watched the city she had loved burn exactly as she had seen it burn a thousand times. The flames were the right color. The smoke smelled the way it always did in her dreams. Every detail was perfect.

She thought she should feel something. Terror. Grief. Rage. But she felt only a terrible stillness, the calm of a story reaching its end.

She had spent her whole life seeing this moment. Now it was here. There was nothing left to see.

She turned and walked down the tower stairs. The Greeks were in the palace. She could hear the screams.

She walked toward them.

Chapter 41: The Temple

Cassandra ran to the temple of Athena. It was the only place she could think of, the only sanctuary she had ever known. Theano was there, barricading the door with the other priestesses.

"Let me in," Cassandra said.

Theano looked at her through the gap in the door. Her face was white, her robes torn.

"You led them here," Theano said.

"I tried to warn you."

"Your warning was the beacon. I saw you light it."

"It was meant to wake the city."

"It woke the Greeks."

They stared at each other through the gap. The screams grew louder. The fire was spreading. The temple shook with the impact of fighting nearby.

"I did what I could," Cassandra said.

"You did what your visions told you. And we are all dead."

Theano stepped back from the door. She did not open it. She left Cassandra standing in the street, exposed, alone.

Cassandra turned. The Greeks were coming. She could see their torches at the end of the street. She had nowhere to go.

She pressed her back against the temple door. If she was going to die, she would die at the altar of the goddess who had never loved her. It was fitting.

The Greeks reached her. Their armor was red in the firelight. Their faces were hard with killing.

The one in front grabbed her arm.

Chapter 42: The Altar

Ajax the Lesser dragged her into the temple. He was a big man, thick-necked, with small eyes and a mouth that smiled too easily. He pulled her past the altar, past the statue of Athena, into the inner sanctum.

Theano and the other priestesses cowered against the wall. Ajax ignored them. He threw Cassandra to the floor.

"This is the prophet," he said. "The one who saw us coming."

Cassandra looked up at him. "I saw you. I saw your death too. You die at sea. Poseidon drowns you for what you do here."

Ajax laughed. "Even now, you warned us. And even now, no one listens."

He reached for her. She did not fight. There was no point. She had known this moment. She had seen this man's face in her visions. She had known his hands would touch her. She had known she could not stop it.

She closed her eyes. She retreated into the visions. She saw the fire. She saw the sea. She saw the storm that would take him, the lightning, the waves, the dark water closing over his head.

She held onto that image while his hands held her down. The storm was coming. It would find him. She had seen it.

When it was over, she lay on the cold stone floor and listened to the temple burn.

Chapter 43: The Fire

The city burned. The flames consumed everything — the markets, the temples, the palaces, the homes. The walls that had stood for a thousand years glowed red with heat. The towers collapsed. The streets ran with fire and blood.

Cassandra walked through it. She did not know where she was going. Her feet carried her through streets she had known since childhood, streets that were now unrecognizable. The landmarks were gone. The houses were shells. The bodies lay where they had fallen.

She passed the baker's shop where Xanthe had worked. The oven was cold, the roof caved in. She passed the well where she had drawn water a thousand times. It was choked with debris.

She reached the Scaean Gate. The horse still stood where they had left it, its wooden flank dark with soot. The Greeks had used it and abandoned it. It stood empty now, a monument to their deception.

She touched its leg. The wood was warm.

"I told them," she said. "I told them all."

The horse did not answer.

She leaned her forehead against the wood and closed her eyes. Behind her lids, the fire burned. It had always burned. It would always burn.

She had seen this moment since she was sixteen years old. She had screamed and fought and begged. She had done everything she could. And here she was, alive, standing beside the wooden beast that had killed her city.

She had survived the fall. She did not know if that was a mercy or a punishment.

Chapter 44: The King

Priam died at the altar of Zeus. The Greeks found him there, an old man in his finest robes, his hands raised in prayer. Neoptolemus, son of Achilles, cut him down.

Cassandra heard his cry from across the city. She was in the courtyard of the palace when the sound reached her. She stopped. She knew that voice. She had known it her whole life.

She did not run to him. There was no point. He was already dead.

She walked instead to the great hall. The bodies of her brothers lay where they had fallen. Deiphobus. Polites. Helenus. She stepped over them without stopping. There would be time for grief later. There would be nothing but time later.

The throne room was empty. Priam's throne stood at the far end, untouched by fire or violence. She walked to it and sat down.

It was wrong. She was a woman. A princess. A prophet no one had believed. She had no right to this seat. But there was no one left to claim it.

She sat in her father's throne and looked at the empty room. The torches flickered. The smoke drifted through the broken windows. The sounds of destruction continued outside, muffled by stone.

"I am the queen of ashes," she said.

Her voice echoed. No one answered.

She sat there until the Greeks came for her.

Chapter 45: The Captive

They found Andromache with Astyanax in her arms. The Greeks pried the child from her grip. She fought them. She bit and clawed and screamed. They threw her to the ground and took the boy.

Odysseus himself gave the order. The child of Hector could not be allowed to live. He was a prince of Troy, a symbol. If he grew, he would raise an army. He would seek revenge.

Andromache watched from her knees as her son was carried to the wall. The soldiers stood him on the parapet. He was three years old. He did not understand what was happening. He looked for his mother and did not find her.

Cassandra was brought to the courtyard in chains. She saw Andromache's face. She saw the soldiers on the wall. She knew what was coming.

"Look away," she told Andromache.

"I cannot."

"Then remember him as he was. Not this."

Andromache did not look away. She watched as her son was thrown from the wall. She watched his small body fall. She watched it hit the stones below.

She did not scream. She did not cry. She simply stopped. Her face went blank. Her eyes went dead. She was still breathing, but the woman Cassandra had known was gone.

The soldiers bound her hands and led her toward the beach. She went without resistance. She did not look back at the city. She did not look at Cassandra.

She looked at nothing.

Chapter 46: The Prophecy

Agamemnon's tent was the largest in the Greek camp. It was made of leather and cloth, decorated with captured Trojan banners. The king of Mycenae sat inside on a folding chair, a cup of wine in his hand, a smile on his face.

They brought Cassandra before him. Her chains clinked. His smile widened.

"So," he said. "The prophet."

"I am."

"I have heard much about you. The Trojans say you speak only doom."

"I speak only truth."

"And what truth do you have for me, prophet?"

Cassandra looked at him. She looked past him. She saw the homecoming that waited. She saw the red carpet. She saw the ax.

"You return to Mycenae in glory," she said. "Your wife greets you at the door. She spreads a carpet of purple for you to walk on. You step into your house. You step into your bath."

Agamemnon's smile faltered. "What is wrong with that?"

"She spreads the carpet. She draws your bath. And when you are clean and naked and unarmed, she drops the net over you."

The tent went silent. Agamemnon set down his cup.

"Your wife," Cassandra said. "Clytemnestra. She has taken your cousin Aegisthus as her lover. Together they will kill you in your bath. I see the knife. I see the blood. I see your face when you realize she has waited ten years for this moment."

Agamemnon stood. His face was pale. "You lie."

"I have never lied. Ask anyone. It is my curse."

She was dragged from the tent. Behind her, she heard him shouting. She did not care. She had told him. Whether he believed her was not her concern.

She had done her duty. She had spoken her truth. It was all she had ever done.

Chapter 47: The Sea

The Greek fleet sailed the next morning. Cassandra stood at the prow of Agamemnon's ship, her hands bound, her eyes fixed on the horizon. Behind her, Troy burned.

She watched the smoke rise. She watched the flames eat the towers. She watched the walls she had walked every morning grow smaller as the ship carried her away.

The sea was calm. The oars rose and fell. The sailors sang as they worked, their voices rough and cheerful. They were going home. They had won.

Cassandra did not sing. She stood at the rail and watched her city die.

She thought about Hector, falling to Achilles' spear. She thought about Andromache, her eyes gone dead. She thought about her father, cut down at the altar. She thought about the fire, spreading through the streets, consuming everything she had loved.

She thought about Apollo. The god had given her the sight. He had cursed her with truth. And now, as she watched Troy disappear into the smoke, she understood the full weight of what he had done.

He had given her the gift of knowing. And he had made sure that knowing would never be enough.

The smoke faded. The sea stretched endless in every direction. She was alone, surrounded by enemies, sailing toward a death she had already seen.

She did not weep. She had no tears left.

Chapter 48: The Queen

Clytemnestra received them in the great hall of Mycenae. She was a tall woman with red hair and cold eyes. She wore a dress of deep purple and a necklace of gold. She smiled when she saw her husband.

Agamemnon stepped forward. He had not told anyone what Cassandra had said. He had tried to put it out of his mind.

"Welcome home, my king," Clytemnestra said.

"Thank you, my queen."

She embraced him. She kissed his cheek. She took his hand and led him into the palace.

Behind them, Cassandra was brought forward. Clytemnestra's eyes found her.

"This is the prophet?"

"She is."

"The one who speaks only truth?"

"She claims."

Clytemnestra studied her. Her smile did not reach her eyes. "Then she will be useful. A prophet in the palace. What stories she must have to tell."

Cassandra looked at the queen. She saw the plan in Clytemnestra's mind as clearly as she saw the stone walls of the palace. The bath. The net. The knife. Every detail was in place.

"You should kill me now," Cassandra said. "Before I tell you what I see."

Clytemnestra's smile tightened. "Tomorrow. Tonight, we celebrate."

"Tonight, you prepare."

The queen's hand went to her throat. For a moment, her composure cracked. Then she laughed, a false sound that echoed in the stone hall.

"Take her to the cells," she said.

Guards took Cassandra's arms. As she was led away, she looked back at Clytemnestra. The queen was watching her with an expression that had nothing to do with hospitality.

Chapter 49: The Bath

Agamemnon went to his bath alone. The palace was quiet. The servants had been dismissed. The guards had been reassigned. Everything had been arranged.

Cassandra sat in her cell and listened. She heard the water running. She heard the king singing softly to himself. She heard footsteps in the corridor above.

She closed her eyes. She did not need them.

She saw the bath. The steam rising. Agamemnon's body, relaxed, trusting. The door opening. Clytemnestra standing in the doorway, a knife in her hand. Aegisthus behind her, holding the net.

The king did not have time to scream. The net fell. The knife rose. The water turned red.

Cassandra opened her eyes. Her cell was dark. The sounds of the palace continued above her — footsteps, whispers, the clink of metal. They were cleaning up. They were hiding the body. They were preparing the story.

She had told him. She had warned him. He had not listened.

She wondered if this was what Apollo felt. To see clearly, to speak truth, and to watch the world ignore you. To know exactly what would happen and to understand that knowing changed nothing.

She sat in the dark and waited for her turn.

It came sooner than she expected.

Chapter 50: The Final Vision

They came for her at midnight. Clytemnestra herself stood at the cell door, a torch in one hand, a knife in the other.

"The prophet," the queen said. "The one who knows everything."

"I know what you are going to do."

"Then you know there is no point in begging."

"I have never begged."

Clytemnestra opened the cell. She stepped inside. The torchlight cast her shadow huge against the wall.

"Before you kill me," Cassandra said, "let me tell you what I see after."

"After what?"

"After the knife. After the blood. After the darkness."

Clytemnestra hesitated. The blade wavered.

"I see your son," Cassandra said. "Orestes. He is in exile now, but he will return. He will walk into this palace. He will stand where you are standing. And he will kill you."

"You lie."

"I never lie. It is my curse. He will kill you, Clytemnestra. Not because he hates you. Because the gods demand it. Because the blood of his father cries out from the ground."

Clytemnestra raised the knife. Her hand was shaking.

"Kill me," Cassandra said. "It will not change what is coming. Your death is already written. Mine is already written. We are all just walking through the words that were set down before we were born."

The knife came down.

But Cassandra did not feel it.

She was already gone. She was standing on the walls of Troy, looking out at the sea. The sun was rising. The water was gold. The city was whole. Her brothers were alive. Her father sat on his throne. Andromache held her son in her arms.

She saw it. Not as a vision of the future. As a memory of a world that might have been. A world where someone had listened. A world where the fire had never come.

It was beautiful. It was heartbreaking.

"One more step," Andromache said from beside her. "One more step and you will see what comes after."

"What comes after?"

"The new walls. The new city. The people who will build on our bones."

Cassandra looked at the horizon. The sun continued to rise. The sea continued to shine.

She took the step.
