# The Rain Archive

## Overview

In the dry basin city of Las Lomas, rain is not only weather. It is law, debt, ration, memory, and title. Municipal archivist Mara Venn tends eighty years of handwritten gauges and field sheets until a records clerk dies beside an empty rain gauge and the digital archive begins contradicting the jars of roof water citizens bring to her desk. The official record says July was dry. The cisterns say otherwise. As Mara follows ledger scars, checksum errors, canal valves, and erased neighborhoods, she uncovers a water-rights fraud large enough to reorder the city under emergency powers. The fight becomes a question of custody: who gets to say whether rain happened, and what a city owes the people who measured it honestly.

## Story Engines

In a drought-struck near-future city, municipal archivist Mara Venn discovers that rainfall records have been altered to transfer public water rights to a private citrus consortium. With a mechanic, a reporter, a farmer, and a compromised database engineer, she must prove that the archive itself has been made to lie before emergency powers seal every record that can save the city.

## Key Themes

- Evidence needs custody, not only conviction.
- Public memory can be stolen by quiet administrative edits.
- Technical expertise matters most when it admits its limits.
- Ordinary witnesses carry facts institutions prefer to smooth away.
