Chapter 1: The Duplicated Umbrella

The umbrella sat in a wire evidence tray on Maren's desk, dripping onto the intake form beneath it. Black, wood-handled, nothing special. The Pane Three version had been found in the Caldwell district post office; the Pane Four version had been found at the same post office fourteen minutes later by a different postal worker who didn't know the first one existed.

Two umbrellas, one address, no visible seam to account for it.

Maren pressed her thumb into the spine of the tray to stop it rocking. The hinge clicked once and settled. She pulled the intake form free, blotted it against her sleeve, and read it again even though she'd already read it twice. The postal workers had been separated and processed. No exposure. The Pane Four umbrella had been re-routed; someone from the Pane Four sub-office would collect it this afternoon. Her job was the Pane Three one — provenance, leak-source, closure.

Routine. Good. Routine was what she wanted.

The office around her was Bureau standard: low ceilings, fluorescent strips, the smell of old coffee and the particular chemical tang of seam-sealant that soaked everything in the building eventually, even the upholstery. Six reconciler desks in two rows. Three were empty. Ferris was in, reading a file. Okura was on the phone. Nobody looked up when Maren reached for her coat.

"Vos."

Director Quist's assistant, a young man named Pell, stood in the corridor doorway with a tablet. He had the expression of someone who had been told to deliver news they did not fully understand.

"You're getting a partner today," he said. "Orientation finished an hour ago. Director Quist said to tell you he's waiting in Room Four."

"I know," Maren said.

She'd been told three times. Once by the assignment memo, once by Quist directly, and once by Ferris, who had found it worth mentioning with the particular tone of someone who thought Maren working alone was a problem that needed solving. She hadn't responded to any of the three.

Room Four was a briefing room with one window that looked onto the building's interior courtyard, where nothing ever happened. The man waiting was seated with a tablet open and a pen held loosely in one hand, not reading, just thinking. He was younger-looking than his file — thirty-eight but carrying it lightly, good posture, a quality of attention that she clocked before she clocked anything else. He stood when she came in.

"Idris Lan," he said. "Junior reconciler, Grade Two."

"Maren Vos. We have a case."

He looked at her for a moment before he reached for his coat. He didn't say anything about introductions or a handshake or the fact that she hadn't offered one. That was something.

They took the Bureau vehicle to Caldwell. Idris sat in the passenger seat with the case file open on his tablet, reading. She watched him in peripheral vision — the way his eyes moved, whether he flipped back to recheck things. He did. He read it twice before he put it down.

"The postal worker who found it," he said. "Callum Drest. He'd never seen a seam?"

"Most people haven't."

"He just thought it was — what, someone left a second umbrella?"

"He thought the first postal worker misplaced it and brought it back to the front. That's what most people think. They make the story fit what they know."

"So the seam opened in the post office itself."

"That's what we're going to find out."

He nodded. He looked out the window at the Caldwell streets — the grey mid-morning light, the shop fronts, a woman walking a dog at the particular resigned pace of someone who'd been out too long in damp weather. He had an expression she didn't quite have a name for. Not tourist-wonder at the city. Something more restrained than that.

The post office was a narrow building on a corner, red brick, a bell over the door that rang when they pushed it open. Callum Drest was behind the counter, a man in his fifties with the slightly bruised look of someone who'd spent the morning answering the same questions. He nodded at Maren with recognition. She'd called ahead.

"The back room," she said. "Show my partner."

Idris followed Drest through the counter gate. Maren stayed in front and looked at the floor near the umbrella stand. The wood was old, scarred, unobjectionable. She crouched and looked from a lower angle. There — a faint discoloration in the grain, a strip maybe four centimeters wide running at a diagonal, the particular oxidized look she knew well. A seam trace. Already closed, already cold.

She took out her gauge, a slim device the size of a pen, and ran it along the trace. The reading was flat. Fully closed. She photographed the trace, logged the coordinates, marked it in the case file as a passive leak — a seam that had opened briefly, likely overnight, long enough for the umbrella to duplicate, then resealed on its own.

The case was effectively done.

Idris came back from the storeroom with Drest and stood looking at the floor where she'd marked. He crouched the same way she had. He didn't have a gauge yet — Grade Two reconcilers were issued them after first field assessment.

"Can you see it?" she said.

"The discoloration. Yes."

"What does it tell you?"

He thought for a moment. "Brief opening. Hours, maybe. Low-volume. Nothing else came through — if it had been open longer we'd have more duplicates."

"Correct." She stood. "The post office runs twenty-four-hour sorting in the back. Whoever left the umbrella did so around midnight according to the duty log. The seam would have opened during the night shift and closed before morning."

"What opened it?"

"That's what we'd need a seam-map to determine. Could be a natural stress point in the building — old post offices sometimes sit on nodes. Could be something in the Pane Four substrate." She capped the gauge. "Or it's nothing. Sometimes membranes flex."

"Do they flex on their own?"

She looked at him. He held the question neutrally, not pushing, just wanting the true answer.

"Less often than we used to think," she said.

She filed the passive-leak documentation with Drest, gave him the standard non-disclosure acknowledgment to sign, and walked Idris back to the car. He was quiet for the first two blocks.

"So we seal it?" he said.

"Already sealed itself."

"And we're done?"

"We write the report. We close the case. We wait for the next one."

He turned the tablet over in his hands. Outside the window the Caldwell streets continued their ordinary business — a café, a flower stall, a man reading a paper under an awning. Nothing to see.

"Does it always feel like this?" Idris said.

"Like what."

"Like you walked into something large and walked out without touching it."

Maren started the car. "No," she said. "Sometimes you touch it."

Chapter 2: Orientation, Continued

Bureau protocol required that a new reconciler's first three field assignments be accompanied by an orientation debrief — a conversation in which the senior partner recorded observations about the junior's fieldwork and the junior asked formally authorized questions. This was designed, in Maren's experience, to serve neither party particularly well. It created conversation for conversation's sake, and she had never found much use for that.

She ran the debrief in the Bureau canteen at noon, because it needed to happen and the canteen was close.

"Twelve questions," she said, sliding the debrief form across the table. "You can ask them now or save them for after cases two and three. Most people split."

Idris picked up the form and read it the way he'd read the case file — attentively, checking twice. "These are pre-written questions."

"Yes."

"Who wrote them?"

"The training division. About thirty years ago, from what I can tell. They haven't been revised."

He set the form down. "Can I ask my own?"

"The twelve are required. You can ask whatever you want on top of them."

He nodded. He looked at the canteen — the round tables, the long window with its view of the courtyard, two analysts from the cartography division eating sandwiches without speaking. There was a quality of absorption about the Bureau that she'd stopped noticing years ago. The canteen smelled like soup and the particular institutional quiet of a place that ran on duty rather than enthusiasm.

"Tell me about the seam stations," he said.

She walked him through it. The seam stations were fixed transit points — doorway-sized apertures, mechanically maintained, certified by Cyriac Holm's cartography team. They were the only authorized points of crossing between panes. You filed a transit permit forty-eight hours in advance except in emergency cases, which required Director Quist's sign-off. You carried a tether — a signal device that kept your Pane Three position live in the Bureau's tracking system. You had a return window. You came back.

"What's on the other side?" he said.

"Halloway," she said.

"I know. But — what's different?"

She considered how to answer this. It was the question every new reconciler asked, and it was the hardest one, because the answer was: very little, and everything.

"Same city," she said. "Same streets, mostly. Same buildings, same general shape of things. Small differences. A building that burned down here is still standing there. A shop that opened last year has been open for five years there. Someone who died here is alive there."

She said the last one the way she said all of it, in the same register. He didn't miss it.

"That's the hardest part," he said. It wasn't a question.

"The hardest part is the paperwork," she said. "Drink your coffee."

He smiled — not charmed, not deflected, just choosing not to push. That was also something. She revised her estimation of him slightly upward and said nothing about it.

"The twelve questions," he said, pulling the form back. "I'll ask them now."

He worked through them systematically. Most of them were logistical — transit permit procedure, tether protocols, how to handle an unauthorized crossing (report, contain, document; never attempt solo retrieval). He asked them without impatience and she answered without padding. It took twenty minutes. The soup came and went. He asked the twelfth question, which was about emergency seam sealing, and then he set the form down.

"The discoloration in the post office floor," he said. "You knew what it was immediately."

"Yes."

"Is that experience or something you can teach?"

"Both. You'll learn the visual markers in training. Experience speeds it up. After a few years you see them before you consciously look."

"How many years?"

"Three, for most people. Some people never fully get it." She paused. "You looked at the right angle. Most new reconcilers look straight down. You crouched."

"You crouched first."

"I did."

"So I copied you."

"Yes. That was correct." She stood and collected the debrief form. "Case two is this afternoon if the log has anything. If not, tomorrow morning."

He stood also and picked up his coat with the same economy of motion she'd noticed earlier. "What was the case eight years ago?" he said. "The one you were thinking about at the post office."

She stopped. The fluorescent strip above them hummed once and steadied.

"The form doesn't have a question about that," she said.

"I know." He held her gaze without apology. "You don't have to answer it."

"No," she said. "I don't."

She walked out of the canteen first and he followed at a professional distance, which was the appropriate distance, and they said nothing else about it. The Bureau building smelled of old coffee and sealant and the pressed-down weight of things nobody talked about, and Maren moved through it the way she always did — efficiently, with no wasted motion, checking the case log as she walked.

There was a new entry. A woman named Orda Rein, declared dead in Pane Three six weeks ago, had been identified at her own memorial service by two attendees who knew her well.

Maren entered it as Case Two and sent the assignment to Idris's tablet without comment.

Chapter 3: The Living Dead

Orda Rein's memorial service had been held at the Rein family house in the Selwick district, two weeks after her death — which had been, by all records, a cardiac event at fifty-three, unexpected, at home. Fifty-seven people had attended. Two of them had pulled the widower aside and told him his wife was standing in the doorway.

The widower, a man named Tor Rein, had not looked. He said later that he had decided not to, and Maren found this the most believable detail in the entire report.

She and Idris drove to Selwick the next morning. The Rein house was on a residential street — brick, modest, a garden that had been let go slightly, the way gardens go when someone in the house has been ill or grieving. Tor Rein answered the door himself. He was a compact man in his late fifties with the careful courtesy of someone who had been answering difficult questions for weeks and had chosen to keep answering them.

"You're from the Bureau," he said.

"Tessera Bureau, yes. Senior Reconciler Vos and my partner Lan. This is about the incident at the memorial."

"I know what it's about." He stood back and let them in.

The sitting room was tidy in the particular way of rooms that had been tidied by someone who needed something to do. Tea was already made. He hadn't been expecting them this early but he'd made tea anyway.

"The two people who saw her," Maren said, accepting a cup she didn't need. "Wallis Prek and Genna Fors. We spoke to them yesterday. They both said she was standing in the doorway to the back hall. That she looked — their word — present."

"Not like a ghost," Tor Rein said.

"No. Not like a ghost." She set her cup down. "Were you aware at the time?"

"I felt something. I chose not to look." He folded his hands. "Is that foolish?"

"No," Idris said, and it was the right answer, said in the right way, and Maren let him have it.

"What happens now?" Tor said.

"We're going to determine where your wife crossed from and how she got back. In all likelihood she's in Pane Four — the adjacent version of Halloway — confused about where she is and possibly about when she got there. This happens occasionally with seam events. People cross without awareness, sometimes without any physical sensation at all."

"She's alive," he said.

"Her Pane Four counterpart is alive, yes. That's what the witnesses saw. The seam was thin enough at the memorial site to create a visual overlay — her Pane Four presence briefly appeared in Pane Three space." She watched him process this. "The person in Pane Four is not your wife. She's a version of your wife who lives in a city that is almost identical to this one and who, as far as we can tell, is alive and well and does not know any of this happened."

Tor was quiet for a moment. "Do they know each other? The two of them?"

"They don't. They've always been separate."

"But they're the same person."

"They're variant persons," Maren said. "It's a meaningful distinction."

He nodded slowly. He picked up his cup and set it back down. "What do you need from me?"

"The location of the memorial. Specifically the back hallway where she was seen. We need to check it for seam traces."

He took them through. The back hallway was narrow, with a coat rack and an old mirror and a door to the garden. Maren ran the gauge along the baseboard and found it immediately — a live trace this time, faint but warm, a seam that had opened and partially closed but left a hair-thin stress line in the wall's substrate.

She photographed it and gave Idris the gauge to read. He crouched and ran it the way she had and read the numbers correctly on the first pass.

"That's a live trace," he said.

"Yes."

"Which means—"

"Which means this location is a potential spontaneous seam site. We'll need a cartography assessment." She flagged it in the log. "We'll also need to file a Pane Four notification so their Bureau can check on Orda Rein over there. Make sure she's unaffected."

"Does she have to know?"

"The Pane Four Orda Rein? Not unless she was affected. If the membrane was thin enough for her image to bleed through, she may have had a headache. A sense of displacement. Sometimes they notice; mostly they don't."

Tor Rein stood in the hallway behind them, listening. He had the look of a man learning a language he hadn't known existed. Maren turned and handed him a Bureau notification card — standard form, contact details, the minimal script about confidentiality that they were required by procedure to deliver.

"If anything changes," she said. "If you see something else."

"What do I do," he said, "with the fact that she's alive somewhere?"

Maren looked at him. The hallway was small and the question was large and she had a version of it herself that she kept in a locked box behind her sternum.

"You live in the pane you're in," she said. "It's the only one that belongs to you."

She said it the way she always said it — evenly, as policy. It was policy. It was also, for her, the whole difficulty.

Chapter 4: The Incomplete Log

The transit log for the Selwick district seam station showed a blank.

This was not absence of data — the system differentiated between no-crossing-occurred and data-not-recorded. This was the second kind: a gap where a record should be, the system noting its own incompleteness in the flat bureaucratic language of a corrupted timestamp. Maren read it three times before she flagged it.

She did this at her desk at seven in the morning, before Idris arrived, with a second coffee going cold beside her keyboard. The office was quiet. Ferris's desk had last night's case file still open on it; he'd been on the Linton bridge anomaly, which was taking longer than expected. Okura wasn't in yet.

The gap in the Selwick transit log was thirty-two minutes wide, centered on the night of Orda Rein's memorial.

Someone had crossed. The tether system had a record of the aperture opening — she found that in the station's raw feed, three layers below the standard log — but the identification data, the permit number, the returning transit signature, all of it had been stripped. Not failed to record. Stripped.

She sat back and looked at the ceiling.

Stripping a transit record required a Bureau-level access code. There were twelve people in the building with that access. She did not write them down; she wasn't ready to make this official yet. She pulled up the case file on Orda Rein and added a flag: seam station log anomaly, Selwick, cross-reference requested. She addressed it to Cyriac Holm in cartography because he was the only person she trusted to read it without immediately elevating it to Quist.

Idris arrived at ten past seven. He looked at her as he sat down.

"You've been here a while," he said.

"Reading the Rein file."

"Find something?"

She turned her monitor slightly toward him and let him read the log gap. He read it with the same two-pass attention he gave everything.

"Someone cleaned the record," he said.

"It looks that way."

"Which means the Selwick seam was used deliberately. Someone crossed, used Orda Rein's memorial as a cover or a coincidence, and then removed the record."

"That's one interpretation."

"Is there another?"

"System corruption. It happens rarely but it happens. The raw aperture feed surviving while the upper log is stripped is unusual for a system error, though." She closed the window. "I've flagged it for Cyriac."

"Should we escalate to Quist?"

"After Cyriac." She picked up her coffee, found it cold, and set it back. "I want to know what the cartography charts say about the Selwick station before I sit down with the Director. If it's a known stress node, that changes the picture."

Idris accepted this without argument. He opened his own terminal and began pulling the files she'd pre-assigned for the day — the standard checks on the two Rein witnesses, the notification to Pane Four. He was methodical and he didn't ask questions he didn't need to ask, and Maren worked alongside him in the useful silence of two people who had decided to trust each other provisionally.

At nine she got a message from Cyriac: *Come see me today. Not urgent. Just — come.*

That was Cyriac's version of urgent.

Chapter 5: The Tampered Station

Before the cartography visit, Idris asked to go back to the Caldwell post office seam station.

He framed it as wanting to practice his gauge readings on a closed trace. This was a legitimate training exercise. Maren said yes because she was curious what he was actually after.

The Caldwell seam station was a sub-street installation, accessed through a maintenance hatch in the alley behind the post office. It was one of the older stations — the equipment dated from the Bureau's third-decade expansion, heavy solid-state units bolted to the wall with brass fittings that nobody used anymore. The new stations used polymer housings. The Caldwell one smelled of rust and the particular damp of underground rooms.

Idris ran his gauge over the trace she'd found yesterday. His readings were accurate. Then he moved to the station's main aperture housing and ran the gauge along the frame.

He stopped. "This is odd."

She came and looked. The aperture housing's calibration plate — the panel that recorded the station's last scheduled maintenance — showed a date two months ago. Standard. But along the lower housing, where the sealant joined the wall, there was a thin line of new compound over old compound. The colors were slightly different. Someone had applied fresh sealant recently.

"That's not from maintenance," Idris said.

"No. Maintenance uses the Bureau compound. This is—" She took a sample with her kit. "I'll need to have it analyzed. But visually, this is consumer-grade sealant. The kind you buy."

"Someone fixed a gap themselves."

"Or created a gap and then covered it."

He crouched and looked at the housing from below. "If you opened the housing without a maintenance permit, you'd have to seal it yourself to hide the tampering."

"Correct."

"Could you forge an aperture? Using the housing mechanics, could you—"

"A trained engineer, yes. The apertures are the physical scaffolding of the seam. The seam itself is in the substrate — you can't create one where the geology doesn't support it. But you can widen a stress point. You can coax a thin spot into a full aperture if you know what you're doing."

Idris was quiet, looking at the new sealant over old. "Someone was practicing," he said.

She hadn't thought of it in those terms, but it was right. Someone had been learning.

She photographed the housing, flagged the sample, and entered a maintenance review request for the Caldwell station. She filed it as a routine review, not an investigation, because she wasn't ready to signal what she was thinking.

On the way back to the Bureau, Idris sat with his tablet dark in his lap, looking at the city.

"The umbrella case," he said. "It's not closed, is it."

"The leak is closed. The question of what opened it isn't."

"Is that different?"

"For the case file, yes. For me, no."

He nodded. He looked out at the Caldwell streets, and for a moment there was something in his posture that Maren couldn't parse — a particular tension, not anxiety exactly, more like someone holding very still in order not to look at a specific thing. She filed it and said nothing.

They had a cartography visit to make.

Chapter 6: Cyriac's Vault

The cartography division occupied the Bureau's east wing, sub-level two, in a complex of rooms that had expanded organically over decades into something that felt more grown than built. Maps covered the walls — large-format prints of Halloway in its various panes, overlaid and annotated in Cyriac's particular hand, which was small and exact and crowded every available margin.

Cyriac Holm was a tall man, slightly stooped from decades of bending over drafting tables, with the particular alert stillness of someone who spent most of his time working with data and had learned to be patient with people who didn't. He waved Maren and Idris in without looking up from his current print.

"Two minutes," he said.

He was adding annotations in red ink — small symbols at specific coordinates. Maren looked at the map as she waited. It was a combined overlay of Panes Three and Four, covering the central districts. She recognized the Caldwell area in the lower right, and the Selwick district north of it. There were red symbols at both.

"Sit," Cyriac said, and put down his pen.

He looked at Idris with the appraising directness he gave all newcomers, which was not unfriendly but was comprehensive. "Lan. I've read your file. You scored high on the seam-geometry module."

"I found it intuitive," Idris said.

"Good. It mostly isn't." Cyriac turned to Maren. "Your flag on the Selwick log. I've been watching Selwick for six weeks."

"You didn't tell me."

"I wasn't sure yet." He turned the overlay map so they could see it fully. "These marks — Caldwell, Selwick, three others in the Varek and Thornside districts. In the last three months, I've had spontaneous seam events at all five locations. Not major events. Small ones. The kind that produce duplicated objects, brief visual overlays, the odd displaced person who thinks they got confused on their way to the shops."

"The Rein visual was one of those."

"Yes. But here's the thing." He picked up his pen and tapped three of the marks. "These three — Caldwell, Selwick, and this one here in the Varek quarter — are not on any historical stress-node map. We have records going back to the original tessellation. These locations have no prior seam history. No geological support for spontaneous aperture. None."

Maren looked at the three marks. "They opened anyway."

"Something opened them."

The room was quiet except for the hum of the ventilation system and, somewhere deeper in the archive stacks, the mechanical tick of a chart printer.

"Forged apertures," Idris said.

Cyriac looked at him. "What makes you say forged?"

"The Caldwell station housing had new sealant over old. Someone had opened and re-closed it. If they were practicing aperture engineering at a location with no geological support—"

"Then they'd need to access the station housing to understand the mechanics," Cyriac finished. He nodded once. "That's what I think."

"Who would have the expertise?" Maren said.

Cyriac looked at his map. "My division, theoretically. A few people in seam engineering. And—" He paused. "Anyone who'd spent significant time with the old mechanical stations. They're more manually manipulable than the new polymer units. Caldwell is old stock."

"Someone running a test series," Maren said.

"At minimum, yes."

"You flagged this to me instead of the Director."

Cyriac set his pen down very carefully. He was not a man who evaded things; he went at them obliquely, which was different. "I flagged it to you," he said, "because you've worked more seam incidents than anyone currently active, and because—" He stopped. "I wanted to know what you thought before it went further."

He was protecting something. She didn't know what yet. But Cyriac was the most careful person she knew, and the fact that he'd sat on this for six weeks and brought it to her now, this way, meant the thing he was protecting was something he believed had a shape that needed understanding before it became official.

"The Varek quarter," she said. "What's there?"

"An old residential district. Dense buildings, mixed use. The mark is at a corner of Rue Varek and the Linden cut." He tapped it. "That one had the strongest aperture signature of the three. I would have described the Caldwell and Selwick events as test runs." He let that sit. "Varek is where someone did something real."

Maren studied the map. Three points, irregularly spaced but — she tilted her head — not random. There was a geometry to them, faint but present, like a triangle that wasn't quite showing itself.

"I need the full charts," she said. "All five locations. Timeline, aperture readings, anything in the sub-feeds."

Cyriac handed her a sealed folder. It was already prepared. He'd had it ready when she walked in.

Chapter 7: Orda Rein's Account

The Bureau's standard interview protocol for seam-event witnesses involved a waiting period — three days minimum between the incident and the formal interview, to allow what the protocol called "temporal normalization." Maren thought the phrase was a bureaucratic euphemism for "giving people time to stop being upset," but the underlying principle was sound. Frightened people remembered inaccurately. People who'd had three days to settle remembered a particular kind of detail: the small, oddly specific thing that hadn't made sense at the time.

Orda Rein herself — the Pane Four version — had been notified by the Pane Four Bureau within forty-eight hours of the memorial event. Their reconciler had visited her, assessed her for seam-exposure symptoms, found none, and filed a clean report. A copy came through to Maren's desk.

But Maren had also, on a separate track, been given access to speak with Orda Rein directly: not the Pane Four version, but the Pane Three version's widower again, who had gathered something from the experience he wanted to share.

Tor Rein called her.

"She reached out," he said. "My wife. The one over there." His voice was even. "The Pane Four Bureau apparently told her there'd been an incident. An overlap event. Gave her the standard form about not making contact." He paused. "She called anyway."

Maren sat up. "She made cross-pane contact?"

"She wrote a letter. Physical letter. Addressed it to — I don't know how she knew the address. Put it in a post box and it came here." He was quiet for a moment. "She said she'd felt something at the time. A kind of pressure behind her eyes. Like looking through glass at something very close. She said she thought she saw the memorial room. She saw me."

"What did she write in the letter?"

"She said she was sorry. For whatever that was worth, coming from someone who isn't — who I've never—" He stopped. "She said she hoped I was all right."

Maren held the phone and looked at the wall.

"Mr. Rein. You need to bring that letter to the Bureau. The cross-pane contact is an incident itself — it needs to be documented."

"I know. I'll bring it today." A beat. "Is it very dangerous, what she did?"

"It's unauthorized. It complicates things. But—" She chose the honest answer. "The membranes are already thin in Selwick. The letter is a symptom of that. It's not the cause."

He brought the letter in at noon. It was one page, handwritten in a firm, slightly tilted script, the kind that had been taught in schools and then kept through practice. It said exactly what Tor had described. It also said, at the end: *I don't know why I'm writing this, except that it seemed wrong to have been that close to something important and to have said nothing.*

Maren read it twice. She filed it as evidence and put the case flag at elevated status.

That evening, walking to her car, she thought: thin membranes. Seams opening where they shouldn't. Someone practicing in the old station housings. A geometric pattern in Cyriac's charts that wasn't quite showing itself.

She did not think about the fact that the seam signature at the Varek quarter was the same type, the same texture, as the one she'd last seen eight years ago, in the collapsing aperture where her partner had gone through and not come back.

She was very careful not to think about that.

She thought about it all the way home.

Chapter 8: The Varek Storefronts

The anomaly in the Varek quarter logged at 6:42 a.m., submitted by a building registry clerk who had been doing routine address verification and discovered that two adjacent storefronts — numbers 14 and 16 on Rue Varek — were simultaneously listed under addresses in both Pane Three and Pane Four's municipal registers. Both addresses were active. Both were occupied. The clerk, to their credit, had not called the police or a journalist; they had called the Bureau's overflow line, which routed to Okura, who had routed it to Maren by seven.

She and Idris were on site by eight.

The storefronts were ground floor of a four-story building — number 14 a key-cutting shop, number 16 a place that sold religious icons, small plaster saints and rosaries and framed images of various prophets, the proprietor of which was standing outside with his arms crossed when they arrived.

"I want to understand what's happening," the icon seller said. His name was Berin Cass, sixty-one, and he had the look of a man who had decided the polite options were exhausted.

"We're here to find out," Idris said.

"Someone from your office called me this morning and told me my shop doesn't exist in one of them — one of the—"

"Panes," Maren said.

"Panes. Right. It doesn't exist in Pane Four, they said. My shop, that I've had for twenty-two years, doesn't exist. So what does that mean for the listing?"

"It means the listing is an error on the registry side," Maren said. "A data anomaly. We're going to check the building and file a correction." She watched him. "The shop exists, Mr. Cass. This is a paperwork problem."

It wasn't only a paperwork problem.

She and Idris went inside both shops with their gauges. The key-cutter's was clean — no anomaly in the walls or floor, the building substrate reading normal. The icon shop was different. Along the shared wall between numbers 14 and 16, running from floor to ceiling at the left-hand side, there was a seam trace that was not cold. It was warm. Not active — no aperture had opened yet — but charged, the substrate pressured from both sides, a membrane under stress.

Maren ran her gauge slowly along the full height of the wall. The charge was consistent. This wasn't a stress point. This was a seam that had been placed here, the pressure built deliberately, the membrane being pushed from the Pane Four side toward Pane Three.

She knew the signature.

It was the same type as the Caldwell station. The same hand.

And it was the same signature — the same precise geometric quality, the same choice of node point in the building's substrate — as the seam she had been called to seal eight years ago. The one she had been sealing when the collapse began. The one she had barely crawled out of while her partner didn't.

She held the gauge against the wall for a moment longer than she needed to, pressing her palm flat against the plaster above it.

"Maren." Idris's voice was quiet.

"Mm."

"Your reading is logged. We should—"

"I know." She took her hand away. "Get the standard containment notice on both shops. Temporary closures, building integrity review, the standard language. We'll need a cartography team out here."

"Do you want me to call Cyriac?"

"Yes."

She walked out of the icon shop onto Rue Varek. The morning was overcast, grey light flat on the cobbles, a tram moving somewhere nearby, its bell muffled by distance. She stood on the pavement and breathed, which she sometimes needed to do.

Idris came out and stood a respectful distance away. He didn't ask anything. He looked at the street with her.

"Good call on the containment notice," she said after a moment.

"You already knew what to do. I was just saying it out loud."

"Sometimes that helps."

She pulled out her tablet and began writing the escalation report. It needed to go to Quist today — the convergence pattern was clear enough now, the geometry resolved enough in her head, that she couldn't reasonably hold it back. She wrote efficiently, without editorializing, sticking to what the gauge had told her and what the registry anomaly confirmed. She did not write what the signature felt like, standing in that room with her palm on the wall.

That part was not in the report.

Chapter 9: Known Signatures

She found the old case file that evening, at home, on the secondary work terminal she kept in her flat's back room. Bureau regulations technically required all case files to be accessed through the Bureau network. The secondary terminal was technically a personal device. The file was technically in the archive, technically available to active senior reconcilers, and she was technically accessing it through a correctly logged request.

She was also sitting in a cold room in her coat because she hadn't turned the heating on, which was the kind of thing she did when she was in a state she preferred not to name.


The dead agent's name was recorded as Wynn Across, Grade Three reconciler, age thirty-one at time of incident.

The case file contained his photo — standard Bureau ID, taken at certification, the kind of photo in which nobody looks quite like themselves. But she knew the face. She had known it for a long time before and a long time after that photograph existed.

The cause of death was recorded as seam-collapse asphyxiation, which was the technical term for what happened when an aperture folded. The aperture didn't close like a door. It compressed, like a wave, fast and total. If you were inside it when it went — if you hadn't cleared the threshold — the substrate simply no longer contained the space you had been occupying.

There was no body. There never was, in a collapse.

She'd been at the other end of that aperture, just outside the threshold, when the collapse signal went. She'd pulled herself through with approximately two seconds left on the safety window. She had not gone back in for him because there was nothing to go back in to.

She sat with the file open for a while. She wasn't reading it. She had it memorized.

The seam signature at the Varek icon shop — the warm, precise, geometrically placed charge she'd felt through the wall this morning — was not the same seam as the collapsed one eight years ago. The geography was different; the mechanics were different. But whoever had placed it had used the same technique as whatever had built that original seam. Old methodology. Specific methodology. The kind that was in Bureau manuals from the period when the Varek district had been a major transit hub before they'd consolidated the stations.

Someone who knew old seam-building methodology. Someone with access to the old station housings.

She closed the file and sat in the cold room.

Then she opened the current escalation report, added two lines — *seam signature consistent with pre-consolidation methodology; recommend cartography comparison against 41-Omega-Varek case record* — and sent it to Quist's office.

She turned on the heating. She made dinner. She did not sleep particularly well.

Chapter 10: The Director's Office

Quist's office was on the Bureau's third floor, with a window that actually faced outward — the only office in the building with an exterior view. On a clear day you could see the Halloway bridge and the river, the western districts stretching away gray and ordinary. Maren had never found the view remarkable. Quist, she suspected, found it useful: a reminder that the city was concrete and real and continuing, which was the thing that most needed remembering in a building full of people who spent their days dealing with the city's less concrete and real aspects.

Quist was sixty-three — Maren always had to recalculate; the file said fifty-seven and Maren knew it was off by more than she was supposed to know — with gray hair cut practically and the particular authority of someone who had learned long ago that a calm voice was more frightening than a loud one. She read the escalation report while Maren stood.

"Sit," Quist said without looking up.

Maren sat.

Quist read to the end and set the report on her desk face-down, which meant she'd finished with it rather than setting it aside. "The Varek storefronts are now under a temporary closure order?"

"As of this morning, yes."

"And you believe the seam charge there is deliberately placed."

"The cartography readings support it. Cyriac is preparing a formal comparison."

"The signature comparison to 41-Omega-Varek." Quist's voice was level. "You added that this morning."

"Yes."

"That case is archived. Why did you pull it?"

"Because I recognized the signature."

Quist looked at her then. The look was measured and thorough and — Maren had known Sable Quist for eleven years — not entirely surprised. "Recognized it from memory, or from a direct comparison?"

"Memory. The comparison will confirm or refute."

"If it confirms — if someone is using the same pre-consolidation methodology used in the 41-Omega collapse — what does that tell you?"

"That whoever is placing these seams is either very old, very well-read in old Bureau methodology, or had access to someone who was."

Quist picked up a pen and put it down. "I want this investigation contained to your unit for now. Yourself, Lan, and Cyriac on the technical side. No other field agents."

"That's a limited team for what this might—"

"I know what it might be." Her voice was still level. "I also know what an announcement of a potential deliberate convergence event would do to this Bureau's relationship with both pane administrations and to the three thousand people who live in the affected districts. Keep it quiet until we know what we're dealing with."

Maren held Quist's gaze. Quist held hers. They had done this before — the negotiation of how much truth got said in what room.

"Agreed," Maren said. "With the condition that if the convergence accelerates, we escalate immediately."

"Of course." A pause. "And Vos — I want daily reports. Not summaries. Reports."

"You'll have them."

She left the office and walked down the two flights to her own floor. On the landing she stopped and stood for a moment, because on her way out she had noticed, on the corner of Quist's desk, a folder she hadn't been invited to look at. It bore a transit authorization stamp — not a case authorization, not a maintenance authorization, but a transit authorization, which was the paperwork that permitted the physical opening of a seam station.

Dated two weeks ago. Directed to a station address she hadn't had time to read.

She walked down the last flight. She filed the observation in the part of her mind she kept for things she wasn't ready to name yet.

Chapter 11: The Calibration Run

The standard calibration run for Pane Two — an annual check of the Caldwell and Selwick stations against their Pane Two counterparts — had been on the books for three months. Maren would have sent another reconciler; she'd been sending other reconcilers for years, because the Pane Two run was genuinely routine, the pane having been stable since the consolidation, and she had other work.

She went herself this time because she needed to think, and crossing into a different pane had the particular effect of clarifying her mind. Something about the physical act of it — the gauge-check, the tether connection, the three steps through the aperture — imposed a useful deliberateness.

She brought Idris because she was required to pair with him for training purposes, and because she wanted to watch what he did when he crossed for the first time.

He was waiting at the Caldwell station when she arrived at seven, with his transit paperwork already completed and his tether already attached to his kit. She ran her own checks silently, clocking his. His paperwork was clean. His tether connection point was correctly positioned. He had not done what most new reconcilers did, which was to stand too close to the aperture before it was open, drawn by the low hum of the active housing.

"You've read the transit protocol," she said.

"Three times."

"What do you do if you lose the tether signal inside?"

"Stay still. Don't attempt to re-cross. Wait for the station operator to send a retrieval signal."

"And if retrieval doesn't come within the window?"

He paused. "The protocol says to use the emergency return switch on the housing panel."

"The protocol says that. The reality is that the emergency return switch on Pane Two's Caldwell station is a physical lever that requires forty-two kilograms of force to engage, because the station was built in the decade when the Bureau thought physical resistance to accidental triggering was a good idea." She handed him a pry bar from her kit. "Carry this."

He took it. "Is this standard?"

"It's the standard I use for Pane Two Caldwell. I have different standards for different stations. You'll learn them."

The station operator, a Bureau employee named Fen who had been running the Caldwell crossing for eight years, opened the aperture at 7:15 exactly. It was a clean opening — no resistance, no delay, the low amber light of the housing going to white as the membrane parted. Maren stepped through first and waited.

The other side was Halloway. It looked like Halloway. The Caldwell post office was there, the same red brick and corner position, the same bell above the door. The morning light was the same grey. A woman walked past on the pavement looking at her phone; she glanced toward them without seeing anything, because the Bureau's apertures were cloaked to non-Bureau personnel by a visual deflection field. They were there and not-there: ghosts in a city that looked exactly like home.

Idris came through thirty seconds behind her. He stood on the other side and looked.

She watched what he looked at. He looked at the post office, the street, the woman with the phone. Then he turned, deliberately, and looked south, toward the Pane Four seam corridor. It was visible from this side — a faint shimmer in the air at the end of the street, the kind of thing that looked like a heat haze in summer and nothing at all in grey morning light unless you knew.

He looked at it for two seconds. Then he looked back at Maren.

"Pane Four is that direction," he said.

"Yes."

"This pane — Two — it borders both Three and Four?"

"Adjacent membranes. Yes." She watched him. "You're not supposed to know what Pane Four's shimmer looks like from this side."

"I read the geography charts."

"The geography charts don't include cross-pane shimmer visibility. That's a field observation." She kept her voice even. "Have you been here before?"

A beat. Just a beat — one second, maybe less. "No," he said.

She decided to let it sit. She picked up her calibration kit and led him toward the first station checkpoint.

He had been here before. She was almost certain. But she wasn't certain enough to name it, and the moment wasn't right, and they had a calibration run to complete. She noted it in the part of her mind she kept for things she needed to return to.

On the way back through, she noticed his hand had been in his jacket pocket for most of the run. Not holding anything. Just resting there, the way a hand rests near something familiar.

Chapter 12: The Convergence Pattern

Cyriac's briefing was held in his vault at six in the evening, after the rest of the cartography division had gone home. He had prepared a revised overlay — three maps, large format, spread across two drafting tables pushed together. Maren and Idris stood on either side and looked.

The pattern was a convergence scaffold.

Cyriac explained it with the precise, undramatic tone he used for things that were actually frightening. The twelve anomalous seam events from the last three months — all the irregular signatures, including Caldwell, Selwick, and the Varek storefronts — formed a structured array across Panes Three and Four. Individual seams at specific node points, each one a single piece of a larger mechanism. Not random drift. Not geological stress expressing itself. A designed structure, placed over months, with the geometry of something engineered by a person who understood exactly how pane membranes worked under sustained distributed pressure.

"If you apply equal pressure to enough node points simultaneously," Cyriac said, "the membrane between two panes doesn't tear. It softens. It becomes permeable over the entire pressured area, not just at the seam sites." He tapped the overlay. "The Varek quarter is the center of the array. The other sites radiate from it in a pattern that corresponds to the natural resonance frequencies of this district's substrate."

"Someone mapped the substrate first," Idris said.

"Yes. A geological survey, or access to Bureau substrate maps, which are classified." Cyriac looked at him. "Good."

"How long has this been building?" Maren said.

"At the current spacing and charge levels — approximately seven months for the outer sites, three months for the inner ones. The Varek storefronts were the last piece placed."

"The array is complete."

"The array is complete."

Maren looked at the center of the map — the Varek quarter, the icon shop, the warm charge in the wall. "What happens when it activates?"

"The membrane between Panes Three and Four becomes permeable across the entire Varek district. Not a seam — not a doorway you can walk through. A blurring. Objects, then spaces, then—" He stopped. "The historical record from the Pane One-Two event forty years ago is incomplete, but the available data suggests that sustained membrane permeability begins affecting residents. People who live and work on both sides of the membrane in the same location experience—" He used the technical term: "co-location stress."

"What does that mean for a person?" Idris asked.

"They begin to occupy both spaces simultaneously. The membrane that keeps each pane's version of a person distinct starts to fail. Both versions of a person begin pulling toward the same point." He was very careful with the next part. "The historical record uses the word 'merger.' It's not well-documented. The Pane One-Two event was stopped before it completed."

"And if it completes?"

Cyriac didn't answer immediately. He looked at his maps. "Then the two panes are one pane. And whatever version of each merged person survives that — we don't know."

The room was quiet. Maren looked at the Varek quarter on the map. She looked at the outer points of the scaffold — Caldwell, Selwick, the others — the months of careful, incremental work they represented.

"This took patience," she said.

"Extraordinary patience," Cyriac said.

"And knowledge."

"Yes."

"Someone who loves someone very much," Idris said.

They both looked at him. He was still looking at the map. His face was neutral, but his hand had found his jacket pocket again.

"That's a hypothesis," Maren said.

"I know," he said. "But it's the right shape."

She thought, for the first time, about what she might be willing to do for eight years of patience, seven months of careful placement, and the chance to stop something irreversible. She thought about the warm charge in the wall of the icon shop, and the face in the old case file.

She wrote it down in no report.

Chapter 13: The Transit Officer

The official business was a case file retrieval.

A Pane Three resident named Ollo Fen — no relation to the station operator — had misfiled a property claim that intersected with a cross-pane duplicate incident from six months ago. The Pane Four Bureau held the original documentation. Standard bureaucratic inconvenience, the kind that required a reconciler to cross personally because the document-transfer protocols were, as Idris had observed after two weeks on the job, "written by someone who thought paper was a security feature."

Maren filed a transit permit for a solo crossing to Pane Four's Caldwell station and cleared it with Quist's office by the standard forty-eight-hour process.

She packed her kit with unnecessary care that morning.

The crossing was at noon. She went through the Caldwell aperture into Pane Two, walked the two-block connector to the Pane Four aperture, and went through that one also. Two crossings, each clean and flat and routine.

Pane Four looked like Halloway. It always looked like Halloway. Same brick, same river, same grey overcast sky doing its grey thing. The streets in the immediate Caldwell area were identical. One building had a different shopfront — a bakery here that was a stationer's in Pane Three; she clocked it automatically, filed it automatically.

The Pane Four Bureau's Caldwell sub-office was in the same building as Pane Three's, which was a coincidence of architecture that the Bureau used for its own administrative convenience. You entered through a different door, because the two pane-offices occupied different floors, but the building was the same building.

The transit desk was on the second floor.

She knew this from the building diagram. She had looked up the building diagram two days ago.

She found the document she needed at the reception office on the first floor, which took four minutes. She had an evidence bag ready. She signed the transfer log. She was done.

She took the stairs to the second floor.

The transit desk handled cross-pane coordination for the Pane Four sub-office — permits, seam calibrations, the administrative side of what reconcilers did in the field. It was staffed by a rotation of qualified transit officers. She had looked up the current duty roster three days ago when she first accessed the Pane Four personnel directory.

She had told herself she was doing background research on the Varek convergence case.

The desk was in a long room with three workstations. Two were empty. One was occupied.

The man at the occupied station was looking at a screen. He had dark hair with grey at the temples that hadn't been there in the case-file photo. He was leaning forward in the specific way of someone reading something complicated. His left hand was turning a pen over and over, not writing with it, just turning it. She had known that habit. She had spent three years watching that habit and finding it reliable.

He looked up.

It was the face from the file. It was also a face that had lived eight more years than the file knew about. He looked at her with the simple, immediate recognition of someone who processed faces quickly — he was good at his job, he always had been — and then with the confused recognition of someone who is looking at a person they don't know who is wearing the face of someone they do.

"Can I help you?" he said.

She had planned to keep walking. To come in, establish the context she needed, and then do what she was here to do. But she was standing in the doorway and he was looking at her, and the pen was turning in his hand, and she was forty-four years old and she had been careful for eight years and she was not prepared for how ordinary it was to see him.

"I'm Maren Vos," she said. "Pane Three, Tessera Bureau. I'm here on a document retrieval." She held up the evidence bag. "I've got what I needed. I was checking the floor plan." She let a beat pass. "I didn't realize transit was on this level."

He looked at her for a moment. Something in his face — not recognition, because they'd never met — but the particular attention of someone who has caught a tone that doesn't match the words.

"Wynn Across," he said. "Chief transit officer. Do you need anything else?"

"No," she said. "Thank you."

She walked back down the stairs.

Chapter 14: The Personnel File

She requested Wynn Across's Pane Four personnel file the following morning through the standard inter-pane records channel, citing the Varek convergence investigation as the access reason. This was technically accurate.

The file arrived in her queue by noon. She read it at her desk with the deliberate care she gave to things she needed to read correctly.

He was thirty-nine years old. He'd joined the Pane Four Bureau twelve years ago as a junior reconciler — the same year she'd made senior in Pane Three. He'd worked field assignments for four years and then moved to transit administration, which was a lateral shift that most people made because they'd had an incident that made field work harder. His record didn't specify what the incident had been; the notation simply said *voluntary role transfer, medical review completed, cleared for administrative duty*. She read that twice.

He'd been promoted to chief transit officer for the Caldwell sub-office five years ago. His evaluations were consistently high. He was described by his supervisors as methodical, trustworthy, occasionally terse. She found that last descriptor almost unbearably accurate.

He had no cross-pane incidents on record. No unauthorized contacts. He lived in the Selwick district — the Pane Four Selwick, which would be almost identical to Pane Three's version — in an address that the Bureau's standard geo-comparison tool told her was fourteen meters from the spot where, in Pane Three, Orda Rein's memorial had been held.

She sat with that for a while.

Then she wrote a case note in the Varek investigation file: *Pane Four transit officer Wynn Across confirmed as personnel at seam station most proximate to convergence array center. No evidence of involvement. Cross-checking transit permit logs for Pane Four Caldwell station, dates spanning the scaffold construction period.*

She checked the transit permit logs.

He had been on duty for six of the sixteen irregular seam events. Not all of them. Just six. His duty hours would have overlapped with six of the nights when the scaffold's outer nodes were being placed.

She did not write what she did with that number. She held it and turned it over and looked at it from several directions and eventually put it down where she could reach it later.

Idris arrived at his desk and looked at her.

"You went to Pane Four yesterday," he said.

"Document retrieval. It's in the log."

"I know it's in the log. You didn't mention you were going before you went."

"It was a solo crossing. I don't need to brief you on solo crossings."

"No," he said. "You don't." He opened his terminal and started his own work. After a moment: "Did you find what you were looking for?"

She pulled up the convergence case notes and began reading them. "The document retrieval was successful."

He made a sound that was not quite a laugh. "Right," he said, and went back to his screen.

Chapter 15: Friction

They had their first real argument on a Thursday, about nothing and everything.

The Varek containment required daily monitoring — gauge readings at the outer nodes to track any change in the scaffold's charge level. Maren had been doing the morning runs herself; she assigned Idris the afternoon runs, with a reporting protocol. He had been doing them correctly. The readings had been stable.

On Thursday evening he came back from the afternoon run and reported a 0.3-unit increase at the Linden cut node. Standard reporting. She logged it. Then she noticed the time gap between his arrival at the station and his reading.

"You stopped somewhere," she said.

"I walked the route."

"The route doesn't pass through the Thornside connector."

He was quiet for a moment. The Thornside connector was a pedestrian cut-through that ran between Varek and the district where the Pane Two aperture was located. It was not on the monitoring route.

"I took a different path back," he said.

"Were you accessing the Pane Two aperture?"

"No."

"Idris." She kept her voice even. "If you've been making unauthorized crossings—"

"I haven't crossed." He met her eyes. "I walked past the aperture housing. I didn't access it."

"Why?"

He looked at her for a long moment with the expression of someone choosing between several true things to say.

"Because sometimes I walk past things," he said. "It helps me think."

"What are you thinking about?"

"The case."

"Specifically."

He stood up and moved to the window, which was unlike him — he wasn't a pacer. He stood looking at the courtyard. The grey afternoon light came through at a low angle and lit the side of his face.

"My brother," he said. "Saul. He's in Pane Four."

She waited.

"He got caught in a seam event four years ago. Not a major one — a thin spot near the Thornside residential area. He walked through it without knowing. The Bureau here classified it as a natural drift event and sent a notification to Pane Four that the individual had been restored to his home pane." He stopped. "But they made a mistake. They restored the wrong person. The man who came back to Pane Three was a Pane Four resident who'd come through the same thin spot from the other direction. Saul never came back."

"Where is he now?"

"Living in Pane Four. Under the same name, same address on the Pane Four side. The Pane Four Bureau didn't classify it as anything because as far as their records were concerned, Saul Lan had always been in Pane Four."

"And you've been—"

"I've been trying to get the Bureau to correct the misclassification for four years." His voice was steady with the particular steadiness of someone who has been angry about something for too long to keep being loud about it. "The paperwork keeps disappearing into inter-pane dispute resolution, which has a three-year backlog. I took this job to have access to the seam stations."

Maren sat back.

"Have you crossed to see him?"

"Yes."

She took a breath. Unauthorized crossing by a Bureau employee was a formal infraction. It was also, given everything she was carrying in the back of her mind, the least surprising thing she'd heard in weeks.

"How many times?"

"Eleven. All logged on the Pane Four side — I used their transit process, submitted their forms. I've never violated Pane Three protocol."

"You violated it by not disclosing you have a family member in an adjacent pane when you were hired."

"I know."

She looked at him. He looked back, clearly, without excuses. This was, she thought, the truest thing about him so far: he knew exactly where he stood and he was willing to stand there.

"You muttered something last week," she said. "About keeping things you love on the wrong side of a membrane."

He nodded once.

"You were talking about Saul."

"Among other things."

She looked at her desk. At the convergence case file, the overlay maps, the transit logs with their gaps, and Wynn Across's personnel file sitting in her queue.

"I'm not going to report this yet," she said. "I should. But I'm going to decide what order I do things in." She met his eyes. "You're going to stop walking past the Pane Two aperture and you're going to tell me if you make contact with your brother through any channel. Not because I'm managing you. Because I need to know what all the moving parts are."

He nodded.

"And Idris." She paused. "I'm sorry about your brother. That was a Bureau mistake and it should have been corrected."

He looked at her for a moment with something that was not quite surprise. "Thank you," he said.

They went back to work.

Chapter 16: Two Front Doors

The Ellisen call came in at eight in the morning — a building in the Varek quarter, not on Rue Varek itself but on a parallel street called the Linden cut, which Cyriac's map had identified as a secondary node in the scaffold array. The report from the resident, a man named Brand Ellisen, was submitted through the non-emergency Bureau line:

*My apartment now has two front doors. Both open. Both lead to different places. I don't know what to do.*

Maren and Idris were there within twenty minutes.

The building was a three-story residential block, brick and plaster, the ground-floor apartments opening off a shared hallway. Ellisen's apartment was number three, at the end of the hallway. He was standing in the corridor when they arrived, wearing yesterday's clothes with the look of a man who had slept badly or not at all. He was perhaps fifty, thick-shouldered, with a ceramic mug held in both hands for warmth rather than for drinking.

"They're both there," he said. "I checked this morning. Last night I thought I was imagining it."

They followed him in.

The apartment was an ordinary one-bedroom — a kitchen, a sitting room, a narrow hallway leading to the bedroom and bathroom. The front door was at one end of the entrance hall. At the other end, where the wall should have been, there was a second door.

It was solid. Painted the same green as the front door, same hardware, same threshold strip. Maren ran her gauge along both frames. The second door's frame read hot — active seam, a real aperture, holding open at minimal pressure but definitely open, the membrane maintained in a permanently parted state.

She opened the second door.

On the other side was the same hallway — same dimensions, same light fixture, same peeling paint on the left wall — but the view through the window at the far end showed a slightly different building across the street. The Pane Four version of the building opposite, which had an extra floor.

"Don't step through," she said to Idris.

He had already put his foot back.

"Mr. Ellisen." She closed the second door. "When did you first notice this?"

"Yesterday. I came home and it was there. I thought—" He looked at the door. "I thought someone had broken in and put a door in my wall."

"How do you feel physically? Any headaches, dizziness, confusion about where you are?"

"I'm very confused about where I am."

"I mean—" She stopped. "I mean, do you feel like yourself? No gaps in time, no double impressions?"

He thought about it carefully, which she appreciated. "No. Just the door."

"Good. The door is a seam aperture — a passage between two adjacent versions of this building. It's held open by a mechanism in the wall structure. We're going to seal it today." She kept her voice calm and practical. "You'll need to stay in the building's communal area while we work. Approximately six hours. Is there someone you can call?"

He looked at the door. "Does my apartment also exist somewhere else?"

"Yes."

"Is there another version of me there?"

"There is."

He looked at the mug in his hands. "Is he confused too?"

"His Bureau is aware of the situation. He's been notified and assisted." She watched his face. "He's all right."

Ellisen nodded slowly. "Good," he said. "That's good." And he went to call someone to wait with him, with the particular stoic decency of a person who, confronted with an impossible thing, is trying very hard to be decent about it.

Chapter 17: Structural Node

The sealing took nine hours, not six.

The aperture in Ellisen's wall was what Cyriac's technical documents called a "pinned seam" — not a free-floating membrane event but a seam held in place by a physical anchor. The anchor was a small engineered insert in the wall's substrate, roughly the size of a fist, made of the same synthetic sealant compound they'd found at the Caldwell station housing. Someone had placed it in the wall's structure at a point where the building's substrate was naturally close to the membrane, and it had been maintaining the aperture from the inside.

Maren spent two hours reaching it, cutting through plaster and old brick with the precision tools from her kit. The insert was professionally installed — the surrounding material was undamaged, the insert's edges clean and deliberate. This was not improvisation. Whoever had placed this had done it with practiced hands.

She extracted the insert, photographed it, bagged it, and sent a sample notification to Cyriac.

He called back twenty minutes later. "The material matches the Caldwell compound. Same source."

"Manufactured or synthesized?"

"Synthesized. It's not a commercial product. Someone made this."

"Someone in the Bureau."

"Someone with access to Bureau materials synthesis lab. That's—" She could hear him checking. "Twelve authorized users. I'll send you the list."

Idris handled the actual seam sealing while she was on the phone — he'd been watching her technique closely for two weeks and was ready to try it supervised. She watched him work. He was careful and precise and he made one adjustment she would have made differently, not wrong, just different, and she let him make it.

He sealed it clean. The aperture closed. The substrate flattened.

"Good," she said.

He looked at the wall, at the smoothed-over area where the door had been. "It's gone."

"Yes."

"How does he explain it to himself? Ellisen. How does he explain to himself what was in his wall for however long it was there?"

"He'll receive a standard Bureau explanation letter. Building infrastructure anomaly, surveying error, corrected during maintenance review. Most people accept it."

Idris picked up his tools. "Most people."

"Some people don't. They live differently after they know. Usually they're all right — the Bureau has a counseling protocol." She paused. "The ones who don't accept it are the interesting cases."

"Interesting how?"

She thought about Orda Rein's letter. She thought about Tor Rein, who had felt something at the memorial and decided not to look. She thought about herself at nineteen, before the Bureau, walking past a shimmer in the air in the Varek quarter and thinking it was a heat haze.

"Interesting in that they adjust," she said. "Humans are very good at incorporating impossible things. They just put them in a different drawer."

She filed the debrief report that evening and added a note to the convergence case: *Pinned seam at Linden Cut node confirmed. Same compound as Caldwell. Scaffold mechanism understood. This node was not a natural aperture widened from existing stress — it was a manufactured aperture placed at a location chosen specifically for its position within the array. The Architect knows this district at the structural level.*

She used the word Architect for the first time in the written record. It was not a name. It was a recognition.

Chapter 18: The Geometry

Cyriac's revised chart arrived on a Tuesday and she and Idris spread it across the briefing room table and looked at it for a long time.

All twelve seam events — the full scaffold, all nodes charted, all charge levels current. Cyriac had added a new overlay: a projection of the membrane between Panes Three and Four if the scaffold activated at its current charge levels. The membrane, shown in a gradient from blue to red depending on permeability, was currently blue across most of the map.

In the Varek quarter — a roughly circular area perhaps eight city blocks across — it was beginning to turn amber.

"Amber means," Idris said.

"Elevated permeability. Below threshold for active bleeding, but above the stable baseline." Maren traced the circle on the map. "If all twelve nodes fire simultaneously—"

"They don't fire simultaneously by themselves," Cyriac had written in the margin. "They'd need to be triggered. Someone has to activate the array."

She had called him about that.

"Manual trigger?" she'd asked.

"Or remote, but the scaffold's design suggests manual. You'd need to be at the center node — the Varek storefront location — to send the activation pulse. The outer nodes are passive receivers."

"So whoever built this has to go to the Varek quarter and stand in a specific location to set it off."

"Within a thirty-meter radius of the center node, yes."

She looked at the icon shop on the map. Small red dot at the center of the amber circle.

"They also haven't triggered it yet," Idris said.

"No."

"Why not? The scaffold has been complete since the Varek storefronts. That's two weeks."

She had been thinking about this. "They're waiting for something."

"What?"

"Either a specific condition in the membrane — a particular thinning state that makes the trigger more effective. Or—" She looked at the amber area. "Or they haven't decided yet."

Idris looked at her.

"Whoever built this," she said, "invested seven months in it. The patience you noted." She straightened. "People who are fully committed don't wait. They build toward something and they do it. The delay suggests doubt."

"Or the delay suggests they're watching us," he said. "Watching the Bureau close in, waiting to see how much we know."

That was also possible. She added it to the list of possibilities she carried.

"Either way," she said. "We need to find them before they decide."

Chapter 19: Pane Four, Formally

The official crossing was a jurisdictional transfer case — a property dispute in which a Pane Three resident had acquired a lease that was simultaneously held by a Pane Four resident, the result of a duplicate document event three years ago that had never been properly resolved. It was exactly the kind of case that required a reconciler to cross and speak to the relevant parties in person, and it had been sitting in the queue for two months.

Maren took it.

She went through the Caldwell aperture and into Pane Four at nine on a Wednesday morning. She had her full kit, her tether, her evidence bag with the lease documents, and a transit permit that had been authorized by the standard process. She had not told Idris she was going, which was her right on a solo crossing and which she recognized as a form of concealment she was choosing in full awareness that she was choosing it.

She resolved the property dispute in ninety minutes. The Pane Four residents involved were cooperative and the documentation was relatively clean; it was the kind of case that had been delayed by bureaucratic inertia rather than complexity. She signed off the transfer, notified both parties' lawyers through the standard inter-pane legal channel, and filed the closure paperwork with the Pane Four sub-office.

Then she walked to the Caldwell transit wing on the second floor.

She had thought about this carefully. She had spent two days thinking about it. What she had decided was: she was conducting a legitimate background investigation into transit personnel in proximity to the convergence scaffold, and she had a professional reason to speak to the duty officer, and the fact that she had other reasons was her own business so long as they didn't compromise the investigation.

It was a flimsy structure but it was the structure she had.

He was at his desk again. This time he saw her coming — she was still in the doorway of the transit room when he looked up, so there was a moment before she reached his desk in which they were both aware of each other and doing nothing about it.

She crossed the room and stood at the desk and put her case closure forms on it for his administrative sign-off. He looked at them. He signed them without fuss. He slid them back.

"Vos," he said. "Pane Three."

"Yes."

"You were here about ten days ago."

"Document retrieval."

"You took the wrong floor."

"I did." She picked up her forms. "I wanted to check something."

He put down his pen. He had an expression that was not quite wariness — more like the alertness of someone who has learned to pay attention to things that don't quite add up, which was exactly the expression she would have expected. He had always been like that. She had to keep stopping herself from finishing the thought.

"What did you need to check?" he said.

"Whether the transit logs for this station show any irregularities in the six months preceding the Varek anomaly."

He was still. "Is this an official inquiry?"

"It's background work. I can file a formal request if you prefer."

"What are you looking for?"

"Whether any unofficial crossings used this station as a waypoint. Someone building a scaffold of unauthorized seams over a wide area would need transit access." She held his gaze. "I'm not suggesting your station was compromised. I'm checking adjacencies."

He looked at her for a long moment. "You know my name," he said. "You knew it when you were here before."

She could lie. The lie would be easy and plausible and she had been preparing it.

"Yes," she said.

He turned his pen over in his hand. Once, twice. She watched it.

"We've never met," he said.

"No."

"But you know me."

"I know your record." A pause. "And I know — I knew — someone who looked like you."

The room was quiet for a moment. He was working something out. She could see him doing it — the patient intelligence she'd watched every day for three years, the same quality still visible under the eight years of additional living.

"You're going to need to tell me what that means," he said.

"Not here," she said. "Not officially." She picked up her kit. "I'll file the formal transit log request through the standard channel. You'll receive it tomorrow."

She walked out. On the stairs she stopped and stood for a moment with her hand on the banister, looking at nothing.

Then she walked out of the building, crossed back through the Caldwell apertures, and was in Pane Three by noon.

Chapter 20: The Director's Caution

Chapter 21: The Dog Named Holt

The first confirmed convergence event in the Varek quarter was recorded on a Thursday, and the subject was a dog.

The dog's name was Holt. He was a medium-sized terrier mix, brown and white, about four years old, belonging to a woman named Yse Mander who lived on Rue Varek in the apartment above the key-cutting shop. Holt had a collar with his name and Yse Mander's address. He also, as of Thursday morning, appeared to exist in two places at once.

Not almost simultaneously. Simultaneously. The Pane Three dog-warden service received reports from two locations — Rue Varek and a square three blocks east — at the same time, on the same morning, describing the same dog sitting in two separate locations doing nothing except existing.

Maren and Idris went to Rue Varek first. Holt was there, under the window box outside number twelve. He was looking at something in the middle distance with the alert vacancy of dogs who have seen something humans cannot. Maren crouched and ran her gauge along his collar.

The gauge made a sound she'd never heard it make.

The collar was reading from two substrate locations simultaneously — the Pane Three Rue Varek and the Pane Four equivalent. Holt was physically present in Pane Three. His substrate signal was present in both.

"He's not crossing," Idris said quietly. He was crouching too, looking at the gauge.

"No. The membrane is thin enough here that his signal is bleeding through. He's in Pane Three but he's also registering in Pane Four's substrate."

"Both signals are live?"

"Both signals are live."

He looked at the dog. Holt turned his head and looked at them with the calm assessment of a creature who has long since decided humans are unpredictable and has made his peace with it. Then he looked back at the middle distance.

"What happens to him?" Idris said.

"The signals resolve. Either the membrane thickens and the Pane Four signal fades, or—" She stood. "Or both signals keep strengthening and they start pulling toward the same point."

"And then?"

"And then he's in two places at once in a more complete sense. Not a signal bleed. An actual co-location."

"Which means—"

"For an animal it's probably disorienting. The Bureau's literature on co-location stress in non-humans is limited. The Pane One-Two event affected several animal populations in the affected district." She flagged the case and called the containment team. "We need a signal dampener on his collar and we need Yse Mander in the loop."

Yse Mander was not in the loop. She was at work. Idris called her; she came home; she listened to the explanation with the particular focus of a person who loves a specific animal very much and has abruptly been handed a problem that requires more technical vocabulary than she possesses.

"Is he in pain?" she asked.

"No evidence of that," Idris said. "He seems calm."

"He's always calm. He has no idea anything's wrong, does he."

"We don't know how animals experience seam-related stress. But his vital signs are normal."

She looked at Maren. "You're not going to fix this today, are you."

"We're going to fit a signal dampener that will reduce the substrate bleed while the membrane is thin." Maren met her eyes. "The underlying cause is the membrane. We're working on that."

"Is he going to be all right?"

Maren made a decision. She owed Yse Mander a real answer.

"If the membrane returns to normal thickness, yes. If the convergence continues—" She stopped. "We're going to stop the convergence."

Yse Mander looked at her dog. She nodded once, firmly, the way people nod when they've decided to believe something because the alternative is unbearable. She didn't ask what happened if Maren was wrong. Some questions are better as questions.

Chapter 22: Two People with the Same Name

Over the next four days Maren interviewed two Varek quarter residents who had reported what the Bureau's incident form classified as "temporal displacement events" — which was the technical term for co-location stress in human subjects. She had read about these in the historical records from the Pane One-Two incident. Reading about them and interviewing the people experiencing them were different.

The first was a retired schoolteacher named Orvie Strand, sixty-one, who lived in a first-floor apartment on Rue Varek and had been there for twenty-three years. She described, methodically and without drama, waking on Tuesday morning to find that she had an extra hour she couldn't account for. Not a gap — not missing time — but extra time, an hour that she had experienced as herself and also as someone else who was also herself, overlapping. She had been making tea. She had also been walking to work, which she no longer did, having retired three years ago. Both had happened. She remembered both.

The second was a man in his thirties named Corl Vane who lived two streets from Orvie Strand and had come to the Bureau's public inquiry desk — not knowing anything specific about seam events, just knowing something was wrong — because for two days he had been unable to remember whether his name was Corl or Kael. He knew Corl was correct. He also had clear, detailed memories of being Kael Vane, who lived at the same address, same apartment, same furniture, same view from the window. Kael had a different job. Different daily routes. Different minor habits. Corl-Kael sat in the Bureau's interview room and told Maren that the worst part wasn't the confusion. The worst part was that both sets of memories felt real. Both felt like him.

She conducted both interviews with the measured care she used for all interviews, recorded everything, filed the forms. When she was done with the second one she sat in the interview room for a few minutes before calling her next appointment.

Co-location stress was not a metaphor. It was a specific experience with symptoms she could now map to the technical literature. What the literature called "identity destabilization" — the blurring of one person into two — was exactly what Corl Vane had described. It was exactly what would continue to happen to everyone in the affected district if the scaffold activated.

It was also, in a different way, exactly what she had been living with for eight years — the knowledge that somewhere there was a version of Wynn who was alive, who was living a life, who was the same person and not the same person and both at once. She had kept that destabilization at a professional distance through policy and paperwork and the discipline of staying in her own pane.

She did not record that observation anywhere. It was not relevant to the case.

It was extremely relevant to the case.

Chapter 23: The Pane One-Two Archive

The Pane One-Two convergence event was forty-one years old and the records were partially redacted. The redactions were old ones — thick black bars through portions of the field reports, a standard practice from the era before the Bureau's current documentation protocols — and they were thorough enough to be frustrating without being thorough enough to be uninteresting.

Cyriac had the full archive. He also had, it turned out, annotations in the margins of a physical copy that he had made sometime in the previous decade and kept in a folder at the back of his bottom drawer, labeled with a date and the notation *personal reference*.

He gave Maren the annotated copy without being asked. She understood that this was significant.

"You've studied this before," she said.

"The geometry of the Pane One-Two scaffold — the one that was stopped — is different from what's happening now. Different methodology, pre-consolidation tools. But the underlying mechanics are the same. When I started seeing the pattern in the current seam events, I went back to the archive to compare."

She looked at his marginal notes. They were thorough and old — the handwriting slightly different from his current annotations, which meant he'd made them years ago.

"You started watching this before you told me," she said.

"Yes."

He was quiet for a moment. "When I identified the third irregular seam. That was four months ago."

Four months. She held that.

"I know." He looked at his maps. "I knew what it meant. I knew what stopping it would mean. I needed time to think."

"Someone built this scaffold, Maren. "Rendel Osk," he said.

"Yes," she said.

"Because I know why he's doing it," Cyriac said. "And I've known for two months that I had to decide what to do with that."

Chapter 24: The Confession

Idris told her everything on a Tuesday, in the Bureau cafeteria, at seven-fifteen in the morning before anyone else arrived. He chose the time and place deliberately — she recognized the deliberateness — because the empty cafeteria gave him nowhere to retreat to and her nowhere to avoid it.

She listened.

His brother Saul had been caught in the seam event in the Thornside residential area four years ago. The event itself was a natural thin-spot opening — the Bureau had correctly identified it as spontaneous drift. What the Bureau had gotten wrong, disastrously wrong, was the subsequent management. There had been two individuals caught in the crossing: Saul Lan from Pane Three, and a man named Brak Follen from Pane Four, who had drifted through in the other direction through the same thin spot. The Bureau reconciler on the scene — a junior reconciler named Desso, since transferred out — had processed them both and sent each home. Follen went back to Pane Four. Saul, according to the documentation, was returned to his Pane Three address.

But Desso had mixed up the tether signals. He'd sent Saul into Pane Four and Follen back to Pane Three, and by the time Idris found out — three weeks later, when Saul called him on the inter-pane communication protocol that Idris had known about from his Bureau research — Desso had already submitted the closure report, which was locked. And the Bureau's official position, when Idris raised the mistake, was that the tether signal data showed a correct return, the documentation was clean, and any claim of misclassification would require a formal appeal through the inter-pane dispute resolution process, which had a three-year waiting list.

"I submitted the appeal," Idris said. "Four years ago. I got an acknowledgment. I've gotten no other communication."

"You took the job to access the seam stations."

"Yes."

"You've made eleven unauthorized crossings to see Saul."

"Eleven on the Pane Four filing system. On this side — none. I never used a Pane Three station to cross. I always went through the Pane Two aperture using the Pane Four transit forms, which means Pane Four has the full record of every visit."

"That's a technical non-violation of Pane Three protocol."

"Yes."

She turned her coffee cup on the table. "You knew I suspected."

"I knew when you noticed the time gap on the afternoon run."

"Why didn't you tell me then?"

He looked at his hands. "Because I needed to know if you'd—" He stopped. "I needed to know what you were doing first."

She looked at him. "What I was doing."

"The Pane Four transit officer. You've crossed twice in a week on cases that could have been handled by communication. You requested his personnel file." He met her eyes. "I'm not accusing you of anything. I'm saying I was waiting to see what you'd do with something you weren't supposed to want."

They looked at each other across the empty table.

"He was my partner," she said. "The version of him here — the Pane Three version — died in the 41-Omega collapse eight years ago. I was there."

"I know." His voice was quiet. "I read the case file when I was researching the Bureau's history. I know what his name was."

She turned the cup again. The cafeteria smelled of coffee and institutional detergent.

"What are we doing?" she said. Not to him specifically. Just out loud.

"I think," he said carefully, "that we're both very clear about why the Architect is doing what they're doing, and that is making it harder to be simple about stopping it."

It was the most accurate thing anyone had said in this investigation.

"The appeal for Saul," she said. "The inter-pane dispute. I can't shortcut the queue but I can flag it to Quist as an active misclassification affecting a Bureau employee. That has a different channel."

He looked at her.

"It's the right thing to do," she said. "It's also true that telling you I'll do it is not a bargain for your silence about the unauthorized crossings. Those stand. I'll deal with them according to protocol when this is over."

"I understand."

"And Idris." She waited until he looked at her again. "If the panes merge, what happens to Saul's co-location?"

He was quiet.

"Two versions of Saul," she said. "One here, one there. If the panes converge, both versions pull toward the same point. One dissolves."

"I know." His voice was flat. "I've thought about it."

"That's why you haven't—"

"That's why I haven't told the Architect to hurry up, yes." He picked up his coffee. "I thought about it. I decided what I thought about it. I'm here, doing this job, and I'm not going to compromise the investigation." He set the cup down. "That's what I decided."

She nodded.

"Good," she said. "That was the right decision."

Chapter 25: Saul's Case

The inter-pane misclassification for Saul Lan was a procedural disaster.

Maren spent a morning with the Bureau's dispute archive and the afternoon with a claims analyst she trusted named Ferris, who had been dealing with the inter-pane adjudication queue long enough to know every mechanism for moving something from one channel to another. The misclassification of Desso's field report was clear once you had the tether signal data next to the transit authorization: the signal codes had been swapped at entry, and both men had been sent to the wrong pane. This was not a judgment call. This was a data error.

Ferris flagged it as a procedural misfile — not a contested claim but a correction — which put it in a different administrative bucket entirely. Corrections didn't have a three-year queue. Corrections had a thirty-day review.

She sent Idris the notification. His reply was one sentence: *Thank you.*

She went back to the convergence investigation.

The picture of Rendel Osk was developing in the case file's background, assembled from cross-references and access logs in the way that all Bureau investigations assembled their pictures: by accumulation of small, certain things. His access block had generated the stripped transit log for the Selwick event. His name was on the materials synthesis list. His desk in the seam-modeling division was, by architectural circumstance, located in the sub-level where the primary seam-mapping data was stored — which gave him access to the substrate charts, the geological surveys, the technical documentation that someone building a convergence scaffold would need.

Cyriac had given her one more piece: the Pane One-Two archive's un-redacted footnotes mentioned, in passing, that the scaffold-builder who had ultimately sealed the Pane One-Two event had been motivated by the death of a close relative who was alive in the adjacent pane. The redacted sections of the file were the evidence trail and the outcome of any formal proceedings. The footnotes were the bones showing through.

Maren looked at Rendel Osk's Bureau personnel record. His emergency contact, listed as such for eight of his twelve years of service, was a man named Fenwick Osk. The emergency contact had been removed from the record four years ago. In the same month, there was a notation in Rendel Osk's personnel file: *brief administrative leave, personal bereavement.*

She pulled the Bureau's general mortality records — accessible for any Bureau employee with a senior reconciler clearance, because the Bureau tracked deaths that might have involved seam events. Fenwick Osk, age forty at time of death, transit accident near the Thornside aperture station. Four years ago.

She sat very still.

The Thornside aperture station. Where Saul Lan had been misdirected. The same incident cluster.

She checked the Pane Four Bureau's mortality records through the standard inter-pane query channel.

Fenwick Osk was alive in Pane Four. Same name, same age, same address — the Pane Four version of whatever address they'd shared. Living. Working. Not dead.

She looked at the wall.

Then she wrote it up. Clean, factual, without editorializing. The motive was not an excuse. Understanding the motive was part of the work.

Chapter 26: The Unlogged Crossing

The crossing she did not log happened at ten on a Wednesday evening.

She told herself she was confirming a cross-pane contact protocol before filing a formal information request. She told herself that the timing — late, after the building was mostly empty — was because the Pane Four transit desk ran extended hours on Wednesdays, which was true. She told herself a great deal.

The Caldwell aperture was unmanned at that hour; it operated on an automated protocol after six. She entered her authorization code, logged it under the Varek investigation case number, and crossed.

She found Wynn in the transit room's secondary office, not at the main desk — the main desk was manned by a junior officer at this hour, a young woman who glanced up when Maren came through the door and accepted the Bureau ID she presented without difficulty. The secondary office was down a short hall, used for extended shifts and case review. The door was open.

She knocked on the frame.

He looked up. He had a case file in front of him, coffee at his elbow going cold, the pen turning in his hand. He had clearly been there for some time.

"Vos," he said. "Pane Three."

"Yes."

"It's late."

"I know."

He looked at her for a moment, and then he did something she didn't expect — he got up and closed the office door. Not a gesture of hospitality; a gesture of privacy.

"Sit down," he said.

She sat.

He went back to his chair and sat also. He looked at her with the honest, patient attention she remembered, which was harder to look at than she'd expected and which she had expected to be very hard.

"The thing you said before," he said. "Someone who looked like me."

"Yes."

"Who."

She told him. She told him about the 41-Omega collapse, about the case designation and the date and the aperture mechanics and what had happened at the threshold, and she told him that the person she was describing was dead in Pane Three and had died eight years ago and that she had been the other reconciler on the case.

He listened without interrupting. She had expected him to look for himself in it, to try to map the dead man onto his own life. He didn't. He listened to the story as the story it was: a woman telling him about someone she had lost, who was also him and was not him.

"He was your partner," Wynn said when she finished.

"My field partner. Yes."

"How old were you?"

"Thirty-six."

He was quiet for a moment. "What was he like?"

She looked at the wall. "Methodical. Impatient sometimes. Good instincts. He turned his pen when he was thinking." A pause. "You do that too."

He looked at the pen in his hand and set it down.

"I'm sorry," he said. "That's — I don't know the right thing to say to that."

"There isn't a right thing." She looked at him. "I'm not here because I think you're him. I want to be very clear about that. I'm here because you're adjacent to this investigation and because—" She stopped. "Because I needed to tell someone what I knew, and you were the right person. Which makes no logical sense."

"It makes sense," he said.

They sat in the secondary office of the Pane Four transit wing, and she told him what she knew about the convergence investigation — not classified details, nothing that would compromise the case, but the shape of it: the scaffold, the motive she'd found, the fact that the person who'd built it was doing it for the same reason she'd been standing in this room for the last hour. The pull toward a version of the world where the person you lost was still there.

He listened.

"You know what you're going to have to do," he said at the end.

"Yes."

"The master seam. The scaffold needs a trigger, which means it needs a master node. When you seal that, the scaffold can't activate."

"Yes."

"You know where it is."

"Not precisely. Close." She stood. "I needed to tell you before I found it."

He looked at her. "Why?"

She didn't have a clean answer for that. "Because it seemed wrong," she said, "to have been this close to something important and to have said nothing."

He recognized it — the echo of Orda Rein's letter, though she hadn't told him about Orda Rein. But he was a reconciler too, in his way, and he heard the frequency of the thing.

"All right," he said. "Tell me when you find it."

She crossed back to Pane Three at eleven forty-five. She logged the return time and listed the purpose as extended case review.

Chapter 27: Orvie Strand

The incident with Orvie Strand was not dramatic. That was the thing that struck Maren most, afterward.

It began on a Monday morning when two residents of Rue Varek called the Bureau's non-emergency line, within twelve minutes of each other, to report that a woman they knew — their neighbor, a retired schoolteacher named Orvie Strand, same woman who'd reported the lost hour two weeks earlier — was simultaneously at her usual table in the café on the corner and walking her usual route along the Linden cut.

Both callers described her correctly. Both locations were confirmed when Maren and Idris arrived.

Orvie Strand was sitting in the café with a cup of tea and the morning paper, looking at the crossword. She was also walking along the Linden cut with a canvas bag and the particular determined pace of a morning errand. Both versions of her were solid. Both had the same coat, same hair, same glasses. Both were, by all observable measures, fully present.

Maren went to the café first, because the café was enclosed. Idris went to the Linden cut.

"Ms. Strand," Maren said, sliding into the seat opposite. "I'm Maren Vos, from the Tessera Bureau. You spoke to one of my colleagues a few weeks ago."

"The Bureau." Orvie Strand put the paper down. "I wondered when you'd come back."

"Have you been aware of anything unusual this morning?"

"I've been aware," Orvie said carefully, "that I have two full sets of memories for today. I woke up, made tea, came to the café. I also woke up, made tea, and went to the market for bread." She looked at the table. "Both mornings are complete. I remember both."

"Are you in any distress?"

"I'm in a state I don't have a word for." She picked up her tea. "The other one — the one on the Linden cut, I presume — does she know she's here too?"

"My colleague is speaking to her now."

"Is she all right?"

"Yes."

Orvie Strand looked at the window. Outside, the morning continued: a tram, a couple walking a pushchair, the ordinary motion of the city.

"Which one of me is the real one?" she said.

"You're both real," Maren said. "The membrane between this pane and the adjacent one is thin in this district. Your counterpart in that pane — who looks and thinks and is like you in almost every respect — is overlapping with you. Both of you are experiencing the other's day. It's called co-location stress."

"Is it going to resolve?"

"If we do our job correctly, yes."

"And if you don't?"

Maren made the calculation she'd made with Yse Mander. The honest answer and the useless answer.

"If the membrane continues to thin," she said, "the two of you will continue to converge. Eventually the co-location becomes complete. At that point—" She stopped.

"One of us goes away," Orvie Strand said.

"We're not going to let that happen."

Orvie looked at the crossword. "I'd like to finish this first," she said. "If that's all right. I've almost got twelve across."

Maren waited while she finished the crossword. It took four minutes. Orvie Strand had a clear, precise mind and she used it even under conditions that would have sent most people to the floor.

The Bureau's containment team arrived and deployed a signal dampener in the Rue Varek corridor — a broader version of the collar device they'd used on Holt, installed in the building's infrastructure, designed to maintain the membrane integrity in this specific block at an artificial threshold that prevented full co-location. It was a temporary measure. It worked by requiring constant power input.

The Orvie Strand event went into the case file as Priority One. It was also, Maren knew, the moment when the investigation became urgent in a different way — not months, not weeks. The scaffold was activating under its own pressure. Whoever the Architect was, whether they triggered it or not, the convergence was beginning.

Chapter 28: The Media Silence

Director Quist called an emergency internal session on Friday. Not the full Bureau — twelve people in a secure briefing room, the heads of each division, Maren and Cyriac and two senior reconcilers. She distributed the convergence summary and let people read it.

The room was very quiet while they read.

Then there were questions, and Quist answered them with the calm that was her primary leadership tool. Yes, the Varek district was experiencing active co-location events. Yes, the signal dampeners were in place and functioning. No, the public communications office was not to be briefed at this stage. No external notification, no public statement.

"The legal basis for containment without public notification—" one of the division heads began.

"Is the same basis we use for every seam event we've ever managed," Quist said. "Bureau Protocol Nine. The public has never been told the panes exist. That has not changed."

"The people in the affected district—"

"Are receiving individual notification through the remediation protocol. Case by case. This is how we've always managed it."

Maren sat and listened. She thought about Orvie Strand finishing the crossword. She thought about Corl-Kael Vane and his two names. She thought about the scale of what was beginning in the Varek quarter, and the fact that the signal dampeners had an operational limit of thirty days before the membrane thinning would exceed their capacity.

"What's the plan for the master seam?" she asked.

Quist looked at her. "That's your division's work to complete."

"I need to know who's authorized for the sealing operation."

"You are. And Lan if you need a second."

"I need Cyriac."

"You'll have Cyriac."

The other question — the Architect question — she didn't raise in this room. She raised it afterward, in the corridor, with Cyriac, just the two of them.

"Osk is still in the building," she said.

"Yes."

"He's watching," Cyriac agreed. He looked at the wall with the expression he used for things he'd decided. "I need to tell you something I should have told you earlier."

She looked at him.

She began preparing for the end of the investigation. She did not know yet what the end looked like. She had most of the shape of it, and the shape was hard.

Chapter 29: The Access Block

The access block trace had been sitting in the case file for two weeks, waiting for confirmation. She had the name. She needed the trail.

She spent a day in the Bureau's system records with Ferris, who had the patience for this kind of work that she lacked and who asked good questions while maintaining the professional incuriosity that made him useful. They followed the access block — the code cluster assigned to Rendel Osk's Bureau workstation — through six months of transit log entries, maintenance records, and seam-modeling division outputs.

The pattern that emerged was methodical.

Osk had been accessing substrate maps for the Varek district at irregular intervals over eight months — always at odd hours, always through legitimate work requests that were technically within his division's scope. The seam-modeling division was responsible for predictive modeling of membrane behavior in high-stress zones, which meant Varek was legitimately in his portfolio. Every access was defensible individually. Collectively they were a blueprint.

He'd also accessed the pre-consolidation seam-building manuals, which were in the Bureau's technical archive. Three separate requests over six months, each for a different section. Together they covered the complete methodology for manufactured aperture construction using pre-polymer materials.

The materials synthesis lab logs — cross-referenced against the list Maren had — showed six authorized uses of the lab by Osk over four months, all listed under routine equipment testing. The compound he'd been synthesizing was the same compound she'd found in the Caldwell housing and Ellisen's wall.

Ferris noted, without looking up from his screen: "Thorough. Slow build. Very patient."

"Yes."

"This is a person who has been thinking about this for a long time."

"Three years, probably. Based on when his husband died."

Ferris looked up. "His husband."

"Fenwick Osk. Died at Thornside. Four years ago." She kept her voice flat. "Alive in Pane Four."

Ferris was quiet. He was not a sentimental man, but he was a fair one. He made the calculation she could see him making.

"If the convergence succeeded," he said.

"Fenwick Osk would presumably be drawn toward the merged point along with all the other Pane Four residents. Yes."

"But the co-location problem—"

"Fenwick is dead in Pane Three. There's no Pane Three version to conflict with." She paused. "That's probably the one thing Osk has in his favor, logistically. No co-location stress for Fenwick. Just a man who exists in Pane Four crossing into a merged Pane Three-Four reality."

Ferris processed this. "That's not nothing."

"No. It's not nothing."

She closed the access block trail file. She had the full documentation for the formal case against Rendel Osk. She had motive, means, methodology, and timeline. It was thorough and clean and would be prosecutable if Quist allowed a prosecution, which was the part she still hadn't resolved.

Chapter 30: Two Threads Meet

Idris came to her desk on a Thursday morning with a printed memo in his hand. He put it on her desk without speaking and looked at her.

The memo was from the Bureau's inter-pane dispute archive. It was four years old. It was a records classification decision — the decision to classify Saul Lan's misdirection event as a natural drift event rather than a Bureau procedural error.

The authorization signature on the classification decision was from the seam-modeling division.

The authorization code was Rendel Osk's.

She read it twice.

"He was the one who buried it," Idris said. His voice was steady.

"He was a senior analyst in the division that handled inter-pane incident classifications. Yes." She put the memo down. "He classified your brother's misdirection as natural drift."

"Which protected the Bureau from a procedural liability claim."

"Yes."

Idris sat down. He sat carefully, the way you sit when you need to not do something with your hands. "He was cleaning up a mess that wasn't his. Doing his job. Being thorough. Protecting the institution."

"He didn't know about you," she said. "The misclassification was four years ago, a year before he started building the scaffold. He didn't know there was a Bureau employee with a personal stake in it."

"I know."

"The decision was wrong. The misclassification hurt you. But I don't think he made it because he was a bad person."

Idris looked at the memo. "He made it because that's what the Bureau does."

She didn't argue with that. It was accurate.

"The correction is in process," she said. "Ferris has it in the thirty-day channel. It'll be corrected."

He nodded.

"And Idris." She looked at him directly. "The fact that Osk's access block shows up in both threads — the scaffold investigation and Saul's classification — that's not connected. I don't want you making it connected."

"It connects through the Bureau's culture," he said quietly.

"Which is a real thing and not what this investigation is about."

He was silent for a moment. Then: "What do we do now?"

"We go find Osk," she said. "And we have a conversation."

Chapter 31: The Name

The name had been in the file for days before she said it aloud.

She said it to Idris first, on the way to the seam-modeling division: Rendel Osk, twelve-year Bureau analyst, desk in sub-level two, the person whose access block had done all of it.

Idris had had the name for a day, since the memo. He heard her say it without reacting visibly, which told her he'd been sitting with it in the same way she had — turning it over, looking at the angles, deciding what to do with the fact that the Architect was a specific, legible person with a specific, legible grief.

"What's the plan?" he said.

"There isn't one yet."

"That's not—"

"I mean I don't have a plan for the conversation. I know what information I need. I need to know the scaffold mechanics — specifically whether he has a dead-man mechanism that activates it automatically if he's removed, or whether manual triggering is the only option."

"Because if it's only manual, removing him stops it."

"And if it's automatic, we need to know the trigger conditions before we move."

She also needed to know whether the master node — the center of the scaffold, the icon shop or the area around it — was the only viable sealing point. Cyriac's analysis suggested it was, but Cyriac's analysis was based on the external evidence. Osk would know the internal mechanics.

"What do we do about Quist?" Idris asked.

"She's getting her daily report. It'll include that we've identified the account holder."

"Just the account holder? Not the name?"

"She'll have the name tonight. I want to talk to him first."

They walked the rest of the way to sub-level two in silence. The Bureau basement had a different smell from the upper floors — older, more mineral, the damp of building infrastructure and old seam-sealant in the walls. Maren had worked down here for two weeks during a cartography audit years ago and had developed a relationship with the particular quality of its quiet.

The seam-modeling division was a set of six workrooms off a central corridor. Three of the workrooms had people in them. The fourth had Rendel Osk.

She had looked up his ID photo: a man in his late forties with a compact, concentrated look and the slightly hunched posture of someone who spent most of his time reading small print. He was at his desk when they arrived in the doorway, and he looked up, and she saw him do the calculation that intelligent people do when they recognize something has arrived that they've been expecting.

"Reconciler Vos," he said.

She had not introduced herself. He knew her face.

"You know who I am," she said.

"I know who you are," he confirmed. His voice was quiet and careful. He looked at Idris. "And you're Lan." He paused. "I know your brother's situation. The correction is in process."

Idris said nothing.

"May we come in?" Maren said.

"Yes," he said. "Please close the door."

Chapter 32: The Other Maren

Before she confronted Osk properly — before the sealing and the report and all of it — she needed to see Petra.

She told herself she needed to see Petra for investigative reasons. This was not entirely false: the Pane Four version of herself was likely aware of the convergence events at Pane Four's end, might have information, might illuminate aspects of the case. This was a legitimate professional reason for an interview.

She also told herself that she'd felt, ever since Cyriac had described the Pane One-Two scaffold-builder who'd stopped their own convergence, a specific need to look at the choice from the other side.

She crossed on a Thursday afternoon with a single-day permit and found the Pane Four Bureau's personnel directory.

Petra Vos was a Senior Analyst — not a reconciler, an analyst — in the Pane Four Bureau's statistical modeling division. She had been an analyst for six years. Before that she had been a reconciler for three years and then a field supervisor for two. The gap in her Bureau record — the point where field work stopped and administration began — corresponded to a date eight years ago. A transfer request, voluntary, signed off by the Pane Four Director of that era.

Maren stood in the corridor outside the statistical modeling division for slightly longer than she'd intended.

The door was open. The woman at the far desk looked up and across the room before Maren reached the threshold. Recognition — not the personal kind, the structural kind. The mirror kind.

Petra Vos was forty-four. Of course she was. She was heavier than Maren, in a way that suggested not letting that become a point of concern. Her hair was different — shorter, practical. She had a quality of administrative calm about her, the kind that Maren associated with people who had made peace with a particular desk.

"Maren Vos," Petra said. She said it the way you say a fact.

"Yes."

"From Pane Three."

"Yes."

Petra looked at her with the peculiar equanimity of someone who had clearly been expecting this, or something like it, for a while. "You'd better sit down."

They sat. The office was quiet, the same kind of institutional quiet as the Bureau's offices in every pane.

"The convergence in Varek," Maren said.

"We're aware of it on this side." Petra folded her hands. "My Bureau has been monitoring for four months. The seam events have been visible in our substrate data." She paused. "We know it's being built from your side."

"It is." Maren looked at her. "I wanted to ask you about what you know. And—"

"And about the choice," Petra said.

"Yes."

Petra was quiet for a moment. She had the unhurried quality of someone who had already processed the difficult parts.

"I was on the Varek station team," she said. "Eight years ago. Same assignment you were on — the 41-Omega collapse, whatever you call it here. I was the senior reconciler." She paused. "I had filed a transfer request two weeks before. The Pane Four Director at the time wanted me for a supervisory role in the analysis division — I was better suited to that work, I'd been told, I'd been telling myself. I was tired. I was frightened of the field in a way I hadn't admitted."

"So when the collapse happened—"

"I was already partially out. My tether authorization was still active but my head wasn't. I was at the outer threshold when it went. I was already half-thinking about the desk in the analysis division and the work I'd be doing there." She met Maren's eyes. "I got out. My partner didn't. His name was Aldo. Different from your Wynn — different person, different story — but he didn't come back, and I had been the one who was supposed to be next to him."

Maren said nothing.

"I've lived with it eight years," Petra said. "I'm not going to tell you how. I'll just tell you I have." She paused. "I didn't transfer because I was tired. That's the simple version. The true version is that I was frightened and I let the fear make the decision, and then I rationalized it, and then there was no Aldo. And I've been an analyst ever since."

"That's—" Maren stopped.

"Not the same as what you did," Petra said. "You stayed. You were there all the way through the threshold. You lost him the same way but from closer." She looked at her desk. "Our losses are not the same shape. But I think you came here partly because you wanted to see what it looks like to have made the choice you didn't make."

"Yes," Maren said. "I did."

"And?"

Maren looked at her. At this woman who was her, sideways. At the administrative calm, the shorter hair, the slight extra weight and the peace that might or might not be real.

"You seem all right," she said.

"Most of the time," Petra said. "Is that what you needed to know?"

"I think so."

"Then go seal the master seam," Petra said. "Don't wait too long. The membrane on our side is at thirty-eight percent permeability and climbing."

Chapter 33: The Motive, Confirmed

She went back to Osk's office the day after Petra.

She had left the first visit short — introductions, confirmation that he knew who she was and what she was investigating, a single question about the dead-man mechanism (there was none; manual trigger only). She had not asked everything she needed. She had told herself she was letting him settle.

The truth was she had needed to see Petra first. She needed to understand the shape of the grief that drove this before she could look at it without prejudice.

She went alone this time. Idris was on the afternoon monitoring run.

Osk was in his office. He had a quality of waiting about him now — not anxiety, more like a person who has put everything down and is seeing what happens next. His desk was unusually tidy, which she noted.

"Tell me about Fenwick," she said.

He looked at her for a long moment. Then he told her.

Fenwick Osk had been forty years old when the transit accident at Thornside killed him. It was a real accident — not a seam event, not a Bureau misclassification. A vehicle accident on the street adjacent to the Thornside aperture station. Wrong place, specific moment. There was nothing the Bureau could have prevented, nothing anyone could have done.

And in Pane Four, Fenwick was alive. He had been a street away from the accident, in Pane Four's equivalent of that moment. Same man, same morning, five meters and one membrane between alive and dead.

"I found out about Pane Four from the Bureau's records," Osk said. "I'd been here twelve years. I had clearance. I looked him up because I needed to know if it was real."

"And it was real."

"He's teaching at the Halloway Technical Institute. He has the same laugh. He cooks the same things on Sunday. I know because I've been watching the Pane Four sub-office transit reports for signs of him. He comes to the transit station occasionally for cross-pane documentation on a project." Osk's voice was flat and methodical. "He doesn't know I'm watching. He doesn't know I exist."

"What did you think the merger would give you?"

"A world where he exists in the same space as I do. Where I can—" He stopped. He looked at his hands. "I know how it sounds. I built a convergence scaffold to collapse the membrane between two inhabited panes affecting thousands of people, because I wanted my husband back."

"That's what you did," she said. Without judgment in her voice. Stating it.

"Yes." He looked up. "And I know what the co-location events mean. I've been reading the case reports — your case reports, the ones you file through the standard system. Orvie Strand. The dog. The man with two names. I know what I'm doing to them." His voice was unchanged, which was worse than if it had broken. "I've known for weeks. I haven't triggered it anyway."

"Why not?"

"Because I haven't been able to not know what it costs." He looked at his desk. "If Corl Vane dissolves into Kael Vane, or the other way around, that person — that specific person — is gone. I've been telling myself the merged person would be a synthesis. That no one would really be lost. The archive data doesn't support that." He was very still. "One person is erased."

"Yes."

"And I've been standing here for two weeks with the trigger and not using it." He looked at her. "I don't know what to do with that."

"I do," she said.

Chapter 34: The Report That Didn't Mention Petra

She wrote the full convergence case report over two evenings, in her flat's back room, with the heating on this time.

The report was accurate and complete. It named Rendel Osk as the architect of the convergence scaffold. It documented the methodology — the manufactured apertures, the forged permits, the materials synthesis, the substrate access. It documented the motive: grief, the death of Fenwick Osk, the fact of Fenwick's survival in Pane Four. It included the evidence trail.

It did not mention Petra. The visit to Petra was her own business and produced no material evidence relevant to the case.

It also did not, in the formal report, include the observation that Maren had spent approximately forty minutes in the Pane Four transit wing on an evening two weeks ago, in conversation with the chief transit officer there, for reasons she had listed as extended case review. That observation was also her own business.

She submitted the report to Quist's office by the secure document channel at eleven on a Thursday night.

She also wrote, separately, a report to the Bureau's oversight board — the inter-pane administrative body that had jurisdiction over the Director and the Bureau's internal conduct. She wrote this one more carefully, because she was reporting two things: the convergence event and Rendel Osk, and the Thornside aperture authorization that had used Cyriac's signature and had been issued from Quist's office on a date that did not align with any standard maintenance review.

She did not know what that authorization had been for. She knew what it looked like.

She sent the oversight board report two hours after the Quist report. She addressed it to the Board's senior member, a man named Vass who she had met twice and whose reputation was for exactness and impartiality.

Then she made tea and sat in the kitchen and looked out the window at the Halloway night — the lights of the city's south quarter, the bridge in the middle distance, the ordinary permanence of a place that existed and was sufficient.

Chapter 35: Osk's Motive Laid Out

She told Idris the full shape of it on a Friday morning, before the day started.

She told him about Fenwick. She told him about the three years of scaffold construction, the seven months of active placement, the two weeks since the Varek storefronts when Osk had held the trigger and not used it.

Idris listened. He was quiet through all of it.

When she finished he said: "He knew what the co-location events were costing."

"He knew."

"So he stopped. He just — stopped. He has the trigger, he's done the work, and he stopped himself."

"He described it as not being able to not know." She paused. "Which I think is—"

"Human," Idris said.

"Yes."

He turned the case summary over on his desk. Outside the window the Bureau courtyard had its usual morning light — grey, flat, sufficient. Sparrows on the window ledge. A maintenance worker crossing the concrete with a broom.

"The difference between him and me is very small," Idris said.

"I know."

"I've been making unauthorized crossings to see Saul. I knew the protocol and I did it anyway because I needed to. He built a convergence scaffold for the same reason, just bigger."

"The scale is not the same."

"No. But the impulse." He looked at the summary. "He's a person who did something very wrong for the most understandable reason I can imagine."

"Yes."

"What happens to him?"

She had been thinking about this. "That depends on Quist. She wants it quiet. The oversight board report changes that — they have independent jurisdiction, and Vass runs a clean ship. There'll be a formal review." She paused. "I don't know what a prosecution looks like, in the end. What I know is that the scaffold needs to be sealed and Osk needs to be removed from access to Bureau systems."

"And Fenwick."

She looked at him.

"Fenwick is alive in Pane Four," Idris said. "He's going to continue being alive in Pane Four. Osk is going to continue knowing that." He paused. "That doesn't resolve."

"No," she said. "It doesn't." She stood. "Some things don't resolve. You live alongside them."

She went to make coffee and said nothing else about it, because there was nothing else to say that would not be either untrue or unbearable, and she had learned to distinguish between those two categories.



Chapter 36: The Difference

The question stayed with her after Idris went home. The difference between him and you is what.

She did not answer it at her desk and she did not answer it on the tram. She answered it at eleven that night, standing at the Caldwell seam station with her badge in her hand.

The station was empty at that hour. The duty officer had logged off. The corridor mouth stood open, its membrane shimmering faintly, the way still water shimmers when something has just moved under it. A crossing to Pane Four took her badge and a permit code and four minutes of walking. She had both halves of what she needed. She had the code memorized. Wynn finished his shift at midnight and walked home along the Verrand embankment, and she knew that, because she had read his file, and reading his file had been its own small crossing she had not filed.

She stood at the corridor mouth and did not move.

It would be easy. That was the whole horror of it. Osk had not begun with a scaffold. He had begun, surely, with a night like this one, a badge in his hand, a man on the other side who was dead where he was standing and alive four minutes' walk away. He had stood here, or somewhere like here, and he had asked himself why the membrane should be allowed to keep what it kept. And he had not stepped back.

Maren stepped back.

She did it the way she did everything now, deliberately, one motion at a time, because she did not trust the motions to happen on their own. She closed the permit screen. She returned the badge to her coat. She walked out of the station into the ordinary cold of her own pane, where the man she wanted was eight years gone and no corridor led to him, and she let that be true, because it was, and because the alternative was a scaffold.

The difference was nothing. The difference was a single step, taken or not taken, every night, forever. She understood Osk completely. That was not the same as forgiving him, and it was not the same as joining him, and she would have to hold all three of those things in the same hand for the rest of the case.

She walked home. She did not sleep. In the morning she was at her desk before Idris, and when he came in she said, "I have an answer to your question," and he looked up, and she said, "The difference is that I went home. That's all it is. It isn't much."

"It's enough," Idris said.

"It has to be," she said. "It's all there is."

Chapter 37: Cyriac's Record

Cyriac Holm kept his office colder than the rest of the Bureau, because the charts kept better and because he liked it. Maren found him at the long table under the lamps, a revision spread out in front of him, the convergence scaffold drawn in red against the grey of the city's structural grid.

"You knew," she said. She did not sit.

He did not pretend to misunderstand. He set down his pen. "I knew someone was forging permits in March," he said. "I knew the signatures were being routed through a modeling block. I did not know the name and I told myself that mattered."

"Why didn't you report it."

"Because reporting it meant stopping it," Cyriac said. "And stopping it meant going down to that lab and looking at whoever it was and telling them no. And I have found, in my life, that I am very good at charts and very bad at telling a grieving person no." He looked at her. "I lost my wife to a transit reclassification fourteen years ago. Not a death. A reclassification. She crossed on a maintenance permit and the permit was found to be improperly filed and she was returned to Pane Two, where she had been born, and the Bureau called it a correction. We had been married for nine years in Pane Three. The correction did not care."

Maren sat down then.

"I am not telling you this so that you will be gentle with me," Cyriac said. "I am telling you so that you understand what you are walking into when you walk into that lab. The man down there is not a monster and he is not a stranger. He is the most ordinary thing the Bureau produces. He is someone who could not tell the membrane no." He pushed a data spindle across the table. "Everything I have. Permit signatures, routing, the scaffold geometry, the projected merge timeline. It is complete. I should have given it to you in March."

She took the spindle. "Why now."

"Because you asked me a direct question," he said, "and I am too old to have another fourteen years of telling myself the name didn't matter." He picked his pen back up. "Seal it, Vos. Whatever it costs. Seal it and let the rest of us live in one city like we're supposed to."

Chapter 38: What Quist Wants

Director Quist received Maren at the end of the day, when the Bureau had thinned to the night staff and the windows had gone to lamp-yellow squares. She did not offer tea. She offered a chair, and the chair was its own kind of message.

"You've identified him," Quist said.

"Rendel Osk. Seam-modeling division. Twelve years." Maren kept her hands still in her lap. "His husband died in a Pane Three transit accident four years ago. The same man is alive in Pane Four. Osk has been building the scaffold for three."

Quist nodded slowly, the way a person nods at a diagnosis they have already been given and are hearing confirmed. "And the convergence?"

"Past forty percent, by Holm's projection. It can still be sealed cleanly. Not for much longer."

"Then it will be sealed." Quist folded her hands on the desk. "Quietly. The scaffold dismantled, the seams sealed, Osk removed from access. No oversight referral. No public record. The convergence never reaches a name in a file."

"He merged two people, Director. Orvie Strand spent four hours as two women. The Bureau put a blackout on it."

"I am aware of what the Bureau put a blackout on." Quist's voice did not rise. It never rose; that was the frightening thing about her. "I authorized the blackout. I am asking you to understand what an oversight referral does. It does not punish one analyst. It opens the entire seam-permit system to external review. It tells the oversight board, and through them the city, that the membranes are not secure. Do you know what happens to a population that learns the wall between worlds has a forger's door in it?"

"They learn the truth."

"They panic," Quist said. "And panic at a seam is how people die. I have buried reconcilers, Vos. I have buried civilians who crossed in a crowd because someone shouted that the other side was safer. I am not protecting the institution. I am protecting the eleven thousand people in the Varek quarter who currently believe the ground under their beds is solid." She let that sit. "Seal it. Remove him. Bring me the scaffold geometry. And let me decide what reaches the board."

Maren said nothing. She had already decided what would reach the board. She found she could sit in the same room as Quist and hold that decision and not show it, and she understood that this, too, was a skill the Bureau had taught her, and that it was not a clean one.

"Of course, Director," she said.

Chapter 39: The Worst Part

She crossed to Pane Four to tell Wynn herself. She told herself it was procedure — the seal would close his pane's access at the Varek node, and he was the chief transit officer there, and he had a right to be informed. It was even true. It was just not the reason.

He was at his desk when she came through. He looked up and saw her face and stood and walked her out to the embankment without a word, because some part of him that was not the dead man and was entirely the dead man knew how to read her.

"You found him," Wynn said, when they were at the rail above the Verrand.

"I found him. I'm going to seal the master seam at Varek. When I do, the scaffold collapses and your pane drifts back out of range. The corridor I used to come here closes. There won't be another."

She had practiced saying it. She had not practiced the silence after, which was the part she could not control.

"I've known since you came to see me," Wynn said. "The first time. You came and you told me my counterpart died, and you looked at me like I was a window you weren't allowed to open." He watched the water. "I'm not him, Maren. I never was. I have my own pane and my own desk and a man named Corrin who's waiting for me to come home and complain about the night shift. I'm sorry your Wynn died. I've been sorry for weeks. But you didn't come here for me. You came here for him, and I can't be him, and the kindest thing either of us can do is let the corridor close."

"You're not angry," she said.

"No."

"I keep waiting for you to be angry. It would be easier."

"I know it would." He turned to face her. "I'm not angry. I'm alive. I'm going to go home tonight and be alive, and you're going to seal the seam and go home and be alive, and that's not a tragedy, that's just two people in two cities." He almost smiled. "That's the worst part, isn't it. That it's not a tragedy. That it's only sad."

It was. She had wanted it to be enormous and it was only sad, and she walked back to the corridor carrying the only-sadness of it like a stone she would set down somewhere private later, when there was time, which there was not yet.

Chapter 40: The Lab

The seam lab was three levels below the Bureau's public floors, in the part of the building that had no windows because it had no need of them. Maren went down alone. She had not told Quist she was going and she had not brought Idris, because what she was about to do was not an arrest, and she did not want a witness for the part of it that was not procedure.

Rendel Osk was at the console when she came in. He was a narrow man with grey at his temples and reading glasses pushed up onto his forehead, and he looked exactly like what he was, which was an analyst who had been at the same console for twelve years. He did not run. He saw her badge and her face and he simply turned his chair to face her, the way a man turns to face a guest he has been expecting for a long time.

"Reconciler Vos," he said.

"Mr. Osk."

There was a second chair against the wall. She pulled it over and sat down across from him, close, the way you sit with someone whose statement you are taking, or with someone at a deathbed. The scaffold's master readout glowed behind him, the convergence figure ticking in slow tenths.

"I could call this in," she said. "Containment would be here in four minutes. You'd be removed from access, the scaffold would be frozen, and you'd spend the rest of tonight in a holding room answering questions from people who are frightened of you." She set her hands on her knees. "I don't want to do it that way. I want you to tell me how it started. From the beginning. The real beginning, not the technical one."

Osk took the glasses off his forehead and folded them and held them in both hands, and for a moment he looked not like a criminal and not like a monster but like every grieving person she had ever sat across from in fourteen years of this work, which was to say he looked like her.

"It started," he said, "with a night at a seam station. I had a badge in my hand."

"I know," Maren said. "Tell me the rest."

Chapter 41: Forty Percent

He told it plainly, without performance, which made it worse.

Fenwick had died on a Tuesday. A transit accident in the Caldwell interchange, a maintenance seam that failed during a routine recalibration, a queue of seven people and Fenwick third in line. Three died. The Bureau called it equipment fatigue and paid the families a settlement and resealed the seam, and Osk, who modeled seams for a living, who knew the structural grid of Halloway better than the men who built it, had gone back to his console the following Monday because there was nowhere else to put himself.

"And one day I pulled the Pane Four registry," he said. "I don't know why. To hurt myself, probably. And he was in it. Fenwick. Alive. Chief horticulturist at the Varek glasshouses, married to a man named Aldous, no memory of me, no reason to have one. And I sat here and I thought: he is four minutes away. The membrane is the only thing between us. And the membrane is not a law of nature. The membrane is a structure. And I model structures."

"So you built the scaffold."

"I built it slowly. A seam at a time. I told myself I would only bring the panes close enough to see. Then close enough to cross without a corridor. Then—" He stopped. "You know how it goes. You stood at a station tonight. I can see it on you. You know exactly how it goes, because you almost went, and the only reason you didn't is that you've had eight years of practice not going and I'd had four days."

Maren did not deny it. "Where is it now."

"Forty-one percent." He gestured at the readout without looking at it. "If you seal the master node at Varek tonight, the scaffold collapses and the panes separate cleanly. No further merging. The people already affected stabilize in whichever pane they're strongest in. If you wait—" He set the glasses down on the console. "If you wait three weeks, the panes cross fifty percent, and after fifty there is no separating them. They finish merging on their own. And then there is only one Halloway, and everyone who exists in both panes becomes one person, and no one chooses which one."

"How many exist in both."

"In the Varek quarter? At full merge?" He looked at her. "About nine hundred."

Chapter 42: Which One Survives

"What happens to them," Maren said. "The nine hundred. When two versions become one."

Osk was quiet for a moment. He had clearly asked himself this question many times and had clearly never found an answer he could live behind.

"One self persists," he said. "The other dissolves. The memories don't merge — that's the thing people assume, that you'd become some sum of both lives. You don't. One continuity survives intact and the other simply stops, mid-sentence, and the surviving one doesn't even feel the loss, because the loss is of a self they never experienced being." He turned his glasses over in his hands. "Orvie Strand was two women for four hours. When the local convergence relaxed, she resolved back to one. We don't know which one. She doesn't know there was a question. There's a version of her that spent four hours alive and then was gone, and the version sitting in her kitchen tonight has no memory of it and never will."

"And you can't predict which survives."

"No one can. It isn't strength or health or anything you could weigh. It's substrate-dependent in a way the models can't resolve. It's a coin." He said the word flatly. "Nine hundred coins. That's what full merge is. Nine hundred people who become one person each, and a coin decides which one, and the one who loses doesn't even get to know they were in the game."

Maren sat with it. She had come down here half-prepared to find a man she could hate, and instead she had found a man who had done a monstrous thing and could describe its monstrousness more precisely than she could, which was its own kind of punishment, and not enough of one.

"You knew this," she said. "You knew it was a coin. And you built it anyway."

"I told myself Fenwick would be the one who survived." His voice did not break; it was past breaking, worn smooth. "I told myself I'd model it so he'd be strongest in the merged substrate. I couldn't. There's no modeling it. So I told myself a different thing. I told myself that a coin was better than a wall. That a fifty percent chance of having him was better than a certainty of not." He looked up. "It isn't. I knew it wasn't even while I was telling it to myself. But grief isn't an argument, Reconciler. You can lose an argument with grief every single day and still do what it tells you."

"Not every day," Maren said. "I went home tonight."

"Yes," Osk said. "You did. I've been wondering all evening how you managed it. I never could."

Chapter 43: Saul

Idris was waiting at her desk when she came up from the lab, and she saw before he spoke that he had done the arithmetic.

"If they merge," he said. "Panes Three and Four. At Varek." He had a memo in his hand and he did not look at it. "Saul's in Pane Four. He was born in Pane Three. He's in the Pane Three registry as deceased — that's how they classified the accident, they didn't move his record, they closed it. So if the panes merge—"

"Idris."

"There's a Pane Three record that says he's dead and a Pane Four man who's alive. Those conflict. That's exactly the kind of conflict that resolves at merge." He set the memo down very carefully, as if it might go off. "I've spent two years trying to get my brother home. And the thing that would actually erase the membrane between us might also erase him. I don't even know if it would. Osk doesn't know. Nobody knows. It's a coin."

"It's a coin," Maren agreed.

"So I have to want you to seal it," Idris said. "I have to stand here and want the wall to stay up, because the wall is the only thing keeping Saul from being a coin toss. The wall I've hated for two years." He laughed, once, without any humor in it. "Do you know what the worst part of this job is, Vos? It's not the grief. It's that the kind thing and the safe thing are almost never the same thing, and you have to pick safe, every time, and call it kindness so you can sleep."

"You don't have to call it anything," Maren said. "You just have to pick it."

He nodded. He picked it. She watched him pick it and understood that he had just become a reconciler in the only way that counted, which was not at orientation and not with a badge, but in the moment he chose the wall over his own brother and did not let himself off the hook for it afterward.

"Go home," she told him. "I need you sharp tomorrow. We seal the day after."

"And Saul stays where he is."

"Saul stays alive where he is," Maren said. "On the wrong side of a wall. It's not nothing, Idris. I'd have given a great deal, eight years ago, for my dead to be alive on the wrong side of a wall."

Chapter 44: The Node

The day before the seal, Maren crossed to Pane Four one last time to look at the master node with her own eyes, because she did not trust a readout for a thing this large, and because forty minutes of technical work on a structure this hot left no room for a surprise.

The node was housed in a Varek substation that existed, now, in both panes at once — that was what a master seam was, a place where the membrane had been worn so thin it was structurally shared. She came through the Pane Four corridor and found the substation humming at a pitch she felt in her teeth. The walls were warm. Condensation stood on the conduit housings. In the corner of the room the air had the faint doubled quality she had learned to recognize over the last weeks, the look of a space that could not quite decide how many of itself there were.

She walked the node slowly, reading it with her hands and her instruments. Osk's scaffold was elegant; she would give him that. He had threaded the convergence through the substation's own structural grid so that the panes pulled toward each other along the path of least resistance, the way water finds the crack already in the stone. The master seam at the center was the size of a doorway and it was not shimmering now. It was open. She could see, through it, the same room from the other pane's angle, offset by a few degrees, lit slightly differently, and she understood that the two rooms were most of the way to being one room, and that in three weeks they would be, and that she was the only thing scheduled to prevent it.

The seam was threading into the Pane Three substrate already. She could read the filaments of it, fine as frost, reaching into the wall that should have been solid and inviolate and was neither. Sealing it would not be cutting a thread. It would be cauterizing a wound that had grown into the flesh around it. Forty minutes, if nothing went wrong. Longer, if the substrate fought her.

She made her measurements and wrote them down by hand, and she was capping her pen when she heard the corridor cycle behind her, and she turned, and it was Wynn.

Chapter 45: Do It Right

"You shouldn't be here," Maren said.

"Neither should you, by your own rules." Wynn stayed near the corridor mouth, hands in his pockets. "I'm the transit officer for this node. It flagged a Pane Three reconciler doing structural readings on a shared seam. I came to see who was about to close my station." He looked at the doorway-sized seam, at the offset room beyond it. "It's you. Of course it's you."

"Tomorrow," she said. "I seal it tomorrow."

He came and stood beside her at the node, not touching, looking through the seam at the doubled room the way she had been looking at it. For a while neither of them said anything. The substation hummed in their teeth.

"It's beautiful, in a way," Wynn said. "What he built. Terrible. But beautiful. He found the exact path and he walked the whole city down it." He shook his head. "People will never know how close it came. You'll seal it and file it and in a year there'll be a procedure named after it and nobody will know there was a man down here who almost gave nine hundred people to a coin because he missed his husband."

"You sound like you admire him."

"I understand him," Wynn said. "Same as you do. That's not admiration. That's just the cost of being a person who's lost someone." He turned to her. "Seal it right, Maren. Don't rush it because I'm standing here and don't drag it because I'm standing here. Forty minutes, clean, the way you'd do it if it were any other node and any other man on the far side. Promise me that."

"I promise."

"Then I'll go home and let you work." He stepped back toward the corridor. At the mouth of it he stopped. "Are you angry?" he asked. "At me. For being here. For being alive and not being him."

"No," she said, and found it was true. "I'm not angry."

"Good." The old smile, almost. "Neither am I. I'm alive. Go close your seam, Reconciler. Live in your one city. It's enough. It has to be." He went through, and the corridor cycled shut behind him, and she stood alone at the node with her measurements and did not let herself watch the place where he had been.

Chapter 46: The Report

She wrote the full report that night, in her own pane, at her own desk, with the building dark around her.

She wrote all of it. Rendel Osk, twelve-year veteran of seam modeling, and the scaffold, and the geometry, and the forty-one percent. Fenwick, and the Caldwell interchange, and the coin. Cyriac Holm, who had known since March and covered it, and his reasons, which she recorded without softening, because he had asked her not to be gentle and she would honor that by being accurate. Orvie Strand and the four hours. The nine hundred at risk in the Varek quarter. And Director Sable Quist, who had ordered the convergence sealed and the record buried, who had wanted no oversight referral, who had reasoned with Maren in a quiet room about panic and dead reconcilers and the eleven thousand sleepers of Varek, and who had been, in every word, persuasive, and wrong.

She wrote that too. She wrote that the Director's reasoning was sound and her conclusion was unacceptable, and that a system with a forger's door in it could not be made safe by pretending the door had never opened, and that the people of Halloway had a right to know the membrane could be picked, precisely so that they would build the locks that kept the next Osk out.

Then she addressed it to the Bureau's external oversight board, to Commissioner Vass, who ran a clean ship and held independent jurisdiction and could not be told by Quist to bury anything. She attached Cyriac's complete technical record. She read it through once. She sent it before she could find a reason not to, at four minutes past two in the morning, and then she sat in the dark and felt the particular emptiness that comes after you have done a thing that cannot be taken back and have not yet been punished for it.

Quist would know by morning. There was no version of this in which Quist did not know by morning. Maren had just ended her own career, or begun a war, or both, and she found that her hands were steady, and that she did not regret it, and that she was, at last, a little tired in a way that might, tonight, permit sleep.

Chapter 47: Two Reasonable People

Quist knew by morning.

She summoned Maren at nine. The summons was polite. Everything Quist did was polite; it was the medium she worked in. Maren went up and sat in the chair that was a message and waited.

"You sent it to Vass," Quist said.

"I did."

"Without coming to me first."

"If I'd come to you first," Maren said, "you'd have given me three good reasons not to send it, and they'd all have been true, and I'd have sent it anyway, and we'd both have wasted an hour."

Quist looked at her for a long moment. There was no anger in it. That was the thing Maren had learned to watch for in this office — the absence of anger, which in Quist meant the machine was running at full power. "You've opened the entire permit system to external review," she said. "Do you understand what the next year looks like now? Audits. Hearings. Every reconciler's crossings pulled and examined. The press will have 'a door in the wall between worlds' by the weekend. People will be frightened of their own bedrooms."

"Yes."

"And you think that's better."

"I think," Maren said, "that you're right about the panic and wrong about the cure. The membrane has a door in it. Osk found it. Cyriac found Osk finding it and stayed quiet, because he was afraid, and his fear cost us three years and nine hundred people very nearly cost us themselves. Fear of the truth is the thing that built this case, Director. I'm not going to seal it with more of the same." She kept her hands still. "You buried Orvie Strand. I understand why. But a Bureau that can bury one merged woman can bury nine hundred, and the only thing that stops it is a board that doesn't answer to us reading the file I sent."

Quist was silent. Outside, Halloway went about its morning, eleven thousand sleepers awake now and unaware.

"You'll be investigated too," Quist said at last. "Your crossings. The one to see him. The one you didn't log."

"I know. I put it in the report. All of them."

That, finally, moved something in Quist's face — not anger, but a kind of weary recognition, one professional acknowledging another's price paid in full. "You included yourself."

"I couldn't include Cyriac and not myself. It wouldn't have been accurate." Maren stood. "Seal's at two o'clock, Director. I'd like your authorization on the record, whatever else is on it."

Quist signed the seal order. She signed it without another word, and Maren understood that this was as close to standing down as a woman like Quist came, and that it was enough, and that it would have to be.

Chapter 48: Understandable

Idris escorted Osk out of the building at noon.

It was done without spectacle, on Maren's instruction. No containment squad, no restraints in the public corridor. Two oversight officers from Vass's office waited in a grey car at the service entrance, and Idris walked Osk down through the sub-levels and out the back, the long way, so that the analyst would not have to cross the public floor where people who had worked beside him for twelve years were eating their lunches.

Osk went quietly. He had a small bag. He had packed it, apparently, some days ago, which told Maren he had known how this ended long before she sat down across from him in the lab.

At the service door, in the grey noon light, Osk stopped and looked at Idris.

"You're the partner," he said. "Lan. You have a brother in Pane Four."

Idris said nothing.

"I read the personnel files," Osk said, almost apologetic. "I read everyone's. It's a hard thing to stop doing, once you've started, looking across the wall at who's there." He shifted the bag in his hand. "Do you think what I tried to do was wrong?"

Idris considered the question. He could have given the easy answer and the man would have been in the car in thirty seconds. He gave the true one instead, because he had decided, somewhere in the last days, that he was going to be the kind of reconciler who gave the true one.

"No," Idris said. "I think it was understandable. I think I understand it completely. My brother's four minutes away and I've spent two years filing forms to reach him, and there were nights I'd have built your scaffold myself if I'd known how." He opened the car door. "That's worse than wrong, Mr. Osk. Wrong I could hate. Understandable I have to carry. You built a thing that almost ate nine hundred people and I can't even hate you for it. That's the cost you've put on me. I'll be paying it a while."

Osk got into the car. "Yes," he said, before the door closed. "That sounds about right. I'm sorry. It doesn't help, but I am." The car pulled away. Idris stood in the service alley until it turned the corner, and then he went back inside, because it was a quarter to one, and they had a seam to close.

Chapter 49: The Seal

The seal took forty-three minutes.

Maren worked the master node at the Varek substation with Idris reading the substrate behind her and a Pane Four officer she did not know holding the far side. The room was hot and doubled and humming. She cauterized the scaffold filaments one at a time, in the order Cyriac's record specified, watching the convergence figure on the readout fall in slow tenths — forty-one, thirty-eight, thirty-one — as each seam she closed released its pull on the panes.

It did not want to close. The substrate had grown around the seam the way flesh grows around a splinter, and twice the figure stalled and ticked back up a tenth before she found the filament that was holding and closed it too. At thirty percent the doubled quality of the room began to thin. At twenty the condensation stopped beading on the conduits. At twelve the air in the corner stopped trying to decide how many of itself there were and simply became air, in one room, in one pane.

At the end the master seam itself stood open in front of her, doorway-sized, the offset Pane Four room visible through it at its few degrees of angle. She set the final seal. She did it the way she had promised Wynn she would, not rushed and not dragged, forty seconds of clean technical work, and the seam closed from the edges inward like frost reversing, and the offset room dimmed and slid and was gone, and the wall behind it was a wall, solid, ordinary, inviolate, the way a wall between worlds is supposed to be.

The readout showed zero. The scaffold was gone. On the far side, in a pane now drifting slowly back out of range, a chief transit officer named Wynn Across would log the closure and go home along the Verrand embankment to a man named Corrin and complain about the night shift, and Maren would never see him again, and that was correct, and it was only sad.

She stood in the substation a moment longer. It was one room now. She had made it one room.

"That's it," Idris said quietly behind her. "It's holding. Twenty-two minutes stable. It's done."

"It's done," Maren said. She capped her pen. "Log it and let's go home. We're in our own city. There's only the one. It's enough."

Chapter 50: A Duplicated Mug

Three weeks later she was at her desk filling in a routine leak report — a coffee mug in the Caldwell district, reported in duplicate, one too many for the household's records, the kind of small membrane hiccup that the Bureau processed a dozen of a week and always would, because the panes were separate now but they were still neighbors, and neighbors leak.

The oversight review was underway. Vass's people had been in the building for two weeks, pulling crossing logs, interviewing reconcilers, reading the file Maren had sent. Cyriac had given his statement and kept his job, on probation, which he said suited him, since the charts kept better than people did anyway. Quist was still Director; the board had found her judgment flawed and her conduct defensible, which was the most that institutions ever found about the people who ran them, and she and Maren passed each other in the corridors with a cool, complete professional respect that was its own kind of truce. Maren's own unlogged crossings had earned her a formal reprimand and no suspension, on the grounds that she had reported them herself, which Vass's officer had called, with a straight face, a mitigating factor.

Saul was still in Pane Four. Alive, on the wrong side of a wall. Idris had filed a new application for a sanctioned family reunification crossing under the post-review protocols, and this time, with the permit system under external audit and the old quiet misclassifications coming to light, it might actually move. Might. He didn't talk about it much. He'd become careful with his hopes, the way reconcilers learned to be.

He was at the adjacent desk now, filling in his first solo assignment form, frowning at it the way everyone frowned at that form, and he looked up and said, "I don't have a pen," and Maren slid one across the gap between the desks without looking up from the mug report.

"Caldwell again?" he said, reading her form upside down.

"Caldwell again. A mug." She signed it. "The whole city, two worlds, a forger in the wall, nine hundred people on a coin — and it ends with me filling out a form about a mug."

"Does that bother you?"

She thought about it. Outside the window Halloway went on, one city, lamp-yellow and ordinary, eleven thousand sleepers in the Varek quarter going about a Tuesday on ground that was solid under their beds because she had made it so and they would never know her name. The mug was nothing. The mug was the whole point.

"No," Maren said. She handed him the pen for good. "No, it doesn't bother me at all. Fill in your form, Lan. One city's plenty of work."
