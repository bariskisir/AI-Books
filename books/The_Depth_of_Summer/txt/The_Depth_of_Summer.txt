Chapter 1: The Ferry

The ferry left the mainland at four o'clock. Wren watched from the rail as the town she had known her whole life shrank into a smear of gray and white on the horizon. Her suitcase sat at her feet, a brown vinyl thing with a broken clasp her mother had fixed with a rubber band.

She was twelve. She had never traveled alone before.

The wind off the water was colder than she had expected for June. She pulled her jacket tighter and counted the seagulls following the boat. Seven of them. Then eight. Then she lost track.

A woman in a pink windbreaker smiled at her. "First time to the islands?"

"No," Wren said. It was a lie. She turned back to the rail.

Her mother had cried at the dock. Her father had not come to say goodbye. He was already in the apartment across town, the one with the pullout couch and the kitchen that smelled like the previous tenant's cigarettes.

Wren had heard them on the phone the night before. Your sister said she would take her for the summer. It is just for the summer. Until we figure things out.

She had figured things out already. She just was not sure anyone wanted to hear it.

The ferry horn sounded. The island was coming into view — a dark green hump rising from the water, dotted with white houses and a single church steeple. Wren picked up her suitcase and walked toward the bow.

She did not look back at the mainland again.


Chapter 2: Aunt Margie

Aunt Margie was waiting at the dock. She stood apart from the other people meeting the ferry, her hands in the pockets of a denim apron that had clay smeared across the front. Her hair was gray in a way that looked intentional, cut short and uneven, as if she had done it herself with a pair of scissors.

She spotted Wren before Wren spotted her and raised one hand in a small wave.

"Hello, Wren."

"Hi, Aunt Margie."

They did not hug. Wren was grateful for that.

Margie took the suitcase. "The car is this way." She walked without looking back to see if Wren was following.

The car was a station wagon from the 1980s, pale blue, with a cracked dashboard and a pine-tree air freshener that had long since stopped smelling like anything. Wren sat in the passenger seat and watched the town slide by — a grocery store, a post office, a diner with a neon sign that said EAT in pink letters that buzzed.

"How was the ferry?" Margie asked.

"Fine."

"Seasick?"

"No."

"Good." Margie turned onto a road that was more gravel than pavement. "I made soup. You like soup?"

"I guess."

Margie nodded. She did not ask about Wren's parents. She did not ask about school or friends or how Wren was feeling. She just drove, one hand on the wheel, the other resting on the open window frame.

Wren watched the trees get thicker and the houses get farther apart. The air smelled different here. Salt and pine and something green.


Chapter 3: The Cottage

Aunt Margie's cottage sat at the end of a dirt road that was barely a road at all. It was small and white with blue shutters and a porch that wrapped around three sides. A clay pot by the front door held a cluster of herbs Wren did not recognize.

"Your room is the one at the end of the hall," Margie said, carrying the suitcase inside. "Bathroom is shared. Towels are in the linen closet. Help yourself to anything in the kitchen."

Wren stood in the front room and turned in a slow circle. There were pots on shelves, glazed in shades of green and blue. A kiln sat in the corner like a small spaceship. Books were stacked on every surface — on the coffee table, on the floor, on the windowsills.

"Down the hall," Margie said, pointing with her chin.

The room was small. A bed with a quilt the color of the sea. A window that looked out onto a field of tall grass. A wooden desk with a lamp and an empty jar.

Wren put her suitcase on the bed and opened it. She had packed too quickly. Everything was wrinkled. She took out the photograph of her dog, the one her mother had said she could not bring because the cottage did not allow pets. She set it on the desk next to the empty jar.

Through the window, she could see the grass moving in the wind, and beyond the grass, a line of dunes against the sky.

She wondered what was on the other side.


Chapter 4: The Dunes

The next morning, Wren walked to the end of the road.

She had woken before the sun, dressed in the dark, and slipped out the front door before Aunt Margie's bedroom door had opened. The grass was wet with dew. Her sneakers were soaked by the time she reached the gravel.

The road ended at a wooden post with a faded orange reflector nailed to it. Beyond the post, a path of beaten sand led into the dunes. The dunes rose in golden curves, covered in coarse grass that rattled in the wind.

Wren climbed.

The sand was loose and difficult. She sank to her ankles with every step. Her calves burned. She kept climbing anyway, grabbing handfuls of grass to pull herself up.

At the top, she stopped.

The estuary opened before her like a revelation. Channels of dark water wound through a vast plain of mud and marsh grass. The water caught the low morning sun and threw it back in shards of gold and silver. A blue heron stood motionless at the edge of a channel, its reflection sharp in the still water.

Wren had never seen anything like it.

She stood at the top of the dune for a long time, letting the wind hit her face. The air smelled of salt and mud and something sweet she could not name. A bird called from somewhere in the marsh — a single note, repeated.

Wren sat down in the sand and watched the water move.


Chapter 5: The Skiff

She saw the boy before he saw her.

He came around the bend of a channel in a small wooden skiff, rowing with long, even strokes. The boat was old and weathered, painted white once but now mostly gray. He shipped the oars and let the skiff drift toward a warped plank that served as a dock.

Wren watched from the dune. The boy stepped onto the plank and tied the skiff to a metal ring. He was lean and brown-skinned, older than her by a couple of years, with dark hair that fell across his forehead.

He looked up and saw her.

She did not move. Neither did he. For a long moment, they just looked at each other across the distance — her on the dune, him on the plank, the estuary breathing between them.

Then he said, "You know how to row?"

His voice carried across the water. It was not unfriendly. It was just a question.

Wren stood up and brushed the sand off her pants. "No."

He nodded. "I figured. Come on. I'll show you."

He waited while she climbed down the dune, picking her way through the marsh grass. When she reached the plank, he held out his hand. She took it. His palm was rough and warm.

"I'm Kit," he said.

"I'm Wren."

He untied the skiff and held it steady while she climbed in. The boat rocked under her weight. She grabbed the sides and sat down hard on the wooden seat.

Kit pushed off and stepped in smoothly, taking up the oars. "Hold on," he said, and pulled.


Chapter 6: The Rising Tide

The skiff moved differently on the water than Wren had expected. It did not glide. It slid, quietly, the hull parting the surface without complaint.

Kit rowed standing up, facing forward, the oars crossing in front of him. He did not speak. His eyes moved constantly — from the water to the sky to the distant line of marsh grass.

Wren sat in the bow and watched the estuary unfold. The channels were deeper than she had thought. The water was dark, almost black in places, the color of strong tea. Tidal creeks branched off the main channel, narrow and twisting, disappearing into stands of tall reeds.

"Where are we going?" she asked.

"Nowhere," Kit said. "Everywhere."

He turned the skiff into a side channel so narrow that the reeds brushed against both sides of the boat. The water shallowed. Wren could see the bottom — sand and mud and the occasional crab skittering sideways.

The channel opened into a wide pool. The sun was fully up now, burning the mist off the water. A great blue heron stood at the far edge of the pool, motionless as a sculpture.

Kit stopped rowing. The skiff drifted to a halt.

"Watch," he said.

They sat in silence. The heron took three deliberate steps into the water. Its head snapped down. When it rose, a silver fish wriggled in its beak.

Wren stopped breathing.

Kit smiled, just a little, and picked up the oars again.


Chapter 7: The Heron Rookery

Kit rowed them to the far side of the estuary, where a stand of dead pine trees rose from the marsh like white bones. Their branches were bare and bleached, and in the crowns of the tallest trees, Wren could see the nests — a half-dozen platforms of sticks, large as wagon wheels.

"Heron rookery," Kit said.

They drifted closer. The birds stood in the nests, gray shapes against the pale sky. Some had chicks — small, fuzzy creatures that bobbed their heads and made sounds like rusty hinges.

"How many are there?" Wren asked.

"Counted nineteen this spring. Might be more now."

One of the adult herons lifted off, its wings wide and slow. It banked over the skiff, so close that Wren could hear the air moving through its feathers. For a moment, she was looking up at the underside of its body — the gray belly, the long legs trailing behind — and then it was gone, a shadow crossing the marsh.

"What do they eat?" she asked.

"Fish. Frogs. Anything that moves in the shallows." Kit pointed to a smaller nest. "That one had three chicks last week. Now there's two. The third one probably got eaten by a raccoon or fell out."

"That's sad."

Kit shrugged. "That's how it works. Not everything makes it."

Wren thought about that. She thought about her parents. She thought about the apartment across town with the cigarette-smelling kitchen.

"Can we stay here a while?" she asked.

Kit nodded. He shipped the oars and leaned back in the stern. The skiff turned slowly in the current, the heron nests circling around them like a carousel.


Chapter 8: The Telephone Call

That evening, Aunt Margie called Wren to the phone.

It was a cordless handset, pale yellow, with a crack in the casing. Margie held it out. "Your mother."

Wren took the phone and walked onto the porch. The sun was going down, the sky turning the color of a bruise.

"Hi, Mom."

"Hi, sweetheart. How are you? How is the cottage?"

"Fine."

"Is Margie feeding you?"

"Yes."

"Are you making friends?"

Wren thought about Kit and the skiff and the heron rookery. "I met a boy. He has a boat."

"That sounds nice. What's his name?"

"Kit."

"Kit? That's an unusual name."

"He lives here year-round."

There was a pause. Wren could hear her mother breathing. It was the same pause that happened before the hard part of every conversation now.

"Sweetheart, I wanted to tell you — Daddy and I are still working things out. But we both love you. You know that, right?"

"Yes."

"We'll figure this out. I promise."

Wren said nothing. She watched a line of clouds move across the orange sky.

"Baby, are you okay?"

"I'm fine, Mom. I have to go. Aunt Margie made soup."

"Okay. I love you."

"Love you too."

Wren hung up and stood on the porch for a long time. The air was cool and smelled like the tide going out.

She did not go inside until the stars came out.


Chapter 9: Alone

Wren woke before dawn again. She lay in bed and listened to the house breathe — the creak of old wood, the hum of the refrigerator, the distant cry of a gull.

She got dressed in the dark and went outside.

The estuary was different in the half-light. The water was the color of slate, flat and still. The marsh grass was gray. The sky was the same gray as the water, so that the horizon line disappeared and everything became one continuous plane.

Wren walked to the plank where Kit had tied the skiff. The skiff was not there.

She sat down on the plank anyway. She wrapped her arms around her knees and watched the water.

She had never been this alone before. Not really alone — she had her family, her school, her friends. But she had never been in a place where no one knew her. Where she could be anyone or no one.

It was terrifying. It was also, she realized, a little bit good.

A fish jumped in the channel, making a sound like a stone dropped into water. The ripples spread outward, catching the first light of the sun, and then they were gone.

Wren checked her watch. Six-thirty. The tide was coming in.

She decided to wait.


Chapter 10: The Waiting

She was still sitting on the plank when Kit appeared.

He came over the top of the dune, his silhouette sharp against the morning sky. He was carrying two paper bags and a thermos. He saw her and did not look surprised.

"You're up early," he said.

"So are you."

He climbed down to the plank and sat beside her. He handed her one of the paper bags. It was warm. Inside was a biscuit with butter and honey.

"I stole them from my mom's kitchen," he said. "The thermos has coffee. You drink coffee?"

"No."

"Me neither. But my mom thinks I do. It's mostly milk and sugar." He poured her a cup anyway.

They ate their biscuits and watched the tide rise. The water crept up the mudflats, covering the sand inch by inch. A line of shorebirds flew past, turning as one, their white bellies flashing.

"My mom called last night," Wren said.

"Yeah?"

"She said she and my dad are still working things out."

"Are they?"

Wren took a bite of the biscuit. "No. I don't think so."

Kit nodded. He did not offer platitudes. He did not say it would be okay.

"I lost my dad three years ago," he said. "Boat accident. They never found him."

The words sat in the air between them. Wren did not know what to say. She had never known anyone who had lost a parent like that. She had never known anyone who had lost a parent at all.

"I'm sorry," she said.

Kit shrugged. "I don't talk about it much." He stood up and brushed crumbs off his shirt. "The tide's high enough. Let's take the skiff out."


Chapter 11: The Tide Charts

Kit kept the tide charts in a spiral-bound notebook, the pages soft and wrinkled from humidity. He spread it open on the plank and showed Wren how to read the numbers.

"So this is today," he said, pointing to a row of figures. "Low tide at 9:47 AM. High tide at 3:52 PM. Tomorrow it shifts by about fifty minutes."

"Why?"

"The moon. It's not on a twenty-four-hour cycle. The tides move with the moon's orbit, not the sun's."

Wren studied the page. There were columns for every day of the month — times for high and low water, the heights in feet, little symbols that Kit had drawn to indicate the phase of the moon.

"Full moon on the fifteenth," Kit said. "The tides will be higher then. Much higher. The water will come up past those marsh grasses." He pointed to the edge of the channel. "Up to the base of the dunes."

"Does that happen every month?"

"Every month. But summer full moons are the highest. Something about the earth's orbit. I don't remember the science exactly."

Wren traced her finger along the tide chart. The numbers formed a pattern, a rhythm as old as the planet. The water knew when to come in and when to go out. It did not ask permission.

"There's a channel that only opens on the full-moon high tide," Kit said. "I'll show you when the time comes."

He closed the notebook and tucked it under his arm. "Come on. Low tide in an hour. We can walk the flats."


Chapter 12: The Mudflats

They walked where the water had been.

The mudflats stretched for what looked like miles — a dark brown plain pocked with holes and rippled with the marks of the receding tide. The mud sucked at Wren's sneakers with every step. Within minutes, her shoes were caked and heavy.

"It's like walking on the moon," she said.

Kit laughed. It was the first time she had heard him laugh. It was a small sound, but real.

"Watch your step," he said. "Some of these holes are deeper than they look."

They picked their way across the flats. Kit pointed out the signs of life — the tiny jets of water that meant a clam was burrowing, the trails of periwinkles, the black dots of mud snails moving in slow colonies.

"Everything here lives between the tides," he said. "They have to be able to breathe underwater and in the air. They have to survive the sun and the rain and the gulls."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is. But they've been doing it for millions of years. They know what they're doing."

Wren stopped to look at a small crab that had burrowed into the mud, its eyes on stalks, watching her. It did not run. It just sat there, waiting for the water to come back.

"Does anything live out here permanently?" she asked.

"Nothing lives anywhere permanently," Kit said.

He kept walking.


Chapter 13: The Bottle

Wren saw it first — a glint of green in the brown mud.

She bent down and worked it loose with her fingers. It was a bottle. Old, thick glass, the color of sea kelp. The lettering on the side was worn smooth, barely readable. Something about soda. A city she did not recognize.

"Look what I found."

Kit took it from her and turned it over in his hands. "That's old. Maybe fifty years. Sixty."

"How do you know?"

"The shape. And the glass is thicker than modern bottles. They don't make them like this anymore."

Wren took it back and held it up to the light. The glass was cloudy, etched by years of tumbling in the sand and water.

"Do you think someone threw it away?" she asked. "Or did it fall off a boat?"

"Could be either. Or it could have floated here from somewhere else. The currents can carry things a long way."

She imagined the bottle traveling through the dark water, crossing the ocean floor, rising and falling with the tides. She imagined someone throwing it overboard from a ship, watching it bob away.

"What are you going to do with it?" Kit asked.

"Keep it," she said. "For now."

She tucked the bottle into her jacket pocket and followed Kit across the flats. The glass was cool against her hip, a small weight she was glad to carry.


Chapter 14: The Wreck

The lowest tide of the month revealed the bones of the boat.

Kit led Wren to the edge of the main channel, where the water had pulled back to expose a dark shape in the mud. It was the ribbed skeleton of a wooden fishing boat, half-buried, its planking long since rotted or scavenged.

"There it is," he said.

They stood at the edge of the channel and looked down at the wreck. The boat's frame rose from the mud like the ribs of a whale. The bow was mostly intact, pointing toward the sea as if still trying to get there.

"How long has it been here?" Wren asked.

"Nobody knows. My grandfather said it was here when he was a boy. So at least sixty years. Maybe more."

"Does it have a name?"

"Someone said it was the Mary Belle out of Rockland. But that might just be a story."

Wren stepped closer. The wood was dark and waterlogged, soft under her fingers. She could see where the hull had been painted — traces of white and blue, almost gone now.

"Does it make you think of your dad?"

Kit was quiet for a moment. "Sometimes. Not today, though."

He turned and started walking back across the flats. The tide was beginning to turn, the first thin fingers of water reaching across the exposed mud.

"We should get back," he said. "The water comes in faster than you think."

Wren followed him. She looked back once at the wreck, half-expecting it to be gone. But it was still there, dark against the mud, holding its position in the current.


Chapter 15: Aunt Margie's Studio

The fog came in the following week. It rolled in overnight, thick as cotton, muffling every sound.

Wren spent the morning in Aunt Margie's studio. The studio was a converted shed behind the cottage, with a potter's wheel in the center and shelves of greenware along the walls. The windows were fogged over, the glass beaded with moisture.

Aunt Margie sat at the wheel, her hands buried in a spinning lump of clay. Her hands moved in slow, deliberate motions — pressing, lifting, shaping. The clay rose into a cylinder, then flared into a bowl.

Wren watched from the doorway.

"Want to try?" Margie asked without looking up.

"I don't know how."

"Nobody knows how until they try."

Margie stood up and motioned for Wren to take her place at the wheel. The seat was still warm. Wren sat down and put her hands on the wet clay.

"Center it first," Margie said. "Press your palms against the sides and push until it stops wobbling."

Wren pressed. The clay wobbled. She pressed harder. It wobbled more.

"Too much pressure. Ease up. Let the clay find its own center."

Wren loosened her grip. The wobbling slowed. The clay began to spin more smoothly.

"There," Margie said. "Now you're talking to it."

Wren shaped the clay into a small lopsided cup. It was not pretty. But it was hers.

"You can fire it when it's dry," Margie said. "Put your initials on the bottom so I know which one is yours."

Wren carved a W into the base with a wooden needle. She set the cup on the shelf next to Margie's work and washed the clay off her hands.

The fog was still there, pressing against the windows. But it did not feel as heavy as it had before.


Chapter 16: The Fog

Kit appeared on the third day of the fog.

Wren was on the porch, reading a book she had found on Margie's shelf — something about a woman who lived alone on an island and talked to seals. She heard footsteps on the gravel and looked up.

Kit was walking up the road, his hands in his pockets. He was wearing a yellow rain jacket that was too big for him.

"I figured you'd be bored," he said.

"I was."

He sat down on the porch steps. "The fog should lift by tomorrow. The wind is shifting."

"How do you know?"

"I can feel it. The air pressure changes. My mom says I got it from my dad. He could smell a storm coming from a day away."

They sat in silence for a while. The fog muffled everything — the birds, the water, the distant sound of a boat engine somewhere out in the bay.

"I brought something," Kit said. He pulled a folded piece of paper from his jacket pocket and handed it to her.

It was a map. Hand-drawn, in pencil, with careful lettering. The estuary was rendered in fine detail — every channel and sandbar and island named in Kit's neat handwriting. The Winding Way. The Heron Reach. The Sunken Channel. Kit's Pond.

"You drew this?" Wren asked.

"Over the last two years. I keep adding to it."

Wren traced the lines with her finger. She found the plank where they tied the skiff. She found the heron rookery. She found the wreck.

"Where's the hidden pond?" she asked.

Kit pointed to a spot on the map, a small circle labeled with no name. "It only shows on the full-moon tide. I'll take you when the time comes."

Wren folded the map carefully and handed it back. "It's beautiful."

Kit took it and looked at it for a moment. "You can keep it. I have another copy at home."

He got up and walked back into the fog before she could thank him.


Chapter 17: The Map

Wren spread Kit's map on her desk that night.

She studied it by the light of the desk lamp, tracing every line. The estuary was a maze — a labyrinth of water and land that changed shape twice a day. Kit had captured it all. The deep holes. The shallow bars. The places where the current ran fast and dangerous.

She found the cottage, marked with a small square. She found the path through the dunes. She found the skiff's mooring.

She took out her journal and copied the names of the channels into it. The Winding Way. The Heron Reach. The Sunken Channel. She wrote them in blue ink, spelling each one carefully.

On the back of the map, Kit had written something in small letters: The tide does not ask permission. It comes. It goes. You learn to read it or you get left behind.

Wren read the sentence three times.

She folded the map and put it in the drawer of her desk, next to the photograph of her dog. Then she turned off the lamp and lay in the dark, listening to the fog drip from the eaves.

She thought about the tide. About how it never stopped. About how it came and went without asking anyone's opinion.

She thought about her parents. About the apartment across town. About the phone calls that ended too quickly.

Maybe the trick was not to hold on. Maybe the trick was to learn to read the signs.


Chapter 18: The Clam Flats

The fog lifted on the fourth morning, burned off by a sun that felt like it meant business.

Kit met Wren at the plank before sunrise. He had two buckets and a garden rake.

"Clam flats," he said. "Tide's going out. We have about two hours."

They walked across the wet sand to a part of the estuary Wren had not seen before — a wide, flat area dotted with small holes and dimples. Kit showed her how to look for the signs: tiny jets of water, small bubbles in the sand, the telltale dimple that meant a clam was below the surface.

"See that?" He pointed to a small indentation. "That's a neck. The clam sticks its siphon up to breathe. You dig next to it, not on top of it."

He knelt and dug with his hands, scooping wet sand to the side. Six inches down, his fingers touched something hard. He pulled out a clam — brown and oval, the size of his palm.

"Your turn."

Wren found a dimple and dug. Her fingers hit something hard. She pulled out a clam, smaller than Kit's but real.

"Good," he said. "Keep going."

They dug for an hour. Wren's hands got cold and numb. Her knees got wet. But the bucket filled — clams, a few crabs, a piece of sea glass that she slipped into her pocket.

When the tide turned, they walked back to the cottage. Kit cleaned the clams in the sink while Wren boiled water. Aunt Margie came in from the studio and melted butter in a small pot.

They ate on the porch, the clams steaming in a bowl between them, the butter golden in the late light. No one talked about the future. No one mentioned the word divorce.

It was the best meal Wren had eaten all summer.


Chapter 19: The Evening

After the clams were gone and the butter had cooled, Kit walked to the edge of the porch and looked at the sky.

"Still light for another hour," he said. "Want to take the skiff out?"

Wren looked at Aunt Margie. Margie was already collecting the bowls, her hands busy, her eyes elsewhere.

"Go," Margie said. "Be back before dark."

They walked to the plank in the blue evening light. The skiff was there, riding low in the water. Kit untied it and held it steady while Wren climbed in.

They did not go far. Kit rowed to the middle of the main channel and let the skiff drift. The water was the color of pewter, flat and quiet. A single gull sat on a channel marker, preening its feathers.

"Does your aunt always let you do whatever you want?" Kit asked.

"She doesn't really know me. I think she's still figuring out what to do with me."

"My mom was like that at first. After my dad died. She didn't know what to do with me either."

Wren looked at him. In the low light, his face was hard to read. "What changed?"

"I don't know if anything changed. I think we just got used to each other." He paused. "She wants to move to Ohio. To be near her sister. She thinks it'll be easier there."

"Do you want to go?"

"No."

The word hung in the air. Wren did not know what to say to it.

"I've lived here my whole life," Kit said. "The estuary is the only thing that makes sense to me. The tides. The birds. The way everything comes back, even after it's gone."

He picked up the oars. "We should head back."

He rowed toward the plank. The skiff cut through the water, leaving a wake that spread and faded and disappeared.


Chapter 20: The Journal

That night, Wren wrote in her journal.

She had not written in it since the spring. The last entry was from May, the week her father moved out. She had written: Dad is gone. Mom is crying. I don't know what to do.

Now she opened the journal to a fresh page and wrote the date: June 28.

She wrote about the estuary. The way the water changed color with the light. The heron rookery. The wreck buried in the mud. The skiff and Kit and the way he rowed like he had been doing it his whole life.

She wrote about the clam flats and the bottle she had found and the map Kit had given her.

She wrote about Aunt Margie and the pottery wheel and the feeling of clay spinning under her hands.

She wrote: I do not want to go home.

She stared at the sentence. It was true. She had not let herself admit it before.

She wrote: I do not know how to tell Mom.

She closed the journal and put it in the drawer next to Kit's map. Then she lay in bed and listened to the sound of the tide coming in, the water rising, covering the mudflats inch by inch.

She did not fall asleep for a long time.


Chapter 21: The Full Moon

The full moon rose over the estuary on the fifteenth of July.

Wren felt it before she saw it. The air changed. The light changed. The water seemed to hold its breath.

Kit met her at the plank at dusk. The skiff was already in the water, the oars resting across the seats.

"This is it," he said. "The highest tide of the summer."

They pushed off and rowed into the main channel. The water was higher than Wren had ever seen it — lapping at the base of the marsh grass, covering the mudflats completely. The familiar landmarks were half-submerged, transformed into something strange and new.

"Where does the hidden channel go?" she asked.

"You'll see."

Kit rowed to the far end of the estuary, where the marsh narrowed into a thick stand of cattails. In the daylight, it looked like a solid wall of green. But the full-moon tide had opened a gap — a narrow passage, barely wide enough for the skiff.

Kit guided the boat into it. The reeds scraped against the sides. Water dripped from the leaves.

And then they were through.

The hidden pond opened before them — round and still, ringed by cattails, the surface reflecting the rising moon. It was small, maybe fifty yards across, completely enclosed by marsh. No channels led out. No wind reached it.

The water was black and silver, the moon doubled — one in the sky, one in the pond.

"Wow," Wren said.

Kit shipped the oars. The skiff drifted to the center of the pond and stopped.

"I found it two years ago," he said. "I've never shown anyone else."

They sat in silence, the skiff turning slowly in the still water. The moon rose higher. The cattails stood like sentinels around the edge of the pond.

Wren thought she had never seen anything so quiet in her life.


Chapter 22: The Hidden Pond

The air over the hidden pond was still and cool. The cattails did not move. The water did not ripple.

Wren lay back in the skiff and looked at the sky. The stars were coming out, one by one, faint at first and then bright. She found the Big Dipper. Cassiopeia. The North Star, steady and fixed.

Kit lay down too, his arms behind his head.

"How did you find this place?" Wren asked.

"I was following a heron. It flew into the reeds and disappeared. I thought I imagined it. But I kept coming back, looking for the opening. It took me three months to find it."

"Why did you keep looking?"

"Because I wanted to know where the heron went." He paused. "And because I needed a place where no one could find me."

Wren understood. She understood completely.

"Is it different at low tide?" she asked.

"The pond stays. It's deep enough that it never drains completely. But the channel closes. You can only get in on the highest tides."

"Like a secret."

"Yeah. Like a secret."

They lay in the skiff and watched the stars. A bat flickered overhead, chasing insects. An owl called from somewhere in the marsh.

"Do you think things can stay the same?" Wren asked. "Even when everything else changes?"

Kit was quiet for a long time. "I don't know," he said finally. "I used to think the estuary was always the same. But it's not. It changes every day. Every tide. The channels shift. The sandbars move. The birds come and go."

He turned his head to look at her. "But the water always comes back. That's the thing. The water always comes back."


Chapter 23: The Black Ducks

A flock of black ducks erupted from the cattails.

They had been hidden, invisible, and then they were not — a hundred wings beating at once, a sound like a sheet tearing. The birds lifted into the evening sky, climbing fast, their dark shapes silhouetted against the orange horizon.

Wren sat up so fast she nearly tipped the skiff.

"What was that?"

"Black ducks," Kit said. "They nest in the reeds. We scared them."

The ducks circled once, twice, and then headed out over the marsh, their calls fading into the distance.

"I didn't even see them," Wren said.

"That's the point. They're good at hiding."

Kit picked up the oars and turned the skiff toward the channel. The water was starting to recede — thin streams of it flowing back through the reeds, carrying leaves and twigs with them.

"We should go before the tide drops too much. We don't want to get stuck in here."

They navigated the narrow channel back to the main estuary. The water had already dropped six inches. The mudflats were beginning to show, dark and wet in the moonlight.

When they reached the plank, Kit tied the skiff and held out his hand to help Wren. She took it.

"Thank you," she said. "For showing me."

He nodded. "Same time next month?"

"Yes."

She walked up the path to the cottage, her shoes wet, her hair smelling of marsh water. The moon was high now, casting long shadows across the grass.

She stopped at the door and looked back. Kit was still at the plank, a dark shape against the silver water.

She raised her hand. He raised his.

Then she went inside.


Chapter 24: The Phone Call

Wren's father called two days later.

She was in the studio, trimming her cup, when Aunt Margie appeared in the doorway with the phone.

"It's your father."

Wren wiped her hands on a towel and took the phone. She walked to the porch, where the afternoon sun was hot on the boards.

"Hi, Dad."

"Hi, sweetheart. How's the island?"

"Good. I made a cup on the pottery wheel."

"That's great. You'll have to show me when you get back."

There was a pause. Wren leaned against the porch railing and watched a gull circle the yard.

"Sweetheart, I wanted to talk to you about something."

"I know."

"You know?"

"Mom told me. You're getting divorced."

Another pause. Longer this time.

"We're still working out the details," he said. "But yes. We've decided it's best for everyone."

Wren said nothing. The gull landed on the roof and looked at her.

"I know this is hard," her father said. "It's hard for me too. But I want you to know that I love you. And none of this is your fault."

"I know."

"We'll figure out a schedule. You'll spend time with both of us. It'll be different, but it'll be okay."

"Okay."

"Are you sure you're all right?"

"I'm fine, Dad. I have to go. Aunt Margie needs help in the studio."

"Okay. I love you, Wren."

"Love you too."

She hung up and stood on the porch. The gull was still on the roof, watching her with one eye.

She did not cry. She had known it was coming. She had known for months.

But knowing and hearing were different things.


Chapter 25: The Beach

Wren walked to the beach on the ocean side of the island.

She had not been to this beach before. It was different from the estuary — open and exposed, the waves rolling in from the Atlantic, the horizon a straight line with nothing to interrupt it.

She sat on a driftwood log and watched the waves.

The water here was not like the estuary water. It was clear and cold and restless. It did not stay in channels. It did not follow paths. It just moved, endlessly, in patterns too large to see.

She picked up a stone and threw it into the surf. It disappeared without a sound.

"You okay?"

She turned. Kit was walking toward her across the sand, his hands in his pockets.

"How did you find me?"

"Aunt Margie said you went this way."

He sat down on the log beside her. He did not ask what was wrong. He just sat.

"My dad called," Wren said. "They're getting divorced."

"I figured."

"I knew it was coming. I've known for months. But hearing him say it..."

"It makes it real."

"Yeah."

Kit picked up a stone and turned it over in his hands. It was flat and gray, worn smooth by the water.

"My mom sold my dad's boat last year," he said. "She kept it in the driveway for two years. She couldn't bring herself to do anything with it. And then one day she just called a guy and he came and took it away."

He threw the stone into the water. It skipped three times before sinking.

"I didn't think anything could make it realer than him not coming home," he said. "But that did. Watching his boat drive away."

Wren looked at him. His face was still, but his jaw was tight.

"How did you get through it?"

He shrugged. "I went to the estuary. Every day. It was the only place that made sense."

They sat on the log until the sun went down, watching the waves, not saying much.

It was enough.


Chapter 26: The Letter

Wren wrote her mother a letter.

She had never written her mother a letter before. Phone calls were easier — you could hang up when things got hard. But a letter had to be finished. You could not put it down and pretend it did not exist.

She sat at her desk with a piece of paper and a blue pen.

She wrote: Dear Mom. I am okay. The cottage is nice. Aunt Margie is teaching me pottery. I made a cup.

She crossed out the last sentence and started again.

She wrote: I know you and Dad are getting divorced. You do not have to pretend it is not happening. I am old enough to understand.

She stared at the words. They looked different on paper than they did in her head.

She wrote: I am not mad. I think I have been mad, but I am not anymore. I just wish someone had told me the truth from the beginning.

She signed it: Love, Wren.

She folded the letter and put it in an envelope. She wrote her mother's address on the front in careful letters.

She did not mail it that day. She put it in the drawer with the map and the journal and the photograph of her dog.

Maybe tomorrow. Maybe the next day.

She closed the drawer and went to find Aunt Margie.


Chapter 27: The Kiln

Aunt Margie was firing the kiln.

Wren found her in the studio, loading the final pieces onto the shelves inside the heavy metal box. The kiln was old and round, its exterior rusted in places, the door held shut with a latch that looked like it belonged on a barn.

"This is the bisque fire," Margie said. "It hardens the clay so it can be glazed."

Wren watched as Margie arranged the pots on the kiln shelves — bowls and mugs and vases, her own work alongside a few lumpy pieces that looked like they had been made by children.

"Is my cup in there?"

"Bottom shelf. In the back."

Wren peered through the small window in the kiln door. Her cup sat among the other pieces, small and crooked, paler than she remembered.

"It doesn't look very good," she said.

"It looks like someone's first cup," Margie said. "That's the only way to start."

She closed the door and latched it. She turned a dial on the control panel and the kiln began to hum.

"Tomorrow it'll be ready for glazing," she said. "You can pick whatever color you want."

Wren looked at the kiln. The small window glowed orange as the elements inside began to heat.

"What color did you use for your first pot?" she asked.

"Blue," Margie said. "The same blue as the bay on a clear day. It came out ugly. I loved it anyway."

She smiled, and Wren smiled back.

They stood in the studio, watching the kiln heat up, listening to the hum of the elements and the crackle of the clay transforming inside.


Chapter 28: The Low Tide

The lowest tide of the month exposed a sandbar that connected the island to a smaller island — a rocky hump covered in stunted pines, normally separated from the mainland by twenty feet of water.

Kit and Wren walked the sandbar at noon.

The sandbar was wide enough for two people, firm underfoot, covered in a thin layer of water that reflected the sky. Small fish darted in the shallows. A seal lifted its head to watch them pass and then submerged without a sound.

"Where does this go?" Wren asked.

"Ledge Island. Nobody lives there. There's a shack on the back side, but I don't think anyone has used it in years."

They walked to the island. The sandbar narrowed as they approached, then widened again into a small beach of cobblestones. The island was smaller than it had looked from the mainland — maybe a hundred yards across, covered in pitch pines and blueberry bushes.

Wren climbed to the highest point, a rocky outcropping that overlooked the estuary. From here, she could see the whole shape of the water — the channels and sandbars and marsh islands, the distant line of the mainland, the open ocean beyond.

"It's like a map," she said.

Kit climbed up beside her. "It is a map. You just have to be high enough to read it."

They sat on the rocks and looked out at the water. The light was different here — sharper, more exposed. Everything seemed clearer.

"My mother is coming to get me at the end of August," Wren said.

"Figured."

"School starts the week after Labor Day."

Kit nodded. He picked up a piece of lichen and crumbled it between his fingers.

"I start high school this year," he said. "If we stay."

Wren looked at him. "Do you think your mom will change her mind?"

"I don't know. I hope so." He tossed the lichen crumbs into the wind. "But I'm not counting on it."

They sat on the rocks until the tide began to rise, cutting off the sandbar, stranding them on Ledge Island until the next low tide.

Neither of them minded.


Chapter 29: Stranded

The water rose around Ledge Island, isolating it from the mainland.

Kit and Wren sat on the beach, their feet in the water, watching the sun move across the sky. They had an hour until the tide dropped enough to let them cross back.

"There's food in the shack," Kit said. "Canned stuff. My grandfather stocked it years ago."

They walked around to the back side of the island. The shack was small and weathered, the door held shut by a loop of rope. Kit unlooped it and pushed the door open.

Inside: a cot, a wooden table, a shelf with a few cans of beans and a bottle of water. A lantern with no fuel. A stack of old fishing magazines.

"Who built it?" Wren asked.

"Nobody remembers. My grandfather said it was here when he was a kid."

Wren sat on the cot. The mattress was thin and musty. A spiderweb spanned the corner of the window frame.

"It's like a secret," she said.

"Yeah. There are a lot of secrets out here."

Wren looked around the shack. She tried to imagine the person who built it — someone who needed a place to be alone, a place to get away from whatever they were running from.

She understood that person.

"We should head back," Kit said. "Tide's dropping."

They walked back to the beach. The sandbar was just beginning to emerge, a thin ribbon of wet sand connecting the island to the mainland.

They crossed in silence, the water swirling around their ankles, the sun warm on their backs.

When they reached the other side, Wren looked back at Ledge Island. It looked different already — smaller, farther away, already starting to belong to memory.


Chapter 30: The Glaze

Wren glazed her cup the next day.

Aunt Margie had laid out the glazes on a tray — small plastic containers in every color of the sea. There was a green like marsh grass in spring. A blue like deep water. A white like the bellies of gulls.

Wren chose the blue.

She dipped the cup into the glaze, holding it by the base, letting the liquid cover the surface in a thin, even coat. The cup came out chalky and pale, nothing like the color in the container.

"It'll fire bright," Margie said. "The kiln brings out the color."

Wren set the cup on the shelf to dry. It was small and crooked, her initials scratched into the bottom. It was not beautiful. But it was hers.

"Can I make another one?" she asked.

"You can make as many as you want. That's the whole point of clay."

Wren sat at the wheel and threw another lump of clay. This time it centered more easily. This time the walls rose straighter.

She shaped a bowl. Wide and shallow, good for the kind of soup Margie made.

"You're getting the feel of it," Margie said.

"It's easier than the first time."

"That's how everything is. The first time is always the hardest."

Wren thought about that. She thought about the estuary, which she had been afraid of at first, the wide water and the unknown channels. She thought about Kit, who she had watched from the dune before she had the courage to climb down.

She thought about her parents' divorce, which she would have to face sooner or later.

Maybe the first time was always the hardest. Maybe the second time would be easier.

She kept her hands on the clay.


Chapter 31: The Fog Returns

The fog came back in August.

It rolled in on the first of the month, thicker than before, turning the world white. Wren woke to a sky that was not there. The cottage was surrounded by nothing.

She ate breakfast in the kitchen with Margie, the fog pressing against the windows.

"No boat today," Margie said. "Can't see ten feet in front of you."

Wren nodded. She had made plans to meet Kit at the plank, but she knew he would not come in this weather. Nobody went out on the water in fog like this.

She spent the morning helping Margie in the studio. She threw three more bowls, each one better than the last. She trimmed the cup she had glazed, smoothing the edges, carving a small wave pattern around the rim.

"Where did you learn to do that?" Margie asked, looking at the wave.

"I don't know. I just did it."

"That's the best way."

The fog did not lift by afternoon. It did not lift by evening. It settled over the island like a blanket, muffling every sound.

Wren went to bed early. She lay in the dark and listened to the foghorn in the distance, a low moan that came every thirty seconds.

She thought about Kit. She wondered if he was listening to the same horn. She wondered if he was thinking about Ohio.

She fell asleep with the sound of the foghorn in her ears, regular and steady, guiding invisible ships through the white.


Chapter 32: The Fourth Bowl

The fog lifted on the third morning.

Wren woke to sunlight streaming through her window. The sky was blue. The marsh was green. The world had returned.

She dressed quickly and walked to the estuary. The water was flat and clean, the air fresh, the mudflats steaming in the morning sun.

Kit was already at the plank. He was sitting on the edge, his feet dangling over the water, whittling a piece of driftwood with a pocket knife.

"You missed a good fog," he said.

"Was it like this at your house?"

"Same fog. Different windows."

He put the knife away and stood up. "Want to take the skiff out? I want to check the eelgrass beds."

"What's in the eelgrass?"

"Everything. Fish. Crabs. Baby lobsters. If the eelgrass is healthy, the estuary is healthy."

They took the skiff to the shallows, where the eelgrass grew in dense green patches beneath the surface. Kit leaned over the side and scooped up a handful of the long, ribbon-like leaves.

"Look," he said.

Wren leaned over. Tiny shrimp darted through the eelgrass. Small fish hovered, their bodies almost transparent. A crab no bigger than her thumbnail clung to a leaf.

"It's a whole city in there," she said.

"That's what my dad used to call it. The city under the water."

Kit let the eelgrass slip back into the water. He watched it sink, the leaves settling back into place.

"He'd like you," he said. "He'd like that you notice things."

Wren did not know what to say to that. She dipped her hand in the water and felt the eelgrass brush against her fingers, soft and alive, holding a thousand tiny creatures in its green embrace.


Chapter 33: The News

Kit's mother had made a decision.

He told Wren about it on the porch of the cottage, the afternoon sun hot on their shoulders. He had walked up the road without saying hello, sat down on the step, and just said it.

"She booked the movers. Three weeks from Saturday."

Wren felt something close in her chest. "Ohio?"

"Cleveland. Her sister lives in a suburb called Shaker Heights."

"When are you leaving?"

"End of August." He picked at a splinter in the porch railing. "Same time as you."

Wren sat down beside him. She did not know what to say. She had known this was coming. He had told her weeks ago. But knowing and happening were different things.

"Are you going to be okay?" she asked.

"No." He said it without self-pity, just as a fact. "But I don't have a choice."

They sat in silence. A bumblebee worked its way through the lavender by the steps. The tide was coming in, the water rising, the channels filling.

"I won't forget this summer," Kit said. "Even if everything else changes."

Wren looked at him. His face was turned toward the estuary, toward the water he had spent his whole life learning to read.

"Me neither," she said.


Chapter 34: The Last Map

Kit brought his map of the estuary to the cottage and spread it on the kitchen table.

They stood over it, looking at the lines he had drawn over two years of exploration. Every channel and sandbar and island. Every hidden cove. Every place where the water did something interesting.

"I want you to have this," he said. "The real one."

"Kit, I can't — "

"Take it. I have it memorized anyway."

He pointed to a spot on the map, a small mark he had not shown her before. "This is my name. I carved it into a rock on Ledge Island when I was ten."

She looked at the mark. It was a K, cut into a triangle shape.

"If you ever come back," he said, "you can find it."

Wren traced the outline of the estuary with her finger. She followed the Winding Way to the Heron Reach. She found the hidden pond and the wreck and the clam flats.

She folded the map carefully and put it in her pocket.

"I don't know if I'll ever come back," she said.

"Doesn't matter. The map will remember."

He walked back toward the dunes, his hands in his pockets, not looking back.

Wren watched him go. She touched the folded map in her pocket.

She would keep it forever.


Chapter 35: The Ceremony

Aunt Margie had a small kiln-opening ceremony for the glazed pieces.

She invited Wren and Kit and a neighbor named Esther, who ran the island's library. They gathered in the studio as Margie unlatched the kiln door and pulled it open.

The heat washed over them. Inside, the pieces glowed — bright blues and greens and whites, the colors transformed by the fire.

Margie lifted Wren's cup out first. The blue had come out exactly as Wren had hoped — the color of deep water, the color of the estuary at noon. The wave pattern around the rim stood out in darker blue, like a line of surf.

"That's beautiful," Esther said. "Did you really make that yourself?"

Wren nodded. She took the cup in her hands. It was warm. It was real.

She held it up to the light. The glaze was glossy and smooth. She could see her reflection in the curve of the bowl — a small, distorted face, looking back at her.

"It's your first piece," Margie said. "You'll keep it forever."

Wren thought about that. She thought about taking the cup home to her mother's house, setting it on the windowsill of her room, looking at it on the days when she needed to remember.

She would remember the blue. The clay. The heat of the kiln.

She would remember everything.


Chapter 36: The Cattails

The cattails in the hidden pond had gone to seed.

Kit and Wren took the skiff through the channel one last time. The full moon had passed, but the tide was still high enough to squeeze through the narrow opening.

The pond was different now. The green had faded. The stalks were brown and dry, bursting with white fluff. Every time the wind blew, the fluff lifted into the air like snow.

"It's almost over," Wren said.

"Yeah."

They drifted to the center of the pond. The cattail fluff settled on the water, a thin layer of white on the black surface.

Kit reached over the side and scooped up a handful of the fluff. He let it run through his fingers.

"Next summer, these will all be green again," he said. "New stalks. New leaves. It'll look like nothing changed."

"But it will have changed."

"Yeah. It always does."

Wren took out the green bottle she had found on the mudflats weeks ago. She had been carrying it in her jacket pocket every day, not sure why, not sure what to do with it.

She held it up to the light. The glass was cloudy, worn smooth by years of tumbling in the sand and water.

"I want to leave it here," she said.

"Why?"

"Because then I'll know a piece of me is still in the pond. Even when I'm gone."

Kit nodded. He took the bottle and placed it carefully on the bank of the pond, half-hidden in the cattails.

They sat in the skiff and watched the fluff drift across the water. Neither of them spoke.

There was nothing left to say.


Chapter 37: The Last Week

The last week of August arrived with a heat wave.

The air was thick and heavy, the estuary flat and still. Even the gulls seemed to have given up, sitting motionless on the channel markers, their beaks open.

Wren spent the mornings at the beach, swimming in the cold Atlantic water. She spent the afternoons in the studio, throwing pots that she would not have time to fire.

She did not say goodbye to Kit. Not yet. There was still time.

On Wednesday, she helped Margie clean the kiln. On Thursday, she walked to the library and checked out a book on coastal birds. On Friday, she sat on the porch and looked at the map Kit had given her, tracing the lines with her finger.

Her mother was coming on Sunday.

She would take the ferry at noon. She would pack her suitcase and close the drawer and leave the room exactly as she had found it.

She would take the cup. The map. The green bottle was gone, left in the pond, but she had its shape in her memory.

On Friday night, she walked to the estuary alone. The skiff was tied to the plank. She sat in it for an hour, rocking gently on the water, not going anywhere.

She did not cry. She was saving it for later.


Chapter 38: The Last Rowing

Kit found her at the plank on Saturday morning.

"One more trip," he said.

They took the skiff out for the last time. Kit rowed past the heron rookery, where the chicks had fledged and the nests were empty. Past the wreck, which was fully covered by the tide. Past the clam flats, which were underwater.

He rowed to the middle of the estuary and stopped.

"The tide is going out," he said. "In six hours, all this water will be gone. And then it'll come back."

Wren looked at the water. It was green and brown and gray, layered and complex, full of things she could not see but knew were there.

"I never learned to row," she said.

"You learned other things."

She thought about that. She had learned the names of the channels. The habits of herons. The way the moon pulled the water. The silence of a hidden pond at dusk.

She had learned that some things end. That most things end. That ending does not mean they did not matter.

Kit shipped the oars and let the skiff drift. They sat facing each other, the estuary spread around them like a living map.

"I hope Ohio is okay," she said.

"It won't be the same."

"No. But maybe it'll be okay anyway."

She did not tell him she would miss him. He did not tell her he would miss her either. They did not need to say it.

The skiff turned in the current, pointing toward the open sea.

"We should head back," Kit said. He picked up the oars.

Wren nodded. She looked at the estuary one last time — the channels and the marsh and the sky — and tried to print it on her memory, every detail, every shade of blue and green and gray.

She knew it would fade. She did not want it to. But she knew it would.


Chapter 39: The Goodbye

They tied the skiff to the plank for the last time.

Kit stepped onto the plank and held out his hand. Wren took it. His palm was rough and warm, the same as it had been on the first day.

"Thank you," she said. "For showing me everything."

He nodded. His jaw was tight.

"I have something for you." He reached into his pocket and pulled out a small object — a piece of sea glass, worn smooth, the color of pale jade.

"I found it last winter," he said. "I was saving it for something."

Wren took it. The glass was cool in her palm, the edges soft, the surface frosted by years of tumbling in the sand and water.

She put it in her pocket, next to the folded map.

Kit looked at her. For a moment, she thought he was going to say something else. But he just nodded, once, and turned away.

He walked up the dune without looking back. His silhouette crossed the ridge and disappeared.

Wren stood at the plank for a long time, holding the sea glass in her hand, watching the place where he had been.

Then she walked back to the cottage to pack.


Chapter 40: The Packing

Wren packed her suitcase in the late afternoon light.

She folded her clothes and put them in the brown vinyl suitcase with the broken clasp. She wrapped her blue cup in a t-shirt and tucked it in the center, surrounded by soft things. She put Kit's map and the sea glass in the front pocket of her jacket, which she would wear on the ferry.

She took the photograph of her dog out of the desk drawer. She took her journal. She left the empty jar on the desk for the next person who might stay in this room.

Aunt Margie appeared in the doorway. She leaned against the frame, her arms crossed, her hands still stained with clay.

"You don't have to go, you know," she said. "You could stay. Finish the school year here."

Wren looked at her. The offer was real. She could see it in Margie's face.

"I have to go home," Wren said. "My mom needs me."

Margie nodded. She did not argue.

"I'll save your pieces," she said. "When you come back to visit, they'll be waiting."

Wren zipped the suitcase. It was heavier than it had been when she arrived, though she had packed the same things.

She did not know if she would ever come back. But she liked knowing that the choice existed.

That night, she ate Margie's soup for the last time. It tasted the same as the first night. She was glad.


Chapter 41: The Morning

Wren woke early on the last day.

The sun was just rising, the sky pink and gold, the estuary visible through her window. She got dressed and went outside.

The air was cool and clean. The grass was wet with dew. She walked to the top of the dune and looked out at the water one last time.

The estuary was beautiful in the morning light. The channels glowed. The marsh grass was green and gold. A heron stood at the edge of a channel, motionless, watching the water.

Wren sat down in the sand. She did not know how long she sat there. Long enough for the sun to clear the treeline. Long enough for the heron to catch a fish and swallow it.

She thought about the summer. The skiff and the mudflats and the hidden pond. The heron rookery and the wreck and the clam flats. Kit's map and the green bottle and the sea glass in her pocket.

She thought about her parents, who would be in separate houses now. She thought about her mother, who would be waiting at the dock.

She thought about how she was not the same person who had arrived here in June.

She stood up and brushed the sand off her pants. She walked back to the cottage.

It was time to go.


Chapter 42: The Ferry

The ferry left the island at noon.

Wren stood at the rail and watched the island shrink. First the details went — the windows of the cottages, the boats in the harbor, the people on the dock. Then the shape went, the island becoming a dark green hump against the blue water. Then it was just a line on the horizon, indistinguishable from the clouds.

She stayed at the rail for the whole crossing.

Aunt Margie had hugged her at the dock. A real hug, not the awkward half-hug of the first day. Margie had smelled like clay and wood smoke. She had held on for an extra second.

"Come back anytime," she had said. "The room will always be there."

Wren had nodded, not trusting her voice.

Now she watched the wake of the ferry — the white line spreading behind the boat, slowly dissolving into the gray water. She thought about Kit's map, folded in her jacket pocket. She touched the sea glass, smooth and cool, a small weight that was hers.

The mainland grew larger. She could see the buildings now. The cars on the road. The shape of her mother standing at the end of the dock.

Wren straightened her jacket. She lifted her suitcase.

The ferry horn sounded once, long and low, announcing their arrival.


Chapter 43: The Other Side

Wren's mother was waiting at the terminal.

She looked the same as she had in June — the same hair, the same coat, the same way of holding her hands in front of her. But something was different. She looked smaller, somehow, or softer, or just older.

She saw Wren coming and raised her hand in a small wave.

Wren walked down the ramp. When she reached the dock, her mother opened her arms.

Wren stepped into them. Her mother held her tightly, the way she used to hold her when Wren was small and the world seemed too big.

"I missed you," her mother said.

"I missed you too."

They stood on the dock for a moment, holding each other. The ferry horn sounded again. Seagulls circled overhead.

"Are you okay?" her mother asked.

Wren thought about it. She thought about the estuary and Kit and Aunt Margie. She thought about the blue cup wrapped in her suitcase and the map in her pocket.

"I think so," she said. "I think I will be."

Her mother smiled. It was a small smile, tentative, but real.

"Let's go home," she said.

They walked to the car together. Wren did not look back at the ferry. She looked forward, at the road ahead, at the town she had left in June.

It looked the same. But she knew it was different.

She was different.


Chapter 44: The House

The house looked the same from the outside.

The same blue door. The same cracked step. The same rose bush that had never quite thrived in the front yard.

But inside, everything had changed.

Her father's chair was gone from the living room. The books he had been reading were no longer on the coffee table. The closet by the front door held only her mother's coats.

Wren stood in the hallway and looked around. The house was quiet. Not the comfortable quiet of an empty afternoon, but a different quiet — a silence that had settled into the walls.

"Your room is the same," her mother said. "I didn't change anything."

Wren went upstairs. Her room was exactly as she had left it. The same bed. The same desk. The same posters on the walls.

She set the blue cup on the windowsill. It looked right there, against the glass, the light coming through it, turning the glaze a deeper blue.

She took out Kit's map and spread it on her desk. The estuary stretched across the paper, every channel and sandbar and island named in his careful handwriting.

She touched the spot where Ledge Island was drawn. She touched the hidden pond.

She thought about the green bottle, half-hidden in the cattails.

She thought about the sea glass, smooth and cool, in her jacket pocket.

She was home. But home was not the same as it had been. And she was not the same either.


Chapter 45: The Bedroom

Wren spent the first afternoon back in her bedroom.

She did not unpack her suitcase. She lay on her bed and looked at the ceiling. She could hear her mother moving around downstairs, opening cabinets, running water.

The house felt strange. Too big. Too quiet. The walls seemed to remember things Wren was trying not to think about.

She took out the piece of sea glass Kit had given her. She held it up to the light. It was the color of shallow water over sand, pale and translucent, the edges worn to velvet.

She wondered what Kit was doing right now. If he was at the plank. If he was in the skiff. If he was looking at the same sun, the same clouds, the same sky that she was looking at.

She wondered if he was thinking about Ohio.

She put the sea glass on her nightstand, next to her lamp. It caught the light and glowed.

Her mother called her for dinner. Wren got up and went downstairs.

The kitchen was different too. Her father's mug was gone from the hook by the stove. The magnets on the refrigerator had been rearranged. A new plant sat on the windowsill — a succulent in a terra-cotta pot.

"We're having pasta," her mother said. "I know it's not clam chowder, but — "

"It's fine, Mom."

They ate at the kitchen table. Wren told her mother about Aunt Margie's studio and the pottery wheel and the blue cup she had made. She told her about the heron rookery and the hidden pond and the skiff.

She did not tell her about Kit. Not because she was hiding him, but because she did not have the words yet. He was still too close, too real.

She would tell her later. When she had figured out how.


Chapter 46: The First Night

Wren could not sleep that night.

She lay in her bed and listened to the sounds of the house — the creak of old wood, the hum of the refrigerator, the wind in the trees. It was different from the cottage. There was no salt air. No distant foghorn. No tide going in or out.

She got out of bed and went to the window.

The street was quiet. The houses across the road were dark. A single streetlight cast a pool of orange light on the pavement.

In the cottage, she had looked out at the estuary. At the water that was always moving, always changing. Here, she looked at lawns and driveways and mailboxes. Everything was still. Everything stayed where it was put.

She missed the movement. She missed the way the light changed on the water. She missed knowing that the tide would turn, that nothing stayed the same, that change was the only constant.

She went back to bed and closed her eyes.

She thought about the hidden pond at dusk, the cattails bursting with white fluff, the skiff drifting in the still water.

She thought about Kit, sitting across from her in the boat, not saying anything, not needing to.

She fell asleep with the image of the pond in her mind, the water black and silver, the moon doubled.


Chapter 47: The Phone

Wren called Aunt Margie the next morning.

"Hello?"

"Hi, Aunt Margie. It's Wren."

"Wren. It's good to hear your voice. How are you?"

"I'm okay. I wanted to say thank you. For everything."

"You don't have to thank me. That's what aunts are for."

Wren held the phone closer. "Is the skiff still there?"

"Still there. I saw Kit this morning. He was walking toward the estuary with a bucket."

"He's still there?"

"For a few more days. The movers come next week."

Wren's chest tightened. She had known this. He had told her. But hearing it made it real in a different way.

"Can I talk to him?"

"He doesn't have a phone, sweetheart. But I'll tell him you called, if I see him."

"Okay. Thank you."

"Take care, Wren. The door is always open."

Wren hung up and sat on the stairs. Her mother was in the kitchen, washing dishes, the water running.

She thought about Kit, walking toward the estuary with a bucket. She thought about what he was carrying. About what he was leaving behind.

She hoped Ohio would be kind to him. She hoped the lakes there had herons. She hoped he found a pond somewhere that understood him.

She had given him a scallop shell, once, on the day he told her about his father. She wondered if he still had it.


Chapter 48: The Return

A week later, Wren was sitting in her room when she heard the mail slot click.

She went downstairs and found an envelope on the floor. No return address. Her name written in careful handwriting she recognized.

She opened it with her finger.

Inside was a single sheet of paper, folded in thirds. She unfolded it.

Dear Wren,

I am writing this on the porch. The tide is going out. The herons are in the rookery. Everything is the same, except you are not here.

We leave for Ohio next Tuesday. I do not want to go. But I have been thinking about what you said about the tide. It comes. It goes. You learn to read it or you get left behind.

I am trying to read it.

Thank you for coming to the estuary. Thank you for noticing things. Thank you for the shell. I still have it. I keep it in my pocket.

I do not know if I will ever see you again. But I will remember you. I will remember that there was a summer when someone else knew the hidden pond.

Take care of the map.

Your friend,
Kit

Wren read the letter three times. Then she folded it carefully and put it in the drawer with the map and the sea glass and the letter she had written to her mother.

She would keep it forever.


Chapter 49: The Glass

Wren found her mother in the kitchen that evening.

Her mother was sitting at the table, a cup of tea in front of her, staring at nothing. She looked tired. She looked like someone who had been holding her breath for a long time.

Wren sat down across from her.

"Mom? I have something to tell you."

Her mother looked up. "What is it?"

Wren took a breath. "I'm okay. I mean, I'm sad about you and Dad. I'm sad about a lot of things. But I'm okay. And I want you to know that you don't have to pretend with me. You can tell me the truth."

Her mother's eyes filled with tears. She did not try to hide them.

"I'm sorry," she said. "I'm sorry you had to go through this. I'm sorry I sent you away."

"You didn't send me away. You sent me to Aunt Margie. It was different." Wren paused. "It was good, actually. The summer was good."

Her mother reached across the table and took her hand. "Tell me about it. Tell me everything."

So Wren did. She told her about Kit and the skiff and the heron rookery. About the hidden pond and the clam flats and the wreck. About the map and the bottle and the sea glass.

She talked for an hour. Her mother listened.

When she finished, her mother was crying and smiling at the same time.

"I'm glad you had that summer," she said. "I'm glad you had that."

Wren nodded. She was glad too.


Chapter 50: The Tide

Wren put the map on her wall.

She pinned it above her desk, where she could see it every morning. The estuary stretched across the paper, every channel and sandbar and island drawn in Kit's careful hand. The Winding Way. The Heron Reach. The Sunken Channel. The hidden pond, marked with no name.

She looked at it before school each day. She looked at it when she came home. She traced the lines with her finger and remembered.

Her father had moved to a different apartment. A nicer one, with a balcony. She visited on weekends. He was trying. They were all trying.

Her mother had started therapy. She laughed more now, a real laugh, not the hollow sound from the spring.

The blue cup sat on Wren's windowsill. The sea glass sat on her nightstand. The letter from Kit was in her top drawer.

She did not know if she would ever go back to the island. She did not know if Kit was happy in Ohio. She did not know if the hidden pond was still there, or if the bottle she had left was still half-hidden in the cattails.

But she knew the tide was still turning. Somewhere, right now, the water was rising or falling, the channels filling or emptying, the herons standing motionless at the edge of the shallows.

Some things ended. Most things ended. But the water always came back.

Wren looked at the map one more time. She touched the spot where Ledge Island was drawn, where Kit had carved his name into a rock when he was ten.

Then she turned off the light and went downstairs for breakfast.

The tide was going out. The tide was coming in.

She was going to be okay.
