# The Depth of Summer

## Premise

Twelve-year-old Wren is sent to her Aunt Margie's coastal cottage for what her parents call "a summer by the sea" — a polite fiction for the season before their divorce is finalized. Adrift in a strange town, Wren discovers a vast tidal estuary behind the dunes, a hidden world of channels and mudflats that reveals itself only at low tide. There she meets Kit, a quiet boy of fourteen who navigates the estuary as if it were his own private kingdom. Together they explore the secret geography of the water — the old wreck, the heron rookery, the clam flats, the channel that appears only twice a month. But the tide always turns, and Wren must learn that some things, like families and summers, cannot be held onto forever. Set on the coast of Maine, this is the summer Wren stops being a child who believes permanence is possible and becomes someone who understands the beauty of what passes.

## Core Cast

- **Wren** — Twelve years old, watchful and quiet, the youngest of three children, used to being overlooked. She notices things others miss: the way light changes on water, the tension in her mother's voice, the shapes of clouds. She is about to learn that noticing does not mean you can stop anything from ending.

- **Kit** — Fourteen, lean and sun-browned, lives year-round in the small coastal town. He knows the estuary's every channel, every sandbar, every bird. His father died in a fishing accident three years ago, and he has not spoken much since. The estuary is where he goes to be alone — until Wren follows him there.

- **Aunt Margie** — Wren's mother's younger sister, in her early forties, unmarried, a potter who works from a studio behind her cottage. She is patient and perceptive, does not pry, and understands that some things must be discovered on their own. She is the only adult who tells Wren the truth: that endings are part of everything.

## 50 Chapter Plan

### Part I: The Incoming Tide (Chapters 1-10)

1. Wren watches the mainland shrink from the ferry window, her suitcase on the seat beside her.
2. Aunt Margie meets her at the dock — a woman with clay under her fingernails and no questions about Wren's parents.
3. Wren explores the cottage: the spare room, the pottery wheel in the back studio, the salt air through every window.
4. She walks to the end of the road and finds the dunes — a wall of grass and sand hiding something beyond.
5. Wren climbs the dune and sees the estuary for the first time: channels of dark water winding through mudflats, a skiff tied to a warped plank.
6. She meets Kit at the skiff — he does not introduce himself, just asks if she knows how to row.
7. They take the skiff out on the rising tide, the water filling the channels like veins filling with blood.
8. Kit shows her the heron rookery — six nests in dead pine trees, birds standing like gray statues.
9. Wren's mother calls that night. Wren says the cottage is fine. Her mother says nothing about coming home.
10. Wren wakes early and goes to the estuary alone, but she does not take the skiff — she just sits on the bank and waits.

### Part II: The Estuary's Grammar (Chapters 11-20)

11. Kit finds her sitting there. He says the water teaches you if you sit still enough.
12. He shows her how to read the tide charts — the moon's phases drawn in a spiral-bound notebook.
13. They walk the mudflats at low tide, squelching through silt that grabs their ankles.
14. Kit points out the creatures: ribbed mussels, fiddler crabs, a horseshoe crab dragging its helmet shell.
15. They find a bottle half-buried in the mud — old, green glass, the lettering worn smooth.
16. Wren takes the bottle back to the cottage. Aunt Margie says it was probably tossed from a ship fifty years ago.
17. Kit shows her the wreck: the ribs of a fishing boat sunk in the main channel, visible only at the lowest tides.
18. He tells her his father died on a boat like that. Not this one. But like it.
19. Wren does not know what to say. She picks up a scallop shell and hands it to him. He takes it.
20. That night, Wren writes in her journal for the first time in weeks.

### Part III: The Full Moon Tide (Chapters 21-30)

21. The full moon brings the highest tide of the summer — the water reaches the cottage's back step.
22. Kit and Wren take the skiff out at dusk, the water like dark glass under the rising moon.
23. Kit points to a channel that only appears on full-moon tides, a secret path through the marsh.
24. They navigate the narrow channel into a hidden pond — round and still, ringed by cattails.
25. A flock of black ducks lifts off the pond, their wings making a sound like rain on a tin roof.
26. Kit tells Wren that his mother wants to move inland, to her sister's in Ohio. He does not want to go.
27. Wren tells Kit about her parents — how her father is already living in an apartment, how her mother cries in the kitchen at night.
28. They sit in the skiff on the hidden pond until the stars come out. Neither of them speaks.
29. On the walk back, Kit takes her hand once, briefly, to help her over a slick rock. He lets go on the other side.
30. Wren lies in bed and tries to remember what silence sounded like before she came here.

### Part IV: The Turning (Chapters 31-40)

31. A fog rolls in and stays for three days — the estuary disappears into white, the skiff moored invisible.
32. Wren helps Aunt Margie in the pottery studio, learning to center clay on the wheel.
33. Kit appears out of the fog on the third day. He has a map of the estuary he drew himself.
34. They spread the map on the cottage porch. Kit has named every channel and sandbar. Wren traces the lines with her finger.
35. Wren's father calls. He says he hopes she is having a good summer. She says she is and hangs up quickly.
36. Kit takes her to the clam flats at dawn, showing her how to find the dimples in the sand that mean clams are below.
37. They dig a bucket of soft-shell clams and cook them on a driftwood fire on the beach.
38. Kit talks about his father: what he remembers, what he wishes he remembered, what he has made himself forget.
39. Wren tells him her parents are getting divorced. It is the first time she has said it out loud to anyone.
40. The tide that evening is lower than any they have seen, exposing a sandbar that connects to a small island.

### Part V: The Ebb (Chapters 41-50)

41. They walk the sandbar to the island. The island has a single pine tree, bent sideways by the wind.
42. From the island, the mainland looks different — smaller, less solid, more like something that could disappear.
43. Wren realizes the summer is running out. She can feel it the way she can feel the tide turning.
44. Kit and Wren take one last trip to the hidden pond. The cattails have gone to seed, the stalks bursting with white fluff.
45. Kit says his mother has decided. They are moving to Ohio at the end of August.
46. Wren's mother arrives to take her home. She looks smaller than Wren remembered. Softer. Sadder.
47. Wren says goodbye to Aunt Margie at the cottage door, clay dust still on the front step.
48. She walks to the estuary one last time, alone. The skiff is tied to the plank. Kit is not there.
49. She leaves the green bottle on the plank seat of the skiff — a message for whoever finds it next.
50. On the ferry back, Wren watches the coast shrink again. But this time she is looking forward, not back.
