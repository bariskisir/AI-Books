Chapter 1: Stand To

The hut smelled of damp wool and cigarette smoke. Miller sat on his cot, honing the edge of his Fairbairn-Sykes with a stone he had carried from North Africa. Outside, the rain fell steady over Lincolnshire.

Flynn watched him from the bunk opposite. The boy could not have been more than nineteen. He kept checking his own knife, flipping it end over end.

"You will cut yourself," Miller said.

"Just getting a feel for it."

"You will not feel a thing. That is the point."

The door banged open. Lieutenant Ashford stood there, a torch in one hand, maps under his arm. His face was pale, freshly shaved, the skin tight across his cheekbones. He looked like he had not slept in days.

"Briefing in twenty," he said. "Full kit."

The men stirred. Someone coughed. Smithy, the Bren gunner, began wrapping his weapon in an oily cloth. The rest of the section rose on creaking springs.

"You hear that?" Flynn whispered.

Miller slid the knife into its sheath. "I hear it."

"Rhino, Horsa, Pegasus. Code words. Means it is tonight."

"Means it is a job like any other."

Flynn shook his head. "My mum thinks I am in Aldershot. A kitchen clerk. She sends me biscuits."

"Eat them while you can."

The whistle blew across the camp. Stand to. Miller stood, hoisted his webbing, and felt the familiar weight settle across his shoulders. The weight of everything he carried.

Chapter 2: The Briefing

The briefing tent was crowded and warm. A kerosene lamp hissed on the table, casting shadows across the map pinned to the easel. Ashford stood at the back, taking notes. Miller sat in the front row, elbows on his knees.

The major pointed with a stick. "Your objective is this bridge. Crossings here, here, and here are intact. Intelligence says light resistance. A platoon of Osttruppen, maybe a section of engineers."

Someone laughed. A low, bitter sound.

"You will drop at zero one hundred hours. Assemble at the rally point marked Victor. Move to the bridge and hold until the commandos reach you."

Ashford raised his hand. "Sir, the flak situation?"

"Minimal. The Luftwaffe has pulled back. You will be in and out before they know you are there."

Miller studied the aerial photo. The bridge looked small, insignificant. A ribbon of grey stone over a dark thread of water. The fields around it were flat. The kind of ground where a man could be seen from a mile away.

After the briefing, Ashford caught Miller at the door.

"You do not look convinced, Sergeant."

"I have seen flat ground before, sir. It is never as flat as the map says."

Ashford nodded. He seemed to want to say something else, but the words would not come. He folded his map and walked out into the rain.

Chapter 3: White Horse

The Albemarle taxied through puddles, its engines coughing and catching. Flynn sat on the bench, the canvas straps cutting into his thighs. He could feel the vibration through the floor.

Through the small window, he watched the English countryside fall away. Fields and hedgerows, grey and green. And then, on a hillside, a white horse carved into the chalk. An old thing. Ancient.

He had seen it on training exercises. Always thought it looked like a creature running, caught mid-stride. Now it looked like something trying to escape.

Smithy sat across from him, the Bren across his knees, his eyes closed. His lips moved. Prayer or curse, Flynn could not tell.

"Ten minutes," the dispatcher shouted.

The engines changed pitch. The aircraft began to climb. Flynn felt his stomach rise with it.

He thought of his mother, asleep in the flat above the bakery on Bethnal Green Road. She would wake to the milkman and the blackout blinds and the silence of a boy who was not there.

Miller sat motionless, his face blank. He had done this before. North Africa. Sicily. He had the look of a man who had stopped being afraid and had not found anything to replace it.

A red light came on above the door.

Chapter 4: Green Light

The dispatcher slid the door open. The wind roared in, cold and raw. The night was a black wall with no detail, no edge.

"Stand up!"

The men rose, heavy with equipment. Flynn could barely move. Forty pounds of kit, his rifle, the folded bicycle he was supposed to ride to the bridge. He had asked why a paratrooper needed a bicycle. Nobody had laughed.

"Check equipment!"

Miller checked Flynn's harness, yanked the straps hard. Flynn checked his own. The routine was the only thing that made sense.

"Hook up!"

The snap of the static line hooks onto the cable. A sound he had heard a hundred times in training, but never like this. This was the real one.

"Red on!"

The light burned red. Flynn stared at it. His mouth was dry.

"Green on! Go!"

The men shuffled forward. Smithy vanished into the dark. Then Jackson. Then Miller, without a pause.

Flynn reached the door. The wind hit him like a wall. The aircraft shook. He could see nothing below. No earth. No sea. Nothing.

The dispatcher grabbed his arm. "Go, son, go!"

Flynn jumped.

The slipstream tore at him. His body tumbled, arms and legs spinning. He grabbed for the D-ring and pulled.

The parachute cracked open above him. The silence came all at once. He hung in the black, swinging, alone.

Chapter 5: Silk and Static

The flak came from nowhere. Tracer rounds climbed past him, lazy red beads that seemed to float. Then the sound hit. A hammering, tearing noise.

He looked up. Above him, an aircraft was burning. It did not bank or dive. It simply fell, trailing a tail of flame, and struck the earth somewhere to his left.

The ground rushed up. Trees. He pulled on the risers, trying to steer, but the wind had him. The canopy caught branches. He crashed through them, hit something hard, and the world went dark.

He woke face-down in mud. The parachute was tangled above him, a white smear in the branches. His ears rang. He tasted blood.

He lay still and listened. Crickets. The distant crackle of fire. And then, closer, a sound he did not recognize. A gurgling breath.

He turned his head. A few feet away, a man lay twisted in his harness. The man's chest was wet and dark. His eyes were open, staring at nothing. Not Smithy. Not Miller. A face Flynn had seen at the briefing but never learned the name.

He forced himself to stand. His legs held. He drew his knife and cut himself free.

He was alone in a field in France, and he did not know where anyone was.

Chapter 6: Wrong Field

Miller hit the ground hard, his ankle twisting under him. He rolled, came up on one knee, and scanned the darkness. Cows watched him from the corner of a field. Their eyes reflected the moon.

He gathered his parachute, dragged it into the hedge, and buried it under dead leaves. The field was wrong. He had memorized the terrain from the aerial photos. This was not the drop zone. No pond. No windmill. No church steeple.

He pulled out his compass, took a bearing. The bridge should be east. But east of where?

He clicked his locator beacon three times. Nothing but static. The set was dead.

He moved to the edge of the field and found a sunken lane. The hedgerows on either side were high, dense. He could hear the rumble of engines in the distance. German engines. He knew the sound.

He crouched and waited. One man could not take a bridge. He needed to find the others.

A shape moved in the darkness fifty yards down the lane. Miller raised his Sten.

The shape stopped. A click. Then a sound. The whistle of a skylark.

Miller smiled, despite everything. He answered with the same call.

Chapter 7: The First Dead

Ashford landed in a tree. His parachute caught in the upper branches, suspending him twenty feet above the ground. He hung there, struggling, his rifle pressing into his ribs.

The tree was an apple tree. He could smell the blossoms.

He drew his knife and cut the risers. He fell, hit the ground badly, and lay there with the wind knocked out of him. His ankle screamed when he tried to stand.

He heard movement. He crawled to the trunk of the tree and waited.

A man walked past in the dark. German. Ashford could see the shape of the helmet, the rifle slung across the back. The man was looking up, scanning the trees.

Ashford held his breath. He did not move.

The German passed. Ashford counted to sixty, then moved.

He found the first dead man in a ditch. A British paratrooper, still in his harness. The body had been stripped of weapons and equipment. The face was grey in the moonlight.

Ashford recognized him. Corporal Davies. He had shared a cigarette with him on the tarmac.

He knelt beside the body. He did not know what to do. There was a protocol for everything in the army except this. The moment you found a man you knew, dead, before the fighting had even begun.

He took the corporal's identity disc and moved on.

Chapter 8: The Whistle

Flynn lay in a ditch and listened to the sound of boots on the road. German boots. The nailed soles clicked on the cobblestones. A patrol. Six, maybe seven men.

He pressed himself into the mud. His heart beat against the ground.

The patrol stopped. One of the men lit a cigarette. The flame briefly illuminated a young face, no older than Flynn. The man said something, and the others laughed.

Flynn had his service revolver in his hand. He had been told to use it only if he had to. He did not want to use it. He wanted to be invisible.

The patrol moved on. The footsteps faded.

He waited. The moon slid behind a cloud. He counted his breaths.

Then he heard it. A whistle. The skylark call. The recognition signal.

He answered. Two short bursts through his teeth.

A shape appeared at the edge of the ditch. A big man, moving quietly despite his size.

"You a replacement?" the man said.

"Flynn. Private. I am with Sergeant Miller's section."

"Miller bought it. I saw him go down. Parachute did not open."

Flynn's stomach turned. "You saw it?"

"I saw enough. I am Sergeant Price. A Company. How many others have you got?"

"Just me."

Price swore. "Come on. We are moving."

Chapter 9: Strangers in the Dark

The skylark call led Miller to a crossroad. A stone crucifix stood at the junction, the figure of Christ weathered and faceless. Beneath it, a man crouched.

"Sergeant Miller?"

"The same. Who is asking?"

"Private Crouch. Signal Corps. I have got a radio, but I cannot raise anyone."

Miller knelt beside him. "How many have you found?"

"Two. Both dead. I have been walking for an hour."

They waited. The night was full of sounds. Distant gunfire. The low thrum of bombers overhead. The occasional crack of a flare.

A figure emerged from the hedgerow. Then another. Miller's hand went to his knife.

"Stand fast," a voice said. "I have got a pistol and I will use it."

"Ashford?"

The lieutenant stepped into the moonlight. His uniform was torn, his face scratched. He looked nineteen years old.

"Sergeant. Thank God."

"How many, sir?"

"Seven. I have seven men in the woods east of here. Two wounded."

"Any NCOs?"

"Not yet."

Miller nodded. "We have Price and a signaller. That is ten. Maybe more out there."

"We have to move," Ashford said. "I heard engines. A lot of them."

"Where to, sir?"

Ashford looked at the crucifix, the faceless saint. "The farmhouse. Half a mile east. I saw it from the air."

Chapter 10: The Farmhouse

The farmhouse was abandoned. The family had left in a hurry. A loaf of bread sat on the table, half-cut, the knife still resting beside it. A child's shoe lay under a chair.

Ashford gathered the men in the main room. The windows were blacked out with blankets. A single candle burned on the mantel.

Miller counted. Eleven men, including himself. Two with minor injuries. One with a broken arm, set roughly by a medic who was dead now.

"The drop was scattered," Ashford said. "We knew that could happen. We have to proceed to the rally point."

"Victor," Miller said. "Four miles east."

"Correct."

"Victor is a copse. We need to be there by zero six hundred. That gives us four hours."

The men looked at each other. No one spoke.

"I need a headcount by section," Ashford said. "Sergeant Miller, you are senior NCO. Sort them out."

Miller went among them, assigning tasks. Sentries. Water detail. A man to carry the spare ammunition.

Flynn found himself assigned to the rear. He did not argue.

Before they left, Miller took Ashford aside. "Sir, did you see the road?"

"Which road?"

"The one through the village. It has a Tiger battalion written all over it. Tank tracks, fresh. If they are here, the intelligence is wrong."

Ashford was quiet for a moment. "We proceed as planned. We adjust when we have to."

"That is all I needed to hear."

Chapter 11: A Boy Named Fritz

They found him at dawn. The company had stopped to rest in a stone barn, and Flynn, checking the hayloft, had found a boy in a German uniform.

The boy could not have been sixteen. He held a Mauser with both hands, the barrel aimed at Flynn's chest.

"Don't shoot," Flynn said. "Do not shoot."

The boy's hands trembled. His eyes were wide, wet.

Miller appeared at the bottom of the ladder. "What is this?"

"German. Young."

Miller climbed up, moving slowly. He spoke in broken German. "Waffen niederlegen. You are safe."

The boy lowered the rifle. He started to cry.

They brought him down. He was wearing a uniform that did not fit. The sleeves were rolled. His boots were too large, stuffed with newspaper.

"Osttruppen," Miller said. "Polish or Ukrainian. They conscript them."

"What do we do with him?" Ashford asked.

"We leave him here. He is not a threat."

The boy did not understand English, but he understood the tone. He sat in the corner, watching the paratroopers with the expression of a beaten dog.

"His name is Fritz," Flynn said.

"How do you know?"

"I asked."

Ashford looked at the boy, then at his watch. "We move in five."

Chapter 12: Miller's Count

Miller sat with his back against the barn wall and did the maths in his head. Eighteen men. One Bren gun. Two Stens. Rifles. A radio that did not work.

The original company had been one hundred and twenty men. Four sticks, three aircraft. Now he had eighteen.

He thought about the battalion. Somewhere out there, other sticks were gathering. Maybe a hundred men. Maybe fewer.

He thought about the bridge. The aerial photos showed a single concrete span, fifty yards long. No pillboxes visible. But the photos were a week old. And the tank tracks on the village road told a different story.

Price came over. "We are not going to make the bridge, are we?"

"We are going to try."

"That is the same thing."

Miller did not answer. Price was not wrong.

Flynn sat nearby, eating a biscuit from a crumpled tin. His mother's biscuits. He offered one to Miller.

"Eat," Miller said. "You will need the energy."

"I am scared," Flynn said. "All the time."

"Good. That means you are not stupid."

"I thought I would be brave."

"Brave is doing it scared. There is no other way."

Flynn nodded. He wrapped the biscuit back in the tin and tucked it into his pocket.

Chapter 13: The Roadblock

The road to the bridge ran through the village of Sainte-Marie. Ashford's map showed it as a straight shot. But as they approached the edge of the village, they saw the roadblock.

Two trucks, parked side by side. Sandbags. A machine gun position.

"Can we go around?" Ashford asked.

Miller studied the terrain. "The fields are mined. Or flooded. I can see the water from here."

"Then we take the roadblock."

"With eighteen men?"

"We surprise them. Hit hard. Move fast."

Miller said nothing for a long moment. "It could work. If we come from two directions."

"Good. You take the left. I take the right. We go on my signal."

The men spread out. Flynn found himself beside Miller, his heart hammering.

"Stay close," Miller said. "Keep your head down. When I say go, you run straight at them and do not stop."

"What if they shoot me?"

"Then you fall. And the next man keeps running."

Miller's voice was flat. It was not cruelty. It was arithmetic.

A bird call from the other side of the road.

"Now," Miller said.

Chapter 14: Flynn's Watch

The roadblock fell in four minutes. Miller's section killed the machine gunner with a grenade. Ashford's men came in from the flank, firing. The Germans broke and ran.

Two of their own were wounded. A bullet had clipped Evans in the shoulder. A piece of shrapnel had opened Taggart's arm.

They moved through the village quickly. Shutters were closed. No one came out.

Beyond Sainte-Marie, the country opened up. Flat fields. Hedgerows. The sound of artillery in the distance.

Ashford called a halt in a wood. He set sentries. Flynn drew the first watch.

He sat at the edge of the trees, facing the direction they had come. The sun was up now, a pale disc through the clouds. He could see smoke rising from somewhere to the east.

He heard the engines before he saw them. A low rumble, building. He pressed himself flat.

The tanks came down the road. Three of them. Panthers. They moved slowly, their turrets turning, like beasts scanning for prey.

He watched them pass. He did not breathe. He counted the seconds until the sound faded.

When his watch ended, he went to Miller. "Panthers. Three of them. Heading east."

"Toward the bridge?"

"Yes."

Miller's face was unreadable. "Then we have a problem."

Chapter 15: What Ashford Knows

Ashford sat with the map spread across his knees. The pencil in his hand had worn down to a stub. He traced the route again. The same route. The same dead end.

Miller sat down across from him. "The tanks changed things."

"I know."

"The bridge is not lightly defended."

"I know."

"Sir, with respect, what do we do?"

Ashford looked up. His eyes were red. He had not slept in thirty hours.

"The intelligence was bad," he said. "I knew it before we dropped."

Miller stared at him. "You knew?"

"I saw the photos. The original ones, from forty-eight hours before the drop. They showed construction. Pillboxes. But Brigade told me the photos were outdated. They said the work was from last year and the positions were abandoned."

"And you believed them?"

"I had to. I am a lieutenant. I do what I am told."

Miller was silent. His jaw worked.

"We are dead men," he said.

"No. We find a way."

"There are Panthers in the field, pillboxes on the bridge, and we have eighteen men with one Bren gun. That is not a fight. That is a massacre."

Ashford folded the map. "Then we do not fight. Not like they expect."

"Sir?"

"We draw them out. Make them come to us."

Miller looked at him, a new expression on his face. Not respect exactly. Something closer to surprise.

Chapter 16: The Forgotten Order

The dispatch rider lay in a ditch, his motorcycle half-submerged in water. He had been dead for at least a day. The papers in his dispatch case were soaked but legible.

Miller pulled them out. German. He could read enough to understand the gist.

"Ashford," he called. "You need to see this."

They spread the papers on the ground. Orders. Movement orders for the 12th SS Panzer Division. They were bivouacked in the area. The bridge was on their supply route.

"This is not a quiet sector," Ashford said. "This is a major logistics hub."

"There is a whole division sitting on top of our objective."

"Not the whole division. A regiment. But yes."

Flynn listened, his stomach cold.

"What about the invasion?" he asked. "Is it still happening?"

Ashford looked at his watch. "The first waves hit the beaches at dawn. They are on the ground. But without the bridges secured, they cannot push inland."

"Then we have to take it," Flynn said.

Both men looked at him. He stood a little straighter.

"We have to. Or what was the point?"

Miller picked up his rifle. "The boy has a point."

Ashford stood. "We move. We find a place to observe the bridge. And we figure out how to take it with what we have."

Chapter 17: The Canal

The canal ran east to west, cutting across their line of march. On the map, it was a blue line no thicker than a pencil stroke. In reality, it was thirty feet wide and deep.

"We swim," Miller said.

"I cannot swim," Flynn said.

"No one can. Not with this kit. We hold onto logs, branches, each other. We will get across."

They found a spot where the reeds grew thick. The water was black, cold. Miller went first, his Sten held above his head. The water came up to his chest, then his chin.

Flynn followed. The cold hit him like a shock. He could not feel his legs. He gripped a floating branch and kicked.

Halfway across, Henderson went under. He did not come up. The men called his name. Nothing.

Price dove down, came up shaking his head. "Caught on something. A bicycle. He is stuck."

There was nothing to be done. They dragged themselves out on the far bank, soaking, shivering. Henderson stayed in the canal.

Ashford counted. Seventeen.

He did not stop. He did not let them stop. They had to keep moving.

Chapter 18: Dead Ground

The fields stretched ahead of them, bare and flat. The wheat had been cut early. The stubble offered no cover.

"Dead ground," Miller said. "We go one at a time. Fast."

Ashford nodded. "Who goes first?"

"I will."

Miller stepped out of the hedge and ran. He did not zigzag. He did not take cover. He ran straight, his boots pounding the dirt. He reached the far hedge in thirty seconds.

He signalled. The next man went.

Artillery began to fall before the fifth man made the crossing. The first shell landed fifty yards away. The second was closer. The third landed in the middle of the field.

Flynn was waiting his turn behind the hedge when the shelling started. He pressed himself into the earth. Each explosion was a punch to the chest.

"Go!" Ashford shouted.

Flynn ran. The ground shook beneath him. He saw smoke. He saw a crater where the field had been a moment before.

He reached the hedge. Miller grabbed his webbing and pulled him through.

"Good. You are alive."

Flynn could not speak. His ears were ringing. His legs were shaking.

The shelling stopped as suddenly as it had started. They counted heads. Sixteen.

Chapter 19: A Change of Plan

They gathered in a ruined chapel, its roof gone, its walls scarred by shrapnel. Ashford laid out the map on the altar stone.

"The bridge is here. The Panthers are here. The pillboxes are here." He marked each position. "If we try to cross the river here, we die."

"Then where?" Price asked.

Ashford pointed to a secondary crossing. A smaller bridge, two miles upstream. "This is a timber bridge. No pillboxes on the photos. A single company of engineers, maybe."

"The main objective," Miller said.

"Intelligence says the secondary bridge is not on the German supply route. It is not defended. But if we take it, we can hold it and let the commandos cross. The main bridge becomes irrelevant."

Miller studied the map. "It adds eight miles. Through occupied territory. With sixteen men."

"Can we do it?"

"We can try."

Flynn looked at the map. The secondary bridge was just a thin line on paper. But it was something.

"I say we do it," he said.

Ashford folded the map. "It is decided, then. We go for the secondary bridge."

Chapter 20: The Signal

They were moving through a copse of birch trees when the sound came. A low rumble at first, then a rolling, building roar. The ground vibrated.

"The navy," Miller said.

The naval bombardment had begun. Hundreds of guns, miles away, pounding the coastal defences. The sound was like continuous thunder. The sky to the northeast flickered orange.

Flynn stopped, turned, and listened.

"That is the invasion," Ashford said. "They are going in."

"Then we are too late," Price said.

"No. We are exactly where we need to be."

The sound rolled on for ten minutes. Then twenty. It did not stop.

Flynn thought of the beaches. The men wading through the surf. The bullets. The blood.

He thought about his mother, listening to the wireless in the flat above the bakery.

"They need us to hold the flank," Miller said. "All of this, the landings, the bombing, every man on the ground, it all hinges on us holding that bridge."

"No pressure," Price muttered.

"Put your back into it."

They started moving again. The sound of the guns grew fainter as they headed inland. But it never fully stopped.

Chapter 21: The Forge

The blacksmith's forge sat at the edge of a hamlet, its chimney cold, its door hanging off the hinges. Ashford sent two men to check it. They came back with the all-clear.

"Rest here. One hour."

Flynn collapsed against a wall. His feet were blistered, raw. Blood had soaked through his left sock. He pulled off his boot and winced.

Miller saw him. He knelt and examined Flynn's foot with rough hands. "You should have said something."

"What good would it do?"

Miller shook his head. He took a field dressing from his kit and wrapped the foot. "Keep it clean. You cannot march on a rotten foot."

Flynn nodded. He was grateful.

The forge was warm. Embers glowed in the hearth. Someone had built a fire recently. The blacksmith, probably, before he fled.

"Where are they now?" Flynn asked. "The people who live here?"

"Gone. Or dead."

"You think about it?"

"I try not to."

"But you do."

Miller sat down next to him. He was quiet for a long time. "I think about a baker in Tunis. He had a daughter. She used to bring me bread. I do not know what happened to them."

Flynn said nothing.

"After a while, you stop asking," Miller said.

Chapter 22: A German Dinner

They found the field kitchen by following the smell. Smoke rising from behind a hedge. The clatter of pots. A voice singing, tuneless and loud.

Miller crawled to the edge of the hedge and looked. Three German soldiers, sitting on crates, eating from metal bowls. A pot of stew hung over a fire.

"Hungry?" Miller asked.

"Starving."

"Cover me."

He went over the hedge low and fast. The first German did not even see him. Miller put the knife in his ribs and covered his mouth.

The second one stood up, reaching for his rifle. Miller threw the knife. It hit the man in the throat.

The third ran. Miller caught him in three strides and brought him down.

"Clear."

The men came through the hedge. They ate the stew standing up, using German mess tins. It was good. Pork and potatoes.

"Never ate so well in England," Price said.

Flynn ate until his stomach hurt. Then he ate more.

They left the bodies in the hedge. The pot was still warm when they moved on.

Chapter 23: The Prisoner

They took the German NCO during a night patrol. He was checking a field telephone line, alone, a tool roll in his hand. Price and Miller jumped him. He did not make a sound.

They brought him back to the barn where Ashford had set up the temporary command post. He was a big man, fortyish, with a hard face and calm eyes.

"Ask him what he knows," Ashford said.

Miller spoke in German. The man answered. His voice was steady, unhurried.

"He says the bridge has a company of engineers. Three machine guns. A mortar section. He says the SS arrived yesterday."

"Does he know about the secondary bridge?"

Miller translated. The NCO shook his head. He did not know.

"He says we should surrender. The SS does not take prisoners."

Ashford looked at the man. "Tell him we are not surrendering."

The NCO heard the tone before the translation. He smiled. It was not a kind smile.

"He says he knows," Miller said. "He says we are dead men anyway."

"Keep him with us. We might need him."

"He will slow us down."

"Then carry him."

Chapter 24: Interrogation

Miller took the prisoner aside, away from the others. He sat him down on a log and offered him a cigarette. The NCO took it.

Miller did not speak German well. But he understood it. And he understood men.

He asked the same questions in different ways. The NCO gave the same answers. Bridge. Engineers. SS.

But something did not add up. The man was too calm. Too specific. He answered questions he should not have known the answers to. He was an engineer NCO. He should not have known about the SS arrival until the day before.

Miller kept him talking. About his family. About the war. About how he had been in Poland, then France.

"Where were you in Poland?" Miller asked.

"Near Warsaw."

"What did you do there?"

"I was a military policeman."

Miller stopped. An MP would know troop movements. He would know the SS. He would know everything.

"You are not a line NCO," Miller said in English. "You are a staff man. A headquarters man."

The NCO's face flickered.

"You were sent out here as bait. To feed us information."

The NCO said nothing.

Miller stood up. He walked back to Ashford. "He is a plant. The Germans know we are here. They have probably known since the roadblock."

"Then everything he told us is wrong."

"Every word."

Ashford closed his eyes. He was very still.

"Then we have nothing."

"We have the map. And we have sixteen men."

Chapter 25: The Tenth Man

Ashford made the decision before dawn. Two men could not continue. Evans had a fever from his shoulder wound, shaking under his blankets. Dennis had shrapnel in his leg, the flesh around it black and swollen.

"They will die if we move them," the medic said.

"They will die if we leave them," Ashford said.

But he made the choice anyway. He left them in a farmhouse with water and ammunition and a pistol. Evans took the pistol. He did not say goodbye.

"I am sorry," Ashford said.

Evans nodded. "Just make sure it counts."

The company moved out. Fourteen men now.

Flynn looked back at the farmhouse until it disappeared behind a ridge.

He thought about what it meant, to be left behind. To wait, alone, for the sound of boots in the yard.

"What happens to them?" he asked Miller.

"Best case, the Germans find them and they become prisoners."

"Worst case?"

Miller did not answer. He did not have to.

They marched. The sun came up. The artillery continued, a constant mutter on the horizon.

Chapter 26: Night March

They marched through the night. The moon was a sliver, barely enough to see by. The men moved in single file, each man's hand on the pack of the man in front.

Flynn was third in line. He kept falling asleep as he walked. His legs moved on their own. His mind drifted.

He dreamed of Bethnal Green. The bakery. The smell of bread in the morning. His mother's voice, calling him down for breakfast.

He woke to find himself stopped. The column was still. He could hear voices up ahead.

"Road," Price whispered back. "Cattle."

A herd of cattle blocked the road, their eyes glowing in the dark. A farmer stood among them, an old man with a stick. He saw the soldiers and did not move.

Miller approached him. He spoke bad French, mixed with German. The farmer listened. He pointed east.

"The bridge?" Miller said.

The farmer nodded. He said something in rapid French that Miller could not follow.

"What did he say?" Ashford asked.

"He said the devil sleeps under the bridge. I do not know what that means."

"Ask him how many Germans."

The farmer held up three fingers. Then five. Then ten.

"He is not sure. Or he is scared."

The farmer turned and walked away, driving his cattle before him into the dark.

Chapter 27: The Wood

The wood was old, the trees thick and tangled. The path they followed was no more than a game trail. Branches caught at their uniforms. Roots tripped their feet.

The shot came from ahead. A single rifle crack. The man in front of Flynn, a private named Webb, stopped and looked down at his chest. There was a hole in it.

He fell.

"Sniper!" Miller shouted.

The men scattered, diving behind trees. Flynn pressed himself into the leaf litter. The wood was silent.

"Where?" Ashford hissed.

"I do not know."

Another shot. A tree trunk beside Flynn exploded. He felt the splinters hit his face.

"Left. High."

Miller raised his Sten and fired a burst into the canopy. Leaves fell. Nothing else.

"He is moving."

"Everyone stay down."

They lay in the leaves, not moving, not speaking. Minutes passed. The wood was still.

Then, from somewhere in the trees, a bird called. A moment later, another answered.

Miller stood up. "He is gone. They do not stay after a miss."

Webb was dead. They carried him to a clearing and covered him with branches.

"Say something," Price said.

Ashford looked at the body. He did not know what to say. He had known Webb for three weeks.

"He was a good soldier," Ashford said. It was not true, but it was what you said.

They moved on, leaving Webb in the wood.

Chapter 28: A Sound in the Fog

The fog came in from the river, thick and white. It swallowed the landscape. A man could disappear ten feet away.

Flynn kept his hand on the pack in front of him. He could barely see Sergeant Price's back.

The sounds started then. Voices. Whispers. They came from everywhere and nowhere. German voices, calling in the fog. Or were they?

"Are they there?" Flynn whispered.

"Keep moving."

A laugh. Close. Flynn spun, his rifle up. Nothing but white.

"It in your head," Price said. "The mind plays tricks in this."

But Flynn heard it again. A word. His name.

He stopped. "Who said that?"

"Nobody said anything."

"Flynn..."

It came from behind him. He turned. A shape emerged from the fog. A man in a long coat, mud-covered, his face pale.

"Flynn, it me."

It was Webb. The dead man from the wood. His chest was still wet.

Flynn fired.

The fog swallowed the shot. The shape was gone.

"Flynn!"

Miller was there, grabbing his arm. "What are you shooting at?"

"Webb. I saw him."

Miller looked into the fog. There was nothing there.

"You did not see anything. It the fog. It does things."

Flynn was shaking. Miller held his arm until he stopped.

Chapter 29: The Spandau

The machine gun opened up as they crossed the valley. Tracers ripped through the dark, stitching the air above their heads. The men hit the ground.

"Spandau!" someone shouted. "Spandau, two o'clock!"

Miller crawled to the top of the bank and looked. The gun was in a stone farmhouse, the muzzle flashing from a first-floor window. The field of fire covered the entire valley.

"Suppressing fire!" Ashford ordered.

The men opened up. Rifles, Stens, the Bren. The Spandau did not stop.

"We cannot cross with that thing active," Price said.

"Then we take it out."

Miller checked his grenades. He had two left. He took off his webbing, stripped down to his belt and shovel.

"Cover me."

He went down the bank and into the field. The tracers passed over him. He crawled, using his elbows, dragging himself through the mud.

The Spandau gunner saw him. The tracers dropped. They ripped the ground around him.

Miller rolled into a ditch. He was twenty yards from the farmhouse. He pulled the pin on a grenade, counted, and threw.

The grenade went through the window. The explosion shook the house. The Spandau stopped.

Miller got up and ran.

Chapter 30: Breaking Contact

The company withdrew into the trees. The Spandau was gone, but the noise had drawn a response. German infantry were advancing across the valley, spreading out in a skirmish line.

"They have got us bracketed," Price said. "Mortars next."

"Move. Fast."

Ashford led them deeper into the wood. The trees were thicker here, the undergrowth dense. The sounds of the German pursuit faded.

Miller brought up the rear. He walked backward, watching, his Sten ready. The enemy did not follow.

"They have stopped."

"They know we are bottled," Price said. "The river is on three sides of this wood. They will wait."

"Then we cannot stay."

Ashford called a halt. The men collapsed. Flynn sat against a tree, his chest heaving.

"Options," Ashford said.

Miller squatted, drew a rough map in the dirt. "River runs north-south. Bridge is south. If we go north, we loop around and approach from the far bank."

"That is another five miles."

"Better than walking into a killing ground."

"Agreed."

They moved north, following the river. The ground softened. Marshland. The water seeped into their boots.

Flynn's blistered foot screamed with every step. He did not complain. He had seen what happened to men who could not keep up.

Chapter 31: The Ridge

They reached the ridge at dawn. The sun rose behind them, painting the landscape in long shadows. Below, the river curved through the valley. And there, at the narrowest point, was the bridge.

Flynn crawled to the edge and looked. The bridge was bigger than he had imagined. Stone arches, a road wide enough for two trucks. On the near bank, a cluster of buildings. On the far bank, the ground rose into a bluff.

"They have pillboxes," Price said.

Three of them. Concrete bunkers, their gun slits facing the approach road. One on the near bank, two on the far. Sandbagged positions around them. Trenches.

"There is the Panther."

The tank was parked in a hollow, hull-down, only its turret visible. It was positioned to cover the bridge approach.

"God," Flynn whispered.

"Count them," Ashford said.

"Infantry. At least a company. Maybe more."

Miller lay beside them, studying the defences. His face was blank.

"The secondary bridge?"

Ashford pointed upstream. "Half a mile. Smaller. Wooden. No pillboxes."

"It is something."

They watched for a long time. The sun rose higher. Men moved in the German positions. A detail changed over at the machine gun. A truck delivered something to the bunker.

"We need a better look," Ashford said. "I am going down."

Chapter 32: What They Saw

Ashford crawled down the ridge alone, using the cover of a dry streambed. He reached a point two hundred yards from the bridge. He lay in the reeds and watched.

The timber bridge was a narrow structure, barely wide enough for a single vehicle. The road leading to it was unpaved, rutted. A single machine gun position guarded the approach. No sandbags. No bunker. A gun on a tripod with a canvas cover over it.

He counted the guards. Four. Maybe six.

He lay there for an hour. He watched the pattern of their movement. They were not alert. They smoked, talked, looked at the sky. They did not look at the riverbank.

He crawled back.

When he returned, he found the company eating cold rations. Miller had set up a watch rotation. The men looked tired but steady.

"The timber bridge is lightly guarded," Ashford said. "No armour. No bunkers. But it is open ground. We would have to cross two hundred yards of mud with no cover."

"Can we do it at night?" Miller asked.

"Maybe. But the moon is nearly full."

"They will expect us to hit the main bridge. They will not expect a raid on the secondary."

Ashford nodded. "We hit the timber bridge at midnight. We take it, hold it, and signal the commandos."

"And the main bridge?"

"We let it starve. Without the secondary, their supply route is compromised. They will have to pull back."

Miller looked at the sky. "It a plan."

"It is the only plan."

Chapter 33: Ashford's Decision

Ashford sat apart from the men, his back against a tree. He held his pistol in his lap. He had not fired it yet. Not in combat.

He thought about what his father would say. His father was a colonel in the Indian Army. He had served in Burma, in the desert. He had written Ashford letters full of advice that sounded good on paper and meant nothing here.

He thought about the men. Miller, who was worth ten of him. Flynn, who was just a boy. Price, who had seen too much and said too little.

They were following him. They believed in him. He did not know why.

Miller came over. He sat down without asking.

"You did well today, sir."

"I gave a speech and looked at a map."

"That is what officers do."

Ashford laughed. It was a short, bitter sound. "They did not train me for this, Sergeant."

"No one trains you for this. You learn it."

"How?"

"You decide. And you live with the decision."

Ashford was quiet. He thought about the two men he had left behind in the farmhouse. He thought about Henderson, drowning in the canal. He thought about Webb, shot in the chest.

"There is a word for what I am doing," Ashford said. "Sending men to die."

"No, sir. There is a word for what we are doing. Fighting."

Ashford looked at Miller. The man's face was unreadable.

"I hope you are right."

"I am usually not," Miller said. "But I am right about this."

Chapter 34: The Plan

Ashford gathered them at dusk. He drew the plan in the dirt with a stick.

"Price takes the left. Miller takes the right. I take the centre. We move at midnight. No shooting until we are across the bridge."

"And if they see us?" Price asked.

"Then we shoot."

"Where do I go?" Flynn asked.

"You are with me," Ashford said.

Flynn nodded. He did not know if that was good or bad.

"The German position is here. Four men, one machine gun. The gun has a limited traverse. If we come from the riverbank, we are outside its arc until we are fifty yards out."

"Can we do it silent?" Miller asked.

"We try. Knives and clubs. If it goes noisy, it goes noisy."

"Casualties?"

"We do not plan for casualties. We plan for success."

The men checked their weapons. They wrote letters, short ones, that they gave to Ashford. He put them in his map case without reading them.

Flynn did not write a letter. He did not know what to say.

Chapter 35: Waiting

The hours before the attack were the longest. Flynn lay in the grass, staring at the sky. The stars were coming out, one by one.

He thought about home. The bakery. The smell of the ovens. The way the floor was warm in the morning because the fire had been burning all night.

He thought about his father, who had died when Flynn was twelve. The funeral. The black car. The way his mother's hand had gripped his shoulder.

"I never told her I was coming here," he said.

Miller was beside him. "They know. They always know."

"She thinks I am a clerk."

"Better that way."

Flynn turned his head. "Do you have anyone? Back home?"

"Had a wife. She left. Took the boy."

"How long ago?"

"Two years. I was in North Africa. She wrote me a letter. I got it three months late."

"I am sorry."

"It is what it is."

The moon rose. It was bright, too bright. It cast shadows.

"Ten minutes," Price whispered.

Flynn stood. He checked his rifle. He checked his ammunition. He checked his knife, the same knife he had been flipping in the Nissen hut a lifetime ago.

"Stay close," Ashford said. "Stay low. Do not stop for anything."

Flynn nodded.

The minutes passed. The German guards smoked, their voices carrying across the water.

Chapter 36: The Bombardment

The naval shells began to fall at eleven fifty-eight. They were two minutes early.

The first salvo landed a mile away. The ground trembled. The second was closer. The third struck the far bank of the river, throwing up gouts of earth and water.

"Move!" Ashford shouted.

The company rose and ran. Flynn followed, his legs pumping. The mud sucked at his boots.

The German guards turned toward the shelling. They did not see the shadows coming up from the riverbank.

Miller hit the first man with his knife, low and hard. The man went down without a sound.

Price tackled the second. They rolled in the mud. Price's hands found the man's throat.

The third guard saw them. His mouth opened. Flynn threw himself forward, driving his knife into the man's chest. The man made a wet sound and crumpled.

The fourth guard ran for the machine gun.

Ashford shot him twice in the back.

"Clear!" Ashford shouted. "Take the bridge!"

They ran onto the wooden planks. Their boots thundered. The river rushed below.

Flynn looked up. A flare was rising, trailing white fire.

They had been seen.

Chapter 37: Crossing

The flare hung above them, turning the world white. Flynn saw his shadow stretch across the bridge. He saw the bullet impacts before he heard the shots.

"Run!" Miller screamed.

They ran. The bridge was a hundred feet long. A hundred feet of open, exposed wood.

The machine gun on the far bank opened up. Tracers skipped across the planks. Flynn's foot hit a hole. He fell.

He was on his hands and knees, the tracers passing inches above him. He could not move.

Miller grabbed his webbing and hauled him up. "Up! Up!"

They ran. Flynn could hear the bullets hitting the wood. He could smell splinters and smoke.

The far bank was close. Fifty feet. Thirty.

Ashford reached it first. He dove behind a pile of sandbags, firing his pistol.

Then Price. Then the others.

Flynn stumbled onto solid ground. His legs gave out. He crawled behind the sandbags and lay there, gasping.

"You hit?" Miller shouted.

"I... I do not know."

Miller checked him. No blood.

"You are fine. Get up."

Flynn forced himself to stand. They had taken the bridge. The timber bridge. Their bridge.

Chapter 38: The First Pillbox

The near-bank pillbox had not seen them. Its gun slits faced south, toward the approach road. The company was behind it.

"Grenade through the vent," Miller said. "I need two men."

"I will go," Flynn said.

"No. You stay. You are the CO's runner."

Price and Miller crawled around the pillbox. It was a squat concrete dome, built into the bank. The ventilation shaft was on the roof, a metal pipe six inches wide.

Price climbed onto the roof. He pulled the pin on a grenade, held it for two seconds, and dropped it down the pipe.

The explosion was muffled. The ground shuddered.

"One down," Miller said.

The men cheered. Ashford silenced them with a gesture.

"We are not finished. There are two more on the far side."

He pointed across the river. The other pillboxes were firing now, their guns sweeping the valley. German infantry were emerging from the trenches, forming up.

"They know we are here," Price said.

"Then we do not give them time to react."

Ashford looked at his watch. The commandos were due in three hours. They had to hold until then.

"Miller, take the left pillbox. I will take the right."

"Yes, sir."

Chapter 39: Flynn's Charge

The left pillbox was a hundred yards across open ground. Miller's section moved out at a crawl. Flynn followed, his rifle scraping the dirt.

The German infantry opened fire. The air filled with lead. Flynn pressed his face into the mud.

He could hear Miller shouting. He could hear the Bren, firing in short bursts.

He looked up. The pillbox was fifty yards away. The ground between was flat, exposed.

He thought about the men in the farmhouse. Evans and Dennis, waiting for the end.

He thought about Webb, shot in the chest.

He thought about Henderson, at the bottom of the canal.

He stood up.

He did not think about it. He did not plan it. His legs lifted him and he ran.

The Germans saw him. They fired at him. The bullets passed so close he felt the wind.

He ran. He did not shoot. He just ran.

He reached the pillbox. He pressed himself against the concrete, in the blind spot below the gun slit. He pulled a grenade from his belt.

He did not have a clear throw. The vent was on top, out of reach.

He heard the gun inside. The muzzle blast shook the wall.

He crawled to the door. It was steel, bolted from inside.

He hammered on it with his rifle butt. "Open up!"

The firing stopped.

They were listening.

He had no plan. He had nothing.

The door cracked open.

Flynn threw the grenade inside.

He ran.

Chapter 40: Miller's Gambit

The second pillbox fell to Miller's grenade. But the third was still firing, and the German infantry were pushing hard.

"Mortars!" Price shouted.

The first mortar round landed twenty yards behind them. The second was closer. The third landed among the section.

Flynn was thrown by the blast. His ears went dead. He could see men moving, their mouths open, but he could not hear them.

He got to his knees. He saw Miller, on the ground, his leg twisted beneath him. Blood was spreading across his thigh.

"Miller!"

Miller's face was white. He shouted something Flynn could not hear.

Ashford appeared. He knelt beside Miller, applied a tourniquet. Miller pushed him away.

"Leave it," Miller said. His voice sounded far away.

"You are hit."

"Leave it. Take the pillbox."

"I am not leaving you."

"I said leave it. That is an order."

Ashford stared at him. Miller's eyes were hard.

"I am not asking, sir."

Ashford stood. He looked at the pillbox. He looked at Miller.

"Flynn, with me."

Flynn followed. He looked back at Miller, lying in the dirt, his hands working a tourniquet on his own leg.

"Go," Miller said. "Go."

Chapter 41: The Flank

The Panther tank had been silent. Flynn had forgotten about it. They all had.

It came to life with a roar. The engine coughed, caught, and settled into a deep rumble. The turret began to turn.

"Tank!" someone screamed.

The Panther rolled forward, crushing the sandbags at the entrance to the bridge. It swung its turret, searching.

Ashford saw it. He was twenty yards from the third pillbox. He could not reach it with the tank covering the approach.

"Gammon bomb!" he shouted. "Who has a Gammon bomb?"

No one answered.

"I have one," Flynn said.

He pulled it from his pack. A canvas bag filled with plastic explosive and a detonator. The army's answer to armour.

"You need to get close," Ashford said. "Under the hull. The belly armour is thin."

Flynn nodded. His mouth was dry.

He went over the sandbags and into the open.

The tank was a hundred feet away. The turret was traversing, the gun lowering. He could see the muzzle, black and wide, swinging toward him.

He ran.

The tank's machine gun opened up. Rounds kicked up dirt at his feet. He zigzagged.

The turret stopped. The main gun fired.

The shell passed over his head and exploded behind him. The shock wave knocked him down.

He got up. He kept running.

He was ten feet from the tank. He pulled the pin. The fuse was four seconds.

He threw the Gammon bomb under the hull.

And he dove.

Chapter 42: Close Quarter

The explosion cracked the Panther's belly. Flame erupted from the hatches. The tank brewed up, black smoke pouring from every opening.

Flynn lay on the ground, his ears ringing. He could not feel his body. He tried to move. His arms worked. His legs worked.

He was alive.

The German infantry were falling back. The loss of the tank had broken their momentum. They ran for the cover of the buildings.

Ashford saw the opportunity. "Push! Push now!"

The company rose and advanced. They fired as they moved. The Germans did not stop to fight. They fled.

Ashford reached the third pillbox. The door was open. A German officer was emerging, his hands raised.

Ashford gestured with his pistol. "Get out. All of you."

The crew filed out. Four men. They looked young and scared.

Ashford pointed to the rear. "Go. Run. Do not stop."

They ran.

The bridge was quiet. The firing had stopped.

Ashford stood in the middle of the road. His men were all around him. They had done it.

"Check the bridge," he said. "Make sure it is clear."

He walked back to where Miller lay. The sergeant was grey now, his lips pale. The tourniquet had slowed the bleeding, but he had lost too much.

"Medic!" Ashford shouted.

"Busy, sir."

"Medic, now."

The medic came. He looked at Miller's leg and shook his head.

"We need to get him to a surgeon."

"There is no surgeon."

"Then we do what we can."

Chapter 43: The Last Pillbox

The medic worked on Miller while the company held the bridge. Flynn pulled security on the near bank, lying in a ditch, watching the road.

The sun was up now. Full daylight. He could see the damage they had done. The burning tank. The shattered pillboxes. The bodies.

He felt nothing. He was too tired to feel.

A movement on the road. A vehicle. He raised his rifle.

"Stand fast," Price said. "It is ours."

A jeep came around the bend. British markings. A major sat in the passenger seat, a map spread across his knees.

The major got out. He looked at the bridge. He looked at the burning tank. He looked at the men.

"Who is in charge here?"

Ashford stepped forward. "Lieutenant Ashford, sir. B Company, 6th Airborne."

"Major Corrigan, 1st Commando. We were told this bridge was not secured."

"It is now, sir."

The major walked the length of the bridge. He tested the planks with his boot. He looked at the river.

"Good work, Lieutenant."

"Thank you, sir."

"How many men did you lose?"

Ashford opened his mouth. He closed it. He did not know the number.

"Five," Price said. "Maybe six. We have not counted."

The major nodded. He did not ask for names.

Chapter 44: The Bridge

The commandos crossed at eight thirty. They came in columns, moving fast, their weapons ready. They took up positions on the far bank.

Ashford handed over the bridge. He gave the major a situation report. The major listened, took notes, and told Ashford to stand his men down.

They sat by the side of the road. Someone produced a flask. It went around.

Flynn had never tasted whisky before. It burned. He drank anyway.

"What happens now?" he asked.

"We wait," Price said. "More men will come. Then we push inland."

"And Miller?"

"He will be evacuated when the ambulance gets here."

"Will he make it?"

Price shrugged. "He is tough. Tougher than most."

Flynn looked at the bridge. The timber bridge. Their bridge. It was not the main objective. It was not the one they had been sent to take. But they had taken it.

He thought about the major. The way he had looked at them, measuring, assessing. They were not heroes to him. They were men who had done their job.

He thought about his mother. The bakery. The flat above it.

He thought about the white horse, cut into the hillside.

Ashford came over. He sat down next to Flynn.

"You did good tonight."

"I did what I had to."

"That is what good means."

They sat in silence. The commandos marched past. The sun climbed higher.

Chapter 45: Counterattack

The counterattack came at nine forty-five. German infantry, reinforced by a company from the SS, hit the bridge from the east.

The commandos took the brunt. They were professional, experienced. They set up their Vickers guns and waited.

The Germans came across the open ground. They were disciplined, well-led. They did not break when the machine guns opened up.

"Get your men into the line," the major said.

Ashford looked at his company. Eleven men now. Exhausted, wounded, out of ammunition.

"We are not done," he said.

They joined the line. Flynn found a rifle from a dead commando and took his position.

The fighting was close. Hand-to-hand in places. Flynn saw a commando bayonet a German, then fall to a bullet from another.

The line bent. It did not break.

Ashford ran along the trench, shouting, rallying. He carried a Bren gun, firing from the hip. His face was streaked with mud and blood.

"Hold the line! Hold!"

The Germans attacked three times. The third time, they reached the bridge. But the commandos pushed them back.

When it was over, the bridge was still theirs.

Chapter 46: Ashford Stands

Ashford reloaded the Bren with hands that did not shake. He was surprised at that. He had expected to be terrified. But there was no room for fear. Only the next target. Only the next burst.

The commandos had taken casualties. The major was down, hit in the shoulder. A sergeant was running the defence now.

"Lieutenant, they are forming up for another push."

"I see them."

The Germans were assembling in a farmyard, a quarter-mile east. Trucks. A half-track. At least a hundred men.

"We cannot hold against that," the sergeant said.

"Then we make them think we can."

Ashford called for every man who could carry a weapon. He spread them thin along the line, ten feet apart. He told them to fire three rounds, then shift position.

"Make noise," he said. "Make them think there are more of us."

He took the Bren and moved to the centre of the line. He set the gun on its bipod and waited.

The Germans attacked at ten-thirty. They came in a wave, shouting.

Ashford fired until the barrel glowed. He fired until the ammunition ran out. He drew his pistol and fired that too.

The line held.

When the Germans pulled back, there were forty of them dead in the field.

Ashford sat down. His hands began to shake.

Chapter 47: The Panzer

The sound of tracks came from the east. Heavy tracks. The men in the line looked at each other.

"Another tank," Price said.

It emerged from the smoke. A Panther, identical to the one Flynn had destroyed. It moved slowly, its turret traversing.

"We have no Gammon bombs left," Flynn said.

"Then we use the PIAT."

The PIAT was a spring-loaded anti-tank launcher. It was inaccurate and unreliable. It was the only weapon they had.

Price manned it. He loaded a bomb and braced the weapon against his shoulder.

The tank stopped. Its gun lowered.

Price fired.

The bomb flew wide, exploding in the field.

"Reload."

The tank fired. The shell hit the trench, ten feet from Price. He was thrown clear.

The PIAT was gone. Destroyed.

The tank advanced. It reached the bridge. It began to cross.

Flynn watched it. He had nothing left. No weapons. No strength.

He picked up a stone.

Miller grabbed his arm.

Miller was on his feet. His leg was wrapped, bloody, but he was standing. He held a Gammon bomb in his hand.

"I kept one back," he said.

He limped toward the tank.

"Miller, no!"

Miller did not stop. He limped onto the bridge. The tank saw him. The machine gun opened up.

Miller fell. He fell on his side. He threw the bomb.

The bomb rolled under the tracks.

The explosion lifted the tank. It settled, twisted, the tracks broken.

Miller lay still.

Chapter 48: Dawn

Flynn ran to Miller. He turned him over. Miller's eyes were open.

"Did it work?"

"The tank is dead. You stopped it."

A smile crossed Miller's face. It was the first time Flynn had seen him smile.

"Good."

"Stay with me. The medics are coming."

"Tell Ashford... the bridge is secure."

"You can tell him yourself."

But Miller's eyes were already fixed on something Flynn could not see.

Flynn held him until he stopped breathing.

He stayed there, kneeling in the road, until Price pulled him away.

The sun was rising. A red sun through the smoke.

The commandos came forward. They patched the line. They brought ammunition and water.

Ashford stood at the centre of the bridge. He did not speak. He looked at the river.

Flynn walked to the bridge parapet and sat down. He was too tired to cry.

He thought about the white horse. The running horse, cut into the chalk.

He thought about his mother's biscuits, wrapped in a tin.

He thought about Miller, sleeping in the Nissen hut, the stone in his hand.

Chapter 49: Relief

The relief column arrived at midday. A battalion of infantry, supported by engineers and armour. They crossed the bridge without stopping.

The commandos pulled back. The fighting moved east.

Ashford's company was gathered on the near bank. A medical officer came through, checking wounds, writing tags. Flynn had a cut on his forehead he had not noticed. The medic cleaned it and moved on.

A major approached Ashford. "You are relieved, Lieutenant. Your men will be taken to the beachhead for evacuation."

"Thank you, sir."

"How many?"

Ashford looked at his company. Eleven men. Ten, without Miller.

"Eleven," he said.

"Effective?"

"Seven."

The major nodded. He made a note.

"There will be medals."

"I do not think they want medals, sir."

The major looked at the men. Their torn uniforms, their blank faces.

"No," he said. "I suppose not."

The trucks came. Flynn climbed into the back of one. He sat beside Price. No one spoke.

The truck moved. The bridge disappeared behind a ridge.

Flynn closed his eyes.

Chapter 50: The Ledger

Four days later, Ashford sat in a tent on the beachhead and wrote the casualty report. He wrote the names from memory. His handwriting was careful, precise.

Millar, Thomas. Sergeant. Killed in action.

Evans, David. Private. Missing, presumed dead.

Dennis, Arthur. Private. Missing, presumed dead.

Henderson, James. Private. Drowned.

Webb, George. Private. Killed in action.

He wrote until the list was complete. He folded the paper and sealed it in an envelope.

He walked out of the tent. The beach was busy. Ships were unloading. Men were moving inland.

Flynn sat on a pile of sandbags, watching the sea. He looked older. His eyes had changed.

"Sergeant Miller's body was recovered," Ashford said. "He will be buried here."

Flynn nodded.

"They will send a letter to his next of kin."

"He did not have any. A wife who left. A son somewhere."

Ashford sat down beside him. "He had us."

Flynn looked at the sea. It was grey and endless.

"What do we do now?" he asked.

"We go back."

"And fight again?"

"Yes."

Flynn was quiet for a long moment. "He used to say that brave is doing it scared."

"He was right."

Flynn stood up. He looked toward the horizon. The sun was breaking through the clouds.

He thought about Miller. He thought about the knife, the stone, the quiet way he had of watching the world.

"Let us go," he said.

Ashford stood. They walked down the beach together, two men among thousands.

Behind them, beyond the ridge, the river ran under the timber bridge. The water was clear and cold and kept moving.
