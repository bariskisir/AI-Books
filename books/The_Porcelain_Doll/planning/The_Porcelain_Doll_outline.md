# The Porcelain Doll — Outline

## Premise

In post-WWII Kyoto (1948-1950), Miyako Tanaka, a young dollmaker trained in the traditional art of Japanese dollmaking (*ningyo-zaiku*), discovers that her porcelain dolls carry the souls of children who died in the war. Captain William Miller, an American officer grieving the loss of his seven-year-old daughter Sarah, seeks her out after hearing rumors of her unusual creations. Their meeting bridges two worlds of grief — the Japanese dollmaker haunted by the spirits of her nation's lost children, and the American soldier carrying his own private devastation through the ruins of a foreign land.

---

## Core Cast

- **Miyako Tanaka** — 28, dollmaker, lives in the Higashiyama district of Kyoto. Trained by her grandmother. Unmarried, lives alone with her craft.
- **Captain William Miller** — 42, U.S. Army civil affairs officer stationed in Kyoto. Widower. His daughter Sarah died of illness in 1945 while he was deployed.
- **Grandmother Hana** — deceased (d. 1945), Miyako's teacher in the art of dollmaking, believed in the spiritual nature of the craft.
- **Colonel Hayes** — Miller's commanding officer, pragmatic, disapproves of Miller's involvement with a Japanese civilian.
- **Mrs. Sato** — 65, Miyako's neighbor, a widow who lost her son in the war.
- **The Dolls** — Kiku (firebombing victim), Takeshi (young soldier), Aiko (illness), Sarah (Miller's daughter), and others.

---

## Part One: The Dollmaker (Kyoto, Autumn 1948)

1. **The Workshop** — Introduces Miyako's workshop in the Higashiyama district, with its shelves of porcelain faces staring from the shadows.

2. **Kiku** — The story of the first doll that "spoke" — a girl killed in the March 1945 firebombing of Tokyo, whose spirit Miyako unknowingly captured.

3. **Grandmother's Hands** — Flashback to 1935: Miyako as a child, watching her grandmother transform clay into faces, learning that every doll carries a fragment of its maker.

4. **The Kneading** — The physical craft of making doll bodies from wood and straw, the discipline of the hands, the meditation of repetition.

5. **The Clay** — Miyako travels to the hills outside Kyoto to dig her own clay, as her grandmother taught her. A ritual of connection to the earth.

6. **Kiln Fire** — Firing the porcelain in the kiln. The heat, the waiting, the uncertainty of what will emerge.

7. **The Neighbor** — Mrs. Sato brings a photograph of her dead son, Takeshi, and asks for a doll. Miyako cannot refuse.

8. **Takeshi** — Making Takeshi's doll. The spirit of the young soldier — killed in the Philippines at nineteen — settles into the porcelain.

9. **The Rumors** — Word spreads through the district. Miyako's shop begins to draw visitors who do not buy — they watch, they weep, they ask questions.

10. **The American** — Captain William Miller first appears in Gion, asking questions about the dollmaker. A merchant directs him to Higashiyama.

11. **The Meeting** — Miller arrives at the workshop. Language falters. But the dolls speak for themselves. He sees Sarah in one of the unfinished faces.

12. **Sarah** — Through a translator, Miller tells Miyako about his daughter. A fever in the winter of 1945. He was in Europe and could not return.

13. **The Photograph** — Miller shows Miyako a worn photograph of Sarah. A small girl with braids and a gap-toothed smile.

14. **The Refusal** — Miyako refuses the commission. She cannot make a doll for a foreigner. She cannot contain a spirit she does not know.

15. **The Persistence** — Miller returns. Day after day. He sits in the garden. He waits. He watches. The chrysanthemums begin to fade.

## Part Two: The Making (Kyoto, Winter 1948–49)

16. **The Agreement** — Miyako accepts. She will make the doll. But she tells Miller he must not expect to meet his daughter again.

17. **The Photograph Study** — Days spent studying Sarah's photograph. The curve of the jaw, the set of the eyes, the way childhood sits in the bones of the face.

18. **First Form** — The rough shaping of the head from clay. Miller watches, silent, from the doorway.

19. **The Garden Talks** — Brief conversations in the garden. Miller's Japanese improves. He learns about Miyako's grandmother, the war, the years of silence.

20. **The Colonel's Warning** — Colonel Hayes summons Miller to headquarters. An American officer fraternizing with a Japanese woman is not appropriate. Miller does not listen.

21. **The Gift of Silk** — Miller brings a piece of silk for the doll's kimono. Red with white flowers, Sarah's favorite color.

22. **The Bowl of Water** — The tradition of the water bowl — dipping the brush before painting the eyes. A spirit enters through the eyes.

23. **The Face** — The porcelain face emerges from the kiln. Flawless. It is Sarah's face, caught in the clay.

24. **Recognition** — Miller sees the fired face. His hands tremble. He cannot speak.

25. **The Hair** — Painting Sarah's hair, stroke by stroke. Dark brown, braided on each side. Miyako's brush feels guided by something beyond her hand.

26. **The Kimono** — Dressing the doll in the silk Miller brought. Tiny fingers, posed just so. The doll sits on the workbench, waiting.

27. **The Eyes** — The final morning. Miyako paints the eyes. A single breath, a single brushstroke for each. The doll is no longer clay and silk.

28. **Sarah** — A moment of stillness. The air in the workshop shifts. Something has arrived. Miyako knows. Miller, standing in the doorway, knows.

29. **The Delivery** — Miller takes the doll to his quarters. He places her on the windowsill. He talks to her through the night.

30. **The Snow** — The first snow of winter falls on Kyoto. Miller watches it with the doll in his hands.

## Part Three: The Reckoning (Kyoto, Spring–Summer 1949)

31. **The Growing Demand** — More families come. Mothers who lost children to the bombs, to the camps, to the sickness of the last war years.

32. **The Weight of Souls** — Miyako grows exhausted. Each doll takes a piece of her. She sleeps little. She eats less. The workshop is crowded with faces.

33. **The Visitor** — A Buddhist priest visits the workshop. He warns her that she is becoming a vessel for too many spirits. She must be careful.

34. **The Journalist** — A reporter from the Kyoto Shimbun writes an article. "The Dollmaker Who Finds the Dead." Miyako is embarrassed by the attention.

35. **The Exhibition** — Miller arranges for Miyako's dolls to be shown at a cultural exhibition sponsored by the occupation authority.

36. **The Opening** — The exhibition draws a crowd. Americans and Japanese alike stand before the dolls. Some weep. Some scoff. Some are silent.

37. **The Critique** — A Western art critic calls the work "morbid curiosity dressed in tradition." A Japanese critic defends it as authentic *ningyo*.

38. **The Doubt** — Miyako reads the critiques. She wonders if she is doing harm. She nearly destroys the kiln.

39. **Mrs. Sato's Comfort** — Mrs. Sato brings tea and tells Miyako that the dead need the living to remember them. The dolls are not ghosts — they are memory made solid.

40. **Miller's Choice** — Miller's tour ends. He must return to the United States or extend his service in Japan. He tells Colonel Hayes he is staying.

41. **The Summer Festival** — Gion Matsuri. Miller and Miyako walk through the lantern-lit streets together, not touching, but present.

42. **The Confession** — In the garden, under the summer stars, Miyako admits that she has come to care for him. She does not use the word love.

43. **The Impossible** — They both know the barriers. An American officer and a Japanese woman. The occupation. The judgment of both their peoples.

44. **The Letter from Home** — Miller receives a letter from his sister in Boston. She tells him to come home. To heal. To start again.

45. **The Refusal II** — Miller writes back. He will not return. His healing is here, in Kyoto, in the presence of a woman who holds the dead with grace.

## Part Four: The Porcelain Peace (Kyoto, Autumn 1949 – Spring 1950)

46. **The Master** — The Kyoto Traditional Arts Association recognizes Miyako as a master dollmaker. She is the youngest woman to receive the honor.

47. **The Question** — Miller asks Miyako to marry him. Not in grand words. In the garden, with the chrysanthemums in bloom again.

48. **The Consideration** — Miyako takes three days to answer. She visits her grandmother's grave. She tends the kiln. She makes a small doll — just a beginning, no face yet.

49. **The Acceptance** — She accepts. On the fourth day, she tells Miller. They will find a way. The world will not make it easy, but they will try.

50. **Porcelain and Light** — Epilogue. Spring 1950. Cherry blossoms fall on the garden. A new doll sits on the workbench — a child's face, but not a dead child's. A face of hope. Miyako and Miller watch the blossoms together. The dolls on the shelves keep their silent vigil.

## Themes

- Grief and its embodiment in art
- The meeting of Eastern and Western mourning traditions
- The ethics of representing the dead
- The porous boundary between craft and spirituality
- Post-war reconstruction of self and nation
- Love across cultural chasms

## Tone

Literary, spare, imagistic. Short sentences. Attention to physical detail — the texture of clay, the weight of silk, the quality of light through paper screens. Dialogue is minimal but precise. The narrative moves between the tangible (hands, clay, fire) and the intangible (spirit, memory, grief).
