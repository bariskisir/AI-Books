Chapter 1: The Proof Sheet

Ada Kern did not touch the proof sheet at first. She stood with her hands clasped behind her back and let the conservation room settle into its small noises: the hum of filtered air, the tick of a metal cart cooling near the wall, the soft drag of Livia Crane's sleeve against her clipboard. The paper lay under museum glass, held flat by clean weights at the corners. Its margins were broad. Its ink was black. Its one paragraph declared, in handsome nineteenth-century type, that Bell & Vale Pressworks had sold the worker trust in 1889 and released all claims to the yard.

"Beautiful, isn't it?" Livia asked.

Ada looked at the centered lines, the deliberate modesty of the signatures, the way the paper seemed to offer itself as an answer to a problem many living people wanted solved.

"Too beautiful," she said.

Livia's mouth tightened. She was the museum's head of collections, and the last year had put hollows under her eyes. A leaking roof, a suspended grant, a donor board that spoke warmly about heritage while asking which galleries could be rented for weddings. Ada knew the look. Institutions developed manners as they starved.

"You understand the urgency," Livia said.

"I understand the hearing is in three weeks."

"And the trust has been contested for twelve years."

"That does not make the paper older."

Livia set the clipboard down. "I didn't ask you here for comfort."

"Good."

The proof showed no printer's corrections, no smears from a composing room, no thumbed corner where a foreman might have lifted it from a galley. One paragraph, three signatures, a date. If genuine, it would let Hugo Senn's development company dissolve the worker trust and claim the old foundry yard for apartments, studios, and a glass-roofed market advertised with words like artisanal and legacy. If false, someone had forged history with type, and with enough confidence to place it in a museum case before the court had asked its first question.

Ada bent closer. She did not need magnification yet. The typeface was familiar from Bell & Vale specimen books: a narrow roman, economical, with short serifs and a lowercase e that usually looked as if it had been cut by a man who disliked ornament. On this sheet, the e in transfer carried a nick at the lower bowl. Not a blob of ink. Not paper fiber. A wound repeated where the same sort had been used again.

"How long has it been here?" Ada asked.

"Accessioned six weeks ago. Cataloged two days later. It came with other foundry material from a private donor."

"Name?"

Livia hesitated.

Ada straightened. "If you want private judgment, hire a decorator. If you want authentication, I need custody."

"Hugo Senn's foundation handled the transfer. The donor was an intermediary."

Of course. Ada looked back at the proof. The paper lay still, but the room had changed around it. Money, law, memory, and a line of type had entered the same small space.

"May I open the case?"

Livia unlocked it with the care of someone handling both evidence and hope. Ada put on nitrile gloves, lifted the glass, and waited for the old-paper smell. It came, but faintly, with another note beneath it: starch, perhaps, or pressed linen warmed by recent hands. She did not say so yet. Early impressions were useful only when disciplined.

She photographed the full sheet, then details: the signatures, the trimmed edges, the line spacing, the suspect e. She measured the margins twice. The declaration sat a little too perfectly in its field, as if emptiness around it had been composed for viewing rather than work.

"Can you tell already?" Livia asked.

"No."

"But you have an opinion."

"I have a suspicion."

"Is there a difference?"

"In court, all the difference there is."

Ada replaced the glass and sealed the case with Livia watching every movement. The proof sheet, relieved of scrutiny for the moment, resumed its pose of authority. Ada had seen documents do that. People often mistook stillness for truth.

At the door, she paused and looked once more at the lowercase e. A small damage mark could outlive the hand that made it. A small damage mark could also betray the hand that borrowed it too late.

Chapter 2: Bell and Vale

Theo Mott met her at the pressworks gate with keys on a brass ring and soot under one nail. He was fifty, perhaps, though the furnace had written older weather into his face. His gray hair was tied at the back with twine. He wore a canvas apron over a wool shirt, as if the building might ask him to work at any hour and he meant to be ready.

"Ada Kern," she said.

"I know. Livia said you look at letters until they confess."

"Letters are more reliable than people. They don't improve their stories."

He considered that, then opened the gate. "Watch your step. The floor remembers more than the trustees."

The yard behind Bell & Vale Pressworks had once taken wagons, coal, rag paper, barrels of ink, apprentices late to their benches, men with burns wrapped in flour paste, women from the sorting room carrying lunches in tins. Now weeds pressed through the brick joints. Rainwater shone in shallow depressions. A painted sign on the wall had faded until BELL & VALE looked less like a name than a weather stain.

Inside, the composing room smelled of oil, paper dust, cold iron, and rain that had found a patient route through the roof. Type cases stood in ranks beneath high windows. Some drawers wore brass labels, some paper, some nothing at all. A furnace slept behind iron rails in the back shop. The old proof sheet's words had supposedly come from this building, from one of these benches, from a worker leaning over a galley in a year when the trust was meant to protect wages from ownership.

"How much survives?" Ada asked.

"Enough to break your heart. Not enough to satisfy a grant committee."

"The face on the proof. Could it still exist here?"

Theo led her to a bank of cases and opened a drawer with both hands. The compartments were small and gray with metal. Letters lay face upward in some, face down in others, like sleep interrupted.

"Bell & Vale eight-point text roman," he said. "Not complete. Some sorts original, some later casts, some I don't trust."

Ada lifted a lowercase e with tweezers and turned it under her loupe. The body was worn, the face clean. No nick at the lower bowl.

"Who has access to this room?"

"Museum staff, foundation catalogers last winter, a private restorer before that, three trustees who don't know an em from a window latch, and me."

"That's a long list."

"It used to be longer. When the roof started failing, people came through with opinions and plastic sheeting."

They walked between stones, chases, forms tied with string, cabinets whose drawers stuck in the damp. Theo moved through the room without touching anything unnecessarily. Ada liked that. Men who loved old machinery often wanted to pet it. Theo behaved as if the objects were tired colleagues deserving privacy.

"Do you believe the proof?" she asked.

He stopped near the imposing stone. Outside, a truck passed on the street, and the windows trembled in their frames.

"No," he said.

"Why?"

"Because if Bell & Vale had sold the trust in 1889, my grandfather would have known. His father sorted type here during the strike. Family stories lie about bravery, drinking, and who paid for funerals. They don't usually invent a trust surviving when the landlord can disprove it with one page."

"Family memory is not evidence."

"No. That's why I kept the drawers."

He opened another case. This one held mixed sorts, damaged letters, substitute spacing, pieces that belonged to different decades but had been gathered under one convenient label. Ada saw how easily a clever hand could build a plausible alphabet from it. The room had not preserved the past intact. It had preserved tools, mistakes, repairs, substitutions, all the sediment from a working shop.

"Could someone print a lie from this?" she asked.

Theo looked down at the type and shut the drawer softly. "Enough of it to print a lie. Not enough of it to hide one forever."

That was an honest beginning.

Chapter 3: Lowercase E

Ada asked for the old specimen books. Theo brought three from a cabinet that smelled of cedar shavings and mouse paper, each volume wrapped in unbleached cloth and tied with cotton tape. He cleared a bench, wiped it with a clean rag, and set the books down as if they might bruise.

"No coffee near them," he said.

"I don't drink while working."

"No leaning."

"I don't lean on evidence."

"No turning pages from the corner."

"Mr. Mott."

"Theo."

"Theo, if I harm a specimen book, you may lock me in the furnace room until I reconsider my profession."

He untied the first tape. "Fair."

The Bell & Vale text face appeared in a specimen dated 1887, printed on rag paper gone the color of old cream. The lowercase e was narrow and modest, its crossbar slightly high, the aperture open enough to give the letter a listening posture. Ada traced nothing with her finger, only followed with her eye. Printers' specimens were salesmanship, but they were also records of intention. The first form of a typeface lived there, clean and uninjured, before work gave it scars.

She set the proof photograph beside the book. The disputed e in transfer showed the nick she had seen at the museum. The same mark appeared in three other words: release, herewith, and executed. Same lower bowl, same interruption in ink, same small bite out of the letter's smile.

"Could be a damaged sort," Theo said.

"Could be."

"Could be dirt."

"Repeated in the same shape?"

"No. Not dirt."

They moved to the second specimen, dated 1896. The e remained clean. Some spacing practice had changed. A new ligature supplement had been tipped into the back, but the main face still bore no wound. Theo fetched a magnifying lamp. Ada photographed the page and logged the date in her notebook.

The third specimen was slimmer, a 1902 circular produced after a period of repairs and recuts. Its cover had a burn hole near the lower right corner. Ada turned to the text roman sample and found the e almost at once. There it was: the same nick at the lower bowl, not in every printed e, but in those pulled from a damaged matrix or a damaged sort repeated through the line.

"The document says 1889," she said.

Theo leaned over the page. "Then the letter traveled backward."

He did not smile. Ada was grateful. Jokes could make evidence feel clever, and cleverness was a temptation in cases like this. Type damage had dates. Metal recorded accident with more discipline than people recorded deals. A nick could begin as a slip of punch, a crack in a matrix, a bad casting, a dropped sort striking stone. After that it traveled through impressions until someone noticed or until the damaged piece was melted. It belonged to a sequence.

"Do you have production records for the 1902 recut?"

"Some. The alloy book. Repair tickets. Matrix numbers if the rats didn't find them useful."

"We'll need them."

"You think this is enough?"

"No. One defect is not a case. It's an opening."

Ada photographed every e in the proof sheet from Livia's high-resolution files, then every comparable e in the specimens. She built a quick grid on Theo's worktable, not with software yet, just printed enlargements under small weights: clean 1887, clean 1896, nicked 1902, disputed proof. The sequence stared back with plain patience.

Theo watched her arrange the photographs. "I wanted it to be false."

"Wanting does not disqualify you. Hiding it would."

"I don't want to be useful only because I'm angry."

"Then be useful because you know the room."

He nodded once, and something in the silence changed. Before, the pressworks had felt abandoned, a place being asked to defend itself after years of neglect. Now it felt awake in patches. A specimen book, a damaged letter, a man who knew which drawers stuck in damp weather. Evidence did not arrive as a thunderclap. It gathered, piece by piece, until the air around it became harder to ignore.

Chapter 4: The Trust Clause

Neris Holt explained the trust clause in a law office with no books visible. The shelves behind her desk held ceramic bowls, framed degrees, and three identical boxes labeled ACTIVE, CLOSED, and BLEEDING. She was a probate attorney with a courtroom voice and the posture of someone who had slept badly for practical reasons. Rain struck the windows behind her in quick silver lines.

"The Bell & Vale yard has been held in trust since 1874," Neris said. "For workers, retired workers, widows, widowers, and descendants with demonstrable ties to the company. The language is unfashionable, paternal, full of holes, and still binding."

Ada sat across from her with a copy of the trust deed. The paper was modern. The trouble was old.

"Unless a valid sale document proves earlier transfer," Ada said.

"Unless a valid sale document proves earlier transfer before the protective clause vested in the way the current board argues it did."

"That's a lawyer's sentence."

"It has to survive other lawyers."

Neris slid a photocopy of the proof sheet across the desk. "The proof is not the sale. Hugo's team knows that. They argue it verifies a sale circular was set, approved, and ready for distribution in 1889. If the circular existed, the missing sale instrument becomes more credible. If the workers received notice, the trust board's continuity argument weakens."

"A proof can verify intent."

"Exactly."

"Or it can forge intent."

"Also exactly."

The case had the shape Ada disliked most: not a single lie, but a lever. No one needed the proof to bear the whole weight of ownership. It only had to tip a judge toward believing that other lost documents once existed. A proof sheet could make absence seem respectable. It could dress speculation in ink.

Neris opened the ACTIVE box and removed a folder with colored tabs. "Hugo Senn's company has an option agreement with several heirs. If the court accepts the sale theory, he can consolidate title within weeks. Studios, offices, boutique manufacturing, a cafe. The renderings leave the furnace in place."

"Behind glass."

"You saw them."

"I have seen that kind of mercy before."

Neris's mouth moved, not quite a smile. "The trust board is broke. Some heirs want the sale. Some hate Hugo but want money. The museum wants a major acquisition. The city wants tax revenue. The building wants a roof."

"Buildings don't want."

"This one does. Loudly. Through invoices."

Ada looked again at the photocopy. A paragraph, three signatures, all the clean force of a story that solved too many problems at once.

"Who hired me?" she asked.

"The trust board, officially. The museum agreed. Hugo's counsel did not object, which I find more worrying than generous."

"Why?"

"Because he believes the document will stand."

"Or because he believes speed will do what truth cannot."

Neris rubbed her eyes with thumb and forefinger. Her nails were short. Ink stained one cuticle, a small human flaw in the polished room.

"I need a preliminary expert report in ten days," she said. "I need enough to prevent an uncontested authenticity finding. I don't need poetry about old type. I don't need outrage. I need sequence, custody, material facts, and language a judge can repeat without embarrassment."

"Then don't ask me to authenticate quickly."

Neris looked at her directly. "I want you to authenticate accurately."

Ada listened for the practiced answer under the fatigue. She did not hear it. Neris sounded tired enough to mean what she said, and tired enough to know that accuracy might cost her own clients money. Ada wrote that down too, not as evidence but as orientation.

"I have a later damaged e," Ada said. "Not conclusive. I need access to all type cases, specimen books, matrices, repair records, restoration invoices, paper analysis, ink sampling, accession files, and the people who touched the proof."

"That will annoy everyone."

"Good. Annoyance makes custody visible."

Neris pulled a legal pad toward her. "Names."

Ada listed them. Livia Crane. Theo Mott. Hugo Senn. The private restorer. The donor intermediary. Paper expert. Ink chemist. Union archive if it survived. Anyone who had reason to remember wages instead of renderings.

When Ada left the office, the rain had stopped. The street smelled of wet stone and hot brakes. Across from the law building, a construction hoarding showed a future courtyard full of clean tables and smiling strangers. In one corner of the image, the old furnace stood under pendant lamps, harmless as sculpture.

Ada folded the photocopy into her case. The trust clause had not been written to be beautiful. It had been written to hold a line. A century and a half later, the line was still there, thin, legal, and under pressure.

Chapter 5: Museum Light

The museum conservation room had soft light and hard pressure. Its walls were painted a gray chosen to disappear behind objects. Drawers closed with expensive quiet. A humidifier worked in the corner, and every surface seemed to have been designed to make dust feel like a moral failure.

Livia closed the door before she spoke. "The donor wants an announcement before the hearing."

Ada was setting out her tools: loupe, measuring scale, raking light, cotton tape, evidence tags, a notebook whose pages had no perforations. She did not look up.

"The donor being Hugo?"

"One of his foundations."

"Foundations don't want. People want through them."

Livia took that without answering. Today she wore a black dress and flat shoes, the uniform of a curator expecting either donors or disaster. "The board believes public interest will help secure emergency funding."

"Public interest in an authenticated document."

"In a significant document."

"Those are not the same."

The proof lay on a padded table now, released from its case. Ada placed it beneath angled light. The fibers rose and fell in a shallow landscape. Near the edges, the surface showed abrasion: a controlled thinning of paper skin, small scuffed patches where dirt had settled too conveniently. Real age was uneven. It came from use, storage, fingers, damp, neglect, a folded corner repaired badly by someone who needed the page for work. This sheet looked old in the places a viewer would inspect first. The center, where the text sat, had a steadier cleanliness.

Livia watched her face as if the verdict might appear there before the words. "Is it bad?"

"It is theatrical."

"That is not a category."

"It becomes one when evidence performs too hard."

Ada turned off the overheads and used ultraviolet light. The sheet answered in dull patches and pale halos. Adhesive near one edge. A repair at the lower margin. Something erased or trimmed away where a printer's note might once have lived. The ink itself did not fluoresce in any immediately damning way, but its surface caught light with a confidence she disliked.

"Who conserved it?" Ada asked.

"A private restorer arranged before formal accession."

"Name."

"Marlo Venn."

"Records?"

"Requested."

"Received?"

Livia's silence was answer enough.

Ada made a note and took more photographs. "Was it framed?"

"Not yet."

"Was it displayed privately?"

"At a donor dinner."

Ada looked up then. "Before authentication?"

"It was introduced as a recent discovery."

"To whom?"

"Board members. Hugo's heritage consultants. Two city council staffers. A journalist from the property section, though we asked that nothing be printed."

The room felt suddenly over-warm. Ada had seen this before: an object invited into public life too early. Once people had admired it, pledged money around it, imagined brochures and lawsuits and saved institutions, the object stopped being paper. It became embarrassment waiting to happen.

"Livia," Ada said, "I need every photograph from before the dinner, during the dinner, after the dinner, and during accession. Original files. Not exported images."

"That will be difficult."

"So is explaining why evidence was handled like a centerpiece."

Livia looked down at the proof. "You think I'm careless."

"I think you are underfunded and overflattered. Carelessness may have entered by that door."

The words struck. Ada let them. Comfort could come later from people paid to provide it.

They worked for two hours. Ada measured the sheet against standard paper sizes from the period, examined the cut edge under magnification, and mapped abrasions with a transparent overlay. She asked Livia to read the accession description aloud. The curator's voice faltered at "excellent original condition."

"Change nothing yet," Ada said. "No corrected labels, no quiet edits."

"I know."

"Do you?"

Livia removed her glasses and pressed the bridge of her nose. "I know now."

Near noon, a courier knocked and handed Livia an envelope. Inside was a glossy invitation proof for the museum's proposed announcement: THE BELL & VALE TRUST DISCOVERY. Below the title, the proof sheet glowed in a photograph warmer than the room had ever allowed it to be.

Ada glanced at it once. "Cancel that."

"I may not have the authority."

"Then borrow some."

Livia stood with the invitation in both hands. For a moment she looked not like a curator but like a woman watching a bridge fail in slow motion, knowing she had waved traffic onto it.

Ada returned to the paper. The fibers held still under the lamp. They did not care who needed them to be old. That, at least, was clean.

Chapter 6: Punch Marks

Theo unlocked the punch cabinet with something like reverence and something like distrust, as if the old lock might resent being asked for testimony. The cabinet stood in the back room beyond the casting shop, where the air held a mineral chill. Its drawers were shallow and lined with baize gone nearly black. Steel punches lay in labeled trays, each one a small bar of metal with a letter cut in relief at the end: the first wound from which matrices had been struck, the ancestor of every printed character the foundry had sold.

"These should have been in a bank vault," Ada said.

"They should have been in use," Theo answered. "But I take your point."

He lifted the tray marked B&V TEXT ROMAN LC. Lowercase. The labels had been written by more than one hand over many decades. Some were copperplate. Some were ballpoint. One was a strip of masking tape that had browned and curled at the edge.

Ada set the lowercase e punch under the microscope. The lamp made the steel look wet. She adjusted focus until the tiny counter, bowl, and crossbar came clear. No nick. No wound at the lower bowl. The original design was clean.

"If this made the disputed e," she said, "the damage happened later."

"In the matrix or in a cast sort."

"Or in a recut punch."

"Not this one."

They examined the punch from three angles. Ada photographed the face, shoulder, and any tool marks she could find. The steel had scratches, but none in the place that mattered. A punch could wear, but slowly; it could chip, but the chip would mark every matrix struck after the accident. The proof's wound did not belong to this first generation.

"Where are the matrices?" Ada asked.

Theo's jaw moved once. "Some here. Some missing. Some sold during the bad years."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough that men traded alphabets for boiler repairs."

He said it without drama. That made it worse. Ada followed him to a second cabinet whose drawers stuck and released with a dry complaint. Matrices sat in small envelopes or loose in wooden channels. Some had numbers. Some had none. Several compartments were empty but labeled, the absence almost more articulate than the metal.

"When were they sold?"

"Mostly the seventies. Some in the nineties. There was a collector in Boston who bought anything with a founder's mark. A college took display pieces. A restaurant bought cases for decoration and dumped the contents in a barrel."

"And records?"

"A ledger. Not complete. People who are ashamed of selling things often keep poor records."

Ada logged every tray, every empty slot, every label that did not match its contents. Absence had to be counted, not merely lamented. In court, a missing matrix could not sigh for itself. Someone had to give its absence dimensions.

At the end of the row, Theo paused. "This is where people get sentimental."

"About lost craft?"

"About lost control. They imagine there was a day when everything was in its right drawer and every man was honest because the work was difficult."

"There was no such day."

"No."

He handed her a small envelope. Inside lay a different lowercase e matrix, later than the punch, with a faint roughness near the lower bowl. Not the disputed wound, but a cousin to it.

"Damage travels through systems," Theo said. "A dropped matrix. A hurried casting. A foreman who says good enough because orders are late. Then fifty years later someone calls it character."

Ada smiled despite herself. "Do you dislike character?"

"I dislike laziness wearing a waistcoat."

They worked until dust marked the cuffs of Ada's jacket. She did not mind. The punch cabinet had given them a negative fact, which was often the kind that held: the original lowercase e did not carry the mark. The proof's damaged letter belonged to a later accident, and later accidents could be dated if the records had not all been turned into restaurant decor.

Before leaving, Ada asked Theo to lock the cabinet and sign a custody sheet for the keys.

"You don't trust me?"

"I trust signatures more when they are fresh."

He signed. His name looked cramped, as if he had spent his life giving more precision to other people's letters than to his own.

Chapter 7: The Alloy Book

The alloy book lived beside the furnace in a tin box, bound in black cloth and burned at one corner. Theo carried it to the workbench as if carrying a sleeping thing whose dreams might be useful. The cover had no title. Inside, the pages were ruled in red and blue, with dates, weights, temperatures, complaints about suppliers, and the occasional note from a foreman whose patience had failed him in permanent ink.

"Bell & Vale used a softer mixture through most of the eighteen-eighties," Theo said. "Lead, tin, antimony. Ratios varied because men vary and invoices lie. But the shift to harder metal comes after 1897."

"Why?"

"Sharper demand. New presses. Customers wanted cleaner edges and longer runs. More antimony. More brittleness too, if you didn't know what you were doing."

Ada had brought enlargements of the disputed proof and confirmed Bell & Vale circulars from the 1880s. She laid them beside the ledger. The old letters in the genuine pulls had a slight softness at the shoulders, especially where pressure had been heavy. Ink gathered kindly there. On the disputed proof, the shoulders bit cleanly into the paper. The edges had a brightness she associated with later alloy or modern pressure, not a working proof from 1889.

"Can a printer make soft type look sharp?" she asked.

"By under-inking and light pressure, maybe. But this sheet has deep impression in places and clean edges. That's the wrong marriage."

"Good phrase. I won't use it."

"Please don't."

Theo turned pages with careful fingertips. Ada noted dates: 1888 wage dispute, 1889 night casting, 1894 paper complaint, 1897 revised alloy, 1901 experimental recipe. Then the rhythm broke. One page had been cut from the ledger. Not torn. Cut with a blade close to the gutter. A stub remained, pale where the paper had been protected from soot and handling.

Ada leaned in. "What was there?"

Theo looked at the preceding and following pages. "Experimental recipe. Around 1901. The index says hard body text, revised antimony."

"After the proof's date."

"Very."

"Who cut it out?"

"Someone with a knife."

"Theo."

He exhaled. "I don't know. I saw the stub after the foundation cataloging. I can't swear it wasn't missing before."

Ada wrote that exactly. Can't swear mattered. People damaged cases by converting suspicion into certainty too soon.

They carried the book to the furnace room. Theo showed her the old casting pots, the ingot shelf, the hand mold wrapped in cloth. Above the furnace, a brick arch had blackened from decades of heat. The room had the severe beauty of use: hooks, scales, ladles, a cracked enamel cup full of nails. No decorative object in Hugo's rendering had carried that fatigue.

"Would the missing recipe help a forger?" Ada asked.

"Only if the forger knew enough to want it. Most would use whatever old type they found."

"This proof seems to know later metal."

"Or it was printed from later type and pretends not to know."

Ada liked that better. Objects did not know; makers did.

They tested hardness on loose sorts already marked as expendable, comparing confirmed early casts to later recuts. The difference was small but measurable. Theo showed how the early sorts rounded under repeated pressure, how later ones kept a cleaner shoulder until they cracked. Ada photographed the test marks and logged the method. She would need an independent metallurgical analysis eventually. For now, the alloy book gave chronology.

At the ledger again, she studied the cut stub. The missing page mattered less as a romance of concealment than as a practical fact. Someone had removed information about a later recipe. The disputed proof bore characteristics consistent with later type. Hugo's foundation had funded cataloging before the proof appeared. None of that proved a hand, but it made the room narrower.

"I hate missing pages," Theo said.

"Everyone hates them. That is why they work."

"You think someone took it to hide the recipe?"

"Maybe. Or to use it. Or because collectors are fools. We keep all three possibilities alive until one dies."

Theo closed the alloy book and tied it with cotton tape. "You make uncertainty sound like labor."

"It is labor."

Outside the furnace room, rain began again, tapping through a leak into a bucket. The sound was irregular, not enough to be music, too persistent to ignore. Ada packed the photographs and thought of a forger studying this same room, not lovingly, but hungrily, taking from it the exact parts needed to build a convincing past.

Chapter 8: Watermark Noon

Owen Pike preferred paper to people and said so without apology within the first five minutes. He worked in a narrow studio above a frame shop, where every window was shaded and every visitor had to pass a sign that read DO NOT BRING DRINKS, PLANTS, OR PERFUME BEYOND THIS POINT. He was a small man with a white beard clipped close to his jaw and spectacles that magnified his eyes into permanent surprise.

"Paper forgives idiots less often than people think," he said, clearing space on the light table. "People say paper survives. Mostly it endures mistreatment while planning revenge."

Ada set the proof sheet in its temporary folder on the table. Livia had signed it out under court-noticed chain of custody after several tense emails. Owen checked the seals, signed the log, and placed the sheet over transmitted light with a care that made even Theo's handling look casual.

The watermark appeared slowly: a crown facing upward when the sheet was oriented with the text readable.

"Noon-facing crown," Owen said.

"Is that good?"

"For 1889, no."

He pulled a binder from a shelf labeled MILL MARKS: DOMESTIC 1870-1910. The relevant paper mill had used a different crown until 1894, then adopted the noon-facing version after a change in ownership and machinery. Owen showed Ada dated samples. The differences were slight: the angle of the points, the shape of the base, the direction of the shadow line. Enough.

"Could old stock have been used later?" Ada asked.

"Of course."

"Could new stock bear an old mark?"

"Sometimes. Mills reused screens. Papermakers were not priests."

"But this mark begins after the document's date."

"This version, yes."

The watermark did not scream. It whispered from inside the sheet. Ada liked evidence that did not know it was evidence. A forger might distress edges, choose brownish paper, imitate signatures, and still forget that paper carried its own quiet calendar.

Owen took fiber samples from an existing loss near the trimmed edge, so small that Ada had to trust his tweezers more than her eyes. Under the microscope, the sample showed linen and cotton, as expected, but also a small amount of wood pulp that did not make the sheet impossible for the period. Only awkward for the claimed source and use.

"Awkward is not false," Owen said before Ada could ask.

"I wasn't going to."

"Your eyebrows were."

He prepared slides, photographed fibers, and dictated notes into a recorder in a dry, precise voice. "The sheet is compatible with late nineteenth-century manufacture but inconsistent with the claimed 1889 date if provenance depends on the identified mill mark. Fiber content does not exclude the period. Surface treatment requires separate analysis."

"You testify often?"

"Enough to know adjectives are traps."

Ada smiled. "Ink chemists say the same."

"Ink chemists are dramatic people pretending to be tables."

They examined the sheet under raking light. Owen frowned at the border. "This edge has been trimmed."

"Recently?"

"I can say the cut edge lacks embedded grime consistent with the rest of the sheet. Recent is a courtroom word. I would rather say materially later than the paper's primary aging."

"That is a courtroom sentence."

"Yes, but a useful one."

At noon exactly, light from a gap in the shade fell across the table and brightened the watermark crown. Owen noticed and adjusted the shade with annoyance. "Romantic timing. Ignore it."

Ada did. She asked about bathing, pressing, flattening. Owen pointed out tide lines near one corner, faint deposits where liquid had dried, a subtle stiffness where the paper had been re-sized or pressed after wetting. These observations would need tests. He would not give them more weight than they had earned.

"Forgery?" Ada asked when he finished the first pass.

Owen removed his spectacles and cleaned them with a cloth that looked older than several institutions. "Paper is a bad witness alone. It travels. It waits in drawers. It can be used years after manufacture. It can be used innocently by fools and deliberately by criminals. But this paper is looking at the door."

Ada wrote that down, then crossed out the metaphor and kept the substance. Later watermark. Mixed fibers. Trimmed edge. Possible bath. Careful limits.

When she left, Owen called after her, "Do not let a lawyer make the watermark carry the whole case."

"I won't."

"Good. Paper hates being overburdened."

On the stairs, Ada thought of Neris's trust clause, Theo's damaged e, the cut page in the alloy book. None of them could stand alone without wobbling. Together they had begun to make a structure. That was how truth usually arrived: not as a perfect witness, but as several imperfect ones refusing to fall in the same direction.

Chapter 9: A Clean Margin

The proof's margin had been trimmed recently. Ada saw it first under Owen's raking light and confirmed it two days later in the museum lab: blade pressure along the lower edge, faint compression of fibers, no century of dirt settling into the exposed cut. The other edges carried the uneven darkness of storage and handling. The lower edge looked too clean, like a sentence with its last word removed.

Livia stood beside the table with both hands wrapped around a paper cup she had not drunk from. "Printer's notes?"

"Maybe."

"Or?"

"A modern sheet edge. A watermark clue. A dealer's mark. Anything inconvenient enough to trim away."

The declaration text sat too centered after the cut. Ada measured from baseline to edge, edge to paragraph block, paragraph to top. The proportions were elegant in the way display objects are elegant. Old proof sheets were work objects. They were pulled to check type, not to flatter a wall. They carried corrections, thumb marks, ink smears, pinholes, pencil slashes, sometimes a foreman's insult in the margin. This one carried emptiness.

"Do you have the accession photographs?" Ada asked.

"Yes."

"Original files."

Livia's throat moved. "I can retrieve them."

"Now."

They crossed the hall to Livia's office, where archival boxes competed with donor packets and a dying fern. Livia logged into the museum server and opened a folder named BVP_ACQ_SENN_PRELIM. Ada noticed the naming convention but said nothing. The first images had been taken on a phone, then transferred. The next set came from the museum camera. The final set had been edited for the proposed announcement.

"Open the earliest one."

The proof appeared on a walnut dining table under warm light. A wineglass stem showed at the edge of the frame. Ada leaned close to the screen. Near the lower margin, below the final signature line, a faint pencil mark crossed the paper at an angle. Not text, perhaps. A registration note? A stock number? It was gone in the museum photographs.

"There," Ada said.

Livia enlarged the image. Pixels broke, but the mark remained visible enough to matter.

"Could be shadow."

"It's graphite or dirt. Either way, it existed before the trim."

Livia sat back. "I should have noticed."

"Yes."

The single word was not kindness, but it was clean. Ada had no patience for consoling professionals out of responsibility. She had less patience for shaming them so badly they became useless.

"Who trimmed it?" she asked.

Livia looked at the file list, then away. "The private restorer."

"Marlo Venn."

"Yes. The donor foundation said the edge was unstable."

"Did the museum authorize trimming?"

"We authorized stabilization."

"That is not the same."

"I know."

Ada asked for all correspondence about the treatment. Livia printed the emails rather than forwarding them, a reflex toward control that Ada recognized and disliked. Hugo's foundation coordinator had written in the language of helpful urgency: ensuring presentation standards, preventing further loss, preparing object for responsible review. Marlo Venn had described "minor edge regularization." The phrase made Ada's pen press harder into her notebook.

"Do you have the offcut?"

Livia looked at her as if Ada had asked for a living witness.

"The trimmed strip," Ada said. "If this was conservation, the removed material should have been retained."

"I don't know."

"Find out."

They returned to the lab. Ada placed a transparent grid over the proof and marked the likely original edge based on the earliest photograph. The pencil mark would have fallen just below the current margin. Removed. The object had not merely aged; it had been edited.

Livia stood quietly for a long time. "The board wanted a clean object."

"They may have gotten one."

"You make that sound like an accusation."

"Cleanliness is often where evidence goes to die."

Outside the lab, a group of schoolteachers toured the museum's education wing. Their voices came faintly through the wall, cheerful and practical. Ada imagined the proof in a case, its clean margin lit from above, while visitors read a label about rediscovered heritage. A trimmed edge would not catch their eye. That had been the point.

Before leaving, she copied the earliest image to the evidence drive and logged its hash. Livia watched the progress bar as if it were a medical test.

"If this becomes public," Livia said, "the museum will look compromised."

"The museum is compromised. Public is a separate question."

Livia nodded once. "Then we do the separate question later."

It was the first useful thing she had said all morning.

Chapter 10: Hugo's Model

Hugo Senn presented the conversion plan at a polished table in a sales suite three blocks from the pressworks. The suite had been designed to look temporary and expensive: pale wood, black metal chairs, a model beneath acrylic, coffee in cups too small to satisfy anyone. On the wall, a rendering showed the Bell & Vale courtyard washed in afternoon light, its cracked brick cleaned to a fashionable softness, its weeds replaced by planters full of disciplined grasses.

Neris had asked Ada to attend as an observer. Theo came because the trust board insisted someone from the building should hear what was being promised about it. He wore his cleanest jacket and looked as if he would rather be under a leaking roof.

Hugo entered with two associates and no papers in his hands. He was in his early forties, silver at the temples by choice or good fortune, with the informal confidence of a man whose lawyers made formality unnecessary. He shook hands with everyone, remembered names, and mispronounced Theo's only once.

"We value continuity," Hugo said after the lights dimmed. "Bell & Vale is not merely a property. It is an industrial memory site."

Ada had heard developers use memory as trim. She opened her notebook.

The model glowed when an associate touched a switch. Tiny people appeared in the courtyard. Tiny tables. Tiny trees. The old casting shop roof had been replaced with glass. The composing room had become a lounge for residents and visitors. In the corner, under a miniature spotlight, the furnace remained.

"The furnace will be preserved behind glass," Hugo said.

"Will type still be cast?" Ada asked.

He turned toward her with a polite pause, as though the question had wandered in from a less profitable meeting. "That is not economically feasible."

Theo folded his hands until the knuckles paled. Ada saw it and asked nothing more for the moment.

Hugo spoke of heritage retention, adaptive reuse, artisan retail, public memory. A coffee bar would occupy part of the old packing room. Local makers could rent stalls. A small interpretive display would honor the workers' trust, "once the legal questions are resolved." He said resolved as if resolution and removal were cousins.

"The trust has outlived its purpose," he continued. "That is not a criticism. Structures age. Communities change. The question is whether we allow a dead instrument to keep a dead building from living again."

Theo made a sound too small to be an objection and too honest to be a cough.

"Mr. Mott?" Hugo said.

"The building isn't dead."

"No one doubts your devotion."

"That wasn't my point."

Neris intervened before Theo could answer with something more expensive. "Purpose is precisely the issue before the court. The trust board does not concede that the instrument is dead."

"Of course," Hugo said. "The court will decide, with evidence."

He gestured toward a mounted reproduction of the proof sheet at the end of the room. It had already been framed, or at least mocked up to look framed, and placed beneath a caption: BELL & VALE SALE PROOF, 1889. Ada stared at the caption long enough for one of Hugo's associates to shift in her chair.

"That label is premature," Ada said.

Hugo smiled. "It says proof, not sale."

"It says 1889."

"That is the date on the document."

"A document can lie."

"Which is why we welcome independent review."

"Do you?"

The room cooled. Hugo did not lose his smile; he refined it.

"Ms. Kern, I respect expertise. My foundation paid to stabilize materials that might otherwise have been lost. I have no interest in false history."

"False history is rarely interested in announcing itself."

Neris closed her folder. "We should keep this meeting productive."

Productive meant ending it before someone said what everyone could use later. Hugo walked them through tax credits and preservation easements. He showed a projected revenue stream in which the trust beneficiaries received payments larger than any they had seen in decades. He did not show where type cases would go, or who would own the furnace once it became scenery, or how public memory survived behind cafe hours and private security.

Afterward, in the elevator, Theo stared at the floor numbers.

"He made it look clean," he said.

"That is his job."

"What's yours?"

"To find out what the paper can bear."

Outside, traffic moved through wet streets. Across the road, the real Bell & Vale roofline showed above a row of shops, uneven and blackened, with a tarpaulin lifting in the wind. The rendering had not included the tarp. It had not included the smell of oil, the dust in the cases, or the small damaged e that refused to belong to the year printed beneath it.

Ada had seen men mistake a document for obedience. Paper could still resist, but only if someone let it speak in its own unattractive detail.

Chapter 11: Union Ledger

Celia Ward kept the union archive above a bakery on Mercer Street, in two rooms reached by a staircase that smelled of yeast, steam, and old varnish. The bakery ovens made the floorboards warm. Cardboard boxes lined the walls in numbered stacks, and a folding table stood beneath a window filmed with flour dust from the alley vent below.

"No bags on the table," Celia said before greeting Ada.

Ada set her case on the floor. "No coffee near the paper, no leaning, no corner turning. I have been disciplined by better archivists than you."

Celia's expression did not change. She was in her sixties, square-shouldered, with cropped white hair and a cardigan mended at one elbow. "We'll see."

She laid out wage books, strike minutes, and circulars from 1889. Unlike the museum proof, these objects had not been made pretty. The wage book was stained along the fore edge. The strike minutes were folded and refolded until the creases had become pale hinges. One circular had a thumbprint in ink beside a line about night casting rates.

"The foundry was not sold that year," Celia said. "They were fighting over unpaid rates. Men don't strike against a trust that has already vanished."

"They might if they had not been told."

"Then the trustees would not be answering them in writing."

She opened the wage book to June 1889. Ada read names, hours, piece rates, deductions for damaged sorts, small advances marked in a careful hand. In July, after the alleged transfer date on the proof, Bell & Vale trustees still signed off on wage negotiations. In August, a committee of workers demanded arrears under the protection of the trust. In September, a settlement recorded partial payment from the trust account.

A dissolved trust could not negotiate as if alive unless everyone in the building had chosen to perform a pointless fiction for months. Possible, Ada thought. Human beings loved pointless fictions. But the entries had the resistant plainness of accounting. They had not been written to persuade a future judge. They had been written because someone needed to know who was owed three dollars and fourteen cents.

"Experts have come before," Celia said.

"And?"

"They admired the handwriting and missed the wages."

Ada photographed entries with a scale and color card. "Then we begin with the wages."

Celia relaxed by a fraction. Trust was sometimes a measurable thing.

They worked through the afternoon while the bakery below sent up warm air and the smell of rye. Celia told Ada how the archive had survived: a union hall closure, a flood, two indifferent nephews, one librarian with a van, and forty years of small donations from people who still knew the difference between a printer and a publisher.

"Hugo offered to digitize it," Celia said.

"Generous."

"Branding on every page. Search access through his foundation. I told him the dead had suffered enough."

Ada looked up from the ledger. "Did he ask for 1889 material?"

"His consultant did. Polite woman. Expensive shoes. She wanted anything about the trust dispute, especially circulars. I gave her copies, not originals."

"When?"

"Eight months ago."

Ada wrote the date. "Did she mention a sale circular?"

"She asked whether one existed. I said if it did, it would have been used in every lawsuit since 1900, and no one had seen it."

"What did she say?"

"That absence can mean loss."

"It can."

"It can also mean invention."

By the time Ada left, Celia had placed a small stack of photocopies in a folder and written RECEIVED BY ADA KERN across the top in block letters. "You return these or I make your life unpleasant."

"Fair."

"And don't let them turn this into a quarrel between nostalgia and progress. That is lazy."

"What is it?"

Celia tapped the wage book. "It is a quarrel between records and convenience."

Outside, Ada paused in the bakery doorway. Workers moved behind the counter with quick hands, wrapping loaves, counting change, calling orders. The archive above them held older hands in ink and figures. The proof sheet had tried to speak in a single polished paragraph. The wage book answered in columns.

Chapter 12: Ink Under Glass

Dr. Iman Aral examined ink as if it had manners to reveal. Her laboratory at the university occupied half a basement, and everything in it was labeled in a precise hand: solvents, slides, swabs, waste, patience. She wore a white coat over a green dress and had pinned her dark hair high to keep it away from the microscope. When Ada arrived with Livia and the proof sheet, Iman made them wait while she finished calibrating an instrument.

"Evidence does not become more truthful because people are anxious," she said.

"I agree," Ada answered.

"Good. Many people agree in theory."

Iman inspected the seals, the chain-of-custody log, and the court notice authorizing micro-sampling. Then she examined the proof under glass, moving from word to word with the slow discipline of a reader who did not care what the sentence meant yet.

"The black is lively," she said.

Livia frowned. "Is that bad?"

"It is an observation."

Old iron gall and carbon blends aged with duller surfaces, especially on stored proofs. Ink could shine for innocent reasons: burnishing, additives, pressure, conservation treatment. But this black sat too confidently in the paper. It had not sunk the way the strike circular's ink had sunk. It seemed to rest with a thin surface authority, like a freshly polished shoe at a funeral.

Iman took microscopic samples from a damaged edge of one printed stroke and from Celia's genuine strike circular, which had arrived in a separate folder with Celia's warning written across it. The samples were so small that Livia stopped breathing while the scalpel moved.

"Do not ask me for certainty by Friday," Iman said.

"I would prefer accuracy by whenever it arrives," Ada said.

Iman glanced at her. "Good. We may work together."

She prepared chromatography plates and refused to dramatize them. Ada watched the solvent climb, carrying components upward in faint separations. Livia stood back, arms folded tight against her body. The room smelled sharp and clean, a chemical order far from the foundry's oil and damp paper.

"Can conservation contaminate ink?" Livia asked.

"Yes."

"Can it make old ink look modern?"

"It can complicate interpretation. It cannot change the century in which all components were manufactured."

"If modern components are present."

"If."

Ada appreciated the severity. Good experts protected the conditional mood until evidence earned better grammar.

While plates dried, Iman asked for the restorer's notes. Ada handed over copies. Iman read the phrases aloud without expression: visual integration of age, edge regularization, stabilization bath, final surface adjustment.

"This is not a treatment report," she said. "This is a mood board wearing gloves."

Livia shut her eyes.

"I need exact materials," Iman continued. "Solvents, adhesives, humidification process, any toning media, any surface coating, whether the object was pressed between blotters, whether ink was consolidated."

"We have requested them," Ada said.

"Request harder."

Ada wrote that down, then crossed out harder and wrote subpoena.

At the microscope, Iman showed Ada the difference between ink sitting over compressed fibers and ink drawn into them. The proof's printed strokes showed dense edges and a fine gloss. The strike circular's ink had feathered in tiny ways consistent with its paper and age. The differences were not theatrical. They required looking, which made them stronger.

"Ink chemistry is a chorus," Iman said. "One voice can be misleading."

Ada liked the sentence but did not write it down. She was avoiding beautiful phrases, especially when they were true.

Before leaving, Livia asked, "If it is false, will you know?"

Iman removed her gloves. "I may know what the ink can and cannot be. False is a human arrangement."

In the hallway, Livia leaned against the wall for a moment. "Everyone keeps saying things like that."

"Like what?"

"That the materials are innocent and people are not."

Ada looked through the glass panel into the lab, where the proof lay under cool light. "Materials are not innocent. They are indifferent. That is why I trust them."

Chapter 13: Rafe's Press

Rafe Bell printed in a narrow shop between a locksmith and a tailor, under a sign that read BELL LETTERPRESS in flaking green paint. His family name gave him standing and suspicion in equal measure. He was not descended from the founding partner, he told Ada before she asked; his grandfather had been a cousin, then a foreman, then a man who left after a fight nobody described the same way twice.

The shop was warm from machinery and smelled of ink, mineral spirits, and cut paper. Wedding invitations dried on racks near posters for union meetings and small theater companies. A platen press stood at the front. In the back, beneath a skylight patched with tape, an older proofing press waited with its rollers covered.

"I did not make Hugo's miracle," Rafe said.

"You begin there often?"

"Since the newspaper called me for comment, yes."

"What did you tell them?"

"That miracles require better margins."

Ada looked at him. He was younger than Theo, lean, with ink under two fingernails and a silver hoop in one ear. His manner was quick, defensive, and not foolish.

"Can you print from old Bell & Vale type?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Can you print the disputed text convincingly?"

"Probably."

"Will you show me?"

He studied her for a moment, then nodded. "Observation only. No photographs of client work."

"Agreed."

He pulled a drawer of old sorts, not Bell & Vale's complete face but enough to demonstrate. He composed a short line in a composing stick, spacing by feel, correcting one reversed letter before Ada had time to mention it. His hands were fast but not showy. He locked the forme, inked the rollers, and pulled a sheet. The old press worked with a heavy sigh, metal accepting pressure from metal, paper taking the result with a small clean sound.

The impression looked convincing, especially to anyone who wanted conviction more than proof. The ink was black. The bite was crisp. The line had the uneven warmth people liked to call authentic when they were really admiring imperfection at a safe distance.

"A modern printer could forge an old document," Ada said.

"A careless one could. A careful one would still leave fingerprints."

"Type fingerprints?"

Rafe tapped a battered e in the forme. "Every letter has a limp."

He showed her what he meant. One sort printed heavier on the left because its foot had been filed. Another had a cracked serif. A spacer sat slightly high and left a blind mark when pressure increased. A damaged letter repeated its injury wherever it appeared. Printing was not a font file. It was a crowd of individual bodies carrying individual histories.

"Why are you suspected?" Ada asked.

"Because I can do it. Because my name is Bell. Because Hugo would rather point at a man with ink on his hands than at anyone wearing a suit."

"Did Hugo approach you?"

Rafe wiped the rollers. "His foundation asked whether I could print commemorative material if the trust matter resolved. I said I don't print funeral programs for living buildings."

"Did they ask about old type?"

"They asked whether I knew where to find Bell & Vale sorts. I told them to ask Theo. Then I called Theo and told him not to be charmed."

"Was he?"

"Theo? By Hugo? He is more likely to be charmed by a properly labeled drawer."

Ada allowed a small laugh. Rafe did not.

"Look," he said, "I know what everyone thinks. A printer needs money. A developer needs a document. Old type exists. Therefore the man with the press must have sold his soul between lunch and closing."

"Did you?"

"No."

"I have to ask."

"I know. I still hate it."

He printed a second sample, this time changing pressure and ink. Ada watched the differences emerge: deeper impression, shinier surface, crushed fibers, filled counters. Method left pattern. Skill could disguise much, but not all.

Before she left, Rafe handed her the demonstration sheet. "For your files."

"You don't mind?"

"I mind plenty. But if someone used this craft to steal the building, I want the craft to answer."

On the sidewalk, Ada held the fresh print by its edges until the ink dried enough to folder. Rafe's shop had proved possibility, not guilt. That distinction would matter. Possibility was a door. People liked to mistake doors for destinations.

Chapter 14: The Broken Matrix

Theo found the broken matrix in a box marked WRONG SORTS, which was the kind of label that could mean anything in a foundry: damaged letters, miscast bodies, pieces saved for teaching, pieces saved because no one had the heart to melt them. The box sat beneath a bench behind two cracked galleys and a stack of old packing boards.

"I hate that box," Theo said.

"Why keep it?"

"Because wrong things explain right things."

Inside, wrapped in tissue that had gone brittle, lay a lowercase e matrix with a hairline crack near the lower bowl. Ada did not touch it at first. She brought the proof enlargement close, adjusted the lamp, and looked from paper to metal. The crack's position matched the printed wound. Not roughly. Persuasively. The nick in the proof was the visible consequence of this damaged matrix or of sorts cast from it.

Theo became very quiet.

"When did it crack?" Ada asked.

He went to the repair files. They were kept in a drawer whose handle had been replaced with a strip of leather. Ada heard paper shifting, Theo muttering dates, the slap of one folder against another. He returned with a ticket written in purple copying pencil.

"Matrix e, text roman, lower bowl flaw," he read. "Pulled from regular casting. Recut requested. 1902, maybe the work carried into 1903."

"Before your grandfather's time."

"Before my grandfather's time."

The sentence mattered to him. Ada let it stand.

That placed the damaged e after the claimed proof date. It also proved the disputed sheet had not been made from a digital imitation by someone with a novelty printer. The forger, if forger was now the right working word, had used authentic material incorrectly. That made the case both stronger and more difficult. Old tools gave lies a borrowed dignity.

"Who had access to this matrix?" Ada asked.

Theo looked around the room as if the cabinets might answer. "Originally? Foundry workers. Later? Me, museum catalogers, foundation people, maybe Marlo Venn, maybe volunteers during the roof move. Too many, once Hugo paid for cataloging."

"Was it cataloged?"

"Supposedly." He checked a binder. "Listed as damaged educational material. No image."

"Convenient."

"Common. Don't make every mess clever."

Ada nodded. He was right. Neglect created enough holes for wrongdoing to pass through without designing them.

They staged the matrix beside the proof enlargement and a clean e from the original punch series. Ada photographed all three with a scale. Then she asked Theo to cast one sort from the damaged matrix if doing so would not harm it.

"It may already be too fragile."

"Then don't."

He studied the metal under the loupe, deciding. "One controlled cast. Low pressure."

The casting took longer to prepare than to perform. Theo heated the mold, checked the alloy, and explained each step because Ada asked him to. Molten metal entered the matrix with a bright motion almost too quick to see. When the sort cooled, Theo broke it free and placed it on the stone. The tiny e looked ordinary until ink made its injury visible.

They printed a test on scrap under light pressure. The same lower-bowl wound appeared.

Theo leaned both hands on the bench. "Damn."

"Evidence?"

"No. Just damn."

Ada logged the test, bagged the print, and had Theo sign the temporary removal and return of the matrix. A small wound had become chronology: clean punch, damaged matrix after 1902, disputed proof dated 1889. The sequence did not shout, but it closed a door.

At the end of the day, Theo wrapped the matrix again. "You think whoever did this understood what they had?"

"Enough to use it. Not enough to fear it."

"That describes many criminals."

"And some curators."

He gave her a hard look, then conceded it with a short nod. In the foundry's late light, the matrix box looked less like clutter and more like a room of witnesses who had been waiting for someone to ask the right, narrow questions.

Chapter 15: Probate Notice

Judge Maren Quill issued a probate notice for an evidentiary hearing on a Tuesday morning. The document arrived by email and courier, because Neris believed redundancy was a kind of prayer. Ada read the notice at her kitchen table while coffee went cold beside her. It was plain and efficient: date, time, parties, scope, preliminary issues, preservation order. No grand language about heritage. No indulgence toward urgency. Ada respected that.

Neris called ten minutes later. "The court wants a preliminary expert report."

"The court or you?"

"Both. The court wants it filed in five days. I wanted it yesterday and did not say so because you frighten me."

"Good instinct."

"What will it say?"

"Unresolved."

Neris exhaled through her nose. "That helps no one."

"It helps the truth."

"The truth is not paying filing fees."

"Then send it an invoice."

Neris was silent long enough for Ada to regret the joke. Lawyers liked truth best when it arrived dressed as strategy, but Neris had so far earned better than cheap irritation.

"I have enough to prevent an easy authenticity finding," Ada said. "Not enough to finish."

"List it."

Ada did. Damaged lowercase e associated with post-1902 matrix. Watermark inconsistent with 1889. Modern or materially later trimming. Missing alloy ledger page. Disputed accession route through Hugo's foundation. Union wage records contradicting the sale theory. Potential artificial aging. Ink analysis pending. Custody gaps severe.

"That sounds like more than unresolved."

"It sounds like serious doubt. Serious doubt is not final proof of forgery."

"Can I use that phrase?"

"Use the facts."

She spent the day writing. Preliminary reports were ugly animals. They had to carry enough detail to be useful without pretending that incomplete work had become complete because a calendar demanded it. Ada wrote in short sentences. She cited photographs, specimen dates, ledger entries, paper analysis, chain-of-custody gaps. She avoided motive except where documented. She did not call Hugo a forger. She did not call the proof a forgery. She wrote that the document contained multiple material features inconsistent with its asserted date and stated provenance.

At three, Theo called to say a local reporter had been at the pressworks gate. At four, Celia called to say Hugo's consultant had asked whether the union archive intended to "contextualize" its opposition. At five, Livia sent an email with no greeting and one attachment: the canceled announcement invitation.

Ada filed the report at 6:12 p.m. Neris responded with a single word: received.

Outside the courthouse the next morning, Hugo's consultant stood before two cameras and spoke about a major heritage discovery needing careful stewardship. Her coat was the color of fresh paper. She did not mention the damaged e, the watermark, or the wage book. She said the community deserved closure.

Ada watched from the steps with Neris beside her.

"Do you want to respond?" Neris asked.

"No."

"Good. I didn't want you to."

"Why ask?"

"To enjoy your restraint."

Ada turned away before the cameras could find her. Forgery loved a deadline. So did speculation, litigation, fundraising, and grief. The only defense was a slower pace enforced by facts. Inside the courthouse, the clerk stamped her filed report with a sound that pleased her more than it should have. A stamp was not truth, but it meant the record had opened its mouth.

In the elevator, Neris read the filed copy again as if the words might rearrange themselves into strategy. "Unresolved," she said. "Judges dislike that." "Good judges tolerate it when the alternative is false certainty." The doors opened on the records floor. A clerk carried boxes past them, each labeled in marker, each heavy with other people's disputes. Ada stepped aside and let the boxes pass. Their case was not special to the building. That steadied her.

At the filing window, the clerk checked the exhibit list against the report and asked whether the damaged e was a person or an object. Neris opened her mouth. Ada answered first. "Object." The clerk wrote it down without smiling. Outside, Neris said, "You hesitated." "It has been more cooperative than several people." That small absurdity carried them through the lobby without speaking to the cameras. Even humor, Ada thought, had to know when to keep moving.

Chapter 16: Galley Rumor

Theo remembered the galley while cleaning his spectacles with the hem of his shirt, which Ada had already told him not to do near old paper. They were in the pressworks office, a glass-walled room with a rolltop desk and a radiator that clanged whenever conversation became useful.

"There was a locked galley after the 1889 strike," he said.

Ada looked up from an inventory binder. "What galley?"

"Proofs of the workers' circular. Not the printed circular. The type itself. Locked standing after the dispute because the trustees wanted a record of what had been set."

"You are remembering this now?"

"I am remembering my grandfather telling my mother while both were angry about something else. Family history hides in arguments."

"Where was it kept?"

"Story says cabinet six. Cabinet six says empty."

They went to cabinet six anyway. It stood against the north wall, a tall oak case with warped doors and a broken escutcheon. Theo opened it with a key from his ring. Inside were shelves, dust, a dead moth, and a label scar where something had once been pasted. No galley.

"When did you last see it?" Ada asked.

"I never saw it. My grandfather said his father did. Or said someone said he did. This is why I don't offer family stories until I have no better tools."

Ada appreciated the honesty and disliked the problem. Rumor could poison an investigation if it became a destination. It could also point toward a record everyone had stopped naming because it sounded too convenient.

Celia had heard the rumor too. Ada met her that afternoon above the bakery, where the windows were fogged from the ovens below.

"Locked galley," Celia said before Ada finished. "Yes. The workers' circular defending the trust. My predecessor wrote a note about it in 1978. Said it proved the trustees had the text set after refusing a private sale."

"Did she see it?"

"No. She was told by a retired compositor named Hal Brough."

"Alive?"

"Dead twenty years."

"Of course."

Celia slid a folder across the table. Inside was the 1978 note, a transcription of Hal Brough's account, and a photocopy of the genuine strike circular. The circular defended the trust, named unpaid casting rates, and called for workers to hold the trustees to their obligations. If the galley survived, it could show what Bell & Vale actually set that year, in metal, with spacing and sorts intact.

Ada searched inventory lists at the museum, then the pressworks, then the foundation-funded catalog. Cabinet six appeared in a restoration intake form six months earlier: opened, contents assessed, conservation priorities identified. No details. No photographs.

"Hugo's foundation funded that restoration," Ada said when Celia called for an update.

"Of course."

"Evidence does not become better because it is obvious."

"No," Celia said. "But irritation keeps us warm."

Ada allowed that.

She returned to cabinet six with Theo and a flashlight. The interior dust showed rectangles where objects had rested. One shelf had scratches consistent with a long metal galley being dragged out. The back panel sounded dull when tapped on the left, hollow on the right.

"Has this been altered?" Ada asked.

Theo crouched and ran his fingers along the panel seam. "Hard to say. Cabinets swell. Men repair badly. Time is a poor carpenter."

"We need photographs before touching anything."

"I know."

They documented the cabinet, then sealed it with tamper tape pending court permission for closer inspection. Theo looked annoyed by the tape on the old wood, but signed the log without complaint.

On the walk back to her car, Ada thought about standing type locked after a strike: words held in metal because paper could be lost, denied, burned, or trimmed. A galley was awkward to store, heavy, impractical. That made it plausible. People preserved things for strange reasons, especially after quarrels. The forged proof claimed the trust had been surrendered. Somewhere, perhaps, the workers' own words remained standing, not eloquent, not polished, simply refusing to be distributed into silence.

Chapter 17: Dust in the Cases

The type cases held dust in different dialects. Ada would not have said so in a report, but it was true enough in the room. Some compartments had gray settled powder that had not moved in years. Others showed darker channels where fingers had recently searched. A few held clean-bottomed hollows in the exact shape of missing sorts. Dust could not give dates with scientific confidence, but it could show disturbance, and disturbance could guide better questions.

Theo set the case for Bell & Vale text roman on the imposing stone. Ada photographed it before he removed the drawer. They worked by quadrants. She lifted letters with tweezers, logged samples, and noted which compartments contained mixed forms. Theo identified substitutions as quickly as another man might identify cousins.

"Later recut," he said, pointing. "Wrong shoulder. This one too. That thin space is machine-cut, not hand."

"How did it get here?"

"Laziness. Desperation. Or someone shopping."

The lowercase e compartment was the most disturbed. Several sorts were missing. Others were from a later recut, including at least two that could have been cast from the damaged matrix. They sat where earlier forms belonged, as if someone had tried to assemble enough letters to print a short declaration without caring whether every body belonged to the same year.

"Someone built a period font from leftovers," Theo said.

"Not period. Plausible."

"That's worse."

"Usually."

They found a modern spacer wedged under a row of old ens. Its edges were machine-smooth, its metal slightly different in color, its height too perfect. It had no business in the old case. Ada bagged it, labeled it, and had Theo sign the evidence slip.

"A spacer won't hang anyone," he said.

"I don't hang people. I arrange facts."

"That sounds more civilized and more frightening."

Small modern things often carried large modern stories. The spacer suggested recent composition, perhaps by someone working quickly among old sorts. It might have come from a volunteer demonstration or an innocent workshop. It might have been dropped by a forger building the proof. Ada kept both possibilities alive, though the latter now stood nearer the front of the room.

Theo grew more subdued as they worked. Each mixed compartment seemed to bother him personally. He closed one drawer, opened another, frowned at the labels.

"This place was never tidy," he said. "But it had order."

"Order leaves traces too."

"So does betrayal."

Ada did not answer. The word was too large for a spacer and an e compartment, but she understood why he reached for it. In a working shop, a type case was not a relic. It was a map of labor. Someone who disturbed it for fraud had not merely stolen metal. They had used the habits of dead workers as camouflage.

Near the back of the drawer, Ada found three sorts wrapped in tissue. The tissue was modern, from a conservation supply company, and labeled B&V 8 ROMAN MIX. The letters were clean, later casts, with tiny founder marks on the shoulders.

"Foundation cataloging?" she asked.

Theo nodded. "They wrapped samples for display."

"Who supervised?"

"Marlo Venn some days. Hugo's heritage consultant other days. Museum staff in theory."

"In theory is where many bad things happen."

Theo closed the drawer gently, as if ashamed on the room's behalf. "Will the judge understand this?"

"Not all of it."

"Then why do all of it?"

"Because the part the judge understands has to be anchored in the part she doesn't."

He considered that. "You would have made a difficult compositor."

"Too impatient."

"No. Too fond of proof."

Outside, the afternoon light thinned. The cases stood in rows, each drawer a small disordered republic of metal. Ada sealed the modern spacer in her case and looked once more at the disturbed e compartment. The proof sheet had seemed clean because someone had made the mess elsewhere.

The mess mattered.

Chapter 18: Developer Visit

Hugo visited Ada's office with a leather folder and no appointment on a Thursday when the radiators had failed. Ada's office occupied two rooms above a surveyor, with a view of a brick wall and a sliver of sky generous enough to record weather but not mood. She was wearing her coat indoors and comparing photographs of the damaged e when the outer door opened.

"Ms. Kern," her assistant called from the front room, using the voice that meant money had entered without permission.

Hugo appeared a moment later. He admired her shelves, which was how rich men apologized for entering. "You have a remarkable collection."

"I have a locked door."

"Your assistant said you had ten minutes."

"My assistant said she would ask."

He accepted the correction with a small tilt of his head. "Then I apologize."

"Use the minutes you took."

He sat only after she did not invite him to. The leather folder rested on his lap. His coat was dark wool, still beaded from rain. He looked less polished than at the sales suite, or perhaps the room was too plain to flatter him.

"I value independent expertise," he said.

"Then wait for mine."

"I intend to. But I also think contentious processes sometimes make enemies where partners would serve better."

"Is that what we are? Potential partners?"

"In a broader sense. The city has many heritage properties. My foundation funds documentation, stabilization, interpretation. Someone with your skills could help ensure those projects are rigorous."

He opened the folder. Inside was a consulting offer for future heritage projects, printed on thick paper with a number large enough to be insulting. Not extravagant by corporate standards. Extravagant by the standards of independent experts who chased invoices from museums.

Ada read the first page without touching it. "You misunderstand."

"I hope not."

"No. You do."

She pushed the folder back across the desk with one finger.

Hugo's expression stayed composed. "This is separate from the Bell & Vale matter."

"Nothing is separate when a hearing is scheduled."

"The offer begins after the hearing."

"That makes it worse, not better."

His face cooled then, not in anger exactly, but in disappointment that she had declined the elegant version of pressure. "Old buildings die without capital."

"So do facts."

"Facts do not repair roofs."

"Lies do not either. They only make the invoice someone else's problem."

For a moment the rain against the window filled the room. Ada's assistant had gone quiet outside. Hugo closed the folder halfway, then left it on the desk.

"You are making me into a villain," he said.

"I am making notes."

"I have saved buildings this city would have demolished."

"Then you know saving is not the same as owning."

He stood. "You should consider whether perfect integrity is a luxury."

"It is usually an inconvenience. That is how I recognize it."

After he left, Ada did not touch the folder barehanded. She photographed it in place, sealed it in an evidence sleeve, and wrote a memo while the conversation was fresh. Then she sent a copy to Neris with the subject line UNSOLICITED CONTACT.

Neris called within two minutes. "Tell me you did not throw it at him."

"I did not."

"I am both relieved and disappointed."

"Preserve your disappointment for court."

"This helps."

"It is pressure. Pressure is evidence when preserved quickly."

Ada hung up and looked at the folder through plastic. She did not know whether Hugo had intended bribery, temptation, or simply the ordinary rich man's belief that every independent person was waiting for a better patron. Intent would be argued later, if at all. The act was enough for now: he had come privately, before a hearing, with money dressed as future work.

She returned to the damaged e photographs. The little wound looked almost cheerful under magnification. It had no idea what anyone would pay to make it disappear.

Chapter 19: The Wrong Ligature

The proof contained an fi ligature with a shortened terminal in the word filed. Ada noticed it late, which irritated her more than the clue deserved. She had been looking so closely at the lowercase e that other letters had enjoyed temporary privacy.

She found the ligature while building a chronology chart for Neris. The proof's f and i were not separate sorts. They shared a single body, the f bending into the i with a small clipped terminal. In the 1887 specimen, Bell & Vale's text roman used separate f and i for that size. In the 1896 supplement, a new ligature set appeared, advertised as reducing collisions in legal and commercial work.

Ada called Livia and asked for the museum's highest-resolution scan. Then she called Theo.

"Did Bell & Vale use fi ligatures in 1889?"

"Not in that face at that size."

"How sure?"

"Sure enough to be annoyed you asked before I checked. Give me an hour."

He called back in forty minutes, which meant he had been very sure. "Supplement introduced 1896. Some special casting perhaps a year earlier, no evidence before that. Why?"

"The proof has one."

Theo was silent. "Of course it does."

Ada met Livia at the museum that afternoon. They projected the enlargement onto the conservation room monitor. The ligature filled the screen, awkward and damning.

Livia stared at it. "Most readers would never see that."

"Most readers were not meant to."

"I didn't see it."

"You were looking at the story."

The words sounded harsher than Ada intended, but Livia did not defend herself. She leaned closer to the screen. "So this is another date problem."

"Yes. Before 1896, this character should not be in this face. The document claims 1889."

"Could a printer have used a different face for one word?"

"Not without leaving other differences. And this matches the later Bell & Vale supplement."

The forged document relied on authority, not close reading. It expected curators to admire paper, lawyers to admire signatures, and judges to admire experts. It expected everyone to accept the whole page and not bother with a union between two letters. Ada kept admiring the wrong things: a nick, a ligature, a margin.

She added the ligature to the chronology chart in red. The chart now showed three independent impossibilities: damaged e after 1902, watermark after 1894, ligature after 1896. Around them clustered supporting concerns: trim, custody, alloy, union contradiction, ink pending.

"This should be enough," Livia said.

"For what?"

"To stop it."

"To slow it. Stopping requires court, and court requires patience."

Livia folded her arms. "Do you ever say anything pleasant?"

"Yes. Usually about lunch."

For the first time in days, Livia laughed. It was brief and surprised, like a dropped object caught before breaking.

They spent another hour comparing the ligature to specimen books. Ada photographed the supplement, including title page and date, then asked Livia to initial the copies. The curator did so without complaint.

"If the museum label changes," Livia said, "I want it to explain this. Not just say modern forgery. I want visitors to see why."

"Good."

"Is that approval?"

"It is a beginning."

Impossible was not yet culprit. It was enough to keep going. But the proof was now impossible in ways that did not depend on anyone's memory, hope, or institutional embarrassment. It had borrowed letters from the future and trusted the room not to notice.

Ada sent the ligature comparison to Theo before leaving the museum. He replied with a photograph of a drawer label and two words: later, definitely. A minute later he sent another message: hate this. She understood both messages as evidence of care. The forger had known enough to choose old metal and not enough to know which small conveniences belonged to which year. Convenience, Ada thought, was often the first anachronism. It smoothed the past until the seams showed.

Chapter 20: Livia's Inventory

Livia's inventory showed the proof had arrived with three related items: a brown wrapper, a handwritten note, and a printer's waste sheet. Ada found the references in a preliminary intake spreadsheet rather than the formal accession record. The formal record mentioned only the proof. The discrepancy sat in a cell labeled associated material, which someone had hidden by narrowing the column.

"Where are the others?" Ada asked.

They were in Livia's office. Outside, museum visitors moved through the corridor with the soft, uncertain steps people use near glass cases. Livia stood behind her desk and looked at the spreadsheet as if it had betrayed her personally.

"Transferred to donor custody for framing decisions."

Ada closed her eyes briefly.

"Evidence left the museum?"

"Temporarily."

"Evidence does not understand temporarily."

"I know how it sounds."

"It sounds like the truth wearing a bad coat."

Livia sat down. The dying fern had finally been removed from the window. Its absence made the office look cleaner and less kind.

"The wrapper was ugly," she said. "Brown paper, torn, with string marks. The note was short. The waste sheet had ink smears. Hugo's consultant said they cluttered the visual presentation and could be photographed later. Marlo agreed they needed flattening."

"Who authorized transfer?"

"I signed the form."

"Show me."

The form used the museum's outgoing-loan template, altered by hand. Purpose: donor review. Condition: stable. Return date: TBD. Ada photographed it and asked who transported the material.

"Courier."

"Which one?"

"Foundation courier."

Ada waited.

Livia looked down. "No independent receipt."

The room seemed to shrink around the desk. Ada had seen professionals reach this point before: the moment when carelessness stopped being a general atmosphere and became a sequence of chosen acts, each small enough to excuse at the time, all of them together making disaster possible.

"Did you read the note?" Ada asked.

"Yes."

"From memory."

Livia pressed her fingers to her forehead. "It said something like: pulled before final correction, keep with trust papers. No signature. Maybe initials."

"Initials?"

"I don't remember."

"The waste sheet?"

"A partial line. Ink too heavy. I didn't study it."

"Because the proof was the object."

"Because I wanted the proof to be the object."

There it was. Not fraud, not yet. Wanting. The quiet pressure under so many false records. Livia had wanted a discovery, a gallery, a saved institution, a way to turn donor money into public good without asking too hard how clean the money was. Hugo had offered urgency. The proof had offered a story. The ugly associated materials had complicated the story, so they had been allowed to leave.

"I should have stopped it," Livia said.

"Yes."

The word landed hard, but it steadied the room. Ada preferred responsibility to performance.

"What now?" Livia asked.

"You produce a corrected inventory. You disclose the transfer to Neris and the court. You request immediate return of the wrapper, note, and waste sheet. You preserve every email and message. You do not call Hugo first to warn him."

"The board will panic."

"Let them. Panic is not an evidentiary category."

Livia opened a new document and began typing while Ada watched. Her hands were steadier now. Shame, once accepted, could become labor.

When the corrected inventory was finished, Livia read it aloud. The sentences were plain. They did not excuse. They named the missing items, the transfer, the lack of independent receipt, and the need for recovery.

"Send it," Ada said.

Livia clicked.

For a moment neither of them spoke. Then somewhere beyond the office wall a child laughed in the museum corridor, bright and unaware of custody chains, donor pressure, and trimmed margins.

"I used to love this work," Livia said.

"Then do it."

She looked at Ada with irritation, gratitude, or both. "You are very hard to like."

"That is not among my deliverables."

Before Ada left, Livia opened the museum's outgoing-loan drawer and removed the altered form. "This template is gone today," she said. Ada watched her write VOID across it in red pencil. "Keep it," Ada said. "As a specimen?" "As a warning." Livia placed it in a folder labeled custody failure review. That was not absolution. It was better: a place where the mistake would remain visible enough to instruct the next hurried person.

Chapter 21: Strike Circular

Celia's strike circular was stained, folded, and alive with use. She brought it to the museum herself in a gray portfolio and refused to release it until Livia signed a receipt in ink and Ada signed beneath her. The circular had been printed on cheap paper, then carried in pockets, pinned to a wall, folded into a wage book, and unfolded by hands that had cared more about being paid than about archival condition.

It named the trust as a workers' protection and demanded unpaid rates for night casting. The language was practical, angry, and exact: hours, amounts, names of trustees, dates of unanswered petitions. No flourish. No romance. A printer had set it to be read quickly by tired people.

"This is what 1889 sounds like," Celia said.

Ada did not answer at once. The same Bell & Vale face appeared in the headline, but the letters belonged to the earlier set. The e was clean. The later fi ligature was absent. The spacing was tighter after periods, less polished in its justification, with a slight river through the third line where the compositor had chosen speed over beauty. The ink had sunk into the paper without shine.

She read the circular aloud once. Not for drama. For rhythm. Printers set words with habit, and habit had evidence inside it. The line breaks showed a working compositor solving practical problems under time pressure. The disputed proof, by contrast, had wider spacing after periods, cleaner justification, and an air of ceremonial balance. It looked like someone imitating old printing after studying examples selected for display, not after handling job work.

"You hear it?" Celia asked.

"I see it."

"That will do."

Theo arrived halfway through the examination with a specimen book under one arm and a paper bag of rolls from the bakery below Celia's archive. Livia nearly objected to food in the lab, and Theo held up the bag without apology.

"For the hallway," he said. "Civilization can survive thirty feet away."

They compared the circular, specimen pages, and proof sheet on three stands. Ada marked differences on acetate overlays. Clean e. Separate f and i. Earlier spacing. Ink absorption. Paper fibers consistent with Owen's known 1880s samples. Wear that interrupted both ink and paper in the same places, not abrasion arranged around the edges.

"The circular is ugly," Livia said softly.

Celia looked at her. "No. It is busy."

Livia accepted the correction. "Busy, then."

Ada photographed the fold intersections. Ink had broken naturally along creases that had been opened and closed many times. Dirt collected unevenly at the folds. The proof sheet had none of that. It had the polite distress of an object prepared to be looked at, not a document that had done work.

"Someone copied the age," Ada said, "not the hand."

"Put that in the report," Celia said.

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because Hugo's lawyer will enjoy it. I prefer boring him."

Celia smiled for the first time. "Good."

Before returning the circular to its portfolio, Ada asked Celia how it had survived.

"A woman named Mara Voss folded it into the wage book," Celia said. "Sorter. Widow. She was owed six weeks' pay. Her niece gave the book to the union archive in 1931. There is a note."

"Adult worker?"

"Thirty-two. Burn scar on her left arm according to the pension file. You want the file?"

"Yes."

Celia raised an eyebrow. "You begin with wages and end with people."

"They are harder to separate than lawyers think."

The circular went back into its folder. Ada watched Celia tie the tapes with blunt fingers. The forged proof had claimed a tidy surrender. The strike circular had not surrendered anything. It had argued, named debts, and kept the trust present in the room long after the alleged sale. Its stains were not decoration. They were use, and use had a grammar no forger had quite learned.

Chapter 22: Paper Bath

Owen found starch and tea residues near the trimmed edge and called Ada at seven in the morning, which meant either urgency or contempt for sleep. His voice was brisk.

"Your theatrical paper has had a bath."

"Good morning to you too."

"Bring the custody log."

In his studio, he had prepared comparison slides and a page of notes in his severe handwriting. The proof sheet lay under a cover, and the room smelled faintly of warm dust and the frame shop's cut wood below. Owen pointed to the lower margin first.

"Starch residue here. Tannins consistent with tea or a similar plant-based toning bath. Not conclusive as tea from someone's kitchen, no matter how much a jury would enjoy that. But the sheet was wetted, toned, dried, and pressed."

"Conservation treatment?"

"Possibly. Ethical conservation documents its materials and intent."

"We don't have that."

"Then we have a problem with manners and perhaps law."

He showed her the fibers near the edge. Artificial aging had softened some and darkened the sheet unevenly, but the center remained too clean. The treatment aimed at the border because viewers notice edges first. A sheet aged by storage does not politely concentrate its history where human suspicion is most likely to begin.

"People age paper with worse recipes," Owen said. "This one is competent."

"How recent?"

"Recent enough that I would like the restorer's sink."

"That is not a date."

"It is a desire supported by evidence."

Ada wrote down starch, tannins, wetting, pressing, edge concentration. Owen dictated a formal paragraph while she listened. He would say the residues were inconsistent with untreated nineteenth-century storage and consistent with later toning or conservation. He would not say fraud unless the full context required it. Paper alone remained a bad witness. Paper with type, ink, custody, and missing associated material was becoming a better one.

"Can a legitimate restorer use starch?" Ada asked.

"Yes. For mending, lining, hinging. Can a legitimate restorer tone paper? Under narrow circumstances. Can a legitimate restorer trim, tone, flatten, and return no exact record while a developer needs an old document to look persuasive? I am a paper expert, not a priest."

"That answer will not go in the report."

"Good. It was for you."

Ada updated the evidence chart on her laptop while Owen made tea and then insulted himself for the choice. Paper: later watermark, artificial aging, modern trim, possible removal of lower notation. Type: later damage, later ligature, later alloy. Ink: pending. Custody: compromised. Associated materials: missing.

Forgery no longer felt like a possibility. It felt like a workshop.

Before she left, Owen handed her a sealed sample packet. "For independent confirmation if needed. Do not let anyone say I built a cathedral on one slide."

"I won't."

"And tell your curator that panic is humidifying her judgment."

"She knows."

"Knowing is not correction."

Ada took the stairs down rather than the elevator, partly to think. The proof had been made old in stages: paper toned, edge trimmed, type selected, ink chosen, story framed. None of it required genius. It required access, patience, and a room where no one asked an inconvenient question soon enough.

At the street door, she called Livia and asked for the name of every person present when the treated proof returned to the museum. Livia began with titles. Ada stopped her and asked for names. Titles made responsibility look institutional and far away. Names could be called, questioned, contradicted, remembered. By the time Ada reached her car, she had four new names in her notebook and a clearer sense of how quietly a forged object could enter a public collection: wrapped, admired, and passed along by people who mistook procedure for distrust.

Owen followed with a message containing only a photograph of the edge under higher magnification and the subject line MORE TROUBLE. Ada enlarged it in the car before starting the engine. The cut fibers lay in a clean row, too disciplined for age. She called him back. "You enjoy bad news," she said. "I enjoy being right," he replied. "Bad news is simply overrepresented." She added the image to the paper file and drove to the museum with the feeling that the sheet was losing its disguises one thread at a time.

Chapter 23: Ink Split

Iman's chart arrived without flourish. She did not send a dramatic email or call with a verdict. She sent a message that read: Results ready. Bring counsel if desired. Ada brought Neris, who arrived with two legal pads and the expression of a woman trying not to hope in public.

The lab was colder than before. Iman had pinned the chromatograms and spectra to a board, each labeled with sample number rather than story. The proof ink. The strike circular. Rafe's demonstration sheet. Control samples from known nineteenth-century carbon ink and modern letterpress ink. Peaks rose and fell like a skyline no one had built for beauty.

"The proof ink contains a cobalt-based drying package and an alkyd component," Iman said. "The specific formulation is not available in nineteenth-century foundry ink. It is consistent with modern restoration and specialty letterpress materials."

Neris gripped her pen. "Could contamination explain it?"

"At trace levels, one may discuss contamination. Not at this concentration, and not distributed within the printed stroke."

"Meaning?"

Iman showed a cross-section image. "The component is not sitting on top from later handling. It is in the ink body. Present at printing."

Neris wrote quickly, then stopped. "So the ink is modern."

"The ink formulation is modern. Say only what the test says."

Ada smiled slightly. "Of course."

"Do not smile at my caution. It is saving you from appellate trouble."

"I know."

Iman compared the strike circular beside the proof. Even Ada could see the difference: older ink with simpler components, oxidation patterns, absorption into paper fibers; modern ink with synthetic binders and dryers, a surface behavior that explained the sheen. Rafe's demonstration ink did not match the proof exactly. That mattered. The method was possible, but his shop's materials were not the same.

"Can you date the printing?" Neris asked.

"No. I can exclude the claimed period for this formulation. I can say it was printed with materials available in modern practice. If you ask me for a year, I will become unpleasant."

"Noted."

Ada asked about conservation contamination again, because Hugo's expert would. Iman answered with diagrams. If the alkyd had come from a later surface application, it would present differently at fiber interfaces. If dryers had migrated from a coating, concentration would differ at the exposed surface. The proof showed integration consistent with ink as applied during printing.

"This is strong," Neris said.

"It is strong within its lane," Iman replied.

"Your lane just ran over their document."

Iman's face did not move. "Counselor, please do not say that in court."

The precision became the report's strength. The forged sheet could no longer hide inside romantic language about rediscovery. Chemistry had turned down the lights. What remained on the table looked less like a miracle and more like a job performed with tools.

After Neris left to file a supplemental notice, Ada stayed behind.

"Does this bother you?" she asked.

"Modern ink on old paper? Often. People want objects to be what they need."

"No. This case."

Iman removed her gloves. "The false document bothers me less than the readiness around it. The ink is only ink. The appetite was waiting."

Ada thought of Hugo's renderings, Livia's invitation, the heirs' needs, the trust board's exhaustion. Appetite did not make the proof false. It made falsehood useful.

She left with copies of the charts, each sealed, each dull as a ledger and more damaging than any accusation.

In the corridor, Neris waited beside a vending machine and read the summary twice. "This is the first thing Hugo cannot prettify," she said. Ada was less sure. Money could prettify almost anything for a while. But the chart resisted narrative. Its peaks did not care about redevelopment, family hardship, museum funding, or Ada's temper. They showed what had been mixed into black ink before it touched the page. Sometimes dullness was the highest form of force.

Iman came out to hand Ada a corrected label for one axis. "If you use the draft, I will deny knowing you." Ada took it. "Your affection is overwhelming." "My standards are." Neris looked between them and tucked the corrected label into her folder as if it were a fragile settlement. The small correction mattered. If they let one axis remain sloppy, Hugo's expert would pull on it until the chart seemed careless. Precision was not decoration. It was armor fitted one strap at a time.

Chapter 24: Night Pull

Rafe agreed to a night pull under observation because his shop was closed then and because he did not want customers walking into an argument with the nineteenth century. Ada, Theo, and Iman arrived after eight. Neris sent a paralegal to witness the chain-of-custody forms, and Rafe made everyone sign a sheet confirming that the demonstration did not imply his involvement in the disputed proof.

"I have learned from unpleasant people," he said.

"That is the law's main public service," Neris's paralegal replied.

Rafe had composed the disputed text from a mixed set: old Bell & Vale sorts, later recuts supplied under observation by Theo, modern spaces, and a contemporary black ink selected for similarity to Iman's findings. The actual damaged matrix remained too fragile for casual use, but Theo had cast a controlled sort earlier for demonstration. It sat in a small tray like a guilty seed.

The shop at night felt different. The street sounds were muffled by rain. The press light made a hard circle on the bed. Ink smelled sharper in the closed room. Rafe moved with care, narrating each step for the record: lockup, packing, ink distribution, pressure adjustment, paper placement.

"No theater," Ada said.

"This is printing. Theater is a surcharge."

He pulled the first sheet. The press sighed. Paper lifted. The disputed paragraph appeared in black, centered, plausible, and ugly in its plausibility.

Theo said nothing.

The result looked dangerously close. Under magnification, the control proof shared the same sharp shoulders, the same ink sheen, and a similar impression depth. The spacing was slightly off, but Rafe corrected it on the second pull. By the fourth, the demonstration could have fooled anyone who wanted the document to be real and many who did not know what questions to ask.

"That is why I hate clever nostalgia," Rafe said. "It teaches people to admire the costume."

Iman took a sample from a sacrificial stroke after the sheet dried. Ada photographed the process. The demonstration did not prove Rafe had forged the document. It proved the method was possible with old tools, later sorts, modern ink, and a knowledgeable printer.

Ada asked him to print another sheet with the damaged e sort.

Rafe looked at Theo. Theo nodded once.

The lowercase e produced the same small wound. There it was again, the little injury that had opened the case, now appearing under controlled conditions on fresh paper in a modern shop. Ada placed the demonstration beside the proof enlargement. Similar enough to teach. Not identical enough to accuse.

"If Hugo's people see this, they'll point at me harder," Rafe said.

"They may."

"Comforting."

"The ink and press pressure differ. Your records are open. Their custody is not."

"That is better than comforting."

Theo looked at the pull for a long time. The fresh sheet lay between them, a ghost made on purpose.

"History is not fragile," he said. "But records are."

No one joked. Rafe hung the demonstration sheets to dry. Iman packed samples. Ada sealed photographs and notes. Outside, rain thinned to mist, and passing headlights slid across the shop window.

Before leaving, Rafe washed the ink from the rollers with quick, angry strokes.

"I love this work," he said. "I hate seeing how easy it is to make people trust the wrong part of it."

"Most tools can do honest and dishonest work."

"Yes. But some tools remember better than we deserve."

Ada folded the signed observation sheet into her case. Possibility had become method. Method would need custody. Custody would need a hand.

Before they locked the shop, Rafe gave Theo the failed pulls. "For teaching," he said. Theo examined them and found fault with three things before accepting the stack. Iman sealed her samples without looking up. The room smelled of solvent and wet wool, and everyone seemed older than when the night began. Ada paused at the door to watch the fresh ink dull as it dried. False age could be applied later, but the first fact was always this: a sheet came clean from a press, and someone decided what lie it would carry.

Chapter 25: The Locked Cabinet

Cabinet six opened properly only after Theo found a second key taped beneath a drawer runner in the old office desk. He discovered it while looking for a screwdriver and came to the composing room holding the key between thumb and forefinger.

"Either this belongs to cabinet six," he said, "or someone here had a disappointing imagination."

Ada called Neris before they touched anything. By late afternoon, a court order allowed inspection under observation. The clerk arrived in a navy raincoat, Livia came from the museum with red eyes and a camera, and Theo stood beside the cabinet as if waiting for surgery.

The key turned more smoothly than the first one had. Inside were rejected matrices, old galleys tied with string, two bundles of correspondence, and a folded inventory sheet tucked behind the top shelf. No strike galley.

Ada photographed everything before movement. Dust patterns, shelf marks, labels, the old scratches she had noticed before. Then she unfolded the inventory sheet.

There it was in brown ink: Workers' trust circular, locked after dispute. Below, in a later hand, someone had written removed for conservation.

"By whom?" Livia asked, though she sounded as if she already feared the answer.

The initials beside the note were H.S.

No one spoke. Rain ticked against the high window. The clerk leaned closer and wrote the initials in her own log.

"Hugo Senn?" Theo said.

"Hugo Senn's foundation. Hannah Stowe, his consultant. Henry something. Initials are not proof," Ada said.

"They are a direction," Neris answered from the phone on speaker.

They searched the cabinet shelf by shelf. At the bottom, beneath a torn piece of felt, lay one loose type sort. Ada saw the letter before she lifted it: lowercase e, later body, lower-bowl wound. Under magnification, fresh black ink sat in the nick.

Theo stepped back.

Ada bagged it without speaking. The room had begun to name its visitor.

"Could it have fallen there during old use?" the clerk asked.

"The sort is old," Ada said. "The ink residue is not necessarily old. We will test."

Livia photographed the empty shelf where the galley should have been. Her hands shook once and steadied.

"I signed the restoration access," she said.

"We know."

"I want that in the record."

Ada looked at her. "It will be."

The inventory sheet carried more notes. Galleys moved to basement, 1954. Cabinet repaired, 1968. Trust circular retained. Removed for conservation, no date, H.S. The lack of date mattered almost as much as the initials. Legitimate movement creates boring completeness. Illicit movement prefers the useful blur.

Theo crouched before the open cabinet after everything had been removed and logged. "They came in here."

"Someone did."

"They opened the cabinet, took the galley, used the damaged sort, and left it like sweepings."

"Maybe."

"You won't say it."

"Not yet."

He stood, angry and grateful for the restraint in equal measure. "Then what do you say?"

"I say we have a missing galley, an inventory note, initials, a loose e from the cracked matrix, fresh ink residue, and a custody path through the restoration."

"That is a long way to say theft."

"It is a shorter way to prove it."

The clerk sealed the cabinet after inspection. The tamper tape looked obscene against the old oak, but necessary. As they left, Ada turned back. Cabinet six stood open no longer. Its emptiness had become evidence, and its evidence was more precise than outrage.

Theo stayed behind after the clerk left. He ran his palm near the tape without touching it. "I used to think locked meant safe," he said. Ada packed the evidence forms into her case. "Locked means accountable if the key is known." He looked at the desk where the second key had been hidden for years. "And if it is not?" "Then locked means hopeful." He gave a short, bitter laugh. The building had lived too long on hope. Now it would have to survive paperwork.

Chapter 26: Hugo's Receipt

Neris found the receipt in estate correspondence because one heir had printed every email for tax purposes and kept them in a shoebox with hospital bills. She called Ada from her car, engine running, voice tight with the pleasure of a useful fact.

"Hugo's foundation paid for private restoration of 'foundry paper object and proofing materials' six months before the proof appeared."

"Read it exactly."

Neris read the line twice. The invoice was from Marlo Venn. Services included deacidification, humidification and flattening, edge stabilization, visual integration, and trial pulls.

"Trial pulls matter," Ada said.

"I thought they might."

"They mean someone printed experiments."

"He will say preservation."

"Then he preserved the wrong things in secret."

They met at Neris's office an hour later. The receipt was creased, coffee-stained, and beautiful in the way ugly documents become beautiful when they answer a question. Attached emails showed Hugo's foundation coordinator arranging payment outside the museum's formal conservation budget. The subject line read BVP trust materials, confidential review.

"Why would estate correspondence have it?" Ada asked.

"Hugo's team sent selected expenses to heirs to show diligence before the option agreement. They wanted everyone impressed by how much care he was taking."

"Care is proud until it becomes evidence."

Neris smiled. "That one I may steal."

"Don't."

They drafted a request for Marlo Venn's complete records: treatment notes, material lists, photographs, correspondence, transport receipts, and any trial pulls or discarded sheets. Hugo's lawyer objected within a day, arguing that private conservation materials were irrelevant to the authenticity of the discovered proof.

Judge Quill held a short conference by video. Her face on the screen was calm enough to unsettle everyone.

"Authenticity cannot be severed from custody," she said. "Produce the records."

Ada wrote the sentence in her notebook and underlined custody once.

Custody was the word. Who held the tools, paper, type, ink, and story? Who had access to the cabinet, the damaged matrix, the missing galley, the old paper, the modern ink, the restorer's bath, the museum's hunger, the heirs' need? The proof sheet had traveled through hands with motive, but motive alone could not print a page. The receipt showed a workshop forming around the lie.

When Marlo's partial records arrived, they were worse for being incomplete. There were photographs of brown paper, a cropped image of a waste sheet, and notes about test impressions from historic type. There was no full image of the missing wrapper. No retained offcut. No material list sufficient for Owen. No explanation for the lower-edge trim beyond "regularized for stability."

Neris read the packet with rising color. "This is evasive."

"Yes."

"Can we compel more?"

"Ask Iman and Owen to list what is missing. Specific gaps are harder to dismiss than outrage."

Neris looked at Ada over the pages. "Do you ever allow yourself outrage?"

"After it becomes useful."

That evening, Ada reviewed the cropped waste-sheet image from Marlo's file. It showed a partial line, over-inked, with the same shortened fi ligature and a wounded e. The photograph had been taken on a worktable, beside a jar whose label was cut off. Ada saved the file, logged its metadata, and sent it to Iman.

The proof was no longer a mysterious arrival. It had a receipt, a treatment path, trial pulls, and a widening trail of omissions. It had traveled not from 1889, but through a modern process built to make 1889 appear obedient.

Ada pinned a copy of the invoice to the evidence wall between the paper bath notes and the cabinet inventory. The wall had begun to look less like a mystery and more like a schedule: request, remove, treat, test, display, litigate. Neris stood beside her and crossed her arms. "This is planning," she said. "Yes." "Planning feels colder than fraud." Ada looked at the invoice total, the neat description, the paid stamp. "Fraud is often administrative before it is dramatic."

That evening, Marlo Venn's assistant returned Ada's call by mistake, thinking she was speaking to museum staff. The conversation lasted less than a minute, but it confirmed that trial pulls had been stored separately from treatment photographs. Ada did not press. She wrote down the time, the number, and the assistant's exact words, then sent them to Neris. Accidents had rules too. If a door opened by mistake, one still documented the light.

Chapter 27: The Founder Mark

Theo showed Ada the founder's mark on a morning when the pressworks smelled of wet brick and hot dust from a space heater doing its best near the office. He had slept badly. Ada could tell because he had become more precise, not less.

"I should have thought of this sooner," he said.

"That sentence rarely helps."

"It annoys me into helping."

He placed three sorts on black cloth under the microscope. The first was an early confirmed 1880s cast. The second was a later cast from after the repair period. The third came from the mixed case that matched the proof's face. On the shoulder of the later type, not on the printing face itself, were two tiny notches, one high and one low. They were invisible in print unless pressure bit too deep or the paper was soft enough to catch a blind impression from the shoulder.

"Founder mark," Theo said. "Inventory control after a theft in 1899. Not every sort. Enough to identify foundry stock."

"Why didn't you mention it?"

"Because sane printing doesn't show it."

Ada brought out the proof photographs. Under ordinary light, nothing. Under raking light at high enlargement, faint blind marks appeared beside several letters, not inked but pressed into the paper at the edge of the character body. Two tiny notches. One high. One low.

"There," Theo said, and his voice changed.

They compared the genuine 1889 strike circular. No founder marks. Not even under aggressive light. The early sorts had not carried them. The disputed proof had.

"When exactly after the theft?" Ada asked.

"Records say late 1899. Implemented more widely in 1900."

Ada added it to the chart. The proof was not merely later than claimed. It was later in the exact ways a knowledgeable forger might miss if studying printed pages instead of metal bodies. The type itself had testified from the side.

Theo seemed angry at the elegance of it. "They knew enough to choose old type, enough to use the cracked e, enough to tone paper. But they didn't know the shoulder."

"Most people don't."

"Most people aren't trying to steal a trust."

"No."

They made controlled impressions with early and later sorts, changing pressure until the shoulder marks appeared. Rafe came by to confirm how excessive packing or uneven pressure might cause the body to kiss the paper. He did not enjoy being useful.

"A careful printer would avoid this," he said.

"A careful printer making a document look old might increase pressure for a strong impression," Ada said.

"A careful printer would know better."

"A knowledgeable amateur might not."

Rafe considered that. "Or a professional told to make it look convincing for a camera."

Theo looked at him. "You think Marlo printed it?"

"I think restorers love surfaces. Printers love consequences."

"That is almost useful."

"From you, I'll treasure it."

Ada let them argue softly while she photographed the blind marks. The room had begun to work as a team without admitting it. Theo knew the metal. Rafe knew pressure. Iman knew ink. Owen knew paper. Celia knew wages. Livia knew the institution's failures. Neris knew how to put their separate truths before a judge.

The founder marks were small enough that Hugo's lawyer would call them speculative. Ada prepared for that by building a simple demonstration: marked sort, unmarked sort, controlled pressure, resulting blind impression, disputed proof. No speech about heritage. No indignation. Just side-by-side evidence.

As they packed up, Theo held one marked sort in his palm. "They marked type because someone stole from the foundry in 1899."

"Yes."

"And the mark catches another theft now."

Ada said nothing. The thought was too tidy, and she distrusted tidy thoughts. Still, she wrote the date carefully. Sometimes the past had the decency to leave a notch.

Rafe arrived as Theo was returning the sort to its tray. He listened to the explanation and shook his head. "A thief caught by an anti-theft mark from a different thief." Theo looked offended by the neatness. "Do not make it charming." Rafe lifted both hands. "Nothing charming. Just efficient." Ada photographed the tray one last time. Efficiency was not justice, but it had its uses.

Chapter 28: Court Sample

Judge Quill ordered comparative printing in a controlled room after Hugo's expert suggested that Ada's demonstrations were "interesting but informal." The controlled room turned out to be a municipal records annex with fluorescent lights, bad coffee, and a loading dock large enough for Rafe's portable proofing press. The absurdity of a nineteenth-century printing problem being argued beside file cabinets labeled PARKING APPEALS did not make the order less useful.

Rafe would print. Ada, Theo, Iman, Owen, Neris, Hugo's expert, two attorneys, and the court clerk would observe. Livia attended as museum representative and looked as if she had not forgiven herself, which Ada considered appropriate but not sufficient.

The clerk read the protocol aloud. Three pulls: confirmed early sorts, later damaged e with associated recut material, and a mixed set matching the disputed proof as closely as possible. Same paper where permitted, comparable paper where not. Ink selected in two formulations. Pressure recorded. Packing recorded. All sheets photographed, labeled, and sealed.

Lawyers argued over roller pressure for twenty minutes.

"If the original was forged," Hugo's expert said, "we cannot assume the forger used this pressure."

"We are not assuming," Ada said. "We are bracketing."

"That's convenient."

"It is experimental method."

Judge Quill, attending by video for the beginning, looked over her glasses. "Proceed."

Rafe worked without humor. The first pull, from genuine early sorts, showed softer shoulders and no damaged e, no ligature, no founder marks. The second, using the later cracked e and associated recut material, produced the wound and sharper edge behavior. The third, the mixed set, aligned with the proof in damage, spacing, impression depth, and faint shoulder marks when pressure increased.

Owen examined paper behavior. Iman took ink samples. Theo confirmed sorts before and after printing. Ada photographed every stage, then stood back while Hugo's expert examined the sheets.

"Suggestive," he said at last.

Ada agreed. "Suggestive is what evidence does before accumulation makes it decisive."

Judge Quill wrote that down. Ada regretted the elegance of it immediately. Elegant sentences made people trust the speaker when they should trust the evidence.

Neris saw her expression and whispered, "Too late."

The court sample did not reproduce the forgery perfectly. It should not have. Perfect reproduction would have been suspicious in its own way. What it showed was more useful: the disputed proof could be made by a modern hand using old Bell & Vale materials from later than 1889, modern ink, treated paper, and pressure choices that revealed founder marks. The genuine early materials did not produce the disputed features.

Hugo's lawyer tried one more line. "Mr. Bell, you have demonstrated skill sufficient to create a deceptive document."

Rafe wiped ink from his hand. "I have demonstrated that skill leaves evidence."

"That was not my question."

"It was my answer."

The clerk sealed the samples. Ada watched each sheet enter its envelope. Controlled work had a calming effect on her. It converted argument into sequence. Even when people disagreed, the paper on the table had happened in front of them all.

Outside the annex, Rafe loaded his press into a borrowed van. Theo helped without being asked. For once, neither man spoke. Ada stood with Neris under a concrete overhang while rain began again.

"Will this carry?" Neris asked.

"With the ink, paper, type, and custody, yes."

"That sounded almost certain."

"Do not become greedy."

Neris laughed, tired and brief. The case had not ended, but the burden had shifted. The proof sheet no longer stood alone in a glow of museum light. It stood beside samples that showed how a lie could be printed and how it could fail.

The clerk came out carrying the sealed envelopes in a plastic court bin, absurdly ordinary for the trouble they contained. Everyone watched the bin go into the records van. Ada liked that too. Evidence should travel without glamour. Rafe closed his van doors and rubbed ink from his wrist. "Next time someone asks for historic authenticity," he said, "I may send them to court." Theo answered, "Court would over-ink it." The joke was thin, but it let them breathe.

Chapter 29: Celia's Testimony

Celia testified with the wage book open before her and a magnifying strip tucked beside the spine. She had refused Neris's suggestion to use enlarged exhibits only.

"If they want to discuss the book," Celia had said, "the book can attend."

The hearing room was smaller than Ada expected, with pale walls, a clock that clicked too loudly, and microphones that made everyone sound both nearer and less human. Judge Quill sat with the calm attention of a person who did not confuse silence with passivity. Hugo sat behind his lawyers, hands folded, face composed. Livia sat near the back. Theo took a seat beside Rafe. Iman and Owen waited in the hallway until called.

Neris began simply. "What is this book?"

"Wage ledger for Bell & Vale workers, 1888 through 1891," Celia said.

"How did it come into your archive?"

Celia gave the chain: Mara Voss, her niece, the union hall, the archive. She did not embellish. She did not need to. Then she read entries from after the alleged sale date: payments authorized by trustees, strike committee meetings, arrears negotiated under the trust clause.

"The trust was the workers' shield," she said. "They argued under it, struck under it, and returned under it."

Hugo's lawyer rose for cross-examination with the mournful air of a man forced to be reasonable in public.

"Ms. Ward, you are emotionally invested in this matter, are you not?"

"Yes."

"You oppose development of the Bell & Vale site?"

"I oppose lies about it."

"Please answer the question."

"That was my answer."

He tried again. "Your archive benefits if the trust survives."

"The archive benefits if records are read correctly."

"You have strong feelings about labor history."

"Yes," Celia said. "That is why I kept the receipts."

The judge allowed the answer. Something in the room shifted. Not dramatically. No one gasped. But people leaned differently. The proof sheet had tried to turn labor into a technicality. Celia returned names, rates, and dates to the table.

Ada watched Hugo during the testimony. He did not look bored. He looked irritated by irrelevance, which told her he understood the relevance perfectly. The wage book did not prove the proof sheet false by itself. It made the sale story socially and administratively absurd. It showed a trust still functioning in the months when Hugo needed it dead.

On redirect, Neris asked Celia to explain the strike circular.

Celia held it by its edges. "This was printed so workers knew what they were owed and what protection they had. It is not sentimental. It is accounting with a spine."

Ada nearly wrote that down and then did not. Celia's sentences could defend themselves.

After testimony, Celia stepped into the hallway and sat on a bench. Ada joined her.

"Did I talk too much?" Celia asked.

"No."

"Did I sound angry?"

"Yes."

"Bad?"

"Useful."

Celia looked toward the closed hearing room door. "They always want anger to disqualify the person who remembers the debt."

"The ledger helped."

"The ledger is angrier than I am."

Ada thought of the columns, the small amounts, the repeated arrears. History gained weight when wages were counted. Not enough to crush a lie by itself, perhaps, but enough to make the lie sweat.

When Celia returned to the room, she carried the wage book herself. The clerk offered to help. Celia said, "It has been heavier." Ada watched Hugo's lawyer glance at the book as if it were no longer merely an exhibit but an adversary with a spine. That was the day's quiet success. The workers had not become symbols. They had remained creditors, committee members, injured hands, names written beside sums due. The proof sheet's polished paragraph looked thinner after that.

During the lunch break, Celia showed Ada a loose receipt tucked into the wage book's back pocket. Coal delivery, November 1889, paid from the trust account. "Not central," Celia said. "But useful." Ada photographed it anyway. Cases were built from central facts and peripheral stubbornness. A trust that bought coal after its alleged sale was not a dead instrument. It was cold weather, a furnace, an invoice, and someone authorized to pay.

Chapter 30: Mott's Furnace

Theo fired the furnace for the court sample on a cold morning that made everyone grateful for heat even before the metal began to glow. The firing had been authorized under strict limits. Fire marshal notice. Insurance rider. Court observation. Ventilation check. Theo complained about every requirement and complied with all of them.

"Bureaucracy is how people who fear work prove they exist," he said while checking the crucible.

"Bureaucracy is also why you are not burning down an evidentiary site," Ada said.

"Two things can be true."

He melted period alloy and later alloy in separate pots, using documented recipes reconstructed from the ledger and known Bell & Vale practice. Ada had insisted on metallurgical oversight, so a materials engineer from the university stood beside Iman and looked delighted to be somewhere less sterile than his lab.

The furnace changed the building. The pressworks, usually cold and damp, gathered heat in its bricks. Old smells woke: metal, soot, oil, a faint sweetness from hot dust. Theo moved differently near the furnace, less like a caretaker and more like a worker entering conversation with a dangerous friend.

He cast sorts from each mixture. Early alloy first. Later alloy second. Each batch was labeled, cooled, measured, and photographed. Rafe printed test lines under controlled pressure. The early mixture rounded faster under repeated impression. The later mixture held edge. Under magnification, the disputed proof matched the later edge behavior far more closely than the reconstructed 1889 mix.

"Metal has memory," Theo said, forgetting himself.

Hugo's expert, who had arrived in a tailored coat inappropriate for furnace work, objected immediately. "Metaphor is not analysis."

Theo looked at him. Sweat had darkened the collar of his shirt. "Metal has measurable hardness."

That survived.

The materials engineer provided numbers: hardness values, deformation under pressure, edge retention after repeated pulls. Ada included them in her report. The alloy evidence did not need romance. It had measurements, dates, and printed consequences.

During a break, Hugo approached the furnace rail and looked around as if evaluating square footage. "Impressive," he said.

Theo did not answer.

"A public demonstration program could draw visitors."

"The trust board can consider that," Neris said.

"If there is a viable trust board."

Ada heard the conditional and wrote it down.

When the furnace cooled, the room lost heat slowly. Theo stood beside it as if beside an old colleague who had performed well under questioning. Ada packed the test sheets and samples while he shut valves and checked the floor for stray metal.

"It worked," he said.

"The test?"

"The building."

For a few hours, Bell & Vale had not been a rendering, a lawsuit, a museum object, or a liability schedule. It had been a foundry again, making letters in defense of its own record. Ada distrusted sentiment, but she did not refuse the fact. Use had returned briefly, and use revealed what display could not.

At the door, Theo turned off the last light. The furnace ticked as it cooled in the dark, patient and articulate in a language of contraction Ada did not need to translate.

Outside, Hugo's expert removed soot from his cuff with visible disgust. Theo noticed and smiled for the first time all day. "Metal is inconsiderate," he said. The expert pretended not to hear. Ada marked the reaction in no report, but it clarified the divide. One side wanted an old furnace picturesque and harmless. The other knew it as heat, risk, labor, and measurable result. In that difference, the whole case seemed briefly compressed.

Theo saved the rejected casts in a tray for the court. "Failures explain the good ones," he said when the materials engineer asked why. Ada liked that enough to forgive the phrasing. The rejected sorts showed rounded shoulders, poor fill, brittle breaks, and one warped foot. They made the successful comparison less magical. Anyone could point to the tray and see that metal did not obey desire. It obeyed mixture, heat, pressure, and the hand that knew or failed to know them.

Chapter 31: The False Wear

Ada proved false wear by comparing ink breakage to paper abrasion, which sounded dull until the images sat side by side. She made a grid: genuine strike circular, Rafe's fresh control, a naturally worn proof from the museum's teaching collection, and the disputed sheet. Each image showed the same problem from a different angle. On a genuinely handled proof, wear interrupts ink and paper together. Folds break both. Fingers soil both. Abrasion thins the paper and disturbs the ink because the two have aged in contact.

On the disputed sheet, the relationship failed. In some places paper had been abraded over intact ink, as if someone had scuffed the surface without understanding what the printed stroke should do beneath. Elsewhere, ink was scratched while the paper below stayed smooth. The wear did not grow from use. It had been applied.

Iman looked through the microscope and leaned back. "Distressed after printing."

"By someone with patience," Ada said.

"Patience is not honesty."

Owen joined by video, his face too close to the camera. He disliked remote examination and made sure everyone knew it. Still, he confirmed the edge abrasion pattern and the toning residues.

"Artificial age often flatters the viewer," he said. "Real damage is impolite."

Ada kept the useful part and ignored the rest.

The restorer's notes, received under court order in a second production, described "visual integration of age." That phrase could mean ethical repair or careful fraud. Context made it ugly. Beside it were photographs of the proof before treatment, cropped but not useless. In one image, the lower edge was untrimmed. In another, the surface looked paler before toning. A third showed test strips of paper drying on a rack. No inventory of materials. No retained samples. No explanation for trial pulls.

Neris read the notes in Ada's office. "Could Marlo claim this was standard restoration?"

"Some parts, maybe. Not all together. Not without documentation. Not with trial printing and missing associated materials."

"So we say the sheet was aged after printing."

"We say the wear pattern is inconsistent with natural aging and consistent with post-print distressing."

"I hate how useful your sentences are and how little they satisfy me."

"Good legal sentences rarely satisfy anyone."

Ada placed the notes beside Owen's paper bath results and Iman's ink integration images. The forged sheet had been aged like a prop. Useful objects accumulate time. Props receive it by instruction. She would not write that, but it helped her see the shape of the act. Someone had imagined how age should look to a hurried viewer: dark edges, softened paper, handsome ink, no inconvenient working marks. They had created an object that performed age rather than bore it.

Livia came by near evening to review the images. She stood quietly until Ada asked what she saw.

"It looks old where I wanted it to look old," Livia said.

"Yes."

"And wrong where I did not know to look."

"Also yes."

Ada expected tears or defense. Instead, Livia took notes. That was better. Remorse without new attention was just decoration.

They spent another hour marking the exhibit photographs. Livia suggested showing visitors a genuine worn fold beside the proof's staged abrasion. Ada approved. "People understand comparison," she said. Livia wrote that down, then stopped. "I should have understood it sooner." Ada capped her pen. "Sooner is gone. Better is available." It was as close to comfort as she could offer without lying.

Iman returned with a final micrograph near closing. The image showed a scuffed ink stroke beside an untouched fiber ridge. It looked like a landscape after a small fire. "This is the one," she said. "It will survive cross." Ada taped a copy to the exhibit draft and wrote no adjective beside it. The photograph did not need one. It showed injury applied without life behind it, wear without use, age without memory. That was the proof sheet's deepest flaw. It had been taught to look tired but not to have worked.

Chapter 32: Livia Breaks

Livia brought donor correspondence to Ada before the subpoena deadline in a blue folder with the museum's crest on the cover. Her hands did not shake in the doorway. They did not shake when she removed her coat or declined tea. They shook only when she set the folder down.

"I hid this because I thought delay would kill the museum," she said.

Ada did not open the folder yet. "Tell me what it is."

"Emails. Board notes. Messages from Hugo's foundation. Some were already produced. These were not."

"Why?"

"Because I moved them to a private folder when the announcement planning began. I told myself they were donor relations, not accession records."

"Were they?"

"No."

Ada opened the folder. The first email was from Hugo to Livia and two board members, sent months before the proof appeared: We need a proof that resolves the trust problem, not another interpretive fragment. The sentence sat there with the bluntness of a man used to having desire converted into planning.

Later messages discussed announcement timing, donor dinner staging, city outreach, and the need to avoid "premature technical objections." One exchange referred to associated materials as "visual clutter." Another praised Marlo Venn's ability to "make the piece legible to non-specialists."

"Did you know it was false?" Ada asked.

Livia looked at the window. "No. I knew I did not want to know quickly."

Ada disliked the answer and respected it for being exact.

"That distinction will matter less to other people," she said.

"I know."

"It may cost you your position."

"I know."

"Why bring it now?"

Livia's face tightened. "Because the museum has already been damaged by my fear. I would prefer not to finish the job."

Ada called Neris. Within an hour, they had scanned, logged, and produced the correspondence with a supplemental statement from Livia correcting her prior inventory and donor-communication disclosures. Neris's voice was controlled in the way that meant fury had been postponed until after filing.

"You understand opposing counsel will use this to attack the museum's entire chain," Neris told Livia.

"Yes."

"They will say the museum hid evidence because the proof is real and you fear embarrassment."

"The proof is not real."

"They will say it."

"Then I will answer."

The museum would suffer. It might also survive with a spine. Ada added the correspondence to the custody chain and marked Hugo's phrase separately. A proof that resolves the trust problem. Not a document. Not a record. A solution.

After Neris left, Livia remained in Ada's office. The rain had cleared, and the brick wall outside the window held a pale rectangle of late sun.

"Do you think institutions can apologize?" Livia asked.

"People can. Institutions can document, repair, and change policy."

"That's bleak."

"It is measurable."

Livia laughed once, without joy. "You would make even confession administrative."

"Confession without administration is theatre."

The folder lay between them. Ada did not soften the room. Livia had broken, but not into useless pieces. She had brought evidence. That was the only break that mattered now.

Before Livia left, she asked to sit for a minute without talking. Ada allowed it and sorted photographs at the other desk. Silence could be useful when it did not pretend to heal. At last Livia stood and put on her coat. "I am going to tell my registrar first," she said. "She warned me." "Then begin there." Livia nodded. Accountability, Ada thought, often started with the person whose advice had been inconvenient.

The registrar called Ada the next morning. Her name was June Bell, no relation, and she sounded angry enough to be reliable. "I told them associated material does not go out under donor review," she said. "Put that in your timeline." Ada did. June supplied message records, courier notes, and a copy of her unanswered objection. Livia's confession had opened another drawer. Inside was not innocence, but resistance that had been ignored. Ada added June's warning to the custody chain. Institutions failed in layers; sometimes so did their repair.

Chapter 33: The Substitute Sort

The loose lowercase e from cabinet six matched the proof and Rafe's controlled pull. That was the visual finding. Iman made it more dangerous. Under ultraviolet light and chemical comparison, ink residue lodged in the nick matched the disputed sheet's formulation closely enough to link them within the limits of material analysis.

"Say that again without making a lawyer faint," Neris said.

Iman did not look amused. "The residue is consistent with the ink used on the proof and inconsistent with the inks sampled from Mr. Bell's shop."

"Thank you."

"It is not a fingerprint. Do not call it one."

"I had begun to care for my career, so I won't."

Rafe's shop used a different black. His modern inks had their own additives, none matching the proof's specific package. That mattered when Hugo's team began pointing toward him more directly. Rafe could print a similar lie, but this lie had touched a different ink and, likely, a different press.

Theo identified the proofing press from pressure marks. He did it reluctantly, after Ada asked him to look at impression width and roller behavior in the disputed sheet and trial images from Marlo's records.

"Not Rafe's Vandercook," he said. "Different bed wear. See this faint pressure falloff? There was a small Vandercook in the foundry basement. We used it for demonstrations before the roof leak."

"Where is it?"

Theo checked the basement inventory. The line read: proofing press, temporary off-site storage during restoration.

"Where is it now?" Ada asked.

No one answered that day. Livia did not have a return receipt. Theo remembered it being wrapped in plastic and moved by foundation contractors. Marlo's notes referred to "client press" but gave no address.

Neris found the storage invoice in the same estate packet that had yielded the restoration receipt. Hugo's foundation had paid for a climate-controlled unit outside the city. Description: BVP equipment and interpretive materials.

The substitute sort had led them to machinery.

Judge Quill authorized inspection. The storage facility smelled of concrete, cardboard, and disinfectant. The manager produced access logs showing visits by Marlo Venn, Hugo's consultant Hannah Stowe, and one contractor whose signature was illegible. The small Vandercook stood in unit 114 behind wrapped display panels and crates of salvaged brick.

Rafe examined it under observation. "This press could have made the trial pulls."

"Could?" Neris asked.

"Could. I can compare pressure pattern."

He inked nothing. He only measured, photographed, and showed Ada worn spots on the bed. Theo found a dried fleck of black near the roller hook. Iman collected it. The fleck was tiny, but by then Ada had learned not to despise small things.

Hugo's lawyer called the inspection speculative. Ada called it sequential. Cabinet, sort, ink, missing press, storage invoice, access logs. None of it leapt. It walked.

As they left the storage facility, Rafe stood beside his van and looked back at the unit. "They used an old press to make a new lie, then stored it like furniture."

"Maybe."

"Still won't say it?"

"Soon."

The word surprised them both. Ada did not withdraw it. The case had entered the narrow part, where caution no longer meant refusing shape. It meant naming each step so the shape could hold.

At the facility office, the manager printed the access logs on thin paper that curled as it cooled. Ada watched each page come out. Dates, times, signatures, key codes. No poetry, no memory, no righteous anger. Just entries made because a gate had opened. She placed them beside the storage invoice and felt the sequence tighten. A lie could be eloquent on paper, but it still needed doors, carts, locks, and people willing to sign badly.

One signature appeared three times as a slanting line that might have been Stowe or Stone. Neris wanted handwriting analysis. Ada wanted the key-code records first. The manager produced them after a call to the regional office and a complaint about privacy that ended when Neris read the court order aloud. The code belonged to Hannah Stowe. Not proof of printing. Proof of access. Ada wrote the distinction in the margin because someone would try to blur it later. Access did not convict; it narrowed the hallway.

Chapter 34: Estate Pressure

Neris admitted the heirs wanted certainty because certainty paid. She did it after a long hearing day, in a diner near the courthouse where the coffee was severe and the waitress called everyone honey with no affection at all.

"Some descendants have medical bills," Neris said. "Some have debts. One lost a business during the winter shutdown. One is raising two grandchildren. Ordinary needs. Hugo's offer turned a murky trust into checks."

Ada stirred coffee she did not intend to drink. "Need can make people welcome a convenient document."

"Yes."

"Did you?"

Neris looked across the table. For a moment she seemed younger, or only more tired. "For a week."

Ada did not judge her aloud. A forged proof worked because it offered relief. It let people stop arguing with old obligations. It made fatigue feel like prudence. Everyone in the case had been offered something: money, funding, clarity, professional triumph, a saved building without the trouble of saving its purpose.

"Will the heirs fight us?" Ada asked.

"Some will. Some will thank us later. Some never."

"Does that change your strategy?"

"It changes my sleep."

The trust had protected workers from owners. Now it protected memory from heirs, developers, curators, and fatigue. Law often looked cold because it had to outlast appetite. Ada disliked that thought for being too balanced, but the diner had encouraged bleak fairness.

Neris opened a folder and showed Ada letters from heirs. One accused the trust board of stealing family wealth. One begged for a settlement before surgery. One remembered a grandmother who sorted type and said the yard should never become luxury housing. Another said he did not care what happened to the building if his children could pay rent.

"They are not villains," Neris said.

"I know."

"Hugo is easier if they are."

"Most cases are easier with better villains."

"Do we have one?"

Ada thought of the folder in her office, the receipt, the missing galley, the storage unit, the phrase resolves the trust problem. "We have conduct."

"That is your answer to everything."

"It is the only part I can prove."

Outside, courthouse workers crossed the street under umbrellas. The city went on spending money, needing roofs, wanting jobs, making compromises with yesterday because tomorrow had invoices due.

Neris closed the folder. "If the proof falls, the trust survives. Then we still have a broke building, divided heirs, and a city impatient with complexity."

"Truth does not repair roofs."

"You told Hugo lies don't either."

"Both remain true."

Neris gave a tired laugh. "You are expensive for someone who offers so little comfort."

"Comfort is extra."

They paid in cash. On the sidewalk, Neris turned toward the courthouse to file another motion, and Ada turned toward the pressworks. The case was no longer only about whether a document was false. It was about whether false clarity could be resisted long enough for a harder, poorer, more honest future to be built.

Ada paused before crossing the street and watched a delivery driver argue with a loading dock clerk over six missing boxes. Both held papers. Both pointed at signatures. The quarrel was ordinary and therefore instructive. Documents did not settle life by existing. They entered life and were tested by need, memory, and power. The proof sheet had tried to end argument. The better records had forced argument to become specific.

At the pressworks that evening, one heir waited by the gate. His name was Martin Vale, and his coat was too thin for the weather. "My mother sorted type here after the war," he said. "I still need the money." Ada had no answer that would not insult him. "I know," she said. He nodded toward the dark building. "If it stays, it better be more than a monument." "It has to be," Ada said. He left without shaking her hand. She respected that. Anger honestly placed was better than gratitude demanded.

Chapter 35: Iman's Chart

Iman's final chart was devastating because it was dull. Columns, peaks, dates, components, confidence limits. No adjectives. No colored arrows except those required for legibility. She had prepared a demonstrative exhibit that looked like a tax schedule for molecules, and Ada loved it for that.

In the hearing room, Hugo's expert suggested contamination from conservation. He did it skillfully, without sounding desperate. Conservation treatments could introduce modern materials. Handling could transfer residues. Storage environments were complicated. The proof had passed through multiple hands before formal accession. All true enough to deserve an answer.

Iman stood beside the screen and pointed to a cross-section image. "The modern components are present beneath impressed fibers within the printed stroke. They were present at printing."

"Could pressure have driven later contamination inward?" Hugo's lawyer asked.

"Not in this pattern. And not selectively within ink bodies while excluding adjacent unprinted fibers."

"You cannot give a printing date."

"Correct."

"You cannot identify the printer."

"Correct."

"You cannot identify the exact workshop."

"Correct."

"So your conclusion is limited."

"All conclusions should be."

The judge looked down, perhaps to hide a reaction. Ada kept her face still.

Iman moved through the chart. Modern alkyd resin. Cobalt drier. Synthetic component consistent with materials introduced long after the claimed date. Distribution inconsistent with surface contamination. Comparison with Rafe's ink negative. Comparison with residue from the loose sort positive within stated limits. The room understood, not because the chemistry was simple, but because Iman refused to overstate it.

Ada watched Hugo stop taking notes.

Modern ink, later type, later paper, artificial aging, false custody, hidden restoration, financial motive. The proof sheet had lost its alibi one layer at a time. It did not collapse dramatically. It became unable to stand.

Judge Quill asked for a plain summary from Ada after the technical testimony.

Ada rose. "The sheet was printed later than 1889 by someone using old tools incorrectly."

"Explain incorrectly," the judge said.

"The printer used Bell & Vale material, but from later periods: a damaged lowercase e associated with a post-1902 matrix, a ligature introduced after the claimed date, type bearing founder marks introduced after 1899, and alloy behavior inconsistent with the 1889 mixture. The paper bears a later watermark and treatment consistent with artificial aging. The ink formulation is modern and present at printing. The custody history shows access to the relevant tools and materials during private restoration."

"Could this be an innocent later reproduction mistaken for an original?"

"Not with the trimming, artificial aging, withheld associated materials, and presentation as an 1889 proof."

Hugo's lawyer objected to intent. Judge Quill sustained in part. Ada accepted it. She had not meant intent as confession. She meant behavior.

After the hearing, Iman packed her charts with the care of a woman who trusted paper when arranged correctly.

"You did well," Ada said.

"I was accurate."

"That is what I meant."

"Then say that."

Ada nodded. Outside the room, Neris was already fielding calls. Livia sat alone on a bench, staring at her hands. Theo and Rafe stood near the window, not speaking. The case had become public enough to wound everyone differently.

Ada looked back through the hearing room door at the empty witness table. Evidence was work, not revelation. Today the work had held.

Neris came out last, carrying the exhibit binder against her chest. "I used to think expert testimony was persuasion," she said. "It is." "No. Today it felt like refusing to be hurried." Ada considered that and found no reason to disagree. Iman's chart had slowed the room. It had made everyone walk through each component, each limit, each consequence. Slowness, properly used, could be a blade.

Iman joined them near the elevator and corrected one of Neris's notes before saying hello. "Do not write cobalt equals modern. Write cobalt drying package in this formulation." Neris changed it on the spot. Ada watched the exchange with satisfaction. The case was becoming harder to simplify badly because the people carrying it had begun to hear the shape of precision. That was rare. Most rooms wanted certainty shaved smooth. This room, for the moment, tolerated edges.

Chapter 36: Rafe Accused

Hugo accused Rafe through implication first, which was the civilized way to throw mud in a hearing room. His lawyer asked about family resentment, access to old type, financial pressure on independent printers, and Rafe's admitted ability to produce a convincing document using historic materials. Each question was shaped like curiosity and aimed like a nail.

Rafe stood very still at the witness table. He had worn a dark jacket over a white shirt, and there was no ink under his nails that day. Ada wondered if he had scrubbed his hands raw.

"You dislike Mr. Senn's development plan," Hugo's lawyer said.

"Yes."

"You have publicly criticized it."

"Yes."

"You possess old Bell & Vale type."

"Some."

"You demonstrated that you can print a document resembling the disputed proof."

"Under observation, to show method."

"And before that demonstration, you could have done so privately."

Neris objected. The judge allowed the question in limited form. Rafe answered, "I could have printed many things. I did not print that."

The direct accusation came after lunch, when Hugo's expert suggested that an independent printer with access, motive, and skill was a more likely source than a development foundation employing professionals for preservation work. The sentence was polished. It left Rafe's name hanging without saying it until Hugo's lawyer did.

"Mr. Bell is that printer, is he not?"

Rafe looked at Ada once, then back at the judge.

Ada answered with evidence when recalled. Rafe's ink did not match. His press pressure did not match. His paper stock did not match. His possession records were open. His shop logs showed no relevant commission. The loose e's residue matched the proof, not his materials. The storage invoice pointed to Hugo's foundation. Access logs placed Marlo Venn and Hugo's consultant near the press that could have made the trial pulls.

"Skill is not guilt," Ada said.

Hugo's lawyer objected. Judge Quill allowed the phrase as a summary of the evidentiary distinction and told Ada to keep to facts. She did.

The accusation failed, but it left a mark. That was how desperate arguments worked. They tried to stain before collapsing. Rafe's face remained controlled, but Theo's did not. Celia, sitting behind them, looked ready to stand and say something that would harm no one but procedure.

Afterward, in the courthouse hallway, Rafe said, "Thank you."

"The evidence did it."

"You arranged it so people could see."

That was fair, though Ada did not enjoy the credit. Evidence unseen was often indistinguishable from evidence unfound. Arrangement mattered. Sequence mattered. The case against Rafe had relied on a simple story: the man with ink made the false paper. The answer had to be just as visible and more accurate.

Rafe leaned against the wall. "People will still wonder."

"Some."

"Because I could have."

"Yes."

"That is a miserable standard."

"It is not the court's standard."

"The street has lower requirements."

Ada had no comfort for that. His name would remain near the accusation in some minds, even after the evidence walked away from him. A false document damaged more than title. It spent suspicion freely.

Theo approached and, after an awkward pause, held out his hand. Rafe looked at it, then shook it.

"Your pressure settings are still too heavy," Theo said.

"Your cases are still a disgrace."

They both looked relieved to dislike each other normally again.

Rafe left by the side door to avoid the reporters. Ada saw him pause outside and look at his ink-clean hands as if they belonged to someone else. Accusation had a residue even when disproved. She made a note to include the negative ink comparison prominently, not buried in an appendix. Exonerating facts deserved architecture too.

Later, Rafe sent Ada a photograph of his shop window. Someone had taped a note to the glass: FORGER. He had removed it before customers arrived, but the adhesive remained in four pale corners. Ada forwarded the photograph to Neris, then sat with it longer than necessary. Falsehood spread unevenly. The forged proof had been technical, expensive, planned. The accusation against Rafe had become cheap paper on glass. Both needed answering, but not in the same way. One with charts. One with visible trust.

Chapter 37: The Old Galley

The old galley was found behind a false back in a storage crate assigned to Hugo's foundation. The crate was in the same unit as the Vandercook, labeled INTERPRETIVE PANELS, and would have been ignored if Theo had not objected to its weight.

"Panels don't sit like that," he said.

The facility manager frowned. "Sit?"

"Heavy at the bottom. Metal heavy. Not plywood heavy."

Neris called Judge Quill's clerk before anyone opened it further. They waited forty minutes in the concrete hallway beside unit 114, drinking bad vending-machine coffee while the crate sat under fluorescent light and seemed to grow more important by refusing to explain itself.

When authorization came, Ada photographed every side, every screw, every label. Rafe removed the false back with a screwdriver while Theo hovered and criticized his angle. Inside, wrapped in oilcloth and tied with darkened string, lay a galley.

Ada did not celebrate. She photographed before movement, logged position, and called the court clerk again. Only after the chain was established did Theo lift the oilcloth.

The workers' trust circular stood in metal, dusty but intact.

No one spoke for a long moment. The type filled the galley in tight lines, locked with furniture and quoins. The headline used the clean 1889 face. The spacing matched Celia's printed circular. There was no later fi ligature, no damaged e, no founder shoulder marks. Ink residue in the counters was old, dull, and consistent with the strike circular's formulation when Iman later tested it.

Theo read it with his hand hovering above the type. "They kept their words standing."

Celia arrived an hour later after Neris called her from the parking lot. She wore her coat over a flour-dusted sweater, having come directly from the archive above the bakery. When she saw the galley, she cried without apology.

"Mara Voss," she said.

"You don't know she touched this," Ada said gently.

"No. But she kept the paper. Let me have one generous thought."

Ada let her.

The genuine galley did not only disprove the forged proof. It restored the document the forgery had tried to replace. The false proof claimed surrender. The galley preserved resistance in the same material language the forger had abused. It was heavy enough that two people had to move it. That pleased Celia, who said some truths should inconvenience the arms.

Back at the pressworks, under court observation, Theo examined the lockup. He identified furniture marks, old quoins, and a damaged capital T noted in an 1889 repair list. Ada photographed every line. Iman took samples. Owen examined a paper proof pulled later under controlled conditions from the standing type.

Livia stood at the edge of the room, not touching anything. "It was in their storage."

"Yes," Ada said.

"We put it there by letting them move things."

"Yes."

Livia nodded as if accepting a sentence.

At the end of the day, the galley was placed in secure custody. Theo watched the clerk seal the transport crate.

"It waited better than we did," he said.

"Objects don't get tired."

"No. People do, and then they sell things."

Ada had no answer. The galley had waited in a crate mislabeled by people who needed it hidden or simply out of the way. Now it stood in the record, heavy, specific, and hard to trim.

Celia walked with Ada to the parking lot after the crate left. "I thought finding it would feel cleaner," she said. "It rarely does." "Because now we know it was taken." Ada nodded. Recovery carried its own injury. The galley had survived, but not untouched by the fact of being hidden. Celia wiped her face with the heel of her hand and straightened. "Then we make it useful." That was the archive's answer to grief.

At the pressworks, Theo made a space for the galley record in the cabinet inventory, writing the entry by hand before adding it to the digital sheet. "Why both?" Luis asked, already hovering though he had not yet become an apprentice officially. Theo said, "Because one system will fail when the other is needed." Ada approved. Redundancy was not romance. It was humility about future incompetence. The galley had been lost once in a room full of labels. It deserved more than one way back.

Chapter 38: Matrix Number

The forged sheet matched a recut matrix numbered BV-1902-R. Theo found the number after removing old tissue from the side, where someone had written a later catalog code over the original stamp. Under angled light, the older mark appeared: BV-1902-R, text roman lowercase e, recut after bowl flaw.

"R for recut?" Ada asked.

"Repair, likely. Bell & Vale used both. Naturally."

"Naturally."

Records showed the matrix had been removed for demonstration during Hugo's private restoration and returned with no sign-out initials. The removal entry had been made in pencil. The return entry used black pen. No date on the return. No condition note. The kind of small administrative fog that wrongdoing loved and neglect supplied.

Theo examined the matrix under higher magnification and found a modern mold scrape inside. Fresh, narrow, made by recent casting. The scrape cut across older oxidation and had not darkened. Someone had used the matrix recently enough for the metal to remember it plainly.

"Could your controlled cast have made this?" Hugo's expert asked during review.

Theo shook his head. "We photographed before casting. The scrape was present."

Ada had the photographs ready. Before. After. Scale visible. Lighting consistent. The proof was now tied to a specific matrix after a specific custody breach.

Neris smiled for the first time in days. "That sounds like court."

"It sounds like metal."

"Court likes metal when it is numbered."

Ada prepared the exhibit: matrix, printed control, proof enlargement, custody log, repair ticket, removal record, before-and-after scrape photographs. The chain was simple enough to survive attack. Forgeries fear simplicity. Complexity is where they rent space.

Hugo's lawyer tried to complicate it anyway. He suggested old scrapes, undocumented demonstrations, accidental use, mislabeled matrices, and the possibility that the disputed proof had been a later reproduction never intended as original. Ada answered each point in order. The scrape was visible before controlled testing and absent from older photographs. Demonstration use was undocumented during private restoration. The proof had been presented, labeled, and litigated as an 1889 document. The associated materials had been removed. The paper had been aged. The edge had been trimmed.

"You are building intent from materials," he said.

"I am building sequence from materials."

"And inviting the court to infer intent."

"The court may infer what the sequence supports."

Judge Quill looked at the exhibit for a long time. Numbered metal, printed mark, custody breach. Ada could see why Neris smiled. This was not romance, not expert aura, not a curator's regret. It was a labeled tool used after it should have been resting in a drawer.

After the hearing, Theo asked to see the matrix once more before it went back into evidence storage. The clerk allowed him to look without touching.

"You all right?" Ada asked.

"It is strange to be grateful to a damaged thing."

"Not that strange."

He considered the small metal rectangle under plastic. "Someone used it badly. It still told the truth."

Ada did not cross that sentence out in her notebook. She did not write it down at all. Some things were better left in the air, where they could not be turned into captions.

The clerk sealed the matrix in a padded evidence box. Theo signed the log more slowly than usual. "I keep thinking about the man who cracked it," he said. "Angry with himself, probably. Late order. Bad light." Ada closed her case. "He could not know this." "No." Theo looked toward the door. "That comforts me." It comforted Ada too, though she would not have used the word. Accidental traces could outlast deliberate stories.

Neris asked for the matrix number three times while drafting the exhibit list. BV-1902-R, Ada repeated, then wrote it on a card and placed it above Neris's keyboard. By midnight the number had become almost a character in the case, stubborn and unlovely. Hugo's team could argue about motives, memories, and museum embarrassment. They could not make that stamped sequence belong to 1889. Numbers were not pure. They could be copied, misread, forged. But this one sat in metal beside a repair ticket and a fresh scrape, refusing to become atmospheric.

Chapter 39: Hearing Room

Ada explained type evidence in plain terms because plain terms were harder to escape. She stood at the witness table with enlarged exhibits arranged in sequence and reminded herself not to perform. Letters are tools. Tools wear. Wear has sequence. The disputed proof used damage, alloy, spacing, ink, and paper that belong after 1889. It was not one anachronism. It was a cluster.

She showed the lowercase e first. Clean punch. Damaged matrix. 1902 repair ticket. Disputed proof. Controlled pull. The wound appeared where it should not have been if the document were truly from 1889.

Then the ligature. 1896 supplement. Disputed word. Absence from the strike circular.

Then the founder's mark. Unmarked early sort. Marked later sort. Blind impressions on the proof.

Then the ink chart. Iman had already testified, so Ada only connected the material finding to the printing sequence.

Then the old galley. Clean 1889 face, period spacing, genuine strike text, custody recovery from Hugo's foundation storage.

Judge Quill followed. The clerk followed. Even Hugo's lawyer stopped interrupting for a while, perhaps because interruption would have made him look less patient than a lowercase e.

"Could all these later features appear by accident?" the judge asked.

"Not together, not on one sheet, not with this custody history."

"Could the sheet be a later educational reproduction?"

"A later reproduction would not need artificial aging, a trimmed margin, missing associated materials, and presentation as an original sale proof."

"Could the museum's handling account for your concerns?"

"It accounts for some custody problems. It does not account for modern ink present at printing, later type features, or a later watermark."

Ada did not raise her voice. The evidence did not need rescue by volume.

Hugo's lawyer cross-examined with skill. He pressed her on uncertainty, on reliance on other experts, on whether she had a personal preference for preserving the foundry. She answered narrowly.

"You admire the building, don't you?"

"I understand its evidentiary value."

"That was not my question."

"I have no useful admiration to offer the court."

"Are you biased against development?"

"No."

"Against Mr. Senn?"

"I received an unsolicited consulting offer from him during this matter and disclosed it."

The lawyer moved on faster than Ada expected.

By late afternoon, the hearing room smelled of coffee, wool, and fatigue. The exhibits remained bright on their boards. The proof sheet, in its sleeve, lay on the table like an object that had lost the right to be mysterious.

When Ada stepped down, Celia touched her sleeve in the hallway. "You made them look at the letters."

"That was the assignment."

"Most people think looking is easy."

"Most people glance."

Celia nodded. "My predecessor would have liked you poorly."

"High praise."

It was nearly dark when Ada left the courthouse. She had spent the day saying simple things against expensive confusion. That kind of work never felt victorious. It felt like sweeping water away from a door while rain continued. Still, for that day, the threshold held.

On the steps, a reporter called her name and asked whether this was a win for preservation. Ada kept walking. The question was too broad to answer honestly in the space between two camera flashes. It was a win for a damaged e, for a wage book, for custody logs, for people who had noticed small things late but not too late. Preservation was only the name people gave the bundle afterward.

At home, she reheated soup and read the day's transcript while standing at the counter. Her own answers looked flatter on the page, which improved them. Spoken testimony always carried breath, impatience, and the body of the speaker. The transcript stripped those away and left structure. She marked two places where she had said likely and one where she should have said consistent with. The corrections would go to Neris in the morning. Even after a good day, precision had chores.

Chapter 40: Hugo's Workshop

Search records revealed the hidden workshop two days after Ada's testimony. Criminal review had begun quietly once Judge Quill referred the custody issues to the district attorney's office. Ada learned about the search from Neris, who called before dawn and sounded as if she had not slept.

"They found a rented workshop," Neris said. "Not the storage unit. Separate. Industrial block near the river."

"Whose lease?"

"Shell company tied to Hugo's foundation contractor."

The inventory arrived under seal, then partly entered the probate record because Hugo's counsel insisted rogue contractors had acted beyond authorization. Alongside a small proofing press were aged paper scraps, trial pulls, modern ink, toning materials, photographs of Bell & Vale matrices, a paper cutter, and practice sheets bearing fragments of the disputed declaration.

Hugo claimed rogue contractors. His notes were in the room.

They did not confess. They instructed. Older margin. Less ink. Damage visible but not obvious. Use trust language. Avoid union phrasing. The handwriting matched notes from the sales suite meeting. There were printed emails too, including one from Hugo asking for "a version that will withstand ordinary inspection." Ordinary inspection. Ada underlined the phrase once, then stopped before she tore the page.

The workshop gave the forged proof a birthplace. Birthplaces matter, even for lies.

At the supplemental hearing, the district attorney's investigator described the room. Concrete floor. Two worktables. Ventilation fan. Shelves with paper. A locked cabinet containing Bell & Vale photographs and photocopies of union materials. No dramatic lair. No theatrical villainy. Just a rented place where a false document had been improved through drafts.

Livia sat in the back of the hearing and covered her mouth.

Celia did not. She looked straight ahead.

Ada read the inventory with a tired anger. The forgery had not been impulsive. It had been managed. Someone had studied just enough history to abuse it. Someone had adjusted margins, ink, damage, and language until the page became persuasive to ordinary inspection. The phrase stayed with her because it named the intended victim: not experts, not even judges, but the tired public gaze. The glance.

Hugo's lawyer argued that notes lacked context. Perhaps they related to interpretive reproductions. Perhaps a contractor had prepared display copies. Perhaps the original proof had inspired educational material later mistaken for evidence by overzealous staff.

Judge Quill listened without expression. Then Neris placed the sequence against the argument: proof appears after private restoration; associated materials removed; paper altered; old galley hidden in foundation storage; damaged matrix used after custody breach; workshop contains trial pulls and instructions matching the final proof; Hugo's notes request a version to withstand ordinary inspection.

Context, once assembled, became less friendly to innocence.

After the hearing, Ada walked with Livia to the courthouse steps.

"I keep thinking of the donor dinner," Livia said. "People leaned over the case and said how moving it was."

"They were supposed to."

"I was supposed to."

"Yes."

Livia breathed in the cold air. "Ordinary inspection."

"We can teach better inspection."

"Can we?"

Ada looked back at the courthouse doors, then toward the city where Bell & Vale's roofline lay hidden among taller buildings. "Sometimes. Not always."

It was the only honest answer she had.

That night, Ada reread the workshop inventory at her kitchen table. Trial pull after trial pull. Small adjustments. A lie learning posture. The sequence disturbed her more than a single forged sheet ever could. It showed revision, and revision meant time to stop. Whoever made the proof had not stumbled into fraud. They had improved it. Ada closed the file and sat for a while with the lights off, listening to traffic move through wet streets.

When she turned the light back on, the inventory looked smaller but not less ugly. Paper scraps. Ink tins. Photographs. Notes. Ordinary nouns arranged into harm. Ada added one page to her working report before sleeping: a timeline of revision. First trial too dark. Second margin too wide. Third damage too obvious. The page mattered because it showed choice repeated. In court, people often hid behind confusion. Revision made confusion harder to claim. It was the workshop's most honest record.

Chapter 41: Trust Survives

Judge Quill blocked dissolution of the trust in an order that arrived at 9:03 on a gray morning. Neris sent it to Ada with no message, which was how Ada knew it mattered. The PDF opened slowly. The order was spare, eighteen pages without decorative outrage. The proof sheet is not authentic. The sale theory fails. The trust remains in force. Evidence relating to the creation and presentation of the false proof is referred for criminal review.

Ada read it once at her desk. Then again for the details. Judge Quill cited the damaged matrix, the ligature, the founder marks, the modern ink, the later watermark, the false wear, the custody gaps, the hidden galley, the workshop notes. She did not call anyone a villain. She did not need to. The facts had been arranged and allowed to stand.

Theo arrived at Ada's office before noon because he refused to read the order on a phone. He held the printed pages in both hands and stopped at the operative paragraph.

"Read it aloud," he said.

Ada did.

He took the pages from her and read the sentence twice.

"The building survives?"

"The trust survives," Ada said. "The building still needs work."

"That is always true."

His voice did not break. He folded the order carefully and put it in his inside pocket, as if carrying a letter from someone dead.

By evening, Hugo's renderings disappeared from the development website. The page that had shown clean brick and tiny happy people now displayed a bland notice about project review. The museum postponed its exhibit and issued a statement acknowledging serious failures in custody and interpretation. It was not as strong as Ada would have written, but it did not lie. That put it ahead of many statements.

Reporters called Ada, Iman, Owen, Celia, Rafe, Theo, Neris, Livia, and anyone else whose name had entered public record. Ada declined interviews. Iman sent a written technical comment and refused television. Owen agreed to speak only if the interviewer promised not to use the phrase old paper secrets. The promise was not made, so he hung up. Celia accepted one interview and brought the wage book.

That seemed right. Experts had their place. So did the people who kept the record warm.

At the pressworks, a small group gathered without planning to. Theo unlocked the gate. Celia arrived with pastries from the bakery below the archive. Rafe brought a stack of blank paper and said nothing about why. Livia came late, standing at the edge of the composing room until Celia handed her a roll and made room on the bench.

Neris read the order's final page aloud. The room listened. Rain ticked through the roof into the old bucket, impertinent and practical.

"What happens now?" Rafe asked.

"Governance hearing," Neris said. "Restoration plan. Heirs' settlement. Criminal process separate. Nothing quick."

"Good," Theo said.

They all looked at him.

"Quick brought us Hugo's paper."

Ada leaned against the imposing stone. She felt no triumph, only the familiar exhaustion that came after evidence had done its work and left people with consequences. The order had saved the trust from dissolution. It had not repaired the roof, reconciled the heirs, restored public confidence, or paid for furnace work. Truth had cleared the table. Work remained to be placed on it.

Celia raised a paper cup of coffee. "To the dead who kept receipts."

Rafe lifted his cup. Theo did too. Livia hesitated, then followed. Ada lifted nothing. She watched the furnace room door, the rows of cases, the repaired cabinet seal, the places where neglect and greed had met. Survival was not clean. It was a sentence with costs attached.

Still, the trust survived.

Before they left, Theo pinned a photocopy of the order to the office corkboard, then removed it because the corners curled. He found a frame in a storage closet and wiped the glass with his sleeve. "This is temporary," he said. No one believed him. Livia offered museum-grade mounting materials, and Theo told her not to make a shrine out of a court order. Celia said a shrine would have better minutes than most boards. The room laughed, not because anything was easy, but because the order had given them permission to argue about the next thing. That, Ada thought, was one sign of life.

Chapter 42: Restoration Plan

The restoration plan changed shape in a room that smelled of damp wool, marker ink, and old plaster. Neris borrowed a community college classroom because the trust board could not afford a hotel meeting room and because Hugo's absence had lowered everyone's tolerance for polished tables. The whiteboard at the front read BELL & VALE NEXT STEPS in Neris's square hand.

Instead of offices, the pressworks would become a public print shop, archive, and training foundry if the money could be found. Smaller money. Slower money. Honest money. It would not satisfy every heir. It would not make the city rich. It would not convert the furnace into decor. That, for the people gathered, counted in its favor.

Neris negotiated with the heirs in careful language. Licensing revenue from limited editions. Paid workshops. Museum partnership. Grants. A modest hardship fund from donations already pledged for "heritage interpretation," now redirected under independent oversight. Some heirs looked relieved. Some looked betrayed. One man in the back asked whether principles could pay his mortgage, and no one pretended the answer was simple.

Livia offered museum partnership without donor control: conservation support, exhibition space, shared cataloging, staff training, and a public correction of the previous accession failures. She had brought a written policy draft. Ada noticed the new custody procedures in the appendix and said nothing. Sometimes improvement should be allowed to arrive without applause.

Rafe proposed editions to fund repairs. "Plain work," he said. "No luxury nonsense. Print the order, the strike circular, maybe a specimen sheet explaining the type evidence."

"People will buy that?" an heir asked.

"People buy worse things every day."

Theo offered to teach if no one called him inspirational.

"We can avoid that," Ada said.

"Put it in writing."

The furnace needed relining. The roof needed more. The electrical system needed mercy and money. The cases needed cleaning. The basement needed drying. Insurance premiums would be cruel. Survival began as a list of costs, each one less romantic than the last.

Celia insisted on a public trust board with worker seats, neighborhood seats, and descendants represented without allowing any one group to sell the place quietly later.

"The old clause already says so," she said, tapping a photocopy of the trust deed. "Workers and their successors are not decorative. They govern."

"There are no Bell & Vale workers now," one heir said.

Theo lifted a hand. "There will be."

The room shifted. It was a small sentence, almost foolish. But the plan depended on it: adult apprentices, paid training, casting and printing work, archival labor treated as labor, public access that did not turn work into costume. The past had left instructions after all, though they required translation and invoices.

Ada stayed mostly silent. She had been hired to authenticate, not to design governance. Still, Neris asked her to review the evidentiary display plan, and Celia asked her to help write labels that would not turn the forgery into a glamorous crime story.

"No villain portraits," Celia said. "No genius forger. No miracle detective."

"Agreed."

"And no making workers into saints."

"Also agreed."

By the end of the meeting, the whiteboard was full: roof bids, board bylaws, grant deadlines, press assessment, archive digitization, furnace inspection, public opening, security, volunteer training, deaccession review. It looked impossible in the ordinary way of honest projects.

Outside, Theo stood under the classroom awning and lit a cigarette, then remembered the campus rule and put it out before smoking.

"You're learning governance," Ada said.

"I hate it."

"Then you are learning accurately."

He looked toward the city, where the foundry's roof was not visible. "A building is easier when it is only endangered."

"Than when it has to live?"

"Yes."

Ada understood. Rescue stories ended at the moment of rescue because maintenance made poor drama. But the trust had not been written for drama. It had been written to make maintenance someone’s duty.

Back inside, the heirs were still circling the coffee urn, talking in pairs that changed shape whenever Neris approached. Ada watched one older man ask Mina what typefounding paid. Mina answered plainly: not enough yet, but more than being sentimental in public. He laughed, then asked what training would cost. That was how the plan became real, not through the whiteboard's arrows but through awkward questions about wages, insurance, hours, rent, and who would unlock the door on winter mornings. The trust had survived as law. It would survive as schedules or not at all.

Chapter 43: Livia's Correction

Livia wrote the corrected label herself and brought it to Ada in three drafts. The first was too defensive. The second was too ashamed. The third named the proof sheet as a modern forgery made with historic type, modern ink, artificially aged paper, and access gained during private restoration. It also described the genuine galley, the workers' trust, and the museum's failure to maintain proper custody.

"This is the one," Ada said.

Livia looked surprised. "No cuts?"

"One."

"Of course."

Ada crossed out significant in the phrase significant lapse. "Lapse is already soft. Don't cushion it further."

Livia nodded and made the change.

The museum opened the case three weeks later, not as the triumphant exhibit once planned, but as a public correction. The case held the forged proof and the genuine galley, separated by enough space that neither object seemed to explain the other entirely. Enlargements showed the lowercase e, the ligature, the founder's mark, the ink cross-section, the trimmed margin. Another panel showed the wage book entries and the strike circular. The label did not hide the museum's error.

"Will people come to see a forgery?" Livia asked before the doors opened.

"People come to see a story."

"That is what got us here."

"Then tell a better one."

The first visitors were not many: a retired printer, two students, a local reporter, three members of the trust board, and a woman who said her great-aunt had worked in the sorting room. Celia stood near the wage book display, watching people with the sternness of a librarian guarding both fact and weather. Theo refused to stand near the galley because, he said, he would start correcting strangers' posture.

Ada watched visitors lean toward the lowercase e. Tiny damage had become public evidence. A man in a blue cap read the label twice, then called his wife over to show her the ligature. A student took notes on the difference between artificial wear and use wear. The reporter asked Livia whether the museum had been humiliated.

"Yes," Livia said. "And instructed."

Ada turned away before the answer could become too polished.

In the afternoon, a board member asked whether the exhibit should include more about Hugo. Livia said no. The criminal process would proceed elsewhere. The museum's job was not to turn him into a dark attraction, but to show how the object had been made persuasive and how it had been disproved.

"Ordinary inspection failed," Livia said. "We teach closer inspection."

Celia, passing behind her, said, "And better custody."

"And better custody," Livia repeated.

The genuine galley drew fewer immediate gasps than the forged proof. It was dark, dense, and hard for casual visitors to read. But those who stayed with it saw the lines emerge: the workers' demands, the trust language, the old spacing. One object showed deception. One showed resistance.

Museums could still do that: teach looking after failing to look. Ada did not forgive the failure because of the correction. Forgiveness was not a professional tool. But she recognized repair when it took a public form and accepted being measured.

As the museum closed, Livia stood before the case alone.

"I thought a clean discovery would save us," she said.

"Clean discoveries are rare."

"What saves us, then?"

"Dirty records. Good procedures. People willing to be embarrassed in writing."

Livia laughed softly. "I'll put that in the strategic plan."

"Please don't."

They left the gallery lights on low. In the case, the forged proof looked less powerful beside its own explanation. The lie had not vanished. Better. It had been made to work honestly at last, as evidence against itself.

Two days later, Livia sent Ada a visitor comment card. The handwriting was large and uneven: I came for the scandal and stayed for the letters. Ada left the card on her desk longer than necessary. It was not vindication. It was one person learning to look past the story sold at the entrance. Museums could not guarantee that for everyone. No institution could. But they could arrange objects so that attention had a chance. After everything, a chance seemed worth the labor.

Chapter 44: The Wage Book

Celia updated the public archive from the warm room above the bakery, where the scanner sat on a table reinforced with two crates of old union newsletters. She had obtained a small grant, rejected a large donor, and bullied three volunteers into learning file naming conventions.

"No spaces, no mysteries, no dates written like poetry," she told them.

Ada helped on Saturdays when she could. She had not intended to become part of the archive's practical life, but Celia had asked with the assumption of obedience, and Ada found herself arriving with coffee she kept downstairs until work was done.

The public archive would include digital images of the wage book, strike circular, hearing exhibits, trust order, corrected museum label, and selected photographs of type evidence. Celia refused a donor platform that wanted branding on the search page.

"No logos on wages," she said.

"They offered hosting," a volunteer said.

"They offered a leash with server space attached."

Ada wrote descriptions. Dates, names, rates, context. No flourish. She described Mara Voss as sorter, not heroine. She described the strike circular as a labor document, not a relic of courage. She described the forged proof as modern, not notorious. Celia approved most entries and returned several with comments in red pen: too clean, say who paid, where is source, do not call women assistants if the ledger calls them sorters.

The entries showed workers paid by the thousand ems, women sorting type in back rooms, founders burned by splashed metal, apprentices becoming masters slowly, trustees arguing over coal, widows collecting small stipends, men docked for damaged casts, a shop negotiating with its own idea of obligation. None of it was grand. That was its strength.

The forged proof had tried to reduce them to a sale clause.

The wage book did not shout back. It counted.

Counting, Ada thought, was one of the older forms of defiance.

One afternoon, a descendant came in with a photograph of a woman in a dark dress standing beside a crate of type. The woman was not smiling. Her name, written on the back, was Mara. Celia took the photograph as if accepting a witness.

"We can scan it," Ada said.

"We can ask permission first," Celia replied.

The descendant, a man in his seventies named Paul, sat at the table and looked at the wage book entry for Mara Voss. "She made twelve dollars that week?"

"Before deductions," Celia said.

"For how much work?"

"Too much."

He touched the table beside the book, not the page. "My mother said she was difficult."

"Good," Celia said.

The archive went online at midnight because Celia did not trust scheduled launches. Ada was there with her, as were two volunteers and a bakery worker who brought unsold rolls. The first search that returned properly was TRUST STRIKE 1889. Celia stared at the results page and said nothing.

"Satisfied?" Ada asked.

"Never."

"Accurate?"

Celia looked at the screen. "Closer."

That was enough for the night.

The next morning the archive had eleven messages. Three were from researchers, two from descendants, one from a crank who believed all trusts were foreign plots, and one from a retired printer who corrected a caption about quads with unnecessary heat and perfect accuracy. Celia printed that one and taped it above the scanner. "The public has arrived," she said. Ada opened the wage book again and checked the entry the printer had cited. He was right. Celia changed the caption without ceremony. Accuracy, like repair, had to remain open after publication.

In the afternoon, Paul returned with the photograph of Mara Voss and a permission form. He had written her name in full, then added sorter, worker, creditor. Celia read the words and looked at Ada. "Creditor," she said. "Good." The archive page went live before closing. Mara's image appeared beside the ledger entry, not as decoration but as a person attached to a debt once recorded and now readable. The forged proof had tried to make a clean transfer. The wage book kept making claims.

Chapter 45: Rafe's Edition

Rafe printed the hearing transcript in a limited edition, not for luxury but for record. He said this three times to anyone who suggested deckled edges, fancy binding, or a collector's box.

"Plain paper," he said. "Honest impression. Notes where the notes help. If anyone says artisanal, they leave."

The shop became a temporary annex of the case. Theo cast a small run of clean lowercase e sorts for the edition, grumbling that Rafe's equipment was arranged by a person hostile to elbows. Celia wrote the foreword in a tone so dry it could have preserved fruit. Livia supplied the museum note and insisted that the custody failure remain in the text. Iman refused to write anything decorative and provided a chart. Owen sent a paragraph about paper that began, "The sheet did not betray anyone; it was enlisted."

Ada contributed a short technical note after Rafe rejected her first draft as "too much like being scolded by a staircase."

"That is not criticism," she said.

"It is if the staircase is right and still unpleasant."

He included one page showing the forged e beside the genuine e. The images were printed large, with arrows kept discreet and labels plain.

"Too didactic?" he asked.

Ada studied the page. The genuine e was clean, narrow, almost shy. The forged proof's e carried the lower wound, now famous among perhaps forty people and a small museum audience.

"No."

"You hesitated."

"I was deciding whether to say useful or severe."

"Both are acceptable."

The edition took two weeks to print because Rafe refused to hurry and because Theo kept finding things to correct. The first sheets dried on racks. The transcript pages carried the court's questions, Iman's limits, Celia's receipts, Theo's measurable hardness, the judge's order. Rafe printed the corrected museum label at the back, followed by the trust's restoration plan.

On the day they collated the first copies, the shop filled with the sounds of paper handled carefully. Celia tied bundles. Livia checked page order. Theo complained about Rafe's lockup and then fixed it without being asked. Ada folded errata slips for a minor date correction in the paper appendix.

"You know," Rafe said, "for people who dislike sentiment, you all spend a lot of time preserving things."

"Preservation is not sentiment," Celia said.

"I knew someone would say that."

The edition sold enough in preorders to repair two windows and shame three wealthy people into funding four more. Progress had stranger currencies. A local paper ran a story with a photograph of the wounded e and the headline SMALL LETTER SAVES OLD FOUNDRY. Ada hated the headline. Rafe framed it in the shop bathroom.

"To remind visitors what we endure," he said.

Ada bought one copy of the edition and gave it to no one. She kept it in her office on the shelf where she placed cases that had taught her something unpleasant. The book was plain, sturdy, and a little severe. It suited the matter.

When the final sheet came off the press, Rafe handed it to Theo. "Clean pull."

Theo inspected it. "Adequate."

Rafe grinned. "From you, a hymn."

Theo did not smile, but he did not deny it.

Ada stayed after the others left and helped Rafe sweep paper trimmings into a bin. "You know people will collect this," she said. "Let them," Rafe answered. "Collectors can accidentally fund roofs." He stacked the finished copies by order number, not by donor importance, and placed Celia's archive copy first for delivery. That small decision pleased Ada more than she expected. The edition did not erase suspicion from his name, but it gave his skill a public answer. He had printed the record of the accusation and the evidence that defeated it. There were worse ways to reclaim a press.

At the door, he handed Ada a misprinted sheet. The wounded e had printed too dark, almost comic in its importance. "For your wall," he said. "I do not decorate with errors." "Then keep it in a drawer and pretend that is different." She took it, because it was easier than arguing and because he was right.

Chapter 46: Theo's Apprentice

Theo took on two adult apprentices from the neighborhood print cooperative after insisting for a month that he was not a teacher and had no patience for anyone who confused craft with therapy. Their names were Mina Cho and Luis Ortega. Mina had worked nights in a packaging plant and printed protest posters on weekends. Luis repaired bicycles, set type for broadsides, and asked questions with the persistence of a tax auditor.

Theo warned them that typefounding was hot, slow, repetitive, dangerous, and unforgiving.

"Good," Mina said. "So is rent."

He liked them immediately and hid it by criticizing how they stood.

"Feet," he said.

Luis looked down. "I brought both."

"Then use them."

The first casting lesson took place on a Saturday morning with the furnace newly inspected and the roof patched above the casting room. Not repaired, Theo said. Patched. Repair was a word for people with endowments. Ada came because Celia had asked her to document the training program for the trust record, and because she wanted to see whether the building could hold work without turning it into exhibit.

Metal entered the mold with a quick bright motion. A letter appeared, dull and small, not magical, only precise. Theo showed the apprentices how to inspect body, face, shoulder, foot. He made them reject the first casts. Then the second. Mina frowned but did not argue. Luis asked whether the flaws would print visibly.

"Some," Theo said. "Some only matter later."

"Later when?"

"When someone depends on the letter and you are dead."

Ada, standing near the door, wrote that down and then crossed it out. Too neat. The lesson continued without needing her sentence.

They cast lowercase e sorts from a clean matrix, then compared them with the damaged e exhibit sealed in the case nearby. Theo did not allow anyone to handle the evidence, but he made them look.

"This little wound nearly cost the trust," he said.

"No," Mina said after a moment. "The people did."

Theo stared at her, then nodded. "Yes. The letter only told on them."

That answer seemed to please him more than a perfect cast.

At lunch, they ate in the old office with the window open to the yard. Celia came by with copies of the wage book entries for former apprentices. Rafe brought a stack of misprinted sheets for practice and was told by Theo that he had over-inked them. Livia arrived with a museum loan form revised so completely it seemed to belong to another institution.

"The apprentices sign custody logs too," she said.

"We sign everything now," Luis said.

"Good," Ada answered. "Future people will bless your irritation."

In the afternoon, Mina managed a clean cast. She held it in her palm and said nothing, which Ada liked. Too many people filled first success with noise. Luis followed twenty minutes later and grinned openly.

Theo inspected both letters. "Adequate."

"Is that high praise?" Luis asked.

Rafe, from the doorway, said, "From him, you should frame it."

Theo pretended not to hear. He set the two clean sorts beside the rejected ones and made the apprentices write down what had gone wrong in each attempt: metal temperature, hand position, mold closure, timing. No shame. Just record. Craft learned by remembering failure accurately.

As Ada left, the furnace clicked behind her and voices continued in the casting room. The foundry was beginning to make future evidence, not because every letter would become a case, but because people were learning that every object carried the conditions of its making. That was a severe education and a decent one.

At the gate, Mina caught up with Ada holding a notebook smudged with graphite. "How much of this do we write down?" she asked. Ada looked at the notes: temperatures, failed casts, Theo's corrections, one sketch of a letter foot. "More than you think you need," she said. "Enough that someone can disagree with you later." Mina seemed to like that. "That sounds like trust." Ada considered the word. "It is one form of it." Behind them, Luis dropped something metal and Theo's voice rose at once. The lesson had not ended. Good lessons rarely did.

Chapter 47: Neris Settles

Neris settled the estate dispute with a compromise that pleased no one enough to worry Ada and satisfied everyone enough to hold. Heirs received modest payments from licensing, workshops, event rentals, and a restricted preservation fund. The trust retained ownership. Any future sale or long lease required public hearing, worker-board approval, descendant notice, and court review. The language was stiff, repetitive, and necessary.

"No one is thrilled," Neris said when she brought the final draft to Ada's office.

"Durable agreements often begin there."

"You sound like a judge."

"Take that back."

She laughed, tired and real. The settlement had taken weeks of calls, private meetings, angry letters, revised charts, and one disastrous family session in which two cousins argued about a funeral from 1976 until Celia threatened to read wage tables aloud until they stopped. They stopped.

Some heirs complained publicly. One accused the trust board of choosing bricks over blood. Another wrote Ada a letter saying she had cost his family a life-changing sum. She read it once, filed it, and did not answer. He was not entirely wrong. Truth often cost people who had not manufactured the lie. That was one reason lies worked so well.

Others visited the pressworks and found grandparents' or great-grandparents' names in wage books. A woman named Denise discovered that her great-grandfather had been paid for night casting during the very strike Hugo's proof claimed had followed a sale. She stood over the ledger with Celia beside her and cried quietly, embarrassed by her own reaction.

"I came for the settlement explanation," Denise said.

"You found one," Celia replied.

Money did not vanish as a concern, but it stopped being the only language in the room. The hardship fund made that possible. So did the public embarrassment of wealthy people who had praised Hugo's plan and now needed to be seen repairing what their praise had helped endanger.

Hugo's company withdrew without apology. The notice cited changed market conditions, ongoing legal uncertainty, and respect for community process. Ada filed that under expected. Apology was not required for repair. Absence could be useful too.

Criminal proceedings moved slowly elsewhere. Hugo's lawyers issued statements. Marlo Venn's license came under review. Hannah Stowe, the consultant, resigned from two boards and took a position in another city. None of it repaired the trust. Neris kept her attention on bylaws, revenue, insurance, and making sure no future board could be charmed into private custody again.

"The settlement is ugly," she said one evening after the signing.

"It is legible."

"That may be the kindest thing anyone says about it."

"Good agreements should be read, not admired."

Neris leaned back in the chair. "You know, I did want the easy document to be real for a week."

"I remember."

"I still think about that."

"You changed course."

"Because you made the document harder to want."

Ada accepted that. It was perhaps the most useful thing her work could do: make a convenient falsehood less comfortable to hold.

At the final signing, Theo wore a tie badly. Rafe noticed and fixed it without asking. Celia brought the original trust deed in a protective box, not because it was needed, but because she said the old paper deserved to see what its troublesome children had done. Neris pretended not to hear that.

When the last signature dried, no one applauded. They were too tired and too aware that paper began obligations more often than it ended them.

Afterward, Neris stood alone at the copier making certified sets. Ada joined her and began collating without being asked. "This is the glamorous part," Neris said. "It is the durable part." The machine warmed the pages and released them with small mechanical sighs. Settlement, Ada thought, was not peace. It was organized disagreement with signatures at the bottom. That would have to be enough. When the copier jammed, Neris swore so precisely that Ada admired the sentence structure. Then they opened the tray and fixed it.

Chapter 48: The Clean Pull

Ada printed from the genuine galley under Theo's supervision on a morning chosen for no symbolic reason except that the press was ready, the humidity was acceptable, and Celia had stopped asking when it would happen. The galley had been conserved enough for a limited pull. Every movement was logged. Every person in the room signed in. The trust board wanted one clean sheet for the pressworks, one for the museum, one for the archive, and one for the court record.

"You are sure I should do this?" Ada asked.

Theo looked offended. "No. That is why I will stand here and prevent disaster."

Rafe prepared the ink but made Ada roll it out. She did it too cautiously at first, and the line printed pale. Theo held the sheet up without comment.

"Too light," Ada said.

"At least you know."

The second pull was too heavy. Ink filled small spaces and darkened the headline. Rafe made a sympathetic noise that Theo described as theatrical and unhelpful.

The third pull was clean enough. Not perfect. Clean. The workers' circular emerged in black lines: demands, dates, names, trust language. The face was spare and firm. The spacing carried the pressure of hurried work. It was not beautiful. Better than beautiful.

Celia held the sheet by its edges. "This is what they tried to bury."

"Replace," Ada said.

Celia looked at the paper. "Worse."

"Yes."

Replacement was a quieter violence. It left a record-shaped object where the record had been and asked everyone to be grateful for clarity. The false proof had not merely hidden the strike circular. It had offered a different past, one in which workers had surrendered neatly and on time for a development schedule.

They pulled three more sheets. Ada improved with each one and disliked how satisfying it felt. The press demanded attention of a kind she trusted: immediate, physical, intolerant of vague intention. Too much ink, and the page told you. Uneven pressure, and the page told you. Poor lockup, and the page told you in public.

Mina and Luis watched from the doorway. Theo made them explain the differences between the pulls before he allowed them closer.

"The third has cleaner counters," Mina said.

"The fourth has better pressure at the edge," Luis added.

"Adequate," Theo said.

They both looked pleased.

The clean pull would hang beside the forged proof in the museum and in the pressworks. Ada liked that. Let the lie stand where it could be defeated daily. Not erased, not hidden in shame, not allowed to become glamorous. Defeated by comparison, by labels, by the stubborn presence of the thing it tried to replace.

After the sheets dried, Celia took one to the archive in a flat case. Livia took one to the museum. Theo kept one at Bell & Vale and stood for a long time deciding where it should hang.

"Not the entrance," Ada said.

"Why not?"

"People will glance."

He nodded and chose the composing room instead, near the type cases, where anyone who came to learn would have to pass it slowly.

When Ada washed ink from her hands, black settled briefly in the lines of her skin before the soap took it. She thought of all the people whose hands had carried older ink without the luxury of washing away the consequences. Then she dried her hands and returned to the room. The sheet was only paper. It was also work restored to view.

The fourth pull went to Celia's archive after she insisted on walking it there herself. Ada accompanied her through light rain, the flat case held between them under a plastic cover. "Ridiculous," Celia said. "It has survived worse than drizzle." "Custody," Ada replied. Celia snorted but kept her side level. At the bakery stairs, the warm smell of bread met the damp paper smell from the case. Two kinds of work, both early, both repetitive, both judged by whether they fed anyone. Celia logged the pull before removing her coat.

Chapter 49: Foundry Morning

On reopening morning, the pressworks made honest noise. Rollers turned. Cases slid. The furnace clicked and breathed under careful demonstration temperature. A ladder clanged in the yard. Someone laughed in the packing room. Visitors walked carefully because the floor still remembered more than trustees and because Theo had posted a sign that read WATCH YOUR STEP, THIS IS NOT A THEME PARK.

The roof no longer leaked in the obvious places. The windows were repaired, though two still rattled in wind. The trust board had painted nothing that did not need paint. Brick remained brick. Scars remained visible. The building had been cleaned, not flattered.

Livia guided the first tour. Her script was plain and better for it. She explained the trust, the forgery, the museum's custody failure, and the restoration partnership. Celia corrected her twice and was invited to keep doing so. Rafe printed bookmarks with a small specimen of the clean 1889 e. Mina and Luis demonstrated casting with Theo watching like a weather system.

"He is enjoying this," Rafe whispered.

"No," Ada said. "He is enduring joy."

Near the repaired cabinet six, the cracked lowercase e rested in a small mount, labeled without drama. Evidence of forgery. Evidence of sequence. Evidence that small damage can preserve truth when large voices fail. Beside it lay a clean e, a controlled pull, and a short explanation of how type carries time.

A visitor asked whether the damaged letter was famous.

Theo answered before Ada could. "No. It is useful."

The visitor accepted that, perhaps because Theo made usefulness sound like a title.

In the archive corner, Paul, Mara Voss's descendant, showed his granddaughter the wage book image on a screen. The granddaughter was an adult, a graduate student in urban planning, and she asked better questions than the city council staffer standing beside her. Celia gave her a card and told her to come back when she had read the trust deed.

The first public pull from the press was not ceremonial. Rafe refused ceremony. It was a bookmark, slightly over-inked because a visitor distracted him with a question about wedding invitations. Theo noticed and said nothing until the visitor left.

"Too much ink," he said.

"The public is watching."

"Then improve the public."

Ada stood near the matrix cabinet and let the morning move around her. She had expected to feel apart from it, and mostly she did. Her work had belonged to the dispute, not to the governance that followed. Still, the building's noise reached her with a physical steadiness she had not anticipated. Not triumph. Continuation.

Neris arrived late, carrying a box of signed bylaws and looking as if paper had become a medical condition. "Speeches in ten minutes," she said.

"My condolences."

"You are on the list."

"Remove me."

"Theo already did. He said you would leave."

"Good man."

A childless old machine threw light from its oiled rails. Ada corrected the thought before it became foolish; machines had no children, only users, damage, repair, and records. But the foundry had begun to teach again. That was close enough to inheritance for a working definition.

Ada left before speeches began. At the gate, she looked back once. Through the open door, she could see Livia answering a question, Celia interrupting, Theo pretending annoyance, Rafe feeding paper into the press, and two apprentices watching the work as if it had consequences.

It did.

Half a block away, applause reached her, thinned by traffic. She did not turn around. Speeches had their use; they marked a public promise, and public promises could be cited later when money grew shy. Still, Ada preferred the sound that followed: the press again, a drawer closing, someone asking where to put a form. The foundry would not be saved by the best sentence spoken that morning. It would be saved, if at all, by the next thousand ordinary acts done with enough attention to be checked.

Chapter 50: The Typefounder's Proof

Ada's final report was shorter than the case deserved and longer than anyone would enjoy. It named the proof sheet false, the galley genuine, the trust intact, and the method of forgery probable beyond serious dispute. It avoided the word miracle. It avoided the word villain except in quoted correspondence. It contained appendices for type evidence, paper analysis, ink chemistry, custody sequence, controlled printing, restoration failures, and public correction.

The title was plain: Report on the Bell & Vale Proof Sheet and Associated Materials. Rafe said it sounded like a punishment. Ada told him accuracy often did.

She delivered copies to the court, the museum, the trust board, and Celia's archive. The court received a bound copy and a digital record. The museum received two, one for collections and one for institutional review. Celia received hers in person because she wanted to stamp it with the archive accession number while Ada watched.

"You enjoy stamps too much," Ada said.

"Stamps are civilization admitting it needs help."

Theo asked for one more copy when Ada stopped by the pressworks afterward.

"For the furnace?" she asked.

"For the apprentices. They should know what bad printing costs."

Ada gave him the extra copy.

He weighed it in his hand. "Too much paper."

"You asked."

"I did not say I would approve of your habits."

They walked through the composing room. The clean pull hung near the cases. The forged proof exhibit had been duplicated in a smaller teaching panel. Mina was labeling newly cast sorts. Luis was sweeping metal scraps into a pan, slowly enough that Theo told him the floor charged rent for lingering.

Outside, rain darkened the foundry brick. Inside, someone pulled a sheet from the press and checked the impression before speaking. That was the lesson Ada trusted most: look closely before accepting the page.

In her office that evening, Ada filed the case materials. She placed Rafe's limited edition on the shelf and slid the final report beside it. The unsolicited consulting folder from Hugo remained sealed in a box marked DISCLOSED CONTACT. She did not keep trophies. She kept records because forgetting was rarely neutral.

Livia sent a message after nine: Board approved permanent custody policy. Public audit next month. Thank you for being difficult.

Ada replied: Difficulty was warranted.

Celia sent no message, but the archive database updated with the final report before midnight. Neris sent a billable joke about never trusting a beautiful margin. Iman sent a corrected caption for the ink chart. Owen sent a note complaining that the museum label used aged where it should have used toned. Ada forwarded it to Livia and closed her laptop.

The rain continued. Across the city, the pressworks would be leaking somewhere, because old buildings always found a way to remind people that survival was active work. Someone would place a bucket. Someone would write it down. Someone would ask for money. The trust would argue, pay, fail, repair, and argue again.

Ada turned off the office light. In the dark window, her reflection stood over the desk for a moment with the shelves behind her, all those reports, all those objects that had once demanded to be believed. She thought of the lowercase e, the trimmed margin, the galley hidden in a crate, the wage book counting what rhetoric had tried to erase.

A proof sheet was only a proof. It asked a question of the work before the work went public. Is this right? Is this ready? Who has checked it?

Bell & Vale had answered late, but not too late.

The next morning, Ada found the misprinted sheet from Rafe in her case. She had forgotten placing it there. The wounded e sat too dark in the line, almost absurd, a small mark overburdened by meaning. She put it in a folder rather than a frame. Evidence belonged in folders. On the tab she wrote BELL & VALE - TEACHING COPY. Then she stopped, added one more word, and closed the drawer: LOOK.

At noon she returned to the docket room and filed one final note: not a triumph, not a sermon, only the correction and where to find its proof. The clerk read it, stamped it, and slid it into the tray. Ada listened to the small impact of paper on paper. It was not dramatic. It was durable.
