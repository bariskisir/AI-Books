Chapter 1: The Offer

The gym smelled of chalk and old rubber. Vera Sorensen hung from a plastic crimp twelve feet up, working a problem she had set herself, when the man in the down jacket called her name from the mats.

She did not look down right away. She finished the move, matched her hands, and let go, dropping with bent knees onto the pad.

"You're hard to find," the man said. He had a soft handshake and a brochure-clean smile. "Daniel Quayle. I think we have friends in common."

"We don't," Vera said.

He laughed as if she had made a joke. He set a folder on the bench beside her shoes. On the cover was a photograph she had spent ten years not looking at. The west face of Khumri, sheer and white, the summit ridge cutting the sky like a saw.

"I'm putting together a small team," Daniel said. "Eight weeks. Fully funded. We'd be honored."

Vera wiped her hands on her thighs. The chalk left pale marks. The photograph was very good. Whoever had taken it had stood, she thought, almost exactly where Jonas had stood.

"I don't climb eight-thousanders anymore," she said.

"You climbed seven of them."

"And stopped."

"On the eighth." He said it gently, as if he knew. He did know; everyone who read the magazines knew. "Khumri is the eighth. You never finished it."

She picked up her shoes. Outside the wide windows the Colorado afternoon was the color of dust.

"Leave the folder," she said, and walked to the showers without deciding anything, which was its own kind of decision.


Chapter 2: The Sister

Jonas's sister still lived in the house above Boulder Creek, the one with the warped porch and the climbing rope coiled on a nail by the door like a relic nobody dared throw away.

"You're going back," Astrid said. It was not a question. She poured coffee and did not sit.

"I'm thinking about it."

"You said the mountain could keep him."

"I did say that." Vera turned the mug without drinking. "I was angry. At it. At myself."

Astrid sat then, finally, across the scarred table. She had her brother's eyes, gray and level. "You weren't angry. You were guilty. Those aren't the same."

"No."

"He chose to keep going. You told him to turn around. I read the report a hundred times." Astrid's voice did not rise. "You didn't put him on that ridge."

"I let him stay on it."

The clock over the stove ticked. Somewhere down the canyon a train sounded, long and low.

"If you go," Astrid said at last, "don't go to bury anything. There's nothing to bury. Go and come back. That's the only thing I'd ask."

Vera nodded. She thought she could promise that. She was wrong about a great deal that spring, and she would learn it slowly, but she did mean the promise when she made it.


Chapter 3: Kathmandu

The team gathered in a hotel courtyard where caged birds sang and the air smelled of diesel and marigolds.

Daniel made the introductions like a host. Mara Lindqvist, the producer, slim and quick-eyed, shaking hands too long. Owen Reyes, young, twenty-eight maybe, with the easy shoulders of someone who had never been hurt. Priya Anand, the doctor, who shook hands once, firmly, and watched everyone else.

"And Tenzin you'll meet at Lukla," Daniel said. "Our sirdar. Best on the mountain."

"I know Tenzin," Vera said.

Daniel's smile flickered, recovered. "Of course. You've climbed together."

She had not corrected him. She had climbed with Tenzin once, on Khumri, ten years ago, and they had carried the same grief down off it. But she said nothing, because Owen was looking at her with an expression she could not place — recognition, or something near it, quickly hidden.

That night she could not sleep. The city's noise came up through the window, and under it, beneath it, she felt the mountain the way you feel a held note. She had thought she was past this. She lay in the dark and counted her own breaths and found she could not stop.


Chapter 4: Lukla

The plane banked between green walls of hill and dropped onto the short canted runway at Lukla, and then they were walking, and then the clouds opened.

Khumri stood north and east, far off, white above the brown ridges, and Vera stopped on the trail with her pack half-shrugged and looked at it.

The body remembers before the mind allows it. Her hands had gone cold. Her breath had shortened though the air here was still thick and easy. She had stood like this once with Jonas beside her, both of them grinning like fools, and he had said, *that one's ours,* and she had believed him.

"It's beautiful," Mara said, coming up beside her, lifting a small camera.

"It isn't," Vera said. "Beautiful is the wrong word. It doesn't care about us. That's not the same as beautiful."

Mara lowered the camera and looked at her, and for the first time her quick eyes were still. "Then what's the word?"

Vera did not answer. She shouldered her pack and started walking. Behind her she heard Mara take the photograph anyway.


Chapter 5: The Sirdar

Tenzin Norgyel waited at the lodge in Phakding with the loads sorted and the porters paid and a thermos of tea already poured. He had more gray in his hair than she remembered. He looked at her across the smoke of the stove and did not smile, and that was how she knew him.

"Vera," he said.

"Tenzin."

He poured her tea without asking. They sat. The others were loud in the next room, Owen's laugh rising over Daniel's voice.

"You came back," Tenzin said.

"I was asked."

"Many people are asked. They do not come." He drank. "I told them I would only take this work if you were on it. They did not know why. I did not tell them."

Vera held the warm cup in both hands. "Why, then?"

"Because you are the only one of these who will turn around," Tenzin said simply, "when the mountain says turn around. The others will not. I have watched them three days. I would rather climb with one who is afraid in the right way."

She looked at him. The old grief was in his face, banked but not gone. She understood that they would not speak of Jonas yet, and that they would, eventually, have to.


Chapter 6: The Boy at Base Camp

They walked for days, gaining height slowly, the air thinning by inches. At Namche the houses climbed the hillside in tiers and the market sold cabbages and carabiners side by side.

It was there, over dal bhat, that Owen let it out.

"My dad ran an expedition on Khumri," he said, not to Vera in particular, to the table. "Years ago. I came as far as base camp. I was, what, sixteen. Stayed in the cook tent the whole time, scared of everything."

The room went very quiet around Vera's ears, a private silence.

"Which year," she said.

Owen named it. It was the year. He said it lightly, reaching for the rice, and then he caught her face and stopped, the spoon halfway.

"You were there," he said slowly. "You were on the mountain. That was—" He set the spoon down. "I'm sorry. I didn't—Daniel said you'd climbed it, I didn't think—"

"It's all right," Vera said. It was not. She stood, said she needed air, and went out into the cold street where the prayer flags snapped, and stood breathing until her hands stopped shaking.


Chapter 7: The Day on the Trail

The memory came whole, on the trail above Tengboche, unbidden and exact.

The summit ridge, ten years ago. Two in the afternoon, which was too late. The clouds stacking up in the south like something poured. Jonas thirty feet above her on the fixed line, slow, his head down, the altitude in him.

*Jonas. Turn around. We turn around now.*

He had not answered. He had taken another step. The summit was a hundred meters off, no more, a clean white dome, and they had wanted it for years, and he had taken another step toward it, and the cloud had come up the face like a hand closing.

She had clipped past him. She had grabbed his sleeve. *Now,* she had said. And he had looked at her with a strange peace and said, *you go down, I'll be right behind you,* and she had believed him because she had wanted to, because turning meant safety and she was afraid.

She had gone down. He had not been right behind her.

Vera stood on the trail with her hand against a mani wall, the carved stones cold under her palm, and let it pass through her. Then she walked on. There was nowhere else to go but up.


Chapter 8: Base Camp

Base camp sprawled on the moraine, a town of yellow tents on broken rock, prayer flags strung between the cook tent and the radio mast. The mountain stood over it, near now, immense, the icefall tumbling off its shoulder in frozen chaos.

Vera knew the place. She knew the curve of the glacier and the sound the séracs made at night, the deep gunshot crack of ice shifting. She set her tent where she had set it ten years ago, from habit, and only after realized what she had done.

The gear was wrong. That was the first thing she noticed properly.

The loads Tenzin's porters had carried up included a dozen sealed black cases stamped with a logo she did not know — AEROLEX, in clean sans-serif — and a tent set apart from the others that Daniel kept zipped.

"What's in the cases?" she asked Priya, who was organizing the medical kit on a tarp.

Priya did not look up. "Suits. Some prototype heating system. We're not supposed to open them until Daniel says." She tightened a strap. "I've been a high-altitude doctor for fifteen years. I have never been told not to inspect equipment my patients are going to trust their lives to."

The wind came down off the icefall, cold and dry.


Chapter 9: The Producer's Camera

Mara found Vera at the edge of camp at dusk, where the glacier ran out into shadow.

"Can I ask you something off the record?" Mara said.

"I didn't know we were on a record."

Mara almost smiled. She had a way of holding her camera that was less like a journalist and more like a clerk holding an inventory. "What do you think I'm filming?"

"A summit. The famous Vera Sorensen returns to the mountain. Redemption. That sort of thing."

"That's the story Daniel sells." Mara looked back toward the tents, where the cook fire glowed. "I'm not making a documentary. I work for the people who made those suits. My job is to verify the system reaches the summit and functions. I document performance. The film is — incidental."

Vera turned to look at her fully. "You're telling me this why?"

"Because I watched your face on the trail," Mara said. "You're the only one here who looks at this mountain like it can kill you. I thought you should know what you're being used to sell."

The dusk thickened. Somewhere above them the icefall cracked, once, like a door slamming in an empty house.


Chapter 10: The Schedule

Daniel called the meeting in the mess tent the next morning, a satellite map taped to the table, weather printouts fanned beside it.

"We move fast," he said. "Two rotations to acclimatize, then we wait for a window and we go. Summit no later than the nineteenth."

"Why the nineteenth?" Priya asked.

"Because that's when the window's modeled."

"That's not how windows work." Priya's voice was flat. "We don't know when the window is. We watch and we wait."

Daniel smiled the brochure smile. "We have very good forecasting."

Vera said, "You have a date. Not a forecast. What happens on the nineteenth?"

The tent was quiet. Owen looked between them. Tenzin, by the door, watched Daniel without expression.

"The launch," Mara said, when Daniel did not. "The product launch is the twenty-fifth. They need summit footage by the twentieth to make the edit."

Daniel's jaw tightened. "Thank you, Mara." He folded the map. "We climb the mountain like professionals. The dates are a coincidence. That's all."

Nobody in the tent believed him, and he knew it, and he kept smiling anyway, which Vera found she trusted less than open lies.


Chapter 11: First Rotation

They went up into the icefall before dawn, headlamps bobbing, the cold a solid thing in the lungs.

The icefall was a ruin of blue towers and black gaps, a place the mountain remade every week. Tenzin led, reading it, choosing the line between séracs that leaned like drunk men. They crossed ladders lashed over crevasses, the aluminum flexing, the dark below them going down past any light.

Owen climbed too fast. He reached the anchors first and stood breathing hard, grinning, while the others came up.

"This is nothing," he said. "I expected worse."

"It's six in the morning at fifty-six hundred meters," Priya said, clipping in beside him. "Talk to me at eight thousand."

They turned around at Camp One, by plan, to sleep low. But Vera had watched Owen on the descent, watched him sprint the easy sections and tire on the hard, and she thought: he climbs like a man who has never been afraid, which means he has never learned the thing fear teaches. That was the most dangerous kind, on a mountain. She had been that kind once.


Chapter 12: The Crevasse

On the second day of the rotation it nearly went wrong.

Mara crossed a ladder badly. She had crossed three already and gotten confident, and on the fourth she looked down instead of ahead, and her crampon caught the rung, and she pitched sideways over the void with a small cut-off sound that was worse than a scream.

The rope caught her. Vera had her on belay and the rope caught, and Mara hung half over the lip of the crevasse, scrabbling, the blue depth under her boots.

"Don't look down," Vera said, very calm. "Look at me. Hand on the rope. Now your knee. Up. Up. Good."

They hauled her out. She lay on the ice shaking, and then she was sick, and then she sat up and wiped her mouth and said, "I'm fine. I'm fine," which she was not.

Daniel had not slowed. He was forty meters ahead, at the anchors, and he called down, "Everybody good? We're behind."

Priya looked at Vera. Vera looked at the rope in her hands. Neither of them answered him. That silence, Vera thought, was the first true thing the team had agreed on.


Chapter 13: Camp One

Camp One sat on a flat shelf above the icefall, six tents in the wind, the glacier falling away below to the brown distance.

That night Vera and Tenzin shared a stove, melting snow for water, the blue flame hissing.

"You set your tent in the old place," Tenzin said.

"I noticed after."

He fed a chunk of ice into the pot. "I think about him. Every season I come here, I think about him on the way past the shoulder. I leave a stone."

"I didn't know that."

"You were not here to see." He said it without accusation. "You went home and you did not come back, and I thought, good, she is wise. And now you are here and I think, she is not wise, she is only ready. There is a difference."

Vera watched the water begin to move in the pot. "I don't think I'm ready. I think I just couldn't carry it any longer the way I was carrying it."

Tenzin nodded slowly. "That is closer to true," he said. "The mountain is honest with people who are honest. Be honest, Vera. It is the only protection there is, and it is not much."


Chapter 14: Ambition

Owen sought her out at Camp One in the thin morning light, his breath smoking.

"I want to ask you something," he said. "Climber to climber."

"Ask."

"Were you scared? On the summit days. The real ones. The seven."

Vera considered the question seriously, because he had asked it seriously. "Every time," she said. "More each time, not less. The mountains got bigger and I got older and I knew more about what could happen. By the seventh I was afraid the whole way up and the whole way down."

Owen frowned. "That sounds awful."

"It kept me alive."

"I'm not scared," he said, and he said it like a confession, like he half-knew it was a flaw and could not feel why. "Up there I just feel — clear. Like everything's simple."

"That clearness is the altitude," Vera said. "It's your brain running out of oxygen and calling it peace. Don't trust it. The day you feel certain on a mountain is the day it's about to take you."

He laughed, not unkindly, and did not believe her. She had not expected him to.


Chapter 15: The Doctor's Warning

Down at base camp between rotations, Priya cornered Daniel by the radio tent with her clipboard.

"I've been logging oxygen saturations," she said. "Owen's are fine. Mara's are not adapting. Yours are borderline. The schedule you've drawn doesn't allow enough nights high. If we push for the nineteenth we're taking people to the death zone who aren't ready."

"People summit faster than this all the time."

"People die faster than this all the time," Priya said. "I'm putting my objection in writing. I want it on the record that I advised against the timeline."

Daniel's smile was gone now. "Put it wherever you want. The window's the window."

"There is no window yet. You keep saying it like saying it makes it real."

Vera, passing with a load of rope, heard this and stopped pretending not to. Daniel saw her listening and his face did something complicated, and she understood, fully, for the first time: he was not stupid, and he was not careless. He was frightened of something that was not the mountain. He was frightened of the people who had sent him.


Chapter 16: The Window

The forecast came down the satellite line on the eleventh, and Daniel read it aloud in the mess tent like a man reading a reprieve.

"High pressure building. Winds dropping above eight thousand on the seventeenth, eighteenth, nineteenth. Then it closes — a system coming up from the Bay of Bengal, big one, hits the twentieth or twenty-first."

"So we have three days," Owen said.

"We have two, to be safe," Priya said.

"We have enough." Daniel spread his hands. "This is the window. This is exactly what we planned for."

"You didn't plan for it," Vera said quietly. "You hoped for it and it happened to come. That's luck, not a plan. And the storm behind it is a real storm. If we summit on the nineteenth we descend into the front edge of it."

"Then we summit on the eighteenth."

"With one acclimatization rotation less than anyone here should have." Priya again.

Daniel looked around the tent. "We go," he said. "We've come too far to wait for a perfect day that doesn't exist. Pack tonight. We move at three."

Outside, the flags had gone still. The wind had dropped already, and the stillness was its own kind of warning, and only Tenzin and Vera seemed to feel it as one.


Chapter 17: The Old Line

Above Camp One, on the long traverse to Camp Two, Vera found Jonas's rope.

She knew it at once. The old fixed lines were faded to gray, all of them, but this one had a splice she remembered tying herself, a particular knot Jonas had teased her for, ten years gone and still there, frozen into the ice screw, leading up and out toward the shoulder where the route bent away.

She stopped with her glove on it. The others climbed past, clipped to the new line beside it. Tenzin slowed.

"His," he said. Not a question.

"Mine. I fixed this pitch." She turned the gray rope in her fingers. It was brittle, useless, a thread of the past welded to the mountain. "We came up exactly here."

"Leave it," Tenzin said gently. "It belongs to the mountain now. So does he. There is no shame in that. We all belong to it in the end. The mountain only lets us borrow ourselves for a while."

She let go of the rope. She climbed on, past the bend in the route, toward the shoulder, and she did not look back at the gray line, but she felt it behind her the whole long way, like a hand let go of.


Chapter 18: The Suits

At Camp Two, on the broad snow shoulder at sixty-four hundred meters, Daniel finally opened the black cases.

The suits were beautiful objects — sleek, dark, threaded through with fine heating filaments, a battery pack at the small of the back, a control unit on the chest. Aerolex had spent a fortune on them. You could see it.

"Body-mapped heating," Daniel said, almost reverent, handing them out. "Keeps your core and extremities warm down to minus fifty. No more frostbite. This is the future of high-altitude climbing."

Vera pulled hers on. It was warm at once, unnaturally warm, the filaments humming faintly against her skin. It felt like cheating. It felt, she thought, like wearing a machine you did not understand into a place that killed machines.

"What happens if the battery dies?" she asked.

Daniel did not answer for a moment. "It won't."

"What happens if it does?"

"Then you're in a normal suit that's a little heavier than normal," he said. But Mara, beside him, had gone still, and Vera saw it, and filed it. The producer knew something about the suits that the leader was not saying, and the not-saying had a shape, and the shape was a flaw.


Chapter 19: Retreat

The storm scare came that night, a freak gust front off the south face, winds the forecast had not promised, hammering the tents at Camp Two until the poles bowed.

Daniel ordered a retreat to base at first light. It was the right call and he made it badly, shouting it through the dark, and the team came apart a little in the chaos — Owen slow to pack, Mara fumbling with frozen fingers, Priya snapping at everyone.

They got down. They sat in the mess tent at base, gray with exhaustion, while the wind screamed up at the mountain they had just left.

"It wasn't in the forecast," Daniel said, mostly to himself, staring at the printout as if it had betrayed him.

"Nothing up there is in the forecast," Vera said. "The forecast is a guess. The mountain is the truth. You've been climbing the forecast this whole time. You have to start climbing the mountain."

He looked at her with something close to hatred and something close to pleading. "I can't," he said quietly, so only she heard. "You don't understand. I can't."

And she thought, no, I'm beginning to. And she resolved to find out from Mara exactly what it was that Daniel could not do, and why.


Chapter 20: Mara's Confession

Vera found Mara that evening alone in the producer's tent, hunched over a satellite phone she snapped shut when Vera ducked in.

"Tell me the rest," Vera said, sitting. "You started. Finish it."

Mara was quiet for a long time. Outside the wind had dropped again, that false calm.

"Aerolex is in trouble," she said at last. "The company's overextended. They sank everything into this suit. They've got a launch event booked, investors flying in, a stage, the works — the twenty-fifth. If they don't have summit footage by the twentieth, the launch falls apart, and if the launch falls apart, the company falls apart. People lose everything. Daniel was paid up front. A lot. His contract pays the rest only if we summit by the nineteenth."

"So he's not climbing a mountain. He's meeting a deadline."

"He's drowning," Mara said. "We all are, a little. They told me to verify the product. They didn't tell me I'd be standing on it." She looked at her hands. "I'm not a climber, Vera. I shouldn't be above base camp. I know that now. I knew it on the ladder."

"Then go down."

"If I go down, there's no verification. No footage of the system on a person to the top. The launch dies." She met Vera's eyes. "I'm in it too. That's the trap. We're all carrying their problem up the hill."


Chapter 21: The Contract

That night Vera could not sleep and lay listening to the séracs crack and did the arithmetic in her head.

A company that would collapse without a summit. A leader paid to deliver one by a date. A producer who could not climb but could not turn back. A young man hungry for a name. A doctor outnumbered. A suit with a flaw nobody would name. A storm coming on the twentieth.

It was a machine, she saw, and the machine was built to drive people up past where they should stop. Every incentive pointed up. There was no incentive anywhere in it pointed at the only thing that mattered, which was coming down alive.

Only Tenzin was outside the machine. And herself. She had no contract, no name to make, no company to save. She had only come to set something down, and she realized now that she could set it down and still get killed by other people's reasons.

She thought about Astrid on the warped porch. *Go and come back.* She had meant the promise. She saw now it might not be hers alone to keep.

She got up before dawn and found Tenzin already awake, watching the mountain go from black to gray, and she sat beside him and said, "I need to know what happened to Jonas. The real thing. You were there. Tell me."


Chapter 22: What Tenzin Knew

Tenzin did not look at her. He looked at the summit ridge catching the first light.

"You went down," he said. "You came past me at Camp Three half-dead, telling me Jonas was behind you. I waited. He did not come. So I went up."

"You went up into that storm."

"I was younger." A faint smile, gone at once. "I found him below the step. He had fallen. Not far — twenty meters, onto the slope. But his rope had failed. The fixed line his expedition fixed that season. It parted under load. New rope, parted clean." He paused. "He was alive when I reached him. He could not move his legs. The storm was full on us. I could not carry him alone, and there was no one else, because his own team had turned around lower down and gone to their tents."

Vera sat very still.

"I stayed with him until he died," Tenzin said. "It was not long. He was not in pain by then; the cold does that. He said your name. He said, tell her she was right. Tell her to go home. That is all. Then he was gone, and I came down, because to stay was to die, and dying with him would not have helped him."

The light came down the ridge toward them, slow and gold.

"His rope parted," Vera said. "New rope."

"New rope," Tenzin said. "Bad rope. Someone fixed that line who should not have."


Chapter 23: The Corner Cut

It took her a day to put it together, and when she did it sat in her chest like swallowed ice.

Owen's father had run that expedition. Owen's father's team had fixed the lines high on Khumri that season — including the pitch below the step where Jonas fell. She remembered now, from the inquiry that came after, a detail she had buried because it had not mattered to her grief: the other expedition had used a discount rope, an industrial line not rated for the cold and the load, to save money on a trip that had run over budget.

Jonas had clipped his life to another team's economy and the economy had failed.

She found the old report in her memory the way you find a stone in a field with your boot. The name of the expedition leader. Reyes. She had not connected it to the easy-shouldered boy. Why would she. He had been sixteen in a cook tent.

But he was here now, on the same mountain, and his father's name was on the rope that had killed Jonas, and the whole expedition was once again being driven by money to cut corners against the cold.

She sat with it until the anger cooled into something colder and more useful. Then she went to find Owen.


Chapter 24: Collision

Owen was sorting gear by his tent. He looked up and smiled, and the smile died when he saw her face.

"Your father," Vera said. "His team fixed the high lines the year I was here. The year my partner died."

Owen straightened slowly. "Yeah. I know. I figured you'd worked it out."

"You knew."

"I've known since Namche. Since you told me which year." He set down the rope he was holding. "I didn't know how to say it. There's no good way. 'Hi, my dad's expedition probably killed your partner.' I've carried that since I was sixteen, Vera. I heard the men talk about it. The cheap rope. My father drinking himself stupid over it for years after. It's why I climb. It's the whole reason I climb. To do it right. To not be him."

The anger went out of her in a slow tide and left something she had not expected: a kind of recognition. He carried the same dead weight from the other side of it.

"He never said sorry," Owen said. "He couldn't. He's gone now too — heart, four years ago. So I'll say it. I'm sorry. For what it's worth at fifty-eight hundred meters, which is probably nothing."

"It's not nothing," Vera said, and was surprised to find it true.


Chapter 25: The Second Window

The forecast updated on the fourteenth and tightened the team like a turning screw.

"The window's holding," Daniel said. "Seventeenth through nineteenth. Storm on the twentieth, confirmed now, and it's a monster — they're modeling a meter of snow and hurricane wind above seven thousand. After that the season's done. The mountain shuts."

"So it's now or not at all," Owen said.

"It's now or not at all."

Priya put both hands flat on the table. "I'll say it once more for the record. We have not acclimatized enough. We retreated from Camp Two. Mara should not be going above base. The margin is too thin."

"Noted," Daniel said. "And overruled. We go for the seventeenth. Summit the nineteenth. Down ahead of the storm."

"There is no ahead of that storm," Vera said. "You summit the nineteenth, you descend into the nineteenth turning into the twentieth. You'd be on the ridge when it changes."

"Then we move fast." Daniel's hands were shaking slightly, she saw. "We've all done hard things. We do one more. Three days up, one day down. We can do this."

It was the *we* that did it. He needed them to choose it with him, so it would not be only his choice. And one by one, around the table, in the trap of incentives, they chose it.


Chapter 26: The Choice to Go

She could have refused. She thought about it all that night.

She could have said no, and gone down, and kept the promise to Astrid clean and whole, and let the others climb their deadline without her. No one would have blamed her. She had nothing to prove and a dead partner's memory to honor by living.

But Tenzin was going, because he would not abandon a team on the mountain, it was not in him. And Priya was going, because someone with a medical kit had to. And Mara was going, untrained, into the death zone with a flawed machine on her back. And Owen, carrying his father, would push too hard.

If Vera went down, the only two people on that mountain who would turn around when the mountain said turn around would be reduced to one. Tenzin could not watch everyone.

She thought: I came to set down a weight, and instead I am being handed everyone else's.

She went to find Daniel in the gray hour and said, "I'm coming. But you take my call on the ridge. If I say turn, we turn. That's my condition."

He looked at her for a long moment. "Agreed," he said. He even meant it, then. The hard thing about Daniel, she would think later, was that he meant almost everything when he said it.


Chapter 27: Into the Icefall Again

They left base at two in the morning on the seventeenth, six headlamps strung up into the dark, the cold a knife in the lungs.

The icefall had changed in the days they'd been gone. The mountain had shifted it. Towers that had stood now leaned; a ladder they'd used was gone, swallowed, a new crevasse opened beside it. Tenzin read it all in the headlamp beam, patient, choosing.

They moved well. Even Mara moved well, frightened into competence, watching Vera's feet and placing her own where Vera placed hers. Owen wanted to lead and Tenzin would not let him.

"You go where I go," Tenzin said. "Not faster. The mountain is not a race. The first one to the top is sometimes only the first one to die."

They reached the top of the icefall as the sky paled, and Vera turned and looked back down it, the whole shattered river of ice they'd threaded, and felt the old clean fear come into her like cold water, and was grateful for it. Fear was the instrument that worked up here. Everything else lied.


Chapter 28: A Suit Fails

At Camp Two the first suit failed.

It was Owen's. He noticed it at dusk, melting snow, when his hands went suddenly cold inside the gloves and the hum at his back stopped. He checked the chest unit. The display was dead.

"Battery," he said. "It just — quit. Half a charge showing this morning."

Daniel was on it fast, too fast, swapping the pack, and the suit came back to life, the filaments warming. But Vera had seen his face in the headlamp before he controlled it, and it was the face of a man whose worst fear had just tapped him on the shoulder.

"The batteries," she said to him, low, away from the others. "They don't hold in the cold. That's the flaw. That's what Mara knows and you know and nobody's saying."

Daniel zipped the dead pack into a stuff sack. "They hold long enough," he said. "We carry spares. It's managed."

"And if a person can't reach a spare? In a storm? With dead hands?"

He did not answer. The wind came up the shoulder, and far to the south, low on the horizon, a smear of cloud sat that had not been there in the morning, and Vera looked at it for a long time.


Chapter 29: Camp Three

Camp Three sat on a narrow ledge hacked into the slope at seventy-three hundred meters, two small tents clinging to the ice, the world dropping away on every side into white and blue and the brown haze of the distant lowlands.

This was the edge of the death zone. Above here the body did not recover; it only spent itself. Every hour up here was borrowed against the climb down.

They crowded into the tents, three and three, the cold so total that the breath froze on the inner walls and sifted down on them like indoor snow. Vera, Tenzin, and Priya in one. Daniel, Owen, and Mara in the other.

"Sat's are low across the board," Priya said quietly, packing her oximeter away. "Mara's the worst. She's at fifty-eight percent. That's a person who should be in a hospital, not a tent at seventy-three hundred."

"Will she make the summit?" Vera asked.

"She might make it up. Up is adrenaline and stubbornness." Priya zipped her bag. "Down is the test. Down is where these numbers come to collect."

Outside, the wind had risen. The smear of cloud in the south had become a wall, low still, but coming. Tenzin watched it through the tent door without speaking. He did not have to speak. Vera knew that look. The storm was early.


Chapter 30: The Watch

They could not sleep at seventy-three hundred meters, none of them, only doze and wake gasping as the breathing reflex faltered in the thin air. Vera and Tenzin took turns watching the south.

"It's early," she said, at two in the morning, the two of them sitting up in the dark, watching the cloud wall by starlight. "The storm. It's a day early."

"Yes."

"If we summit tomorrow we descend into it."

"Yes."

She pulled her knees up inside the bag. "I told Daniel I'd make the call on the ridge. If I say turn, we turn. He agreed."

Tenzin was quiet. "He agreed at base camp," he said finally. "Where it was easy. On the ridge, with the summit a hundred meters off and a company falling down behind him, he will not turn. Owen will not turn. The young man wants the top more than he wants to live, he only does not know it yet." He looked at her. "You and I will make the call. But we may make it alone. Be ready for that."

Vera looked at the cloud wall, low and patient in the south, and thought of Jonas on the ridge ten years ago, taking one more step, and one more, toward the white dome. She thought: it is happening again. The same machine. The same mountain. And I am here again, and this time I will not go down and leave anyone to it.


Chapter 31: Before Dawn

They left Camp Three at four in the morning, headlamps small in the immensity, the cold beyond anything words held, the suits humming against their skins.

The ridge went up ahead of them, a knife of snow and rock against a sky so deep with stars it looked solid. Below, on both sides, the faces fell away into nothing.

Vera climbed near the back, watching them all. Tenzin led. Owen pressed close behind him, impatient, his headlamp swinging. Daniel in the middle, breathing hard. Mara behind Daniel, slow, slower than yesterday, and Priya beside her, hand on her pack.

The route bent toward the shoulder, toward the place. Vera came around the corner and there it was, the spot where she had clipped past Jonas and grabbed his sleeve, recognizable even in the dark by the shape of the rock, and she felt him there, not as a ghost but as a fact, the way a scar is a fact.

*You go down, I'll be right behind you.*

She climbed past the place. She did not stop. There was a job to do above it now, six living people to get up and back down, and the dead did not need her stopping. They needed her not making their mistake.


Chapter 32: The System Fails

The sun came up the world's edge and the temperature dropped, the way it does at altitude before it climbs, the dawn cold the killing cold, and Mara's suit died.

She did not notice at first. She was too deep in the trance of the slow climb, one step, breathe four times, one step. It was Priya, beside her, who saw her stop moving her fingers, saw her start to weave.

"Mara. Mara, look at me. Your suit's off. Your suit's dead."

Mara looked down at the dark chest unit. Her face was waxen, the skin at her cheeks gone the dead white of frostbite beginning. "It's cold," she said, with a vague surprise. "It got cold."

Priya was already digging for the spare battery, fingers clumsy in the thick gloves. She got the pack swapped. The suit hummed. But the warmth came back into a body that had already been robbed, and the white patches on Mara's face did not flush, and her fingers, when Priya peeled a glove to check, were the color of candle wax.

"Daniel," Priya called up the ridge, sharp. "Daniel. Stop. We have a problem."

Up ahead, Daniel did not stop. He called back, thin on the wind, "How bad? We're close. We're so close. Can she keep moving?"

And Vera, watching, understood that the moment had come, the one Tenzin had foreseen, and that the machine was about to ask its question for real.


Chapter 33: Turn Her Around

They clustered on a wide place in the ridge, the wind picking up now, the cloud wall in the south risen to half the sky.

"She has early frostbite, face and hands," Priya said. "Her suit failed in the cold and it'll fail again, spare or no spare. She needs to go down now, while she can still walk. Every meter higher is a meter she may not get back."

"We turn her around," Vera said. "Someone takes her down. The rest is just argument."

Daniel's eyes were fixed up the ridge, where the summit dome sat catching the new sun, close, so close, a hundred and fifty meters of height left, no more. "If she goes down we don't get the footage of the system summiting on a person. That's the whole—" He stopped, hearing himself.

"That's the whole reason. Say it." Vera's voice was very level. "Her hands or the footage. You're standing on a ridge at eight thousand meters choosing between a woman's hands and a company's launch."

"That's not—"

"It is exactly that. That's all it's ever been." She turned to the others. "She goes down. Now. Who takes her?"

And the team stood on the knife of the ridge in the rising wind, and the storm came on in the south, and the question hung in the cold thin air waiting for an answer.


Chapter 34: The Split

In the end it split the way these things split, by who could not say no to the top and who could not say no to a person.

"I'll take her down," Priya said. "I'm the doctor. It's mine."

"You can't lower her alone if she stops walking," Tenzin said. "It needs two." He looked at the summit, then at Vera, a long look, and she read it: *I will go up, because if these fools go up someone must be with them who knows the way down in a storm.* "I will go up with them and bring them down fast. You take Mara with the doctor."

It was the right division and it cut Vera like a blade. To turn here, a hundred and fifty meters below the summit she had failed to reach for ten years, to turn her back on it again.

"No," she heard herself say. "You take Mara down with Priya, Tenzin. You're the strongest. You're the only one who can lower her through the steep ground below Three in this wind. I'll go up with Daniel and Owen and turn them around at the top whether they like it or not, and bring them down."

Tenzin held her eyes. Then he nodded, once. He understood the trade. He took the surer task and gave her the harder one, because she had asked, and because between the two of them there were no longer any debts.


Chapter 35: Owen on Top

Owen reached the summit first.

Vera was forty meters below when she saw him stop, saw him stand on the small white dome with nothing above him but sky, saw him lift both arms. She expected a shout. There was none. He stood with his arms up and then he let them fall, and by the time she came up the last slope to him he was sitting in the snow, and his face inside the frost-rimed hood was not triumphant. It was empty.

"That's it?" he said. His voice was small. "I thought it would feel like something."

"It feels like the air running out," Vera said, kneeling beside him, checking his eyes, his hands. "That's all it ever feels like up here. The feeling you came for is at the bottom. It's the going home."

He looked out over the curve of the earth, the brown world far below, the white peaks ranked away to the horizon like waves. "My dad never made it up here," he said. "All that, the cheap rope, the budget, the dying afterward — he never even stood here. I did. And it's nothing. It's just cold and there's nothing here."

"Then you learned the thing," Vera said. "Now stand up. We have to go down. The mountain doesn't give credit for the summit. Only for the return."


Chapter 36: The Eighth

And then she stood on it herself.

She had not meant to make a moment of it. She had meant to tag the top, turn the men around, and go. But she came up the last three steps to the highest point and there was nowhere higher, the mountain ran out under her boots into sky, and she stopped.

The eighth summit. The one she had sworn the mountain could keep. She had climbed seven of the world's highest places and turned her back on this one for ten years, and now she stood on it, and the wind tore at her, and the storm rose black in the south, and she had perhaps ten minutes she could afford.

It did not feel like triumph. Tenzin was right; the mountain was honest, and the honest thing was that standing here changed nothing. Jonas was still dead below the step. The rope had still parted. She had still gone down.

But she had come back. She had carried the weight all the way up here, and she found, standing on the white dome with her lungs failing, that she could set it down. Not throw it off. Set it down, gently, the way you set down something you have carried a long way and will not need below.

"All right," she said aloud, to no one, to him. "All right, Jonas. I came back. Now I'm going home."


Chapter 37: The Relic

She found it as she turned to go.

Three feet below the summit on the lee side, where the wind had scoured the snow thin, a corner of color showed in the ice. She knew it before she crouched to it. The old orange of a particular jacket fabric, faded near to white, frozen flat into the slope. A scrap, no more. Torn loose somewhere below years ago and blown up here and welded into the mountain's skin near its very top.

His. It could only be his. The color was discontinued; he had been proud of that ugly orange jacket, had worn it on all seven of their climbs.

She did not dig it out. To dig it out would be to take it from the mountain, and the mountain had earned it. She crouched over the scrap of orange with the wind screaming and put one gloved hand flat on the ice beside it, not on it, beside it, for the space of three breaths, which was all she could spare.

"You got to the top after all," she said. "A piece of you. Stay, then. It suits you. You always wanted this one."

Then she stood, and turned her back on the summit and the scrap of orange both, and started down to where the living needed her, because that was the whole of the lesson and she had finally learned it.


Chapter 38: The Storm Arrives

It came up the south face like something released.

One moment the ridge was clear and the summit behind them, the next the cloud wall was on them, and the world went white, and the wind that had been rising became a thing that could throw a person off a mountain. The temperature fell past where the suits could fight it. Snow drove level, stinging, filling the air so the rope to the climber ahead vanished at six feet.

"Stay on the line," Vera shouted, and the wind took the words. "Hands on the rope. Don't let go of the rope."

Three of them: Vera, Owen, Daniel, roped together, Owen leading down because he was strongest, Vera last because she was surest. Below them somewhere, an hour ahead, Tenzin and Priya and Mara descending toward Camp Three. Above and around them, nothing now but white and wind and the long invisible fall on either side of the ridge they could no longer see.

This, Vera thought, with a clarity that was not the altitude lying but the truth, this is the day. This is exactly the day. Ten years gone and the mountain offers me the same examination over again. She gripped the rope and went down into the white after the others, counting steps, feeling for the line under the snow.


Chapter 39: Whiteout

There was no up or down in it, only the rope and the next step and the wind.

Owen, in front, kept losing the route. Twice he started down a slope that fell away too steep and Vera, feeling the rope pull wrong, hauled him back, shouting into the white. The fixed lines from the ascent were buried or gone. They navigated by the shape of the ridge under their feet, by the lay of the rock, by Vera's ten-year-old memory and Tenzin's two days of teaching.

Daniel, in the middle, was failing. He had spent everything driving up and had nothing for the down. He stopped too often. He sat once and Vera kicked his boot until he stood. The frostbite was in his face now too; the suits, in this cold, with the batteries draining fast, were not enough, had never been enough, were a machine sold by drowning men.

"How far?" Owen screamed back through the storm.

"Camp Three's below the step," Vera screamed. "We find the step, we find the fixed line below it, we find the camp. Keep going."

She did not say what she was thinking, which was that the step was where Jonas had fallen, where the rope had parted, and that they were descending toward it now in a storm worse than the one that killed him, three of them on a line, and the arithmetic of altitude was beginning, quietly, to do its work.


Chapter 40: The Fall

It happened at the step.

They reached it by feel, the short steep pitch of rock and ice that dropped off the ridge toward the easier ground above Camp Three. The old fixed line should have been there. It was gone, or buried. Vera set Owen to down-climb it first, on belay from a hasty anchor, the wind trying to peel them off the mountain.

Daniel went second. Halfway down the step his crampon skated on rock under thin snow, and his exhausted hands did not catch, and he came off.

He fell the length of the pitch and twenty meters of slope below it before the rope to Owen came tight and the anchor held, snow flying up where he plowed to a stop. The line did not part. Vera had checked the anchor twice; the rope was good; it held. That, at least, was different from ten years ago. The rope held.

But Daniel did not get up.

"Daniel!" Owen, below, reached him first, floundering through the drift. "He's awake — Vera, he's awake but his leg, his leg's wrong—"

Vera came down the step into the wind, into the white, toward the two dark shapes below, and the mountain howled around her, and somewhere far down the slope Camp Three waited, and she began, as she came, to do the arithmetic she had come all this way to refuse to do.


Chapter 41: The Math

Daniel's lower leg was broken; she could see the wrong angle of the boot even through the spindrift. He was conscious, gray, gulping at the thin air.

"Can you put weight on it?" she asked, kneeling.

He tried. He screamed, short and bitten-off, and went grayer. No.

The arithmetic laid itself out, cold and exact, the way it does. A man who cannot walk, at seventy-six hundred meters, in a full storm. Camp Three four hundred vertical meters below, unreachable by nightfall if they had to lower him every step. Two able climbers — herself and Owen — and one of them, Owen, with sat-levels that had been failing since the summit. No radio contact in the storm. No help coming up into this. The suits draining.

To lower Daniel meter by meter through this would take all night and they did not have all night in them; the cold would take all three before they reached the camp.

To leave him and go for help was to leave him to die alone, and there was no help to go to.

To stay with him was to die with him.

She had run this calculation once before, in a way, ten years ago, and the mountain had run it for her, and Jonas had died. Now it was hers to run, fully, with her own hands.

"Owen," she said. "Anchor here. We dig. We're not lowering him in this. We dig in and we wait it out and we lower him at first light when we can see. Move."


Chapter 42: Tenzin Below

Four hundred meters below, Tenzin had gotten Mara and Priya to Camp Three.

It had taken everything. Mara had stopped walking twice on the steep ground and he had short-roped her, lowered her, hauled her, while Priya broke trail and the storm came down. They reached the tents as the white closed in, and got Mara into a bag, and got the stove going, and Priya worked on Mara's hands and face in the roaring dark.

Tenzin sat in the tent door and looked up into the white where the others were, and could not see ten feet, and knew.

"They won't make it down tonight," Priya said, not looking up from Mara's blackening fingers.

"No."

"If they're caught above the step in this—"

"Then they dig in, if Vera is leading, and they live, if they are strong enough." He pulled the hood of the dead suit off his head; the battery had gone an hour ago and he had climbed the last of it on his own warmth, the way he had climbed before any of these machines existed. "She will not leave anyone. I know her. The danger is not that she leaves a man. The danger is that she stays too long with him."

He could not go back up. To go up into that, exhausted, after lowering Mara, would only add a fourth body to the count. He sat in the door instead, all night, and watched the white, and left the stove burning bright as a signal that no one above could possibly see, and did the only thing left, which was to keep three alive at Camp Three so that someone would be there if the others came down.


Chapter 43: Mara's Hands

In the tent, Priya did the work she hated most.

Mara's fingers were frozen to the second joint, both hands. Her face would scar but live. The hands were the question. Frostbite this deep, this high, with no chance of evacuation for days — Priya had seen where it went. She did not say it. She warmed what could be warmed and protected what could not and kept Mara talking to keep her conscious.

"Did we get it," Mara murmured, half under. "The footage. Did the system — did it work."

"No," Priya said. "It didn't work. The batteries died in the cold. The system failed. It failed on you, Mara. It was always going to fail on someone. That's the truth and you can take it down the mountain with you."

Mara's cracked lips moved. Almost a laugh. "Good," she said. "Good. Then it's over. Then there's nothing left up here worth dying for." She closed her eyes. "Tell them. Tell the investors. The suit doesn't work. Somebody has to—" She drifted.

Priya pulled the bag tighter around her and looked at the ruined hands and thought about all the ways a company's spreadsheet could reach eight thousand meters and take a woman's fingers, and she put it in her memory, exact, to say later, loudly, to the right people. That was a thing she could still do. Up here there was little else.


Chapter 44: The Long Night

They dug a snow trench below the step, the three of them, Daniel useless with his leg, Vera and Owen hacking at the slope with their axes while the storm tried to kill them.

It was not a shelter. It was a slot in the snow out of the worst of the wind, barely. They got Daniel into it, and themselves around him, and pulled the one emergency bivouac sack over the three of them, and there they stayed while the night came down and the temperature fell past anything the dead suits could pretend to fight.

The arithmetic kept running in Vera's head and she kept refusing its answer.

To survive an open bivouac at seventy-six hundred in a storm you needed to keep moving, keep the blood going, fight the sleep that was the cold's quiet assassin. So she made them move. She made Owen recite, hour by hour — his sisters' names, the route they'd climbed, anything — and she pinched Daniel's face when he drifted, and she moved her own toes inside her boots and could not feel them and moved them anyway.

"Why," Daniel said once, in the black middle of it. "Why didn't you just go down. You and Owen. You'd have made it. You stayed for me."

"Because I went down once," Vera said into the wind. "Ten years ago. I went down and left a man on a ridge in a storm and he died. I'm not doing it twice. So you're going to live, Daniel, whether you want to or not, because I need you to."


Chapter 45: Owen Carries

By the gray middle of the night Daniel had stopped answering, and Vera knew that when the wind dropped enough to move, they would have to carry him, and that she could not carry him, and that Owen, failing, was the only engine they had.

The wind dropped a little before dawn. Not much. Enough.

"We move now," Vera said. "Owen. You and me. We get him up and we get him down to the camp. Tenzin's there. There's a stove. Four hundred meters. We can do four hundred meters."

Owen's face in the first gray light was old. The night had aged him a decade. But he got up, and he got Daniel up across his back, the broken leg dangling, Daniel half-conscious and moaning, and he started down the slope with a man on his back at seventy-six hundred meters, and Vera went below him on the rope, anchoring, guiding his feet, taking what weight she could.

It should not have been possible. Owen's numbers said it was not possible. But he carried his father's name down that mountain with another man on his back, step after step, while Vera called the route below him, and somewhere in the second hour of it she understood she was watching a man become the thing he had climbed his whole life to become.

"Don't stop," she said. "Don't you stop. There's a stove down there. There's people. Don't stop."


Chapter 46: The Descent

Hour after hour, down through the clearing storm.

The wind fell as they lost height, and the white thinned, and the slope eased, and still it took everything. Owen fell twice with Daniel on his back and got up twice. Vera's own hands had stopped working; she belayed by wrapping the rope and leaning her body into it because her fingers would not close. The world had narrowed to the next step, the next anchor, the dark shape of Owen above her with the burden on his back.

Below, the storm began to tear open, and through a gap she saw, four hundred meters down and a little east, a point of orange light in the gray — the stove at Camp Three, burning, exactly where Tenzin had said he would keep it.

"Light," she croaked up to Owen. "Owen. There's the camp. There's the light. Look down. We're almost there."

He did not look. He could not spare the motion. But he made a sound that was not a word and kept going, down toward the orange light in the gray, and Vera went below him, and behind and above them the summit stood unchanged in the clearing sky, white and indifferent, having taken nothing this time, this once, because four exhausted people had refused to let it.


Chapter 47: The Count

They reached Camp Three at midmorning, and Tenzin came up the last slope to meet them, and between them they got Daniel into the second tent.

Then there was the count, the one Vera had been dreading, the cold arithmetic of who had come down off the high ground.

Vera: alive, hands frostbitten, would keep most of her fingers. Owen: alive, spent past the bottom of himself, would recover, changed. Daniel: alive, broken leg, frostbite, would walk again on a mountain or anywhere else only with luck and time, and would never be the same man, which might be a mercy. Mara: alive, the hands the question, the launch dead and good riddance. Priya: alive, the one who had done the saving in the tents. Tenzin: alive, who had carried three to safety and then watched all night.

Six up. Six down. It was not nothing. It was, Vera understood, sitting in the tent door with the sun coming out at last on the cleared peak, the rarest thing the mountain gave: everyone home.

She thought of Jonas. Six down, and one still up there, ten years now, a scrap of orange near the top. The grief did not lessen. But it sat differently. It sat the way a thing sits that has been set down on purpose, in the right place, rather than dropped or thrown.


Chapter 48: The Icefall

The mountain was not finished with them. It never is until you are off it.

They rested a day at Camp Three, then two more days getting down to base, Daniel lowered the steep ground on a makeshift sled of ropes and a sleeping pad, Tenzin and Owen working the lines, Priya tending the wounded. The icefall, in the warming days after the storm, had come half to pieces.

They went through it at three in the morning, in the cold, when the ice was hardest and least likely to move, and even so a sérac the size of a house let go a hundred meters to their right as they crossed, the air shuddering with it, and they stood frozen in their tracks until the roar passed and the snow settled and the mountain was quiet again.

"Move," Tenzin said. "Now. Fast. While it's listening to itself."

They moved. They threaded the last of the broken ice with Daniel on the sled, and came out the bottom onto the dead flat glacier as the sky paled, and there was base camp ahead, the yellow tents, the prayer flags, the cook smoke rising straight in the still dawn air.

Vera walked the last hundred meters of the glacier and did not look back at the mountain until she was standing among the tents, and then she turned and looked at it once, the whole white height of it, and felt nothing she could name, and everything.


Chapter 49: The Reckoning

The satellite phone worked at base camp, and the reckoning came down the line in pieces.

Daniel made the calls he had to make, lying in the medical tent with his leg splinted. Vera heard one of them. He told the man on the other end, in a voice gone flat and clear with everything burned out of it, that there was no footage, that the system had failed, that it had taken a woman's hands and nearly six lives, and that he would not be saying any different at any launch or to any investor, and that the man could keep the rest of the contract money, because Daniel had found, at seventy-six hundred meters, that there were things he would not sell after all.

Mara, beside Vera, listened to this with her bandaged hands in her lap and nodded slowly.

"He found it late," Mara said. "But he found it."

"Most people don't find it at all," Vera said.

Priya was already drafting the report that would go to the climbing federation, to the press if it had to, the cold true account of a company that sent an untested machine into the death zone to save its balance sheet. She wrote it in block letters because her fingers, too, were not working well, and she wrote it anyway.

The reckoning would go on for months, down in the world. Up here it was already over. Up here the mountain had said the only thing it had to say, which was the truth, and they had all heard it.


Chapter 50: Home

She flew out of Lukla in clear weather, the small plane lifting off the canted runway and banking south, and through the window she saw Khumri one last time, far off now, white above the brown hills, the summit ridge cutting the sky.

She did not feel the old cold come into her hands. She noticed that. Her body had stopped bracing against the mountain. It had let go of it somewhere on the descent, set it down with the rest.

In Kathmandu she sat in the courtyard where the caged birds sang and wrote a letter to Astrid she would deliver in person, because some things are not for the mail. *I went,* she wrote, *and I came back. I kept the promise. He got to the top after all — a piece of him did, near the summit, in that orange jacket. I think he'd be glad. I think he'd say it suited him.* She did not write the rest, the count, the hands, the broken leg, the company. That was for telling, slowly, on the warped porch, with coffee going cold.

She would teach again in the fall. She would not climb another eight-thousander; there was no eighth left to need, and the seven were enough, and the mountain had given her the only thing she had gone for, which was the way down off it, finally, alive.

The plane leveled. The mountain slid out of the window's edge and was gone. Vera Sorensen sat with her bandaged hands in her lap and her grief set down gently beside her, and went home.
