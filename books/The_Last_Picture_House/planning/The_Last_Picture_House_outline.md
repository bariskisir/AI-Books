# The Last Picture House — Outline

## Premise

Frank Meacham has owned and operated the Picture House in Corinth, Kansas for thirty-seven years. As the town's population dwindles and a multiplex opens thirty miles away, he faces the end of an era. The roof leaks, the projector wheezes, and Mayor Billings sees the theater's lot as prime redevelopment real estate. With Rose, his aging projectionist who has threaded every reel for forty-two years, Frank must decide whether to fight for the fading dream or accept that some things, like small towns and the movies that sustained them, cannot last forever.

## Core Cast

- **Frank Meacham** — Owner of the Picture House, 68. Widower. Stubborn, quiet, knows every seat squeak and floorboard creak in the building. His wife Eleanor died nine years ago.
- **Rose Delgado** — Projectionist, 64. Has worked at the Picture House since she was 22. Sharp, no-nonsense, can diagnose a projector problem by sound alone. She remembers every film that ever screened.
- **Mayor Billings** — Mayor of Corinth, 57. Pragmatic businessman who sees the empty storefronts and declining tax base. Not a villain, but a man who believes in progress over nostalgia.

## 50 Chapter Plan

### Act I: The State of Things (Ch. 1–10)

1. Frank arrives before dawn and opens the theater; he counts the slow Tuesday ahead.
2. The morning ritual — checking seats, heating the oil for popcorn, flipping the neon sign on.
3. Rose arrives and walks through her projection booth routine; she can feel something wrong in the machine's hum.
4. A slow day. Frank counts twelve patrons and knows the numbers don't lie.
5. Mayor Billings visits after hours with news: the building failed its latest inspection.
6. Frank remembers opening night in 1987 with Eleanor beside him, the theater full.
7. Rose proposes a classic film series to bring people back.
8. The town council meeting where Billings presents main street redevelopment plans.
9. Frank visits the diner and hears the old-timers reminisce about the theater's heyday.
10. Rose and Frank drive the county roads posting flyers for the classic series.

### Act II: The Fight (Ch. 11–20)

11. The first classic night — "Casablanca" draws a crowd that hasn't been seen in years.
12. A teenager named Maya wanders in for the first time and discovers old movies.
13. The projector throws a gear mid-show; Rose saves the night with a spare part she kept.
14. Billings makes Frank a buyout offer: enough to pay off debts, not enough to relocate.
15. Frank's daughter Sarah calls from Omaha, worried about her father's health.
16. The second classic night — "The Philadelphia Story" — another good crowd finds the theater.
17. Frank discovers water damage in the basement from a cracked pipe; the repair is urgent.
18. Rose admits she's been offered a job at the multiplex in Hays.
19. The county paper runs a front-page story about the Picture House's struggle.
20. Frank turns down Billings' offer and commits to the fight.

### Act III: Cracks Appear (Ch. 21–30)

21. The roof repair estimate arrives: twelve thousand dollars Frank does not have.
22. A fundraising committee forms from the theater's elderly regulars.
23. Maya brings friends to see "Rear Window" and the balcony fills with teenagers.
24. Billings argues at council that the theater is a money pit the town cannot support.
25. Frank and Rose share a beer after hours and remember Eleanor's last visit to the theater.
26. The bake sale raises three hundred forty dollars — a fraction of what's needed.
27. Frank has chest pains at the concession stand; Rose drives him to the clinic.
28. Rose runs the projector alone for a week while Frank recovers at home.
29. A young couple gets engaged mid-show during "An Affair to Remember."
30. Billings announces a development corporation is interested in the theater lot.

### Act IV: The Long Goodbye (Ch. 31–40)

31. Frank considers Billings' offer again — he is exhausted and the numbers do not work.
32. Rose confronts Frank about giving up; she will not go quietly.
33. Sarah visits from Omaha and sees her father's struggle reflected in the empty seats.
34. The town holds a "Save the Picture House" benefit night with a packed house.
35. Frank discovers Eleanor left a small savings account he had forgotten about entirely.
36. The development corporation increases its offer; Billings brings papers to Frank's door.
37. Rose trains Maya on the projector, passing along forty-two years of knowledge.
38. A spring storm damages the marquee; it hangs crooked and Frank cannot afford to fix it.
39. Frank gives an interview to the newspaper about the theater's forty-year history.
40. The final film night is announced — a double feature of the town's two most-requested films.

### Act V: The Final Reel (Ch. 41–50)

41. Frank makes his decision: one last season through summer, then close the doors.
42. The farewell series is planned — the community chooses its favorites.
43. Billings softens and offers to help pursue a historical designation for the building.
44. Frank and Rose clean the projection booth one last time, finding relics of decades past.
45. The final week arrives; every show is sold out, people driving from three counties.
46. Frank screens "The Wizard of Oz" — Dorothy leaving Kansas hits different now.
47. The last night. Frank stands before the audience and speaks without a script.
48. After the final credits roll, Frank sits alone in the dark theater.
49. Rose turns off the projector for the last time and hands Frank the reel.
50. Frank locks the doors at dawn but leaves the marquee light on — a small defiance.
