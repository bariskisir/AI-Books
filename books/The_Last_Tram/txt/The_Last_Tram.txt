Chapter 1: The Number 28

The depot clock read 11:47 PM when Jorge Mendes signed the logbook. His fingers moved by habit across the page — date, time, tram number, signature. He had done this for eighteen years and did not need to look at the paper.

The number 28 waited on its tracks, the paint faded to a yellow that was almost beige. Older than most of the drivers. Older than some of the buildings it passed. Jorge had driven newer trams when management offered them, but he always came back to this one. It fit him the way an old coat fits.

He climbed into the cab and hung his jacket on the hook. The controls were worn smooth where his hand rested. He pulled the lever and the tram groaned to life. The overhead wire crackled. The lights inside flickered once and steadied.

A quarter to midnight. The city was winding down. Lisbon in the small hours had a different texture — softer, older, like a photograph left in the sun too long. Jorge pulled away from the depot and turned onto the main line.

The first stop was empty. So was the second. At Martim Moniz a man in a brown suit boarded without looking at Jorge. He took a seat near the back and stared out the window.

Jorge drove on. The streets were wet from an earlier rain. The cobblestones gleamed under the streetlights. He checked his rearview mirror. The man in the brown suit had not moved.

The air changed just before midnight. Jorge felt it in his chest, a shift in pressure like descending a hill too fast. The tram's lights dimmed and brightened. The overhead wire began to glow — not with the usual blue spark but a pale gold.

Jorge glanced at his watch. 11:59 PM.

The tram rounded a corner and the street ahead was wrong.

Chapter 2: Cobblestones

The buildings on Rua da Sao Bento were older than they should have been. The shop fronts were different. A grocery store that Jorge knew had been a bank since 1989 was selling wine from a wooden barrel. A woman in a scarf and an apron stood in the doorway, smoke rising from a cigarette.

Jorge blinked. The tram kept moving.

The man in the brown suit had not reacted. He sat with the same stillness, his hands folded over the handle of an umbrella that had not been there a moment ago.

Jorge checked his mirror again. The man's face was gaunt, his eyes fixed on some middle distance. He did not look out the window. He did not look at Jorge.

The streets grew quieter. The modern streetlights had been replaced by older ones — globes of pale yellow on iron posts. The light they cast was softer, less certain. Shadows pooled in the doorways.

Jorge's hands were steady on the controls. Eighteen years of driving had taught him that panic did not help. A tram on rails was committed to its path. He could not stop in the middle of the track. He could only keep going and see where the line took him.

A man in a flat cap waited at the next stop. He carried a newspaper under his arm. Jorge slowed. The man boarded, dropped a coin into the fare box, and sat down. His newspaper was the Diario de Noticias. Jorge caught the date on the masthead: December 5, 1955.

He closed the doors and drove on.

Chapter 3: The Light

The glow from the overhead wire intensified. Jorge could see it reflected in the windows — a golden thread that ran parallel to the tram, pulsing like a vein. The air inside the cab was warm. Not the heat of the motor. Something else.

The tram climbed the hill toward Chiado. The buildings on either side pressed closer. Shutters were closed. A cat darted across the street and vanished into a gap between walls.

Jorge had driven this route thousands of times. He knew every pothole, every gradient, every place where the track pinched and the wheels screeched. But this was not the route he knew. The streets had the same names but not the same faces.

At the Chiado stop, a woman waited. She was young, mid-twenties, with dark hair pinned up under a scarf. She wore a green coat. Even from a distance, Jorge could see that the coat was well-made, carefully kept, a piece of clothing that had been loved.

She stepped aboard. Her shoes made a light sound on the wooden floor. She paid with a coin that Jorge had not seen in circulation in his lifetime.

He looked at it in his palm. A ten-escudo piece from 1954. The face of the ship on one side. The shield on the other.

He put it in his pocket and closed the doors.

The woman with the green coat sat near the front. She smelled of rain and something floral. Violet, maybe. She folded her hands in her lap and looked ahead.

Chapter 4: The Other Lisbon

The tram moved through a city that Jorge recognized and did not recognize. The bones were the same — the hills, the river at the bottom of every descent, the castle watching from above. But the flesh was different.

A black market operated openly on a corner near Carmo. Men in overcoats traded in whispers. A boy of maybe twelve ran between them carrying a package wrapped in brown paper. He disappeared into a doorway.

Jorge saw a cinema with a hand-painted sign. "Um Homem do Rio," it read. The letters were bold, the colors faded. A man on a ladder was changing the lettering by hand.

The passengers who boarded were all from the same world. Women in hats and gloves. Men in suits with narrow lapels. A priest in a cassock that was shiny at the elbows. They paid with coins that no longer existed. They sat without speaking.

The tram was a vessel of quiet. The only sounds were the rattle of wheels on track and the occasional cough from a passenger. Jorge drove with the same steady hand he always used. But his mind was racing.

He tried to make sense of what he was seeing. A dream. A hallucination. A stroke. He tested each explanation and found them wanting. The coins in his pocket had weight. The passengers had shadows. The street had texture beneath the tram's wheels.

At Belem, the green-coated woman stood. She nodded at Jorge as she passed. A small gesture, almost imperceptible. Then she stepped off into the night and walked toward the river.

Jorge watched her go. The tower of Belem stood dark against the water. She walked toward it and did not look back.

Chapter 5: Black Market

The return was as seamless as the crossing. The tram rounded a corner and the light shifted. The glow from the overhead wire faded. The passengers were gone. Jorge was alone.

He checked his watch. 12:47 AM. The crossing had lasted exactly forty-seven minutes.

He drove the rest of the route on autopilot. His body knew the motions. His mind was still in the other Lisbon, watching the green coat disappear toward the river.

He pulled into the depot at 1:15 AM. He sat in the cab for a long time with the motor off. The silence was heavy.

He reached into his pocket and pulled out the ten-escudo coin. It was real. He held it up to the light. The details were sharp. The metal was cool.

He put it back in his pocket and went home.

He did not sleep. He lay in bed and stared at the ceiling and tried to convince himself that the coin did not exist. But it was in his pocket on the nightstand. He could feel its weight from across the room.

In the morning, he took it to a museum.

Chapter 6: The Green Coat

The archivist at the Museu da Cidade was a young man with wire-rimmed glasses and an enthusiasm that Jorge found exhausting. His name was Rui. He examined the coin with a magnifying glass and made small noises of appreciation.

"Where did you get this?" Rui asked.

"Found it," Jorge said.

"Found it. In Lisbon."

"Yes."

Rui turned the coin over. "This is uncirculated. Look at the strike. It never went into regular use. This is collector quality."

Jorge said nothing.

"You should have this appraised," Rui said. "It could be worth — "

"I don't want to sell it."

Rui looked at him with curiosity. "Where did you really get it?"

Jorge took the coin back. "Thank you for your time."

He left the museum with the coin in his pocket. The weight of it felt heavier now. It was proof of something he could not name.

He walked through the streets of modern Lisbon. The sun was bright. The tourists were thick in the Baixa. A tram passed him, the number 28, full of people taking photographs.

He watched it go. He knew what they did not know. The route was not what they thought it was.

Chapter 7: First Words

A week passed. Jorge drove his regular shifts. He did not mention the midnight crossing to anyone. He did not know how.

The conversation played out in his head. "I drove into 1955." The words sounded absurd. He would sound like a fool. Or worse, like a man who needed help.

He did not need help. He needed answers.

The next Sunday, he took the midnight shift. He told himself it was routine. The shift needed covering. Someone had to do it.

He pulled out of the depot at 11:47 PM. The same time. The same tram. The same route.

The glow returned at midnight. The air shifted. The buildings changed.

The man in the brown suit was waiting at Martim Moniz. He boarded and sat in the same seat. He did not acknowledge Jorge.

At Chiado, the woman in the green coat was waiting. She stepped aboard. She paid with another ten-escudo coin. She sat near the front.

This time, she spoke.

"Does this tram still go to Belem?"

Her voice was soft. The accent was Lisbon, but older. A pronunciation that had faded from the city.

"Yes," Jorge said. "It still goes to Belem."

She nodded. She folded her hands in her lap.

"It's late to be out," Jorge said.

She looked at him. Her eyes were dark, steady. "I like the night," she said. "It's quieter."

"It is," Jorge said.

She smiled. A small, careful smile. Then she looked away.

Chapter 8: The Ghost Stop

The tram stopped at a station that did not exist on any modern map. Jorge saw it in the distance first — a shelter of pale stone with a black sign that read "Rua dos Remolares."

The name was familiar. He had read it in old books about Lisbon. The street had been demolished in the 1960s to make way for a road that was never built.

Three passengers boarded. Two women and a man. The man carried a suitcase tied with rope. The women wore black dresses and carried shopping bags made of string.

They paid with coins. Jorge accepted them. He did not look at their faces too long. He felt that looking too long would break something.

The man with the suitcase sat heavily. He was sweating. He kept looking out the window as if he expected someone to follow.

The women talked in low voices. The topic was a shortage of something — sugar, maybe. They discussed it with the practiced resignation of people who had lived through scarcity.

Jorge drove on. The streets grew narrower. The lamps were few. The shadows between them were deep.

At the Belem stop, the woman in the green coat stood. She walked to the front and paused.

"You have a kind face," she said. "Thank you for the ride."

Before Jorge could answer, she stepped off into the night.

Chapter 9: Return

The tram returned to the present at 12:47 AM. The passengers were gone. Jorge was alone again.

He drove back to the depot on autopilot. His mind was with the woman in the green coat. Her face. Her voice. The way she said "thank you" as if it meant something real.

He parked the tram and sat in the silence. The coins in his pocket were heavier now. He pulled them out. Three ten-escudo pieces. A five. A scattering of smaller denominations.

He lined them up on the dashboard. Five coins from a world that no longer existed. Five pieces of proof.

He did not know what to do with proof.

He left them in his jacket pocket. He went home. He ate bread and cheese standing at the kitchen counter. He drank a beer that tasted like nothing.

He lay in bed and stared at the ceiling and thought about her face. The way the streetlight had caught her cheekbones. The way she walked away from the tram, toward the river, toward a different century.

He wanted to see her again.

The thought frightened him. He was a man of routines. He drove a tram. He did not fall in love with passengers who had been dead for sixty years.

But he was falling. He could feel it, a slow tilt in his chest, like the tram rounding a curve he had not seen coming.

Chapter 10: The Museum

Jorge went back to the museum. Rui was at the same desk, reading a book about azulejos. He looked up when Jorge walked in.

"You came back," Rui said.

"I have more coins."

Rui's eyebrows rose. Jorge placed the five coins on the counter. Rui picked them up one by one, turning each over with the same careful attention.

"These are all from the same period," Rui said. "1954, 1955. Uncirculated. Mint condition. This is — " He stopped. "This is a collection."

"It's not a collection."

"Then where — "

"I told you. I found them."

Rui put the coins down. He took off his glasses and cleaned them. It was a delaying tactic. Jorge recognized it because he used the same one himself.

"Listen," Rui said. "If these are stolen, I need to know."

"They're not stolen."

"Then help me understand. Five uncirculated coins from fifty years ago. Found. In Lisbon."

"Yes."

Rui stared at him. Jorge stared back. There was a challenge in the silence, a test of who would break first.

"You're not going to tell me," Rui said.

"No."

Rui sighed. He put his glasses back on. "I'll have a colleague look at these. A numismatist. She'll want to know the provenance."

"Tell her I found them."

"She won't believe you."

"Neither would I."

Jorge left the coins with Rui. He walked out of the museum into the bright Lisbon afternoon. The city was golden in the late sun. The trams clattered past. Everything was normal.

Except that he had coins from a dead world in his pocket. And he was counting the days until he could go back.

Chapter 11: Doubt

The week that followed was long. Jorge drove his shifts. He ate his meals. He slept badly.

Doubt crept in during the daylight hours. The midnight crossing felt impossible in the morning. The coins existed, but maybe they were fakes. Maybe he had picked them up somewhere without remembering. Maybe his mind was playing tricks.

He knew these explanations were weak. He held onto them anyway. Certainty was too heavy to carry.

On Thursday, he went to the library. He found a book about Lisbon in the 1950s. Photographs of streets that no longer existed. People in clothes like the clothes his passengers had worn. A picture of the Rua dos Remolares before it was demolished.

He stared at the photograph for a long time. The tram stop was there, a pale stone shelter. Passengers waited under its roof, their faces blurred by time.

He closed the book. He did not check it out. He left it on the table and walked out.

That night, he dreamed of the green coat. He was following it through streets that kept changing. The coat was always ahead, always turning a corner, always disappearing.

He woke up sweating. The clock said 3:17 AM.

He got up and made coffee. He sat at the kitchen table and watched the night through the window. Lisbon was dark and quiet. The trams were asleep in the depot.

He wanted to be driving. He wanted to be on the route. He wanted to see if the crossing still worked when he was not on shift.

He forced himself to wait. The rules were not clear, but he felt them nonetheless. The crossing happened at midnight. On Sunday. He had to respect the pattern.

He counted the hours.

Chapter 12: The Second Crossing

Sunday came. Jorge took the midnight shift. He was at the depot at 11:30, ten minutes early. He checked the tram himself, walking through the empty car, touching the wooden seats.

The old tram felt different tonight. Alive. The metal was warm under his hand.

He took his seat in the cab. He checked his watch. 11:47.

He pulled away from the depot. The city was quiet. The streets were wet again. Lisbon seemed to be waiting for something.

At Martim Moniz, the man in the brown suit was there. He boarded without a word. He took his seat at the back.

Jorge drove on. The pressure shifted. The glow appeared. The world changed.

The other Lisbon opened around him like a door swinging inward. The buildings were older. The light was softer. The air smelled of coal smoke and bread.

At Chiado, she was waiting. The green coat. The dark hair. She stepped aboard and paid with a coin that Jorge now recognized as part of a set.

She sat near the front. She did not speak immediately. Neither did Jorge.

The tram climbed the hill. The city spread out below, a sea of dark roofs and warm windows.

"Thank you," she said finally. "For last week. For the ride."

"You're welcome," Jorge said.

"I don't usually talk to drivers."

"I don't usually talk to passengers."

She smiled. It was fuller than the last one, less hesitant. "I'm Maria," she said.

Jorge hesitated. The Night Conductor had warned him, somehow, without words. Do not ask their names. Do not follow them.

But she had offered hers.

"Jorge," he said.

Chapter 13: Maria Again

Maria sat near the front for the rest of the ride. She asked questions about the tram. How old was it? How long had Jorge been driving it? Did he like the night shift?

He answered each one. The words came easier than he expected. There was a rhythm to conversation that felt natural, even in a tram that did not belong in its own time.

She told him about her life. She worked in a shop on Rua Garrett that sold buttons and thread. She lived with her aunt in a flat near the cathedral. She had been engaged once, but the war had taken him.

"Which war?" Jorge asked.

She looked at him strangely. "The war. In Europe."

He had forgotten. To her, the war was recent. It was still in the walls, in the rationing, in the faces of the men who carried suitcases and looked over their shoulders.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"It was a long time ago," she said. But the way she said it told him it was not.

They reached Belem. Maria stood. She walked to the front and stopped.

"Will you be here next week?" she asked.

"Yes," Jorge said. "I will."

She nodded. She stepped off the tram. She walked toward the river. The green coat faded into the darkness.

Jorge watched until he could not see her anymore. Then he closed the doors and drove on.

Chapter 14: The Cinema Memory

The next crossing, Maria talked about the cinema. There was a theater on Rua Garrett, the Cinema Sao Jorge, that showed foreign films. She had gone with her aunt to see "Um Homem do Rio."

"I remember the whole thing," she said. "The music. The way the hero walked. My aunt cried at the end."

"I know that film," Jorge said. It was a lie. He had looked it up after the first crossing. He had found a review in an old newspaper archive.

"You've seen it?" Maria asked.

"A long time ago."

"Was it as good as I remember?"

"I think it was better."

She laughed. It was a sound Jorge had not heard from her before. It changed her face. Made her younger. He wanted to hear it again.

"What made you love it?" he asked.

She thought about it. "The hero was not a hero," she said. "He was just a man who kept walking. He did not win because he was stronger. He won because he refused to stop."

Jorge understood. He drove a tram. He had been driving the same route for eighteen years. He did not stop either.

"Maybe that's the only way to win," he said.

She looked at him. Her eyes were dark and steady. "I think that's true."

The tram reached Belem. She did not stand immediately. She stayed in her seat for an extra moment, as if she was not ready to leave.

Then she stood. She walked to the front. She paused.

"Good night, Jorge."

"Good night, Maria."

She stepped off into the 1955 night. Jorge watched the green coat until it disappeared.

Chapter 15: The Return of Doubt

The crossing ended. Jorge was back in the present. The tram was empty.

He drove to the depot. He parked. He sat.

The doubt had not returned. That was the strange thing. He had expected to wake up from this, to find it was a dream or a delusion. But the coins were real. Maria was real. The other Lisbon was real.

He was a sane man in an impossible situation. He accepted it.

He went home. He made dinner. He opened a bottle of wine that had been in the cupboard for months. He drank it slowly, thinking about nothing and everything.

The days passed. He drove his regular shifts. He did not tell anyone about Maria. He did not tell anyone about the crossing.

On Wednesday, he went back to the museum. Rui was not at the desk. A woman with gray hair and glasses was examining a pottery fragment.

"Can I help you?" she asked.

"I'm looking for Rui."

"He's in the back. Do you want me to get him?"

"No. It's fine."

Jorge left. He did not want to talk about the coins. He did not want to explain. The explanation belonged to him alone.

He walked through the Alfama district. The streets were narrow, the washing hung between buildings. A tram passed, the number 28, full of tourists. They were photographing everything. They did not know that the real Lisbon was hiding just beneath the surface, waiting for midnight.

Jorge watched the tram disappear around a corner. He checked his watch. Four days until Sunday.

He could wait.

Chapter 16: The First Warning

The fourth crossing brought a change. The man in the brown suit was gone. In his place, standing at the back of the tram, was a tall figure in an antique uniform.

Jorge saw him in the mirror. A peaked cap. Brass buttons. A face that was all angles and shadows. He was not a passenger from 1955. He was something else.

The figure moved forward. His footsteps made no sound on the wooden floor. He stopped beside the cab.

"Driver," he said.

His voice was low, resonant. It seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his chest.

"You do not ask their names," the figure said. "You do not follow them when they leave."

Jorge's hands tightened on the controls. "I didn't follow her."

"You told her your name."

"That's not following."

The figure was silent for a moment. His eyes were dark, unreadable. "The rules exist for a reason. The seam is fragile. Too much weight will break it."

"What are you?"

"I am the Conductor. I keep the route open. And I decide who rides."

Maria was waiting at Chiado. She stepped aboard. She saw the Conductor and her face changed. A flicker of fear.

"You," she said.

"Senhora," the Conductor said. He nodded once. Then he walked to the back of the tram and stood in the shadows.

Maria sat near the front. She did not look at Jorge. She did not speak for the entire ride.

When she got off at Belem, she did not say good night.

Chapter 17: No Names

The Conductor stood in the shadows for the entire ride. Jorge could feel his presence behind him, a weight at the edge of his awareness.

When the crossing ended and the tram returned to the present, the Conductor was still there. He had not disappeared with the other passengers.

"You can see me," Jorge said.

"I am not bound by the same rules."

"Why are you doing this? Watching me?"

The Conductor moved forward. The light from the street caught his face. He was older than Jorge had thought. His skin was lined, his eyes deep-set. He had the look of a man who had been awake for a very long time.

"I was once a driver," the Conductor said. "I found the seam. I explored it. I thought I could control it."

"And?"

"The seam controls you. I spent too much time on the other side. Now I cannot leave."

"There has to be a way."

"There is no way. The seam takes what it wants. I gave it my life. It gave me this." He gestured at his uniform, at himself. "I keep the route running. I make sure the dead stay dead."

"Maria is not dead."

"She will be. She was. It does not matter. The seam does not follow your timeline."

Jorge gripped the controls. "I can save her."

"You cannot save someone who was already lost. She died in 1957. You are watching a photograph. You cannot change the photograph."

Chapter 18: Jorge Disobeys

The next crossing, Maria did not board at Chiado. Jorge waited. The stop came and went. The tram sat empty on the tracks.

He drove on. The streets of old Lisbon passed in silence. The passengers who boarded did not speak to him. The Conductor stood at the back, watching.

At the Belem stop, Jorge pulled the brake. He sat for a moment, his heart beating too fast.

Then he opened the doors and stepped off.

He walked toward the Torre de Belem. The river was dark, the air cold. The street was empty.

He found her at the cafe. The Cafe Imperial, a small place with a red awning and wooden tables. She was sitting alone, a cup of coffee in front of her, untouched.

He sat down across from her.

"You should not be here," she said.

"I know."

"The Conductor will be angry."

"I don't care."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet. "I don't understand what is happening. I don't understand who you are."

Jorge reached across the table. He took her hand. Her skin was warm. Real.

"I know," he said. "I know you don't. But I need you to trust me."

"Why?"

"Because I think I'm falling in love with you."

She pulled her hand away. "You cannot love me. You do not know me."

"I know enough."

"You know nothing." She stood. "Nothing at all."

She walked out of the cafe. The green coat disappeared into the darkness. Jorge sat alone at the table, the coffee growing cold between his hands.

Chapter 19: The Cafe

Jorge did not drive the midnight route for two weeks. He switched shifts. He stayed home. He sat in his apartment and stared at the walls.

The coins were in a drawer. He did not touch them. He did not want to be reminded.

But the absence was worse. Not driving was a kind of suffocation. The city felt smaller without the route. The days were longer.

On the third Saturday, he called the depot. He asked for the midnight shift back. The dispatcher said yes.

He arrived at 11:30. The tram was waiting. The same tram. The same dent in the metal. The same worn seat.

He sat in the cab. He waited.

At 11:47, he pulled away from the depot.

The crossing came. The other Lisbon opened. The streets changed.

Maria was at Chiado. She was not wearing the green coat. She was wearing a blue dress and a cardigan. She looked smaller without the coat.

She boarded. She paid. She sat.

"I'm sorry," Jorge said.

"For what?"

"For following you. For saying what I said."

"You did not lie."

"No. But I should not have said it."

Maria was quiet. The tram climbed the hill. The city spread out below them.

"Come to the cafe," she said. "Tomorrow. In your time. In my time. I don't know. Just come."

"I can't. The Conductor — "

"Forget the Conductor. Come to the cafe."

She stood. She got off at the next stop before Jorge could answer.

He watched her walk away. The blue dress was harder to see in the dark than the green coat. But he followed it with his eyes until it was gone.

He did not know if he would go to the cafe. He knew that he wanted to.

Chapter 20: The Archivist Again

Jorge went back to the museum. He needed information. He needed to understand.

Rui was at his desk. He looked up when Jorge walked in. "You disappeared."

"I was busy."

"You left your coins. I had them appraised." Rui slid a piece of paper across the counter. "That's the estimate."

Jorge looked at the number. It was more than he made in a year.

"That's for five coins from a collection of fifty," Rui said. "The appraiser thinks there's a full set somewhere. She wants to know where."

"There's no collection."

"She doesn't believe that."

"She doesn't have to."

Jorge pushed the paper back. "I need you to find something for me. A person. Maria dos Santos. Born around 1930. Died 1957."

Rui's expression changed. "That's a specific request."

"Can you help me or not?"

Rui hesitated. Then he turned to his computer. "Give me a minute."

Jorge waited. The clock ticked. Rui typed, clicked, frowned, typed more.

"Maria dos Santos," he said. "Born 1930. Died December 15, 1957. Cause of death listed as tuberculosis. She lived on Rua do Jardim do Tabaco. No immediate family besides an aunt."

"That's her."

"How do you know her?"

Jorge did not answer. He took the paper with the address and walked out.

Chapter 21: Grief

The address was a building near the cathedral. Modern now. A block of flats with a convenience store on the ground floor. No trace of the building where Maria had lived.

Jorge stood across the street. He watched the people come and go. A woman with a shopping bag. A man walking a dog. A child on a bicycle.

They were living in a world where Maria dos Santos had once lived. They did not know her name. They did not know that a woman in a green coat had walked these streets sixty years ago.

Jorge felt the weight of time. It pressed down on him. He was standing in a place where a ghost had once been real.

He walked to the cathedral. He lit a candle. He did not pray. He did not know how.

He sat in the pew and looked at the altar. The gold was dull in the dim light. The silence was thick.

He thought about Maria's death certificate. Tuberculosis. She had died in a hospital, probably. She had been alone. Her aunt had died the year before.

He thought about the green coat. She had been wearing it when she died, maybe. It was the coat of a woman who had owned one good thing and made it last.

He left the cathedral. The sun was setting. The sky was orange and gold.

He went home. He opened the drawer and looked at the coins. They were all that was left of her. Five pieces of metal from a world that had ended.

He closed the drawer. He would drive the midnight shift on Sunday. He would see her again. He did not know what he would say.

Chapter 22: The Conductor's Laws

The Conductor was waiting at the depot. Jorge saw him standing beside the tram, a tall shadow in the dim light.

"You are making a mistake," the Conductor said.

"I'm driving my route."

"You are crossing for her."

Jorge did not deny it. He climbed into the cab. The Conductor followed, standing at the back.

The crossing came. The other Lisbon opened.

"She will die," the Conductor said. "You cannot prevent it. The seam does not work that way."

"I know."

"Then why?"

Jorge thought about it. The answer came slowly, shaped by months of crossings, by the weight of the coins in his drawer, by the sound of Maria's laugh.

"Because she is real," he said. "Because I am real. Because the time we have is the time we have. I don't want to waste it."

The Conductor was silent.

"I spent my whole life doing the safe thing," Jorge said. "I drove the same route. I lived in the same flat. I let my marriage fall apart because I was too afraid to fight for it. I don't want to be that man anymore."

"Love will not save you."

"Nothing saves you. That's not the point."

Maria boarded at Chiado. She saw the Conductor and stiffened. But she sat down anyway.

Jorge drove on. The tram rattled through the lost city. The lights were soft. The passengers were quiet.

For a moment, everything was exactly as it should be.

Chapter 23: The Burden

Jorge carried the knowledge of Maria's death like a stone in his chest. He knew the date. December 15, 1957. He knew the cause. Tuberculosis.

He knew that in her timeline, it was December 1955. He had two years. Two years of crossings. Two years of watching her walk toward the river.

He did not know what to do with the knowledge.

He could not warn her. He could not take her to a doctor. He could not change the past. The Conductor had been clear about that.

But he could be with her. He could sit with her in the cafe. He could listen to her talk about buttons and thread and the cinema. He could hold her hand across a table.

He could love her. That was something.

The next crossing, he brought her a thing from the present. A bar of chocolate. It was wrapped in foil that would look strange to her.

She unwrapped it carefully. She smelled it. She smiled.

"Where did you get this?" she asked.

"It's from where I'm from."

She took a bite. Her eyes closed. She made a sound that was almost a laugh.

"This is the best thing I have ever tasted."

"I have more."

"You are a strange man, Jorge."

"Yes. I am."

She ate the chocolate in small pieces. She saved the foil. She folded it and put it in her pocket.

"I will keep this forever," she said.

Jorge looked at her. He knew that forever was not what she thought it was. But he smiled anyway.

"Good," he said.

Chapter 24: A Night Off

Jorge did not cross every week. He made himself take breaks. The Conductor had warned him about spending too much time on the other side.

He spent the empty Sundays walking through modern Lisbon. The city was the same and different. The river was the same. The hills were the same. But everything else had changed.

He walked to the Chiado stop. The modern one. Tourists waited there. They checked their phones. They took selfies. They did not see the ghost of a woman in a green coat.

Jorge understood now. The city was layered. The present was a thin skin over the past. If you knew how to look, you could see through it.

He bought a notebook. He started writing down what he remembered. The names of the streets in the other Lisbon. The shape of the buildings. The smell of the air.

He did not know why he was writing. It felt important. Like he was making a record of something that would disappear.

On the Sundays when he did not cross, he dreamed of Maria. He dreamed of the green coat. He dreamed of the cafe.

The dreams were vivid. In the dreams, he could touch her. He could feel her hand in his. He could smell her skin.

He woke from the dreams with a sense of loss that took hours to fade.

He waited for Sunday. He waited for the crossing. The waiting was its own kind of torture.

Chapter 25: The Pull

The pull was physical. Jorge felt it on the days leading up to the crossing. A low vibration in his chest, like the tram on the tracks.

He wondered if the seam was calling him. If the other Lisbon was hungry for his attention.

He did not care. He would have crossed every night if he could.

Maria was at Chiado. She boarded. She sat. She was wearing the green coat again.

"I missed you," she said.

"I was here last week."

"I know. I missed you anyway."

Jorge felt something crack open in his chest. He had not expected this. He had expected distance, caution. He had not expected her to miss him.

The tram climbed the hill. The river appeared between the buildings, a sheet of dark glass.

"I think about you," Maria said. "When you are not here."

"I think about you too."

"What are we doing, Jorge?"

He did not have an answer. He was a man from the future. She was a woman from the past. They existed in a crack between times.

"I don't know," he said. "But I don't want to stop."

She looked at him. Her eyes were dark and deep.

"Neither do I."

She reached across the gap between them and touched his hand. Her fingers were cold. Her skin was real.

They stayed like that until Belem. Then she stood. She let go. She stepped off the tram.

Jorge watched her go. The green coat. The dark hair. The woman who was already dead and more alive than anyone he had ever known.

Chapter 26: The Confession

The next crossing, Jorge told her the truth. All of it.

They were sitting at the Cafe Imperial. The coffee was bitter. The lights were low. The other customers were shadows at the edges of the room.

"I am not from your time," Jorge said.

Maria put down her cup. "What do you mean?"

"I mean I was born in 1968. I drive a tram in Lisbon, but the Lisbon I know is different from this one. The buildings are different. The streets are different. You are different."

"You are not making sense."

"I know." He took a breath. "I am from the future. The year is 2026. You are in 1955. The tram I drive crosses between the two."

Maria stared at him. Her face was unreadable.

"I can prove it," Jorge said. He reached into his pocket and pulled out his phone. He pressed the button. The screen lit up.

Maria flinched. She leaned back. "What is that?"

"A telephone. From my time. It does more than make calls."

He showed her the screen. He showed her photographs. The modern Lisbon. The bridge. The river. The buildings that had replaced the ones she knew.

She looked at each image in silence. Her hands were shaking.

"This is not possible," she said.

"It is. I did not believe it either. Not at first."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet.

"Why are you here, Jorge?"

"Because I could not stay away."

She reached across the table. She took his hand. Her fingers were cold.

"I believe you," she said. "I do not understand it. But I believe you."

Chapter 27: The Aftermath

The confession changed everything. Maria was quiet for the rest of the ride. She did not get off at Belem. She stayed on until the end of the line.

Jorge drove through the empty streets of the other Lisbon. The buildings were dark. The lamps were few. The city was sleeping.

"I do not know how to feel," Maria said.

"You do not have to know."

"I am frightened."

"I know. I am too."

She laughed. A small sound. "You? Frightened? You came from the future to find me."

"I was not looking for you. I found you. And I could not leave you."

The tram reached the end of the line. Jorge stopped. The motor hummed. The lights were warm.

"I want to see your Lisbon," Maria said.

"You cannot. The seam does not work that way."

"What is the seam?"

Jorge did not know how to explain it. He did not understand it himself. It was a crack in the world, a place where time folded. The tram passed through it because the route was old and the rails were worn thin.

"I will show you," he said. "When I can. What I can."

Maria nodded. She stood. She walked to the doors.

"I will meet you on Sunday," she said.

"Sunday."

She stepped off into the dark. The green coat disappeared into the night. Jorge sat in the empty tram and listened to the silence.

Chapter 28: The Archive

Jorge dug deeper into the history of 1955 Lisbon. He went to the National Archive. He spent hours in the reading room, turning pages of old newspapers.

He learned about the black markets that operated openly. The political tensions beneath the surface. The hope that ran through the poverty like a river underground.

He found a photograph of the Rua Garrett shop where Maria worked. Buttons and thread. The sign was hand-painted. The window displayed spools of colored cotton arranged in neat rows.

He made copies of the photographs. He took them home. He spread them on his kitchen table.

The shop was gone now. The building had been renovated in the 1980s. It was a shoe store. He had walked past it a hundred times without knowing.

He found a reference to the Cafe Imperial. A small article from 1956. The cafe had closed in 1958. The building was demolished in 1960.

He read the article three times. The cafe had been a meeting place for artists. Poets. Musicians. A woman named Maria dos Santos had been mentioned in a footnote. A regular customer. The author remembered her green coat.

Jorge closed the file. He sat in the silence of the archive.

She was real. She was real enough to leave a mark in the records. Real enough to be remembered by a stranger sixty years later.

He walked home through the rain. The streets were wet. The trams clattered past. The city was alive with light and noise.

He felt closer to her than he had ever felt. And further away.

Chapter 29: The Conductor's Story

The Conductor found Jorge at the depot. It was a Thursday afternoon. The sun was bright. The trams were clean.

"You have been digging," the Conductor said.

"I have been learning."

"It will not help."

"Maybe not. But it's something to do."

The Conductor sat on a bench. He looked old in the daylight. His uniform was faded. His hands were thin.

"I want to tell you my story," he said.

Jorge sat down.

"I was a driver in the 1960s. I found the seam by accident. I crossed into the other Lisbon and I stayed too long. I fell in love with a woman from the past."

Jorge said nothing.

"She died. I could not save her. I tried. I crossed every night. I learned the rules. I thought I could bend them."

"And?"

"And I bent myself instead. The seam claimed me. I became part of the route. I cannot leave. I cannot stop crossing."

The Conductor looked at Jorge. His eyes were old and tired.

"I am telling you this so you understand. You will not save her. You will only lose yourself."

"Maybe," Jorge said. "But I have to try."

The Conductor nodded. He stood. He walked away, his footsteps echoing on the concrete.

Jorge watched him go. He understood now. The Conductor was not a monster. He was a warning.

Chapter 30: The Warning Deepens

Jorge did not stop. He could not stop. The crossing became the center of his life.

He drove his day shifts. He ate. He slept. He waited for Sunday.

Maria was changing. She was thinner. Her face was paler. The tuberculosis was already in her lungs, a seed waiting to bloom.

Jorge noticed. He could not fail to notice. The green coat hung looser on her shoulders.

"You are sick," he said one night.

"I am fine."

"You are not."

She looked at him. Her eyes were bright, too bright. "What do you want me to say, Jorge? That I am dying? That I have been dying since before you arrived?"

"Yes. I want you to say it."

She shook her head. "What good would it do?"

"I could help you."

"You cannot. You told me yourself. The past cannot be changed."

"Then let me be with you. Let me stay."

"You cannot stay either."

The truth of it settled between them. Jorge was from the future. Maria was from the past. They existed in a crack that could not hold them both.

The tram reached Belem. Maria stood. She was unsteady on her feet.

"You should not come next week," she said.

"I will come."

"Jorge — "

"I will come."

She looked at him for a long moment. Then she nodded. She got off the tram.

Jorge watched the green coat disappear into the dark. He knew he was losing her. He knew he could not stop it.

He drove on anyway.

Chapter 31: Maria's Gift

The next crossing, Maria brought him something. A small envelope. She pressed it into his hand as she boarded.

"What is this?"

"Open it later."

He did not open it until he was back in the present. He sat in the cab with the motor off and tore the envelope open.

Inside was a ticket. A lottery ticket from 1955. The numbers were handwritten. The paper was thin.

He turned it over. On the back, in her handwriting: "For luck. M."

He laughed. A broken sound in the empty cab.

A lottery ticket from a dead year. A gift from a dead woman. A piece of paper that had no value in the present.

But it was worth everything. Because she had given it to him.

He put it in the drawer with the coins. It belonged with the other fragments of her world.

He did not check the numbers. He did not want to know. The ticket was not about winning. It was about the gesture. The hope. The love.

He closed the drawer. He went to bed. He dreamed of Maria.

She was standing on the tram platform, wearing the green coat. She was smiling. The sun was behind her.

"Come with me," she said.

He woke up reaching for her. His hand closed on empty air.

Chapter 32: The Trap

Jorge considered staying in 1955. The thought had been growing in his mind like a vine, wrapping around everything.

He could step off the tram. He could walk into the other Lisbon. He could live there. Love her. Die with her.

The Conductor found him at the depot again. This time it was night. The rain was falling. The streetlights made yellow pools on the wet ground.

"You are thinking about it," the Conductor said.

"Thinking about what?"

"Staying."

Jorge did not deny it.

"The seam is not a door. It is a crack. You cannot live on both sides. If you stay, you will unravel."

"What does that mean?"

"It means your body will reject the past. You will get sick. You will die. And she will watch you die the same way you will watch her."

"I could save her."

"You cannot. I tried. The woman I loved died. I watched her die. And when I tried to stay, the seam took me apart."

Jorge looked at the Conductor. The rain was running down his face like tears.

"You are not human anymore," Jorge said.

"No. I am not. I am part of the route. I will exist as long as the tracks exist."

"And Maria?"

"She will die. You will grieve. And then you will decide whether to become like me or to let her go."

The Conductor turned and walked into the rain. He did not look back.

Jorge stood alone at the depot. The rain fell. The trams slept. The route waited.

Chapter 33: December 1955

The air in the other Lisbon was cold. The streets were decorated for Christmas. Wreaths on the doors. Candles in the windows. A nativity scene in the church square.

Maria wore the green coat. She was thinner. Her cheeks were hollow. But her eyes were alive.

"It is almost Christmas," she said.

"I know."

"I used to love Christmas. When I was a child. Before my parents died."

"What do you love about it?"

"The hope," she said. "The feeling that something good might happen."

Jorge wanted to tell her that something good had happened. That he had found her. That the crossing had given him a gift he did not deserve.

But the words felt wrong. He was from the future. He knew how her story ended. The hope she felt was a lie.

"I love the lights," he said instead. "The way they make the street look softer."

She smiled. "Yes. The lights."

They sat together in the tram. The other passengers had left. The route was almost done.

"I wish you could stay," she said.

"I wish I could too."

"But you will come back."

"Always."

She leaned against him. Her weight was light. She smelled of rain and violet soap.

They stayed like that until the end of the line. Then she stood. She kissed his cheek. She stepped off into the 1955 night.

Jorge watched her go. The green coat. The dark hair. The woman he loved.

He did not know how many crossings they had left. He did not want to count.

Chapter 34: The Plan

Jorge made a plan. It was not a good plan. But it was a plan.

He would tell Maria everything. Not just about the future. About her death. About the tuberculosis. About the hospital.

He would give her a choice. She could face it alone. Or she could face it with him.

The Conductor would try to stop him. He knew that. The rules were clear. You could not warn the dead.

But Jorge did not care about the rules. He cared about Maria.

The next crossing, he brought her a letter. He had written it in the present, in his small flat, by the light of his desk lamp.

The letter said everything. The year of her death. The cause. The hospital where she would spend her last days.

He did not give it to her immediately. He held it in his pocket. He felt its weight against his thigh.

Maria boarded. She was wearing the green coat. She was thinner. Her face was pale.

"You look tired," Jorge said.

"I am tired."

"Sit. Rest."

She sat near the front. Jorge drove. The other Lisbon passed outside the windows. The streets were quiet. The shops were closed.

At Chiado, the Conductor boarded. He stood at the back. His eyes were fixed on Jorge.

Jorge felt the letter in his pocket. He felt the Conductor's gaze.

He drove on.

Chapter 35: The Truth

At Belem, Jorge did something he had never done before. He stopped the tram. He opened the doors. And he stepped off.

Maria looked at him in surprise. "What are you doing?"

"Coming with you."

"You cannot. The route — "

"I don't care about the route."

He took her hand. He led her off the tram. The Conductor watched from the back.

They walked to the Cafe Imperial. It was empty. The chairs were stacked. The lights were off.

"Wait," Maria said. "The cafe is closed."

"Yes."

"How did you — "

"I asked someone. In my time. The owner's grandson. He told me where the key was."

Jorge unlocked the door. They stepped inside. The air was cold. The chairs were stacked.

"I need to tell you something," Jorge said. "And I need you to listen."

He took the letter from his pocket. He gave it to her.

She unfolded it. She read it by the light of the streetlamp through the window.

Her face changed. The color drained. She looked up at him.

"This is how I die?"

"Yes."

She held the letter. Her hands were shaking.

"How long?"

"Two years. From now."

She sat down. She did not look at him. She looked at the letter.

"I am going to die," she said.

"Yes."

"In two years."

"Yes."

She was quiet for a long time. Jorge sat beside her. He did not speak.

"Thank you," she said finally.

"For what?"

"For telling me. For not letting me pretend."

She took his hand. Her fingers were cold.

"Stay with me," she said. "Until the end."

"I will."

Chapter 36: Belem 1955

Jorge walked through the streets of the other Lisbon. It was early evening. The lamps were lit. The air was cold.

He had never been here in daylight. The crossing only happened at midnight. He had stayed after the tram left. He was alone in a city that did not exist.

He found the shop on Rua Garrett. Buttons and thread. The sign was hand-painted. The window displayed spools of colored cotton.

Maria was inside. She saw him through the glass. She smiled.

He entered. The bell above the door rang.

"You are here," she said.

"I am."

"In the daytime."

"Yes."

She laughed. The sound was beautiful.

"I did not think I would see you in daylight."

"Neither did I."

They stood in the small shop. The shelves were lined with thread. The counter held buttons in glass jars.

"Can you stay?" she asked.

"A little while."

"Good."

She put a sign on the door. Closed. She took off her apron. She took his hand.

"Come. I want to show you something."

She led him through the streets. The city was different in daylight. The details were sharper. The colors were brighter.

They walked to the river. The water was gray. The tower stood at the edge.

"This is where I come when I need to think," Maria said.

"It's beautiful."

"It is."

They stood at the railing. The wind was cold. Maria leaned against him.

"I have been thinking about dying," she said.

"You do not have to."

"Yes. I do. It is coming. I cannot stop it."

Jorge said nothing.

"I am not afraid," she said. "Not anymore. I was, before you. I was terrified. But now I know you will be with me."

"I will."

"That is enough."

Chapter 37: The Cafe Imperial

The Cafe Imperial became their place. Jorge found a way to cross outside the Sunday schedule. The seam responded to the tram, but it also responded to need. He learned to feel it, the thinning of the air, the shift in pressure.

He crossed on Wednesdays. Thursdays. Whenever he could.

The cafe was always empty. The owner, a man named Senhor Abreu, had been paid well to keep a table reserved and the coffee hot.

They sat at the same table. They drank the same coffee. They talked.

Maria told him about her childhood. Her parents had died in the influenza epidemic of 1934. She had been raised by her aunt, a woman who worked as a seamstress.

"I never had much," she said. "But I never needed much."

"I know the feeling."

"Did you have someone, Jorge? In your time?"

"A wife. She left. I was not good at being present."

"And now?"

"Now I am here. With you."

She smiled. It was the smile he had fallen in love with. Tired. Hopeful. Real.

"Do you regret it?" she asked.

"Coming here?"

"Yes."

"Never."

She reached across the table. She took his hand. Her fingers were warm.

"Good," she said. "I do not regret it either."

Chapter 38: Maria's Choice

Maria faced her death with a calm that Jorge could not understand. She did not rage. She did not bargain. She accepted.

"I have had more time than I expected," she said. "When my parents died, I thought I would not live to be twenty. Then I was twenty-five. Then I met you."

"That is not the same."

"No. It is better."

They were at the cafe. The rain was falling outside. The windows were steamed.

"I want to see your Lisbon," she said.

"You cannot. The seam — "

"I know. But there must be a way. A photograph. A drawing. Something."

Jorge pulled out his phone. He showed her photographs. The modern Lisbon. The bridge. The river. The buildings.

She looked at each one carefully. She asked questions. What was that? What did they sell there? Who lived in those flats?

He answered each one. The words came easily.

"It is beautiful," she said.

"It is different."

"She is still Lisbon."

"Yes. She is."

Maria touched the screen. Her finger traced the outline of a building.

"I wish I could walk those streets," she said.

"You already do. Just not the same ones."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet.

"I love you, Jorge."

The words hung in the air. They were the first time she had said them.

"I love you too, Maria."

They sat in the quiet of the cafe. The rain fell. The world outside was a city that did not exist.

But they existed. In that moment. In that room.

That was enough.

Chapter 39: The Night Conductor Arrives

The Conductor appeared at the cafe. He stood outside the window. The rain ran down his face.

"I have been looking for you," he said.

"I know."

"You have broken every rule."

"I know."

The Conductor entered. He sat at the table. Maria watched him with fear in her eyes.

"You are killing yourself," the Conductor said.

"Maybe."

"Definitely. I can see it in you. The seam is taking you apart. Your time in the past is destroying your connection to the present."

"I know."

"You do not care."

"No. I do not."

The Conductor looked at Maria. Then back at Jorge.

"I understand," he said. "I felt the same way. About her."

He had never mentioned her before. The woman he had lost.

"What happened?" Jorge asked.

"She died. I stayed. The seam consumed me."

"Then I will accept the same fate."

The Conductor shook his head. "You do not understand. It is not glorious. It is not romantic. It is endless. You will never rest."

"I will have her until the end."

"And after?"

Jorge looked at Maria. She was watching him. Her eyes were steady.

"After does not matter," he said.

Chapter 40: The Laws of the Seam

The Conductor explained the laws of the seam in the empty cafe. The rain had stopped. The street was quiet.

"The seam exists because the route is old," he said. "The tracks have been there for a hundred years. They have carried so many people that the boundary between times has worn thin."

"Like a path," Jorge said.

"Exactly. A path worn by feet. The tram follows the path. The seam opens."

"But only at midnight."

"Only at midnight. That is when the weight of the day is lightest. The boundary is thinnest."

Maria listened in silence. She held Jorge's hand.

"Every crossing has a cost," the Conductor said. "You have spent too much time here. The return will hurt."

"How much?"

"More than you expect. You will be sick. You will lose time. The present will feel less real."

"I can accept that."

"You say that now."

The Conductor stood. He looked at them both.

"I will not stop you," he said. "I cannot. You are already lost."

He walked to the door. He paused.

"She dies in March," he said. "Not December. You have three months. Not two years."

He walked out. The door swung shut.

Maria looked at Jorge. Her face was pale.

"Three months," she said.

He pulled her close. He held her.

"We have three months," he said. "We will make them count."

Chapter 41: The Return

Jorge returned to the present. The crossing was harder than before. The tram seemed to resist him. The lights flickered. The air was thick.

He collapsed in the cab. His chest was tight. His vision blurred.

He made it home. He fell into bed. He was sick for three days.

The fever came in waves. He saw Maria in his dreams. She was standing at the edge of the river. The green coat was bright against the gray water.

"Come with me," she said.

He woke up sweating. The clock said 3:17 AM.

He lay in the dark. His body ached. His mind was clear.

He was dying. Not quickly. But surely. The seam was taking him apart.

He did not care. He would trade a hundred days in the present for one hour with her.

He got out of bed. He made coffee. He sat at the kitchen table and watched the night.

The city was quiet. The trams were sleeping. The route was waiting.

He counted the days until Sunday.

Chapter 42: The Cost

The next crossing was harder still. Jorge felt the seam resisting him. The pressure in his chest was worse. The air was thicker.

Maria noticed. She saw the gray in his skin. She saw the effort in his movements.

"You are sick," she said.

"I am fine."

"You are not."

She took his face in her hands. Her fingers were cold.

"You are dying, Jorge."

"I know."

"Then stop. Do not come back."

"I cannot."

"Jorge — "

"I cannot."

She looked at him. Her eyes were wet.

"I do not want to be the reason you die."

"You are not the reason. I am the reason. I chose this."

"But — "

"There is no world where I do not choose you."

She kissed him. Her lips were cold. Her tears tasted like salt.

They stayed on the tram. The other passengers came and went. The route repeated. The city spun around them.

At the end of the line, Jorge pulled the brake. The motor hummed. The lights were warm.

"Three more months," she said.

"Three more months."

"I want to live them."

"Then we will."

She leaned against him. Her weight was light. Her breathing was shallow.

He held her. The tram was quiet. The night was deep.

They had three months. They would make them last forever.

Chapter 43: January 1956

The new year came to the other Lisbon. The streets were decorated. The air was cold. The river was gray.

Maria was weaker. The tuberculosis was progressing. She coughed more. She tired easily.

But she did not stop. She walked with Jorge through the old city. She showed him places she loved. A bakery. A bookshop. A church where she sometimes sat.

"It is the oldest church in Lisbon," she said. "Santa Engracia. It has a dome like the one in Rome."

"It's beautiful."

"It is. I used to come here when I was sad."

"And now?"

"I am not sad anymore."

She took his hand. They walked up the hill. The city spread below them.

"What will happen after?" she asked.

"After what?"

"After I am gone."

Jorge did not answer. He did not know.

"Will you come back here?"

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because you will not be here."

She looked at him. Her eyes were steady.

"You will have to let me go," she said.

"I know."

"But not yet."

"No. Not yet."

They stood on the hill. The city was below them. The river was a sheet of silver.

Maria leaned against him. Her head on his shoulder.

"Not yet," she whispered.

He held her. The wind was cold. The sun was setting.

Not yet.

Chapter 44: The Final Crossing

The Conductor appeared one last time. It was February. The rain had not stopped for a week.

"She has a month," he said.

"I know."

"Maybe less."

"I know."

"You cannot save her."

"I know."

The Conductor looked at him. His face was unreadable.

"There is a choice," he said. "You can stay in her time. Die with her. The seam will take you completely."

"And if I stay?"

"You will not return to the present. You will be part of the route. Like me."

Jorge thought about it. He thought about the seams. The endless crossings. The endless watching.

"If I stay, I can be with her until the end."

"Yes."

"Then I stay."

The Conductor was quiet.

"I do not recommend it," he said.

"I know."

"But you will do it anyway."

"Yes."

The Conductor nodded. He turned to leave.

"One thing," Jorge said.

The Conductor stopped.

"Thank you. For not stopping me."

The Conductor looked back. His face was old. His eyes were tired.

"I did not have a choice. The seam chose you. It always does."

He walked away. The rain swallowed him.

Jorge stood at the depot. The tram was waiting. The route was open.

He climbed into the cab.

Chapter 45: The Decision

The crossing that night was different. Jorge felt it in the air. The seam was wider. The boundary was thinner.

He drove through the other Lisbon. The streets were empty. The lamps were dim. The city was holding its breath.

Maria was at Chiado. She was wearing the green coat. She was pale. Her eyes were bright.

"I am ready," she said.

"For what?"

"For the end."

Jorge pulled the brake. The tram stopped. The motor hummed.

"I am staying," he said.

"No."

"Yes."

"Jorge, you cannot — "

"I can. I am."

She looked at him. Her face was a tangle of emotions. Love. Fear. Hope.

"You will die."

"Yes."

"And you are choosing it."

"Yes."

She started to cry. The tears ran down her cheeks.

"I do not want you to die."

"Then do not ask me to live without you."

She reached for him. He held her. The tram was quiet. The city was still.

"I love you," she said.

"I love you too."

He stepped off the tram. The doors closed behind him. The tram pulled away.

The Conductor was at the window. He watched as Jorge walked into the old city.

Jorge did not look back.

Chapter 46: The Nightmare

The first days were beautiful. Jorge walked through the other Lisbon. He visited the places Maria had shown him. He drank coffee at the Cafe Imperial. He sat in the church and looked at the dome.

But the seam was not kind.

The headaches started on the third day. A pressure behind his eyes that would not fade. By the fifth day, he was coughing. By the seventh, he could not keep food down.

Maria nursed him. She brought him soup. She held his head when he was sick.

"You are dying," she said.

"Yes."

"Faster than me."

"Yes."

She did not cry. She was past crying.

They sat together in her small flat. The windows looked out on the river. The light was gray.

"I thought we would have more time," she said.

"There is never enough time."

"But we have now."

"Yes. We have now."

She lay beside him. Her body was thin. Her breath was shallow.

He held her. The river was gray. The sky was gray.

But she was warm. She was real. She was with him.

That was enough. It had to be.

Chapter 47: Maria Sees

Maria saw him fading. She saw the life draining from his body. She saw the way his hands shook, the way his eyes lost focus.

"You have to go back," she said.

"No."

"Jorge. You have to."

"I am not leaving you."

"You are not leaving me. I am leaving you. There is a difference."

"We have time."

"Not enough. You need to go back before it is too late."

Jorge shook his head. The room was spinning. His chest was tight.

"I will not leave you alone."

"I will not be alone. I will have the time we had. That is enough."

"It is not enough."

"It has to be."

She took his face in her hands. Her fingers were cold.

"I love you," she said. "And I need you to live."

Jorge looked at her. The green coat was draped over the chair. Her face was thin. Her eyes were bright.

"If I go back, I will never see you again."

"You will see me in your memory."

"That is not the same."

"No. But it is something."

She kissed him. Her lips were dry. Her tears tasted like salt.

"Go," she said. "Before I change my mind."

Chapter 48: The Return, Broken

Jorge walked through the streets of old Lisbon. His body was failing. His vision was blurring. His breath came in ragged gasps.

The tram was at the station. The Conductor was waiting.

"You came back," the Conductor said.

"Help me."

The Conductor took his arm. He led him onto the tram. The seats were empty. The lights were dim.

"The crossing will hurt," the Conductor said.

"Everything hurts."

The tram began to move. The city flickered. The lights changed. The air shifted.

Jorge felt the seam tear through him. It was like being pulled apart. Every cell in his body screamed.

Then the tram rounded a corner. The lights steadied. The present returned.

Jorge collapsed in the cab. The Conductor lifted him. Carried him to the depot. Laid him on the bench.

"You will live," the Conductor said. "But you will not be the same."

Jorge did not answer. He could not speak.

He lay on the bench. The sky was gray. The rain was falling.

Maria was gone.

Chapter 49: The Green Coat

Jorge did not drive for a month. He stayed in his flat. He stared at the walls.

The drawer was open on the table. The coins. The lottery ticket. The letter.

He did not look at them. He did not need to. He remembered everything.

On the fifth Sunday, he went back to the depot. The dispatcher looked at him with concern.

"Are you sure you are ready?"

"Yes."

He took the midnight shift. The same tram. The same route.

The crossing came. The lights flickered. The air shifted.

He drove into the other Lisbon.

Chiado stop was empty. No woman in a green coat. No dark hair under a scarf.

He waited. The stop came and went.

She did not board.

He drove on. The streets were quiet. The cafe was closed. The shop on Rua Garrett was empty.

He reached Belem. He stopped the tram. He sat in the silence.

The tower stood against the river. The water was dark. The wind was cold.

He opened the doors. He waited.

No one got off. No one got on.

He closed the doors. He drove on.

The crossing ended. He was back in the present.

He pulled into the depot. He parked the tram. He sat in the dark.

She was gone.

Chapter 50: The Route Continues

Jorge still drives the number 28. He takes the midnight shift every Sunday. He does not know why.

The crossings still happen. The other Lisbon still exists. He drives through it. He watches the streets change and shift.

He does not look for her anymore. He knows she is not there.

But sometimes, in the moment between midnight and the crossing, he feels her. A warmth at the edge of his awareness. The smell of violet soap.

He does not stop. He does not look back. He drives.

The route is the same. The tram is the same. He is not the same.

He carries the coins in his pocket. The lottery ticket is in the drawer. The letter is folded in a book.

He does not need to look at them. He remembers.

The city changes. The buildings rise and fall. The river stays the same.

The tram runs. The wheels turn. The route continues.

He drives through Lisbon. The old one. The new one. The one that exists in the space between.

He drives because it is what he does. He drives because the route needs a driver. He drives because somewhere, in a city that no longer exists, a woman in a green coat is still waiting.

He will never see her again. But he will always be on the route that leads to her.

And that is enough.
