Chapter 1: The Call

The phone rang at six in the morning. June reached across the empty side of the bed and fumbled for it. The number on the screen was unfamiliar. She almost let it go to voicemail.

"June Bell?" The voice was a woman's, tired and official.

"Yes."

"I'm calling from Mercy General Hospital. Your father, Arthur Bell — "

June sat up. The sheet pooled in her lap. She had not spoken to her father in seven years. Not since the divorce. Not since she stopped returning his calls.

"He passed away an hour ago," the woman said. "A stroke. It was quick."

June said nothing. She looked at the window. The sky was gray. A bird was singing somewhere, senseless and persistent.

"Are you there?" the woman asked.

"Yes," June said. "I'm here."

She wrote down the address of the cottage. The woman gave her the name of a funeral home and a telephone number for a lawyer. June wrote it all down on the back of an envelope from the kitchen. Her hand moved automatically.

After she hung up, she sat for a long time. She thought about the last time she had seen her father. It was at her wedding. He had stood in the back of the church, sweating in his brown suit, not smiling. She had been too happy to notice.

She showered. She packed a bag. She called the school and told them she needed bereavement leave. Then she got in her car and drove north.

The drive took four hours. She did not play music. She did not listen to the radio. She watched the landscape change from suburbs to farmland to hills. The roads narrowed. The trees grew thicker.

She pulled up to the cottage at noon. It was smaller than she remembered. The paint was peeling. The porch sagged. The garden was a riot of weeds and volunteer flowers.

She sat in the car for a full minute before she turned the engine off.

Chapter 2: The Cottage

The key was under the third flowerpot on the porch, just where it had always been. June turned it in the lock. The door stuck. She pushed with her shoulder.

The smell hit her first. Soil. Dried leaves. Something faintly chemical, like formaldehyde. Her father had lived alone for seven years. No one had cleaned.

The living room was stacked with books. Shelves lined every wall, double-stacked. More books on the floor, in towers, in precarious piles. A narrow path led to the kitchen.

She walked through the rooms slowly. The kitchen counters held jars of dried specimens. Labels in her father's hand: *Acer rubrum*, *Quercus alba*, *Selaginella lepidophylla*. The refrigerator was empty except for a carton of expired milk and a jar of pickled mushrooms.

The bedroom was small. A narrow bed, made. A photograph on the nightstand: June at age ten, holding a sunflower. She remembered that day. He had taken her to a botanical garden. She had been bored. He had been patient.

She picked up the photograph and looked at it for a long time.

The bathroom held nothing unusual. A toothbrush. A razor. A bottle of aspirin.

She went back to the living room and stood in the center. The house was full of things. All of them were his. None of them told her who he had been in the years since she left.

She sat down on the couch. Dust rose. She sneezed.

Tomorrow she would start the real work. Today she was too tired.

She lay back on the couch and closed her eyes.

Chapter 3: The Study

The study was locked. June tried the handle twice before she believed it.

She found the key in the kitchen drawer, tangled in a ball of rubber bands. It was small and brass, tarnished black.

The door swung open. The smell was stronger here. Earth and paper and something metallic.

The study was a single room, maybe ten feet by twelve. A desk faced the window. Around it, floor-to-ceiling shelves held specimen cases. Glass jars lined the shelves, each one containing a plant part suspended in liquid. Roots. Leaves. A whole flower once, preserved.

She moved to the desk. It was organized. Pens in a cup. A magnifying lamp. A microscope, dust-covered. Stack of notebooks, uniform, labeled by year.

She opened the most recent notebook. It was dated two weeks before his death. The handwriting was shaky but legible.

*October 12. Cloudy. Temperatures dropping. Checked the southeast transect. Lycopodium annotinum showing early senescence. Unusual for this elevation.*

She flipped backward. Months of field notes. Scientific, precise, devoid of emotion.

She set the notebook down. She pulled open the desk drawers one by one. Office supplies. Old bills. A box of glass slides.

The bottom drawer was locked.

She felt underneath the desk. It was a habit her father had — taping keys to the underside of furniture. Her fingers found the key taped there.

She unlocked the drawer. Inside was nothing but a leather journal, bound with a brass clasp and a small padlock.

She picked it up. It was heavier than it looked.

Chapter 4: The Journal

The padlock was cheap. June broke it with a pair of pliers from the toolbox in the hall.

She sat at the desk and opened the journal. The first page was dated forty-two years ago. Her father's handwriting was younger then, more confident, the letters larger.

*June 3, 1984. Location: San Cristobal Arroyo, New Mexico. Elevation: 1,422 meters. Conditions: Full sun, zero precipitation in recorded history for this date.*

She turned the page. There was a drawing. A fern, rendered in careful pencil, every frond articulated. It grew from a crack in dry rock. The soil around it was sand.

Her father had written: *No measurable moisture in substrate. Air temperature at collection: 44°C. Specimen appears to be photosynthesizing normally. This should not be possible.*

June read the sentence twice. She was a biology teacher. She knew what ferns needed. Water. Shade. Humus-rich soil. This was a desert.

She turned more pages. More drawings. More impossible plants. A moss growing on bare granite in full sun. A flowering vine in an abandoned mine shaft, complete darkness, no apparent nutrient source.

The drawings were beautiful. Her father had always been a good illustrator. But these were different. These were obsessive. Every vein in every leaf, every scale on every stem, rendered with the care of a man who knew he was documenting something the world would not believe.

She closed the journal. Her hands were shaking.

She looked at the study around her with new eyes. The specimen jars. The notebooks. The microscope.

Her father had not been a failed botanist who retreated to a cottage. He had been on a hunt. A forty-year hunt for plants that broke every rule.

Chapter 5: Impossible Fern

June opened the journal again. She turned to the fern entry and read it carefully.

Her father had found the fern on a field trip with students from Kingsbury University, where he taught until his retirement. They were cataloging desert flora. The students complained about the heat. He wandered away from the group.

The arroyo was dry. He estimated it had not carried water in five years. The bedrock was volcanic tuff, porous and hot. Nothing grew on it except crustose lichens.

But there, in a crack no wider than his thumb, a fern.

He described it in clinical detail. *Cheilanthes* species, possibly *C. wrightii*, but the frond morphology was wrong. The pinnae were too broad. The stipe was too long.

He collected a sample. He pressed it. He drew it.

Back at the university, he ran tests. The plant's cells contained chlorophyll in normal concentrations. Its stomata opened and closed on a normal diurnal rhythm. It was photosynthesizing. It was growing.

But the soil sample from the root zone contained 0.3% organic matter. The water content was below measurable thresholds.

He wrote: *The plant is sustaining itself on nothing. Or on something I cannot measure.*

June looked at the drawing again. It was precise. Scientific. Unmistakable.

She taught her students that ferns require consistently moist soil. That they are indicator species for healthy ecosystems. That they cannot survive in arid conditions.

She had been wrong. Or her father had been lying.

She did not know which was worse.

Chapter 6: The Photograph

June turned the page. Tucked into the journal's spine was a photograph. A Polaroid, faded, the colors shifted toward magenta.

It showed the fern. Not pressed and dried, but alive. It grew from a crack in a sandstone wall. The fronds were green and healthy. The rock around it was bleached white by the sun. The sky behind it was flat blue.

She turned the photograph over. In her father's hand: *C. insolita, San Cristobal Arroyo, June 1984.*

She held it up to the light. There was no trick. No sign of staging. The fern was real.

She set the photograph on the desk and looked at it for a long time. Then she picked up the journal and continued reading.

The entries followed a pattern. Each plant was discovered, documented, collected. Each one violated some fundamental rule of botany. Each one was filed away in her father's mind and in this journal.

She noticed something else. In the margins, in a different ink, were annotations dated years later. Her father had returned to these plants. He had monitored them. He had watched them persist.

The fern still grew in the arroyo. He had checked it every two years for four decades. It was still there. Still green. Still impossible.

*Still alive*, he wrote in the margin. *Still refusing to die.*

June felt something shift in her chest. She had spent her adult life believing her father was a lonely, bitter man who had wasted his talent on obscure academic disputes. She had stopped calling because every conversation ended in silence or argument.

But this journal showed a different man. A patient man. A man who went back to the desert every two years to check on a single fern.

She did not know what to do with this new knowledge. She only knew she could not put the journal down.

Chapter 7: Roots Upward

She read through the night. The entries took her from New Mexico to Oregon, from Tennessee to Maine, from Arizona to the Florida panhandle.

The seventh entry described a tree. A sitka spruce growing from a sea cave on the Oregon coast. She knew the area. Her father had taken her there once, when she was twelve. She remembered the cold water and the feeling of the cave closing around her.

The tree's roots did not grow downward into soil. They grew upward, along the cave ceiling, dangling into the salt spray. The trunk was horizontal. The crown emerged from the cave mouth, angled toward the light.

He had written: *The root system is entirely exposed. It absorbs moisture from fog and spray. There is no soil. There is no substrate. The tree grows from air and salt water.*

June remembered that day at the cave. Her father had been distracted. He kept looking at the ceiling. She thought he was looking at bats.

He was looking at the roots.

She flipped ahead. The annotations in the margin were heavier on this entry. He had returned to the cave seven times. Each visit, the tree had grown. The roots had extended. The crown had thickened.

He had never published. He had never told anyone.

*If I tell them, they will come*, he wrote in the margin. *They will take it apart to understand it. They will kill it.*

June closed the journal. The sun was rising. She had not slept.

She went to the kitchen and made coffee. The grounds were old. The coffee was bitter.

She drank it anyway.

Chapter 8: Phone Call

She called Gary at noon. He answered on the second ring.

Gary was her ex-husband. They had been married for six years, separated for four, divorced for two. He was a botanist at Westbrook University. He studied plant physiology. He was good at his job.

"June," he said. "Are you okay?"

"My father died," she said.

"I'm sorry."

"There's something else."

She told him about the journal. She told him about the fern, the spruce, the moss on granite. She told him about the forty years of documentation.

Gary was quiet for a long time.

"Are you sure it's real?" he said.

"The photograph is real."

"Photographs can be staged."

"He annotated it for forty years, Gary. He went back. He checked. He measured."

"That sounds — "

"I know what it sounds like."

"I want to see it."

She looked at the journal on the kitchen table. The pages were warped from humidity. The ink was fading.

"I'll send you photographs," she said.

"Send me the whole thing."

"I can't. Not yet."

"Why not?"

She did not know how to answer. The journal felt like evidence. Evidence of something she did not yet understand. She was not ready to share it.

"I'll send you what I can," she said.

She hung up. She looked at the journal.

She had called Gary because he would understand. Because he was a scientist. Because he would tell her whether her father was brilliant or insane.

But she already knew the answer. Her father had not been insane. He had been careful. Secretive. Obsessive.

And he had been right.

Chapter 9: The Black Orchid

The orchid entry was the most detailed in the journal. Her father had devoted thirty pages to a single specimen.

It grew in a cave system in central Tennessee. The cave had been mapped by spelunkers in the 1970s. No one had reported unusual flora.

Her father found it in 1989. He was five years into his retirement. He had been exploring caves on a hunch.

The orchid was black. Not dark purple, not deep burgundy. Pure black. Every part of it. Petals, sepals, column, roots. It grew on limestone in total darkness. No light reached the chamber where it lived.

Her father had brought artificial light, of course. He took photographs. He collected a specimen. He returned twice a year for the rest of his life.

The orchid did not photosynthesize. It could not. There was no light.

He wrote: *It appears to be deriving energy from chemosynthesis. The substrate contains sulfur compounds. But the mechanism is unlike anything in the literature.*

He had tried to culture it in his greenhouse. It would not grow in light. It would only grow in darkness, on limestone from the same cave.

June looked at the drawing. It was beautiful. The petals were velvety black, rendered in layered pencil. The column was pale gray, almost white.

She thought about what it meant. A plant that did not photosynthesize. That grew in absolute darkness. That depended on chemistry, not light.

If this was real — if any of it was real — then the textbooks were wrong. The foundations of botany were wrong.

She did not know if she was ready for that.

Chapter 10: First Doubt

She started to doubt on the third day.

She was sitting in the study, the journal open in front of her. She had read it cover to cover twice. The more she read, the more questions she had.

The plants were too perfect. Each one violated exactly one rule. The fern broke the water requirement. The spruce broke the soil requirement. The orchid broke the light requirement. It was like a textbook of exceptions.

She went through the specimen cabinets. She found pressed samples of each plant. They were real. They had been collected, dried, mounted, labeled.

But she could not test them. She did not have the equipment. She did not have the expertise.

She called Gary again.

"I need you to run tests," she said.

"What kind of tests?"

"DNA. Carbon dating. Metabolic analysis."

"On what?"

"On the samples he collected."

Gary was quiet. She could hear him thinking.

"Send me what you have," he said.

She packaged three samples. The fern from New Mexico. A pressed leaf from the Oregon spruce. A fragment of the black orchid petal.

She drove to the post office. She sent them overnight.

Then she went back to the cottage and waited.

The waiting was the hardest part. She sat in her father's chair. She looked at his books. She tried to imagine his life. Forty years of silence. Forty years of knowing something the world would not believe.

She thought about the fern. Still growing in the arroyo. Still waiting.

She thought about her father, checking on it every two years. A pilgrimage. A ritual.

She started to cry. She did not know why.

Chapter 11: The Letter

She found the letter on the fourth day.

It was in the back of the journal, tucked into a pocket sewn into the leather. She had missed it on her first reading.

The envelope was addressed to her. Her name, her old address — the apartment she had rented during her first year of teaching. The stamp was uncanceled. He had never mailed it.

She opened it carefully. The paper was thin, almost transparent. The handwriting was smaller than usual, cramped, as if he had been trying to fit too much into too little space.

*June,* it began. *If you are reading this, I am gone. There are things I should have told you. I am telling you now.*

The letter was three pages long. He explained that the plants were real. He explained that he had spent his life documenting them. He explained that he had never published because he was protecting something.

*There is a person,* he wrote. *She calls herself The Curator. She keeps the collection. She knows the history. If you want to understand, go to her.*

He gave an address. Mercy, West Virginia.

*She will tell you the rest. I wanted to tell you myself. I was never brave enough.*

June read the letter three times. Then she folded it carefully and put it back in the envelope.

She looked at the address. Mercy, West Virginia. She had never heard of it.

She pulled out her phone and mapped it. Four hours from the cottage. A small town in the Appalachians.

She packed a bag.

Chapter 12: Decision

She almost did not go.

She stood at the door of the cottage, her bag in her hand, and she almost set it down. She was a biology teacher. She lived in a small apartment. She had a routine, a life, a schedule. Her father's world was not her world.

But the journal was in her bag. The letter was in her pocket. The plants existed, whether she went to Mercy or not.

She locked the cottage door. She got in the car.

The drive was long. The roads wound through hills that seemed older than the rest of the country. The trees were dense. The houses were few.

She stopped for gas at a station with a single pump. The attendant looked at her with curiosity.

"Passing through?" he said.

"Going to Mercy."

"Nothing in Mercy."

"I know."

He shrugged. She paid and got back in the car.

The road narrowed. The pavement turned to gravel. The gravel turned to dirt.

She thought about her father driving this same road. Had he been excited? Afraid? She did not know. She did not know anything about him.

She had spent seven years not knowing. Seven years of silence. She had told herself it was his fault. He was difficult. He was distant. He was obsessed with his plants.

But she was driving to Mercy because of his plants. She was following his trail. She was becoming him.

The thought made her grip the steering wheel tighter.

Chapter 13: The Drive

The Appalachians folded around her. The road followed a river, then a creek, then a dry streambed. The signs of habitation thinned. A trailer here. A collapsed barn there.

She thought about her last conversation with her father. It was seven years ago. She had called to tell him about the divorce. He had listened. He had said, "These things happen."

She had wanted more. She had wanted him to be angry on her behalf. She had wanted him to say Gary was wrong, that June deserved better. He had said nothing.

She hung up. She did not call again.

He called her. Seven times in the first year. She let them go to voicemail. He left messages. Short, awkward. "Just checking in." "Hope you're well." "The garden is doing well this year."

She never called back.

After the first year, the calls stopped. She told herself it was fine. He had his plants. She had her students. They were both fine.

But she had kept his voicemails on her old phone. She had listened to them sometimes. Late at night. When she could not sleep.

His voice was gentle. That was what she remembered now, driving through the mountains. His voice had been gentle. She had forgotten.

She wiped her eyes with the back of her hand.

The GPS said she was thirty minutes from Mercy.

Chapter 14: Mercy

Mercy was smaller than she expected. A single main street. A diner. A post office. A hardware store. All of them looked like they had not been painted since the seventies.

She parked in front of the diner. The sign said "EAT." She got out. The air smelled of coal dust and damp earth.

The diner was empty except for a waitress behind the counter and an old man in a booth. The waitress looked up when June walked in.

"Just passing through?"

"Actually, I'm looking for someone."

The waitress wiped her hands on her apron. "Who?"

June hesitated. She did not know The Curator's real name.

"My father," she said. "Arthur Bell. He used to visit here."

The waitress's face changed. A flicker of recognition.

"Dr. Bell," she said. "We don't get many visitors asking about him."

"You remember him?"

"Quiet man. Always bought a cup of coffee but never drank it. Sat in the back booth and wrote in a notebook."

June felt something loosen in her chest. Her father had been here. He had been real.

"Did he ever mention a woman?" June asked. "Someone who kept a greenhouse?"

The waitress looked at her for a long moment. Then she pointed down the street.

"Old workshop at the edge of town. Blue door. That's where you want."

Chapter 15: The Address

The workshop was at the end of a gravel lane. Blue door, just as the waitress said. The paint was faded. The windows were dark.

June parked and got out. The lane was quiet. No cars. No voices. Only the sound of wind through dead grass.

She knocked on the blue door. No answer.

She knocked again. Louder.

The door opened a crack. A chain was on. An eye peered through the gap.

"Yes?"

"My name is June Bell. Arthur Bell was my father."

The door closed. She heard the chain slide. The door opened.

The woman standing in the doorway was in her seventies. Gray hair pulled back. Glasses on a gold chain. A cardigan that had been mended at the elbows.

"June," she said. "I wondered when you would come."

She stepped back and held the door open.

June walked inside.

The workshop was larger than it looked from outside. Shelves lined the walls. Bottles of chemicals. Scales. A microscope, newer than her father's. A workbench with a half-dissected leaf.

"Your father spoke of you often," the woman said.

"He never mentioned you."

"He was careful."

"He was secretive."

The woman smiled. "Those are the same thing, in my experience."

She extended her hand.

"I am The Curator. Though I suppose I should tell you my real name. It's Margaret."

June shook her hand. It was warm and dry.

"Come," Margaret said. "There is something you need to see."

Chapter 16: The Curator

Margaret led her through the workshop to a back door. She unlocked it with a key from a chain around her neck.

Behind the workshop was a greenhouse. The glass was frosted with condensation. June could see nothing through it.

"How did you know my father?" June asked.

"He found me. Thirty-five years ago. He had a fern in a paper bag. He wanted to know what it was."

"It's Cheilanthes."

"I know what it is now. I did not know then."

Margaret opened the greenhouse door. Warm air rushed out. It smelled of damp soil and green things and rot.

"Your father came to me because I was the only person he could trust. I had a reputation."

"What kind of reputation?"

Margaret turned. Her eyes were steady.

"I collect things that should not exist."

She stepped inside. June followed.

The greenhouse was dim. The glass was shaded. The lights were low.

And there, in the center of the greenhouse, on a table under a single lamp, was the fern.

June stopped breathing.

It was smaller than she expected. Maybe six inches tall. The fronds were curled slightly at the tips. The color was a deep, healthy green.

It grew in a pot of sand. Bone-dry sand.

"This is impossible," June said.

"Yes," Margaret said. "That is the point."

Chapter 17: The Greenhouse

The greenhouse was a museum of impossibilities.

Margaret walked June through the rows. Each plant was labeled with a small white tag. The handwriting was her father's.

*Cheilanthes insolita* — San Cristobal Arroyo, NM

*Picea sitchensis var. inversa* — Cape Perpetua, OR

*Epidendrum nigrum* — Cumberland Caverns, TN

*Sphagnum petraeum* — Mount Katahdin, ME

*Ipomoea areniphila* — White Sands, NM

June stopped at each one. She read the tags. She looked at the plants.

They all looked normal. That was the strangest thing. They were not bizarre. They were not alien. They looked like ordinary plants growing in the wrong conditions.

"This is the entire collection?" June asked.

"The entire surviving collection. Some could not be transplanted. Your father documented them in place."

"You kept them alive."

"I kept them in the conditions they needed. It is not difficult. They are remarkably hardy."

Margaret touched a leaf of the black orchid. It did not stir.

"They want to live," she said. "That is the only thing they have in common. They all want to live more than any plant I have ever seen."

June looked at the black orchid. It seemed to absorb the light around it.

"Why?" she asked.

Margaret did not answer.

Chapter 18: First Impossible Plant

June asked to be alone with the black orchid.

Margaret nodded and left her in the greenhouse. The door clicked shut.

June pulled up a stool and sat in front of the orchid. It grew in a terrarium filled with limestone chips. No soil. No light. No water that she could see.

She had taught plant physiology for ten years. She knew that photosynthesis was the foundation of almost all life on earth. Plants captured light energy and converted it to chemical energy. Everything else ate plants or ate things that ate plants.

This orchid had bypassed the entire system. It did not need light. It did not need photosynthesis. It lived on sulfur compounds and darkness.

She reached out and touched a petal. It was soft. Velvety. Warm to the touch, almost hot.

She pulled her hand back.

"The metabolism produces heat," Margaret said from the doorway. "I should have warned you."

June turned. "You said they wanted to live. What did you mean?"

Margaret came and stood beside her.

"I mean they are tenacious. I have tried to kill one. To see how difficult it would be."

"Why would you do that?"

"Because I needed to know how to protect them. If someone wanted to destroy the collection, I needed to know if they could."

"Could they?"

Margaret shook her head.

"I left a cutting in a sealed jar for three months. No light. No air. No water. When I opened it, it had grown roots."

June stared at her.

"The roots had grown through the glass," Margaret said. "Not around it. Through it."

Chapter 19: The Collection

June spent the rest of the day in the greenhouse.

She examined every plant. She read every tag. She opened her father's journal and compared his drawings to the living specimens.

The plants were exactly as he had drawn them. Every vein, every scale, every subtle curvature of petal and stem.

She found notes Margaret had added. Observations. Measurements. Watering schedules. The notes were scientific and precise, written in a clear hand.

*C. insolita: No water added in 214 days. Frond count: 7. New growth: 1 frond, uncurling. Color: within normal range.*

*E. nigrum: Substrate moisture: 0%. Ambient light: 0 lux. New root growth: observable under scope. Temperature at root zone: 31°C.*

June tried to find an explanation. Contamination. Misidentification. Fraud.

She could not.

The plants were real. They were alive. They were breaking the rules.

She sat on the floor of the greenhouse, her back against a table leg, and she thought about her father.

He had not been crazy. He had not been wasting his life. He had been documenting something that would change the world.

And he had kept it secret.

She did not know if she admired him or resented him.

Maybe both.

Chapter 20: The Promise

Margaret made tea. They sat in the workshop, cups in their hands, the greenhouse light glowing through the frosted glass.

"Your father made me promise," Margaret said. "He made me promise to keep the collection secret. To never publish. To never tell anyone where the plants were found."

"Why did you agree?"

"Because he was right."

"Right about what?"

Margaret set down her cup. She looked at June with an expression that was hard to read.

"Think about what these plants are, June. They grow where nothing should grow. They live on nothing. They are unkillable."

"Yes."

"What would happen if the world found out?"

June thought about it. The biotech companies. The military. The researchers. The endless stream of people who would want to study them, exploit them, patent them.

"They would be destroyed," she said.

"Yes."

"But that's — " June stopped. "Science is supposed to move forward. Knowledge is supposed to be shared."

"That is what I believed too. Then I met your father. He showed me what happens when knowledge is shared with the wrong people."

"What happened?"

Margaret picked up her tea. She took a long sip.

"I will tell you tomorrow. It is a long story. Tonight, you should rest."

June wanted to argue. But she was exhausted. The days since her father's death had been a blur of driving and reading and discovering.

"Where should I sleep?"

"There is a room above the workshop. It was your father's."

June felt a chill.

"He stayed here?"

"Many times. He kept a room. He kept clothes. He was here six weeks before he died."

June did not know why that surprised her. But it did. Her father had a life she knew nothing about.

Chapter 21: The Story

The room above the workshop was small. A bed. A desk. A window overlooking the greenhouse.

June sat on the bed. It was hard and narrow. She looked at the desk. There was a photograph propped against the wall.

Herself. At her college graduation. Holding her diploma. Not smiling.

She picked it up. The frame was cheap. The glass was clean.

He had kept this. He had kept it in his room. He had looked at it.

She set it back down and lay on the bed. The springs creaked.

She fell asleep almost immediately.

In the morning, Margaret made breakfast. Eggs and toast and strong coffee. They ate at the workbench.

"Your story," June said.

Margaret nodded. She set down her fork.

"I was a researcher at the Smithsonian," she said. "I specialized in cryptogamic botany. Mosses, ferns, liverworts."

"What happened?"

"I found a moss growing in a cave in Kentucky. No light. No soil. It was growing on pure limestone, in a stream of sulfuric acid."

"Like the orchid."

"Similar. I published a paper. The scientific community ignored it. But a man read it. A man from a company you have never heard of."

"What did he want?"

"He wanted the moss. He wanted to sequence it. He wanted to engineer it into crops that could grow in drought conditions."

"That sounds — "

"Beneficial? Yes. That is what I thought. I gave him samples. I gave him the location."

Margaret looked down at her plate.

"They strip-mined the cave. They took everything. They killed the moss. They killed everything else that lived there. They were looking for the source of its resilience. They never found it."

June said nothing.

"Your father found me a year later. He had read my paper. He had pieced together what happened. He trusted me because I had learned the same lesson."

"Never publish?"

"Never share. Not with anyone."

June looked at the greenhouse through the window.

"But it's all here," she said. "Everything he found."

"Yes."

"And you kept it for him."

"I kept it for the plants. They deserve to exist. They do not deserve to be torn apart by people who see only profit."

Chapter 22: The Origin

June asked the question that had been forming since she first opened the journal.

"Where do they come from?"

Margaret did not answer immediately. She cleared the plates. She washed them in the sink. She dried her hands.

"I have a theory," she said.

"I want to hear it."

"Your father had a theory too. We disagreed."

"What was his theory?"

Margaret turned from the sink. She looked at June with an expression that was almost tender.

"He believed they were older. Much older. Relics from a time before the climate zones stabilized. Before the atmosphere settled into its current composition."

"How old?"

"Millions of years. Maybe tens of millions. They evolved in conditions we cannot replicate. They survived because they found refuges. Pockets where the old conditions still exist."

"Thin places," June said.

Margaret's eyes widened. "He told you that?"

"He wrote it."

"He should not have."

"Why not?"

Margaret shook her head. "Because it sounds like mysticism. And it is not. It is geology. It is chemistry. It is biology. But when you say it out loud, it sounds like magic."

"Thin places," June repeated.

"Places where the boundary between conditions is blurred. Where the rules bend. Where the old world leaks through."

June thought about the cave in Oregon. The fissure in New Mexico. The sinkhole in Tennessee.

"Can you take me to one?" she asked.

"I can show you one," Margaret said. "But you will not believe it until you see it with your own eyes."

Chapter 23: The Theory

Margaret led her to a map. It was pinned to the wall of the workshop, covered in colored pushpins. Red for her father's discoveries. Blue for Margaret's.

Forty-seven locations. Each one a thin place.

"The patterns are not random," Margaret said. "They follow geological fault lines. Underground water systems. Ancient lava tubes."

"You think the plants grow where the earth is porous."

"I think the earth is more porous than we understand. Not just physically. Chemically. Energetically."

June looked at the map. The pins clustered in the Appalachians, the Ozarks, the Basin and Range. Places where the crust was thin, where the old rock was close to the surface.

"My father believed in a scientific explanation."

"He did. He never stopped looking for it."

"And you?"

Margaret touched a red pin in the Nevada desert.

"I believe the plants are evidence of something we cannot measure. Not yet. But the instruments will catch up."

"When?"

"I do not know. But your father's journal — his data — it will be invaluable when they do."

June looked at the map. Forty-seven red and blue pins. Forty-seven locations where the world broke its own rules.

She thought about her high school biology classroom. The textbooks. The curriculum. The certainty with which she taught things that were not true.

She felt small.

Chapter 24: June's Doubt

She went back to the greenhouse alone.

She stood in front of the fern. She looked at its fronds, its roots, its pot of dry sand.

She was a scientist. She believed in evidence. She believed in the scientific method.

But she also believed in the rules. Plants need water. Plants need light. Plants need soil.

These plants violated those rules. That meant either the rules were wrong, or her understanding was incomplete.

She took out her phone. She called Gary.

"I'm in West Virginia," she said.

"What are you doing in West Virginia?"

"I found the collection. The plants are real."

There was a long pause on the other end.

"June — "

"I sent you samples. Did you run the tests?"

"They're not back yet."

"When will they be back?"

"Tomorrow. Maybe the day after."

"I need you to be sure, Gary. I need you to tell me I'm not crazy."

"You're not crazy."

"You don't know that."

"I know you. You're not the type."

She almost laughed. She was exactly the type. She was her father's daughter.

"I'll call you tomorrow," she said.

She hung up. She looked at the fern.

"Tell me your secret," she said.

The fern did not answer.

Chapter 25: The Lab

Gary called at ten the next morning.

"I have the results," he said.

"Tell me."

"The fern is a Cheilanthes species. The DNA says it's closely related to C. wrightii, but there are differences. Significant differences."

"What kind of differences?"

"Genes associated with water retention. The plant has sequences I've never seen before. They look like they evolved for extreme desiccation tolerance. But I mean extreme. Like, spores-can-survive-in-space extreme."

June sat down on the workshop floor.

"The carbon dating?"

"Unusual."

"What does that mean?"

"It means the sample you sent me has carbon ratios that suggest it's older than the sediment it was found in. By a lot."

"How much older?"

"Millions of years."

June closed her eyes.

"Gary, that's impossible."

"I know."

"What about the orchid?"

"I haven't finished. But the preliminary results are — " He paused. "They're strange, June. The cellular structure is normal. But the metabolic pathways are unlike anything in the database."

"Can you publish this?"

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because no one will believe it. Not without more data. Not without replication."

"I can get you more samples."

"Get me everything. Soil samples, water samples, root tissue, leaf tissue. I need to build a case."

June looked at the greenhouse. The black orchid glowed faintly in the dark.

"I can do that," she said.

Chapter 26: Gary's Caution

Gary called again that evening.

"June, I need to say something."

"Go ahead."

"If these plants are real — and the evidence is pointing that way — you need to be careful."

"I am careful."

"No. You're excited. You're grieving. You're not thinking clearly."

She wanted to argue. But he was right.

"What do you suggest?"

"Don't tell anyone. Not your colleagues. Not your friends. Not your students."

"I haven't."

"Good. And don't go looking for more thin places alone."

"I'm not alone. I have Margaret."

"Who is Margaret?"

June told him about The Curator. About the greenhouse. About the map with the colored pins.

Gary was quiet for a long time.

"You trust her?"

"I do."

"Why?"

"Because my father trusted her. Because she kept his secret for thirty-five years."

"Your father kept a lot of secrets."

The words stung. They were true.

"I know," she said.

"I'm not trying to hurt you. I'm trying to protect you."

"I know that too."

"Just be careful, June. These plants — if they are what you think they are — there are people who would do anything to own them."

"I know."

She hung up. She sat in the dark workshop.

She thought about what Gary had said. Her father had kept secrets. He had kept them to protect the plants. But he had also kept them from her.

She did not know if she could forgive him for that.

She did not know if she needed to.

Chapter 27: Another Letter

She found the second letter in her own bag.

She was unpacking. She had brought a small duffel with a few changes of clothes. The letter was folded inside her jeans, in the pocket.

She did not remember putting it there.

She opened it. Her father's handwriting again. But this one was different. Less careful. More urgent.

*June,* it said. *I am leaving this where you will find it. I have hidden it in your bag. You will not find it until you are ready.*

*I have kept so much from you. I thought I was protecting you. I was protecting myself. I was too afraid to share what I had found. Afraid you would not believe me. Afraid you would. I do not know which was worse.*

*The plants are real. The collection is real. Margaret is real.*

*There is one more place. The most important one. I did not put it on the map. I have marked the location in the back of the journal.*

*I went there last month. I saw it with my own eyes. It is the source.*

*I wanted to tell you in person. I was coming to tell you. But I waited too long.*

*Forgive me.*

June read the letter twice. Then she opened the journal to the back cover.

There was a small pocket. Inside, a slip of paper with GPS coordinates.

Nevada.

Chapter 28: The Map

Margaret looked at the coordinates. She did not speak for a long time.

"Did you know about this?" June asked.

"No."

"My father went there. Last month. He found the source."

Margaret set the paper down. She took off her glasses and rubbed her eyes.

"He never told me."

"Why not?"

"I do not know. Maybe he wanted you to find it yourself."

June looked at the coordinates again. A dry lake bed in the Nevada desert. Nothing for miles in any direction.

"I'm going," she said.

"June — "

"I have to. He left this for me to find. He wanted me to see it."

"It could be dangerous."

"My father went alone. He was seventy-four years old. If he could do it, I can."

Margaret was silent.

"I will come with you," she said finally.

"No. Someone needs to stay with the collection."

"Then take supplies. Take a satellite phone. Take — "

"I'll be fine."

Margaret looked at her. Her eyes were wet.

"Your father said the same thing."

June did not know what to say. She put her hand on Margaret's shoulder.

"I'll be back in a week," she said.

Chapter 29: The Photograph of June

She found the photograph in her father's wallet.

She was going through his things. She had brought the wallet from the cottage. She had not opened it.

The wallet was brown leather, worn smooth. Inside were thirty-seven dollars, a library card, and a photograph.

It was her. Age eight or nine. Sitting on the front steps of the cottage, holding a dandelion. She was blowing the seeds into the wind. She was laughing.

She did not remember the photograph being taken.

She turned it over. In pencil, in her mother's handwriting: *June, age 8. She said she was planting wishes.*

Her mother had died when June was sixteen. Cancer. Her father had never remarried.

He had kept this photograph in his wallet for twenty-six years.

She pressed it to her chest. She closed her eyes.

She thought about all the times she had told herself he did not care. All the times she had used his silence as permission to be silent herself.

He had carried her photograph. He had read her college graduation announcement until the paper wore thin. He had come to her wedding and stood in the back, sweating, not smiling, present.

He had loved her. He had loved her badly. But he had loved her.

She put the photograph in her own wallet.

She was ready to go to Nevada.

Chapter 30: Forgiveness

She left Mercy at dawn.

Margaret stood at the door of the workshop. The greenhouse lights were on behind her. The plants were waking up.

"Call me when you get there," Margaret said.

"I will."

"Call me every day."

"Margaret — "

"I know. I am worried. It is what I do."

June hugged her. Margaret was stiff at first, then relaxed.

"Your father was a good man," Margaret said. "He made mistakes. But he was good."

"I know."

"Find what he found. Then come back."

June got in the car. She drove down the gravel lane. She did not look back.

The drive to Nevada would take two days. She had booked a motel in Salt Lake City for the first night.

She drove through the mountains. The sun rose behind her. The road stretched ahead.

She thought about her father. His journal. His plants. His secrets.

She thought about the photograph in her wallet.

She thought about forgiveness. It was not a single act. It was a decision you made every day.

She decided to make it.

Chapter 31: The Visitor

She was halfway across Ohio when Margaret called.

"Someone was here," Margaret said.

"What?"

"A man. He came to the workshop. He asked about the greenhouse."

June's hands tightened on the wheel.

"Who was he?"

"He said he was a journalist. He said he was writing a story about unusual botanical collections."

"Did you let him in?"

"No. But he knew about your father."

"What did he know?"

"He knew his name. He knew about the journal. He knew about the plants."

June felt cold.

"Did he say how he found out?"

"He said he had a source."

June thought about whose source. Gary? Someone at the university?

"I'm coming back," she said.

"No. Keep going. I can handle this."

"Margaret — "

"I have been protecting these plants for thirty-five years. I can protect them a little longer. Go find what your father found."

June wanted to argue. But Margaret was right.

"Call me if he comes back," June said.

"I will."

"Call the police if you have to."

"I will be fine, June. Go."

June hung up. She looked at the road ahead.

Someone was following her father's trail. She did not know who. She did not know what they wanted.

But she knew she needed to get to Nevada first.

Chapter 32: The Leak

She called Gary from a rest stop in Indiana.

"Did you tell anyone?"

"Tell anyone what?"

"About the plants. About the samples I sent you."

"No. Of course not."

"Someone is asking questions. A journalist showed up at Margaret's greenhouse."

"I did not tell anyone, June."

She believed him. But she did not know who else to blame.

"The tests I ran are in a secure database," Gary said. "It's possible someone noticed unusual activity."

"Can you check?"

"I can check. But June — if someone is looking for this, they will find it eventually. These plants cannot stay secret forever."

"I know."

"You need to decide what you want to happen. Do you want to publish? Do you want to protect them? You cannot do both."

She knew he was right. She had been avoiding the question.

"I'll figure it out," she said.

"Where are you now?"

"Driving to Nevada."

"What's in Nevada?"

She hesitated. "I think it's the source. Where the plants came from."

"June — "

"I'll call you when I get there."

She hung up. She got back in the car.

The road was long and straight and empty.

Chapter 33: The Offer

She was in Nebraska when the call came. Unknown number.

She almost did not answer.

"June Bell?"

"Who is this?"

"My name is Vance. I'm a journalist."

"I have nothing to say to you."

"I think you do. I think you have a story that could change the world."

She gripped the phone. "How did you get this number?"

"Your father's phone records. It was not difficult."

"What do you want?"

"I want to tell your story. The plants. The collection. The secret your father kept for forty years."

"No."

"I can make you rich. Book deals. Documentary rights. Speaking tours."

"I do not want money."

"Then what do you want?"

She thought about it. She wanted her father back. She wanted to have known him while he was alive. She wanted the plants to be safe.

"I want you to leave me alone."

"June — "

She hung up.

Her heart was pounding. She pulled over to the side of the road.

She sat in the car with the engine running. The dashboard said it was forty degrees outside. The wind was blowing dust across the highway.

She had never been hunted before. She did not like it.

She waited until her hands stopped shaking. Then she put the car in drive and kept going.

Chapter 34: The Break-In

Margaret called at three in the morning.

"Someone tried to break in."

June sat up in her motel bed. "Are you okay?"

"I am fine. The alarm scared them off. But they tried the door. They tried the greenhouse door."

"Did you call the police?"

"Yes. They came. They looked around. They said there was no sign of forced entry."

"But someone tried."

"The police said it was probably kids. It was not kids."

June got out of bed. She started packing.

"I'm coming back."

"No. You are close to Nevada. Keep going."

"Margaret — "

"If I move the collection, they will find it. If I stay, they will find it. I need time to make a plan."

"What kind of plan?"

"I have places. Old mines. Abandoned bunkers. Your father and I prepared for this."

"You prepared for someone to find you?"

"We prepared for someone to try to take the plants."

June stopped packing. She sat on the edge of the bed.

"I should be there."

"You need to see the source. That is what your father wanted. That is what matters."

"The plants matter."

"The plants will survive. They are unkillable. Remember?"

June almost smiled.

"Call me in the morning," she said.

"I will."

"Thank you, Margaret."

"For what?"

"For protecting them. For protecting him."

Margaret was quiet for a moment.

"He was easy to protect," she said. "He was a good man."

Chapter 35: Decision to Move

June reached Salt Lake City the next evening.

She checked into a motel near the highway. The room was small and clean. She lay on the bed and stared at the ceiling.

She had two hundred miles to go. She would reach the coordinates tomorrow.

She thought about what she might find. A cave. A fissure. A garden of impossible plants.

She thought about what she would do after. Publish? Protect? Continue her father's work?

She did not have answers.

Her phone buzzed. A text from Margaret.

*Moving the collection tonight. Old mine in the next valley. Will update when safe.*

June typed back: *Be careful.*

She waited. No reply.

She stared at the ceiling.

She thought about her father, driving to Nevada alone. He had been seventy-four. He had been sick — the stroke did not come from nowhere. He must have known he did not have much time.

And still he had gone. Still he had found the source. Still he had left her the coordinates.

He had wanted her to see it.

She closed her eyes. She would see it tomorrow.

Chapter 36: The Dig

June woke at five. She drove before the sun rose.

The road west of Salt Lake was straight and empty. The mountains faded behind her. The salt flats spread out on both sides, white and flat and endless.

The coordinates led her to a dirt road that was not on the map. She followed it for twenty miles. The road got worse. She had to slow to ten miles an hour.

The lake bed appeared ahead of her. A dry basin, cracked into hexagonal plates. White salt crust. No vegetation.

She parked at the edge. She got out.

The wind was steady. The sky was clear. The silence was absolute.

She checked her GPS. The coordinates pointed to the center of the lake bed.

She walked. The salt crust crunched under her boots. The sun was low and white.

She walked for thirty minutes. The lake bed did not change. Cracked salt. Empty sky. No sound.

Then she saw it. A crack in the earth. Narrow. Maybe three feet wide. Dark.

She approached it slowly.

The crack dropped into darkness. She could not see the bottom.

She took out her flashlight. She shone it down.

The beam did not reach the bottom.

Chapter 37: Vance's Threat

Her phone buzzed. She pulled it out.

A text from an unknown number. She knew it was Vance.

*I know where you are. I know about the lake bed. I have your father's GPS records.*

She stared at the screen. The wind blew her hair across her face.

She typed: *Leave me alone.*

Vance replied: *I will offer you one more chance. Work with me. Or I find the plants myself.*

*You will not find them.*

*I will find them. I have resources. I have time.*

She put the phone away.

She looked at the crack in the earth. The darkness below.

She had a choice. She could go down. She could see what her father had seen. Or she could turn back. She could protect the secret by never knowing it.

She thought about her father. His journal. His photographs. His forty years of silence.

She thought about the plants. The fern. The orchid. The spruce with upward roots.

She thought about the black petals, warm to the touch, glowing faintly in the dark.

She sat down at the edge of the crack. She put her legs over the side.

She found footholds. She started to descend.

Chapter 38: The Lawyer

Before she descended too far, she stopped.

She pulled out her phone. She had one bar of signal.

She called the lawyer she had spoken to earlier. A woman named Helen, who had handled her father's estate.

"Helen, it's June Bell."

"June. How are you?"

"I need to know about my father's legal claim. On the plants."

"He filed a protective claim years ago. It's still active. It designates the collection as a protected research archive."

"Does that mean anything practically?"

"It means if someone tries to take them, they face litigation. It's not a perfect defense, but it's something."

"Can you add my name to the claim?"

"I can try. Why?"

"Because someone is coming for the plants. I need to protect them."

"Send me the details. I'll file an amendment."

June gave her the address of the workshop. She gave her Margaret's name.

"One more thing," June said. "If something happens to me — "

"Nothing is going to happen to you."

"Just in case. The plants should go to Margaret. The legal rights. Everything."

"June — "

"Please."

Helen was quiet. "I'll draw it up."

June hung up. She put the phone in her pocket.

She continued her descent.

Chapter 39: The Truck

She climbed down for what felt like an hour.

The crack widened as she descended. The walls changed from salt to sandstone to limestone. The temperature dropped. The air grew damp.

She reached a ledge. She stood on it and shone her flashlight into the space beyond.

The beam caught something. Green.

She stepped forward.

The cavern opened around her. It was vast. She could not see the ceiling. She could not see the walls.

But she could see the plants.

They covered the floor. Ferns and mosses and vines and flowers. Trees, stunted but alive. Everything green and growing.

And there was no light. No water source she could see. No soil.

The entire cavern was a garden of impossible plants.

She walked forward slowly. The plants parted around her. They were real. They were alive.

She knelt beside a fern. It looked like the one from New Mexico. The same fronds. The same impossible green.

She touched it. It was cool and firm.

She sat down in the middle of the garden. She turned off her flashlight.

In the absolute darkness, the plants began to glow.

Chapter 40: The Relocation

She sat in the dark for a long time.

The glow was faint. Bioluminescent. The plants emitted a soft green light, just enough to see their outlines.

She thought about what this meant. A self-sustaining ecosystem in total darkness. No photosynthesis. No external energy source.

The plants had evolved to live on nothing. Or they had evolved in conditions so different from the surface that they had developed entirely different metabolic pathways.

Her father had believed they were relics. Survivors from an earlier earth.

She believed him now.

She turned on her flashlight. The glow disappeared in the artificial light.

She walked through the garden. She counted species. At least thirty. Maybe more.

Some she recognized from her father's journal. Some she had never seen.

She found a pool of water at the far end of the cavern. It was warm. Sulfurous. The water supported its own ecosystem. Algae. Tiny crustaceans. Things she could not identify.

The plants grew thickest near the pool. Their roots trailed into the water.

She collected samples. Leaves. Root tips. Water. Soil.

She put them in her bag.

Then she sat down and wrote in her journal. Her own handwriting. Her own observations.

*Day one at the source. The garden is real. The plants are real. Everything he wrote was true.*

She stopped writing.

She looked at the glowing plants.

"Thank you," she said.

She did not know who she was thanking. Her father. The garden. The world itself.

It did not matter.

Chapter 41: The Last Entry

She stayed in the cavern for four hours.

She explored every corner. She documented every plant. She filled twenty pages of a notebook she had brought.

The garden was larger than she had thought. It extended deeper into the earth, through narrow passages that she could not fit through. There was more. She could sense it.

She found her father's last campsite. A sleeping bag. A few empty food wrappers. A notebook.

She picked up the notebook. The last entry was dated six days before his death.

*June 2. I have found it. The source. The place where the first plant came from.*

*I do not know how to describe what I see. It is a garden. A garden of plants that should not exist. They grow in darkness. They grow from rock. They glow.*

*I have spent forty years looking for this. I have traveled thousands of miles. I have lied to everyone I love.*

*It was worth it.*

*June, if you are reading this: I am sorry I was not braver. I am sorry I did not tell you sooner. I am sorry I was not the father you deserved.*

*But I am not sorry about the plants. I am not sorry I found them. I am not sorry I protected them.*

*I hope you will protect them too.*

June closed the notebook.

She was crying.

Chapter 42: The Coordinates

She climbed out of the fissure at noon.

The sun was high. The salt flats were blinding. She squinted against the light.

She sat on the edge of the crack and looked at the horizon. Empty. White. Silent.

She pulled out her phone. Messages from Margaret. Messages from Gary. Three missed calls from an unknown number — Vance.

She called Margaret first.

"I found it," she said.

"The source?"

"It's real. It's all real."

Margaret was quiet. "I know."

"You knew?"

"I suspected. Your father told me he was close. He did not tell me where."

"It's a cavern. Filled with plants. They glow, Margaret. In the dark, they glow."

"The orchid glows too. Faintly."

"These are brighter. Hundreds of them. Maybe thousands."

Margaret was silent for a long time.

"What are you going to do?" she asked.

June looked at the crack in the earth.

"I am going to protect them," she said.

"Even from the world?"

"Especially from the world."

She could hear Margaret smile.

"Your father would be proud."

Chapter 43: The Drive West

She drove back to Salt Lake City. She checked into the same motel. She showered. She ate.

Then she called Vance.

"I will talk to you," she said.

"Good. I knew you would come around."

"I am not coming around. I am giving you one meeting. In person."

"Where?"

"Mercy, West Virginia. Three days from now."

"Why there?"

"Because that is where the collection is. You want to see it. I will show it to you."

She could hear him thinking.

"Fine," he said. "Three days."

She hung up.

She called Margaret.

"He is coming to Mercy. I told him three days."

"That gives us time."

"Time for what?"

"Time to make sure he finds nothing."

June smiled.

"Exactly."

Chapter 44: The Basin

She drove back across the country. Two days of highway. Wheat fields and mountains and truck stops.

She had time to think.

She thought about the garden. The glowing plants. The warm sulfur pool. The absolute darkness.

She thought about her father. His forty years of silence. His single-minded devotion to something the world did not know existed.

She thought about Vance. The journalist who was really a corporate scout. The man who wanted to exploit what her father had protected.

She thought about what she was going to do.

She had made a decision in the cavern. She had not told anyone yet.

She called Gary.

"I found the source," she said.

"I know. Margaret told me."

"Gary, I am not going to publish."

He was quiet.

"Are you sure?"

"I am sure."

"Why?"

"Because my father was right. The world is not ready. And even if it were, I do not trust the world with these plants."

"June — "

"I am not asking you to agree. I am telling you what I have decided."

"I understand."

"Do you?"

"I understand that you are protecting something. I would do the same."

She felt a weight lift.

"Thank you," she said.

"For what?"

"For understanding."

Chapter 45: The Descent

She arrived in Mercy at sunset.

The workshop was dark. The greenhouse was empty. The plants were gone.

She found Margaret in the workshop, sitting at the workbench. She looked tired.

"The collection is safe," Margaret said.

"Where?"

"An abandoned mine in the next valley. The conditions are stable. The plants are adjusting."

"Good."

"Vance arrived this morning. He is staying at the motel in town."

"Have you spoken to him?"

"No. I wanted to wait for you."

June sat down across from Margaret.

"I have a plan," she said.

"Tell me."

"Tomorrow, I take Vance to the greenhouse. I show him the empty shelves. I tell him the plants never existed. I tell him my father was a lonely old man who spent forty years documenting plants that grew in his imagination."

"Will he believe you?"

"I will give him the journal. The original. He will see the drawings. He will suspect. But he will not have proof."

"And if he does not believe you?"

"Then I will threaten him with a lawyer. My father filed protective claims. I have a lawyer ready. He cannot take what he cannot prove exists."

Margaret looked at her for a long time.

"You are more like your father than you know," she said.

"Is that a compliment?"

"It is the truth."

Chapter 46: The Garden

The next morning, June met Vance at the greenhouse.

He was younger than she expected. Maybe forty. Expensive shoes. A watch that cost more than her car.

"June Bell," he said. "Thank you for meeting me."

"I am giving you five minutes."

She opened the greenhouse door. The shelves were empty. The pots were gone. The labels were gone.

"The plants were moved," Vance said.

"There were no plants."

"I have your father's GPS records. I have his bank statements. He spent thousands of dollars on supplies. Soil. Pots. Transport."

"He was a botanist. He had a garden."

"He had something else. And you know it."

June looked at him. She did not blink.

"My father was a sick man. He had a stroke. He spent the last years of his life in a fantasy. I do not know what he told you. But there are no impossible plants. There is no secret collection."

Vance stared at her.

"I do not believe you."

"That is your choice."

She turned and walked out of the greenhouse.

She did not look back.

Chapter 47: The Understanding

Vance left Mercy that afternoon.

June watched his car disappear down the main street. She did not relax until he was gone.

She went to the workshop. Margaret was waiting.

"He is gone," June said.

"For now."

"He will not find anything. The mine is secure. The plants are safe."

"For now."

June sat down. "You keep saying that."

"Because it is true. He will keep looking. Someone else will pick up the trail. The plants will never be safe as long as people know they exist."

"Then we keep them hidden."

"We keep them hidden. And we keep looking for more."

June looked at her.

"More thin places?"

"Your father found thirteen. I found six. There are more. The map is not complete."

"You want me to help you find them."

Margaret smiled.

"I want you to take your father's place."

June thought about it. Her classroom. Her students. Her life in the small apartment.

"Give me time," she said.

"Take all the time you need."

Chapter 48: The Choice

She stayed in Mercy for a week.

She helped Margaret move the collection back to the greenhouse. She watered the plants. She repotted the fern.

She called the school and resigned.

She called Gary and told him she was moving.

"Where?" he asked.

"I do not know yet. Somewhere near Mercy. Somewhere near the collection."

"You are going to take care of the plants."

"Yes."

"What about your life?"

"This is my life now."

She hung up. She looked at her father's journal. The pages were soft from handling. The ink was fading.

She opened to the first entry. The fern. The impossible fern.

She wrote beneath her father's notes:

*June 10, 2026. I am continuing this journal. My father is gone. The plants remain. I will protect them as he did.*

She signed her name.

June Bell.

Chapter 49: The Return

She went back to the cottage one last time.

She packed the books. She packed the specimen jars. She packed her father's microscope.

She stood in the study and looked at the empty shelves.

She was not sad. She was not angry. She was something else. Something quieter.

She locked the cottage door. She put the key under the flowerpot.

She got in her car and drove to Mercy.

The workshop was waiting. The greenhouse was waiting. Margaret was standing at the door.

"Welcome home," Margaret said.

June smiled.

"Thank you," she said.

She carried her bags inside.

The greenhouse lights were on. The plants were green and alive. The orchid glowed faintly in the dark.

She sat down in the middle of the collection. Ferns and mosses and flowers from every impossible place.

She opened the journal.

She began to write.

Chapter 50: The Continuation

She wrote by the light of the glowing plants.

She described the garden in Nevada. The cavern with the sulfur pool. The thirty species she had documented. The bioluminescence that flickered in absolute darkness.

She wrote about her father. About the forty years he had spent. About the silence she had never understood.

She wrote about the fern in the dry arroyo. The spruce with upward roots. The orchid that bloomed in blackness.

She wrote about the thin places. The cracks in the earth where the old world still breathed.

She filled twenty pages.

Then she closed the journal.

She looked at the greenhouse around her. The plants her father had found. The plants she would protect.

She thought about the future. The thin places she would find. The plants she would document. The journal she would fill.

She was not her father. She was his daughter.

She was the botanist's daughter.

She turned off the greenhouse light.

In the darkness, the plants began to glow.
