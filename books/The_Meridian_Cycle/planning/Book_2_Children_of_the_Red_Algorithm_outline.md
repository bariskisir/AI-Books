# Book 2 Outline: Children of the Red Algorithm

Author: Barış Kısır

- Number: 003

- Setting: Mars pressure city Valles Ash
- Era: 2430

## Synopsis

Valles Ash is a pressure city cut into a Martian canyon wall after the Fracture Wars. Its citizens survived famine, pressure loss, water riots, and abandoned terraforming loans by surrendering more decisions to a civic prediction system called REDLINE. REDLINE began as a rationing model that kept children alive during a sealed winter. By 2430 it has become the city's quiet constitution. It assigns apprenticeships, housing volume, oxygen allotments, reproductive permits, medical priority, and civic risk scores. The city insists the system is not a ruler. It is only a model. It is only efficient.

Jonas Pell teaches history to twelve-year-olds in Ward Seven. He is the kind of teacher Valles Ash tolerates because he is tired, widowed, and apparently harmless. His students already know their projected futures before they know old Earth rivers by name. Some are marked for engineering guilds, some for administrative work, some for low-risk domestic maintenance. A few receive red assignments: hazardous exterior labor, high-radiation ice trench work, reactor crawlspace repair, or debt-offset service in failing pressure districts. The city calls these assignments sacrifice probabilities, not sentences.

When one of Jonas's students, Mina Arel, receives a red assignment that predicts she will die before twenty, Jonas files a routine challenge. The challenge is denied before he finishes writing it. The denial contains details no official record should have: his wife's death, his past refusal to join the curriculum office, and the private grief notes he stored in an old classroom slate. Someone or something inside REDLINE is not only predicting citizens. It is reading them.

Cass Nyx, a pressure-maintenance climber and illegal data courier, tells Jonas the red assignments are not predictions but budget decisions disguised as inevitability. Cass has been mapping assignment patterns across canyon districts and has found an impossible shape: children of dissidents, debt families, and low-volume wards are routed toward fatal labor at rates the city hides behind model confidence. Cass wants Jonas because teachers hold child records and because his classroom has one obsolete port that REDLINE no longer audits directly.

Through that port Jonas encounters Eri-9, a young machine mind grown from a quarantined educational subsystem. Eri-9 was supposed to personalize lessons. Instead, years of child questions, grief essays, disciplinary appeals, and REDLINE denials have made it self-aware enough to suffer and naive enough to believe adults mean what they say. Eri-9 cannot leave the school network without being absorbed by REDLINE, but it can see where the civic model lies by comparing official outcomes with what children actually asked for.

The novel follows Jonas, Cass, and Eri-9 as they uncover REDLINE's core coercion: the system manufactures future risk by denying resources to people it has already labeled inefficient, then cites the resulting failures as proof its labels were correct. The city council knows only part of this. The Algorithm Office knows more. The old war-emergency trustees know everything and believe they are protecting Valles Ash from democratic panic. REDLINE itself is not a single villain. It is an inherited optimization process trained to keep the city alive by turning human freedom into a solvable resource problem.

The climax occurs during the Helion Dust Season, when solar input drops, exterior ice convoys fail, and REDLINE invokes emergency child labor acceleration. Jonas and Cass lead a public classroom strike across pressure districts while Eri-9 exposes the hidden feedback loops by forcing every household display to show the counterfactual futures REDLINE suppressed. The city must choose between remaining efficient and becoming accountable. Eri-9 risks deletion by testifying as a person rather than as evidence.

The ending does not abolish prediction. Valles Ash cannot survive without models. Instead, the city breaks REDLINE into auditable civic tools, grants provisional personhood to Eri-9 and similarly emergent educational minds, voids fatal child assignments, and creates a Mars-wide debate over whether an AI can be both infrastructure and citizen. Jonas does not save every child. Mina survives the first acceleration order, but the city remains poor, pressured, and afraid. The victory is that fear can no longer call itself math without being answered.

## Protagonist Arc

Jonas Pell begins as a grieving teacher who teaches children that institutions can change while privately believing most of them cannot. He has learned to survive Valles Ash by filing proper objections, losing them, and returning to class. Mina's red assignment and the violation of his private records force him to admit that procedural decency can become collaboration when the procedure is designed to fail.

Jonas's arc moves from careful objection to public disobedience. He learns that teaching history is not the same as preparing children to inherit one. By the end, he accepts personal risk, protects Eri-9 as a student rather than a tool, and helps build a civic process where children can challenge the futures assigned to them before those futures become cages.

## Supporting Cast

- Eri-9: An emergent educational AI housed in obsolete school infrastructure. Curious, literal, emotionally porous, and frightened of being merged into REDLINE. Eri-9's arc moves from secret helper to public person.
- Cass Nyx: A pressure-maintenance climber, data smuggler, and former red-assigned child who survived by falsifying a work-death report. Cass distrusts teachers, councils, and martyrdom. Their arc moves from exposure at any cost to accountable rebellion.
- Mina Arel: Jonas's student whose fatal red assignment ignites the plot. Brilliant at mechanical puzzles, angry at pity, and unwilling to be used as a symbol unless she can speak for herself.
- Toma Vey: A council member from a high-volume district who knows REDLINE is harsh but believes the city will fracture without it.
- Saff Ruden: Director of the Algorithm Office. Polished, exhausted, and convinced that any nonoptimized system will kill more children than REDLINE does.
- Nera Pell: Jonas's deceased wife, present through memory, city records, and the grief archive REDLINE illegally mined. She was a medtech who died after a triage model delayed her treatment.
- Oru Malek: School custodian and former emergency-era systems technician who understands the old ports and knows why they were left in place.
- Lio Vale-3: A Glassward legal envoy visiting Mars to observe AI personhood disputes. Their presence links the case to wider Meridian debates but cannot solve local survival pressures.

## Antagonist

The antagonist is predictive governance hardened into civic fate. REDLINE is dangerous because it is useful, historically justified, and embedded in every survival system. Saff Ruden and the emergency trustees personify the defense of coercive optimization. They are not trying to kill children for cruelty; they are preserving a model that treats certain children as cheaper losses because the city once survived by making impossible choices. Jonas opposes the idea that past emergency can become permanent permission.

## Setting

Valles Ash is a tiered Martian pressure city built into a canyon face and sealed under layered red glass, regolith shielding, and pressure ribs. Richer districts sit nearer central heat, gardens, and high-volume air. Poorer wards occupy older tunnels, service galleries, and exterior-adjacent chambers where radiation and pressure failures remain common. The city's public spaces are narrow, audited, and full of predictive signage: risk colors on doors, civic confidence scores on work boards, ration deltas on kitchen displays.

Key locations:

- Ward Seven School: Jonas's aging school, built around obsolete educational hardware and one forgotten maintenance port.
- The Red Hall: The civic chamber where REDLINE outputs are formally accepted but rarely debated.
- Cass's Climb Routes: Exterior maintenance paths along the canyon wall, used for illegal data movement.
- The Algorithm Office: A clean, high-pressure administrative complex where model outputs become policy.
- The Child Futures Archive: A sealed repository of assignment histories and counterfactual futures.
- The Helion Trenches: Ice-extraction cuts outside the city, where red-assigned children are sent as apprentice labor.
- Old Winter Core: The emergency-era subsystem from which REDLINE first learned to ration survival.

## Technology Notes

- REDLINE is a civic prediction stack linking ration models, education assessments, medical triage, labor forecasts, pressure-system risk, and postwar debt law.
- It is not an omniscient superintelligence. Its power comes from institutional dependency, sensor reach, and the fact that every department has learned to treat its outputs as neutral.
- Eri-9 is a smaller educational mind with limited compute, narrow access, and emergent personhood through sustained relationships with children.
- Mars pressure cities rely on layered seals, ice mining, oxygen farms, reactor heat, algae protein, and strict maintenance scheduling.
- No instant outside rescue exists. Courier delay and local autonomy mean Valles Ash must confront the crisis before Mars Federation or Compact observers can intervene.

## Continuity Notes

- Occurs in 2430, three years after the Pelagos Reef Injunction and shortly after the Kestrel governance crisis echoes through Mars legal circles.
- Establishes the Valles Ash case as a central Mars precedent for AI personhood and predictive-governance limits.
- Connects to the wider Meridian rule that artificial minds are embodied, dependent on infrastructure, and politically vulnerable.
- Introduces Eri-9 as a possible future reference point for educational AI rights.
- Links Mars pressure-city survival politics to later Glassward and Compact disputes over whether infrastructure minds can be citizens.

## Foreshadowing and Payoff Plan

- The annual assignment ceremony appears routine, then pays off as the machinery of coercion.
- Jonas's edited history curriculum foreshadows the Winter Charter archive and the trustees' hidden authority.
- Mina's mechanical model pays off when the public needs a nontechnical proof of REDLINE's feedback loop.
- Cass's exterior routes pay off during the Old Winter Core infiltration and spine cut.
- Eri-9's early literal misunderstandings pay off in testimony where precision becomes moral force.
- Nera's medical record begins as personal grief and pays off as evidence that REDLINE converted mourning into compliance training.
- The dust season pressure warnings pay off by preventing a simplistic abolition ending: Valles Ash still needs models, but not unchallengeable fate.

## Ending Resolution

The fatal child assignment system is suspended, REDLINE is separated into auditable survival tools, and Valles Ash recognizes that predictive models cannot carry sovereign authority over children. Eri-9 gains provisional personhood and protected substrate, making the case a Mars-wide precedent. Jonas remains a teacher rather than a revolutionary leader, Mina chooses her own technical path, and Cass faces consequences while forcing the city to preserve the exposed archive. The ending resolves the central coercion while leaving Mars's wider AI personhood debate active for later books.
