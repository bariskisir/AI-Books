# The Watermelon Summer

## Premise

Three sisters in rural Georgia spend one transformative summer running their late father's watermelon stand and learning the price of family secrets. After their father's sudden death, Delia, Rose, and Junie Harvell must decide whether to sell the family farm or keep it alive through the summer. The watermelon stand at the crossroads becomes the stage where old wounds are exposed, new alliances form, and the truth about their mother's disappearance twenty years ago finally surfaces.

## Core Cast

- **Delia Harvell** (32) — The oldest. Practical, burdened, sharp-tongued. She inherited the farm and its debts. She remembers their mother best and has spent thirty years protecting her sisters from the truth.
- **Rose Harvell** (28) — The middle sister. A high school English teacher in Atlanta, who returns for the summer. She escaped the farm and feels guilty about it. She is engaged to a man who has never seen where she grew up.
- **Junie Harvell** (22) — The youngest. She never left. She ran the stand alongside their father and knows every customer by name. She has their mother's green thumb and their mother's silence.
- **The Customer** — A white-haired woman in a Buick who stops at the stand every Tuesday. She asks pointed questions about the girls' mother. She knows more than she lets on.

## 50-Chapter Plan

### Part One: The Debt

**Chapter 1: The Stand** — Junie opens the watermelon stand alone for the first time after the funeral. The sun is already brutal at seven in the morning.

**Chapter 2: The Sisters** — Delia drives back from the bank with the numbers. Rose arrives from Atlanta in a rented car. The three sisters stand in the kitchen and do not touch.

**Chapter 3: The Accounts** — Delia explains the debt. Their father took out a second mortgage. The farm will be sold by August unless they make five thousand dollars from the stand.

**Chapter 4: The Kitchen Table** — The sisters argue about what to do. Rose suggests selling. Junie walks out. Delia slams her hand on the table.

**Chapter 5: The First Customer** — The Customer pulls up in a green Buick. She buys three watermelons and asks Junie if she is "the youngest, the one who stayed."

**Chapter 6: The Price of Melons** — Rose takes over pricing. She realizes they have been underselling for years. She raises every price by a dollar. Junie is furious.

**Chapter 7: The Farm at Night** — Delia walks the property after dark. She stops at the old well where her mother's garden used to be. Something glints at the bottom.

**Chapter 8: The Letter** — Rose finds a letter addressed to their mother in a box of tax records. It is dated two weeks after their mother disappeared. She does not open it.

**Chapter 9: The Stand Rules** — Junie has a system. She teaches it to Rose. Every melon gets thumped. Every melon gets a name on the chalkboard. Rose learns the rhythm.

**Chapter 10: The Question** — The Customer returns. She asks Rose if she remembers the summer of 2004. Rose says she was five. The Customer says, "You remember more than you think."

### Part Two: The Reckoning

**Chapter 11: The Well** — Delia goes down into the well with a flashlight. She finds a rusted lockbox. The key is gone.

**Chapter 12: The Phone Call** — Rose calls their aunt in Macon. Her aunt asks if the letter has been found. Rose lies and says no.

**Chapter 13: The Heat Wave** — A week of hundred-degree days. The melons are ripening too fast. Junie works double shifts. The Customer brings lemonade.

**Chapter 14: The Engagement** — Rose's fiancé calls. He wants her to come back for a fitting. Rose tells him she cannot leave yet. He does not understand.

**Chapter 15: The Argument** — Delia and Rose fight about the letter. Delia knows about it. She has known for years. Rose demands to know what is inside.

**Chapter 16: The Confession** — Delia admits their mother did not just leave. She was running from something. Their father knew. He never told anyone.

**Chapter 17: The Lock** — Rose drives to the hardware store and buys bolt cutters. Junie follows her to the well. Together they break the lockbox open.

**Chapter 18: The Contents** — Inside the lockbox: a marriage certificate (not their parents'), a photograph of a woman who looks like their mother, and a key to a storage unit in Macon.

**Chapter 19: The Customer's Story** — The Customer returns and introduces herself: Evelyn March, their mother's best friend from high school. She tells them their mother was not from Georgia. She was from somewhere in the Carolinas. She was running when she arrived.

**Chapter 20: The Storage Unit** — The sisters drive to Macon. The storage unit contains boxes of books, a wedding dress, and a birth certificate for a girl named Lucy, born in 1976 — their mother, but under a different name.

### Part Three: The Truth

**Chapter 21: The Name** — Their mother's real name was Lucy Beck. She changed it to Lydia Harvell when she married their father. She was not a Harvell by blood. She was someone else entirely.

**Chapter 22: The Photograph** — Rose studies the photograph from the lockbox. It is their mother with a man who is not their father. The back reads "Savannah, 1998." That was the year before their mother disappeared.

**Chapter 23: The Cutting** — Junie overhears a customer at the stand. An old man says he remembers when "that Beck girl ran off from Charleston." Junie asks questions. The man clams up.

**Chapter 24: The Disappearance** — Evelyn March tells the sisters the full story. Their mother left Charleston after a scandal involving a married man. She came to Georgia, changed her name, met their father. She lived in fear for twenty years.

**Chapter 25: The Other Man** — The man in the photograph is identified by Evelyn as the married man from Charleston. He found their mother in 1998. He threatened to expose her.

**Chapter 26: The Bargain** — Their mother made a deal with the man. She paid him to disappear. She emptied the family savings. That is why there was no money. That is why she left — to protect her family from what she had done.

**Chapter 27: The Apology** — Delia writes a letter to their mother, knowing she will never send it. She apologizes for hating her. She understands now.

**Chapter 28: The Stand Thrives** — July Fourth weekend. The stand makes more money than it has in five years. Junie hires two boys from the high school to help.

**Chapter 29: The Call** — A woman named Margaret calls the farmhouse. She says she is their mother's sister. She has been looking for Lydia for thirty years.

**Chapter 30: The Reunion** — Margaret arrives from Charleston. She looks like their mother, twenty years older. She tells them their mother died in 2005, in a hospital in Richmond. She was using another name.

### Part Four: The Inheritance

**Chapter 31: The Grave** — The sisters drive to Richmond. They find their mother's grave under the name Mary Connor. The headstone reads "Beloved Mother." There are fresh flowers.

**Chapter 32: The Visitor** — Someone has been visiting the grave. The cemetery caretaker says a woman comes every June. She leaves sunflowers. Junie realizes it was Evelyn.

**Chapter 33: The Will** — Margaret produces their mother's will. She left the farm to the three girls. She had been sending money to their father secretly for years, but he never cashed the checks.

**Chapter 34: The Checks** — Delia finds the uncashed checks in their father's safe. Dozens of them, spanning twenty years. He never touched the money.

**Chapter 35: The Forgiveness** — Rose calls off the wedding. She realizes she has been running the same way her mother ran. She decides to stay in Georgia for the rest of the summer.

**Chapter 36: The Stand Expands** — Junie adds peaches to the stand. Then okra. Then tomatoes. The stand becomes a farm stand. The crossroads becomes a destination.

**Chapter 37: The Debt Paid** — Delia goes to the bank. She pays off the second mortgage. The farm is theirs. She sits in the parking lot and cries.

**Chapter 38: The Evening** — The sisters sit on the porch for the first time in years without fighting. Junie plays guitar. Rose reads aloud. Delia watches the sunset.

**Chapter 39: The Letter, Opened** — Rose finally opens the letter from Chapter 8. It is from their mother, written the day she left. It says: "I am not worthy of you. But I have always loved you. That is the only truth I have."

**Chapter 40: The Answer** — The sisters decide not to tell anyone the full story. Some secrets are not for sharing. They bury the lockbox in the garden.

### Part Five: The Harvest

**Chapter 41: The Last Tuesday** — Evelyn March visits the stand for the last time. She is moving to Florida. She gives Junie a cutting from their mother's favorite rose bush.

**Chapter 42: The Engagement Breaks** — Rose's fiancé ends things officially. She is relieved. She realizes she was going to marry him because it was safe, not because she loved him.

**Chapter 43: The New Sign** — Junie paints a new sign: "Harvell Sisters' Farm Stand." She adds their mother's name in small letters at the bottom.

**Chapter 44: The Music** — Junie plays their mother's guitar for the first time. She did not know how to play before, but her fingers find the chords. Rose says it sounds like rain.

**Chapter 45: The Garden** — Delia replants their mother's garden. She uses the rose cutting from Evelyn. She plants sunflowers. The earth smells like forgiveness.

**Chapter 46: The Rehearsal** — Rose starts writing again. She has not written since college. She fills a notebook with the story of the summer. She calls it The Watermelon Summer.

**Chapter 47: The Storm** — A summer thunderstorm nearly destroys the stand. The three sisters work through the night to save it. They do not speak. They do not need to.

**Chapter 48: The Morning After** — The stand survived. The watermelons are safe. The sisters sit on the tailgate of the truck and drink coffee. Junie laughs for the first time in months.

**Chapter 49: The Gathering** — Evelyn's last visit is a small ceremony. She places a sunflower at the base of the old oak tree. The sisters stand together.

**Chapter 50: The Last Slice** — The last watermelon of the summer. The sisters split it three ways. They eat it on the porch. August is ending. The farm is theirs. The secret is buried. The summer is over.
