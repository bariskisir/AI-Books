Chapter 1: The Stand

Junie unlocked the plywood shutters at 6:47 in the morning. The sun was already a white disc over the pecan grove. Heat rose from the dirt in waves.

She pulled the shutters down and stacked them against the fence. The stand was just a wooden platform with a corrugated tin roof. Her father had built it in 1998, the year before their mother left. He had used cedar planks and galvanized nails. Junie had watched him hammer every one.

The watermelons were stacked in pyramids, thirty-seven of them, the ones she and her father had picked the day before he died. She touched the largest one, a Crimson Sweet she had watched grow from a blossom to a twelve-pound fruit. It was warm from the night's heat.

She flipped the chalkboard sign to read "OPEN." Her father had painted the letters himself, blocky and uneven, the way a man who had never finished high school wrote anything. "Harvell's Melons," it said. "Taste the Summer."

The first car passed without stopping. Then a pickup truck. Then a sedan with a cracked windshield. Junie sat on the wooden stool behind the cash box and watched the road. The road had been dirt when she was a child. Now it was paved but nobody drove it unless they had to.

She thought about her father in the hospital. The machines. The way his hand had gone cold in hers. She had not cried at the funeral. She had stood between her sisters and watched the coffin lower and had felt nothing but a hollow ache where her heart used to be.

A dust cloud rose at the end of the road. A car was coming. Junie stood up and straightened her apron. She could hear her father's voice in her head. "Smile at them, Junie. A smile sells ten pounds of melon."

She smiled. The car got closer. It was a green Buick.

Chapter 2: The Sisters

Delia parked her truck next to the barn and sat for a moment with the engine running. The bank envelope sat on the passenger seat. She had not opened it yet. She knew what it said because she had been there when her father signed the papers.

She killed the engine. The farm went silent. Then the cicadas started.

She walked to the house. The screen door had a new tear in it. She made a mental note to fix it and immediately forgot. The kitchen smelled like coffee and dust. The ceiling fan wobbled.

Rose was already there. She had driven down from Atlanta the night before. She stood at the kitchen window with a mug in her hand, looking at the field. She wore city clothes. Linen pants. Sandals that cost more than Junie made in a week.

"Where's Junie?" Delia said.

"At the stand."

"Of course."

Rose turned around. She looked older than she had at Christmas. There were lines around her eyes Delia had not noticed before.

"The house looks smaller," Rose said.

"It's the same house."

"I know."

They stood in the kitchen and did not touch. They had never been huggers, not even when they were children. Their mother had been the toucher. After she left, they all learned to keep their hands to themselves.

Junie came in through the back door. Her apron was stained with dirt and melon juice. She looked at Delia. She looked at Rose. She did not say hello.

"The Customer came," Junie said.

"Who?" Rose said.

"The woman in the Buick. She comes on Tuesdays."

Delia put the bank envelope on the table. "We need to talk about the money."

Nobody touched the envelope. The ceiling fan wobbled. Somewhere in the field, a crow called.

Chapter 3: The Accounts

Delia opened the envelope and spread the papers across the kitchen table. Her father's handwriting was on the margins, small and neat, the handwriting of a man who had learned to write with a number two pencil in a one-room schoolhouse.

The debt was thirty-seven thousand dollars. The second mortgage was at nine percent interest. The farm had ninety days before the bank started proceedings.

"He never said anything," Rose said.

"He never said anything about anything," Delia said.

Junie picked up one of the papers. She held it like it might bite her. "This can't be right."

"It's right."

"But we made money last summer. I counted the cash."

"Cash he put into the tractor. The new irrigation. The roof on the barn." Delia's voice was flat, the voice she used when she was trying not to scream. "He was always fixing things. The bank was always waiting."

Rose sat down. She put her head in her hands. "Can we sell?"

"Sell what? The farm is the collateral. If we sell, we pay the bank and we have nothing."

"We have the stand," Junie said.

"The stand made twelve thousand dollars last year. We need five thousand more just to stop the bleeding."

Junie picked up a watermelon from the counter. She thumped it with her knuckle. It made a hollow sound. "Then we sell more watermelons."

"How?"

"I don't know yet. But that's what he would have done."

Nobody argued with that.

Chapter 4: The Kitchen Table

The argument started over a bowl of peaches.

Rose had brought them from Atlanta. They sat in a ceramic bowl on the table, perfectly ripe, the kind you bought at a farmers market from a man in a straw hat. Junie picked one up and put it down without eating it.

"We should sell them," Junie said.

"Sell what?" Rose said.

"The peaches. At the stand. People love peaches."

"We're a watermelon stand."

"Then we become a fruit stand. We add peaches. We add okra. We add whatever grows in that field." Junie's voice was rising. "We do something. We don't just sit here and let the bank take it."

Delia slammed her hand on the table. The peaches jumped. "You think I haven't thought of that? You think I want to lose this farm?"

"Then why are you talking about selling?"

"I'm not selling. I'm looking at the numbers."

"The numbers are wrong."

"The numbers are what they are."

Rose stepped between them. "Stop it. Both of you." She looked at Delia. "She's right about the peaches. And the okra. And the tomatoes. We need to expand. We need to make this work."

"And you're going to be here for that?"

Rose paused. "I don't know."

"I thought so."

Delia walked out of the kitchen. The screen door slammed behind her. The peaches sat in the bowl, untouched. Junie picked one up and bit into it. The juice ran down her chin. She did not wipe it off.

Chapter 5: The First Customer

The green Buick pulled up to the stand at 9:15 on Tuesday morning. The woman behind the wheel was old, maybe seventy, with white hair pulled back in a bun and glasses on a chain around her neck.

She got out slowly, using the door for support. She wore a floral dress and orthopedic shoes. She looked like everyone's grandmother.

"You must be Junie," she said.

"Yes, ma'am."

"I knew your daddy. He was a good man." She walked around the watermelons, tapping each one. "These are beautiful. He would be proud."

"Thank you, ma'am."

The woman stopped at a Sugar Baby, a small dark melon near the back. She lifted it with both hands and brought it to her nose. "This one smells like August."

"Yes, ma'am. That's a good one."

"I'll take it. And two of the Crimson Sweets."

Junie loaded them into the trunk of the Buick. The woman watched her work. When Junie was done, she held out a twenty-dollar bill.

"Are you the youngest?" the woman said.

"Yes, ma'am."

"The one who stayed."

"Yes, ma'am."

The woman looked at her for a long moment. Something passed between them, something Junie could not name. Then the woman smiled.

"I'll be back next Tuesday," she said. "Tell your sisters I said hello."

She drove away. Junie watched the Buick disappear down the road. She had not told the woman she had sisters.

Chapter 6: The Price of Melons

Rose took over pricing on Thursday. She spent the morning at the stand with a notebook and a pencil, calculating.

"We've been underselling by two dollars a melon," she said.

"We were selling what Daddy sold them for," Junie said.

"I know. But the market has changed. The supermarket in town sells melons for eight dollars. We're selling for four."

"Because people know us. Because they've been coming here for twenty years."

"Then they'll keep coming. But they'll pay six."

Junie crossed her arms. "Daddy would never do that."

"Daddy is dead."

The words hung in the air. Junie's face went pale. Rose wanted to take them back but she could not. She had said it. It was true.

"I'm sorry," Rose said.

"No, you're not."

"I am. But it's still true. And we need the money."

Rose repainted the prices on the chalkboard. Six dollars for a Sugar Baby. Eight for a Crimson Sweet. Ten for the Jubilee, the big striped ones her father had been most proud of.

The first customer of the day was Mr. Albright, who had been buying from the stand since before Junie was born. He looked at the new prices.

"Six dollars?" he said.

"Yes, sir."

He looked at Junie. She looked at the ground.

"Well," he said. "I reckon they're worth it."

He bought three.

Chapter 7: The Farm at Night

Delia could not sleep. The house was too quiet without their father's snoring. She got up at midnight and walked the property in her bare feet.

The moon was full. The watermelon field looked silver, the leaves shining like coins. She walked between the rows, feeling the sandy soil between her toes. Her father had walked these rows every night of his life. He said it was how he talked to God.

She stopped at the old well. It had been dry for years. Her mother had planted a garden around it, roses and sunflowers and things Delia could not name. After their mother left, the garden died. Her father never replanted it.

Something glinted at the bottom of the well.

Delia leaned over the stone edge. The moonlight caught something metal, maybe a bucket, maybe something else. She had never looked down the well before. She had been afraid of it as a child. Her mother told her a witch lived at the bottom.

She went back to the house and got a flashlight. The beam cut through the darkness. At the bottom of the well, fifty feet down, there was a rusted lockbox.

She stared at it for a long time. Then she went inside and did not tell her sisters.

Chapter 8: The Letter

Rose found it in a box of tax records in their father's closet. The box was marked "1995-2005" in their father's handwriting. Inside were receipts, cancelled checks, and an envelope.

The envelope was beige, the kind you bought at the drugstore. The stamp was from 2004. The address was written in a woman's handwriting, looping and elegant. It was addressed to their mother.

Rose held it in her hands. The paper was thin and soft, worn by years of being moved from box to box. Their father had kept it. He had never opened it.

She lifted the flap. It was not sealed. She could see the edge of the letter inside.

"Rose."

She jumped. Delia was standing in the doorway.

"What is that?" Delia said.

"Nothing. Old papers."

"Let me see."

Rose handed her the envelope. Delia looked at it. Her face did not change.

"I know about it," Delia said.

"You know what's inside?"

"I know it's from her."

"Then why haven't you opened it?"

Delia put the envelope in her pocket. "Because some doors, once you open them, you cannot close."

She walked away. Rose stood in the closet and watched her go.

Chapter 9: The Stand Rules

Junie had rules. She had learned them from her father, who had learned them from his father, who had hauled watermelons to Atlanta in the back of a mule-drawn wagon.

Rule one: Every melon gets thumped. You tap it with your knuckle. If it sounds hollow, it is ripe. If it sounds solid, leave it on the vine.

Rule two: Every melon gets a name on the chalkboard. Sugar Baby. Crimson Sweet. Jubilee. Charleston Gray. People want to know what they are buying. Give it a name and they will remember it.

Rule three: Never sell a melon you would not eat yourself.

Rose learned the rules. She stood beside Junie at the stand and thumped melons. She learned to tell the difference between a hollow sound and a solid one. She learned to stack them in pyramids that did not fall. She learned to weigh a melon in her hands and guess its poundage within a half-pound.

"You're good at this," Junie said.

"I'm a teacher. I'm good at learning things."

"You like it?"

Rose thought about it. The sun on her neck. The weight of the melons. The smell of dust and sweetness. "I think I do."

Junie nodded. She did not smile. But she did not frown, either. For Junie, that was as close to approval as she got.

They worked until the sun went down. They sold forty-seven watermelons. It was the best day they had ever had.

Chapter 10: The Question

The Customer came back on Thursday, not Tuesday. She pulled up in the green Buick at noon, when the heat was at its worst. Rose was running the stand alone.

"I was expecting Junie," the Customer said.

"She's at the house, making lunch. Can I help you?"

The Customer walked around the stand, examining the melons. She picked one up, put it down, picked up another. She was not really looking at the watermelons. She was looking at Rose.

"You're Rose," she said.

"Yes, ma'am."

"The teacher. The one who got out."

Rose felt the words like a slap. "I don't think of it that way."

"Don't you?" The Customer put down the melon. "I heard you went to Emory. You teach at a private school in Atlanta. You drive a nice car. You're engaged to a man your sisters have never met."

"How do you know all that?"

"I ask questions. That's what old women do."

Rose felt her face get hot. "Is there something I can help you with?"

The Customer looked at her directly. Her eyes were pale blue, almost colorless. "Do you remember the summer of 2004?"

"I was five years old."

"You remember more than you think." The Customer reached into her purse and pulled out a photograph. She held it up. "Do you know who this is?"

It was a picture of three girls on a dock. One of them was their mother. The other two Rose did not recognize.

"Ask your sisters," the Customer said. "And tell your sister Delia that Evelyn says hello."

She got back in her Buick and drove away.

Chapter 11: The Well

Delia went down into the well on Saturday morning. She tied a rope to the oak tree and lowered herself into the darkness. The walls were damp and cold. The smell was earth and iron.

She landed on a pile of rocks at the bottom. The lockbox was wedged between two stones. She pulled it free. It was heavier than she expected.

She climbed back up, hand over hand, her arms burning. Junie and Rose were waiting at the top. Rose had told them about Evelyn. Junie had told them about the photograph. Delia had told them about the well.

"Well?" Rose said.

Delia set the lockbox on the grass. It was the size of a shoebox, rusted at the corners, with a brass lock. The key was missing.

"It's been down there for years," Delia said. "I saw it the night of the funeral. I've been thinking about it ever since."

"What's inside?" Junie said.

"I don't know."

"Then how do we open it?"

"Bolt cutters," Rose said.

They looked at the box. They looked at each other. The morning sun was already hot. Somewhere in the field, a tractor backfired.

"I'll go to the hardware store," Rose said.

"No," Delia said. "We all go. Whatever is in there, we see it together."

Chapter 12: The Phone Call

Rose called their aunt in Macon that afternoon. She used the landline in the kitchen, the one that had been there since before she was born.

"Hello?" Aunt Carol's voice was thin and crackly.

"Aunt Carol, it's Rose."

"Well, hello, sweetheart. How are you girls holding up?"

"We're managing. Aunt Carol, I need to ask you something."

"Of course."

"Did you know there was a letter? From our mother? In Daddy's things?"

The pause was long enough to tell Rose everything.

"I thought maybe he threw it away," Aunt Carol said.

"He kept it. He never opened it."

"He was a good man. He deserved better."

"From who?"

Another pause. "From your mother."

"What do you know, Aunt Carol? Please. We're trying to understand."

Aunt Carol sighed. "Your mother was not from Georgia. I know that much. She showed up in Macon in 1983 with nothing but a suitcase and a story. She married your daddy six months later. She never talked about where she came from. And when she left, she left the way she came."

"Did you know where she went?"

"I had my suspicions. But I never asked. And your daddy never told."

Rose hung up the phone. She sat in the kitchen for a long time, watching the dust float in the afternoon light.

Chapter 13: The Heat Wave

The temperature hit a hundred and three on Tuesday. Then a hundred and four on Wednesday. Then a hundred and five on Thursday.

The melons ripened too fast. Junie woke up every morning to find ten-pound fruits that had been fist-sized the day before. They could not pick them fast enough. They could not sell them fast enough.

The Customer brought lemonade. She showed up at noon with a gallon glass jar full of ice and lemons and sugar.

"You'll die out here," she said.

"We're fine," Junie said.

"You're stubborn. Like your father." The Customer poured three glasses and set them on the counter. "Drink."

They drank. The lemonade was tart and cold. It was the best thing Junie had ever tasted.

"Sit down," the Customer said. "I want to tell you a story."

"We have customers," Rose said.

"You have no customers. It's too hot. They'll come back when the sun goes down. Sit."

They sat. The Customer pulled up a folding chair and settled herself under the tin roof.

"I met your mother in 1983," she said. "She was working at a diner in Macon. I was passing through. She served me coffee and we talked for three hours. I had never met anyone like her. She was running from something. I knew it the minute I saw her."

"What was she running from?" Junie said.

"I didn't ask. And she didn't tell. But I can tell you what I found out later."

The Customer leaned forward. Her pale blue eyes were steady.

"She was not who she said she was."

Chapter 14: The Engagement

Rose's fiancé called at nine o'clock on Friday night. She took the phone into the pantry to have some privacy.

"How are things going?" he said. His voice was cheerful, the voice of a man who had never had to worry about anything.

"Complicated."

"I thought you'd be back by now. The fitting is next weekend."

"I know. I'm sorry. I can't make it."

There was a pause. "What do you mean, you can't make it?"

"I mean I can't leave right now. My sisters need me. The farm needs me."

"Rose, it's a dress fitting. It's one afternoon."

"It's not just the fitting. It's everything. I don't know if I can come back."

The silence that followed was different. It was heavier.

"What are you saying?" he said.

"I don't know what I'm saying. I just know I can't leave right now."

"Do you want me to come down there?"

"No. I don't think you should."

"Why not?"

Because you've never seen where I grew up. Because you don't know who I am when I'm here. Because I'm not sure I know, either.

"Just give me some time," she said.

She hung up before he could answer. She stood in the dark pantry, surrounded by canned peaches and jars of honey, and tried to remember why she had said yes to him in the first place.

Chapter 15: The Argument

Delia found the letter in her pocket on Saturday morning. She had forgotten it was there. She pulled it out and looked at the envelope. The stamp was from 2004. The handwriting was their mother's.

She sat on the edge of her bed and held it. The paper was soft from age. The flap was loose. She could open it. She could finally know.

She did not open it.

She put it in her dresser drawer, under her father's watch and her grandmother's Bible. Then she walked into the kitchen, where her sisters were eating breakfast.

Rose looked up. "Did you open it?"

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because I'm not ready."

"Delia, she's our mother. We deserve to know."

"We deserve to know what? That she left us? That she didn't love us enough to stay?"

"She did love us. That's why she left."

"How do you know that?"

"I don't. But I feel it."

Delia's face hardened. "You feel it. That's wonderful. You've been gone for ten years. You show up for two weeks and suddenly you know everything about our mother."

"At least I'm trying to know. You've been here the whole time and you've never asked a single question."

"Because I didn't want to know the answers."

The kitchen went quiet. Junie put down her spoon.

"I want to know," she said softly. "I want to know everything."

Chapter 16: The Confession

They sat on the front porch. The sun was going down, painting the field orange and red. Delia had a glass of sweet tea in her hands. She had not touched it.

"Her name was not Lydia," Delia said.

"What?" Rose said.

"Her real name. It was Lucy. Lucy Beck."

Rose put down her glass. Junie leaned forward.

"She was from Charleston," Delia continued. "She came to Georgia in 1983. She was running from something. Daddy knew. He knew her real name. He knew everything. He never told anyone."

"How do you know this?" Rose said.

"Because she told me. The night before she left."

Junie's face went pale. "She told you? You knew she was leaving?"

"I didn't know she was leaving. I thought she was just talking. She told me about Charleston. She told me she had a different name once. She told me she had done things she was not proud of. I was twelve years old. I did not understand."

"What did she do?" Rose said.

"I don't know. She did not tell me that. She just said she had to go away. She said it was the only way to protect us."

"And you never told anyone?"

"Who was I going to tell? Daddy was a wreck. You were five. Junie was not even born."

The crickets started singing. The sky turned purple. The three sisters sat on the porch and did not speak.

Chapter 17: The Lock

Rose drove to the hardware store on Monday morning. She bought the biggest bolt cutters they had. The man at the counter did not ask questions. He just rang her up and said, "Good luck with whatever you're cutting."

She drove back to the farm. Junie was waiting by the well. Delia came out of the house with the lockbox.

They gathered in the backyard, under the oak tree. Rose positioned the bolt cutters around the lock. The blades were thick and heavy. She squeezed.

The lock snapped.

They stood there for a moment. Nobody reached for the box.

"Well," Delia said. "Open it."

"You open it," Rose said. "You found it."

"It was all of us."

Junie stepped forward. She lifted the lid of the lockbox. Inside, there was a photograph, a document, and a key.

Junie picked up the photograph. It was a woman who looked like their mother, but younger, maybe twenty years old. She was standing next to a man in a suit. The back read "Savannah, 1998."

"I don't understand," Junie said. "She left in 1999. This was taken the year before."

"She was in Savannah," Rose said. "She was with someone."

Delia picked up the document. It was a marriage certificate, but not their parents'. The names were Lucy Beck and a man named Thomas Greer. The date was 1998.

"Who is Thomas Greer?" Junie said.

Nobody answered.

Chapter 18: The Contents

The key in the lockbox was for a storage unit. The address was printed on a tag attached to the keychain: "Macon Mini-Storage, Unit 47."

"She had a storage unit," Rose said. "In Macon. For twenty years."

"Or she got it in 1998," Delia said. "When she married Thomas Greer."

"Married? She was already married to Daddy."

"Not legally."

Rose sat down on the grass. The world felt unsteady. "You're saying our parents were not legally married?"

"I'm saying I don't know what I'm saying. I'm saying there is a lot we do not know."

Junie was still looking at the photograph. The man in the suit was handsome, with dark hair and a sharp jaw. Their mother looked happy. She was smiling in a way they had never seen her smile.

"She looks different," Junie said.

"Who?" Rose said.

"Mom. She looks... free."

They passed the photograph around. Each of them looked at it for a long time. Their mother was beautiful. Their mother was a stranger.

"We need to go to Macon," Rose said.

"Tomorrow," Delia said.

"No. Today."

"It's four in the afternoon. It's a two-hour drive."

"So we get there at six. The storage place closes at eight."

Delia looked at her sisters. Junie was already standing up. Rose had her car keys in her hand.

"Fine," Delia said. "But we go together."

Chapter 19: The Customer's Story

Evelyn March was waiting for them when they came back from Macon. She was sitting on the front porch in a rocking chair, as if she had lived there her whole life.

"The storage unit," she said.

"You knew," Rose said.

"I knew some. Not everything."

They sat on the porch. Evelyn told them the story. She spoke slowly, the way old people speak when they have had a long time to think.

"Your mother's family was from Charleston. Old money. The kind of money that people in Charleston have had for two hundred years. She grew up in a house on the Battery. She went to debutante balls. She was supposed to marry a boy from a good family."

"But she didn't," Junie said.

"She met someone else. A man named Thomas Greer. He was not from a good family. He was a mechanic. Her family did not approve. They told her to end it."

"And she ran away," Rose said.

"Not at first. She tried to do what they wanted. She broke it off. She married the boy from the good family. But she was not happy."

"What happened?"

"Thomas came back. He found her. They started meeting in secret. Her husband found out. There was a scandal. The family disowned her. She came to Georgia to start over."

"And Thomas?"

"He stayed in Charleston. He married someone else. But he never forgot her. And she never forgot him."

Evelyn paused. "He found her again in 1998. He was dying. He wanted to see her one last time. She went to Savannah to say goodbye."

The photograph. The marriage certificate. It all made sense now.

Chapter 20: The Storage Unit

The storage unit was in a row of identical metal garages on the outskirts of Macon. Unit 47 was near the back. The door was rusted but the lock was new.

Rose used the key. It turned smoothly.

They lifted the door. Light fell on a room full of boxes. The air smelled like mothballs and old paper.

They stepped inside. There were boxes of books. A wedding dress in a plastic bag. Furniture covered in sheets. A trunk. A lamp. A life, packed away.

Junie opened a box of books. They were novels, mostly, romance and mystery, the kind you read on the beach. There was a bookmark in one of them, a pressed flower between the pages.

Rose opened the trunk. Inside were photo albums. Baby clothes. A christening gown. A pair of tiny shoes.

"These are ours," she said. "She kept our things."

Delia opened a box of papers. There were letters. Dozens of letters, tied with a ribbon. They were addressed to Thomas Greer, never sent.

"She wrote him every year," Delia said. "She never mailed them."

They stood among the boxes. They did not know what to say.

"She was going to come back," Junie said. "She was waiting. She was going to come back and get us."

Nobody corrected her. Nobody wanted to.

Chapter 21: The Name

They took everything from the storage unit. They loaded it into the back of Delia's truck and drove home in silence. The sun was setting. The sky was orange and pink and purple.

At the farm, they carried the boxes into the living room. They spread them across the floor. They opened every one.

The birth certificate was in a manila envelope at the bottom of the trunk. It was for a girl named Lucy Beck, born in 1976 in Charleston, South Carolina. Mother: Margaret Beck. Father: unknown.

"Lucy Beck," Rose said. "Her name was Lucy Beck."

"Then who was Lydia Harvell?" Junie said.

"Someone she made up."

Delia held the birth certificate. The paper was yellowed and thin. "She was someone before she was our mother. She had a whole life we never knew about."

"It's like she was a different person," Junie said.

"She was," Rose said. "She had to be. She had to disappear to survive."

The room was quiet. The boxes surrounded them. Their mother's life was scattered across the floor.

"She loved us," Junie said. "She loved us and she left. Both things are true."

Delia started to cry. It was the first time her sisters had seen her cry since their father's funeral. She cried without making a sound, the way a woman cries when she has been holding it in for thirty years.

Rose put her arm around her. Junie took her hand. They sat on the floor among the boxes and held each other.

Chapter 22: The Photograph

Rose studied the photograph from the lockbox. Their mother and Thomas Greer in Savannah, 1998. She looked at their faces, the way they leaned toward each other. She looked at their hands, not quite touching.

"He loved her," Rose said.

"Who?" Junie said.

"Thomas. You can see it in his face."

Junie looked at the photograph. "She loved him too."

"Yes."

"Then why didn't she stay with him?"

"Because he was dying. And she had us. And she had Daddy. She had too many people to take care of."

Rose turned the photograph over. There was writing on the back, small and neat.

"My dearest Lucy. You were the best thing in my life. I will love you until I cannot remember my own name. — Thomas."

"That's not from 1998," Delia said. "That's from later. He wrote that after she left him."

"How do you know?"

"Because he says 'until I cannot remember my own name.' He knew he was going to forget. He knew he was going to die."

Rose put the photograph down. "She had two great loves. Our father was one of them."

"And Thomas was the other," Junie said.

Delia picked up the photograph. She looked at it for a long time. "I think our mother had a bigger heart than we ever knew."

"She had secrets," Rose said. "A big heart and secrets. That's what she left us."

Chapter 23: The Cutting

Junie was at the stand on Wednesday when an old man pulled up in a battered Ford pickup. He was thin and stooped, with sun-browned skin and hands that looked like they had been carved from wood.

"You're Jesse Harvell's girl," he said.

"Yes, sir."

"I knew your daddy. We played checkers at the feed store. I'm sorry for your loss."

"Thank you, sir."

The man looked at the watermelons. He picked one up, thumped it, put it down. He picked up another. He seemed to be in no hurry.

"I knew your mama too," he said.

Junie's heart stopped. "You did?"

"I knew her before she was your mama. Before she was Lydia. She was Lucy Beck. I was in Charleston in the seventies. I knew her family."

"What did you know?"

The man put down the watermelon. He looked at Junie with eyes that had seen too much.

"There was a scandal," he said. "About a man. About a baby. I don't know the details. But she left in a hurry. She changed her name. She never went back."

"What baby?"

The man shook his head. "I don't know, sweetheart. I'm sorry. I just remember the talk. People in Charleston love to talk."

He bought a watermelon and drove away. Junie stood at the stand, her hands shaking. A baby. Their mother had a baby. A baby that was not them.

Chapter 24: The Disappearance

Evelyn March came to dinner that night. Delia had invited her. She had made a pot roast and vegetables from the garden.

They ate at the kitchen table. The same table where they had argued about peaches. The same table where their father had spread out the bank papers. The same table where they had learned the truth.

Evelyn told them everything.

"Your mother was seventeen when she met Thomas Greer. He was twenty-two. He worked at a garage. Her family did not approve. They forbade her from seeing him."

"But she saw him anyway," Rose said.

"She did. They were in love. Real love. The kind that makes you do stupid things."

"They had a baby," Junie said.

Evelyn put down her fork. "Who told you that?"

"An old man. At the stand."

Evelyn was quiet for a moment. Then she nodded.

"She had a baby. A little girl. She was born in 1975. She died of pneumonia when she was six months old."

The room went cold.

"Your mother never got over it. She blamed herself. She thought it was her fault for living in a drafty apartment. She thought God was punishing her for loving a man her family did not approve of."

"She left because of the baby?" Delia said.

"She left because of everything. The baby. The scandal. The family. She could not stay in Charleston. Every street corner reminded her of what she had lost."

Evelyn reached across the table and took Delia's hand. "She loved you girls more than anything. You were her second chance. You were her redemption."

Chapter 25: The Other Man

The man in the photograph was not Thomas Greer.

They realized it when they looked at the date again. 1998. Thomas Greer was in Charleston in 1998. Their mother was in Savannah. The man in the photograph was someone else.

"The customer at the stand," Junie said. "He said Thomas Greer died in 1996."

They had assumed. They had been wrong.

Evelyn looked at the photograph again. Her face went pale.

"Oh," she said.

"Who is it?" Rose said.

"I don't know. I've never seen him before."

"But you know something."

Evelyn nodded slowly. "Your mother was seeing someone in 1998. I knew that. I did not know it was someone new. I thought it was still Thomas."

"Who was he?"

"She never told me his name. She just said he was a complication. She said he was dangerous."

"Dangerous how?"

"She would not say. She just said she had made a mistake. She said she needed to fix it."

The photograph sat on the table. A man who was not their father, not Thomas Greer. A man with kind eyes and a hard mouth. A man who looked like he could be anyone.

"1998," Delia said. "That was the year before she left."

"Yes."

"So this man. Whatever he was. It was why she left."

Evelyn did not answer. She did not have to.

Chapter 26: The Bargain

The checks were in their father's safe. Delia found them the next morning, tucked behind the insurance papers.

They were cashier's checks, made out to their father. They were from their mother. They were dated every year for twenty years.

The first one was for five hundred dollars. The last one was for five thousand. They were all signed "Lucy Beck."

"She was sending him money," Rose said.

"He never cashed them."

"Why?"

Delia held up the stack of checks. They were uncashed, unstamped, unsigned. Her father had hidden them away like secrets.

"He was still in love with her," Junie said. "He could not take her money. It would have meant she was really gone."

"He knew she was sending it," Rose said. "He knew she was out there somewhere. As long as he did not cash the checks, she was still coming back."

Delia put the checks on the table. Twenty years of guilt. Twenty years of love. Twenty years of a man who could not let go.

"She was protecting us," Delia said. "She paid someone. She made a deal. She left to keep us safe."

"Who did she pay?" Junie said.

"The man in the photograph. The complication. Whoever he was."

"How much?"

"I don't know. But it was everything she had. It was the price of our safety."

Chapter 27: The Apology

Delia sat at the kitchen table with a pen and a piece of paper. She had not written a letter since high school. She did not know how to start.

Dear Mom,

She crossed it out.

Dearest Lucy,

She crossed that out too.

She put down the pen. The paper was blank. The words would not come.

She thought about her mother's hands. The way she had braided hair. The way she had hummed while she cooked. The way she had tucked Delia into bed and kissed her forehead.

She thought about the morning her mother left. The empty closet. The note on the kitchen table.

"Take care of your sisters."

She had been twelve years old. She had taken the note and put it in her pocket. She had made breakfast. She had gotten her sisters ready for school. She had never told anyone about the note.

She picked up the pen again.

I understand now. I do not forgive you. But I understand.

She folded the paper and put it in the drawer with the letter. She did not sign it. She did not need to.

She walked outside. The sun was setting. The watermelons were ripening. The farm was still there. Her sisters were still there.

It was enough.

Chapter 28: The Stand Thrives

July Fourth weekend was the busiest they had ever been. The stand sold out by noon on Saturday. Junie had to call the farm down the road and buy their entire stock.

Rose managed the money. She had a cash box and a notebook and a system. Every sale got recorded. Every dollar got counted. By Sunday night, they had made three thousand dollars.

"We're going to make it," Rose said.

"We're not there yet," Delia said.

"But we're closer than we were."

Delia looked at the numbers. Rose was right. At this rate, they would have the debt paid by August.

They hired two boys from the high school. Marcus and Leroy, seventeen years old, strong backs and quick smiles. They loaded melons into cars and swept the platform and carried boxes from the field.

The stand expanded. Junie added peaches from the tree in the backyard. She added tomatoes from the garden. She added okra and squash and cucumbers.

People came from town. People came from the county over. They came for the watermelons and stayed for the tomatoes. They bought peaches and told Junie they tasted like childhood.

"My daddy would be proud," Junie said.

"He is," Rose said. "Wherever he is. He is."

Junie smiled. It was the first real smile Rose had seen on her face since the funeral.

Chapter 29: The Call

The phone rang on July seventh. Delia answered it.

"Is this the Harvell residence?"

"Yes."

"My name is Margaret Beck. I am looking for my daughter. Lucy Beck. I believe she lives there."

The world stopped.

"Who is this?" Delia said.

"Margaret Beck. Lucy's mother. Your grandmother."

Delia sat down. "Our mother is dead."

The silence on the other end of the line was absolute.

"When?" Margaret said. Her voice was smaller now.

"2005. In Richmond. Under a different name."

"I see."

"She had a different name. She had a different life. She did not tell us about you."

"She would not have." Margaret's voice was steady, but Delia could hear the cracks. "She was ashamed. She thought I would not understand."

"Would you have?"

"I would have tried."

Delia looked at the phone. Her grandmother. Her mother's mother. Alive all this time.

"Where are you?" Delia said.

"Charleston. I have been in Charleston for seventy years."

"You should come here. You should see where she lived."

Another silence. Then: "I would like that."

Chapter 30: The Reunion

Margaret Beck arrived on July tenth. She drove a blue sedan with South Carolina plates. She was tall and straight-backed, with white hair and eyes that matched her daughter's exactly.

The sisters met her on the porch. Margaret looked at the farm. She looked at the stand. She looked at the three girls who had her daughter's blood.

"You look like her," Margaret said. "All three of you."

Junie stepped forward. "Was she happy here?"

Margaret considered the question. "I do not know. I was not here. I was not allowed to be."

"Why not?"

"Because I made a mistake. I chose my reputation over my daughter. I have regretted it every day of my life."

She walked into the house. She touched the kitchen counter. She looked at the photographs on the wall.

"She was happy here," Margaret said. "I can see it. She had a garden. She had a family. She had love."

"She had secrets," Rose said.

"We all have secrets. The question is what we do with them."

Margaret sat down at the kitchen table. She looked old. She looked tired. She looked like someone who had been carrying a weight for a very long time.

"Tell me everything," she said.

And they did.

Chapter 31: The Grave

The grave was in a cemetery in Richmond, Virginia. The headstone read "Mary Connor, 1956-2005, Beloved Mother."

"Mary Connor," Rose said. "She even changed her name when she died."

"She was running until the end," Delia said.

They stood in front of the grave. The grass was green. The sun was warm. There were fresh sunflowers on the stone.

"Evelyn," Junie said. "She comes every June."

"She kept coming," Rose said. "Even after our mother died. She kept coming."

Delia knelt down. She touched the headstone. The stone was cold. The letters were carved deep.

"I don't know who you were," she said. "I don't know what made you leave. But I know you loved us. I know that was real."

She placed a watermelon seed on the grave. It was from the first melon of the summer. She had saved it for this.

"I hope you found peace," she said. "Wherever you are."

They stood in silence. The wind blew through the trees. Somewhere, a bird was singing. It sounded like their mother's hum.

Chapter 32: The Visitor

The cemetery caretaker was an old man named Raymond. He had been working at the cemetery for forty years.

"The woman in the Buick," he said. "She comes every June. She leaves sunflowers. She sits here for an hour and talks."

"What does she say?" Junie said.

"I don't listen. That's between her and the dead."

"But you've heard some things."

Raymond shrugged. "I hear things. I do not repeat them."

"What did you hear?"

He looked at Junie. His eyes were kind.

"She said 'I'm sorry.' She said it over and over. She said 'I should have told them.'"

"Told us what?"

"I don't know, sweetheart. But she said it like it mattered."

Junie walked back to the grave. Rose and Delia were waiting. She told them what Raymond had said.

"Evelyn knows more than she's telling us," Rose said.

"She's been carrying it for twenty years," Delia said. "Whatever it is."

"We need to ask her."

"She'll tell us when she's ready."

"Will she?"

Delia looked at the grave. She looked at the sunflowers. "Yes. I think she will."

Chapter 33: The Will

Margaret produced their mother's will on July twelfth. She pulled it out of her purse, folded and worn, as if she had been carrying it for years.

"Your mother came to me in 2003," Margaret said. "She asked me to keep this. She said she had a will. She said she wanted to make sure the farm stayed in the family."

"She came to you?" Rose said.

"She drove to Charleston. She sat in my living room. We had not spoken in twenty years. She was sick. She knew she did not have long."

"Why did she come to you?"

"Because she trusted me. Despite everything. She knew I would keep her secret."

The will was simple. It left the farm to Delia, Rose, and Junie equally. It left the contents of the storage unit to them as well. It was signed "Lucy Beck" and dated 2003.

"She made this before she went to Richmond," Delia said.

"She knew she was going to die. She wanted to make sure you were taken care of."

"Did she know Daddy would need the money?"

"She knew. She sent him checks. He never cashed them."

"Why not?"

Margaret smiled. "Because he was a proud man. And because he loved her. He could not accept that she was really gone."

The will sat on the table. It was the last thing their mother had written. It was the only thing she had left them.

Chapter 34: The Checks

Delia spread the uncashed checks across the kitchen table. There were twenty of them, one for every year since their mother left. The amounts increased slowly: five hundred, seven hundred, a thousand, two thousand.

"She was trying to buy her way back," Rose said.

"Or buy her way out of guilt," Delia said.

"Both," Junie said.

They looked at the checks. The signatures. The dates. Their mother's handwriting was steady. It did not waver.

"She signed every one," Rose said. "She put them in the mail every year. She never stopped trying."

"Daddy never stopped refusing."

"He was angry."

"He was heartbroken."

Junie picked up the last check. It was for five thousand dollars. It was dated 2005, the year their mother died.

"She sent this the year she died," Junie said. "She was in the hospital. She was dying. And she still sent a check."

"What did she think? That he would cash it?"

"I don't think she cared. I think she just wanted him to know she was still thinking about him."

The kitchen was quiet. The checks sat on the table, a bridge between two people who loved each other but could not be together.

"She loved him," Rose said. "She loved him and she left him. Both things are true."

"Like she loved us," Junie said.

"Like she loved us."

Chapter 35: The Forgiveness

Rose called her fiancé on July fifteenth. She sat on the front porch with the phone in her hand and watched the sun go down.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

"I've been thinking."

"Me too."

"I don't think you're coming back."

She wanted to lie. She wanted to say she would be there next week. But she could not.

"I don't think I am," she said.

"Can I ask why?"

She thought about the farm. The watermelons. Her sisters. The truth about her mother. The weight that had lifted off her shoulders.

"Because I've been running," she said. "My whole life. I've been running from this place. From my family. From who I am."

"And now you want to stop running."

"Yes."

There was a long pause. She could hear him breathing.

"I understand," he said. "I don't like it. But I understand."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be. You deserve to be happy."

She hung up. She sat on the porch and watched the stars come out. She had been engaged for eight months. She had been planning a wedding she did not want. She had been living a life that was not hers.

She was free.

Chapter 36: The Stand Expands

The stand became a farm stand. Junie added everything that grew in the field. Peaches. Tomatoes. Okra. Squash. Cucumbers. Peppers. Beans.

She learned to arrange them in baskets. She learned to make signs with chalk. She learned to talk to customers about how to pick the best tomatoes and when the peaches would be ripe.

Rose handled the books. She set up a system with a spreadsheet and a cash box. She tracked every sale. Every expense. Every profit.

"At this rate, we'll have the debt paid by the end of July," she said.

"And then what?" Junie said.

"Then we start saving. For next year. For the year after that."

"We're going to keep doing this?"

"Yes. If you want to."

Junie looked at the stand. The chalkboard. The watermelons. The baskets of tomatoes. The customers lining up.

"I want to," she said.

Delia joined them in the afternoons after she finished her chores. She learned to thump watermelons. She learned to weigh them. She learned to make change.

"Taste the Summer," she said, reading the sign.

"That's what it says," Junie said.

"Taste the Summer." Delia smiled. "I like that."

The stand was busy from morning until night. The sisters worked without stopping. They were tired. They were happy.

Chapter 37: The Debt Paid

Delia went to the bank on July twenty-fifth. She wore her good dress. The one she had worn to her father's funeral.

She walked up to the teller. She asked for the loan officer. She waited.

Mr. Patterson came out. He was a tall man with gray hair and a soft handshake. He had known her father.

"Delia," he said. "How can I help you?"

She put the envelope on his desk. "I want to pay off the second mortgage."

He opened the envelope. He counted the money. He looked at her.

"Where did you get this?"

"The watermelon stand."

He raised his eyebrows. "The watermelon stand?"

"Yes, sir."

He counted again. It was all there. Thirty-seven thousand dollars, cashier's checks and cash.

"I'll get the paperwork," he said.

She sat in his office while he typed. The computer hummed. The air conditioning was cold. She looked out the window at the parking lot.

"Done," Mr. Patterson said. "Congratulations. The farm is yours."

She nodded. She stood up. She shook his hand.

"Thank you."

"Your daddy would be proud."

She walked out of the bank. She got in her truck. She sat in the parking lot and cried. She cried for her father. She cried for her mother. She cried for the summer that had changed everything.

Then she drove home.

Chapter 38: The Evening

They sat on the porch that night. The air was warm and thick. The crickets were singing. The stars were coming out.

Junie had her guitar. She had found it in their mother's storage unit. It was an old Martin, worn and scratched, with strings that were rusted.

"I don't know how to play," she said.

"Nobody knows how to play until they play," Rose said.

Junie strummed. The notes were rough and wrong. She adjusted the tuning pegs. She tried again.

It was not music. But it was something.

Rose read aloud from a book she had found in the storage unit. It was a collection of poems by Mary Oliver. Their mother had underlined passages in pencil.

"Tell me, what is it you plan to do with your one wild and precious life?" Rose read.

Nobody answered. But they were all thinking.

Delia watched the sunset. The sky was purple and orange and pink. The fields were green. The watermelons were dark shapes in the fading light.

"This is good," Delia said.

"What?" Junie said.

"Right now. This."

Junie strummed the guitar. Rose turned a page. Delia watched the stars.

The screen door creaked. The wind chimes tinkled. Somewhere, an owl called.

It was the first time they had been at peace in years.

Chapter 39: The Letter, Opened

Rose opened the letter on July twenty-eighth. She did it alone, in her room, with the door closed.

The envelope was beige. The stamp was from 2004. The handwriting was their mother's.

She slid the letter out. The paper was thin and worn. She unfolded it.

"My dearest girls," it began.

Rose's hand started shaking.

"If you are reading this, I am gone. I am sorry. I am sorry for everything. I am sorry I could not be the mother you deserved. I am sorry I could not stay.

I left to protect you. There are things I have done that I cannot undo. There are people who would hurt you to get to me. I could not let that happen.

I have loved you every day of my life. I have watched you from a distance. I have seen Delia grow into a strong woman. I have seen Rose become the teacher she always wanted to be. I have seen Junie, my baby, who I held for only six months before I left.

I am not worthy of you. But I have always loved you. That is the only truth I have.

Do not look for me. Do not try to find me. Remember me as the mother who loved you. Not the woman who left.

I will love you until the end of time.

Mom"

Rose folded the letter. She put it back in the envelope. She put it in her pocket.

She walked downstairs. Her sisters were in the kitchen.

"She loved us," Rose said.

"We know," Delia said.

"But now we know. For sure."

They sat at the kitchen table. The letter was between them. It was the last thing their mother had written. It was the only thing that mattered.

Chapter 40: The Answer

The sisters made a decision on July twenty-ninth. They would not tell anyone the full story. Some secrets were not for sharing.

They took the lockbox. They took the photographs. They took the marriage certificate and the letters and the checks. They buried everything in the garden where their mother's roses used to grow.

"She wanted us to know," Junie said. "But she did not want the world to know."

"She wanted us to understand," Rose said. "Not to tell."

Delia patted down the dirt. The box was buried deep. It would not be found.

"We know," Delia said. "That is enough."

They planted sunflowers on top. The seeds went into the ground. The truth went with them.

"Every summer, they will bloom," Junie said. "Every summer, we will remember."

They stood in a circle. The sun was setting. The sky was gold.

"She was Lucy," Delia said. "She was Lydia. She was Mary. She was a lot of people."

"She was our mother," Rose said.

"That is the only name that matters."

They walked back to the house. The sunflowers were already pushing through the soil. The summer was not over. But the secrets were.

Chapter 41: The Last Tuesday

Evelyn March came to the stand for the last time on the first Tuesday of August. She pulled up in her green Buick. She walked slowly, using her cane.

"Moving to Florida," she said. "My daughter lives there. She says I am too old to live alone."

"You're not old," Junie said.

"I am very old. And very tired."

They sat at the stand. The afternoon was quiet. The customers had stopped coming for a few hours.

"I should have told you sooner," Evelyn said. "About your mother. About everything. I was trying to protect her memory."

"You were being a friend," Rose said.

"I was being a coward."

"Maybe. But you were also being loyal."

Evelyn nodded. She reached into her purse and pulled out a cutting. It was a small branch with green leaves and a single bud.

"Your mother's favorite rose. She planted it the year you were born, Delia. It has survived every drought. Every freeze. Every storm."

She handed it to Junie. "Plant it in her garden. It will bloom forever."

Junie took the cutting. Her hands were shaking.

"Thank you," she said.

"No. Thank you." Evelyn stood up. "Your mother was the best friend I ever had. And you girls are the best part of her legacy."

She drove away. The green Buick disappeared down the road.

Junie held the rose cutting. She watched the dust settle. She did not cry.

Chapter 42: The Engagement Breaks

Rose's fiancé called on August third. She was at the stand, weighing tomatoes for a customer.

"I'm calling to make it official," he said.

"Make what official?"

"We're done. I've met someone else."

Rose put down the tomatoes. "Oh."

"I'm sorry. I didn't want to tell you over the phone. But I figured you deserved to know."

"You're right. I did deserve to know."

"I hope you find what you're looking for."

"I think I already have."

She hung up. She stood at the stand. The customer was waiting.

"Sorry about that," Rose said.

The customer was an old woman with kind eyes. She looked at Rose's face and saw something there.

"Was that bad news?" the woman said.

"No. It was good news. It just takes a minute to recognize."

She finished weighing the tomatoes. She took the woman's money. She made change.

When the woman drove away, Rose sat down on the stool. She felt light. She felt free. She felt like she could breathe for the first time in eight months.

She was not engaged anymore. She was not going to marry a man she did not love. She was going to stay here, on this farm, with her sisters.

She was home.

Chapter 43: The New Sign

Junie painted a new sign on August fifth. She used the same board her father had used. She sanded it down and primed it and painted it white.

"HARVELL SISTERS' FARM STAND," she wrote in block letters. Underneath, in smaller letters: "Lucy's Garden."

Rose watched her work. "Lucy's Garden?"

"It's for her. So people remember."

"Nobody knows who Lucy is."

"We know. That's enough."

Delia came out to look. She read the sign. She nodded.

"It's good," she said.

"It needs something else," Junie said.

She picked up a brush. She painted a small watermelon in the corner. Then a sunflower. Then a peach.

"Taste the Summer," she wrote at the bottom.

She stood back. The sign was perfect.

They hung it on the front of the stand. The old sign came down. Junie carried it to the barn and leaned it against the wall.

"Goodbye, Daddy," she said.

She walked back to the stand. The new sign was bright and clean. The watermelons were stacked. The customers were coming.

The summer was not over. But everything was different now.

Chapter 44: The Music

Junie played their mother's guitar for the first time on August seventh. She sat on the porch in the evening. The strings were new. She had replaced them herself.

She did not know how to play. But her fingers found chords. She had watched her mother play when she was young, too young to remember. But her hands remembered.

"It sounds like rain," Rose said.

"It sounds like Mom," Delia said.

Junie kept playing. The notes were simple. A G chord. A C chord. A D. She did not know the names. She just knew where her fingers went.

"A song," she said. "She used to play a song."

"What song?"

"I don't know. But I remember it."

She closed her eyes. She let her fingers move. The melody came out, halting and rough.

"She played that at night," Delia said. "When she thought we were asleep."

"I remember," Rose said.

They sat on the porch and listened. The music floated into the darkness. The crickets stopped singing to hear it.

When Junie finished, nobody spoke. The last note hung in the air.

"I think she was trying to tell us something," Junie said.

"What?"

"I don't know. But I think it was important."

She put the guitar down. The porch was quiet. The stars were bright.

It was the best music the farm had ever heard.

Chapter 45: The Garden

Delia replanted their mother's garden on August ninth. She used the rose cutting from Evelyn. She planted sunflowers. She planted zinnias. She planted the seeds from the watermelons they had sold.

The soil was dark and rich. She dug with her hands. She pushed the seeds into the earth. She covered them. She watered them.

"What are you doing?" Rose asked.

"Planting."

"I can see that. But why?"

"Because she was a gardener. Because the garden died when she left. Because I want it to live again."

Rose knelt beside her. She took a handful of seeds.

"What are these?"

"Sunflowers. They grow fast. They grow tall. They turn their faces to the sun."

"Like we did," Rose said. "When she left. We turned our faces to the sun and kept growing."

Delia looked at her sister. "When did you get so wise?"

"I've always been wise. I just didn't have a reason to show it."

They planted the rest of the seeds together. Junie joined them after she closed the stand. They worked until the sun went down.

The garden was small. But it was a start.

"It will be beautiful," Delia said. "Next summer. It will be full of flowers."

"Next summer," Junie said. "I like the sound of that."

Chapter 46: The Rehearsal

Rose started writing on August eleventh. She had not written since college. She had been an English major. She had wanted to be a writer. Then she got a teaching job and stopped.

She sat on the porch with a notebook. The same notebook she had bought for her students. She opened it to the first page.

She wrote about the stand. She wrote about the watermelons. She wrote about her mother. She wrote about the summer.

The words came easily. They had been waiting for years. They had been building up behind her ribs.

She wrote for three hours. When she stopped, she had twenty pages.

"What is that?" Junie asked.

"A story. About this summer."

"Are you in it?"

"Yes."

"Am I in it?"

"Yes."

"Am I nice?"

Rose smiled. "You are your mother's daughter."

"Is that a yes?"

"It is the nicest thing I could say."

Junie sat down next to her. She looked at the notebook.

"What will you do with it?"

"I don't know. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything."

"Read me some."

Rose looked at her sister. She looked at the pages. She started reading.

The words sounded different out loud. They sounded true.

She kept reading until the sun went down.

Chapter 47: The Storm

The storm came on August thirteenth. It came without warning. The sky turned green. The wind picked up. The rain came sideways.

The sisters ran to the stand. The watermelons were still stacked. The sign was swinging. The canvas awning was tearing.

"Grab the melons!" Junie shouted.

They worked through the rain. Delia carried armloads to the truck. Rose held the awning down. Junie pulled the chalkboard inside.

The wind was strong. The rain was cold. The thunder was so loud they felt it in their chests.

"We need to get inside!" Rose shouted.

"Not yet!" Junie said. "The melons!"

They kept working. Lightning struck somewhere close. The ground shook.

"Now!" Delia said. "Inside!"

They ran for the house. The rain soaked through their clothes. The wind pushed them sideways.

They burst through the kitchen door. Dripping. Shaking. Breathing hard.

"Is the stand still there?" Junie said.

They looked out the window. The stand was still standing. The awning was torn. But the structure was there.

"We saved it," Rose said.

"We saved the melons," Junie said.

They stood in the kitchen. Wet and tired and breathing hard.

"We saved each other," Delia said.

Chapter 48: The Morning After

The morning after the storm, the world was clean and green. The sky was clear. The air smelled like wet earth and cut grass.

The sisters walked to the stand. The awning was torn. The chalkboard was cracked. But the watermelons were safe.

"We did it," Junie said.

"We did it together," Rose said.

They sat on the tailgate of the truck. Delia made coffee. They drank it from mason jars.

"I thought we were going to lose it," Junie said. "Last night. I thought the whole thing was coming down."

"But it didn't."

"No. It didn't."

Junie laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in months. The sound was strange and wonderful.

"What's so funny?" Rose said.

"I don't know. Everything. None of it. I'm just happy."

Rose smiled. "That's a good reason."

Delia looked at her sisters. Their faces were dirty. Their hair was a mess. Their clothes were stained.

They were the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

"I love you," Delia said.

The words came out before she could stop them.

Junie stopped laughing. Rose put down her coffee.

"We love you too," Rose said.

They sat in the morning sun. They drank their coffee. The watermelons were safe. The summer was not over.

But this moment was perfect.

Chapter 49: The Gathering

Evelyn March's last visit was on August fifteenth. She did not come to the stand. She came to the farmhouse.

"I want to say goodbye properly," she said.

They gathered under the oak tree. The sun was setting. The air was warm.

Evelyn carried a sunflower. She had brought it from her garden. She walked to the base of the tree and placed it on the ground.

"This is for Lucy," she said. "Your mother. My friend."

The sisters stood in a circle. Evelyn spoke.

"She was not perfect. She made mistakes. But she loved. God, how she loved. And she raised three girls who grew into women she would be proud of."

Junie started crying. Rose took her hand.

"I am proud to have known her," Evelyn said. "I am proud to have known you."

She stepped back. The sunflower sat at the base of the tree. The wind moved the petals.

"Goodbye, Lucy," Evelyn said.

"Goodbye," the sisters said.

Evelyn walked to her car. She did not look back. The green Buick pulled away. The dust settled.

The sisters stood under the oak tree. The sunflower was bright against the green grass.

"She was lucky to have Evelyn," Rose said.

"She was lucky to have us," Delia said.

"Yes," Junie said. "She was."

Chapter 50: The Last Slice

August thirty-first. The last day of the summer.

The sisters closed the stand at sundown. They had sold the last watermelon. A Jubilee, the biggest one, the one Junie had saved for this.

They carried it to the porch. Delia sliced it open with a butcher knife. The inside was deep red and full of seeds.

Three slices. One for each of them.

They sat on the porch steps. The sun was going down. The air was cooling. The first hint of autumn was in the breeze.

"To Daddy," Rose said, raising her slice.

"To Daddy," they said.

They ate. The watermelon was sweet and cold. The juice ran down their chins.

"To Mom," Junie said.

"To Mom."

They ate another bite.

"To us," Delia said.

"To us."

They ate until there was nothing left but rind. They threw the rinds into the garden. They wiped their faces.

The farm was theirs. The debt was paid. The secrets were buried. The summer was over.

But the sisters were still standing.

"What happens now?" Junie said.

"Now we get ready for fall," Delia said. "We plant the winter garden. We fix the roof. We start planning for next summer."

"And after that?" Rose said.

"After that, we do it again."

Junie leaned against her sisters. The porch creaked. The stars came out.

"Taste the Summer," she said.

"That's the name of the stand," Rose said.

"No. That's what we did. We tasted it. Every day."

The sisters sat on the porch. The night was warm. The future was uncertain. But they were together.

That was enough.
