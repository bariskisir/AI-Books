Chapter 1: The Morning Lamp

The furnace breathed before the sun rose. Kemal Özden opened the iron door and the orange glow spilled across the stone floor of the workshop, catching dust motes suspended in the cold February air. He had been up since four, as he had been every morning for thirty years, first as a boy sweeping the floors for his father, then as an apprentice, and now as the master.

The glass waited in rods and canes against the wall — cobalt, emerald, amber, the clear crystal they called White Sea. He selected a length of deep blue and held it to the flame. The glass softened. He turned the pipe with his left hand, steady and slow, feeling the weight shift as the gather began to take shape. His grandfather had taught his father this rhythm. His father had taught him. Now there was only him.

Outside, the first call to prayer echoed from the Yeni Cami across the Golden Horn. The muezzin's voice carried over the water, mingling with the cry of gulls. Kemal kept his eyes on the glass. At this hour, the world was small enough to hold.

He was making a lantern for the British Embassy — a commission that had come three months ago through a consular secretary named Harker, who had admired the Sultan's Eye pattern in the window of a Beyoğlu shop. Forty-eight glass panels, each hand-cut and fitted into a brass frame, the whole thing suspended from a chain. It was delicate work. Each panel curved exactly two degrees, enough to catch light from eight directions.

The door opened. Evren came in, jacket collar turned up against the damp, a cigarette behind his ear. He was twenty-three and walked like he owned streets he had never been down.

"You're late," Kemal said without turning.

"The tram."

"The tram is never late." Kemal withdrew the pipe from the furnace and set it on the marver. "Fetch the cobalt rods. We need twelve more panels cut today."

Evren lit his cigarette in the doorway, one eye on the street. "There's a policeman outside."

Kemal looked up. Through the window grille he saw a figure in a dark uniform, standing beside the workshop gate as if waiting for a shop to open. Which was wrong. The police did not visit a glassmaker at dawn.

"Put out the cigarette," Kemal said. He wiped his hands on his apron and went to the door.

Chapter 2: The Body in Pera

Inspector Leyla Aksoy stood in the study of a dead Englishman and counted what was missing. The room was large by Pera standards — high ceilings, French wallpaper peeling in one corner, a desk of English oak that must have cost more to ship than to buy. The body had been removed two hours ago. What remained was the shape of violence.

Blood on the carpet. A chair overturned. A brass letter opener on the floor, blade darkened. And near the east window, a scatter of glass shards catching the morning light.

Leyla crouched beside the glass. She had learned early in her career that the smallest things told the largest stories. A button, a thread, a smear of ash — these were honest in ways that witnesses were not. She picked up the largest shard with tweezers and turned it to the window.

The glass was blue, hand-blown. On its inner surface, barely visible, was a maker's mark — a stylized eye, the pupil formed by three concentric circles.

"Özden," she said.

Her assistant, a young sergeant named Hasan, looked up from his notebook. "Inspector?"

"The glassmaker. Beyoğlu." She wrapped the shard in a cloth. "Tell me about Geoffrey Harker."

Hasan consulted his notes. "British consular secretary, forty-four years old. Attached to the embassy three years ago. Wife is in England — sister's illness, they say. Lived alone, one housekeeper who found the body this morning. The consul general has already telephoned the governor."

"Already." Leyla stood. "That was fast."

The neighbors had heard nothing. The back gate was unlatched. The housekeeper, a Greek woman named Sophia, had been working for Harker for two years and had no key — she arrived each morning at eight and was let in. This morning the door was already open. She found her employer and screamed until a street vendor came running.

Leyla walked the perimeter of the room again. The bookshelves held English novels and several volumes on Ottoman history. A framed photograph on the desk showed a woman in a pale dress, probably the wife. And on the floor near the window, a second set of glass fragments — these ones from a drinking glass, not the lantern. Two glasses. Two men.

"He knew his killer," Leyla said. "Or at least he let him in."

She looked at the shattered lantern one more time. The morning light through the east window had caught it at an angle, splitting blue and gold across the dead man's carpet. Even broken, it was beautiful work.

Chapter 3: The Mark

The workshop smelled of hot sand and linseed oil. Leyla waited by the cooling rack while an apprentice — a young man with restless eyes — went to fetch the master. She studied the space: the brick furnace dominating the center, the iron pipes hanging from wooden beams, the shelves of finished lanterns in amber and green and the deep blue that matched her shard.

Kemal Özden approached wiping his hands on a leather apron. He was a lean man, forty-five or near it, with the kind of stillness that came from decades of working with things that broke. His eyes were pale gray, almost the color of clear glass.

"Inspector." He did not offer his hand. "My nephew said there's been an accident."

"Not an accident." Leyla unwrapped the shard and held it out on her palm. "Is this your work?"

Kemal took the glass between thumb and forefinger. He held it to the light from the furnace, turning it slowly. His face did not change.

"The Sultan's Eye," he said. "Sixteen panels, brass frame, chain suspension. I delivered it to the British Embassy three months ago. To a man named Harker."

"Geoffrey Harker. He's dead. This was found beside his body."

Kemal set the shard down on the workbench. The furnace coughed behind him.

"How?"

"Stabbed. Last night. The lantern was broken — someone knocked it over during the struggle." She watched him carefully. "Or someone broke it deliberately, to send a message."

"What message would broken glass send?"

"I was hoping you could tell me."

Kemal looked at the shard again. His expression was unreadable, but Leyla had spent six years reading the faces of men who wanted to hide things. She saw the slight tightening around his jaw.

"I make lanterns, Inspector. I don't know the messages they carry after they leave this workshop."

"Did you know Harker well?"

"He commissioned a lantern. He came twice. We discussed the pattern, the cost, the delivery date. That's all."

"And last night? Where were you?"

"Here. Working until eight, then home. My daughter can confirm it."

"I'll need her name."

"Defne. She's nineteen. She was sketching when I came in." His voice was steady, but the stillness had changed quality. It was the stillness now of a man holding something back.

Leyla closed her notebook. "I'll want to speak with your daughter. And with your apprentice. And I'll need a list of everyone who has access to this workshop."

"That's a short list," Kemal said. "There's only three of us."

Chapter 4: The Embassy Call

The British Embassy occupied a white palazzo above the Bosphorus, its cypress trees visible from the water. Leyla had passed it a hundred times, a passenger on ferries crossing between Europe and Asia, never expecting to walk up its marble steps with a police notebook in her hand.

Charles Whitmore received her in a high-ceilinged office decorated with maps of the Levant. He was a tall man, forty-eight, with the easy posture of someone born to rooms like this one. He offered tea. She accepted.

"Dreadful business," he said, settling into the chair opposite. "Geoffrey was one of our best. Conscientious. A bit quiet, perhaps. Not the type to make enemies."

"Everyone has enemies, Mr. Whitmore. We just don't always know who they are."

He smiled at that, a fraction too quickly. "True enough. Though in Geoffrey's case, I genuinely can't imagine who. His work was mostly cultural liaison — arranging exhibitions, escorting visiting scholars, that sort of thing. Hardly the stuff of murder."

"And his personal life?"

"His wife Margaret is in England. Her sister is unwell. They corresponded regularly." Whitmore stirred his tea. "I gather he was faithful, if that's what you're asking."

"Did he have any interest in antiques? Ottoman glass, specifically?"

Whitmore's spoon paused against the cup. "Not that I know of. Why do you ask?"

"Curiosity." Leyla let the word hang. "The lantern at the crime scene was a local commission. Made by a glassmaker named Özden. Did you know about that?"

"I saw the lantern in Harker's study. It was a lovely thing. He said he'd bought it to send to Margaret — she collected glass, I believe." Whitmore set his cup down. "Inspector, I want to be helpful. But I must also be frank. The consul general is concerned. This is a sensitive moment in Anglo-Turkish relations. The Lausanne Treaty is barely two years old. We cannot afford a scandal."

"Murder is always a scandal, Mr. Whitmore. The question is what kind."

She left the embassy with a list of embassy staff and a growing certainty that Charles Whitmore was not telling her the truth about several things.

Chapter 5: The Daughter's Eye

Defne Özden worked at a slanted table by the courtyard window, a charcoal stick in her fingers, a sheet of heavy paper pinned to the board. She was drawing the view: the old Galata Tower against a sky of layered gray, the rooftops descending in steps toward the water. She had been drawing since she was six, when her mother first put a pencil in her hand and said, "See the light, Defne. Always look at where the light falls."

Her mother had been dead five years. Defne still looked for the light.

Her father had come home last night at eight, as he always did, his clothes smelling of the furnace. He had eaten his supper, asked about her day, and gone to bed. Nothing unusual. Nothing she could tell the inspector.

But there was something she had not told her father either. Something she had seen.

The night before — the night the Englishman died — she had been riding the tram home from the Academy, standing by the rear door because she liked to watch the streets slide past. The tram had slowed near the Pera Palas Hotel, and through the window she had seen two men arguing under the hotel's awning. One was a European in a dark coat, gesturing with a walking stick. The other was shorter, darker, wearing a fez. The European's face was lit by the hotel's gas lamps — she would know it again.

The tram had moved on. She had thought nothing of it until her father told her about the murder and the policeman at the workshop gate.

Now she looked at her sketchbook and saw that she had drawn the scene, almost without thinking, on the page after her Galata skyline. Two figures. One with a raised hand. The other turning away. There was something about the European's coat — a pin on the lapel, caught by the gaslight.

She had drawn it without knowing what it was. But now, looking at the sketch, she thought: embassy. The man was from the British Embassy.

She closed the sketchbook and put it in her satchel. Her father was in enough trouble. But the inspector needed to see this.

Chapter 6: The Apprentice's Debt

Evren Özden was not where he said he was.

Leyla had checked. The Tokatlıyan Jazz Club kept a doorman's log — a habit left over from the occupation years, when French military police demanded records of every entrance. On the night of the murder, the Tokatlıyan had been closed for a private function: a birthday party for a banker's son, invitation only. Evren Özden was not on the invitation list.

She found him in the workshop courtyard, shaping a brass frame with a small hammer. He saw her coming and his rhythm faltered.

"You weren't at the Tokatlıyan," she said.

The hammer stopped. "I was in the neighborhood."

"The club was closed."

"Then I went somewhere else."

"Where?"

Evren set the hammer down and wiped his forehead with the back of his wrist. "Look, I didn't do anything wrong. I was at a warehouse near the Golden Horn. I do some work there. Private work."

"Private work meaning what?"

"Lanterns. Glass, frames — the things my uncle thinks are beneath me. I sell them on the side. He doesn't know."

Leyla studied him. Twenty-three, ambitious, embarrassed to be an apprentice when others his age had shops of their own. He was lying, but not about the murder.

"Does this warehouse have an address?"

Evren gave her the street name. Unkapanı. Near the old Jewish quarter.

"One more question," Leyla said. "Did you ever meet Geoffrey Harker?"

"The Englishman?" Evren shook his head. "I saw him once, when he came to discuss the commission. I was here. But I didn't talk to him. I'm just the apprentice. I fetch the rods and sweep the floor."

"The way you say it, that bothers you."

"Wouldn't it bother you?" He picked up the hammer again. "To be invisible in your own family's workshop?"

Leyla had no answer for that. She knew something about invisibility.

Chapter 7: The Antiquarian

Madame Eleni Vlahos kept her shop in the shadow of the Grand Bazaar, on a street too narrow for cars, too steep for donkeys. The window displayed a single Ottoman oil lamp and a string of amber prayer beads. The door was unlocked.

Inside, the air was thick with beeswax and dust. Cabinets lined every wall, their shelves crowded with icons and coins, fragments of tile, tarnished silver, a Byzantine cross missing one arm. In the back, behind a desk heaped with papers, an old woman in black was drinking coffee from a tiny cup.

"Inspector," she said without looking up. "You're earlier than I expected."

Leyla stopped. "You expected me?"

"When an Englishman dies in Pera and the police visit every shop in the quarter, it stands to reason they'll reach mine eventually." Madame Eleni set down her cup. "Geoffrey Harker was a client."

"Of what?"

"Antiquities. Ottoman glass, mostly. He had excellent taste." She gestured to a chair. "Sit. You look like a woman who needs coffee."

Leyla sat. The coffee appeared — small, thick, sweet. Madame Eleni poured a second cup for herself.

"Harker came to me six months ago," she said. "He wanted to learn about Sultanic glass — the pieces made for the Ottoman court, seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Lamps from the Ahmed III period particularly. I sold him two small pieces. Properly documented, properly priced."

"Why the interest in court glass?"

"He said it was personal. A passion. Collectors are like that — they find one thing and suddenly it's the center of the universe." She gave Leyla a long look. "But I don't think it was personal. I think he was buying for someone else."

"What makes you say that?"

"Because he never asked the right questions. A real collector wants to know the provenance, the maker, the history. Harker only wanted to know the price and whether I could get more. More of the same period. More of the same quality." She sipped her coffee. "He was a purchasing agent, Inspector. Not a collector. Someone in England, I'd guess, was building a collection through him."

Leyla set her cup down. "Do you know a glassmaker named Özden?"

"Kemal Özden?" The old woman's face softened. "His grandfather made the mosque lamp for Sultan Abdülmecid. His father made the chandelier for the French embassy. And Kemal — Kemal carries all that history in his hands. He is not a man who would kill anyone."

"I didn't say he was."

"You didn't have to." Madame Eleni poured more coffee. "Ask yourself why someone would leave a broken Özden lantern at a murder scene. Either the killer wanted to implicate Kemal. Or the lantern itself was the reason."

Chapter 8: The Second Lantern

Kemal could not sleep. The shard the inspector had shown him kept turning in his mind like a piece of glass in the furnace, softening, changing shape. Something was wrong with it.

At four in the morning he went to the workshop and lit a single oil lamp — not one of his, just a plain brass fixture — and laid out his tools. He had kept a panel from the Harker commission, a spare in case of breakage. He brought it out now and set it beside the photograph Leyla had given him of the shard from the crime scene.

The pattern was the same. The Sultan's Eye — three concentric circles, cut with a diamond wheel, the pupil tinted a deeper blue. But the color was different.

Kemal held the spare panel to the lamp. His glass glowed with a faint cobalt depth, the color of the Bosphorus at dusk on a clear day. The shard in the photograph was paler, thinner, the blue more green than true. Someone had copied his work.

He fetched his father's formula book from the locked drawer — a leather-bound ledger filled with recipes in Ottoman Turkish. The cobalt formula was on page twelve: twelve parts silica sand, four parts potash, one part cobalt oxide ground finer than flour. His grandfather had brought the cobalt from a mine near Kütahya. The imitations used Prussian blue pigment mixed in, cheaper and bluer in the cold light but greenish in the warm.

The shard from Harker's study was made with Prussian blue.

Someone had gone to considerable trouble to make a lantern that looked like his. And then someone had broken it beside a dead man.

He heard the door open. Evren, earlier than usual, looking like he had not slept either.

"I need to tell you something," Evren said.

"About the warehouse?"

Evren's face went slack. "How did you—"

"The inspector came yesterday. She knows you weren't at the jazz club." Kemal set down the formula book. "What's in the warehouse?"

"Lanterns. Twenty of them, maybe more. I've been selling them to shops in the bazaar. I was going to pay you back — the materials cost, the time — I just wanted to make something of my own."

Kemal looked at his nephew — the boy he had raised since he was twelve, the son he never had. "Was Harker one of your customers?"

"No. Never. I never met him." Evren's voice cracked. "But there's something else. The warehouse — I share it with a dealer named Cemil Bey. He sells antiques, mostly. Carpets. But lately he's been asking me questions about our family's old formulas. About the Ottoman court lamps."

Kemal felt the cold settle in his chest. "What did you tell him?"

"Enough." Evren looked at the floor. "I think I told him enough."

Chapter 9: The Consul's Warning

Sir Reginald Croft kept Leyla waiting exactly twelve minutes. She counted them on the embassy clock, a massive English thing that ticked like a slow heartbeat. When the door finally opened, the consul general was all polished politeness.

"Inspector Aksoy. I've heard about you. The governor speaks highly of your work."

"The governor is kind." She took the offered chair. "I'm here about Geoffrey Harker."

"Yes. Terrible thing." Croft settled behind his desk, a vast mahogany piece that must have required six men to move. "I understand you've been questioning our staff."

"Routine procedure."

"Of course. And I want to be entirely cooperative. But I must ask — delicately — whether you've considered the possibility that this was simply a burglary gone wrong. Pera has its rough elements."

"The house wasn't burgled. Valuables were untouched. The killer entered through the back gate, which was unlatched but not forced, suggesting Harker let him in. He died in his study, in conversation with someone he knew. That's not a burglary."

Croft's expression didn't change, but something behind it shifted. "I see."

"What was Harker working on in the weeks before his death?"

"Cultural liaison, as I'm sure Whitmore told you. Art exhibitions, academic exchanges."

"Was he involved in any antiquities transactions?"

The pause was a fraction too long. "Not in his official capacity."

"And unofficially?"

Croft folded his hands on the desk. They were well-manicured hands, soft from decades of paper. "Inspector, I'm going to be direct with you because I think you'll appreciate it. Geoffrey Harker was a good diplomat but not a wealthy man. If he was supplementing his income through private dealings, I knew nothing about it. And I would prefer not to know. What I do know is that this embassy represents His Majesty's Government, and His Majesty's Government does not wish to be embarrassed by a murder investigation that digs into the private affairs of its staff."

"Is that a request, Sir Reginald?"

"It's an observation about diplomatic realities. We've only just normalized relations between our countries. The Lausanne Treaty was a triumph of patient statesmanship. A scandal — any scandal — threatens that. I'm sure you understand."

Leyla stood. "I understand you want a quick resolution. So do I. But I want the right one."

"Those aren't always the same thing."

"No," she said. "They rarely are."

Chapter 10: The Witness in the Alley

The porter's name was Mehmet. He carried loads of eighty kilos up the steep streets of Galata on a back bent like a bow, and he was invisible in the way all porters were invisible — part of the city's furniture, noticed only when needed.

He came to the police station in the afternoon, when the light was flat and the sergeant at the desk was dozing. He said he had information about the Englishman in Pera. He was brought to Leyla's office.

"Sit," she said. "Tea?"

He shook his head. He sat on the edge of the chair as if ready to bolt.

"I was in the alley," he said. "Behind the Englishman's house. The night before they found him. I was resting — I'd carried six loads from Eminönü to Galata that day, my back was finished. The alley is quiet. There's a wall you can lean against."

"Did you see anyone enter the house?"

"One man. Through the back gate. He didn't knock — he had a key, or the gate was already open. He went in like he knew the way."

"What time was this?"

"Late. The ezan for yatsı had sounded. Maybe nine, maybe later."

"Could you describe him?"

The porter shook his head. "Dark clothes. A coat. Not tall, not short. Not fat, not thin. He walked like someone who didn't want to be seen."

"That's not very helpful."

"There's more." Mehmet leaned forward. "He came back out. Not long after — ten minutes, maybe less. He was walking fast. And he was carrying a lantern. A glass lantern, blue, the kind the rich people hang in their halls. I thought it was strange. Who steals a lantern from a dead man?"

Leyla felt her pulse quicken. "You didn't know the man was dead."

"I know it now." The porter met her eyes. "The lantern — he was carrying it carefully, with both hands, like a baby. And then he stopped under the streetlamp and I saw his face."

"Can you identify him?"

"I tried to. I went to the newspaper office yesterday and looked at their photographs. The dead man — Harker — it wasn't him. The man in the alley was someone else."

"Would you know him again if you saw him?"

Mehmet reached into his coat and pulled out a folded page torn from a magazine. It was a photograph of the British Embassy staff at a reception, printed the year before. A circle of ink surrounded one face.

"That one," the porter said. "That's the man."

Leyla looked at the photograph. The circled face was Charles Whitmore.

Chapter 11: The Sketch

Defne had never been inside a police station before. The corridors smelled of cigarette smoke and cheap soap. She clutched her sketchbook against her chest and followed the young sergeant to the inspector's office.

Leyla looked up from a file she was reading. "Defne Özden. Your father told me you were at the Academy."

"I was." Defne sat down, the sketchbook still pressed to her. "I saw something. The night the Englishman died. I didn't think it was important at first, but then I looked at my sketches, and — here."

She opened the book to the page with the Pera Palas scene and pushed it across the desk. Leyla picked it up.

Two men under an awning. Gaslight catching the European's face. And on his lapel, drawn with a student's careful attention, a pin.

"This pin," Leyla said. "You drew it in detail. Why?"

"Because of the way the light hit it. It was gold, with something enameled on it — a crest, I think. Blue and red. The gas lamps made it sparkle."

Leyla studied the sketch. The crest was unmistakable to anyone who had spent the last three days at the British Embassy: the lion and unicorn, the royal arms. The Foreign Office badge.

"The man wearing it — could you describe him?"

"Tall. European. He was angry. He kept pointing at the other man's chest with his walking stick. Not hitting him, but close. The other man — the shorter one — he kept backing away."

"Did you see where they went?"

"The tall one went into the hotel. The shorter one walked toward Taksim. That's all I saw. The tram moved on."

Leyla looked at the sketch again. The tall European matched Charles Whitmore's description exactly. And the shorter man — perhaps Cemil Bey, the missing dealer.

"Defne, this is important. You may have witnessed the last conversation Geoffrey Harker had before he died."

"The dead man — he was the one being shouted at?"

"I believe so." Leyla closed the sketchbook. "I need to keep this. Just for now."

Defne nodded. "Will my father be all right?"

"That's what I'm trying to make sure of."

Chapter 12: The Embassy Pin

Leyla spent the morning in the embassy's personnel files, courtesy of a reluctant clerk who had been told to cooperate. There were forty-three British staff in Istanbul. Of those, twelve held the rank of consular secretary or above and wore the Foreign Office crest on their lapels.

Of those twelve, two were in London on leave. Three were in Ankara for treaty negotiations. Four were women who had been at a dinner party in Şişli the night of the murder with twelve witnesses. That left three men: Sir Reginald Croft, who had been hosting a reception at the consulate with forty guests; a junior secretary named Phillips, who had been on the overnight train to Ankara; and Charles Whitmore, who claimed to have been at home, alone, reading.

Leyla sat back in her chair. The porter's identification. Defne's sketch. The evasions, the careful wording. Whitmore had been outside the Pera Palas, arguing with Harker, hours before Harker died. And then, by the porter's account, he had been seen leaving Harker's house with a lantern in his hands.

But the shard found at the scene — the imitation. If Whitmore took the real lantern, the broken one was the fake. Which meant someone had put it there deliberately.

She called Sergeant Hasan. "Find out everything you can about Charles Whitmore. Where he goes, who he meets, what he eats for breakfast. Quietly."

"Quietly, Inspector?"

"He's a British diplomat. If we accuse him without iron proof, the consulate will have him on a ship to Malta before we can file charges."

After Hasan left, Leyla opened the sketchbook again and looked at Defne's drawing. The girl had talent. The European's face was distinct — the long nose, the angle of the jaw, the way the left shoulder drooped slightly. She was studying the shoulder when she noticed something she had missed before.

The man had one foot turned inward. A slight limp.

Charles Whitmore had told her he played tennis every Saturday. But when she had met him at the embassy, she had noticed the way he favored his right leg when he walked.

Chapter 13: The Second Conversation

She came to Whitmore's office unannounced this time, declining the offer of tea, closing the door behind her.

"I have a witness who saw you outside the Pera Palas Hotel on the night of the murder, arguing with Geoffrey Harker. I have a second witness who saw you leaving Harker's house through the back gate approximately fifteen minutes after that argument, carrying a blue glass lantern."

Whitmore's face went through three expressions in quick succession: surprise, calculation, fear. Then it settled into something like relief.

"Yes," he said. "I was there."

"I think you'd better explain."

He gestured to a chair. This time Leyla sat.

"Harker called me that afternoon. He was agitated — said he needed to meet urgently, that something had gone wrong with a business arrangement. We met outside the Pera Palas. He was waiting for someone — a dealer, I gathered — and he wanted my assurance that if things went badly, the embassy would protect him."

"What business arrangement?"

Whitmore looked at his hands. "Antiquities. Harker had been acquiring Ottoman glass for a collector in London. A Lord Pemberton. I knew about it, and I — I facilitated some of the payments through embassy accounts. Cultural acquisition funds. It was wrong. I know it was wrong."

"So you argued with him about it?"

"He wanted more money. He said he'd found something extraordinary — a piece that could be worth a fortune — and he wanted me to release additional funds. I told him no. I told him to drop the whole business. He stormed off. That was the last I saw of him alive."

"And the house? The back gate?"

Whitmore closed his eyes. "I went to his house an hour later. I couldn't sleep — I kept thinking about how much trouble we were in. I knocked on the front door. No answer. I walked around to the back. The gate was open. I went in and found him in the study. He was already dead."

"You didn't report it."

"I panicked. The lantern — the blue one — was on the floor beside him, intact. I thought if I took it, the police might think it was a burglary. I wasn't thinking clearly."

"You removed evidence from a murder scene."

"I know." He met her eyes. "I know what that makes me look like. But I didn't kill him, Inspector. I was trying to protect the embassy from scandal. And myself."

Leyla stood. "Where is the lantern now?"

"In my apartment. I didn't know what to do with it."

"I'll need it. And I'll need your written statement. And Mr. Whitmore — you don't leave Istanbul without my permission."

"That," he said quietly, "is the least of my worries."

Chapter 14: The Glassmaker's Proof

Kemal had never been inside a police laboratory. It was a small room at the back of the station, crowded with microscopes and specimen jars, but it had good northern light. He laid the spare panel from the Harker commission on the worktable and placed the shard from the crime scene beside it.

"This," he said, touching the spare panel, "is my glass. You see the color — a deep blue with a violet undertone? That comes from cobalt oxide. My grandfather's formula, refined over sixty years. The cobalt is from a specific mine near Kütahya."

"And the shard?" Leyla asked.

"Prussian blue pigment. It's a synthetic color, invented in the eighteenth century. Cheaper than cobalt, easier to work with. But the color isn't stable — it shifts toward green in warm light, toward gray in cold. And"—he held the shard to the light—"the glass is thinner, machine-rolled, not hand-blown. The maker's mark was cut with a steel wheel, not a diamond. My mark is diamond-cut. You can feel the difference under a magnifying glass."

"So this is a forgery."

"An imitation. Someone made a lantern that looks like mine and broke it at Harker's house." Kemal set the shard down. "The real lantern is somewhere else."

Leyla walked to the window and looked out at the Golden Horn. "Whitmore took it. He admitted it — he said he panicked, removed it from the scene."

"That's convenient."

"It's plausible. He's been buying antiquities through Harker for a London collector. The forgery wasn't meant to implicate you — it was meant to be sold to the collector as a genuine Ottoman artifact."

Kemal nodded slowly. "The Ahmed III lamp."

"How did you know that?"

"Harker asked me about it. He came to the workshop twice — the second time, after the commission was finished, he asked if I could reproduce the formula for a specific period. Mosque lamps from the court of Ahmed III. They used a particular shade of blue — sky blue with silver leaf under the glass. It was a closely guarded recipe. I refused."

"Why?"

"Because some things aren't for sale. The Ahmed III lamps were made as diplomatic gifts. They belonged to the sultan, and through him to the people. Reproducing them for a private collector would be"—he searched for the word—"a violation. Of the craft, and of memory."

Leyla studied him. "You knew this could be dangerous. Harker was a diplomat, he could have made things difficult for you."

"He could have. He didn't." Kemal paused. "He accepted my refusal. He was polite about it. I didn't dislike him."

"Then someone else commissioned the replica. Someone who knew your work, knew you would refuse." Leyla flipped through her notebook. "Your nephew Evren mentioned a dealer named Cemil Bey who had been asking questions about old formulas."

"Cemil Bey." Kemal's voice tightened. "I don't know him. But my father used to say — a glassmaker's worst enemy is not the fire. It's the man who wants the flame without the burn."

Chapter 15: The Warehouse

The warehouse stood on a narrow street in Unkapanı, its corrugated iron roof rusting at the seams. Evren unlocked the door with a key he produced from his jacket, hands trembling slightly. Leyla followed him inside.

The space was larger than it looked from the street — a long room divided by wooden shelves stacked with glassware. Lanterns in various states of completion hung from ceiling hooks. In one corner, a small furnace and anvil, clearly used for Evren's sideline work.

"It started two years ago," Evren said. "I wanted to prove I could do it on my own. Make pieces people would buy. My uncle — he's a master. Everything I do in the workshop, he corrects. Here, I could make my own mistakes."

"Who else uses this space?"

"Cemil Bey leases half of it. His crates are in the back."

Leyla walked past the shelves. The rear of the warehouse was stacked with wooden shipping crates, each stenciled with addresses in Arabic and French. Damascus. Beirut. Alexandria. One crate, smaller than the others, was addressed to "G. Harker, Pera, Constantinople."

She pried it open with a crowbar Evren handed her. Inside, wrapped in Syrian newspapers dated three weeks earlier, were four pieces of Ottoman glass: a mosque lamp in pale green, two small bowls, and a fragment of a tile panel. On one of the bowls, a customs stamp. On the customs form, tucked between the wrappings, was a signature: E. Vlahos.

"Madame Eleni," Leyla said.

"The old woman in the bazaar?"

"The same. She told me she sold Harker two pieces. She didn't mention four more in a crate from Damascus." Leyla took the customs form and folded it into her pocket. "Do you have any idea what Harker was really doing with these?"

Evren shook his head. "Cemil Bey handled all the dealings. I just made the lanterns."

"Lanterns? Plural?"

"There were three commissions. All copies of old patterns. Cemil Bey said they were for a collector in London who wanted reproductions for a museum display. I didn't ask questions. The money was good."

Leyla looked at the young man — his fine hands, his expensive shoes, his face full of the particular shame of someone who has been caught not committing a crime but being convenient to one.

"You need to tell your uncle everything," she said. "And you need to stay in Istanbul."

Chapter 16: The Crate from Damascus

The customs form led to the shipping office in Karaköy, where a bored clerk named Rıfat spent twenty minutes searching through carbon copies before finding the matching record. The crate had arrived from Damascus on the steamer Halep, consigned to a company called Levant Trading — which, Leyla discovered after another hour of searching, was registered to one Cemil Karataş.

Cemil Bey.

The shipping manifest listed the contents as "decorative ceramics." It was the same on every crate — ceramics, never glass. Glass would have attracted attention. Glass required special permits under the new antiquities law.

Leyla sat at her desk and spread out the documents. The pattern was emerging: Harker, through Whitmore, funded the purchases. Cemil Bey sourced the artifacts — some genuine, some forged — and shipped them through Damascus to avoid Turkish customs scrutiny. Madame Eleni provided provenance documents that made everything look legitimate. And Kemal Özden's family formulas were being used to create forgeries good enough to fool collectors.

But something had gone wrong. Harker had been killed. The imitation lantern had been broken at the scene. Whitmore had removed the real one. And Cemil Bey had vanished the morning after the murder.

She picked up the telephone and asked to be connected to the Damascus police.

The line crackled and popped. A voice spoke in Arabic, then switched to French. Leyla explained what she needed in French: information on Levant Trading, Cemil Karataş, any known antiquities operations.

The voice on the other end was quiet for a moment. Then: "Inspector, the man you are asking about — he is known to us. But not under that name. He uses three others. And he is not Turkish. He is French."

"What?"

"His real name is Claude Mercier. He worked for the French mandate administration in Syria until last year, when he was dismissed for selling archaeological finds on the private market. He has been operating in Istanbul for at least eight months."

Leyla set the receiver down slowly. The case had just crossed another border.

Chapter 17: Eleni's Ledger

Madame Eleni was arranging a display of silver spoons when Leyla walked in. She did not look surprised.

"You found the crate."

"You lied to me."

"I omitted." The old woman dusted her hands and crossed to her desk. "There's a difference. Please, sit. I think it's time you saw the ledger."

The book she produced was bound in green leather, its pages filled with handwriting in Greek and French. Leyla turned the pages slowly. Each entry recorded a transaction: item description, price, buyer, date. The buyers were varied — a Dutch museum curator, an Italian countess, a French hotelier. But the seller, in every case, was the same: Cemil Karataş, operating through various intermediaries.

"Karataş's real name is Claude Mercier. He's French."

Madame Eleni paused. "I didn't know that."

"Did you know he was using stolen antiquities? That he had a glassmaker creating forgeries?"

"I suspected." She sat heavily in her chair. "I am seventy-three years old, Inspector. I have lived in Istanbul my entire life, through three sultans, two wars, and an occupation. I have seen empires fall and republics rise. I have kept this shop because it is the only thing I know how to do. When a man like Cemil Bey brings me objects and asks for provenance papers, I do not ask where he got them. I look the other way."

"That's not a defense."

"It's not meant to be." She folded her hands. "But I can give you something useful. Cemil Bey was working on a large sale — the largest of his career, he told me. A single piece that would make him rich. He didn't tell me what it was, but I saw a photograph in his briefcase once, when he was careless. It was a glass mosque lamp. Blue, with silver leaf. Very old. He said a lord in England would pay twenty thousand pounds for it."

Twenty thousand pounds. More money than most Turks would see in ten lifetimes.

"Where is the lamp now?"

"With the lord, I assume. Cemil Bey was supposed to deliver it the week Harker died." She met Leyla's eyes. "You should know — Harker was not the buyer. Harker was the obstacle."

Chapter 18: The London Cable

The cable from Scotland Yard arrived at four in the afternoon, delivered by a boy on a bicycle who was clearly impressed by the word URGENT stamped across the envelope.

Leyla read it twice.

LORD ARTHUR PEMBERTON KNOWN TO FOREIGN OFFICE. COLLECTION OF OTTOMAN GLASS UNDER INVESTIGATION BY HM CUSTOMS. THREE PIECES SEIZED AT DOVER LAST MONTH — PROVENANCE DOCUMENTATION FORGED. PEMBERTON CLAIMS PURCHASED IN GOOD FAITH FROM LEVANT TRADING, CONSTANTINOPLE. NO CHARGES FILED. PROCEED WITH CAUTION. FULL REPORT TO FOLLOW.

She folded the cable and tucked it into her file. Pemberton was insulated — a lord, a man of standing, with lawyers and influence. The investigation in London would move slowly, if it moved at all. But the connection was now official. Levant Trading. Claude Mercier, alias Cemil Karataş. The forged provenance documents. It all pointed in one direction.

She called Sergeant Hasan.

"Find out if Cemil Karataş or Claude Mercier has left the country. Check the ports, the railway stations, the passport office. I want to know if he's still in Turkey."

"And if he is?"

"Then we find him before he becomes someone else."

After Hasan left, Leyla spread out the photographs on her desk. The imitation shard. The customs forms. Defne's sketch. The photograph of Whitmore that Mehmet had identified. A case built on fragments, like a mosaic missing half its tiles.

She picked up the telephone again and asked for a connection to Paris.

"Inspector Leyla Aksoy, Istanbul Criminal Division. I need to speak with someone in the Sûreté about a French national named Claude Mercier."

The operator connected her. A man's voice answered, tired and professional. She explained the situation in French.

"Mercier," the voice said. "Yes. We have a file. He worked for the Mandate administration in Beirut before Damascus — dismissed both times for trafficking. He disappeared from Marseille six months ago. You say he's in Istanbul?"

"He was. And I think he killed a British diplomat."

A long pause. "I'll have the file sent by diplomatic pouch. It should reach you in four days."

"Can you send it faster?"

"I can send a summary by telegraph tomorrow morning."

"That will have to do." She hung up and looked at the clock on the wall. It was nearly six. The day had disappeared into a stack of papers and telephone calls, and she was no closer to finding the man at the center of it all.

Chapter 19: The French Connection

The telegram from Paris arrived the next morning, typed on thin paper, the letters slightly smudged.

CLAUDE MERCIER AGE 47 BORN LYON. FORMER OFFICER FRENCH MANDATE ADMINISTRATION BEIRUT 1920-1922 DAMASCUS 1922-1924. DISMISSED FOR TRAFFICKING ARCHAEOLOGICAL ARTIFACTS. KNOWN ASSOCIATES INCLUDE ANTIQUITIES DEALERS IN ALEXANDRIA BEIRUT MARSEILLE. LAST KNOWN ADDRESS RUE DE LA PAIX MARSEILLE. SUSPECTED OF SMUGGLING OTTOMAN GLASS THROUGH SYRIAN PORTS. MAY BE ARMED. EXERCISE EXTREME CAUTION.

Leyla added the telegram to her growing file. Mercier was not just a dealer — he was a professional smuggler with a network that spanned three countries and two continents. And he had been operating in Istanbul for eight months without anyone noticing.

She went to find Whitmore.

He was in his embassy office, packing papers into a leather briefcase. He looked up when she entered, and his face registered something between dread and resignation.

"More questions?"

"One. A man named Claude Mercier. French. Uses the name Cemil Karataş in Istanbul. He was Harker's supplier for the Ottoman glass. Did you know him?"

Whitmore set down his briefcase. "I knew Cemil Bey. I didn't know his real name."

"Where is he now?"

"I don't know. He disappeared the morning after the murder. I tried to reach him — Harker had given him a payment, and I wanted it back. The money is gone. So is he."

"How much money?"

"Two thousand pounds. An advance on the Ahmed III lamp."

Leyla sat down without being invited. "You knew about the lamp. You knew it was stolen."

"I knew Harker had found something valuable. I didn't ask for details." Whitmore's voice was hollow. "That was the arrangement. I handled the payments, Harker handled the acquisitions, Cemil Bey handled the sourcing. I made sure not to know too much."

"And now Harker is dead, Cemil Bey is missing, and you're still here. Why?"

"Because running would confirm guilt." He met her eyes. "And because I'm a coward, Inspector. I've been a coward my whole life. It's why I took this posting — safe, far from London, where no one asks hard questions. It's why I looked the other way when Harker started his sideline. And it's why I stood over a dead man's body and thought only about saving myself."

Leyla studied him. She had interviewed many liars in her career, and she had learned to distinguish the ones who lied to her from the ones who lied to themselves. Whitmore was both.

"I'm going to find Mercier," she said. "When I do, he'll tell me who killed Harker. If you've been honest with me, you have nothing to fear. If you haven't"—she stood—"then you should have run when you had the chance."

Chapter 20: Kemal's Memory

The memory came to Kemal at the furnace, as memories often did — in the rhythm of turning the pipe, the glass gathering heat, the world narrowing to a single point of flame.

Harker had come twice. The first time, he had been polite, curious, asking about patterns and prices. The second time, he had been different — more urgent, less interested in pleasantries. He had produced a photograph from his coat pocket.

"Can you make this?"

The photograph showed a mosque lamp — delicate, bell-shaped, with arabesque patterns in gold leaf under a layer of sky-blue glass. The Sultan's Blue, they called it, a formula that had died with the Ottoman court.

"That's an Ahmed III court lamp," Kemal had said. "There are only three in existence. Two are in the Topkapı. The third was lost."

"I'm aware of the history. I'm asking if you can reproduce it."

The question had hung in the furnace-warm air. Kemal had looked at the photograph and felt the weight of three generations of glassmakers behind him.

"Even if I could — and I'm not saying I can — I wouldn't. Those lamps were diplomatic gifts. They belong to the nation now. Reproducing them for private sale is a crime under the Antiquities Act."

Harker had put the photograph away. "I was hoping you'd be more pragmatic, Mr. Özden."

"Glass isn't pragmatic. It's honest. It shows exactly what it is."

Harker had left after that, and Kemal had not seen him again. He had thought nothing more of it — a foreigner with more money than sense, making improper requests. It happened occasionally. The rich collectors who imagined they could buy anything, even the past.

Now, standing at the furnace with the shard of the imitation on the workbench behind him, Kemal understood. Harker had asked him first. When he refused, they had gone to someone else. Someone who knew his family's techniques but had no loyalty to their ethics.

He walked to the workshop office and found his father's address book in the locked drawer. He turned the pages until he reached a single entry, written in his father's hand, the ink faded but legible.

Yusuf Özden. Murano, Venezia.

His uncle. The one who had left. The one who had become, in the family silence, a ghost.

Kemal had never met him. But he knew his work — it appeared occasionally in auction catalogues, attributed to "Giuseppe Veneziano," the name Yusuf had taken when he settled in Italy. The descriptions always mentioned the distinctive cobalt, the Ottoman influence, the masterful hand.

If someone had needed a forger capable of replicating a lost Ottoman masterpiece, there was one name above all others.

Kemal picked up the telephone and asked the operator to connect him to the police station.

Chapter 21: The Embassy Ledger

Sergeant Hasan had spent two days in the basement of the British Embassy, supervised by a suspicious accountant named Pringle, going through the expense records. He emerged on the third afternoon with a stack of photocopies and the look of a man who had found what he was looking for.

"Cultural acquisition fund," he said, spreading the pages across Leyla's desk. "Established in 1923, annual budget of five thousand pounds. Authorized by the consul general for the purchase of local artworks to be displayed in British government buildings."

"Legitimate?"

"On paper. But look at the disbursements. Every payment went to the same account — a bank in Galata. And the amounts are rounded — five hundred pounds, one thousand, two thousand — not the kind of numbers you get when you're buying paintings from local artists. Artists charge odd amounts. Ninety-seven pounds, a hundred and twelve."

Leyla looked at the ledger entries. Hasan was right. The payments were too clean.

"Who controlled the fund?"

"Charles Whitmore. Sole signatory. The consul general approved the budget but never reviewed individual disbursements." Hasan turned to the last page. "And here — the day before Harker died, Whitmore authorized a payment of three thousand pounds. The largest single disbursement in the fund's history. The notation says 'Acquisition: Ottoman court artifact.'"

"The Ahmed III lamp."

"The purchase price, I'd guess. Except the money never reached the seller — or if it did, someone took it back." Hasan closed the ledger. "There's one more thing. The bank account in Galata — it's in the name of Levant Trading."

Leyla sat back. Whitmore had been paying Mercier directly, through embassy funds, for artifacts that were being smuggled out of the country. Harker had been the middleman. And when Harker discovered that the Ahmed III lamp was a forgery and threatened to expose the operation, someone had killed him.

The question was who. Whitmore had the motive and the opportunity. Mercier had the criminal network and the skill. And neither of them had an alibi for the night of the murder.

"Bring Whitmore in," she said. "Formally, this time. I want him in an interview room."

"Arrest him?"

"Not yet. Detain him for questioning." She looked at the clock. "And send a telegram to Venice. I need information about a glassmaker named Giuseppe Veneziano — original name Yusuf Özden. I need to know if he received any commissions from Istanbul in the past six months."

Chapter 22: The Intermediary

The carpet shop in Sultanahmet was empty when Leyla arrived. The shutters were down, the door locked, a torn notice from the landlord pasted to the frame. Neighbors said the tenant — a middle-aged man, polite, kept to himself — had left in a hurry the day after the Englishman was killed. A truck had come at dawn. Everything was gone.

Leyla found the landlord, a bald Armenian named Hagop who ran a spice shop two doors down. He let her into the shop with a spare key, shaking his head at the state of the place.

"Cemil Bey paid six months in advance," he said. "Good tenant. Never complained. And then — poof. Gone. Left two months' rent on the table."

The shop was a single room with a back office. The shelves were bare except for dust outlines where carpets had been stacked. The office was the same — empty desk, empty cabinets, a single broken chair.

But in the corner of the office, behind where a cabinet had stood, Leyla noticed a loose floorboard. She pried it up with the blade of her pocketknife.

Underneath was a metal box, the kind used for storing documents. She lifted it out and opened it on the desk.

Photographs. Dozens of them, each showing an Ottoman artifact against a dark background — lamps, bowls, tiles, manuscripts. Each with a handwritten notation in French: dimensions, estimated date, asking price. And at the bottom of the stack, a larger photograph of a glass mosque lamp in deep blue with gold arabesques.

The notation read: Lampe de mosquée, Ahmed III, vers 1725. Perdue. Vérification en cours. Prix demandé: £20,000.

Lost. Verification in progress. Asking price: £20,000.

Leyla turned the photograph over. On the back, in different handwriting, someone had written in English: "Not genuine? Check with Y.Ö. — Murano."

Y.Ö. Yusuf Özden. The uncle in Venice.

Mercier had suspected the lamp was a forgery. He had been checking. And Harker — Harker had been caught between a forger who wanted to be paid and a buyer who would ruin them all if he discovered the fraud.

Chapter 23: The Carpet Shop

The metal box contained more than photographs. Beneath the stack, Leyla found a small leather notebook — Mercier's account book. The entries were in French, coded in abbreviations she had to puzzle out one by one.

"E.V." appeared frequently — Eleni Vlahos, supplying provenance documents. "C.W." was Charles Whitmore, authorizing payments. "G.H." was Geoffrey Harker, the front man. And "G.V." — Giuseppe Veneziano — was the forger in Murano.

The most recent entries told the story. Six weeks before the murder, Mercier had written: "G.H. suspicious of lamp provenance. Demands authentication." Three weeks before: "G.V. confirms — replica only. Original lost 1730. Client will not pay for replica." Two days before Harker's death: "G.H. threatens to inform embassy. Must resolve."

Must resolve.

Leyla closed the notebook. Mercier had been pressured from both sides — Harker threatening exposure, Whitmore demanding results, Pemberton expecting delivery. And in the middle, a lamp that was worth either twenty thousand pounds or nothing at all.

"Sergeant," she called. Hasan appeared in the doorway. "I need a warrant for the arrest of Claude Mercier, also known as Cemil Karataş, on suspicion of murder and antiquities trafficking. And I need it today."

Hasan hesitated. "Inspector — the suspect is a French national. The consulate—"

"The French consulate can file a protest after we catch him." She stood, the metal box under her arm. "And send word to every port. If he hasn't already left the country, I want him found."

Chapter 24: The Lost Lamp

Kemal met Leyla at the Topkapı Museum, in a storage room the curator had unlocked after an hour of bureaucratic hesitation. The museum kept its glass collection in climate-controlled cabinets — a luxury the sultans had never needed, but the republic's Ministry of Culture insisted.

The curator, a nervous man named Dr. Aslan, led them to a cabinet in the rear of the room and unlocked it with a brass key.

"The Ahmed III mosque lamps," he said. "Two of the original set of three. Acquired by the museum in 1924 from the imperial treasury."

The lamps were exquisite. Bell-shaped bodies of crystalline glass, painted with gold-leaf arabesques that spiraled upward like calligraphy frozen in light. The blue was the color of a winter sky over the Bosphorus — pale, luminous, alive.

"The third lamp," Dr. Aslan continued, "was lost during the Patrona Halil revolt in 1730. The rebels broke into the palace treasury. Some items were destroyed, some stolen. The lamp was never recovered. It appears in no museum, no private collection, no auction record. It is"—he spread his hands—"gone."

"Could it have survived?" Leyla asked. "Hidden somewhere?"

"It's possible. The revolt lasted only two months. Some palace servants hid valuable objects to protect them from the mob. An inventory from 1732 lists the lamp as kayıp — lost. Not destroyed. There is a difference."

Kemal leaned close to the cabinet, studying the lamps. His reflection in the glass made it look as though he were inside the case himself.

"If someone found it," he said, "they would have documentation. Provenance records going back to 1730. A chain of ownership. Without that, it's just a story."

"And if someone forged those documents?"

"Then you have a crime." Kemal straightened. "These lamps — my grandfather once made a copy of one. A study piece, for his own education. He wrote about it in his journal. He said the difficulty was the blue — getting the silver leaf to reflect through the glass without clouding it. It took him two years to get it right."

"Where is that journal now?"

"In my workshop. In the locked drawer."

Leyla thought about this. "If your grandfather could figure out the formula, could someone else?"

"With enough time and skill, yes." Kemal paused. "There is one person who might have my grandfather's notes. His younger son — my uncle Yusuf. When he left Istanbul, he took some of the family papers with him. My father never forgave him for it."

Chapter 25: The London Collector

The report from Scotland Yard arrived by diplomatic pouch on a gray Tuesday morning — a thick envelope stamped with the lion and unicorn. Leyla read it in her office with the door closed.

Lord Arthur Pemberton was a baron of the second creation, his title purchased by a grandfather who had made a fortune in Manchester cotton. Now sixty-eight, widowed, childless, Pemberton had devoted his retirement to collecting Ottoman art. His London house contained what experts described as "the finest private collection of Islamic glass outside the Middle East."

The report detailed seventeen acquisitions made through Levant Trading over the past two years. Of these, Scotland Yard's experts had examined eleven. Five were genuine. Four were probable forgeries. Two were inconclusive.

The genuine pieces were mostly minor — bowls, tiles, small lamps. The forgeries were the valuable ones: rare court objects that should have been in museums. In each case, the provenance documentation had been supplied by a Greek dealer in Istanbul — Eleni Vlahos — and certified by a French expert in Beirut whose credentials, upon investigation, proved to be entirely fictitious.

Pemberton had not been charged. His lawyers had demonstrated that he purchased all items in good faith, relying on expert certification. The forgeries had been seized, but Pemberton himself was treated as a victim of fraud rather than an accomplice.

The report concluded: "Pemberton's relationship with Levant Trading was managed through Geoffrey Harker of the British Embassy, Constantinople. Harker's death has effectively severed the supply chain. No further prosecutions are anticipated in this jurisdiction."

Leyla set the report down. Pemberton was protected — by his title, his lawyers, and the Foreign Office's embarrassment at having an embassy staffer running a smuggling operation. Justice in London had its limits.

But justice in Istanbul didn't have to.

Chapter 26: The Night of the Murder

Leyla stood in Harker's study for the second time, trying to see the night as it had happened.

Whitmore had argued with Harker outside the Pera Palas at approximately seven in the evening. Harker had refused to back down — he was going to expose the operation unless he was paid more. Whitmore had gone home. Harker had returned to his house.

At approximately eight, according to Mehmet the Porter, a man matching Whitmore's description had entered through the back gate and left fifteen minutes later, carrying a blue lantern. But Whitmore claimed he had arrived after nine, found Harker already dead, and took the lantern in a panic. The timeline didn't match.

And there was another complication. Margaret Harker, the wife, had been in Istanbul that night. Defne's sketch showed a woman in blue standing in the doorway of the Pera Palas. If Margaret Harker had been following her husband — if she had seen him with another woman, or with Mercier, or simply realized the depth of his criminal involvement — she might have confronted him.

Leyla reconstructed the sequence in her notebook:

7:00 PM — Whitmore and Harker argue outside Pera Palas.
7:30 PM — Harker returns home (last seen alive by neighbor).
8:00 PM — Whitmore seen entering Harker's back gate by Mehmet.
8:15 PM — Whitmore seen leaving with a lantern.
9:00 PM — Whitmore claims he arrived and found Harker dead.
Next morning — Housekeeper finds body.

The gap was the hour between 7:30 and 8:30. If Mehmet was correct about the time, Whitmore had been at the house when Harker was killed. If Whitmore was correct — if he had arrived later, at nine — then someone else had entered in the interim.

She picked up the telephone and asked to be connected to the telephone exchange. "I need the records for calls made from the British Embassy on the evening of February 14."

The operator promised to check. Leyla hung up and looked at the broken glass still scattered near the window. The imitation lantern, smashed at the scene. The real lantern, removed by Whitmore. The murder weapon — a letter opener — belonging to the house. Nothing taken. Nothing stolen.

This was not a burglary. It was a confrontation that had gone wrong. And whoever had confronted Harker that night had known exactly where to find him.

Chapter 27: The Second Man

The telephone exchange records showed one call from the embassy switchboard on the evening of the murder, placed at 6:47 PM, from Charles Whitmore's extension to the number of Geoffrey Harker's residence. Duration: four minutes.

Leyla went to Whitmore's apartment on the Grande Rue de Pera, a fifth-floor flat with a view of the water. His housekeeper let her in and retreated to the kitchen.

Whitmore was in the sitting room, a half-empty glass of whiskey on the table beside him. He didn't rise when she entered.

"I've been expecting you," he said.

"The telephone call. You spoke to Harker at 6:47."

"Yes."

"You told me you met him outside the Pera Palas at seven. You didn't mention a call beforehand."

Whitmore took a long drink. "I called to warn him. I'd heard from Cemil Bey — Mercier — that Harker was threatening to expose the whole operation. I told Harker I'd meet him at the hotel, that I'd try to work something out. But I also told him that if he went to the consul general, I'd deny everything. I'd say he was the one running the smuggling, that I had nothing to do with it."

"Blackmail."

"Mutual assured destruction." He smiled without humor. "We learned it from the Great War."

"What happened at the hotel?"

"We argued. I told him the lamp was a forgery — I'd confirmed it with Mercier that afternoon. Harker was furious. He said he'd been made to look a fool, that Pemberton would ruin him. He wanted Mercier's head. I told him to calm down, to let it go, that we'd find another buyer. He stormed off."

"And then?"

"And then I went home. I had a drink. I thought about it. And I decided I had to see him — to make him understand that going to the police would destroy us both. So I walked to his house."

"At what time?"

"Around eight, I think. Maybe a quarter past." He looked at the ceiling. "The gate was open. The back door was unlocked. I called his name. No answer. I went inside. And there he was."

"Already dead."

"Yes."

Leyla studied him. "Then you took the lantern and ran. Without checking his pulse. Without calling for help."

"I told you. I'm a coward." He met her eyes. "But I didn't kill him, Inspector. When I got there, he was already cold."

"The porter saw you leaving. He didn't see anyone else enter."

"Then someone else came through the front door." Whitmore's voice was flat. "Or someone was already inside when Harker got home. I don't know. I only know what I saw — and what I saw was a dead man and a broken lantern, and me standing in the wrong place at the wrong time."

Leyla stood. "I'm going to find out. And if you've been lying to me about the time, about the lantern, about any of it — I'll be back. And it won't be for a conversation."

Chapter 28: Halit's Story

The photograph from Defne's sketch had been circulated to every police station in the city. It took five days for a response to come — not from a police station, but from a post office in Üsküdar, where a clerk recognized the man with the crooked shoulder and called the number on the poster.

Halit Yılmaz was forty-three years old, a former translator at the British Embassy who had been dismissed six months earlier for what the personnel file called "security irregularities." He was living in a rooming house near the ferry dock, taking occasional translation work from shipping companies.

Leyla found him in a tea garden, hunched over a glass of çay, his coat too thin for the February cold. He looked up at her with the resignation of a man who had been waiting to be found.

"I saw the posters," he said. "I knew you'd come eventually."

"Tell me about Geoffrey Harker."

Halit stirred his tea. "Harker hired me. Off the books. He needed someone who could read Ottoman Turkish — the old script, not the Latin alphabet the republic uses now. Old documents, court records, that kind of thing."

"What kind of documents?"

"Provenance. He was trying to authenticate a piece — a mosque lamp, very old. He showed me photographs of court records from the 1730s, inventory lists from the palace treasury. He wanted to know if they were genuine."

"Were they?"

"No." Halit shook his head. "The paper was old, the ink was right, but the language was wrong. Eighteenth-century court scribes used particular formulas, particular honorifics. These documents didn't have them. They were forgeries — good ones, but forgeries."

"Did you tell Harker?"

"I wrote him a report. He was furious. He said he'd been sold a fake and demanded my silence. When I said I had an obligation to report the fraud — the new antiquities law requires it — he had me dismissed from the embassy. 'Security irregularities.'"

Leyla wrote in her notebook. "Did you know a man named Cemil Karataş? Or Claude Mercier?"

"The Frenchman? Yes. He was the one who brought the documents to Harker. I met him once. He was very polite. Very dangerous."

"Why dangerous?"

"Because he didn't care about the glass. He didn't care about the history or the beauty or any of it. He only cared about the transaction. People like that — they'll do anything to protect the profit." Halit drained his tea. "When I heard Harker was dead, I knew. Mercier killed him. Or had him killed. The lamp was worth a fortune only if it was real. If Harker could prove it was fake, the whole network collapsed."

"Mercier has disappeared."

"Then he's left the country. He's probably in Damascus by now. Or Marseille." Halit set down the glass. "He's been running for years. The French have a file on him. So do the Syrians. He always surfaces somewhere else."

Leyla closed her notebook. "Thank you. If you remember anything else—"

"I've remembered enough." The former translator pulled his coat tighter. "I've remembered enough for a lifetime."

Chapter 29: The Replica

Kemal had not worked in three days. The furnace sat cold. The rods hung untouched. Evren had opened the workshop each morning, hoping his uncle would appear, and each morning the furnace remained dark. This morning was different. Kemal was there before dawn, a folder of papers spread across the workbench, his father's formula book open beside them.

"I've been studying the photographs," he said when Evren came in. "The lamp Mercier was trying to sell — the one Harker wanted authenticated."

"The Ahmed III lamp."

"It's a replica. A very good one, but a replica. I've been through my grandfather's journals. He wrote about the technique — the silver leaf under the blue glass. It requires a particular firing sequence: the silver applied at precisely 740 degrees, the blue glass layered over it at 680. Too hot and the silver melts. Too cold and the glass clouds. No one outside our family has ever mastered it."

"But someone did."

"Yes." Kemal turned a page of his father's formula book. "My uncle Yusuf. He took my grandfather's notes when he left Istanbul. He has the formula. And he has the skill."

"So he made the replica?"

"It appears so. But I don't think he knew what it was for." Kemal tapped the photograph of the lamp. "Look at the arabesques. Yusuf signed them — subtly, but it's there. A glassmaker's mark, hidden in the pattern. He was proud of the work. He wouldn't have signed a piece he knew was fraudulent."

Evren stared at the photograph. "If he didn't know, then someone commissioned him in good faith. Or at least pretended to."

"Mercier." Kemal closed the formula book. "Mercier commissioned my uncle to make a replica, probably told him it was for a museum or a historical society. Then he used Madame Eleni's provenance documents to pass it off as the lost original. When Harker discovered the truth, Mercier killed him."

"But the lamp at the crime scene — the broken one — that was a cheap imitation, not Yusuf's work."

"I know. I don't understand that part yet." Kemal looked toward the furnace. "But I think my uncle does. I think we need to go to Venice."

Chapter 30: The Name

Leyla listened to Kemal's theory in silence, turning over the pieces in her mind.

"The broken lantern at the scene was a cheap imitation," she said. "But Yusuf's replica — if it exists — would be much better work. So where is it?"

"With Mercier, presumably. Or with Pemberton." Kemal spread his hands. "The broken imitation was a decoy. Someone put it there to make the murder look like a botched burglary. But they didn't know the glass well enough — they used the wrong color."

"What about Whitmore? He removed the real lantern from the house."

"The Özden lantern I made for Harker — the Sultan's Eye commission — yes. That's a different piece entirely. There were two lanterns involved: my Sultan's Eye lantern, which Whitmore took, and the imitation of my work that was smashed at the scene. Neither was the Ahmed III replica."

Leyla stood and walked to her office window. The Golden Horn was gray under winter clouds, ferries crossing in opposite directions.

"You're suggesting there are three glass objects in play. Your original commission — the Sultan's Eye — which Whitmore removed. The cheap imitation of your work that was left at the scene. And Yusuf Özden's replica of the Ahmed III lamp, which Mercier was trying to sell as the genuine lost artifact."

"Yes."

"And Yusuf is the key to all of it. He has the family formulas. He was commissioned by Mercier. And he may know where the replica is now — or whether Mercier has it."

"I believe so."

Leyla turned from the window. "I've already cabled Venice, asking about Yusuf — Giuseppe Veneziano. The Italian police should respond in a day or two." She paused. "But I think you're right. We may need to go there ourselves."

"I've never met him," Kemal said. "My uncle. He left before I was born. My father never spoke of him — it was a forbidden subject. But I've seen his work in catalogues. He's a master. Greater than my father, perhaps. Greater than me."

"And he's been making forgeries for a French smuggler."

"He's been making art." Kemal's voice was quiet. "What someone else did with it — that's not his crime."

"Maybe not. But he needs to answer questions. And so does Mercier." Leyla returned to her desk. "I've requested a travel permit for Venice. It should be approved tomorrow. If you want to come — if you want to meet your uncle — you're welcome. But this is a police investigation, not a family reunion."

"I understand." Kemal stood. "What about Defne?"

"She stays here. She's a witness, not an investigator."

"She'll want to come."

"Then don't tell her until after we've left." Leyla's expression was not unkind. "Some journeys are better taken alone."

Chapter 31: The Uncle

The Murano workshop sat at the end of a canal so narrow the gondolas had to turn sideways to pass. Kemal stood on the stone jetty and looked at the modest building — a single-story brick structure with a chimney that released a thin ribbon of smoke into the Venetian sky. From inside came the rhythmic sound of a bellows.

"He's working," Leyla said.

"He's always working. Glassmakers are like that."

They knocked. A young apprentice opened the door, a boy of perhaps fifteen with ash on his cheeks. He looked at them without comprehension until Leyla spoke in French, asking for Giuseppe Veneziano.

The old man came from the back of the workshop, wiping his hands on a cloth. He was seventy-two, lean and weathered, with the same gray eyes as his nephew. When he saw Kemal, he stopped.

"You have your father's face," he said in Turkish.

"And you have his eyes."

Yusuf Özden set down the cloth. "You'd better come in."

The workshop was smaller than Kemal's, but the tools were the same — the furnace, the marver, the cooling rack, the rods hanging from the beams. The language of glass was universal.

"Coffee," Yusuf said to the boy. "Then leave us."

They sat on wooden stools around a worktable covered with sketches. Yusuf looked from Kemal to Leyla and back again.

"You've come about the lamp," he said. "The Ahmed III replica."

"You know about the murder in Istanbul?" Leyla asked.

"I read the newspapers. An English diplomat. And a broken lantern." He looked at Kemal. "They accused you."

"They did. The broken lantern was an imitation of my work."

"Yes." Yusuf poured coffee from a brass pot. "The imitation was mine. Not the broken one — that was crude work, someone copying my copy. But the design they copied was mine. I made a replica of the Ahmed III lamp six months ago, for a man who said he was a curator at a museum in Lyon. He sent me the specifications, the photographs, the payment. I did the work."

"His name was Claude Mercier. He's not a curator. He's a smuggler."

"I know that now." Yusuf sipped his coffee. "When the commission arrived, I was suspicious. The details were too exact — they had measurements from the real lamps in the Topkapı Museum. But the money was good, and I told myself it was for educational purposes. I have been telling myself that for thirty years."

"You have our father's journals," Kemal said. "The formula book."

Yusuf looked at him for a long moment. Then he rose and went to a cabinet in the corner, unlocked it with a key from his pocket, and brought out a leather-bound book identical to the one Kemal kept in his workshop. He set it on the table between them.

"Our grandfather's. I took it when I left. I'm not proud of it."

"Why did you leave?"

It was the question Kemal had carried his whole life. The question his father had never answered.

Yusuf sat down again. "Your father and I — we were rivals, not just brothers. He was the elder, the heir. I was the younger, the one who was supposed to assist. But I was better. At twenty-two, I could do things with glass that he could not. And he resented it."

"So you left."

"I left because staying would have destroyed us both." Yusuf looked at the formula book. "I took the journals because they were as much mine as his. But I have regretted it. Every day, I have regretted it."

Kemal reached out and touched the book. The leather was warm from the furnace heat.

"The lamp you made — do you know where it is now?"

"Mercier has it. He sent word a week ago, asking for documentation — a certificate of authenticity. I refused. I told him the lamp was a replica, that I would not help him pass it off as genuine. I haven't heard from him since."

"Then he still has it." Leyla leaned forward. "Signor Veneziano, I need to find Mercier. Do you have any way to contact him?"

"Only an address in Marseille. He used it for correspondence." Yusuf stood and went to his desk, returning with a scrap of paper. "Rue de la Paix, number 17."

Leyla took the paper. "Thank you."

"I have one question," Kemal said. "Your lamp — the replica. What did you use for the blue?"

Yusuf smiled, a ghost of the young man who had left Istanbul forty years ago. "Our grandfather's formula. Cobalt from Kütahya, silver leaf from the Grand Bazaar. I have never used anything else."

Chapter 32: Whitmore's Choice

While Leyla and Kemal were in Venice, Sergeant Hasan had been watching Charles Whitmore. The diplomat's routine had changed — he no longer went to the embassy, no longer dined at the Pera Palas, no longer took his evening walk along the Grande Rue. He stayed in his apartment with the curtains drawn, emerging only to buy bread and newspapers.

On the third day of the Venice trip, Hasan telephoned Leyla's hotel. "Whitmore's packing. He's booked passage on a steamer to Alexandria, departing tomorrow morning."

"Don't let him leave."

"I can't stop him without an arrest warrant, and the prosecutor won't issue one without more evidence."

Leyla thought for a moment. "Tell him I'm on my way back from Venice with new information. Tell him if he leaves now, he'll be a fugitive, and I'll have Interpol waiting at every port between Alexandria and London."

"Will you?"

"No. But he doesn't know that."

She hung up and turned to Kemal, who was reading in the hotel lobby. "We need to go back. Whitmore's running."

"What about Mercier?"

"The French police can handle Mercier. Whitmore is our best witness — and possibly our best suspect." She was already walking toward the door. "I'll send the Marseille address to the Sûreté. If Mercier's there, they'll find him."

The train to Milan left in two hours. If they made the connection in Milan, they could be in Istanbul in three days. Three days for Whitmore to change his mind, or to run, or to do something desperate.

Leyla had been a police officer long enough to know that desperate men were the most dangerous kind.

Chapter 33: The Road to Venice

The return journey was a reversal of the outward one, but nothing felt the same. On the way to Venice, Kemal had been silent with anticipation — the long-deferred meeting with his uncle, the possibility of answers to questions he had carried since childhood. On the way back, he was silent with something else. Resolution, perhaps. Or grief.

"He told me the truth," Kemal said, watching the Lombardy countryside slide past the train window. "My father and his brother — they destroyed each other over pride. Over who was the better glassmaker. And for what? Now I'm the only one left in Istanbul, and my uncle is an old man in a foreign country, and neither of them ever forgave the other."

"Do you forgive him?"

"I don't know." The fields were green, stitched with poplars. "He took the journals. He built his reputation on stolen knowledge. But he also told us about Mercier. He didn't have to."

"Maybe he wanted to help."

"Maybe he wanted to be part of the family again." Kemal turned from the window. "What will happen to him? Legally, I mean."

"Probably nothing. He was commissioned to make a replica. He didn't know it would be used for fraud. The French authorities might want a statement, but he hasn't committed a crime in Italy." She paused. "Unless there's more he hasn't told us."

"You think there is?"

"I think everyone in this case has been holding something back." Leyla opened her notebook. "Your uncle included."

Chapter 34: Murano

The last morning in Venice, before the telegram from Hasan reached them, Leyla had gone back to Murano alone. She wanted to ask Yusuf one more question that she had not wanted to ask in front of Kemal.

The old man was working when she arrived, shaping a gather of clear glass into a goblet. He did not look up.

"The imitation that was broken at the crime scene," she said. "The cheap one. You said someone copied your copy. Who could have done that?"

Yusuf kept turning the pipe. "Anyone who saw my work. I sent photographs to Mercier during the commission. Progress images, to show him the technique. If he had those photographs, he could have shown them to another glassmaker."

"Someone in Istanbul?"

"Possibly. But you'd need someone who could work quickly, who didn't ask questions, who had access to materials and a furnace." He set down the pipe and met her eyes. "The Özden workshop has an apprentice, I'm told. A nephew."

"Evren."

"I don't know the boy. But I know what it is to be the younger one, the one who wants to prove himself, the one who will take commissions the master refuses." He turned back to his work. "Ask him. Ask him if a Frenchman came to him with a photograph and a handful of money."

Leyla had wanted to ask more, but the vaporetto was leaving in twenty minutes, and she had a train to catch. She thanked Yusuf and walked back to the jetty, the question burning in her mind.

Evren. The apprentice. The one with the warehouse and the sideline and the debts. The one who had been invisible in his own family's workshop.

She should have asked him sooner.

Chapter 35: The Commission

The train from Venice arrived in Istanbul on a wet Tuesday evening. Leyla went directly to the police station, where Hasan was waiting with a stack of messages and the news that Whitmore had, after all, decided not to board the steamer to Alexandria.

"He's still in the city. He said he'd wait for your return." Hasan hesitated. "There's something else. I questioned Evren Özden while you were away. About the imitation lantern."

"And?"

"He made it. Three months ago. A man approached him — a Frenchman, Cemil Bey — with a photograph of a Sultan's Eye lantern and asked if he could make a copy. Evren said yes. He used cheaper materials — Prussian blue instead of cobalt — to save money. He didn't know what it was for."

Leyla sat down heavily. "So the broken lantern at the scene — Evren made it. Mercier planted it. And Whitmore removed the real one."

"It appears so."

"Where is Evren now?"

"At the workshop. His uncle doesn't know yet. I thought you should be the one to tell him."

Leyla looked at the clock. It was nearly nine. Kemal would be home by now, reunited with Defne, unpacking from the journey. The news could wait until morning.

But morning came sooner than she expected.

Chapter 36: The Handwriting

Leyla spent the next day comparing documents under a magnifying lamp in her office. On one side: the commission letters Yusuf had given her in Venice, written in French, requesting the Ahmed III replica. On the other: embassy correspondence from Whitmore's files, including a memo he had written to Harker about cultural fund allocations.

The handwriting matched.

She sat back and rubbed her eyes. Whitmore had commissioned the replica. Whitmore had known the lamp was a forgery from the beginning. And Whitmore had lied about it — lied about knowing Mercier's real identity, lied about his role in the smuggling operation, lied about everything except his own cowardice.

She called Hasan. "Bring Whitmore in. Now. No more conversations in his apartment."

This time, when Charles Whitmore sat across from her in the interview room, he was not offered tea. The commission letters lay on the table between them.

"Your handwriting," Leyla said. "You commissioned the replica from Yusuf Özden in Venice. You knew the lamp was a forgery before Harker did. You were never the reluctant accomplice. You were the architect."

Whitmore looked at the letters. His face had aged five years in the past week.

"Yes," he said. "I commissioned it."

"Why?"

"Because Pemberton was threatening to go to the Foreign Office. He'd paid nearly thirty thousand pounds over two years for artifacts I'd certified as genuine. If he discovered half of them were forgeries, his lawyers would tear the embassy apart. I needed one big piece — something so valuable, so authenticated, that it would distract him from the others."

"The Ahmed III lamp."

"A genuine lost masterpiece. Worth a fortune. If I could produce it — with proper documentation, properly certified — Pemberton would be satisfied. The other forgeries would be forgotten. I commissioned Yusuf through Mercier because he was the only glassmaker alive who could pull it off."

"But Harker found out."

"Harker was supposed to be the front man. He was supposed to present the lamp to Pemberton as a legitimate find. But he got suspicious. He had Halit — the translator — examine the documents. Halit told him they were forged. Harker confronted me the night he died."

"And you killed him."

"No." Whitmore's voice was steady. "I argued with him. I tried to make him see reason. He wouldn't. He said he was going to the consul general in the morning. I left him alive. I swear it."

"Then who killed him?"

"I don't know. Mercier, perhaps. Or someone working for Mercier. Or"—he hesitated—"or Margaret Harker. His wife. She was in Istanbul that night. Did you know that?"

"I know." Leyla stood. "You've lied to me repeatedly, Mr. Whitmore. You've admitted to fraud, forgery, and conspiracy. You removed evidence from a murder scene. Whether or not you held the knife, you are culpable."

"Arrest me, then."

"I intend to." She nodded to the uniformed officer at the door. "Charles Whitmore, you are detained pending charges of fraud, conspiracy to traffic in stolen antiquities, and obstruction of justice. The murder charge may follow."

Whitmore did not resist. He held out his wrists for the handcuffs with the same passive grace he had shown throughout the investigation — a man for whom consequences had always been theoretical until they were not.

Chapter 37: The Conspiracy

With Whitmore in custody, the conspiracy came into focus. Leyla spent two days taking statements, assembling documents, mapping the network that had moved Ottoman artifacts from Istanbul to London for two years.

The structure was clear now. Whitmore controlled the money, using embassy cultural funds as a slush account. Harker was the front man, the diplomatic face who met with dealers and collectors. Mercier — Cemil Bey — sourced the artifacts, some genuine, some forged, all funneled through Damascus to avoid Turkish customs. Madame Eleni supplied the provenance documents that made everything look legitimate. Evren Özden, unknowingly, had provided the decoy lanterns. And Yusuf Özden, also unknowingly, had created the centerpiece — the Ahmed III replica that was supposed to be the final, triumphant sale.

But the replica was too good. Harker grew suspicious. He had the documents examined. He realized the fraud. And the whole edifice began to crack.

"Where is Mercier now?" Leyla asked Hasan.

"Last seen in Damascus, according to the French police. But that was a week ago. He could be anywhere."

"Keep looking. He's the only one who can tell us what happened in that room."

And there was still Margaret Harker. The widow. The woman in the blue coat.

Leyla picked up the telephone and asked for the Hotel Moda.

Chapter 38: The Return

Kemal went back to the workshop the morning after his return from Venice. The furnace had been cold for nearly two weeks — the longest it had been silent since his father's death. He opened the iron door, fed in the first coals, and waited for the fire to catch.

Evren came in at seven, earlier than usual. He stood in the doorway, watching his uncle work.

"I need to tell you something," he said.

"The imitation lantern. The one at the murder scene." Kemal didn't turn around. "You made it."

"I didn't know what it was for. Cemil Bey — Mercier — he showed me a photograph. He said he needed a copy of the Sultan's Eye pattern for a client who couldn't afford the real thing. It was good money. I didn't ask questions."

"You should have asked."

"I know." Evren's voice cracked. "I've been a fool. I wanted to be independent. I wanted to prove I was as good as you. And I almost got you arrested for murder."

Kemal turned. The fire was catching now, the coals glowing orange through the iron grate.

"You're my nephew. You're my apprentice. You made a mistake." He picked up a pipe from the rack. "But the glass doesn't care about our mistakes. It only cares about the heat."

"What does that mean?"

"It means you're still here. You're still learning. And the furnace is lit." He held out the pipe. "Now fetch the cobalt rods. We have work to do."

Evren took the pipe. His hand trembled, then steadied.

"The real Sultan's Eye lantern — the one Whitmore took. Is it still at the embassy?"

"Leyla has it now. Evidence."

"When the trial is over, I want to buy it back. For the workshop."

Kemal looked at his nephew — the boy who had made so many wrong choices, who had been seduced by easy money and the promise of independence, who had confessed rather than been caught.

"We'll buy it back together," he said. "Now work."

Chapter 39: Defne's Courage

Defne had not spoken to her father about Venice. She was angry at being left behind, angry at being treated like a child when she had been the one who saw the argument outside the Pera Palas, the one who drew the sketch that identified Whitmore. Her evidence had been crucial. And her reward was to be told to stay home.

She took her anger to the Academy and put it into her work. Charcoal studies of the light falling through the high windows of the drawing studio. Watercolor sketches of the Bosphorus at dusk. A series of small oils on board — hands, always hands, in different positions: reaching, holding, shaping.

Her instructor, a Frenchwoman named Madame Clément, looked at the series and said, "These are your father's hands."

"Yes."

"They're very good. But you're hiding behind technique. Where is the feeling?"

Defne looked at the paintings. The hands were anatomically correct, carefully shaded, expertly composed. But they were hollow. They showed the shape of her father's hands without showing the man.

"I don't know how to paint what I'm feeling," she said.

"Then don't paint it. Let it paint itself." Madame Clément touched her shoulder. "Art is not about control, Defne. It's about letting the light in."

That evening, Defne went home and began a new canvas. Not hands this time. A lantern — her father's signature Sultan's Eye pattern, hanging in a dark room, its light falling on the face of a woman in a blue coat. She painted until midnight, and when she finished, she understood what she had been trying to say.

The woman in the sketch outside the Pera Palas. The woman in the blue coat. She was not just a witness.

She was the key.

Defne called the police station. The inspector needed to see her. Tonight.

Chapter 40: The Woman in Blue

Margaret Harker was staying at a small hotel in Moda, on the Asian shore, a neighborhood of wooden houses and quiet gardens far from the diplomatic bustle of Pera. She had been there for three weeks, ever since the night her husband died.

Leyla found her in the hotel's garden, sitting on a bench under a Judas tree that was just beginning to bud. She was a handsome woman of forty, with graying hair pulled back in a simple knot and eyes that had seen too much and shown too little.

"Mrs. Harker. I'm Inspector Leyla Aksoy."

"I know who you are." The woman did not rise. "I've been expecting you."

"You were in Istanbul the night your husband was killed. You were seen outside the Pera Palas Hotel. Why didn't you come forward?"

Margaret Harker looked at the bare branches above her. "Because I was ashamed. And because I thought no one would believe me."

"Believe what?"

"That I came here to confront him. That I had finally worked up the courage to leave him." She folded her hands in her lap. "Geoffrey and I had not been husband and wife in any real sense for years. He lived here. I lived in Sussex with my sister. We corresponded — polite letters about weather and health. He sent money. I spent it."

"What changed?"

"A letter, six months ago. From a woman in Istanbul. A Turkish woman. She said she was in love with my husband, that he had promised to marry her." Margaret's voice was steady, almost clinical. "I came to Istanbul to ask him, face to face, whether it was true. I arrived the afternoon of the day he died."

"Did you see him?"

"I was going to. I followed him from the embassy to the Pera Palas. I saw him arguing with another man outside the hotel — Whitmore, I know now. I was waiting for the right moment to approach him. But he left before I could. I followed him home. And when I got there"—she closed her eyes—"I saw someone leaving through the back gate. A man, limping. Carrying a blue lantern."

"Whitmore."

"Yes. I waited until he was gone. I went in through the back gate — it was still open. And I found Geoffrey in his study. He was already dead."

Leyla studied the woman's face. No tears. No trembling. Just the flat composure of someone who had exhausted her emotions long ago.

"You didn't report it."

"I panicked. I thought — I was his estranged wife, in the city without telling him, arriving at his house minutes after he was killed. Who would believe I was innocent?" She met Leyla's eyes. "So I went back to my hotel. And I've been waiting ever since. Waiting for you to find me."

"Did you see anyone else? Anyone other than Whitmore?"

Margaret was quiet for a long moment. "There was someone. A man, on the street, watching the house. He was there when I arrived and still there when I left. He was smoking a cigarette in a doorway across the road. I didn't think anything of it at the time — it's a city, people smoke in doorways. But he was watching. I'm sure of it now."

"Can you describe him?"

"Dark coat. Dark hair. Middle-aged. He had a scar — here." She touched her left cheek. "A thin line, like a knife wound."

Leyla felt her pulse quicken. The description matched the photograph in Mercier's file from the French Sûreté.

Claude Mercier had been at the house. He had been watching when Whitmore came and went. And he had been there when Margaret Harker arrived and fled.

Had he gone inside? Had he been the one who killed Harker — or had he arrived after Whitmore left, found Harker alive, and finished what the argument had started?

There was only one person who could answer that question.

Chapter 41: Mrs. Harker

They talked for another hour in the hotel garden. Margaret Harker's story, once she began telling it, came out in pieces that didn't always fit together — the chronology confused, the emotions suppressed, the details sharp but disconnected.

She had married Geoffrey in 1910, when she was twenty-five and he was a junior clerk at the Foreign Office. He had been charming then, full of ambition. The war had changed him — not the fighting (he never served; a heart murmur kept him behind a desk) but the aftermath, the collapse of the world they had grown up in. He had taken the Constantinople posting in 1922, and she had stayed in England, and somehow the distance had become permanent.

"The letters kept coming," she said. "Every week. 'My dear Margaret, the weather is fine, the work is interesting, I hope you are well.' The same words, over and over. As if I were his aunt, not his wife."

"When did you learn about the other woman?"

"Six months ago. An anonymous letter, posted from Istanbul. It said my husband was involved with a woman named Serap, a secretary at the Ottoman Bank. That he had promised to marry her once he obtained a divorce." She paused. "Geoffrey had never mentioned divorce to me. I didn't even know he wanted one."

"Was the letter true?"

"I don't know. I never found the woman. After Geoffrey died, I stopped looking."

Leyla made a note to find Serap, if she existed. Another witness. Another thread.

"Mrs. Harker, I need to ask you directly: did you have any involvement in your husband's death?"

"No." For the first time, Margaret's composure cracked. "I was angry. I was humiliated. I wanted to hurt him — divorce him publicly, ruin his career, make him feel as small as he'd made me feel. But I didn't want him dead. He was still my husband. For fifteen years, he was my husband."

Leyla closed her notebook. "I believe you. But you should have come forward sooner."

"I know. I was a coward."

"You were human." Leyla stood. "I'll need a formal statement, at the station. And you'll need to remain in Istanbul until the investigation is complete."

"I understand."

Leyla walked to the garden gate, then turned back. "The woman at the Pera Palas doorway — the one Defne Özden sketched. She was you."

"Yes." Margaret's voice was barely audible. "Standing in the doorway, watching my husband argue with another man about money. That was the moment I knew our marriage was truly over. Not when I read the letter. Not when I crossed Europe by train. That moment, in the doorway."

Chapter 42: The Wife's Testimony

Margaret Harker's formal statement took four hours. She described everything: the anonymous letter, the journey from England, the surveillance of her husband's movements, the scene outside the Pera Palas, the figure limping from the back gate, the body in the study, the flight back to the hotel.

She had not touched anything in the study. She had not called for help. She had stood over her husband's body for perhaps thirty seconds — she couldn't be sure of the time — and then she had walked back to the hotel in Moda, crossing the Bosphorus on the last ferry of the night, and she had sat in her room and waited.

"For what?" Leyla asked.

"For someone to arrest me. Or for me to wake up and discover it was a dream." She looked at her hands. "Neither happened."

"Did you see the broken glass in the study? The lantern?"

"I saw glass on the floor. I didn't look closely. I was looking at Geoffrey."

"The lantern was intact when Whitmore left with it. By the time the housekeeper found the body the next morning, a different lantern — a broken one — was at the scene. Someone entered the house between Whitmore's departure and the housekeeper's arrival. Someone who brought an imitation lantern and smashed it."

"To make it look like a burglary?"

"Perhaps. Or to implicate someone specific." Leyla thought of Kemal. The broken lantern with his workshop's mark — a copy, but convincing enough to draw police attention to him. If Whitmore hadn't taken the real lantern, the police might never have looked further than the glassmaker whose work was found at the scene.

"The Frenchman," Margaret said. "The one with the scar. You think he did it."

"Claude Mercier. Yes." Leyla closed her statement file. "He had the most to lose if Harker exposed the operation. He had access to the imitation lantern — he'd commissioned it. And he was watching the house that night."

"Then why didn't he kill Whitmore too? Whitmore knew as much as Harker."

"Because Whitmore was still useful. Whitmore controlled the money." Leyla stood. "But Whitmore's usefulness ended when we arrested him. Mercier knows that. Which means he's either running — or he's coming back to tie up loose ends."

Chapter 43: The Running Figure

The French Sûreté responded to Leyla's cable within forty-eight hours. Claude Mercier was not in Marseille. His last known address — the one Yusuf had given them — was a mail drop, emptied months ago. Neighbors had not seen him since before Christmas.

But he had not been idle. The Sûreté had intercepted a letter posted from Istanbul to an address in Beirut, written in Mercier's handwriting, dated five days after Harker's death. The letter was brief and businesslike:

"Shipment delayed. Buyer complications. Contact me through the usual channel in Aleppo after the fifteenth. Burning the Istanbul address. Do not write again."

Burning the address. The language of a professional.

"He's still in the region," Leyla told Hasan. "He didn't flee to Europe. He went east — Syria, probably, or Lebanon. Somewhere he has contacts."

"Should we alert the authorities there?"

"Alert everyone. Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, Egypt. Send the photograph, the description, the list of aliases. Someone will recognize him."

But the borders were porous, and Mercier knew them better than any policeman. A man with his resources could disappear into the Levant and never be found.

Unless he made a mistake.

"He'll come back," Leyla said. "There's too much money involved. The lamp — the real replica, the one Yusuf made — is still out there. Mercier has it somewhere. And it's worth twenty thousand pounds to the right buyer. He won't walk away from that."

Chapter 44: The Cable from London

The diplomatic cable arrived on a Friday morning, stamped with the seals of both the Foreign Office and Scotland Yard. Leyla read it standing in the station corridor, a cup of cooling tea forgotten in her other hand.

PEMBERTON ARRESTED YESTERDAY AT DOVER ATTEMPTING TO BOARD CROSS-CHANNEL FERRY WITH UNDECLARED ARTIFACTS. THREE OTTOMAN GLASS PIECES SEIZED INCLUDING ONE MOSQUE LAMP MATCHING DESCRIPTION OF AHMED III REPLICA. PEMBERTON CLAIMS LAMP DELIVERED BY COURIER FROM CONSTANTINOPLE THREE DAYS AGO. COURIER DESCRIPTION MATCHES CLAUDE MERCIER. MERCIER NOT APPREHENDED. INVESTIGATION CONTINUING.

She lowered the cable. Mercier had been in London. He had delivered the lamp to Pemberton personally, despite the murder, despite the investigation, despite everything. The man was either fearless or desperate.

Probably desperate.

She called Hasan. "Whitmore knows more than he's told us. He knew Mercier was planning to deliver the lamp. He may even have helped arrange the courier."

"Should I question him again?"

"Not yet. Let me think." She walked to her office and closed the door.

If Mercier had been in London three days ago, he could be anywhere by now. But the cable said he had not been apprehended — he had delivered the lamp and vanished, presumably before Pemberton was arrested. A man like Mercier would have a plan for this. He would have travel documents in multiple names, caches of money, safe houses across Europe and the Middle East.

But he had also delivered the lamp. He had fulfilled the transaction, even with Harker dead, Whitmore arrested, and the police closing in. Why?

Because he needed the money. Or because he was tying up the last loose end before disappearing permanently.

Or because he had an accomplice he was still protecting.

Leyla sat very still. An accomplice. Someone inside the investigation. Someone who had been feeding Mercier information about the police's progress, about Whitmore's arrest, about the trip to Venice.

Someone like — no. She stopped herself. Paranoia was the enemy of good police work.

But she made a note in her file: "Leak? Check who knew about Venice trip before departure."

Chapter 45: The Hunt

The search for Claude Mercier expanded across three continents. Leyla's cables went to police forces in Turkey, Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, Egypt, France, Italy, and Britain. Interpol circulated his photograph and description. The file grew daily — new aliases discovered, old associates located and questioned, a paper trail of fraudulent transactions and forged documents stretching back a decade.

But Mercier himself remained invisible.

"His network is too good," Leyla told the prosecutor during a case review. "He was operating in Istanbul for eight months under a false name, running a smuggling operation that moved artifacts worth tens of thousands of pounds, and no one noticed until someone died."

"The French should have warned us about him."

"The French didn't know he was here. They thought he was in Marseille." She spread her hands. "The system worked exactly as Mercier intended it to. Different names in different countries. Different identities, different networks. By the time anyone connects the dots, he's already moved on."

"Then how do we catch him?"

"The same way you catch any fugitive. You wait for him to make a mistake. And you make sure that when he does, you're there."

The prosecutor, a weary man named Özdemir who had seen too many cases end in dusty files and administrative closure, nodded slowly. "And if he doesn't make a mistake?"

"They all make a mistake. It's only a question of time."

But privately, Leyla was less certain. Mercier had been doing this for a decade. He had been dismissed from two colonial administrations, investigated by three police forces, and had still managed to build a new identity and a new operation in Istanbul within six months. He was not just a criminal. He was a professional.

And professionals, Leyla knew, sometimes got away.

Chapter 46: The Arrest

It was a customs officer in Aleppo who finally caught him. Not a policeman, not a detective, but a middle-aged Syrian official named Adnan who noticed that the French passport presented by a traveling wine merchant had a photograph that didn't quite match the man holding it.

The man claimed to be Jean Moreau, wine exporter from Bordeaux. But when Adnan asked him to name the mayor of Bordeaux, the man hesitated just long enough. Adnan pressed a button under his desk. Two policemen arrived.

The arrest was quiet, undramatic — a man in a cheap suit handcuffed in a customs office, his false passport on the table, his expression more annoyed than afraid. He gave his name as Claude Mercier and asked to speak with the French consul.

The French consul, having been briefed by the Sûreté, declined the meeting. Mercier was held in a police cell in Aleppo for three days while the extradition paperwork was processed. Then he was put on a train to Istanbul, escorted by two Syrian policemen and a French detective who had flown in from Paris.

Leyla met the train at Haydarpaşa Station. Mercier stepped onto the platform in handcuffs, blinked in the pale March sunlight, and looked at her with something approaching curiosity.

"Inspector Aksoy, I presume."

"Mr. Mercier. Welcome back to Istanbul."

"I can't say I'm happy to be here."

"You killed a man. That tends to make returns awkward."

Mercier smiled, a thin expression that didn't reach his eyes. "You have evidence of that, do you?"

"I have enough." Leyla nodded to the escort. "Let's go. We have a lot to talk about."

Chapter 47: The Confession

The interview room was small and gray, with a single window too high to see through. Mercier sat on one side of the table, Leyla on the other. A police stenographer sat in the corner, pen poised.

"I killed him," Mercier said without preamble. "But not the way you think."

"Tell me."

He leaned back in his chair. He was calmer than any suspect Leyla had ever interviewed — a man who had accepted his situation and was now simply negotiating the terms.

"Harker was going to expose everything. Not just the forgeries — the whole network. Pemberton, Whitmore, Madame Eleni, the shipping routes, the Damascus warehouse. He had documents, he said. Copies of everything. He was going to take them to the consul general and then to your police, and he was going to trade immunity for testimony."

"That's what the argument with Whitmore was about."

"Yes. Whitmore tried to talk him out of it. Failed. Went home. I was watching — I'd been following Harker for days, trying to figure out how to stop him." Mercier paused. "I went to the house after Whitmore left. The back gate was open. I found Harker in his study, packing a briefcase. The documents were on his desk."

"And?"

"And I told him I'd destroy him. He laughed. He said I was a French criminal with no standing, no credibility, no friends in the embassy. He was right, of course. So I picked up the letter opener from his desk."

"The murder weapon."

"He tried to grab it. We struggled. It went into his chest." Mercier looked at his hands. "I didn't mean to kill him. I meant to threaten him. But once it was done, I had to contain the damage."

"You brought the imitation lantern. The one Evren Özden made."

"I had it in my car. I'd picked it up from the warehouse earlier that day — I was going to use it as a sample for another buyer. After Harker died, I went back to the car and got it. I smashed it near the window — to make it look like a break-in gone wrong, and to point the police toward the Özden workshop. Kemal had refused the commission. I thought he deserved some inconvenience."

"You framed an innocent man."

"I did many things that night, Inspector. Framing a glassmaker was among the least of them." He met her eyes. "I also took Harker's documents. The copies he was going to give the consul general. I burned them in the furnace at the warehouse. The network survived because of what I did."

"Not entirely. Whitmore is in custody. Pemberton's been arrested in London. The network is finished."

"Parts of it." Mercier nodded. "But I was the core. And I got away — for a while."

Leyla sat back. "Why did you deliver the lamp to Pemberton? Three weeks after the murder, with the police hunting you, you personally delivered the forgery to London. Why?"

"Because a deal is a deal." Mercier smiled again, that thin, cold expression. "And because I knew you'd be watching the ports, the banks, the known associates. You weren't watching the London train. Pemberton paid me in cash, at Dover, an hour before he was arrested. I was in Paris by nightfall."

"But you didn't stay in Paris."

"No. I had business in Aleppo. A client who didn't care about the provenance problems." He shrugged. "It seems I underestimated the Syrian customs service."

Leyla closed her notebook. "You're charged with murder, Mr. Mercier. And forgery, and smuggling, and conspiracy. You'll likely spend the rest of your life in prison."

"I know." He was calm, almost serene. "I've been running for ten years, Inspector. It's exhausting. In a strange way, this is a relief."

The stenographer's pen stopped. The room was silent.

Chapter 48: The Glass Lantern Reclaimed

The Ahmed III replica — Yusuf Özden's masterwork — was returned to Istanbul in a padded crate, escorted by a Scotland Yard detective who had drawn the short straw and spent four days on trains crossing Europe. The lamp arrived at the Topkapı Museum on a Tuesday morning in April, and the curator, Dr. Aslan, wept.

"It's not the original," he said, holding the lamp to the light. "But it's the closest thing we have. The craftsmanship — look at the silver leaf, the blue, the arabesque line. This is Ottoman glassmaking at its finest, even if it was made in Venice by a man who left Istanbul forty years ago."

Kemal had been invited to the ceremony. He stood in the museum's marble hall, surrounded by journalists and officials, and watched the lamp being placed in a display case beside the two genuine Ahmed III lamps. The three pieces together — two originals and a replica — told a story that was more complicated than the museum's placard could convey. A story about art and commerce, about family and exile, about what we preserve and what we lose.

"The glassmaker who made this," Dr. Aslan announced to the assembled press, "is Giuseppe Veneziano of Murano, born Yusuf Özden of Istanbul. He is the brother of the late master Mehmet Özden and uncle of the current master Kemal Özden, who assisted the police in this investigation. The lamp will be displayed as an example of the living tradition of Ottoman glassmaking, carried forward by the Özden family across three generations."

Kemal stepped forward to answer questions. The journalists wanted details about the murder, the investigation, his role in identifying the forgery. He answered as briefly as he could.

"The glass told the truth," he said. "It always does. You just have to know how to see it."

After the ceremony, Dr. Aslan took him aside. "We would be honored to commission a piece from you, Mr. Özden. A new work, for the museum's collection."

Kemal considered. "Not a replica. Something new."

"Whatever you wish."

He walked home through the streets of Sultanahmet, past the Blue Mosque and the Hippodrome, past the remnants of empires. The city was changing — the republic was three years old, the caliphate abolished, the Latin alphabet replacing the Arabic script. Everything his father had known was being swept away. And yet the glass remained. The formulas, the techniques, the fire.

He would make something new for the museum. Something that belonged to the future, not the past.

Chapter 49: The Workshop Renewed

Spring came to Beyoğlu in a rush of jasmine and warm rain. Kemal opened the workshop doors wide and let the air move through, carrying the smells of the street — roasting chestnuts, sea salt, the faint perfume of linden blossoms from the embassy gardens.

Evren was at the furnace, shaping a gather of cobalt glass. He had been working since dawn, his concentration absolute, his hands moving with a confidence they had lacked before. The arrest, the confession, the public exposure of his sideline — all of it had burned away the arrogance and left something better behind. Humility. Or perhaps just the beginning of wisdom.

"The curve is off," Kemal said, looking at the nascent lantern.

"I know. The gather was uneven."

"Then add more. The glass wants to be round. Let it."

Evren returned the pipe to the furnace, gathered more glass, and tried again. This time the curve was true.

Defne arrived at midmorning with news. She had been accepted to the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris, starting in September. A scholarship, funded by the new republican government, for promising Turkish artists.

"I'm going to study art restoration," she said. "Like we talked about. Protecting beautiful things, not just making them."

Kemal set down his pipe and embraced his daughter. She was taller than her mother had been, and fiercer, and more certain of her path. Emine would have been proud.

"Paris is far," he said.

"Not so far. There are trains. And letters." She touched his face. "You'll be all right."

"I know." He looked at the workshop — the furnace burning, Evren at the marver, the shelves of finished glass catching the morning light. "There's work to do."

That evening, Leyla came by the workshop for the first time since the arrest. She was off duty, in a plain dress instead of her uniform, and she looked younger without the weight of the investigation on her shoulders.

"I wanted to thank you," she said. "Your knowledge of the glass — the cobalt, the formulas, your uncle. Without it, I might not have found the truth."

"You would have found it. You're a good detective."

"I try to be." She looked around the workshop. "May I see how it's done?"

Kemal led her to the furnace. He took a fresh gather of clear glass on the pipe and began to shape it — a simple goblet, nothing elaborate. Leyla watched the glass take form under his hands, the liquid fire becoming solid, the shape emerging from the heat.

"It's beautiful," she said.

"It's just a glass. But yes — there's beauty in the process." He set the finished piece on the cooling rack. "Stay for tea. Defne is making börek."

They sat in the courtyard as the evening settled over Beyoğlu. The call to prayer sounded from the mosque on the hill. Evren lit the workshop lanterns — the real ones, Kemal's work, the Sultan's Eye pattern in amber and blue. The glass caught the last light of the sun and held it, glowing against the darkening sky.

"Glass remembers the fire," Kemal said. "But it also remembers the hands that shaped it."

Leyla looked at the lanterns. "I used to think of glass as fragile. Something that breaks."

"It does break. But it also holds light." He poured more tea. "That's the paradox of the work. You spend your life making things that can shatter in an instant. And yet you keep making them. Because the light is worth it."

Chapter 50: The Light

On the last evening of April, Kemal hung the new lantern in the workshop window. It was his own design — not a replica, not a commission, not a copy of his father's work or his grandfather's. The pattern was new: a spiral of clear and amber glass, the colors twisting around each other like threads of light, the whole thing suspended from a simple iron chain.

He had been working on it for three weeks, in the hours between commissions and teaching Evren and preparing Defne for Paris. It was the first piece he had made entirely for himself since his wife died.

The workshop was empty. Evren had taken the evening off — his first in months — to meet friends at a café in Galata. Defne was at home, packing her trunk for Paris. The street was quiet, the shops closed, the tram clattering past in the distance.

Kemal lit the candle inside the lantern and stepped back. The spiral caught the flame and scattered it — amber warmth, clear brightness, the two twisting together across the workshop walls.

He thought about the last two months. The body in Pera, the broken glass, the accusation that had nearly destroyed him. The long trail that had led to Venice and back. His uncle's face — so like his father's, and yet so different. The formula book, returned now to its place in the locked drawer, no longer the only copy in the world.

He thought about Whitmore, awaiting trial in a prison cell. Mercier, extradited to France to face charges there. Madame Eleni, who had closed her shop and returned to Greece, leaving only a note that said, "I am too old for this kind of trouble." Margaret Harker, who had gone back to England on a steamer, carrying her husband's ashes in a plain wooden box.

He thought about Leyla, who had believed him — or at least had believed the evidence — and who had done her work with a thoroughness he admired.

And he thought about the glass. The fire. The light.

There was a knock at the door. Leyla stood in the courtyard, a bottle of wine in her hand.

"I'm off duty," she said. "And I wanted to see the new lantern."

He led her inside. She stood before the window and watched the spiral turning in its slow thermal current, the amber and clear glass casting twin shadows on the wall.

"It's different from your other work," she said.

"Yes."

"Why?"

He considered the question. "Because it's mine. Not my grandfather's, not my father's. Not made for an embassy or a collector or a museum. Just mine."

"The investigation changed you."

"The investigation reminded me of something I had forgotten." He took the lantern down from its chain and held it between them, the candle still burning. "That glass is made from sand and fire and breath. The simplest things in the world. And yet it becomes something that holds light."

Leyla reached out and touched the lantern. The glass was still warm.

"Will you make another?" she asked. "For me?"

Kemal looked at her, and for the first time since his wife died, he saw a future that was not just the repetition of days — the furnace, the pipe, the cooling rack, the empty house.

"Yes," he said. "I think I will."

He hung the lantern back in the window. Outside, the city settled into darkness. Inside, the spiral turned and the light held steady, and in the workshop on the hill above the Golden Horn, the glassmaker was no longer alone.
