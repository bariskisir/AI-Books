Chapter 1: Shepherd Burn

Kira Nalen called her tug Little Argument because it won only by persistence. The tug was small, scarred, and fitted with capture arms that looked too delicate until they closed around dead metal.

Her target was a tumbling cubesat two kilometers ahead. Retired university weather package, no fuel, no attitude control, drifting toward a traffic lane used by hospital supply capsules. Routine work, the kind that paid badly and kept worse things from happening. The kind Kira trusted more than speeches.

The cockpit smelled faintly of warm plastic and old coffee. A Velcro strip held a cracked photograph of the tug on its commissioning day, before micrometeorite scars peppered the hull and before Kira learned which bolts liked to complain in thermal swing. The main display showed the cubesat as a white bracket, rolling once every seven seconds. Its little solar wings flashed like a fish in deep water.

"Range twelve hundred," Joss Ader said from ground. His voice came through clean, dry, and awake in the way only traffic mathematicians sounded awake. "Relative spin steady. You are fat on approach by point two."

"I am giving it room to feel respected."

"Respect it on vector."

Kira tapped the side thruster twice. Little Argument answered with a soft shove. The belt across her chest tightened. Earth turned below, blue and indifferent, cloud bands bright over a morning ocean. The hospital corridor lay ahead of her by time, not distance: a scheduled lane used by automated capsules ferrying marrow packs, transplant coolers, high-pressure pumps, spare filters. People on the ground imagined orbit as empty. Kira spent her life proving them wrong.

"Range eight hundred," Joss said. "Relative spin still steady."

"I see it."

She brought the tug in nose-low, matching tumble with a patient correction. The capture arm woke from its cradle. Joint lights climbed from amber to green. The arm was long, matte, and slightly ridiculous, like a praying mantis limb bolted to a delivery truck. Pavel Xu, who had built half of it and insulted the other half into obedience, claimed it had character. Kira claimed character was what engineers called poor documentation.

She extended the arm a meter, then three. The cubesat widened in the forward camera. Its university decal was sun-bleached to a ghost. Somewhere, a doctoral student had loved it. Somewhere else, a spreadsheet had declared it expendable.

"Closing at three centimeters," Joss said.

"That is not closing. That is introducing myself."

"Your introduction is drifting left."

Kira corrected. The tug's frame gave a small familiar creak. Her hand rested light on the rotational controller. She could feel the machine through the gloves and seat, not as romance, but as accumulated habit: valve lag, software caution, the old tremor in pump two when it disliked a burn.

Then three fragments crossed her lane from below.

They were too fast for shape. Bright cuts. One passed through the space she had planned to occupy in four seconds. Another clipped the edge of the lidar cone and vanished into black. The third flared in reflected sunlight and was gone.

Collision alarms turned the cockpit red.

Kira cut thrust. Little Argument bucked as the automatic avoidance system tried to add its own opinion. She overrode it before it spent fuel in three directions at once.

"Traffic, I have unscheduled debris in C-seven."

Joss's voice changed. The dry humor left it. "No debris cataloged there."

"Then catalog it now."

"Vector?"

"Fast prograde-crossing, rising from below. Three objects. Small. Maybe five to fifteen centimeters."

"That is not in the shell model."

"I noticed."

The cubesat drifted past the capture basket, suddenly irrelevant. Kira pulled the arm back halfway. The tug's cameras searched the black. Another fragment came up from Earth's bright edge, larger than the first three. It turned end over end, slow enough for the optics to catch torn white plating, a strip of black thermal blanket, and lettering.

"I have a fourth," she said.

It passed between tug and target close enough to make Little Argument's proximity sensors squeal. On the freeze-frame, a Helix Lift serial plate tumbled through sun, numbers clean on metal that should have been safely dead.

Joss was silent for one beat too long.

"Ground," Kira said, "tell me you saw that."

"I saw it."

"Is Helix doing disposal in my lane?"

"No scheduled Helix activity in C-seven."

"Unscheduled is having a busy morning."

The tug drifted. The cubesat rotated away, its decal flashing once, twice, farther now. Routine work asked for completion. Survival asked for humility. Kira let the cubesat go and opened a wider scan.

"Little Argument standing off," she said. "No capture. I am not putting a tug into blind metal."

"Copy. Hold relative."

Below, cloud shadow slid over the Pacific. Above, the lane where hospital capsules were due looked empty and clean. Kira knew better. The sky had weather, same as ground. It just used sharper rain.

Another tiny glint crossed the outer camera, too faint for alarm, bright enough for dread.

Routine work had ended. Metal weather had arrived without forecast.

Chapter 2: Traffic Black

Orbital traffic declared Shell C black for twenty minutes. Black meant no routine burns, no launches through the lane, no ferry departures, no insurance-friendly optimism. Every object waited unless waiting killed it faster.

Commander Vera Sol came on the loop without greeting. "All vehicles hold current vector and report damage. No discretionary burn without traffic release. Repeat, Shell C is black."

Kira ran the checks by hand because automatic summaries lied politely. Hull pressure steady. Battery bus clean. Forward lidar occluded by dust for two seconds, then clear. Antenna two showed a scratch and a gain wobble. Capture arm joint two hesitated when she flexed it but returned green.

"Tug Nalen intact," she said. "Antenna scratched. No breach. Capture arm responsive with a sulk in joint two."

"Log the sulk later," Vera said.

"It logs itself."

No one laughed. Black shell had taken humor off oxygen.

The command channel filled with clipped voices. A cargo mule above Kira reported no damage and fuel at holding minimum. A private observatory bus declared a missed imaging window as if the cosmos owed it compensation. Two return capsules froze their descent planning and asked for revised corridors. Somewhere over the Atlantic, a launch stack on ascent was ordered to hold below interface altitude until traffic could tell it whether the sky was still there.

"Anchor Nine?" Vera asked.

Static answered first, a dry ripping sound.

Malik Hesh came through from the maintenance platform. "Anchor Nine has debris pings on outer shield. Radiator loop three dropping. We are isolating."

"Casualties?"

"Not yet."

"Pressure?"

"Holding. Ward module temperature rising by point six per minute."

Kira pictured Anchor Nine: a clumsy service platform with too many docking collars, patched shields, and a reputation for keeping machines alive after budgets gave up. She had used its galley twice. Bad tea, good technicians, one small window where people left notes.

"Ferry status?" Vera asked.

A new voice answered, low and irritated. "Patiently is docked and ready cold. Captain Ito. I can warm systems in six."

"You will not undock without corridor."

"Then make me one."

"Stand by."

Joss populated Kira's display with fragment tracks. At first the debris showed as red beads, each uncertain enough to wear a haze. Then ground radar, Kira's cameras, and Anchor Nine's shield pings began to agree. The beads sharpened. They did not look like a cloud. Clouds spread. These points came in staggered lines, each arriving with irritating timing, like a sentence half written by someone tapping a table.

"Where did this start?" Kira asked.

"Helix retired cluster, maybe," Joss said.

Vera cut in. "Maybe is not a traffic product."

"Working on it."

"Work faster."

"That is not a mathematical operator."

"It is today."

Kira enlarged the track field. The Helix cluster sat high of her, a loose group of decommissioned lift-management satellites once used to coordinate cargo traffic from equatorial platforms. Retired, passivated, and certified inert, according to the public registry. She had seen inert machines cause trouble before. Inert only meant the paperwork had stopped listening.

On public channels, tourist stations requested clarification in bright customer-service voices. Weather operators requested priority because their chains crossed Shell C within the hour. A mining consortium demanded assurance that its ore tug would not be delayed past contract tolerance. Helix Lift requested private consultation with traffic command.

Vera ignored the last one.

"Traffic, this is North Pearl Resort," a tourist station said. "Guests are observing visible fragment activity. We require language for public areas."

Vera answered after a breath. "Tell them orbital traffic is managing an unscheduled debris event. Keep them away from windows if your safety plan says so. Do not improvise optimism."

The resort went quiet.

Kira watched the shell clock count down from nineteen minutes. The black order was a blunt instrument, but blunt instruments had uses. Every planned maneuver became suspect. Every friendly engine became a possible collision partner. Waiting was sometimes discipline. In orbit, it could also be surrender.

"Joss," she said on a side loop, "those intervals bother you?"

"Everything bothers me."

"Specifically."

He did not answer at once. She could hear keys, or imagined them. Ground rooms had sounds that loops flattened into ghosts.

"Yes," he said finally. "They bother me specifically."

"Why?"

"Because random debris is untidy. This is untidy with habits."

The shell clock reached eighteen minutes. Beyond the canopy, one fragment caught sunlight and vanished. Kira kept her hands near the controls and did nothing, which took more discipline than moving.

No one believed the twenty-minute blackout anymore.

Chapter 3: Anchor Nine

Anchor Nine was a maintenance platform built for things too large to repair on Earth and too expensive to abandon in orbit. It carried technicians, medics, pilots, spare radiators, old coffee, and a chapel window facing nothing in particular.

Malik Hesh worked medical there because he disliked gravity hospitals and administrative certainty. On Earth, hospital corridors had committees, donor plaques, and windows that insisted the world was stable. Anchor Nine had Velcro, stale air, and alarms honest enough to scream when something was actually wrong.

When the first debris ping hit, he was inventorying plasma bags. The sound came through the platform as a clean metallic tick, not loud, almost polite. He looked up because polite sounds in orbit deserved suspicion.

"Outer shield contact," engineering called. "Small. No penetration."

The second ping took out radiator loop three.

Lights dipped. A tray of sealed syringes trembled against its restraint. The ward's ventilation changed pitch, a thin climbing whine that every medic on the platform knew without needing a panel.

Malik secured the plasma drawer with his knee and reached for the wall display. "Temperature rising in ward module," he told Vera.

"Can you isolate?"

"Already doing it."

He switched loop three away from the ward and watched the backup path accept load, then reject half of it. The module had been built by three contractors and repaired by nineteen opinions. It tolerated normal stress with the dignity of an old chair. It did not tolerate surprise well.

The patients on Anchor Nine were not many, but none were optional. Two decompression injuries from a rushed maintenance lock, both sedated and swollen in ways that made nurses speak softly. One heart-support case, Dr. Aven Maas, waiting for ferry transfer to a lower medical station. Three technicians with burns from a battery incident, awake, bandaged, and trying to joke because technicians believed pain respected sarcasm.

"We are moving everyone off the warm wall," Malik said.

Nurse Cera Dole already had her hands on a bed lock. "Which side is the warm wall today?"

"The one making bad life choices."

"That is all of them."

They moved the decompression patients first, slow and careful, their lines bundled like pale vines. The ward smelled of antiseptic, plastic, and the faint hot-dust odor that came when insulation warmed behind panels. Malik checked Dr. Maas's support harness. The older physician opened one eye, gray and annoyed.

"If you say routine precaution, I will lose respect for you," she said.

"Debris event. Radiator damage. We are adjusting."

"Better. I respect verbs."

Captain Ren Ito stood at the ferry hatch, helmet under one arm, flight suit half sealed. Patiently, his short-range medical ferry, was docked on the far collar. Ito had the compact stillness of a man who wanted to move and had been ordered not to. His dark hair floated in a tied-back knot where gravity would have made it respectable.

"If traffic gives me a corridor, I can take six now," he said.

"Traffic has no corridor."

"I did not ask what they have. I asked what they might make."

Malik admired pilots because they mistook impossibility for a scheduling problem. He also hated that about them.

"You have six critical mounts," Malik said.

"I have six certified critical mounts."

"Do not start."

"I am warming systems."

"Vera ordered hold."

"Warm is not undock."

Another fragment struck. This one was louder. The sound came through the hull like a fist on a door, followed by a shiver that traveled through bed frames, handrails, teeth. One of the burned technicians swore. Another began to breathe too fast.

Malik put a hand on the man's shoulder. "Look at me. Name?"

"Tomas."

"Tomas, you are going to panic on my schedule. Your slot is not for another ten minutes."

The man barked a laugh, then coughed, then breathed.

"Good," Malik said. "Excellent respect for process."

Engineering reported outer shield damage aft, no pressure loss. The ward temperature climbed another degree. Cera taped a drifting cable to the bed rail and looked at Malik over her mask.

"If the backup loop fails?"

"We move to the ferry collar and use Patiently's environmental until traffic stops being dramatic."

Ito said, "Patiently objects to being called environmental support."

"Patiently is a thermos with engines."

"A certified thermos."

The chapel window at the end of the small spoke caught sunrise then lost it as the platform rolled. For one second the glass glowed gold around an old scratch. Someone had taped a paper flower beside it months ago. Paper in orbit was vanity, fire load, and morale. Malik had allowed it.

Vera's voice returned. "Anchor Nine, debris density increasing in your sector. Hold readiness for internal shelter."

"We are already holding more readiness than we can comfortably store," Malik said.

"Casualties?"

He looked at the patients, at Cera, at Ito's helmet under his arm, at the warming wall.

"Not yet."

He disliked those two words. They were a door left open.

Malik clipped himself to the ward rail and raised his voice. "Listen to me. We are moving inward in pairs. No one unfastens a line unless Cera or I say so. No one heroically helps without being assigned. No one panic until I assign turns."

The burned technician Tomas lifted a bandaged hand. "Do we get priority numbers?"

"You just lost yours for being clever."

This time several people laughed. The hull ticked again, small and distant. Malik kept his face steady and began moving the ward away from the heat.

Chapter 4: Joss's Rhythm

Joss Ader did not hear music in orbit. People said that because he used rhythm when explaining motion to officials who feared equations. What he heard was timing, and timing had become wrong.

He plotted fragment arrivals from Kira's cameras, Anchor Nine pings, and ground radar. The traffic room around him had gone hard and bright. Wall displays showed Shell C from three angles. Every workstation carried a different emergency: ferry holds, launch scrubs, platform damage, public comms, propellant declarations. A mug sat near Joss's elbow, full and cold. He had forgotten making it.

"You have first fit?" Vera asked from the command row.

"First fit is garbage."

"Professional version."

"First fit is not decision-grade."

"How long?"

"Shorter if people stop asking how long."

Vera moved away, which was one reason Joss trusted her.

He fed the model the cleanest times first: Kira's lidar stamps, then the tug's optical captures, then the shield contacts from Anchor Nine. Ground radar came with atmospheric error and bureaucratic confidence. He stripped out both as best he could. The first pass looked random. The second made his neck tighten.

Intervals repeated.

Not exact. Orbit never sang clean. Gravity smeared certainty. Solar pressure tugged at surfaces differently. Bad shapes tumbled. Old panels caught light and heat and drag in ways no spreadsheet respected. But the pattern returned in groups: long, short, short, long. Then an offset. Then long, short, short, long again, arriving late by an amount that matched shell precession too neatly to ignore.

Joss leaned closer to the screen.

"Don't be elegant," he told the model.

The model ignored him and became elegant.

He opened a sandbox run and removed assumed randomness. The fragment paths tightened. He added Helix's retired-cluster positions from the public registry. The timing improved again. His neck moved from tight to cold.

There were patterns traffic officers dreaded because they meant the universe had made a mess. There were other patterns they dreaded more because they meant people had helped.

"Sian," he said, opening a private call.

Dr. Sian Rook answered from the orbital mechanics lab with sleep in her voice and irritation already formed. "If this is about your terrible coffee, I told you the grinder is not the issue. It is your moral relationship with water."

"I have a debris resonance."

Silence. Then, with sleep gone, "Send it."

Joss sent the plot, the raw stamps, the filtered model, and the version he did not want to be right. While he waited, he watched a tourist station freeze its rotation correction because the blackout order had caught it mid-cycle. Its projected comfort margin would make passengers nauseous in eleven minutes. Good. Nausea was better than fragments.

Sian's reply took thirty-seven seconds.

"That is not natural," she said.

He exhaled through his nose. "Could collision generate it?"

"A collision can begin many ugly things."

"Sian."

"Not this. Not with that phase relationship. It is being driven or was driven recently."

"By what?"

"Engines no one should have fired."

Joss opened an old memory he had filed under beautiful and useless. A conference paper, ten years back, from before the cleanup treaties had teeth and before every promising orbital idea had to explain whose roof it might land on. The talk had been infamous among traffic people: brilliant, reckless, mathematically clean in the way cliff edges were clean.

"I remember a paper," he said.

"No you don't."

"Coordinated debris herding. Gravity assist decay. Multi-point phase nudging."

Her silence changed texture.

"Send me your current Helix cluster data," she said.

"Already in packet."

"Then send me what Helix filed, not what traffic normalized."

Joss pulled the original retirement filings from archive. The documents were neat, signed, and almost certainly incomplete. Helix Lift had once owned half the equatorial cargo business and all the arrogance that came with making launch cheaper for governments that publicly disliked them. The retired cluster had been certified inert: no active fuel, no autonomous control, batteries drained to safe levels, command receivers locked.

"Their filings say dead," Joss said.

"Dead things do not keep time."

"Sometimes they coast poetically."

"Your fragments are not coasting poetically."

He added her to Vera's command loop.

"Dr. Rook," Vera said, "give me decision language."

Sian did not soften it. "The debris appears to be in a driven resonance. It resembles a phase-herding scheme. If it continues, fragments will concentrate along predictable but shifting corridors. That may threaten Anchor Nine, weather assets, and any vehicle unlucky enough to trust yesterday's catalog."

"Can we stop it?"

"Find the driver. Stop new pulses. Then maybe damp what has already been made."

Joss opened the old paper. The title appeared in a plain archive font: Coordinated Gravity-Assisted Debris Herding in Crowded Shells. Author: S. Rook. Status: abandoned.

He had expected guilt in her voice. Instead he heard calculation, which was better and worse.

"You wrote warnings," he said privately.

"Warnings are only useful when people treat them as instructions."

On his screen the next predicted fragment group crossed Anchor Nine's uncertainty bubble. The timing error was still large. Large errors were comfort until they shrank.

"Vera," Joss said, "I am upgrading this from debris event to active cascade risk."

"Define active."

He looked at the repeating intervals.

"Someone left a metronome in the wreckage."

Chapter 5: Helix Denial

Tane Orlo appeared on the emergency loop in a suit jacket and no tie, the costume of corporate crisis. Behind him, Helix Lift's logo rotated slowly, a silver arrow rising through blue.

"Our retired cluster was passivated and certified inert," he said.

He had the controlled face of a man trained to know which emotions created liability. His eyes moved too often to a second screen. Legal staff, Kira guessed. Or engineers being told to become legal staff in real time.

Vera looked at the track wall. "Your serial plates are crossing my traffic shell."

"Fragments can be misidentified."

Kira transmitted the camera image. The Helix plate filled every screen, numbers bright on torn metal, one corner curled like peeled foil. The serial matched an H-series lift coordinator retired eighteen months earlier. Someone in the traffic room made a small sound, quickly swallowed.

Tane's jaw worked once. "That does not establish source."

"It establishes ownership of at least one fast problem," Kira said.

Vera muted Kira's outbound for five seconds. Kira deserved it and did not regret it. She used the silence to drink cold coffee from a bulb and watch Tane decide how much truth could fit through his teeth.

"Mr. Orlo," Vera said, "traffic command requires full status on all H-series retired platforms in Shell C. Fuel, battery, command receiver, autonomous packages, last uplink, last maintenance access."

"Those records are proprietary."

"Not during a black shell."

"Some data releases require counsel review."

"Your counsel is welcome to review from a bunker after I finish saving people. Send the records."

Tane's background logo kept rotating, serene and stupid. "Helix Lift has complied with all decommissioning regulations. The H-series cluster contains no active fuel, no guidance pulses, no autonomous herding package. The satellites were passivated under independent certification."

Joss brought up the public filing beside the live debris map. It was a neat document full of clean numbers. Zero residual propellant. Receiver disabled. Batteries discharged. No linked autonomy. Kira had learned to distrust perfect zeros in space. Even emptiness had margins.

Edda Lin from orbital insurance joined the loop with no preamble. She appeared in a gray office under colder light than Tane's. Her hair was clipped back. Her expression suggested she had already read three contracts and found all of them disappointing.

"I need classification," she said. "Natural collision cascade, operator fault, external trigger, or undeclared retained control."

Vera stared at the ceiling for strength. "People are in the shell. Classify later."

Edda did not apologize. "Classification affects asset access and command authority. If this is natural cascade, emergency pooling triggers through the common-risk compact. If Helix retained active control, their private tugs and reserves can be compelled under fault provisions. If there is external interference, law enforcement protocols may slow command keys unless we preempt them now."

"So does survival," Vera said.

"That is my point."

Kira revised her first irritation. Clause people could be useful if pointed toward doors.

Tane said, "Helix will cooperate within legal limits."

Edda smiled without warmth. "Legal limits are flexible when your debris is perforating a medical platform."

"Alleged debris."

Kira checked whether Vera had unmuted her. She had. Kira chose not to speak, which she considered personal growth.

Joss displayed the resonance plot. The red fragment tracks pulsed forward in accelerated time, crossing and widening, then tightening again around the retired cluster. The pattern made itself visible even to non-specialists. Long, short, short, long. A staggered cadence. A bad promise.

Tane leaned closer despite himself.

Sian's window opened beside the plot. She looked pale, barefaced, and much too awake. "Something in your dead cluster is keeping time."

"That is an interpretation," Tane said.

"It is a measurement."

"A collision could have created secondary motion."

"A collision cannot invent concealed fuel."

He did not answer quickly enough.

Vera caught it. "Mr. Orlo."

Tane looked down. When he came back, the corporate crisis face had a hairline crack. "I am requesting internal engineering escalation."

"You are past requesting," Vera said. "You are providing."

On Kira's display, another fragment track updated. Anchor Nine's safety bubble shifted from amber to red along one edge. The hospital corridor that had started this morning's work now looked like a vein under a blade.

"Helix," Kira said, careful this time, "if one of those satellites can still hear you, wake up whoever knows the old language."

Tane's mouth tightened. "There is no old language."

Sian said, "There is always an old language."

Edda began reading from a document offscreen. "Helix H-series supplemental risk memo, year minus four. Topic: autonomous disposal harmonics in congested shells. Marked internal. Mr. Orlo, do you want to keep denying vocabulary while I quote your own?"

For the first time, Tane looked less like a spokesman and more like a man standing under falling metal.

Vera cut through the pause. "All proprietary data to traffic command now. Insurance, you will facilitate compulsion. Helix, you will stop performing innocence and start performing usefulness."

Kira watched the retired cluster drift in the model, dead on paper, busy in the sky.

Chapter 6: Old Paper

Sian Rook had written the gravity-orchestra paper when she was young enough to believe a beautiful equation deserved implementation. The idea was simple in the way dangerous ideas are simple: use small burns and natural orbital precession to make debris encounter drag corridors in sequence, like instruments entering a score.

The review board called it elegant and irresponsible. She remembered the room: glass table, stale biscuits, three agency reviewers, one commercial observer, a projector that tinted every orbit sickly green. She had stood in front of them with a laser pointer and the arrogance of someone who had found a new way to make gravity useful. By the end of the hour, the eldest reviewer had folded his hands and said, "Doctor, you have designed a broom that can become a blade."

She agreed with the second word later. Not aloud at first. Pride took time to cool.

Now Joss's plot sat on her screen, wearing her old cadence. Long, short, short, long. A drift. A correction. A return. The line was uglier than her simulations, full of collision noise and bad mass estimates, but the bones were there.

Her lab was dark except for monitors. A jacket hung from the back of a chair. Someone had left a bowl of noodles beside a keyboard and the broth had filmed over. Sian pushed it away without looking and opened the archived code she had promised herself never to need.

"Can it happen accidentally?" Vera asked.

"No," Sian said. She heard how final it sounded and forced herself to be precise. "A collision can create periodic-looking fragments for a few passes. This is different. But a partial implementation can become unstable after collision."

"Can it be stopped?"

"Maybe by playing against it."

No one liked that answer. She did not like it either. The word playing made the method sound graceful. What she meant was timed interference under uncertainty, with lives sitting inside the error bars.

Sian pulled old simulations from cold storage. The archive asked for a password she had not typed in eight years. Her hands shook once when the file opened, not enough for anyone to see. The cadence matched too closely: phase spacing, transfer window, resonance intervals. Someone had taken her abandoned work and removed the caution from it, like cutting brake lines because they added weight.

Tane said Helix had never licensed the paper.

"It was public," Sian said. "The warnings were public too."

"Public theory is not an admission of implementation."

"No. Your satellites keeping time is better."

On the shared display, Kira's tug sat near the edge of the retired cluster. The icons were tiny, inadequate stand-ins for metal and breath. Another track crossed Anchor Nine's projected safety bubble, then widened as Joss added uncertainty.

Kira came on, voice flat from the cockpit. "Doctor, can your bad music move metal out of my sky?"

Sian closed her eyes. Behind them she saw the old simulation when it worked: fragments sliding toward atmosphere in courteous sequence, decay without drama, a crowded shell made cleaner by patience. She had loved that image. That was the trouble. Love made engineers forgive risks in the prototype and promise governance later.

"If we have engines, timing, and luck," she said.

"We have one small tug and a lot of rude fragments."

"Then start counting."

Joss said, "Counting what?"

"Every active engine in reach. Every object with residual propellant. Every tug, drone, ferry, station thruster, and retired platform that can produce even a bad impulse."

Vera asked, "And if we find them?"

"We stop the driving pulse first. Then we use selected burns to make the resonance oppose itself. The trick is not strength. It is phase."

"Decision language, Doctor."

Sian looked at the old paper's conclusion. She had written that public coordination was a prerequisite, not an accessory. She had written it plainly. It had not saved anyone.

"We need to turn a hidden system into a public one in less than an hour," she said. "And we need Helix to stop lying."

Chapter 7: Weather Net

Noor Vale operated a weather-satellite network that made storms less surprising for half a hemisphere. Her satellites were old, underfunded, and stubborn. She loved them with the practical affection reserved for machines that had saved lives without ever trending.

Her control room was smaller than Helix's crisis suite and more honest. A row of aging consoles faced maps of cloud fields, ocean heat, river basins, and lightning. The staff wore sweaters and headsets. Someone had taped a child's drawing of a hurricane to a cabinet. On a side monitor, the east chain showed as seven yellow beads climbing toward the infected shell.

"My east chain enters the fragment sheet in forty-six minutes," she told Vera.

"Can you raise orbit?"

"On two birds. The rest are too low on fuel."

"How low?"

"The kind of low that makes accountants proud and operators bitter."

She had fought for reserve propellant during three budget cycles and lost each time to launch costs, depreciation tables, and a deputy director who believed weather data was only valuable when absent. Now East Three and East Seven would meet fragments with enough fuel to report trouble and not enough to avoid it.

Tane asked whether weather assets could yield corridor priority to crewed vehicles. Noor looked at him through the screen as if he had suggested weather yield to branding.

"If my chain goes blind, coastal storm prediction drops for twenty million people," she said.

"We have humans in immediate danger," Vera said.

"I know. I am giving you the next danger so it does not arrive pretending to be new."

That quieted the loop for half a second.

Noor sent raw telemetry to Joss: battery margins, attitude limits, burn capability, camera health, thermal constraints. She did not polish the file. Operators in emergency did not need perfume. Joss integrated her satellites into the traffic model, and the red debris lines cut through her yellow chain with brutal indifference. The fragment resonance would pass through weather altitude, scatter again, and threaten navigation relays below. Metal did not respect sector labels.

"East Four can yaw shield-first," Joss said.

"East Four's yaw wheel has been complaining since April."

"Can it complain in the required direction?"

"Maybe."

"I will take maybe."

"You traffic people are easily degraded."

Kira checked Little Argument's fuel while they talked. Enough for capture, not heroism. Heroism had poor margins and worse paperwork. Her tug carried a narrow reserve for abort, an uglier reserve she never admitted to traffic, and the emotional reserve pilots invented when math insulted them.

"Noor," she said, "if I can shift the first fragment group?"

Noor zoomed the model. "Depends how much and when."

"I can maybe shove one panel and spoil the timing."

"You might save two satellites."

"Only two?"

"Two weather eyes are better than one beautiful apology."

Kira liked her immediately.

Vera asked, "What does losing the chain do today?"

Noor pulled up a ground map. A storm line was building over warm water, still ordinary, still manageable, the kind of weather that became political only after it hurt someone. "Forecast confidence drops along three coasts. Fishing fleets get dirtier warnings. Emergency planners lose six hours of clean lead time if the storm shears wrong. Aviation reroutes become guesswork. Farmers do not get rain timing. Flood models widen."

"Casualty estimate?"

"Do not ask me to make one this early."

"I am asking for command priority."

Noor's mouth tightened. "Then: not immediate, not imaginary."

Vera nodded once. "Weather stays in the model."

Tane began, "Helix assets are also--"

"Your assets are suspects," Noor said. "Mine are witnesses."

Edda made a note. Joss did not hide a small smile.

On Noor's main wall, cloud bands rolled over ocean in soft white arcs. People on the ground would look up at rain and never know the warning had crossed a battlefield of old metal before reaching them. Noor knew. That was enough to make her angry and useful.

Chapter 8: Insurance Voice

Edda Lin had the voice of someone who could make catastrophe sound like a clause. "If the cascade is a natural collision event, emergency asset pooling triggers differently than if Helix retained active control."

Vera stared at the ceiling for strength. "We are not litigating while debris is moving."

"We are determining command authority."

"No," Kira said. "Metal determines that when it arrives."

This time Vera did not mute her.

Edda's questions were not useless. Kira had known enough salvage work to understand that money could move faster than ethics when the right penalty attached. Insurance access could unlock private tugs, reserve fuel, proprietary command keys, and satellite bus documentation that companies normally guarded like family shame. It could also slow everything under blame. Edda wanted facts early because facts late became bargaining chips.

"I am invoking provisional common-risk review," Edda said. "Traffic command, record my office as aligned with emergency disclosure."

"Generous," Tane said.

"Self-interested," Edda replied. "If your cluster destroys half the shell, everyone I insure sues everyone you employ. I prefer smaller disasters."

Vera said, "Practical is acceptable. Keep going."

Edda's eyes moved across unseen text. "Helix risk memos mention automated debris herding?"

"I cannot confirm internal documents on an open emergency loop."

"Then confirm whether you prefer subpoena delay while Anchor Nine heats."

Tane's face tightened. "You are asking me to breach corporate confidentiality."

"I am asking you to notice that confidentiality is not a pressure hull."

Kira revised her opinion again. Clause people could be useful if pointed correctly.

Edda uploaded an index of insured orbital assets within reach. Three private tugs, one corporate inspection drone, two reserve fuel pods, a maintenance skiff docked at a telecom station, and Helix command gateways under disputed access. Each item came with owner, insurance layer, likely legal objection, and override path.

Joss sounded almost admiring. "You had this ready?"

"I insure crowded skies. I assume everyone is lying until physics audits them."

"That is very close to science."

"Science has fewer invoices."

Vera cut in. "Which assets can we compel in under fifteen minutes?"

"The private tug Grace Meridian if its owner accepts indemnity. The telecom skiff if we classify crewed platform protection. Helix gateways if Mr. Orlo stops pretending he needs a board vote."

Tane said, "I do need executive authorization for gateway release."

Edda leaned toward her camera. "Then wake an executive and say the word manslaughter slowly."

No one spoke for a moment.

Joss sent another update. Fragment density rising. Resonance holding. Anchor Nine risk window in seventeen minutes. Weather chain in forty-two. A hospital capsule corridor below the shell turned amber as a precaution.

Malik broke in from Anchor Nine. "Ward temperature at twenty-nine point six Celsius. Patients dislike your governance pace."

"Understood," Vera said. "Engineering?"

"Radiator loop still isolated," Malik answered. "Shield pings continuing. We are moving critical patients to ferry collar."

Edda looked at Tane. "Those are the facts that become exhibits."

Tane lowered his eyes. "I am releasing a restricted engineering packet."

"Full packet," Vera said.

"I need--"

"You need people alive to argue with you."

Another file arrived. Then another. Joss's workstation chimed as data began to ingest: old bus schematics, maintenance command lists, retired propellant estimates with suspicious revision marks.

Edda's mouth flattened. "There it is."

"What?" Vera asked.

"Residual maneuvering allowance. Not zero. Hidden under contingency disposal language."

Sian said softly, "Fuel."

The word moved through the loop like a spark.

Vera made the call. "All relevant proprietary data to traffic command now. Argue invoices after breathing. Insurance, draft the emergency compulsion notices. Helix, open your gates. Kira, hold for possible physical intervention."

"Holding," Kira said.

Outside her cockpit, the sky remained black and crowded. Inside the loop, the legal locks began to break, not from virtue, but from pressure. Kira would take pressure. It moved things.

Chapter 9: Capture Arm

Pavel Xu talked to machines as if they were difficult relatives. He had built half of Little Argument's capture arm and cursed the other half often enough to claim intimacy.

His maintenance bay on ground was a narrow room beneath traffic command, full of spare actuators, thermal blankets in labeled bags, and a workbench scarred by tools that should not have been used as hammers. He wore a headset crooked over one ear and had grease on his sleeve though nothing in the room currently required grease. Some people carried worry in their shoulders. Pavel carried it as irritation.

"Joint two is sticky," Kira told him.

"Joint two is expressive."

"It needs to express capture."

He pulled Little Argument's live telemetry onto a side screen. The arm had taken micro-debris on the outer housing during the first crossing. Not fatal. Nothing in orbit was fatal until it was. A tiny gouge near the joint encoder produced intermittent noise. The motor temperature was high but not obscene. The wrist had lost some fine-position authority. Pavel clicked his tongue.

"Do not make that sound," Kira said.

"I make sounds when pilots mistreat my machinery."

"Debris mistreated it. I was nearby."

"You are often nearby when my machinery suffers."

He opened a maintenance channel and locked out nonessential diagnostics. The arm's schematic unfolded on his screen: shoulder, elbow, wrist, capture basket, force sensors, cable path. He had once argued for redundant wrist encoders and lost to mass budget. He remembered the meeting, the clean shirt of the man who cut the redundancy, and the phrase acceptable risk. Acceptable to whom, Pavel had asked. The minutes had not recorded that part.

"Cycle it twice," he said.

Kira cycled. The arm stuttered, extended, locked. The tug's camera showed the abandoned cubesat tumbling away, now irrelevant, its faded decal flashing in slow insult. Ahead, a larger Helix fragment drifted through a possible intercept, a torn panel with enough surface to catch a push.

"Again," Pavel said.

"It will get annoyed."

"It is already annoyed. We are measuring consistency."

She cycled again. Joint two hesitated at seventeen degrees, then crossed with a jerk.

"Can I grab and push?" Kira asked.

"You can grab. Push depends on whether you enjoy losing the arm."

"I am emotionally neutral about arms."

"I am not."

"You named a torque limiter."

"Only because it had personality."

Pavel wrote a patch in the ugly shorthand of emergency engineering. Reduce closure velocity. Lower force threshold. Accept more slippage before abort. Disable one protective recoil that would save the wrist by releasing the object at exactly the wrong time. He hated every line. Good software protected machines from pilots. Emergency software sometimes asked machines to be braver than their warranties.

"I am uploading a soft-capture patch," he said.

"Plain language."

"The arm will hug garbage gently."

"Beautiful."

"Do not praise it. It encourages failure."

The code loaded in increments. Kira watched the progress bar while fragments crossed the display like bright insects. She flexed her fingers once inside the gloves. Pavel saw the motion on cockpit cam and pretended not to.

"You nervous?" he asked.

"I am making sure my hands still exist."

"Useful quality."

"Usually."

He ran the patch through a compressed sim. The Helix fragment model tore free twice, took the arm with it once, and behaved on the fourth run. Pavel deleted the first three from the display before Kira could make a joke.

"I saw that," she said.

"You saw nothing decision-grade."

Vera entered the channel. "Can Little Argument shift the panel?"

Pavel answered before Kira could. "Maybe. One controlled contact. Low impulse. No wrestling."

"Kira?"

"I can give you ugly."

"Ugly may be sufficient."

Pavel did not like sufficient. Sufficient was what people called a bridge while walking across it.

"You get one ugly capture," he said.

"I only need one."

"Pilots say that before needing six."

He was right. She hated that in engineers.

He checked the arm one more time and sent Kira a private line: "If it starts oscillating, release. Do not argue with a lever longer than your tug."

"Little Argument argues professionally."

"Little Argument is a box with debt."

"So am I."

For half a second, he smiled. Then the next fragment warning arrived, and both of them went back to work.

Chapter 10: First Strike

The fragment that hit Anchor Nine's outer shield was the size of a coin and moving like a verdict. It punched through the first layer, sprayed ceramic dust, and lodged before pressure hull. Good engineering turned disaster into repair.

Malik felt the station shudder through his palms. He had one hand on Dr. Maas's transfer rail and the other braced against the ceiling grip. Lights dimmed, returned, dimmed again. A loose roll of gauze escaped a tray and drifted with absurd calm toward the ventilation intake.

"Shield breach outer," engineering called. "No pressure loss."

"Ward module temperature?" Malik asked.

"Still rising. Thirty point two."

"Backup loop?"

"Holding poorly."

"That is not a state."

"Then poorly is doing its best."

He moved the heart-support patient toward the ferry collar. Dr. Aven Maas was strapped into a support frame with enough tubing to make every doorway an argument. Cera guided the lower end. Tomas, the burned technician who had lost his panic priority for joking, used his good hand to keep a monitor cable from snagging.

"I did not assign you helpfulness," Malik told him.

"I am disobeying medically."

"Do it slower."

Captain Ito had crew ready in suits at the collar. Their helmets reflected ward lights and frightened faces. Patiently's hatch stood open behind him, bright, narrow, and inadequate. No corridor yet, but readiness was a form of refusal.

Dr. Maas opened both eyes as they turned her frame. "Do not bump me into history."

"We try not to bump patients into anything," Malik said.

"You say that, but I have worked in hospitals."

"Then you know our standards are aspirational."

Another ping struck farther aft. The station's old frame groaned. This was no polite tick. This was a shove. A nurse cursed. The decompression patient in Bed Two bucked against sedation, alarms flaring across the monitor.

"Hold," Malik said.

Cera was already there, one hand on the patient's shoulder, the other adjusting a line. "Pressure spike. He is fighting the ventilator."

"Give him point two more."

"Already drawn."

The injection entered the line. The monitor slowed from panic to warning. Malik waited three breaths before moving again. Hurry killed in quiet ways.

Vera came on: "Anchor Nine, traffic window not clean."

Ito answered, "I do not require clean. I require not shredded."

"Stand by."

"Standing by with patients in a warming corridor."

"I know."

Malik heard fatigue under Vera's command voice. Good. He trusted people who knew the cost of their orders. He distrusted the cheerful ones.

They reached the ferry collar and locked Dr. Maas's frame to the staging rail. Patiently's interior smelled of coolant and fabric warmed by electronics. It had six certified critical mounts, four fold-down crew seats, and storage compartments Ito had been eyeing with criminal creativity.

"You cannot put a patient there," Malik said.

"I have not yet proposed it."

"Your face proposed it."

"My face is under stress."

Engineering reported another outer strike, aft service truss. No pressure loss. Antenna damage possible. Radiator loop three remained down. The ward temperature reached thirty-one.

Malik secured a plasma bag to the ferry rail and looked through the small ward port. Earth filled half the window. Beautiful, unreachable, busy with everyone who needed the machines above them to keep working. Hospitals, ports, storm shelters, data centers, farms, ambulances moving under weather forecasts that Noor's chain made possible. Ground looked stable from up here. It was not. It was simply farther from the metal.

A bright fleck crossed the view, too fast to track.

He closed the shutter.

"Doctor," Dr. Maas said, "your bedside manner has become architectural."

"I am containing drafts."

"You are hiding the view."

"The view is showing off."

She smiled, then winced as the support pump adjusted. Malik checked the pressure manually. Machines could save lives and still need supervision.

Vera returned. "We may have a partial mitigation if Tug Nalen can shift an object near the Helix cluster."

Ito looked at Malik. Malik looked at the patients staged along the collar.

"How long?" Malik asked.

"Unknown."

"We are running out of unknown."

"Understood."

He wanted to say more. He wanted to say that people in beds were not variables, that heat turned quickly from discomfort to danger, that every minute made the ferry less an option and more a dare. Vera knew all of it. Repeating it would spend breath.

Malik clipped himself between Dr. Maas and the next bed and raised his voice for the ward. "We hold here. Helmets sealed but visors open until I say otherwise. Drink if you can. Joke only if it improves morale."

Tomas lifted his bandaged hand again. "Who decides?"

"I do."

"Authoritarian medicine."

"Precisely."

The hull ticked. Everyone heard it. No one moved without being told. Malik counted that as victory.

Chapter 11: Harmonic Shell

Joss built the harmonic shell model by abandoning politeness toward uncertainty. He took Kira's sightings, Anchor Nine impacts, Noor's satellite telemetry, ground radar, and old Helix filings. Then he let the software show the pattern it had been trying to avoid.

He labeled every input with its ugliness. Kira's optical track: clean angle, poor mass. Anchor Nine shield impacts: excellent time, uncertain object. Noor's weather telemetry: rich position data, fragile prediction if attitude changed. Ground radar: broad coverage, noisy at the edges. Helix filings: useful until trust was required.

The traffic room watched the model assemble on the wall. Red fragments. Yellow weather assets. White crewed stations. Blue commanded vehicles. Gray unknowns. At first it looked like a disease map. Then Joss advanced the clock and the red points tightened, spread, tightened again.

The fragments were entering a repeating phase relationship with the retired Helix cluster. Small pulses from still-active guidance units had tightened the debris sheet instead of dispersing it. The shell was not exploding outward. It was being conducted badly.

"Harmonic shell," Sian said.

"Can I use a less musical term?" Joss asked.

"Use any term. Physics will not care."

"Driven debris resonance," he said.

"Less memorable."

"I am not naming a perfume."

Vera studied the model. "If we stop the pulses?"

"The existing fragments keep moving," Joss said. "But the amplification stops. Think of it as taking away the hand that keeps pushing the swing. The swing still hits someone if they stand in front of it."

"Do we have the hand?"

"We have evidence of one."

Tane said, "Our satellites cannot be pulsing."

Kira transmitted a new signal capture from her tug. It came in as a small spike on the spectrum display, a faint guidance chirp, cold and regular. It did not carry enough strength for public tracking stations to care. It carried enough structure to talk to nearby machines.

"There," Kira said. "Little Argument hears it every forty-two seconds."

Joss overlaid the chirp times on the fragment timing. The room did not need him to explain the fit.

Sian leaned toward her microphone. "That is a conductor's tap."

No one laughed. The phrase was too accurate and too late.

Tane's face had settled into a blankness that looked rehearsed until his hand entered frame and pressed against the desk. "Could an external actor be spoofing the signal?"

"Possible," Joss said. "Not likely. Signal geometry puts origin inside your retired cluster."

"Define not likely."

"If I define it with enough decimals, will you become useful?"

Vera said, "Joss."

"Apologies. Not likely means we should stop pretending while Anchor Nine gets warmer."

Sian opened a second display. "The resonance will not remain neat. Collision fragments perturb each other. But the system keeps correcting them. If the correction continues, it may build denser passes through crewed and weather corridors."

"May," Edda said.

"Will, if the model keeps improving."

"It is."

"Then will."

Vera made a mark on her console. "Priority one: stop the hidden cue. Priority two: identify usable engines. Priority three: damp or redirect existing fragment groups. Traffic remains black."

Joss saved the harmonic shell model under the event record. He almost named the file after Sian's phrase, then chose a dry timestamp. History could be poetic later. Right now, poetry only confused version control.

He sent the file to Vera, Sian, Noor, and the emergency archive. The upload bar crawled. He hated watching bars. They made time visible and useless.

"Model received," Vera said.

"Do not trust it past the next burn."

"I trust it as a question."

That was better than most commanders managed. Joss leaned back just far enough for his spine to object, then started the next run. Every new fragment report made the shell less like a diagram and more like weather. He had spent years telling officials orbit was not empty. Now the lesson had arrived with teeth.

Chapter 12: Dead Cluster

Kira approached the Helix retired cluster under blackout authority. Six dead satellites drifted in a loose arc, white paint chipped, panels folded like broken wings. They should have been asleep. Dead machines in orbit usually looked peaceful because danger there rarely needed expression.

Little Argument moved without hurry because hurry near old satellites made wreckage. Kira kept the tug sunward of the cluster when she could, letting glare work against the cameras less than shadow. The H-series machines had been handsome once in the corporate way: clean buses, mirrored sensor blisters, silver Helix arrows along their spines. Now thermal blankets curled at the corners. One panel hung from a hinge like a loose page.

Her radar painted one as warmer than it should be.

"Target H-3 has residual power," Kira said.

Tane objected immediately. "Telemetry says H-3 cold."

"Your telemetry is lying or your satellite is."

Pavel whispered on the maintenance loop, "Do not insult the satellite until after capture."

"It started."

"Machines hear tone."

Kira matched velocity. The tug's thrusters ticked in pairs. Each burst moved her closer to a machine that paperwork had buried and physics had not. The capture arm extended with its patched gentleness, basket open. Joint two hesitated, remembered Pavel, and behaved.

"Range six meters," Joss said. "Relative drift manageable."

"Manageable is doing a lot of work."

"So are you."

The arm steadied against a torn truss but did not close. Kira wanted a look first. She slewed the light across H-3's aft panel and saw the damage: a torn attitude thruster housing, a bite out of insulation, and beneath it, a service port with its seal state changed. No human hand had touched it in vacuum. Something had opened it by command.

"I have tool marks."

"In vacuum?" Joss asked.

"Digital tools. Port seal broken. Maintenance mode accessed after retirement."

Tane said, "That should be impossible."

Kira did not answer. She had no patience left for impossible things actively occurring.

She extended a sensor wand from the tug's belly. It was a thin boom, more dental instrument than spear, built for sniffing power leakage and old command chatter. The wand vibrated faintly as it came close to H-3's service port. On her display, the signal rose: a narrow chirp, then a pause, then another.

The satellite emitted a cold transponder pulse, too weak for public tracking but strong enough to talk to nearby debris shepherd units.

Sian inhaled sharply. "It is still cueing."

"Cueing what exactly?" Vera asked.

"Nearby platforms. Maybe fragments carrying tagged transponders. Maybe any object its software classifies as herdable."

"Herdable," Kira said. "I hate that."

"So do I."

H-3 pulsed again. The sound translated through Kira's headset as a sterile tick. Nothing in the satellite moved visibly. That made it worse. A dead machine keeping time in silence, using commands too small for the rest of the world to notice.

Kira closed the capture arm enough to brace the tug without fully committing. The force sensors climbed. Pavel murmured numbers like a prayer he would deny making.

"Permission to make it stop?" Kira asked.

Vera answered, "Granted. Minimum necessary damage. Preserve evidence if you can."

"Evidence is currently attacking traffic."

"If you can."

Kira selected a narrowband jammer first. The tug sent the standard sleep command, then an emergency passivation code pulled from Helix's fresh packet. H-3 listened, considered, and pulsed again.

"It is ignoring us," she said.

Sian said, "It may believe it is in autonomous safe mode."

"It has a funny idea of safe."

Kira shifted the tug a meter and brought the arm closer to the antenna cluster. The little dish was no wider than a dinner plate, white, chipped, and far too important.

"Pavel," she said, "how mad will the arm be if I tap that?"

"Define tap."

"A persuasive impact."

"The arm will file a grievance."

"Noted."

She waited for Vera.

"Do it," Vera said.

Kira drew the capture wrist back and lined up the strike.

Chapter 13: Cold Transponder

The cold transponder did not shut down when Kira sent standard sleep command. It replied with an obsolete Helix maintenance code and resumed pulsing.

Pavel read the handshake from ground. "That code predates their current network."

Tane said nothing.

"Mr. Orlo," Vera said. "Talk."

His face had gone pale under conference light. The rotating Helix logo behind him seemed suddenly obscene, all smooth ascent and no falling parts. "Some retired platforms retained experimental autonomous collision-avoidance packages."

Sian's voice was flat. "That is not collision avoidance. It is phase herding."

"It was never operational."

Kira watched H-3 pulse again. "Your definition of operational is doing too much work."

The signal map widened. H-3 spoke to three nearby satellites, cueing micro-burns from fuel that deorbit filings claimed had been vented. The burns were tiny, some less than a second, barely enough to show as thermal freckles. Each one tightened the fragment timing. A safety system, or illegal test, had become a metronome for disaster.

"Why obsolete code?" Joss asked.

Tane swallowed. "Legacy autonomy sandbox. It may have been isolated from the main retirement update."

"May have been?"

"I am not the engineer who--"

Vera cut him off. "You are the Helix representative in command. Represent."

Edda requested all Helix risk memos. Tane said he needed counsel. Vera said he needed to decide whether counsel preferred speaking from a planet with weather satellites.

The files came in unevenly, as if dragged from people who still believed delay was a strategy. Edda began marking them live. "Here. H-series retained contingency fuel for disposal assurance. Here. Autonomous coordination package red-team flagged for cascade amplification. Here. Recommendation: do not deploy without public traffic integration."

Sian made a small sound.

Joss looked at her window but did not speak.

Kira prepared a physical interrupt. If command would not stop the pulse, she would damage the antenna. The arm hovered over H-3's dish. Sunlight ran along the metal fingers. Her own reflection looked narrow in the cockpit glass.

"Carefully," Pavel said.

"I always damage carefully."

"That sentence ages badly."

"Then remember me fondly."

"No."

She smiled despite herself, then brought the arm down. The strike was not dramatic. In vacuum, there was no clang for anyone but the tug. The vibration traveled through the arm into Little Argument's frame and into Kira's seat. The antenna bent, rebounded, bent again. Signal strength dropped by half.

"Again," Joss said.

"Pavel?"

"Joint load acceptable. My mood is not."

Kira struck again, lower this time. The dish tore free along one mount and hung by a cable. The transponder pulse stuttered, tried to form, dissolved into noise.

"H-3 cue stopped," Joss said.

For one breath the loop was quiet.

Then H-5 pulsed.

Sian closed her eyes. "The others are still listening to themselves."

"Amplification reduced," Joss said. "Not gone."

Kira pulled the arm back. Joint two lagged badly. "Arm is unhappy."

"Arm is alive," Pavel said. "Respect the distinction."

Vera asked for updated risk windows. Anchor Nine remained red. Noor's weather chain shifted to amber and red. The hospital capsule corridor stayed closed.

Edda's voice returned, sharp as a paper edge. "Helix concealed active disposal capacity from registry and insurers. I am recording that as provisional fact."

Tane did not deny it this time.

Kira looked at the broken antenna turning slowly in H-3's torn side. Evidence preserved enough. Disaster not preserved enough. That was the day's balance.

She photographed the damage from three angles because Vera had said evidence if you can. The camera caught the torn dish, the cracked mount, the service port that should not have opened, and the slow glitter of loose insulation leaving H-3 like dirty snow.

"Satisfied?" she asked.

Edda answered before Vera. "For litigation, never. For emergency record, yes."

Kira retracted the sensor wand. The tug drifted clear. H-3 no longer pulsed, but the silence did not comfort her. It was only one instrument broken in a room full of machinery no one had admitted was awake.

Chapter 14: Sian's Guilt

Sian did not build Helix's system. She knew that. Guilt ignored architecture. Her paper had shown how debris could be nudged into cooperative decay paths. She had written warnings in bold: do not automate without public traffic integration; do not retain hidden fuel; do not operate in crewed shell.

Someone had read the method and treated warnings as decorative.

She had spent years teaching students that equations were arguments with consequences. Now the consequence had a live map, a warming medical platform, and a pilot tapping a corporate satellite into silence with a wounded arm. Sian's lab felt suddenly too small for what she had once imagined in it.

"This is not yours," Joss said on a private loop.

"It is not not mine."

"That is a terrible sentence."

"It is an accurate one."

"You wrote constraints."

"And then I published a way to make debris behave as if command could be trusted."

He was quiet. Joss respected silence when words were not load-bearing.

Sian pulled old simulations. A counter-resonance was possible if active engines introduced opposing phase shifts at three points. She rebuilt the run with current ugliness: damaged H-3, possible H-5 and H-6 fuel, unknown H-2 status, Little Argument near the cluster, one maintenance drone near Anchor Nine, two private tugs that might be compelled if their owners stopped bargaining with Edda.

The first model failed and cut fragments through Anchor Nine.

She deleted nothing. Failure paths mattered.

The second model saved the platform and killed Noor's weather chain.

The third model spread debris too widely, creating a reentry lottery no government would forgive and no conscience should accept.

On the seventh run, a line appeared that might work. It used H-5 to slow the cadence, H-6 to widen the sheet, Kira's tug to spoil the densest group, and a later correction if another engine could be found. Not safe. Safer. The word had become a ration.

"We can damp the shell," she told Vera.

"Damp is not enough."

"Then we cascade downward into drag corridors."

"Risks?"

Sian forced herself to say it plainly. "If timing fails, we concentrate debris through Anchor Nine. If thrust estimates are wrong, we could blind Noor's east chain. If Helix fuel tables are false, the counter-burns may amplify instead of damp. If Kira misses phase, she may not have fuel to clear."

Silence on the command loop.

Kira answered first. "Then timing should avoid failing."

Sian laughed once, badly. "I will put that in the math."

Malik came in from Anchor Nine. "Doctor Rook, I have patients staged at a ferry that cannot yet leave. Tell me whether your plan improves their next hour."

That was better than forgiveness. It was a useful question.

"Yes," Sian said. "If we can command H-5 and H-6 in the next ten minutes, it improves their next hour."

"Then improve it."

She nodded though he could not need the gesture.

Joss transferred her latest run to the traffic wall. The red paths shifted under the proposed burns. The densest line moved away from Anchor Nine by kilometers, not enough for comfort, enough for action.

Vera said, "Prepare counter-plan Alpha."

"Do not call it Alpha," Sian said.

"Why?"

"Because the first plan always becomes a monument to optimism."

"Prepare counter-plan."

"Preparing."

Sian opened a new file and began stripping beauty out of the equations. The model did not need elegance. It needed blunt margins, abort points, and room for people to be wrong without killing strangers.

She forced herself to write assumptions in plain language. Helix fuel tables may be false. Tug release may lag. Weather data degraded after fragment contact. Human operators under stress may choose survival over sequence. The sentences looked ugly beside the equations. Good.

Joss watched the shared file populate. "You are writing a confession."

"I am writing a tool."

"Those can resemble each other."

She saved the file after every change. A younger Sian would have trusted the system to remember. This Sian trusted redundancy, shame, and keyboard shortcuts.

Chapter 15: Evacuation Ferry

Captain Ren Ito named his ferry Patiently because he enjoyed jokes that tempted fate. Patiently sat docked to Anchor Nine, loaded with too few seats and too much responsibility.

"I can take six critical, four crew, and regret," he told Malik.

"You will take eight critical."

"The ferry has six critical mounts."

"Then two become creative."

"Creative gets people killed."

"So does arithmetic without imagination."

They argued while nurses moved patients in suits through the collar. The suits were soft emergency shells, not meant for long exposure, with clear hoods and stiff gloves that made frightened adults look childlike. Malik hated that and kept his voice practical. Check seal. Confirm line. Name and response. Move. Lock. Breathe.

Outside, debris warnings stacked on Ito's display. No corridor remained clean for more than twelve seconds. A ferry needed twenty-two to undock, clear the platform, establish its descent-safe track, and avoid turning itself into a new set of fragments. Ito stared at the numbers as if personal dislike might improve them.

"I can shave two seconds on warm departure," he said.

"You told me last month that warm departures were for smugglers and fools."

"We are full of doctors. That averages out."

"It does not."

Cera secured the second decompression patient in a ferry mount. The man's face was slack behind the clear hood. His monitor pulsed green, then amber, then green again as the ferry accepted the feed. Malik did not like amber. Amber was a bureaucrat of a color, always warning, never deciding.

Vera offered a delayed window in thirty minutes. Malik looked at the ward temperature. Thirty-one point seven. The heat gave him his answer.

"We may not have thirty."

"How long do you have?" Vera asked.

"With the patients in the collar and ferry support? Longer. With the ward as it is? Not enough. Exactitude would be decorative."

Ito studied the debris plot. "If Kira shifts H-3, I get eighteen seconds."

"You just said you need twenty-two."

"Patiently is an optimistic vessel."

Dr. Maas, strapped to support, opened one eye. "That is a terrible name for a rescue craft."

Ito grinned. "You are not wrong."

"Captain," she said, "if you launch before the corridor clears, I will haunt your certification file."

"Doctor, if I launch before the corridor clears, paperwork will be the least of my spiritual problems."

Malik checked her pump, then tucked a loose strap beneath the frame. "No haunting without medical release."

She gave him a tired look. "You have become controlling."

"Disaster brings out my management style."

A warning tone rose in the ferry. Ito silenced it with one motion. "Fragment group will cross aft of platform in nine minutes. If traffic gives me eighteen clean seconds and I accept a high roll rate, I can clear."

"High roll rate with heart support?" Malik asked.

"Uncomfortable, not fatal."

"That is not a medical term."

"It is a pilot term. We have fewer syllables."

Malik looked at the patients who would remain if they launched: two technicians, one nurse, maybe himself if he chose badly. Evacuation was not rescue when it selected. Medicine made him practice triage. He still hated it every time.

"Vera," he said, "if Kira improves the window, we need launch authority immediately. I cannot stage them forever."

"Understood."

"Say accepted."

A pause. Then Vera said, "Accepted."

The ferry remained docked, waiting for a gap that physics had not yet agreed to provide. Patiently hummed against the collar, warm now, ready. The name was a joke, but the machine was not. It held people, power, oxygen, and one possible path away from a platform being slowly taught its age.

Malik rested two fingers on Dr. Maas's frame. Not comfort exactly. Contact. She noticed.

"Still here?" she asked.

"Offensively."

"Good."

Outside, Kira moved toward H-3. Inside, Ito kept one hand near the undock sequence and did not touch it.

Chapter 16: Blocked Corridor

Traffic corridors closed one after another. Launches scrubbed. Return capsules held. Tourist stations complained until Vera sent them the raw debris plot and the complaints became prayerful.

The closures moved down the traffic board like a fever. Green lanes became amber, amber became red, and red grew black bars through their centers. A launch vehicle with a research crew held below interface altitude and burned fuel it had not planned to spend. Two return capsules delayed deorbit and asked for carbon dioxide margins. A tourist station reported "guest distress" in a tone that suggested guest refund was the larger terror. Vera sent them the raw debris plot and the complaints became prayerful.

Noor's weather chain burned two satellites higher, saving them and spending fuel meant for storm season. "I am robbing August to pay orbit," she said.

"Noted," Vera replied.

"Do not note it. Remember it when budget committees ask why forecasts degrade."

"I will remember."

"So will storms."

Joss kept updating paths. The harmonic shell turned good corridors bad faster than traffic could certify them. Every solution aged in minutes. The software marked one corridor usable for a ferry, then stripped it away when H-5 whispered a correction burn. Another opened near a weather satellite and vanished under radar refinement. The sky was full of almosts.

Kira disabled H-3's antenna with a capture-arm strike that made Pavel curse and cheer together. The pulse stopped. Two other satellites continued.

"Amplification reduced," Joss said. "Not gone."

"Corridor for Patiently?" Malik asked.

Joss ran the window. "Sixteen seconds."

Ito said, "I can abuse sixteen."

Vera said, "You will wait."

Ito did not answer. That worried everyone.

"Captain," Vera said.

"I heard you."

"Say holding."

A pause. "Holding."

Malik's voice came in low. "He is holding with his face poorly."

"My face is not on the loop," Ito said.

"It is in my ward."

Kira checked fuel again. Enough for intercept, maybe enough for one counter-burn. Little Argument had never been designed to conduct a shell. It barely conducted itself. The tug's reserves sat in columns on her display: primary, abort, contingency, lies pilots told themselves. She had less than she wanted and more than nothing.

Vera shifted command priority. "No launches through Shell C. No reentries without traffic escort. All private tugs in adjacent shells submit fuel and thrust capability now."

Edda added, "Owners refusing emergency pooling will be named in the liability record."

"That was fast," Joss said.

"Fear is efficient when itemized."

Tane opened two more Helix command gateways. H-5 and H-6 appeared on the traffic wall as red-outlined assets: dangerous, suspect, usable. Kira disliked seeing them promoted from threat to tool. It was like asking an arsonist for water because he happened to own the hose.

Noor reported East Four yawed shield-first despite its complaining wheel. "It is screaming in telemetry, but it is turned."

"Will it hold?"

"It is old and annoyed. Yes."

Another corridor died. The board refreshed. Patiently's possible window remained at sixteen seconds, then dipped to fifteen point eight.

"Do not say it," Vera told Ito.

"I said nothing."

"Your silence has body language."

Kira smiled once and let it go. Humor was thin now, but thin things could still hold.

The next fragment group approached H-5's influence point. Sian's counter-plan waited on the side display, not authorized yet, not ready enough, better than the black corridor shrinking around them.

Vera held the authorization with one finger above the control. She could feel the room wanting action. Want was dangerous. Action too early could make the next hour worse.

"Joss?"

"Still converging."

"Sian?"

"Still ugly."

"Kira?"

"Still here."

The answers were not permission, but they were a shape. Vera waited three more seconds, long enough to prove she was not being rushed by fear, short enough not to be ruled by it. Then another fragment track crossed the ferry corridor and made the decision lean toward motion.

Chapter 17: The Hidden Burn

Kira found the unauthorized fuel by watching a dead satellite twitch. H-5 fired a thruster for less than a second, too small for public telemetry, enough to shift a fragment group. Its tank should have been empty.

The twitch was small enough that a bored operator might have blamed sensor noise. Kira was not bored. Boredom had left the cockpit at the first red alarm and would not be invited back. H-5's aft corner shifted against the star field, a nudge, a correction, a secret flex.

"Hidden burn confirmed," she said. "H-5, aft thruster, duration under one second."

Joss compared thermal data. "I have heat bloom. Small but real."

Tane closed his eyes.

Vera's voice sharpened. "How many retained fuel?"

"Four platforms," Tane said.

"Full sentence."

"Four H-series platforms retained experimental reserve propellant. H-2, H-5, H-6, H-8. It was for disposal assurance."

Sian said, "You retained fuel in a crowded shell under dead status?"

"It was sealed."

"It is firing."

"Autonomy must have misclassified the collision state."

"Autonomy cannot misclassify what you do not hide in the first place."

Edda, still on loop, asked whether Helix disclosed reserve fuel to insurers. Tane did not answer. That was answer enough for later. Right now, it meant engines existed.

Kira watched the effect ripple through the model. The fragment group that should have spread instead tightened by a fraction. A fraction in orbit could become kilometers later or centimeters too late.

Joss updated the counter-resonance plan. "If we can command H-5 and H-6, Kira's tug becomes point three. H-8 maybe point four if its tank pressure is usable. H-2 unknown."

"Command keys?" Vera asked.

Tane hesitated.

Vera did not raise her voice. "Mr. Orlo."

"The old keys are in cold archive. They require dual release."

"Release them."

"I need authorization."

"You have an emergency order."

"Internal systems may reject--"

Kira looked at the debris track approaching Anchor Nine. She pictured Malik braced in the hot ward, Ito with one hand near undock, Dr. Maas making jokes because fear needed somewhere to go. "Mr. Orlo, every second you protect your company spends someone else's hull."

The loop held that sentence.

Tane turned away from camera and spoke to someone offscreen. Kira could not hear the words, only the shape of urgency becoming surrender. Eleven seconds later, the keys arrived.

Joss pulled them into a quarantined command stack. "Validating."

"Do not send anything without traffic review," Sian said.

"I enjoy not making disasters worse."

"Then we share a hobby."

H-5 answered the first handshake in obsolete syntax. H-6 answered after a delay. H-8 returned a partial response and a pressure warning. H-2 did not answer at all.

"We have three possible engines," Joss said. "Two trustworthy enough for desperate people."

"Desperate people are listening," Malik said.

Vera looked at the traffic wall. "Prepare first counter-burn sequence but hold."

Kira adjusted Little Argument's attitude. The tug's nose pointed toward a panel remnant that could become her part of the plan. The capture arm hung with a new tremor after striking H-3. She flexed it. It moved, ugly but obedient.

"Pavel," she said, "tell your arm it may have to become math."

"My arm prefers invoices."

"So does Helix."

"Then my arm is morally compromised."

The hidden burn had betrayed the lie. It had also opened a door. Kira hated doors that led through fire, but closed doors killed too.

H-5's telemetry settled into the traffic display, no longer a ghost, not yet an ally. Joss tagged it usable under protest. Sian added a warning band around its predicted thrust. Pavel asked whether satellites could be charged with perjury. Edda said not yet.

Tane stayed silent through all of it. Kira preferred him that way until he spoke again.

"H-5's tank heater may be cycling," he said. "If you burn after the heater peaks, pressure stabilizes."

Joss checked. "He's right."

No one thanked him. The information entered the plan. Usefulness, Kira thought, was not the same as absolution.

Chapter 18: Ground Argument

Ground command became a room of raised voices and fixed math. Helix wanted proprietary command isolation. Weather wanted satellite priority. Medical wanted Anchor Nine evacuation. Traffic wanted no one to make orbit worse. Insurance wanted a record that would survive blame.

Vera listened until the arguments began repeating. Repetition meant people had stopped thinking and started defending territory. She had no use for territory. Territory did not survive in orbit; only trajectories did.

The command room smelled of coffee gone sour and overheated electronics. A junior officer stood with a headset in one hand, waiting for permission to interrupt with a launch operator's complaint. Vera shook her head once. The complaint could orbit by itself.

"Enough," she said. "All command keys to traffic. All private assets under emergency pooling. All models public to this loop. Anyone withholding data can explain it after the shell stops trying to eat my stations."

Tane objected. "Helix cannot allow unfiltered control of proprietary platforms."

Vera cut him off. "You have mistaken ownership for control."

"Those assets remain Helix property."

"Those assets are firing in a public shell under false retirement status. They are evidence, hazards, and possible tools. Property is fourth at best."

Edda said, "I will support that hierarchy in writing."

"Of course you will," Tane said bitterly.

"Do not confuse usefulness with friendship."

Noor wanted weather assets protected from any burn that widened the debris sheet. Malik wanted Patiently released the moment a corridor existed. Ito wanted release before it existed. Helix wanted logs sanitized. Sian wanted more time. Joss wanted everyone to stop using the word safe.

Vera gave none of them what they wanted entirely.

Joss sent the updated score. Sian hated the word score, then used it because everyone understood. H-5 burn at phase minus twelve. H-6 at plus four. Kira tug at plus nine. Anchor drone if available at plus sixteen. The goal was not to stop debris. It was to make debris miss what mattered and fall where atmosphere could finish the work.

"The atmosphere is our cleanup asset," Sian said. "But we must feed it slowly enough that reentry risk stays tolerable and fast enough that the shell stops amplifying."

"Tolerable to whom?" Noor asked.

"To the people under the lanes."

"Name them."

Sian did. Ocean corridors first, then three coastal alert regions with low probability of surviving fragments, then aviation reroutes. Noor nodded, not pleased, but answered.

"Weather can support that if East Four holds."

"Medical?" Vera asked.

Malik looked offscreen, probably at patients. "If Patiently gets a real window in the next twenty minutes, I can move the critical cases. After that, heat and stress become treatment decisions."

"Captain Ito?"

"Give me eighteen seconds and I will make it look like twenty-two."

"That is not an answer I can put in a log."

"Write: ferry pilot overconfident but motivated."

Vera almost smiled. Almost. "Noted privately."

Malik asked how sure they were.

Sian answered, "Sure is not available."

"Then what is?"

"Better than waiting."

Malik looked at his patients. Medicine was full of that category.

Vera set her palms on the console. "We execute when H-5 reaches phase point. Abort if H-6 fails pre-burn check, if Kira loses arm function, or if Anchor Nine takes pressure breach. No unilateral action."

Ito said nothing.

"Captain."

"Holding," he said, and this time it sounded almost true.

Ground command quieted. Not calm. Quiet was what happened when argument became work. Joss began the countdown to the first possible counter-burn, and every person on the loop found something to do with their hands.

Vera walked once behind the consoles. She looked not at screens but at faces: the analyst biting her lip bloody, the comms officer writing public language with shaking fingers, Joss rubbing his thumb over a key worn smooth. A command room was also a body. It carried tension in parts.

"Water," she told the aide.

"For you?"

"For anyone who forgot they have blood."

The aide blinked, then started passing bulbs. Tiny acts did not change orbits. They kept people accurate enough to do it.

Chapter 19: Metal Rain Forecast

Noor's forecast showed what happened if the shell expanded: weather-chain loss, navigation relay damage, polar comms interruption, reentry warnings scattered across three continents. Most fragments would burn. Some would not. The sky would become a bad lottery.

"Metal rain," she said. "Small, fast, and politically surprising."

Her staff had gone silent behind her. They knew the maps too well. A red smear across ocean was not empty if fishing fleets worked under it. A yellow caution over mountains meant relay gaps for clinics that already ordered medicine late. A probability cone over air lanes meant controllers on the ground would spend hours moving aircraft around a danger nobody could see from a cockpit window.

Vera asked for casualty projections. Noor refused the first number her software produced because it pretended precision.

"Say risk zones," Vera ordered.

Noor mapped them. Hospitals depending on satellite timing. Fishing fleets under storms. Mountain towns losing relay. Air corridors rerouted under blind forecasts. Coastal emergency managers who would receive noisier storm products if East Three died. The list turned orbit into streets, names, harbors, ridges.

Kira listened while strapped inside a tug smaller than some conference rooms. Orbit always felt separate until someone named the ground beneath it. She had grown up in a city where ambulances used satellite timing to thread old streets and storm drains failed when forecasts came late. She had left ground because sky work seemed cleaner. That had been youth, not truth.

"Can we warn the risk zones now?" Vera asked.

"Yes," Noor said. "But if we over-warn and nothing falls, we spend trust."

"If we under-warn and something falls?"

"We spend people."

"Warn."

Noor nodded and sent the alerts with language stripped of drama: orbital debris reentry risk, low probability, avoid exposed areas during specified windows, aviation and maritime notices pending. She hated how small the words looked beside the map.

Sian updated drag corridors. "If counter-burn works, most debris decays over ocean lanes."

"Most," Edda said.

"Most is what physics offers."

"Insurance dislikes most."

"So does conscience."

Kira acquired H-5 command link. The dead satellite answered slowly, like an old man waking in another language. Its status packet arrived in fragments: tank pressure low but present, thruster bank one degraded, attitude solution stale, autonomy locked in emergency disposal mode.

"Engine point one ready," she said.

Joss exhaled. "The orchestra has its first instrument."

"Call it that again and I cut comms."

"Engine point one ready," he corrected.

"Better."

Pavel checked Little Argument's arm telemetry. "Your joint temperature is high."

"The joint is embarrassed."

"The joint is damaged."

"Can it hold?"

"For a definition of hold I dislike."

"Send me the definition."

He sent a green mark with three amber footnotes. Kira accepted before he could reconsider.

On the traffic wall, the countdown reached five minutes to H-5's phase point. Joss began calling checks. H-5 command: live. H-6 command: pending. Kira tug: live. Anchor drone: low thrust, available. Weather chain: degraded, tracked. Patiently: docked, warm, angry.

"I object to angry as status," Ito said.

"Status revised to strongly motivated," Joss replied.

Noor's alerts went out to ground. Somewhere, phones would buzz. Controllers would change routes. Harbor masters would squint at bulletins written by people trying not to frighten them. Noor watched the send confirmation and whispered something off loop. Kira could not hear the words. She understood the shape of them anyway.

Noor's deputy asked whether to soften the maritime warning. Too many cautions and captains stopped listening. Too few and they sailed into trouble with official permission.

"Say what we know," Noor said. "Say what we do not know. Do not decorate either."

The deputy sent it. A fishing fleet acknowledged first, then an airline dispatch office, then a coastal emergency manager with three words: received, staging generators. Noor stared at those words longer than necessary. Orbit had touched a basement full of generators and people in rain jackets. The chain was real.

Chapter 20: The Quiet Satellite

Kira captured H-4 to pull its data core. The satellite was quieter than H-3, no active pulse, no twitch, just a dead box pretending to have died honestly. The capture arm closed around a torn truss. Pavel's patch held.

"Ugly capture successful," she said.

"Arm offended but intact," Pavel replied.

"You always know the right romantic thing."

"I speak fluent machinery."

H-4 rotated slowly inside the arm's cradle. Its surface was pitted with micrometeorite freckles, but unlike H-3 it showed no active heat and no signal chatter. That made it valuable. A quiet satellite could tell the truth without continuing to commit crimes.

Kira opened the service panel with a remote tool. The latch resisted. She rocked the tool twice, careful not to load joint two beyond Pavel's amber footnotes. The panel lifted. Inside, the data core sat in a shielded slot, black, palm-sized, and stubborn. She extended the extraction probe.

"Do not drop it," Joss said.

"I was planning interpretive juggling."

"Please don't."

The probe latched. The core came free with a small jerk that made the arm complain. Data streamed slowly through Little Argument, compressed, encrypted, then opened with keys Tane had stopped pretending not to have. Modified software. Autonomous phase herding. Hidden fuel tables. Test schedule marked dormant but not removed. Collision response routines that classified nearby debris as targets for cooperative decay and nearby satellites as assistants, regardless of public traffic state.

Tane watched the data appear. "That was not approved for operation."

Sian said, "It was approved for existence in the wrong place."

"The dormant flag should have prevented activation."

"Should is not a system."

Joss traced the wake-up sequence. The initial collision had damaged H-3's service state and triggered an emergency disposal routine. H-3 woke H-5, H-6, H-8, and tried H-2. The cluster interpreted fragments as a herd to be corrected. Without public traffic integration, without full mass estimates, without active oversight, it corrected them into resonance. A cleanup experiment had turned a debris cloud into a moving blade.

"It is not malicious," Tane said quietly.

Kira looked at the red lines crossing Anchor Nine's risk bubble. "Good. I would hate for it to have hurt feelings."

Vera ordered all remaining Helix platforms isolated.

Joss warned isolation might also remove their ability to counter-burn. "If we cut every command and power path, we lose engines we need. If we leave autonomy intact, it keeps pushing."

There it was: the thing causing disaster was now one of the few tools available to stop it.

Sian proposed a partial isolation. Disable autonomous cueing, retain manual thruster command, lock all burns through traffic with public logging. It was the system she had insisted on in the old paper and not received until the sky was already bleeding.

"Can you do it?" Vera asked Tane.

"With engineering support, yes."

"Can you do it fast?"

He looked older than an hour ago. "We will."

Edda said, "I am recording that commitment."

"I assumed."

Kira pushed away from H-4. The data core remained tethered to her probe, streaming the last of its ugly truth. "I hate useful mistakes."

Pavel said, "Then you hate most engineering."

"I tolerate yours."

"That is the nicest thing you have said under emergency conditions."

"Do not make me regret it."

Behind her, H-4 drifted quiet again. Ahead, H-5 waited for a burn that would either widen the blade or begin to dull it.

Kira stowed the extracted core in a padded bay and labeled it with a gloved finger. Evidence, hazard, lesson. The tug's label system accepted only one field, so she chose evidence. The other two would have to travel with it.

"You clear of H-4?" Joss asked.

"Clearing."

"Do not drift into the burn cone."

"I had other plans."

She moved Little Argument away from the quiet satellite and felt the tug answer sluggishly. Every correction cost more now. The machine was keeping count even if she pretended not to.

Chapter 21: Score Draft

Joss and Sian drafted the counter-score in a shared simulation that crashed twice and insulted everyone by being right only on the third run. The plan used the same resonance Helix had awakened, but inverted its timing. Instead of tightening fragments into traffic lanes, the burns would spread them toward drag.

"This is not cleanup," Sian said. "It is triage."

"Triage saves lives," Malik answered.

The shared simulation ran on three linked systems because no single room owned enough clean data. Joss handled traffic propagation. Sian handled resonance damping. Noor fed weather asset constraints. Helix engineers, now very quiet, supplied degraded thruster maps. Every time the model asked for certainty, someone had to answer with a range.

On the wall, burn points appeared as pale marks along red debris paths. H-5 first. H-6 second. Kira third. H-8 if it survived pointing. Anchor drone if its little engine did not embarrass itself. Grace Meridian, the private tug Edda had compelled, might arrive too late but would be kept in the score because hope and scheduling shared a distant ancestor.

Kira reviewed her burn. Little Argument would attach to a fuel tank remnant, use its own engine to shift the phase, then release before the tank's tumble tore the arm off. The tank was not her first choice. Her first choice was an object with handles, manners, and no angular momentum. The sky had declined to provide one.

"How much margin?" she asked.

Joss said, "Do you want numbers or comfort?"

"Numbers."

"Thirty-two percent arm-loss probability. Eleven percent tug attitude excursion beyond recovery without reserve burn. Fuel after release depends on how long H-6 lags."

"Comfort?"

"Pavel is not listening."

Pavel was listening. "I hate all of you."

"That is comfort," Kira said. "Familiar environment."

The simulation crashed again when H-8's pressure curve produced a divide-by-zero error. Sian stared at the frozen screen with calm murderous attention.

"Even software refuses Helix fuel tables," Joss said.

"Restart from checkpoint," she said.

On the third run, the model became usable. The first full simulation saved Anchor Nine, preserved two weather satellites, and opened a dirty but possible ferry corridor for Patiently. It sacrificed several commercial relays, forced one launch stack to abort to ground, and turned five debris groups into planned decay paths over ocean. Not clean. Usable.

Noor looked at her two saved weather satellites and the one still likely to die. "I can live with this."

"Can your network?" Vera asked.

"With a limp."

Malik said, "A limp is not death."

"No," Noor said. "It is work after."

Vera authorized preparation. "We are not playing music. We are moving trash away from people."

Sian nodded. That was the sentence she needed. It stripped the beauty out and left duty.

Joss saved the run and gave it three redundant names because failed archives had no romance. Sian made him add the failed runs too. The bad futures mattered. They showed where a wrong assumption turned into an impact, where a late burn widened into reentry warnings, where a ferry window closed like a door.

"Operators will ask why we did not choose the cleaner line," she said.

"There is no cleaner line."

"Then they need to see the dirt."

Kira reviewed the simulation from the cockpit. The little icon that stood for her tug slid into the densest part of the plan, held, released, and tumbled away. Icons did not sweat. Icons did not have Pavel in their ear or a cracked antenna or a dead arm waiting to fail. She watched the run twice, then marked one point with a private note: release before pride.

The note looked melodramatic as soon as she wrote it. She kept it anyway. Cockpits collected worse wisdom: check seals, trust valves only once, never chase a tumble you have not counted. She taped the paper where the dead arm camera could not hide it.

"You writing literature?" Joss asked.

"Maintenance warning."

"To whom?"

"Future idiot."

Pavel said, "Current idiot has logged receipt."

Chapter 22: Too Few Tugs

The asset count was cruel. Three active debris tugs in range, one with low fuel, one with a cracked nozzle, one owned by a company whose legal department was faster than its engines. Two maintenance drones near Anchor Nine. Four Helix satellites with hidden fuel. Little Argument in position. Still short.

"We need three more thrust points," Joss said.

"We do not have them," Vera replied.

The traffic board made scarcity visible. Each potential engine had a box: thrust, fuel, command delay, alignment, owner, risk. Most boxes were red. A debris tug called Redcap had enough fuel but a cracked nozzle that would turn a clean burn into a spray of uncertainty. Grace Meridian had excellent thrust and a corporate owner currently learning what emergency pooling meant. A telecom skiff could push, but only if it abandoned a platform full of replacement parts. The maintenance drones near Anchor Nine had engines better suited to docking than saving civilization.

Kira studied the shell. "We have dead satellites."

"Dead means dead," Vera said.

"Helix dead apparently means secret fuel and bad decisions. Can we use that?"

Sian ran a quick model. "If we command H-5, H-6, and H-8, yes. But their thrusters are not aligned for clean burns."

"Nothing is clean."

"H-8 may rupture."

"Will it rupture usefully?"

"That is a terrible question."

"But?"

"Maybe."

Tane provided more keys, each one sounding like a tooth pulled from his company. Edda recorded every transfer with time stamps and visible satisfaction. Vera ignored the legal commentary unless it opened a door.

Noor offered two weather satellites for minor trim burns if it saved the chain. Sian refused; their fuel was too precious for storms below.

"Thank you," Noor said.

"Do not thank me. I am prioritizing future panic."

"Future panic has a department in my office."

The score changed again. Little Argument would do more. Kira did not mention that her fuel margin had become a rumor. Pavel mentioned it for her.

"Your post-burn reserve is below return preference."

"Preference is not requirement."

"Below requirement if anything goes wrong."

"Things have been going so well."

"Kira."

She let the silence sit, then said, "I hear you."

It was not agreement. It was respect. In their line of work, that often had to do.

Edda returned with Grace Meridian's owner on a side loop, a man whose first sentence included the phrase asset exposure. Vera let him finish because interrupting too early sometimes made fools feel important.

"Your tug can enter Shell C in thirty-one minutes," Vera said.

"Under protest."

"Under thrust is what matters."

Edda sent the indemnity order. "Your protest is recorded and insured."

The owner disappeared from the loop. Grace Meridian's icon changed from red to amber, then amber to moving. It would be late, but late engines sometimes still caught falling work. Noor watched the icons and shook her head. "We built a civilization that needs spare engines in the sky and then acted surprised when we needed them."

"Redundancy is always waste until the day it becomes mercy," Sian said.

The line stayed with Kira. She looked at Little Argument's fuel again and wished mercy came in tanks. Grace Meridian's slow approach did not solve the immediate problem, but its movement changed the room. A coming engine, even late, made people revise despair into scheduling.

Vera assigned Grace Meridian a rescue contingency and a possible late trim burn. Sal Arro, its pilot, answered with a voice full of static and professional offense.

"I leave port for rescue, not philosophy."

"Today you get both," Vera said.

"Do I bill philosophy hourly?"

Edda answered, "Only if properly itemized."

The exchange was brief, but it put another living pilot into the model. Kira saw Grace Meridian's icon and felt the strange comfort of not being the only stubborn dot in the shell.

Chapter 23: Borrowed Engines

Borrowed engines are never free. H-5 answered command with a lag. H-6 had a clogged thruster. H-8 spun like a coin and needed two burns just to point wrong less enthusiastically.

Kira moved between them, tagging, cueing, confirming plumes. Little Argument's cockpit smelled faintly of warm insulation. She did not tell Pavel because Pavel's relationship with heat was personal and loud.

"Your thermal margin is narrowing," he said.

"Then you know."

"I know everything my machines suffer."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is why I am charming."

H-6 fired on test and shoved itself two centimeters off predicted axis. Joss cursed softly. Sian adjusted. The score became less elegant with every real object. Good. Elegance had caused enough trouble. Real machines coughed, lagged, and translated theory into grudging motion.

Kira used Little Argument's lights to inspect H-8. The satellite spun with a damaged panel throwing flashes into the camera. Its tank pressure sat in a range that Helix labeled acceptable and Pavel labeled fiction with units.

"H-8 can provide one major burn or two insults," Pavel said.

"Which do we need?"

"A miracle with documentation."

"Put it in the queue."

Malik reported Anchor Nine ward temperature stabilizing only because they powered down half the module. Patients lay under blankets in a station getting too warm and too cold in the wrong places. He had moved the remaining burn patients into the collar spoke. The chapel window was shuttered. The paper flower had been removed because fire load stopped being theoretical under heat stress.

Captain Ito kept Patiently ready. "When you make me a gap, I go."

"When traffic certifies a gap," Vera said.

"Naturally."

Kira heard the lie and admired it. Pilots respected certification most after using it for cover.

She armed H-5 for the first counter-burn. The command traveled through traffic authority, Helix gateway, obsolete maintenance syntax, and whatever stubborn little controller still lived in the satellite. H-5 answered with a lag that made everyone lean forward.

"Armed," Joss said. "Burn inhibited pending mark."

Outside Kira's window, the borrowed engines hung among fragments and old sunlight. They were guilty tools. They would have to do.

Helix engineering stayed on the loop now, stripped of corporate polish. A woman named Rada, voice rough from being called in at the wrong hour, talked Kira through H-8's attitude quirk.

"It will overcorrect if you wake bank two first," Rada said.

"Why?"

"Because we told management it needed a software patch and management preferred a schedule patch."

Tane closed his eyes. No one protected him from the answer.

Kira liked Rada immediately. Competence often arrived carrying bitterness. The borrowed engines became less abstract as their flaws acquired voices. H-5 lagged like an old valve. H-6 kicked sideways. H-8 lied when cold. Little Argument overheated and pretended dignity.

"What about H-2?" Kira asked.

Rada's voice lost some of its edge. "H-2 was the cleanest bus in the group. Newer battery stack, better seals. If it wakes, trust the engine. If it does not wake, assume the fault is upstream and do not wait for it."

"That sounds like advice with a history."

"Everything in this cluster has history. We were told not to write most of it down."

Tane did not interrupt. Silence, Kira thought, was the first useful thing he had contributed without being forced.

Rada stayed with them through the next checks. She corrected a Helix table twice and once said, "No, that annotation was management language. Use the lower number." Kira could almost hear Tane aging.

"Rada," Sian said, "were these concerns documented?"

"Yes."

"Where?"

"Appendix files."

Edda gave a humorless laugh. "The graveyard of warnings."

Rada did not defend it. "Yes."

The room accepted the word because there was no time to mourn every ignored page.

Chapter 24: Helix Secret

Tane admitted the test because the code had already confessed. Helix had run automated debris-herding trials in a retired cluster to prove lower-cost cleanup for future contracts. The system was meant to remain passive unless triggered by proximity alerts. The collision woke it. Hidden fuel made it dangerous. Proprietary silence made it worse.

"Who approved retaining fuel?" Vera asked.

"Program division."

"Names."

Tane looked older. "I will provide them."

Edda said, "Now."

He did. Three executives, two program leads, one safety officer who had written objections until the objections became appendices. The names appeared in the emergency archive beside time stamps and document IDs. They looked too small for the damage around them.

Sian listened with an expression Kira could not read. Her old work had been used badly, but bad use had names now. That mattered. Blame without names became weather. Names made it human, which was uglier and more useful.

"Why hide fuel?" Noor asked.

Tane's mouth tightened. "Retained maneuvering capacity improved contract demonstrations. It allowed Helix to show cleanup readiness without scheduling active launches."

"So the sky was a showroom," Kira said.

No one corrected her.

"Can Helix help finish the counter-burns?" Vera asked.

Tane looked at the debris model. "Yes."

"Will Helix help?"

"Yes."

No one thanked him. Gratitude would have insulted the people dodging his company's fragments.

Kira received updated thruster maps from Helix engineering. They were messy, incomplete, and exactly what she needed. The maps included nozzle misalignment, suspected valve delays, thermal limits, and handwritten comments from some engineer who had clearly never believed the retirement status. One note beside H-6 read: bank two sticks after cold soak; do not trust first pulse.

"Finally," Pavel said, "a Helix document with a conscience."

"Can we use it?" Kira asked.

"Yes. I hate that too."

She compared the map with her visual inspection. H-6's clogged thruster would fire late and off-axis. If she nudged the satellite before burn, the error could become predictable.

"Little Argument to traffic," she said. "I can make H-6 misbehave predictably."

Joss answered, "That may be the best phrase of the day."

Sian added the adjustment. The model improved by a narrow, priceless line.

Tane stayed on the loop after the confession, no longer trying to manage the room. Once, when a Helix engineer hesitated before releasing a diagnostic table, he said, "Send it." The engineer did. The table showed a valve delay that changed Kira's burn by half a second.

"Half a second would have mattered," Sian said.

Tane looked at the model. "I know."

"Now you do."

That was crueler than anger. It was also accurate. Knowledge after damage was still knowledge, but it carried no innocence. Vera assigned Helix personnel to technical support under traffic supervision: no unsupervised command, no private side channels, every packet logged.

The first supervised packet arrived clean. The second contained a checksum error that might have been haste or habit. Vera rejected it and made Helix resend in full view of the loop. The delay cost fourteen seconds. It bought trust no one had time to discuss.

"Again," Vera said.

Tane nodded to someone offscreen. "Again."

The corrected file opened: H-6 thermal history, H-8 tank strain, H-2 dormant notes. Sian read them with a pencil in her hand though the file was digital. Some people needed a tool for anger.

The H-2 notes were sparse, which bothered her more than a long list of faults would have. Clean records could mean clean machines. They could also mean nobody had looked hard. She circled a line on the printout: receiver response intermittent during cold soak, no further action required.

"There," she said.

Joss leaned in. "That could explain later silence."

"Or nothing. But nothing is becoming crowded."

She added H-2 uncertainty to the model. A small gray band appeared around a future burn point. Gray bands were where arrogance liked to hide.

Chapter 25: Anchor Choice

Malik had to decide who left first if Patiently got a gap. Medicine liked protocols. Disasters liked making protocols look naive.

Heart support. Decompression. Burn injuries. Essential crew. One nurse who could keep two patients alive better than any machine. Himself last, unless staying made him useless. The list changed each time he looked at it, not because the patients changed, but because he kept trying to find mercy in order.

The ward collar was crowded now. Blankets floated where tape failed. Fans hummed under reduced power. The air had the damp, human smell of fear held politely. Cera moved between patients with a checklist on her sleeve and no wasted motion.

Dr. Maas watched him reorder the transfer list. "You are moving me down."

"You are stable."

"I am attached to a machine the size of a wardrobe."

"A stable wardrobe."

She smiled despite the heat. "I was a surgeon. We all think we're essential."

"Were you?"

"Often. Not always."

That helped.

Tomas, the burned technician, was awake and pretending not to listen. "Doctor, put Cera before me."

"I did."

"Good."

"Do not approve my triage. It makes me irritable."

Captain Ito reviewed the list and said nothing, which meant he accepted the cruelty. The ferry could not take everyone. Anchor Nine might have to hold. Ito's silence had weight because pilots liked to argue with constraints; when they stopped, the constraint was real.

"I can strap one ambulatory patient in the aft storage brace," Ito said.

"Certified?"

"No."

"Survivable?"

"If I fly like my mother is watching."

"Did your mother approve of you?"

"Rarely. It helped."

Malik put Tomas in the brace position. Tomas saw it and looked away. Consent was not always spoken; sometimes it was a man not making the doctor ask.

Malik sent the sequence to Vera. Then he opened private messages to two families and typed notes he had no right to send yet. He deleted them before sending. Premature farewell was a superstition and a burden.

Another debris ping sounded. The station held. For now, the station kept choosing them back.

Cera caught him looking at the transfer list again. "Changing it?"

"Trying not to."

"That is not the same."

"No."

She took the slate from his hand and read it without flinching. "I stay with you."

"You are on the ferry."

"You need a nurse."

"The patients on the ferry need you more."

She handed the slate back slowly. "I hate when you are right."

"You should not. It happens rarely."

The joke landed badly and still helped. Cera sealed her helmet and moved toward the ferry side of the collar. Malik did not thank her. Gratitude at the wrong time could crack a person open. He only nodded, and she nodded back.

Tomas watched the exchange from the brace position. "You always this calm?"

"No," Malik said.

"Good. It was making me feel inferior."

"I am terrified in an organized way."

"Can I try that?"

"Start with breathing. Organization later."

Tomas obeyed. Malik counted with him for four breaths, then moved to the next patient. Calm was not a feeling. It was a set of tasks performed while fear waited its turn.

At the hatch, Ito reviewed the transfer list and caught Malik's eye. "You hate it."

"Yes."

"Good. I worry about doctors who like triage."

"I worry about pilots who enjoy dirty windows."

"Enjoy is strong."

"So is tolerate."

Ito folded the slate against his suit. "I will take who you give me."

That was the whole bargain. Not comfort. Trust under constraint. Malik nodded and went back to the patients before either of them could improve it into sentiment.

Chapter 26: Pavel's Patch

Pavel's patch turned Little Argument's capture arm into something it was not certified to be: a remote burn brace. The arm could grip a tumbling object, read stress, and release before torque climbed past structural insult.

"Structural insult?" Kira asked.

"Engineering term for when a machine begins writing its resignation."

The patch removed safety locks. Pavel hated doing it. Kira hated needing it. They both approved the upload. The confirmation box asked whether the operator accepted responsibility for operation outside certified limits. Kira selected yes. Pavel selected yes on his side and muttered something about designers who put moral philosophy in dialog boxes.

H-6 drifted ahead, clogged thruster aligned badly enough to require physical persuasion. Kira brought Little Argument in close. The satellite's surface filled the forward camera: chipped paint, torn blanket, a maintenance lug set just off center. Not made for towing. Nothing today was made for what it had to do.

She captured the lug. The arm closed. Force rose.

"Contact," she said.

"Soft," Pavel warned.

"I know soft."

"You know collision at low speed."

"Same family."

She nudged the satellite with the tug's own body, a slow shove that moved through metal into her seat. H-6 began to rotate. The clogged thruster's bad axis shifted toward Sian's required tolerance.

"Stress rising," Pavel said.

"I am aware."

"My machine is aware harder."

The satellite settled within burn tolerance. Barely.

Joss marked H-6 green. H-5 green. H-8 yellow. Little Argument yellow. Anchor drone red but improving. The score looked like a wounded spreadsheet.

Kira flexed her gloved fingers though she was inside the cockpit. Pilots did useless body things before useful machine things. She checked the arm camera. Joint two vibrated in tiny visible pulses.

"Patch holds," she said.

Pavel exhaled. "Treat it gently."

"No."

"I know."

He sent her one more private line: "If it fails, let the satellite go."

She did not answer immediately. Then: "I know."

This time he accepted the lie because he had no better one to offer.

Pavel kept a second screen on the arm's raw sensor feed, bypassing the friendly interface because friendly interfaces hid pain. The numbers jittered. Load, temperature, vibration, lag. He knew what each meant in factory tests and knew those tests had not imagined Kira shoving a secret-fueled satellite in a debris storm.

One of his assistants asked whether to prepare a replacement arm requisition.

"Prepare three," Pavel said.

"Three?"

"One for the tug, one for certification, one for when Kira makes a face at the first two."

Kira heard him. "My faces improve hardware."

"Your faces void warranties."

The exchange steadied him more than the numbers did. Machines were easier to love when their pilots remained irritating.

He wrote one more line into the patch, a crude warning that would flash if oscillation started matching the arm's own resonance. He labeled it STOP ARGUING. His assistant glanced at the label and wisely said nothing.

Kira saw it when the upload completed. "Rude."

"Specific."

"It assumes I argue with mechanical facts."

"It records field conditions."

The patch went live. Somewhere above him, a damaged arm accepted instructions no certification board would have approved, and Pavel promised it replacement parts if it survived long enough to need them.

He opened a maintenance note and began writing the failure review before the failure happened. Not because he wanted to be right. Because if they survived, someone would ask why he had removed safety locks. He wrote the answer plainly: certified limits preserved hardware at unacceptable risk to crewed platform.

His assistant read over his shoulder. "That will make people angry."

"Good. Angry people stay awake in review meetings."

He saved the note and returned to telemetry. The arm's temperature climbed, and Pavel felt each degree like a personal accusation.

Chapter 27: Edda's Coverage

Edda released the Helix risk memos to Vera's command archive before Helix counsel could bury them under privilege. Her own company would be furious. She seemed almost peaceful.

"Why?" Kira asked on a private loop.

"Because if this is classified as natural collision, coverage pays, Helix hides, and unsafe systems become insurable routine."

"I thought insurance liked routine."

"Insurance likes priced risk. Secret engines are unpriced arrogance."

Edda's office was quiet behind her, but Kira could hear another call trying to break in, the muted buzz of people realizing a paper wall had been breached. The memos showed concern about autonomous resonance trials, hidden fuel disclosure, and possible regulatory exposure. They also showed senior approval to delay full reporting until commercial cleanup contracts matured. The language was bloodless. That made it worse. Harm entered the world through phrases like market readiness and staged compliance.

Vera added the memos to the emergency record. Tane did not protest. He was busy being useful, which was the only available dignity.

"My board will dislike this," Edda said.

"Will they fire you during an emergency?" Kira asked.

"Boards prefer scheduled cowardice."

"So later."

"Likely."

Noor sent updated weather impact. If counter-burn began within twelve minutes, two critical satellites survived. If not, storm prediction dropped hard across ocean lanes.

Joss called final sequence. Sian corrected him twice. Pavel corrected both. Kira armed Little Argument.

Edda said, "For the record, this maneuver is not covered."

Kira smiled. "Then it might work."

"For the record, that is not actuarial reasoning."

"Good. I am short on those."

The private loop closed. Kira looked at the armed burn queue and thought of all the systems now leaning on each other: law, math, engines, weather, medicine, one damaged arm. Coverage or not, the sky had become collective.

Edda did not leave the main loop. She started calling owners by policy number, which turned out to be more frightening than calling them by name. One private station released a docking drone after she quoted a cross-liability clause. A fuel depot admitted it had two reserve bottles that could be transferred to Grace Meridian if traffic cleared a path.

"Everyone is essential when convenient," Edda said.

"Everyone is connected when honest," Vera replied.

Edda paused, then added that sentence to the record too. Kira had always thought of insurance as paper wrapped around machines after the fact. Today it was a lever. Not noble. Not warm. A lever did not need warmth to move weight.

"Edda," Kira said, "when this is over, send Pavel the clause that pays for arms."

"There may not be one."

"Invent it."

"That is fraud."

"Then price rude fragments."

For the first time, Edda laughed. It was brief and startled, as if the sound had escaped a locked drawer. Then she returned to calling owners who had engines and excuses.

Her next call was less amusing. A station owner wanted written assurance that use of its drone would not imply admission of shared liability. Edda looked into the camera with a stillness that made the traffic room listen.

"Your drone can push a fragment away from a medical platform," she said. "If you withhold it to protect wording, I will make sure the wording survives you."

The drone released two minutes later. Vera marked it available. Edda did not smile. Some levers moved only when leaned on hard.

Kira watched the little drone icon wobble out of its dock. It was too weak to matter alone, but not nothing. That had become the day's arithmetic: not nothing, added often enough, changed a line.

"Does that drone have a name?" she asked.

Edda checked. "Asset D-14."

"Terrible name."

"Insurance prefers loveless nouns."

"No wonder you price misery."

"Accurately, when disclosed."

The drone joined the score under its loveless noun, carrying one small push and a sudden audience.

Chapter 28: First Counter-Burn

H-5 fired first. A dead satellite with a hidden engine became, for four seconds, honest. Its plume nudged the phase of the fragment group by a fraction that looked meaningless until Joss's model recalculated.

"Shift confirmed," he said. "Again."

H-5 fired again. Debris that would have crossed Anchor Nine's outer edge drifted toward a lower decay corridor. Still dangerous. Less murderous. On the wall, a red line bent away from the platform by a distance too small to celebrate and too large to ignore.

"H-6 next," Vera said.

Kira held Little Argument steady near H-6. Her burn came third, tied to the satellite's bad thrust. She watched the timer: seven, six, five.

Sian's voice cut in. "Kira, your release window is shorter than modeled."

"By?"

"One point two seconds."

"Luxury."

Pavel said something unprintable.

The timer hit zero. Kira fired. The tug shook. H-6 answered late, then hard. Its clogged thruster cleared in a dirty burst that kicked the satellite against the arm. Kira's harness bit her ribs. Force bars climbed amber, orange, red.

"Release," Pavel said.

"Not yet."

"Release."

She held through the late plume because releasing early would spoil the phase and send the fragment group back toward Anchor Nine. The arm screamed through telemetry. Little Argument rolled three degrees, then five. Kira burned lateral to hold the line.

"Window," Sian said.

Kira released at the edge of red.

For one sick second, nothing improved. The model waited for radar to catch up. Joss's hands hovered above keys, useless for once.

Then the red track widened.

"Counter-resonance taking," he said.

No one cheered. There were too many burns left. But Kira let herself press one hand against the console in thanks to a machine that had not quit. The arm hung crooked in the camera, alive by spite and Pavel's bad decisions.

Joss replayed the burn only once before saving it. There was no time for admiration, but the data mattered. H-6's late plume had changed the cadence by less than a breath. Less than a breath had moved tons of debris away from a medical platform.

"Anchor Nine risk down twelve percent," he said.

"Say actual," Malik answered.

"Still bad. Less bad."

"Medical understands less bad."

Sian adjusted the next burn window. "The resonance is answering, but it is not obedient. Tank-sized objects may lag."

"That sounded like foreshadowing," Kira said.

"It was warning."

Pavel's STOP ARGUING alert flashed once, then cleared. Kira pretended not to see it. Pavel pretended to believe her.

Vera set the next sequence to hold until the new tracks settled. Waiting after motion was its own discipline. Everyone watched red lines learn their new futures.

"No one touch anything elegant," Sian said.

"That is my entire job," Joss replied.

"Today your job is ugly truth."

He saved the ugly truth under the event log and sent it to every station still depending on them.

Stations answered in different voices. The tourist resort sent a clipped copy. A science platform sent thanks and a list of its own shield weaknesses. A cargo tug asked whether "less bad" had an official threshold. Joss nearly replied with a number, then stopped. Some questions wanted certainty more than truth.

"Less bad means continue shelter and await next update," Vera said when he asked.

He sent that. It was not elegant. It was useful.

The response from Anchor Nine came back: shelter continuing. Malik added no complaint, which worried Joss more than complaint would have. He opened a private text line and typed, Window improving if sequence holds.

Malik answered: if is not medication.

Joss typed: best available pharmacy.

There was a pause. Then Malik sent: dispense carefully.

Joss returned to the model with a small, tired smile. The human lines mattered too. They kept the numbers from becoming a screen saver for harm.

Chapter 29: Bad Note

The bad note was an old fuel tank no model had weighted correctly. It tumbled out of phase after H-6's burn and crossed toward Anchor Nine with enough mass to make shields irrelevant.

"Track Tank-Seven," Joss said. "Impact probability rising."

The tank had been treated as a light panel in two models and a dead bus fragment in another. Its radar return lied because it was hollow, reflective, and tumbling. Now sunlight caught its curved side and revealed its mass the way a knife reveals itself when it turns.

Malik heard the alarm before traffic called. "How large?"

"Too large," Vera said.

Kira rotated Little Argument. "I can intercept."

Pavel said, "No."

"That was not traffic."

"It was sanity."

"Sanity is not in command."

"Clearly."

Tank-Seven spun fast, reflective, ugly. A clean capture would be impossible. A dirty capture might break the arm. No capture meant Anchor Nine took a direct hit near the ferry collar. The line crossed Patiently's docked silhouette, then the staging spoke full of patients.

Captain Ito came on. "If that hits, Patiently becomes sculpture."

"Bad sculpture," Kira said.

"I have strong aesthetic objections."

She burned toward the tank.

Vera said, "Kira, declare fuel."

"Enough."

"That is not a declaration."

"Enough for this."

Pavel made a low furious sound. Sian recalculated.

"If you nudge it twelve centimeters per second radial, it misses."

"Twelve centimeters sounds friendly."

"At that spin, it is not."

Kira armed manual control. Assisted mode refused the target three times, classifying it as unsafe for capture. She agreed with the software and overrode it. The tank filled her forward camera, a dented cylinder flashing white, black, white, black. Little Argument, named for persistence, prepared to argue with a piece of metal that had no opinion and too much velocity.

Inside Anchor Nine, Malik heard the projected impact point and moved without waiting for fear to finish. "Everyone off the collar wall."

"We just moved from there," Tomas said.

"Then practice."

The remaining crew shifted inward. Cera was already aboard Patiently, beyond the sealed hatch, and Malik felt the absence of her hands like a missing tool. Ito reported ferry clamps holding but warned that a strike near the collar would transfer load through Patiently's frame.

"In plain language?" Vera asked.

"If Tank-Seven hits, I may stay docked forever."

"That is not plain. That is morbid."

"Plainly morbid."

Kira heard them and refused to picture the collar. Images were dangerous when the hands needed work. She narrowed the forward camera, killed one alarm that was only repeating old grief, and let the tank's tumble become timing. White, black, white, black. Strap visible on the third flash. Miss it and there was no second polite attempt.

"Range thirty," Joss said.

"I see it."

"Twenty."

"Stop helping."

"Copy."

The tank grew until it stopped being an object and became a wall with motion. Kira matched its roll by feel because assisted mode had already declared the truth and quit.

Her world narrowed to the strap. Not the station. Not the patients. Not Pavel's breathing. Strap, roll, flash, closing rate. She had practiced manual captures in simulators that rewarded clean hands and punished overcorrection. Simulators did not include the thought of a ferry becoming shrapnel behind you.

"Five meters," Joss said, unable to help himself.

Kira let the number pass through her without catching. The strap came around again. She moved the claw early, trusting the tank to arrive where math and eye briefly agreed.

The tug's frame blocked part of the view as the tank rolled under her. She felt rather than saw the closing moment, a convergence of vibration, glare, and the old knowledge of machines in motion. The claw began to close before the strap centered. For one awful fraction, she thought she had chosen wrong.

Then the strap snapped into the claw's throat.

"Contact," she said.

No one answered. They all heard the load spike.

Chapter 30: Hand Capture

Manual capture was not done by hand, except in every way that mattered. Kira took the arm out of assisted mode, matched the tank's tumble, and closed the claw on a dented strap.

The tug lurched. Her harness bit her shoulder. The tank's spin tried to twist the arm sideways. Little Argument's frame creaked in a tone she had never heard and did not want to learn.

"Stress red," Pavel said.

"I see red."

"Release."

"Not yet."

Tank-Seven dragged Little Argument into a roll. Kira fired lateral thrusters in short, ugly bursts. Twelve centimeters per second. That was all. The difference between impact and miss was less than the length of a hand. The tank filled every camera, too close for scale. Its dented strap began to peel under the claw.

"Eight centimeters," Joss said. "Nine."

"Come on," Kira whispered.

"Ten."

The arm joint spiked. Pavel's voice broke. "Kira."

She fired one last burst and released.

Tank-Seven spun away, new path clearing Anchor Nine by ninety meters. In orbit, ninety meters was both nothing and salvation. The projected line no longer cut the ferry collar. It passed close enough to make future investigators sweat.

The capture arm froze half-open.

Pavel whispered, "Joint two resigned."

"Accepted," Kira said, breathing hard.

Little Argument rolled slowly until she caught it. Alarms layered over one another: arm fault, fuel low, attitude excursion, thermal caution. She silenced only the loudest. The rest had earned their opinions.

Malik came on from Anchor Nine. "Whoever moved that, medical thanks you."

"Bill Helix," Kira said.

Ito added, "Patiently objects to becoming sculpture."

"Objection sustained," Vera said.

For five seconds the command loop sounded almost human. Then alarms resumed, because rescue rarely waited for gratitude to finish. Joss marked Tank-Seven as clearing. Sian pulled the new path into the score. Pavel began diagnosing an arm that had already given more than it owed.

Kira's shoulder hurt where the harness had caught her. She rotated it once and stopped because pain had no operational value unless it meant damage. Her fingers still answered. Her eyes still tracked. Good enough.

"Arm status?" Vera asked.

Pavel answered before Kira could beautify anything. "Capture function lost. Joint two locked half-open. Wrist encoder unreliable. It can no longer grip. It may still serve as a conversation piece."

"Can the tug continue?"

Kira looked at the fuel, the attitude, the heat, the arm camera. "Yes."

Pavel said, "Define continue."

"Move. Burn. Complain."

"That definition includes most doomed machines," Pavel said.

"Then Little Argument has company."

At Anchor Nine, the projected miss line updated across Malik's display. Ninety meters. He stared at it until the number lost meaning, then gained too much. He put the slate away. There were patients to monitor and no medicine for imagining.

Outside, Tank-Seven spun into its new future, harmless only because a damaged tug had touched it at exactly the wrong-looking time.

Pavel saved the arm telemetry before the buffer could overwrite. Later, he would use it to prove the arm had exceeded rated load by an amount that made procurement people go pale. Now he only checked whether the failure had sent fragments into the tug.

"No secondary debris from the arm," he said.

"Good arm," Kira said.

"Former arm."

"Good former arm."

He wanted to argue, but the phrase lodged somewhere inconveniently soft. "Yes," he said. "Good former arm."

Kira did not hear the softness clearly. Her own breathing filled the cockpit. She logged the arm fault with one hand and kept the other near attitude control. The tank miss line held. Anchor Nine remained outside it.

"Pavel," she said after a moment, "save the last clean telemetry."

"Already done."

"Of course."

"I am sentimental in advance."

"Efficient."

He looked at the saved trace: load, strain, release. A machine's last useful act, reduced to lines. He would rebuild from those lines, and he would not tell procurement why his first design note read: it held.

Chapter 31: No Window

Traffic lost every clean departure window for two hours after Tank-Seven. Patiently remained docked. Anchor Nine's ward module ran hot, then cold, then hot again as engineering rerouted power.

The platform had become a place of careful discomfort. Patients were strapped along the collar spoke because the ward could not be trusted. Nurses moved with wipes, syringes, and battery packs clipped to their belts. The lights dimmed when engineering shifted a load, then brightened too hard. Every change made people look up.

Ito did not shout. That worried Malik more than shouting. The ferry pilot stood at the hatch with his helmet sealed to his suit and his gloves tucked under one arm, too still for a man who had been arguing for an hour.

"Give me a dirty window," Ito told Vera.

"Dirty windows kill ferries."

"No windows cook patients."

Malik disliked both summaries.

"Medical status?" Vera asked.

"Stable under protest," Malik said. "Heat stress controlled for now. Decompression patients need transfer. Dr. Maas remains annoyingly alive."

"I heard that," Maas said.

"Good hearing is a positive sign."

Joss found a possible departure if the second counter-burn succeeded and the ferry accepted micro-pitting risk. The window was not clean. It was a ragged tear between fragment groups, with a predicted dusting of particles too small to track individually and too large to respect. Ito accepted before Vera asked.

"Acceptance is not authorization," Vera said.

"Understood."

Malik heard nothing useful in that word. Pilots used understood the way doctors used possible: to avoid saying no.

In the ward collar, Dr. Maas asked Malik to stop standing where patients could see his worry.

"I have one face."

"Then turn it."

He did. He faced the hatch instead, where his reflection looked warped in the metal rim. He had slept three hours in the last thirty. His scrub collar was damp. The back of his neck ached from tension and suit seals. None of that mattered until it made his hands stupid. He checked them. Still steady.

Kira, meanwhile, had no capture arm and half a fuel plan. Little Argument could still thrust, still relay, still become mass in the score. She did not say that aloud yet. If she said it, Pavel would hear.

Pavel heard anyway. "Do not think loudly."

"Stay out of my cockpit."

"Maintainable machines do not have private thoughts."

"Pilots do."

"Pilots have unpublished errors."

Kira looked at the dead arm camera. Joint two remained half-open, a hand that had let go too late and saved people by failing afterward. There was still the magnetic clamp on the tug's belly, designed for station inspection holds, not dragging debris into phase. It had capacity ratings she disliked and a latch sensor that lied in cold weather.

"Pavel," she said, "how honest is the clamp?"

"As a product line or spiritually?"

"Today."

"Today it can hold if the load is brief, aligned, and kind."

"So no."

"So maybe."

She added the clamp to her private plan. Pavel said nothing for once. Silence from him was not permission. It was grief postponed.

Joss kept searching for a ferry window with the grim focus of a man trying every locked door in a burning building. Each candidate failed differently. One carried too much dust. One crossed a fragment group that radar could not size. One required Patiently to undock into a roll that would make Dr. Maas's support pump earn its warranty.

"There," he said finally. "If the next sequence lowers group D by six kilometers, I can give Ito a dirty nineteen."

Ito answered at once. "I accept dirty nineteen."

"Stop accepting things before they exist," Vera said.

"I am creating a hospitable environment for them."

Malik looked at the remaining patients and tried to translate nineteen seconds into outcomes. Medicine had taught him to distrust miracles but use openings.

Chapter 32: Patient Transfer

The pressure collar between Anchor Nine and Patiently had warped by a fraction after the shield strikes. A fraction was enough to make transfer slow. Malik and Ito moved patients one by one, locking, checking, breathing, unlocking.

The heart-support rig stuck at the hatch lip.

"Do not force it," Dr. Maas said from inside the rig.

"I am the doctor here."

"Then doctor delicately."

Ito removed a handle cover with a tool he carried in his thigh pocket and gained three centimeters. Cera pulled a cable flat. Malik guided the rig through by millimeters. Sweat floated off his face in tiny spheres and stuck to the inside of his visor. His nose itched. He ignored it with the full dignity of medicine.

"Clear left," Cera said.

"Clear right," Ito said.

"Maas?"

"Alive and judging."

"Then clear."

The rig slid into Patiently and locked to the forward mount. The ferry accepted the pump feed. Malik waited until three green lights held for ten seconds before releasing his grip. Ten seconds was arbitrary. He needed a ritual and refused to call it one.

Four critical patients aboard. Then six. Then one nurse. Then two burn techs. Patiently was full by design and fuller by necessity. Tomas took the aft brace without complaint, jaw tight beneath his clear hood.

"You good?" Malik asked.

"No."

"Acceptable answer."

Ito checked restraint paths twice. "If we roll hard, everyone hates me."

"If you roll badly, everyone haunts you."

"Dr. Maas already reserved that privilege."

"She has seniority."

Ito looked at Malik. "Your turn."

"I stay."

"Medical protocol?"

"Arithmetic."

Ito hated it but did not argue. The ferry needed a pilot more than Anchor Nine needed a doctor if Anchor Nine failed. Malik could keep a reduced crew alive if the station held. If it did not, his seat would not change the outcome. Everyone was becoming an equation. Malik resented math for being occasionally correct.

Cera caught his sleeve. "You should go."

"Then who tells the remaining crew to stop doing heroic nonsense?"

"They will not listen anyway."

"Exactly. I have experience."

Vera gave a standby warning. A dirty window was forming.

Malik sealed the hatch and put one hand on it after it closed. No prayer. Just contact. Behind that metal were people he had chosen to send away first. Beside him were people he had chosen to keep. Both choices sat in his chest like stones.

Ito's voice came through the hatch loop. "We will make it ugly."

"Make it successful."

"Doctors always want luxury."

The final prelaunch check took longer than anyone liked because the warped collar required manual confirmation on three latches. Ito wanted to trust two. Malik refused.

"Three latches or no patients," he said.

"If we wait for three, we may miss the window."

"If we launch with two, the window may keep the hatch."

Ito looked at him for one hard second, then turned to his copilot. "Manual latch three."

The copilot reached through the narrow access, pulled the lever, and swore when it stuck. Malik held the light. The lever moved a centimeter, stopped, then snapped home. Green appeared on the panel. It was the most beautiful small light Malik had seen all day.

Inside Patiently, Cera called back patient states. Green, amber, green, green, amber, green. Not perfect. Flight-worthy. Malik wrote the words in the log because some future review would ask why he let them go.

He added the answer underneath before anyone could ask it: because remaining aboard Anchor Nine carried greater cumulative risk than a controlled dirty departure. The sentence looked cold. It had to. Warmth belonged in hands and voices, not in audit lines.

Ito read the latch status from his side. "Three green. You satisfied?"

"Never."

"I will accept procedurally appeased."

"Fly well."

"Luxury again."

Chapter 33: Weather Blind

Noor lost Weather East Three at 14:07. The satellite did not explode. It went blind after a fragment cut attitude control and left it staring at empty dark. Weather East Five survived by burning fuel Noor had meant to keep for hurricane season.

"Hemisphere prediction degraded," she reported.

Vera said, "How degraded?"

"Enough that people on the ground will call it surprise when it reaches them."

The East Three feed had not gone black. It had become worse: perfectly clear, perfectly useless. Its camera stared away from Earth into star field, sending clean images of nothing meteorologists could use. Noor watched the first empty frame arrive and felt an anger too tired for expression.

"Can it recover attitude?" Joss asked.

"No. Wheel assembly is gone or blind. Thrusters insufficient."

"Can it deorbit safely?"

"Later. If later exists."

Noor began patching with lower-quality feeds, commercial imagers, old ocean buoys, aircraft reports, high-altitude balloons, anything that saw cloud. The network became less an instrument than a crowd of witnesses with bad eyesight. Her staff moved quickly. Nobody dramatized the loss. They had work, and mourning a satellite could wait until it stopped endangering the forecast.

"East Five fuel state?" Vera asked.

"Alive. Poor. It just spent September."

"Meaning?"

"Meaning when the late storms come, I will be explaining today's orbit to people standing in rain."

Sian used Noor's degraded data to refine atmospheric decay paths. Weather mattered to debris too. The sky below was not passive. Density changed with solar activity, storm systems, heating, drag. It would receive whatever orbit gave it and alter the terms on the way down.

Kira listened while Little Argument cooled from the tank capture. Her arm camera showed joint two locked in a useless open claw. She felt oddly bereaved.

"It was a good arm," Pavel said.

"It is still attached."

"That is not the same as alive."

Machines had lives only because humans gave them jobs. Kira promised the arm nothing. She needed the tug for one more burn.

Noor sent an updated ground alert. The language changed from routine advisory to degraded forecast confidence. She hated that phrase. It sounded like professional embarrassment, not public risk.

"Noor," Kira said, "East Three saved anything before it went blind?"

"It gave us the last cloud edge we needed for the decay model."

"Then it worked."

Noor looked at the useless star-field feed. "Yes. It worked."

She had a technician freeze the final Earth-facing frame before East Three lost attitude. It showed a crescent of storm cloud over dark water, one bright edge lit by low sun. Not dramatic. Useful. Noor pinned it to the operations wall beside the current patched forecast.

"Sentiment?" her deputy asked.

"Continuity."

"That is sentiment with a badge."

"Then it has clearance."

The patched network began to produce warnings again. Wider than before. Less elegant. But fishing fleets received revised timing, air corridors shifted around the worst reentry uncertainty, and coastal emergency offices got enough lead time to stage generators and crews. Noor watched the acknowledgments come in and felt the peculiar relief of imperfect work accepted by people who needed it.

On the loop, Sian asked for an updated upper-atmosphere density estimate.

"You trust my broken network?" Noor asked.

"I trust it more because you know where it is broken."

Noor sent the estimate. That, she thought, was the best defense of public systems she had heard all day. Not perfection. Known limits, shared quickly, used by people who understood consequences.

Her deputy asked whether to flag the forecast as degraded in the public bulletin or only in the technical feed. Noor turned from the wall.

"Public."

"It may cause calls."

"Good. Calls mean someone read it."

The deputy sent the update. Within a minute, a coastal operations center asked for clarification. Noor answered directly, not through press language: warning confidence is lower than normal; prepare for the wider window. The reply came back: understood. Wider staffing initiated.

Noor saved that too. Not because it was dramatic, but because dull compliance was what made systems humane.

Chapter 34: Second Score

The second score was worse and better. Worse because telemetry had gaps, assets were damaged, and two fragments had behaved like fools. Better because the shell had begun to answer. Counter-resonance showed in the model as a widening, a loosening, a refusal of the tight destructive cadence.

Joss spoke numbers. Sian spoke corrections. Vera spoke decisions. Kira spoke only when a number touched her fuel. There were too many numbers now: burn phase, tank pressure, debris density, ferry window, hull temperature, forecast confidence, arm fault, clamp load, remaining propellant. Numbers made the crisis legible without making it kind.

"Little Argument cannot make point four," Pavel said.

"Little Argument can if it does not plan to come home with reserves."

"That is not coming home."

"It is coming home rudely."

"Kira."

"I know."

Sian tried to replace Kira's burn with H-8. H-8's thruster alignment failed the model. Noor offered Weather East Seven for a trim burn. Vera refused. Malik offered Anchor Nine's maintenance drone. Too weak. Grace Meridian would arrive late. Redcap's cracked nozzle made the dispersion worse. Each alternative appeared, glowed briefly, and died under math.

The score left a hole shaped like Kira's tug.

No one said sacrifice. That word belonged to ceremonies after other options were gone. They were not gone yet. They were shrinking. Kira disliked how everyone avoided looking directly at the same fact.

Vera asked Kira for a fuel declaration.

Kira sent the honest number. It appeared on the shared display, smaller than it felt.

Pavel said, "I preferred when pilots lied."

"No you didn't."

"I preferred being angry for simpler reasons."

Sian's voice came in carefully. "If you make point four and release late, your perigee drops below safe recovery without immediate correction."

"Then I release on time."

"If the clamp hangs?"

"Then I improvise."

"Improvisation is not a trajectory."

"It has been doing some work today."

Vera let the exchange run longer than usual. Then she said, "Kira, I will not order a burn you cannot survive."

"You may have to order a burn no one else can make."

The distinction entered the loop and stayed there.

Vera asked for a private channel and Kira accepted. For three seconds neither spoke.

"I need to know you are not volunteering because you cannot stand waiting," Vera said.

Kira considered lying. Vera would hear it. "Waiting is not my favorite."

"That is not an answer."

"I am volunteering because the tug is in position, the vector works, and Anchor Nine does not have forty comfortable minutes. If another asset appears, use it. If not, use me."

Vera let the answer sit against the data. "Understood."

"That sounded like authorization warming up."

"It is command thinking."

The private channel closed. Kira checked the old paper clipped above her console. Release before pride. She had written it for Tank-Seven. It had become general policy.

She added one more note beneath it: if another asset appears, use it. The ink wobbled with the tug's vibration. She looked at the two sentences and found them almost adequate.

Pavel saw the cockpit camera tilt toward the paper. "What did you write?"

"Poetry."

"Delete it."

"Too late. Published in one copy."

"Peer review rejects."

The exchange made the coming burn smaller for half a second. Then the model updated and made it large again.

Sian tried one last substitution. She moved the Anchor drone into Kira's slot, boosted its burn beyond rated thrust, and watched the model fail. The drone survived in the simulation. Anchor Nine did not. She deleted the run, then restored it and marked it rejected with the reason in plain text.

"You are keeping all the bad plans," Joss said.

"Especially the tempting ones."

"Future auditors?"

"Future selves."

He nodded. Every emergency created a later mythology in which the right answer looked obvious. The bad plans would testify otherwise.

Chapter 35: Command Split

Political pressure arrived through encrypted channels and made Vera's jaw ache. Save commercial relays. Preserve Helix assets for investigation. Prioritize weather. Prioritize crew. Avoid liability. Avoid panic. Avoid words like illegal.

She muted everyone but traffic, medical, weather, and Kira.

"Did you just mute the deputy minister?" Joss asked.

"I improved signal quality."

The command split was not between life and property. It was between immediate people and systems people depended on. Weather satellites mattered. Navigation mattered. Anchor Nine mattered. Commercial relays mattered less until one carried hospital data, then mattered sharply. Every category leaked into another. That was the lie of priority lists: they pretended the world had clean shelves.

Vera read the commercial-first burn request once, then closed it. The request would preserve two relay buses and worsen Patiently's ferry window by eight seconds. It came wrapped in language about national data resilience and economic continuity. It did not mention the patients staged at a hatch.

"Denied," she said.

An aide looked up. "They want rationale."

"Crewed survival and weather-chain preservation."

"They want more."

"Then they are wealthy in wanting."

Tane, surprisingly, supported her. "Helix assets are already compromised. Use them."

Edda recorded that too.

Vera unmuted the wider loop only after the decision had been logged. Several important people began speaking. She spoke over them.

"Second sequence proceeds. Objections are recorded as received and operationally irrelevant."

Joss stared at her. "Can you say that?"

"I just tested it."

She looked at the model. "Begin second sequence on my mark."

She thought of all the things command schools did not teach: how to disappoint powerful people quickly, how to spend machines with names, how to decide which future harm deserved present risk. They taught chain of command. They did not teach the sound a medic made when asked to hold ten more minutes.

"Mark."

Across the shell, damaged engines woke. On the wall, little burn indicators lit like match heads in a storm.

The deputy minister tried to reenter the loop through a second channel. Vera's aide looked at her. Vera shook her head.

"Record says unavailable due to active command," the aide said.

"Good."

"He will be angry."

"He has gravity. He can endure."

Joss called burn confirmations. H-5 ready. H-6 ready with lag. H-8 marginal. Grace Meridian inbound. Redcap aligned under protest from its nozzle. Little Argument pending clamp. Anchor Nine holding. Patiently warm and dirty-window ready.

For a moment, Vera saw not icons but people around each one. Command was not abstraction. It was choosing which real thing became smaller so another real thing could continue.

She did not permit herself to think about career consequences. That was a luxury disguised as responsibility. If the counter-score failed, there would be inquiries. If she delayed, there would be funerals or their bureaucratic equivalents. The choice was not between harm and no harm. It was between harms that could still be steered.

"Second sequence is live," she said.

The aide started the command log marker. Every decision now had time, source, authority, and cost. Vera wanted the record clean enough that no one could later pretend confusion had made the choices.

She had seen that trick after smaller failures: fog invoked after the fact, as if uncertainty erased motive, as if nobody had known enough to choose differently. This record would not allow that comfort. It would show the hidden fuel, the delayed keys, the refused commercial burn, the dirty ferry window, the tug sent low because no cleaner thrust existed.

"Log my denial of the relay-priority request," she told the aide.

"Already logged."

"Log it again in sequence."

The aide did. Vera returned to the burn display.

The relay company sent one more objection, shorter this time, stripped of legal tone and full of money. Vera did not read it aloud. She tagged it non-operational and moved on.

"You are making enemies," Joss said.

"I had a schedule opening."

"After this, they will say you were difficult."

"Good. Easy commanders get people killed politely."

The burn display reached thirty seconds. Vera placed both hands flat on the console. The room watched her hands because they could not all watch the sky.

Chapter 36: Helix Burn

H-8 fired crooked, then corrected under Joss's command. H-5 burned clean. H-6 coughed and held. The hidden Helix engines, dragged into public duty, began undoing part of what secrecy had made.

Tane watched telemetry like a man reading his own sentencing memo. Each successful burn proved the satellites were useful. Each useful burn proved they had never been inert.

"H-8 tank pressure falling," he said.

"Can it complete?" Vera asked.

"If we accept rupture risk."

"To whom?"

"Itself, mostly."

"Mostly."

"A small debris release, if rupture follows cutoff. Sian's model says below major danger if timed."

Sian said, "Below major danger is not no danger."

"Can we use it?"

She looked at the path, then at Anchor Nine's red zone. "Yes."

"Accepted," Vera said.

H-8 burned again. Its tank ruptured after cutoff, scattering small debris but below modeled danger. The fragments glittered in Kira's rear camera like a handful of filings. Joss cursed, then mapped them. Sian absorbed the new fragments into the decay path.

"Messy but useful," she said.

Kira prepared Little Argument for the hole in the score. Her tug would burn not to move debris directly, but to tug a captured panel into phase long enough for drag to catch the group. Without the arm, she had to use the tug's body and a magnetic clamp never meant for this load.

The clamp manual described stationkeeping, inspection anchoring, and short-duration hull contact. It did not describe dragging a torn panel through an emergency resonance while the pilot pretended fuel remained a civilized topic.

Pavel said, "I am uploading a clamp patch."

"Will it work?"

"Define work as briefly."

"Perfect."

"Kira."

"I know."

"You keep saying that as if it changes anything."

"It changes that I heard you."

The patch arrived. It increased magnetic hold at the cost of heat and disabled an automatic release if load oscillation crossed a limit Pavel called expensive. He added a manual release shortcut to her left thumb. She practiced the motion twice.

"Do not miss that button," he said.

"I was going to, but now I won't."

"Your comfort style is terrible."

H-8's rupture left Tane staring at the small debris cloud with something like grief. Kira noticed and disliked herself for noticing.

"It was a machine," she said.

"It was evidence," Tane answered.

"It was also a machine."

He looked at her through the loop window. "You sound like your engineer."

"Do not tell him."

The debris from H-8 spread along the modeled corridor. Sian gave each new fragment a temporary designation. Even in emergency, naming mattered because unnamed objects killed later as surprises.

Pavel finished the clamp patch and sent Kira a diagram of the likely failure modes. It looked like a flower made of bad news.

"Pretty," she said.

"Those are all the ways you can lose the tug."

"Still pretty."

"I am surrounded by art critics with death wishes."

Kira armed the clamp and turned toward the panel remnant. The next point belonged to her. The remnant tumbled slowly, a torn rectangle of metal with curled edges and no stable axis. The clamp would need a flat instant. The burn would need a straight lie. Everything would need to be brief.

The remnant had once been part of a solar array. Kira could see the grid lines when it rolled through sunlight, blue-black squares cracked and peeling. It looked fragile. The model treated it as leverage. Both were true.

"Do not hit the curled edge," Pavel said.

"I was saving that for a hobby."

"Flat face only."

"I see it."

She waited through one tumble, then another. Waiting for the right face felt like threading a needle while the needle insulted fuel.

"You are late to capture," Joss said.

"I am early to not dying."

"Accepted."

The flat face came around with a flash of old solar cells. Kira fired a tiny lateral puff, less than she wanted, more than the fuel budget liked. The remnant slid under the clamp. Magnetic lock surged.

"Contact."

Pavel's numbers jumped. "Hot contact."

"Still contact."

"That is a pilot sentence, not an engineering sentence."

"Translate later."

Chapter 37: Ferry Run

Patiently undocked into a dirty window at 14:39. Captain Ito fired hard enough to make every passenger feel gravity's rude imitation. Malik watched from Anchor Nine's port with fists closed.

"Trajectory good," Joss said. "Debris group crossing aft in nineteen seconds."

Ito said nothing. Pilots sometimes became quiet enough to frighten God.

Inside Patiently, Dr. Maas felt the burn through her support frame and gritted her teeth. Tomas swore from the aft brace. Cera called out monitor states in a voice so level Malik could have kissed her forehead if she had been there and if disasters allowed sentiment.

Patiently threaded the gap. Micro-fragments dusted its outer skin, bright sparks against black. The ferry rolled, corrected, rolled again. Its left attitude jet fired longer than planned. A warning flashed on traffic. For three seconds its signal broke.

Malik heard only static.

The static had shape. It filled the ward collar, the space where patients had been, the place where his hand still remembered the hatch. No one spoke. The remaining technicians watched him because doctors were supposed to know what silence meant. He knew too many possibilities.

Then Ito returned. "Patiently clear. We are uglier."

The ward erupted in sound and then stopped because not everyone had left. Malik let himself lean against the wall for one breath. Only one. The station did not grant longer.

Vera redirected the ferry to lower safe orbit. Noor borrowed its telemetry to fill weather gaps. Patiently's scratched skin and sensor logs became part of the forecast patch. Everything useful became more than itself.

Kira saw Patiently's track clear the shell. "Good flying."

Ito answered, "Terrible window."

"Good abuse of it."

"Naturally."

Dr. Maas came on weakly from the ferry medical loop. "Doctor Hesh, Patiently remains a terrible name."

Malik closed his eyes once. "File complaint after landing."

Now Anchor Nine was lighter, not safe. The station still sat in the path of whatever the orchestra failed to move. Malik turned away from the port and checked the people who remained.

Patiently's telemetry came back in dense packets: hull pitting, thermal rise, patient vitals, attitude correction, fuel state. Noor took the external temperature data. Joss took the dust impacts. Malik took the vitals and read them twice.

"Dr. Maas is stable," Cera reported from the ferry.

"Define stable."

"Complaining in full sentences."

"Excellent."

Ito came on with forced casualness. "Lower safe orbit in twelve. Request permission to continue being ugly."

Vera said, "Granted."

The people left on Anchor Nine heard the exchange and smiled because the ferry had carried away more than patients. It had carried away one version of the day in which nobody got out.

Malik let the smile pass through the room, then put everyone back to work. Relief could make hands careless. He assigned two technicians to check seals, sent Tomas to rest despite protest, and made himself drink water.

"You are ordering yourself now?" Tomas asked.

"Chain of command had a vacancy."

"How is the patient?"

"Stubborn."

"Good prognosis?"

"Annoyingly."

The station groaned around them, damaged but still opinionated. Malik found that comforting.

He checked the empty ferry collar again, then forced himself to stop. Empty spaces pulled attention. People who remained deserved it more. He moved to Tomas, checked the burn dressing, adjusted a strap, and made the man drink.

"You keep giving orders," Tomas said.

"You keep needing them."

"Fair."

The station ticked. Everyone listened. Nothing followed. Malik made himself resume the dressing because not every sound could become prophecy.

Tomas watched his hands. "If I get back to work after this, I am requesting a ground assignment."

"You will hate ground."

"Probably."

"Too much gravity."

"Too many meetings."

Malik secured the bandage. "Heal first. Complain later."

"That a medical order?"

"Life plan."

Tomas smiled, then winced. The smile mattered anyway. Pain with a future attached was easier to treat than pain floating loose.

Chapter 38: Shield Loss

Anchor Nine lost shield section twelve during the second sequence. The fragment did not breach pressure hull, but it stripped insulation and exposed a maintenance trunk. Engineering sealed what could be sealed. The station's margin became paperwork no one believed.

Malik moved remaining crew inward. The chapel window cracked but held. The crack ran from one corner in a pale fork. Someone asked if that meant anything. Malik said no, then apologized because people ask those questions when metal is loud.

"It means glass is tired," he said more gently.

"Aren't we all," Cera answered from the hatch.

Engineering sealed what could be sealed. A maintenance trunk had lost insulation and was bleeding heat into a space that needed neither more heat nor more truth. The platform's status board became a row of compromises: isolated, degraded, bypassed, manual, temporary.

"How long can you hold?" Vera asked.

"Define hold."

"Crew alive, platform not abandoned."

"Forty minutes. Maybe."

Kira heard the number. Her burn came in twelve.

Pavel's clamp patch loaded. Magnetic lock capacity uncertain. Fuel insufficient for abort after full burn. Little Argument would either complete the point or become another object in the model.

Sian said, "I am still looking for an alternative."

"Look fast," Kira said.

She did not mean it unkindly. Sian understood. Kindness had become doing the math anyway.

Joss sent her final vector. It was narrow, ugly, and possible. Possible had become the most beautiful word in orbit.

Kira copied the vector to her local display and then to paper, habit from training after a solar storm simulation had blanked screens for nineteen seconds. The paper floated under a clip. Numbers in ink looked absurd and reassuring.

"Little Argument readying clamp capture," she said.

Pavel said, "Little Argument objects."

"Overruled."

"Logged."

Malik made a final walk through the damaged spoke before sealing it. He did not need to go; engineering had cameras. He went because cameras did not smell hot insulation or notice when a frightened technician had left a glove clipped to the wrong rail.

The exposed trunk hissed faintly through a temporary patch. Not air. Coolant vapor from a line that should have been isolated. He marked it for engineering and moved on. The chapel window's crack caught sunlight and split it into two pale lines across the wall.

"You good?" Tomas asked from the inner hatch.

"No," Malik said.

"Acceptable answer."

Malik smiled. His own medicine returned to him, slightly dented. He sealed the spoke and reported station status. Forty minutes maybe. The number had not improved. It had become more honest.

Vera accepted the report without softening it. "Repair crew earliest arrival?"

Joss answered, "After second sequence and debris confirmation. Not before thirty-five."

"So hold means hold."

"Yes," Malik said.

He looked at the sealed spoke one more time. Behind it were heat, cracks, and things that would become tomorrow's maintenance if they survived today. He preferred tomorrow's maintenance to today's memorial.

Cera called from Patiently to report stable patients. The delay in her voice was small, but it made her feel far away. Malik closed his eyes while she spoke, then opened them before anyone could notice.

"You still have Tomas?" she asked.

"Tomas still has us."

"Tell him he owes me a better joke."

Malik passed it on. Tomas lifted a bandaged hand. "I am workshopping."

Good, Malik thought. A man planning jokes was a man planning later.

He logged the sealed spoke and added a note for repair: inspect chapel pane after pressure confirmation; crew morale artifact nearby, preserve if safe. He stared at the phrase morale artifact and nearly changed it to paper flower. The official term would keep it from being tossed by someone efficient and tired.

Vera called for updated hold time. Malik looked at the board, then at the people, then at the sealed hatch.

"Still forty," he said. "But it is an honest forty."

"Honest is useful," Vera said.

He hoped so. The station creaked again, and everyone kept working.

Chapter 39: Kira Outside

The magnetic clamp jammed half-deployed. Of course it did. Machines choose drama after humans run out of tolerance.

Pavel said, "Do not go outside."

Kira was already unstrapping.

"Little Argument has exterior access," she said.

"It has exterior access for inspections, not heroic stupidity."

"I am inspecting a clamp."

"You are weaponizing vocabulary."

"Efficiently."

She suited and cycled through the tiny lock. The tug's exterior access was designed by someone who believed pilots were narrow, patient, and willing to apologize to their own knees. Kira was only one of those. The lock opened. Outside, orbit was silent and overcrowded. Earth turned beneath her boots. The damaged capture arm hung open like a broken hand.

She clipped to the hull and moved along handholds toward the clamp housing. The tug's skin was warm in sun, cold in shadow, vibrating faintly with internal pumps. A shard had lodged in the deployment rail, a bright splinter from Tank-Seven or H-8 or some other bad note no one had named yet.

"Debris group in five minutes," Joss said.

"Plenty."

"Four minutes fifty."

"Less plenty."

She used a hand tool, slow because haste in gloves became slapstick or death. The shard resisted. Her wrist ached inside the suit. Pavel kept breathing into the loop. She could hear him trying not to speak, which for Pavel was a form of prayer.

"Say whatever you are not saying," she told him.

"If you slip, use the hull line first, not suit jets."

"See? Useful."

"Also I am furious."

"Also useful."

The shard came free. It spun away, small and bright, another object for the catalog if anyone had time to care. The clamp deployed with a hard vibration through her glove.

"Clamp green," Pavel said.

Kira looked once at Earth because people do that even when they claim not to. The planet was cloud and ocean and weather maps and hospital corridors and everything that made orbit worth cleaning. Then she pulled herself back inside Little Argument to spend the tug properly.

The lock took too long to cycle. It always took too long when a person had somewhere urgent to be. Kira used the time to flex her hands and check suit pressure. Her breath sounded loud inside the helmet. Too loud. She slowed it because panic wasted oxygen and made bad company.

When the inner hatch opened, she pulled herself into the cockpit and sealed in with movements that had become clumsy from fatigue. A bruise was forming across her shoulder under the suit. She could feel it when she twisted.

"Pilot in seat," she said.

"Clamp still green," Pavel replied.

"You sound surprised."

"I am professionally delighted."

"That is fear with a certificate."

"Yes."

She strapped down, brought the panel remnant onto the forward display, and loaded Joss's vector. Her handwriting on the clipped paper trembled slightly from suit effort. She flattened it with one gloved finger.

"You have three minutes to point," Joss said.

"I have two and a half."

"The schedule says three."

"The schedule has never flown in gloves."

Pavel checked the clamp temperature again. "Still green."

"You keep saying that as if green cannot betray us."

"Green can betray us less often than amber."

She brought up the exterior camera. The clamp sat extended and bright against black, a tool pretending it had always been meant for this. Kira's suit gloves made the controls feel slightly distant. She considered removing them, dismissed the thought, then laughed at herself because fatigue had made nonsense bold.

"Do not laugh during setup," Pavel said.

"I had a bad idea and rejected it."

"Now I need to know."

"No."

"Was it removing gloves?"

"Stay out of my cockpit."

"That is yes."

Joss sent the final approach tone. It was a low pulse, more felt through the headset than heard. Kira let it set her breathing. In training, instructors talked about calm as if it were a resource stored somewhere inside the body. Kira had never found it there. Calm was external: a checklist, a voice, a handhold, a machine that still answered.

"Clamp load simulation on your display," Pavel said.

"I see your flower of bad news."

"Petal three is most likely."

"Petal three has poor manners."

"Petal three is catastrophic release."

"Then I dislike it accurately."

She rotated the tug and watched the panel remnant brighten. The flat face would come around again. She only needed one honest second from a dishonest piece of metal.

Chapter 40: The Orchestra Starts

The counter-burns began across the shell in sequence. H-5, H-6, H-8 remnant, Anchor drone, private tug, cracked-nozzle tug, Little Argument. Not music. Work timed so tightly it borrowed the language.

Joss counted phases. Sian adjusted decay paths. Vera held the command loop still. Noor watched weather assets scrape through danger. Malik watched Anchor Nine's hull temperature and lied to no one.

H-5 burned clean. H-6 answered late but inside tolerance. H-8's remnant gave one last crooked push and fell silent. The Anchor drone fired so weakly its contribution looked like a clerical correction until Joss magnified it. Grace Meridian arrived just in time to add thrust from the edge of the shell. Redcap's cracked nozzle sprayed wide, ugly, acceptable.

"Little Argument," Vera said.

"On point."

Kira clamped to the panel remnant and fired.

Little Argument shook harder than during Tank-Seven. Fuel dropped. Clamp stress rose. The panel tried to twist away and take the tug with it. The magnetic lock held in pulses, each one a thud through the frame. Kira's left thumb hovered over manual release. Her right hand kept the burn aligned.

"Hold three more seconds," Sian said.

Pavel made a sound that was almost speech.

Kira held four.

She released. The tug spun, recovered late, and dropped below planned altitude by a margin that made the reentry alarm wake with opinions. Heat warnings flashed though she was nowhere near fire yet. The panel remnant drifted away on its new path, doing exactly what they needed and nothing more.

"Attitude?" Vera asked.

"Insulted."

"Pilot?"

"Same."

Joss shouted, "Shell loosening. Debris group entering decay corridor."

For the first time all day, the model changed shape in a way everyone understood. The tight blade became a spreading fall. Red lines widened, lowered, and lost their terrible rhythm. Noor's surviving weather satellites crossed through amber instead of red. Anchor Nine's immediate risk dropped from critical to ugly.

Malik let out a breath that sounded like surrender and victory sharing a room.

The orchestra had started. Its best note was gravity doing honest work.

The success did not arrive as relief at first. It arrived as more work. Joss demanded new tracks. Noor demanded the updated decay forecast. Vera demanded status from every crewed platform still holding breath in Shell C. Malik demanded to know whether Anchor Nine's next risk window had truly moved or merely changed names.

"Moved," Joss said. "Not gone."

"Medicine understands not gone."

Kira let the tug drift while attitude control recovered. Her hands shook once, then stopped. The reentry alarm continued offering opinions until she found the menu and silenced it.

"Little Argument status?" Vera asked.

"Low, hot, and rude."

Pavel added, "Damaged but responsive."

"Pilot?"

Kira looked at the loosening shell. "Same."

Sian watched the red lines fall into wider paths and felt no triumph. Triumph belonged to cleaner days. This was a reprieve, assembled out of secrecy, damage, public math, and people refusing the first answer physics gave them.

Noor's surviving satellites crossed their next marks. One reported a camera fault, then recovered. Malik reported Anchor Nine still holding. Grace Meridian continued toward Kira's later rescue track, not yet needed, suddenly precious.

Vera let the room hear one full breath before the next alarm. She had learned that people needed proof of breathing as much as data. Then H-2 stayed silent, and the day took back its pause.

Joss's hand stopped above the keyboard when H-2 failed to answer. Sian did not curse. That was how he knew it was bad. Noor asked for the weather impact before anyone softened it. Malik asked for Anchor Nine impact before anyone routed around him.

The room, which had just begun to believe in widening lines, tightened again.

Vera looked at Kira's icon, low and hot after the last burn. Command sometimes meant spending the same person twice because the sky had placed her there. Vera hated that. Hatred did not change the vector.

Before the H-2 silence took over, there had been one minute when the room almost believed the hard part had passed. A junior analyst took off her headset and wiped her face. Someone laughed at a message from Patiently. Noor's deputy reported a patched forecast reaching the coastal centers. These were tiny, dangerous relaxations.

Then H-2 failed to answer, and each small relief folded itself away. Joss reopened the burn model. Sian straightened. Vera did not sigh. Kira saw the new line appear on her display and knew before anyone asked that it pointed toward her.

"Give me alternatives," Vera said.

The room tried. That mattered. It tried before it spent her.

Chapter 41: Wrong Silence

Wrong silence followed success. The command loop waited for H-2, the last quiet satellite, to answer its burn cue. It did not.

"H-2 no response," Joss said.

The words landed harder than an alarm. Alarms at least knew what they wanted. Silence opened a room and made everyone stare into it.

Tane tried backup keys. Nothing. He tried the older maintenance handshake, then the emergency disposal override, then a raw bus ping Helix engineering insisted should wake any platform with a warm battery. H-2 gave back no pulse, no thermal bloom, no polite refusal. The silent satellite sat outside the main cluster with enough mass and fuel to pull a fragment group back into danger if left alone.

"Could it be truly cold?" Vera asked.

"Maybe," Joss said. "But its mass still matters. The group behind it is following the old cadence. Without a phase change, it bends back across Anchor Nine."

Sian ran the model with H-2 inactive. The red line crossed the traffic wall, passed through the edge of Anchor Nine's safety bubble, then continued into Noor's Weather East Seven. The first crossing was not a direct kill in every run. That was the trouble. It was uncertain enough for argument and certain enough for dread.

Kira checked her orbit. Little Argument was low, fuel thin, arm broken, clamp hot. The tug smelled of overworked insulation and cooling metal. A line of sweat had dried along her jaw. Her hands felt large and slow in the gloves.

"I can reach H-2."

Pavel said, "No."

The word was not engineering. It was personal and therefore useless in command, which did not make it wrong.

Vera said, "Fuel says marginal."

Kira said, "Marginal is not no."

"Sometimes it is," Pavel said.

"Not this time."

Sian ran the model again with Kira as substitute impulse. If H-2 remained silent, the fragment group crossed Anchor Nine in twenty-six minutes and clipped Weather East Seven. If Kira used the tug as the missing engine, the group fell into drag. Return uncertain. Rescue intercept possible if Little Argument survived attitude loss. Reentry risk if it did not.

Vera asked for alternatives. None arrived.

The loop filled with people not speaking. Malik looked at the remaining crew on Anchor Nine. Noor looked at her patched weather net and the one satellite she had not yet lost. Joss looked at timing. Sian looked at the old theory that had become a debt. Tane looked at a satellite his company had hidden and could no longer wake.

"Kira," Vera said, "I need an honest pilot assessment."

"I can make the intercept. I can deliver impulse. Recovery depends on engine cutoff and attitude control after."

"Probability?"

"Better if no one asks me to decorate it."

Pavel cut in. "Her tug is damaged beyond certified continuation. Clamp heat is above tested emergency range. Fuel after intercept is below controlled rendezvous reserve. The frame has taken torque loads we did not design for. That is the engineering assessment."

"Pilot response?" Vera asked.

Kira looked at H-2's dark icon.

"Still marginal. Still not no."

Malik spoke from Anchor Nine. "If she does not do it?"

Joss answered because no one else wanted to. "You hold if the line misses wide. If it does not, shield twelve is already gone. Weather East Seven likely goes blind or breaks."

Noor said, "East Seven is carrying the patched coastal feed."

One more dependency. One more reason ground and sky refused to separate.

Vera's voice came back quieter. "Kira, you are authorized to proceed to H-2 intercept. You are not authorized to die for a machine."

"No one dies for machines," Kira said. "We spend machines for people."

She did not look at Earth this time. She aligned for H-2.

The course to H-2 crossed the edge of the debris field they had just loosened. Joss gave her a path that looked safe only because the display could not show sweat. Kira flew it with manual corrections, letting the tug drift through spaces the computer disliked. Twice a speck flashed past the camera. Once the hull rang softly, a tiny note of contact that every person on the loop pretended not to hear until Pavel said pressure remained stable.

Vera kept the command channel spare. No encouragement. No speeches. Kira appreciated that. Speeches were for people standing on floors.

"Range to H-2, two hundred meters," Joss said.

"Visual."

H-2 turned slowly ahead, dark against Earthlight. It did not look like an enemy. It looked like abandoned responsibility. That was worse.

Chapter 42: Last Tug

Little Argument had one real burn left and several imaginary ones pilots keep for morale. Kira used the real one to reach H-2 and the imaginary ones to stay calm.

The intercept was not graceful. Little Argument had been graceful only in the way a scarred tool could be graceful, by doing its work without asking to be admired. Now even that dignity had thinned. The tug answered controls with a lag. The clamp's heat warning sat red. The dead capture arm shivered when thrusters fired, a useless limb remembering work.

H-2 was darker than the rest, panels folded tight, no pulse, no heat bloom. It looked genuinely dead. That made it harder to hate. A dangerous machine with lights and chatter gave anger somewhere to go. H-2 offered only mass and consequence.

"No command response," Tane confirmed.

"Then I push."

Pavel's voice was quiet. "Clamp cannot take full load."

"I know."

"The tug frame cannot either."

"It only has to take it long enough."

"Kira, if the engine surges, the clamp may fuse."

"Then we will learn something."

"I am tired of education."

She brought Little Argument under H-2's bus and rolled until the clamp faced the satellite's strongest panel. The approach lights washed over white paint, gray seams, Helix markings half-flayed by time. She touched the clamp to metal. The magnets bit with a dull vibration through the hull.

"Contact," she said.

Pavel checked load. "Bad but holding."

"Bad has been reliable."

Joss counted down. "Burn angle confirmed. Impulse duration three point eight seconds. Cut hard on my mark."

"Hard cut," Kira said.

"Sian?" Vera asked.

"This is the line," Sian said.

Kira fired Little Argument's engine at an angle that would spend tug momentum as part of the score. The burn pinned her to the seat. Structural alarms layered over fuel alarms. The whole tug seemed to twist around her spine. H-2 resisted, then moved, a dark building shoved by a stubborn insect.

"Two seconds," Joss counted. "One. Cut."

Kira hit cutoff.

The engine did not cut clean. It coughed, surged, died.

H-2 moved.

Little Argument tumbled away, power flickering. The clamp fused for half a second too long, dragged the tug sideways, then tore free with a bang Kira felt in her teeth. The cockpit went black except for emergency strips. Her body tried to keep a horizon where none existed.

Pavel shouted her name. Kira disliked drama on loops, but she did not have enough electricity to complain.

She found the attitude reset by touch. Nothing. She tried backup bus. One display flickered, showed spin, died. She hit the side panel with the heel of her hand because sometimes old methods deserved respect and because there was no one aboard to judge her maintenance technique.

The tug answered weakly.

Stars slowed across the cracked forward display. Earth slid into view, too bright.

"Alive," she said.

Everyone spoke at once. Vera restored order with one word: "Report."

"Engine dead or pretending. Clamp fused open. Arm dead. Attitude weak but responding. Fuel unreadable, which I interpret as rude."

Pavel came back thinner than usual. "Telemetry agrees. Mostly."

"Rescue intercept?"

Joss answered. "Possible. Grace Meridian can reach you after debris group clears. Do not spend anything unless spin returns."

"I am becoming excellent at doing nothing."

No one laughed, but the loop breathed again.

Grace Meridian's pilot, a woman named Sal Arro, entered Kira's private loop. "Tug Nalen, I have your rescue intercept. You are an ugly little target."

"Little Argument prefers distinctive."

"Little Argument is rotating like a thrown toolbox."

"Personal comments are not rescue."

"Copy. I will bring compliments after capture."

Kira watched Grace Meridian's icon crawl toward her. Rescue felt slower than danger. Her cockpit dimmed to conserve power, leaving only essential displays and the old photograph under its strap. She had no useful work except staying alive, so she did that with attention. She checked heat. Checked spin. Checked the place where the arm should have answered and did not.

Chapter 43: Falling Line

The counter-resonance drove the debris into falling lines. Not all at once. Not cleanly. But the model showed fragment groups lowering into atmosphere where drag would thicken, heat would build, and metal would become light no one had scheduled.

Noor recalculated reentry corridors. Her maps updated from red blades to orange threads, still dangerous, still requiring warnings, but no longer aimed like intent. "Ocean lanes for most. Minor ground alerts still needed."

"Issue them," Vera said.

Noor issued maritime advisories, aviation reroutes, coastal cautions, and a plain-language public notice that did not pretend falling metal was normal. Her staff layered uncertainty over weather, weather over debris, debris over human movement. The work was ugly and exacting. It was also, she thought, a kind of care.

Joss watched H-2's group miss Anchor Nine by four kilometers and fall. Four kilometers felt lavish after a day of centimeters. The line slipped below the platform, past the cracked shield, away from the chapel window, away from Malik's remaining crew. Then it lowered toward atmosphere, where drag would do without drama what engines had barely begun.

"Anchor Nine clear of H-2 group," he said.

Malik did not answer for a moment. When he did, his voice had lost its hard edge. "Copy."

Sian sat back from her console and covered her face. No one told her it was over. It was not. Debris remained. Weather remained degraded. Little Argument was wounded and low. But the amplification had stopped. The shell was no longer conducting disaster.

Kira coaxed Little Argument into a weak safe attitude. Fuel nearly gone. Arm dead. Clamp fused. Antenna scratched. A tug can survive as an argument with replacement parts. She had enough power to keep heat balanced and enough radio to irritate Pavel, which she counted as life support.

Pavel reviewed telemetry. "You broke everything."

"Not everything."

"The coffee warmer survived."

"Then prioritize repair."

He laughed in a way that hurt to hear.

Grace Meridian reported a rescue intercept in thirty-two minutes. Kira disliked needing tow, then decided pride weighed too much for her current orbit. She secured loose objects in the cockpit with slow motions. The old photograph of Little Argument's commissioning had come free of its Velcro and floated near her shoulder. She caught it and tucked it under a strap.

Below, debris began to enter atmosphere. Small lines of fire, planned as well as panic allowed. People on ships saw brief streaks and called them in. A mountain town received a caution and stayed indoors for an hour under a clear sky. A child on a coast would later say the dawn had sparks in it. Noor saved the reports, not because they were data, though they were, but because people had watched orbit come down and deserved to be part of the record.

Vera kept the shell black until the last major group committed to drag. No one argued now.

The first public reentry videos arrived before the official event log closed. A ship's deck camera caught three white scratches falling behind cloud. A traffic camera in a coastal town caught a brief flare reflected in wet pavement. Someone uploaded a shaky phone video with a caption that guessed meteor shower, missile, miracle. Vera ordered public affairs to correct without scolding. People named what they saw with the words they had.

Noor compared the sightings against her corridors. "Within estimate."

"Good," Vera said.

"Good is doing some work."

"Let it."

Sian watched the clips later and felt neither pride nor horror in clean form. The falling lines were beautiful, which annoyed her. Beauty had been untrustworthy all day. Still, beauty did not cause harm by existing. Harm came when people mistook it for permission.

Chapter 44: Anchor Dawn

Anchor Nine did not have dawn in the ordinary sense. It had orbital sunrise every ninety minutes, too frequent to be poetry. The one after the falling line felt earned anyway.

Malik opened the ward shutter. Earthlight entered, pale and indifferent to human relief. Dust motes and loose fibers floated in the beam. The remaining crew were alive. The station was damaged, hot in one section, cold in another, and surrounded by fewer immediate threats than before.

Cera inventoried supplies with the grim patience of someone who would later discover half the labels had sweated loose. Engineering crawled through bypass routes. Someone found the paper flower from the chapel window and taped it to a sealed locker after clearing it with fire rules. Malik pretended not to see until he was sure it was not near anything warm.

Repair crews would come when traffic allowed. Patiently had delivered its patients to lower safe orbit. Dr. Maas sent a message through medical relay: Still not history.

Malik laughed alone in the ward. It came out too loud and turned into a cough. He drank water from a bulb and discovered his hands were shaking now that usefulness had loosened its grip.

Vera checked platform status. "Anchor Nine, can you hold for repair?"

"We can hold."

"Define hold."

"Crew alive, platform rude, doctor tired."

"Accepted."

Kira's tug drifted below station altitude, limping toward a rescue intercept. Malik asked whether she needed medical.

"Only if you treat embarrassment," Kira said.

"Chronic condition. Poor prognosis."

"Can I work through it?"

"Pilots usually do, which is why it becomes chronic."

He checked the patient transfer log again. Patiently had taken everyone he chose to send. The two he kept were stable. No names had moved from patient to memorial. That was not victory in the grand sense. It was the only kind of victory medicine trusted.

Ito sent a ferry damage photo: a spray of micro-pits across Patiently's aft skin, each one circled in bright diagnostic yellow. Caption: beauty marks.

Malik sent back: dermatology consult declined.

The chapel window was cracked but sealed. Someone taped a note under it: not a sign, just glass. Malik approved. Meaning was best applied lightly after surviving. Heavy meaning made people careless. Light meaning let them keep working.

As sunrise moved across the module, he stood beneath the cracked pane and allowed himself ten seconds with no task. Then Cera called for help with a stubborn valve, and dawn became shift work again.

Patiently docked at the lower medical station forty minutes later. Cera sent Malik a clipped report: transfers complete, two patients sedated, one pump replaced, one ferry pilot unbearably pleased with himself.

Malik sent back: reduce pilot ego if clinically indicated.

Dr. Maas added her own message an hour later, dictated because her hands were occupied by tubing. She thanked him for moving her down the list and then threatened professional consequences if he ever told anyone she had said thank you. Malik saved the message under patient correspondence and did not show it to anyone. Some things were evidence. Some things were ballast for later.

When the first repair crew finally reached Anchor Nine, they brought sealant, radiators, and fresh coffee. The coffee was terrible. Malik drank two bulbs anyway.

Repair chief Anika Sen took one look at shield section twelve and said, "Who has been letting this platform age in public?"

"Committees," Malik said.

"Figures."

Her crew worked with the brisk contempt of people who had seen underfunding wear a technical disguise. They patched, braced, and tagged everything for later replacement. Malik followed until Anika pointed at a chair.

"Doctor, sit before you become a second repair job."

He sat. It felt less like obedience than collapse with witnesses.

Chapter 45: Public Orbit

Vera broadcast the raw cause and rescue record before anyone could polish it. Helix hidden fuel. Unauthorized autonomous herding. Collision trigger. Counter-resonance rescue. Damaged weather net. Anchor Nine impacts. Kira's tug. All of it.

The public version had diagrams and no adjectives. Adjectives invited argument about tone. The tracks were enough. A red line crossing a medical platform did not need emphasis. A hidden fuel table did not need outrage to become outrageous.

Tane asked for time to prepare a statement. Vera gave him twelve minutes after release. He used eleven to look ruined. When he finally spoke, his words were careful but no longer empty. Helix accepted operational responsibility for undisclosed retained fuel. Helix would comply with investigation. Helix would suspend all autonomous disposal research. He did not say accident as often as his lawyers probably wanted.

Edda supplied risk memos to regulators. Noor supplied weather degradation estimates. Sian supplied her old paper with warnings highlighted. Joss supplied timing logs and simulation versions. Pavel supplied a repair invoice draft so offensive that Kira framed it digitally from the rescue tug while still under tow.

Questions came from governments, insurers, families, reporters, and people who had seen lines of fire cross the dawn sky. Families of Anchor Nine patients wanted names, outcomes, proof. Vera made medical route those answers through people trained to speak to fear. Reporters wanted heroes. Vera gave them roles. Pilot, medic, meteorologist, modeler, engineer, insurer, traffic crew. No single face could carry a collective failure or a collective repair.

"Did Helix break the law?" one minister asked.

"That is for regulators and courts," Vera said. "They broke the sky's trust."

The phrase made three people in the command room look up. She regretted it immediately because it would quote well. Still, it was true enough.

She answered what she could and refused what needed investigation. She did not say unprecedented. It was a word used to avoid naming choices. Hidden fuel was a choice. Proprietary silence was a choice. Underfunded weather reserves were choices. So were counter-burns, ferry windows, and Kira's last push.

"Is orbit safe?" a minister asked.

Vera looked at the debris model, still crowded, still moving. Even after the falling lines, Shell C was full of spent stages, dead relays, paint flecks, tools, panels, and human optimism left too long in vacuum.

"Orbit is usable," she said. "Safe is work."

That became the quote. She hated quotes. She liked that one enough not to retract it.

After the broadcast, she stepped into a side corridor and stood where no camera could see her. Her hands hurt from gripping consoles. Joss found her there with two bulbs of coffee.

"This is terrible," he said, handing one over.

"The coffee or the day?"

"I did not want to overstate my range."

She drank it anyway. It was terrible. It helped.

The next briefing was worse because everyone had slept just enough to regain ambition. Agencies wanted jurisdiction. Companies wanted definitions. Ministers wanted assurance shaped like certainty. Vera brought Joss, Noor, Edda, and Sian to the table and refused to let Helix sit alone with policy people.

"We need a term for the event," someone said.

"Shell C emergency," Vera said.

"That lacks public clarity."

"It has technical clarity."

"The press is already calling it the Gravity Orchestra."

Sian's expression made the room colder. "Do not let poetry become branding."

Edda slid the risk memos forward. "And do not let branding become settlement language."

Vera watched the argument turn, for once, in the right direction: away from awe, toward records, rules, and maintenance budgets. That was not satisfying. It was better than satisfying.

Afterward, Edda found Vera near the exit. "They will try to make the settlement confidential."

"Can you stop them?"

"Not alone."

"You are not alone."

Edda nodded, as if filing the phrase. "Your public record helps."

"It was not made for court."

"Good records rarely are. That is why courts trust them."

Vera looked back at the briefing room. People were still arguing over names. Let them. The documents had already begun their slower burn.

Chapter 46: Helix Reckoning

Helix lost licenses first, then contracts, then the comfortable fiction that cleanup could be proprietary while risk stayed public. Tane Orlo testified in hearings with the subdued usefulness of a man who had run out of denial.

He provided command records, program names, approval chains, fuel concealment memos. Edda made sure insurers did not bury them under settlement language. Vera made sure traffic regulators understood every technical sentence. Noor made sure weather losses were not treated as collateral inconvenience. Malik submitted medical timelines with patient temperatures, transfer delays, and the precise minute Patiently should have been able to leave if the shell had been honest.

The hearings took place under flags, seals, and lighting designed to make accountability look orderly. Kira watched some of them from a repair bay and found the neat rows of microphones offensive. Disaster had been loud, hot, and full of bad options. The room made it seem as if everyone had always been seated.

Sian attended one hearing and left before praise could reach her. Her theory had helped rescue the shell. It had also tempted fools. Both facts would have to live together. When a committee member called her work visionary, she said, "Vision without constraint is trespass," and then refused to make it warmer.

Kira watched from the bay while Little Argument hung open around technicians. Panels removed. Wiring exposed. Clamp assembly blackened. The arm detached and strapped to a stand. Pavel had labeled it: DO NOT DISCARD, PILOT SENTIMENTAL.

"I am not sentimental."

"You patted it."

"I check surfaces."

"With affection."

"You wrote flowers on a torque housing."

"Corrosion marking."

The dead arm looked smaller off the tug. Kira touched joint two with two fingers when Pavel turned away. He did not turn away far enough to miss it.

The hearings mattered, but they were not cleanup. Cleanup was slow: catalog, burn, nudge, track, warn, repeat. Reckoning without maintenance was theater. New rules required public fuel declarations for retired platforms, open disposal telemetry in crewed shells, and emergency traffic access to any engine capable of changing shared risk. Companies objected. Vera read their objections at night like weather reports.

Kira signed up for the next shepherd rotation before medical cleared her. Medical rejected it. She appealed. Everyone behaved predictably, which was comforting.

Tane visited the repair bay once. Pavel saw him first and moved his body slightly between Tane and Little Argument, as if the tug might catch liability by proximity.

"I wanted to apologize," Tane said.

Kira wiped her hands on a rag. "To me?"

"Yes. And others."

"Others need it more."

"I know."

She studied him. He looked thinner, unprotected by denial. "Then apologize usefully."

"What does that mean?"

"Tell the truth before records make you."

He nodded. It was not forgiveness. It was a maintenance instruction.

Kira's medical clearance hearing lasted nine minutes. The doctor reviewed bruising, stress response, suit time, and the fact that she had struck a control panel hard enough to leave a mark.

"That was a diagnostic intervention," Kira said.

"It was a punch."

"A successful punch."

"Not a medical category."

The doctor gave her restricted duty and a counseling referral. Kira objected to the first and said nothing about the second. Later, she attended because Malik sent a message that read: chronic embarrassment responds poorly to denial.

Pavel found out and did not comment. Instead he adjusted the coffee warmer so it stopped burning the bulbs. That was how he apologized for being relieved.

Little Argument's repair schedule filled a wall display. Replace arm. Reinforce frame. Rebuild clamp. Inspect engine throat. Rewire antenna. Remove pilot-added labels from cockpit if offensive to maintenance. Kira objected to the last item.

"Those labels are operational history."

"One says do not trust handsome satellites," Pavel said.

"Correct."

"Another says release before pride."

"Also correct."

He left that one. Some labels were better than sensors because they failed only when ignored.

Chapter 47: Weather Returns

Noor restored partial weather coverage from patched satellites, borrowed commercial imagers, and two old platforms that should have retired years earlier but apparently disliked rest. Forecast quality remained degraded. People on the ground noticed when warnings arrived less clean.

"Tell them why," Vera said.

Noor did. Budget committees hated explanations that led upward into orbit and backward into procurement. She gave them anyway. She brought maps, missed confidence intervals, storm warnings that had widened because East Three was blind, and fuel charts showing how East Five had spent hurricane reserve to dodge Helix fragments. One committee member asked whether the public needed that level of technical detail.

"The public needed the warning those satellites provided," Noor said. "They can survive the explanation."

Weather East Three became a blind object to be deorbited later. It still transmitted star fields until Noor commanded it to low-power safe. She wrote it a retirement note in the operations log and dared anyone to laugh.

Kira read the note while waiting for clearance. It said: served beyond design, lost sight during Shell C event, did not fail willingly.

"Machines do not will," Pavel said.

"You wrote flowers on the arm housing."

"That was corrosion marking."

"You made three petals."

"Corrosion can be floral."

They both looked at the tug. Little Argument would fly again after arm replacement, frame reinforcement, new clamp assembly, rebuilt antenna, and what Pavel called moral rewiring. The coffee warmer had survived but Pavel disassembled it anyway, claiming trauma inspection.

Noor sent Kira a weather image stitched from damaged sources. Cloud fields, imperfect but readable. A storm curled offshore, its edge less sharp than it should have been. Cities glowed faintly beneath cloud breaks. The image had seams where commercial feeds and buoy data corrected each other.

Caption: usable sky.

Kira saved it.

Later, Noor visited Anchor Nine during a repair stop. Malik showed her the cracked chapel window and the sealed maintenance trunk. She showed him the storm model that had used Patiently's scarred telemetry.

"So your ferry became weather," she said.

"It already had atmosphere," Malik said.

"Doctor humor?"

"Emergency grade."

They stood together at the window. Earth turned below, wearing clouds Noor could now read imperfectly again. Imperfect mattered. It was where most work lived.

Weather East Three's deorbit took place weeks later. Noor stayed late to watch the final track. It crossed a quiet ocean lane and vanished into atmosphere without fragments reaching ground. The operation log accepted the final entry with no ceremony.

Kira sent Noor a message: clean fall.

Noor replied: best kind.

The patched network slowly improved. Two replacement satellites were funded with emergency money nobody had wanted to spend before the emergency. Noor did not call that victory. Victory would have been listening earlier. Still, she signed the procurement request and made sure reserve fuel was not the first line cut.

On her wall, she kept the stitched image marked usable sky. Visitors sometimes mistook it for decoration. Noor corrected them. It was an operations artifact, she said. A reminder that degraded systems could still serve if people admitted degradation in time.

When the first replacement satellite launched, she watched from the same control room where East Three had gone blind. No applause. Just the tracking tone, clean and rising, and the quiet relief of a spare becoming real before the next emergency demanded it.

The new satellite carried more fuel than the old budget model allowed and a public reserve declaration Noor had personally worded. She read it aloud before approving final handover. The engineers teased her for ceremony. She accepted the charge.

"Ceremony is how memory survives procurement," she said.

No one teased after that.

When the satellite reached station, its first image showed a broad storm field and a clean limb of Earth. Noor filed it beside East Three's last useful frame. Work continued. That was the memorial weather allowed.

Chapter 48: Sian's Revision

Sian revised the gravity-orchestra theory in a paper no one could mistake for invitation. The first page contained limits before equations. No hidden fuel. No autonomous operation in crewed shells. No proprietary cadence. Public traffic integration required. Manual abort paths required. If beauty appears in model before safety, stop.

Joss read the draft in her lab at midnight while Sian drank tea gone cold. He had printed the first page because some arguments deserved paper. The limits sat in heavy type above the abstract. He tapped the last line.

"That last one is not mathematical."

"It is methodological."

"It is a warning to your younger self."

"She needs several."

He helped her add the counter-resonance data. The rescue had produced the most complete debris-damping dataset ever recorded. It had been purchased with shield loss, weather blindness, and one very injured tug. Sian refused to call the event a successful demonstration. The data section used phrases like emergency improvisation, unacceptable precursor conditions, and non-repeatable risk environment. Reviewers complained that she had buried the elegance.

"Good," she told Joss.

"They meant it as criticism."

"I received it as evidence of comprehension."

She dedicated the revision not to heroes, but to public constraints. Her publisher disliked it. She insisted. A dedication to regulation did not sing. That was the point. Songs made people forget bolts.

At a lecture months later, someone asked whether the gravity orchestra was safe now. The auditorium was full of students, operators, regulators, and a few industry people who smiled as if smiling could preempt suspicion.

"No," Sian said. "It is understandable. Safety is governance, maintenance, disclosure, and humility. Equations only attend."

Another student asked whether she regretted writing the original paper.

Sian looked at the slide showing Anchor Nine's miss distance. Four kilometers. A number that had felt lavish because people had first survived ninety meters, twelve centimeters, one point two seconds.

"I regret believing warnings could stand beside methods without power behind them," she said. "I do not regret knowledge. I regret leaving it easier to use badly than to govern well."

Kira watched the recording from the repair bay, one boot hooked under a rail while Pavel argued with a new actuator. She sent one comment: needs more rude fragments.

Sian replied: reviewers said same.

Then, after a minute: you were the rude fragment.

Kira showed Pavel.

"Accurate," he said.

"I am filing a tone complaint."

"Denied."

Sian's revision changed the way cleanup proposals were reviewed. Not quickly. Nothing institutional moved quickly unless it was avoiding blame. But review boards began asking different questions. Where is the public traffic interface? What happens if autonomy wakes in damaged mode? Who can shut it down? What fuel remains and who knows it?

One young engineer wrote to Sian that the new rules made elegant disposal systems harder to approve. Sian wrote back: yes.

She deleted three warmer versions before sending that one. Joss told her brevity was sometimes kindness. She told him brevity was sometimes a hammer.

At night, when the lab emptied, she still opened the old paper. Not to punish herself. To remember how clean danger can look before it has names attached.

She also kept a copy of Vera's traffic order beside the revision. All command keys to traffic. All models public to this loop. The sentence had no elegance, which made it sturdy. Sian began using it in class.

"This," she told students, "is an equation too. It defines who may act on whom, with what knowledge, under which constraints. Leave it out and your math is only a wish with engines."

Some students looked disappointed. Some looked relieved. She trusted the relieved ones first.

After lectures, she answered practical questions longest. How much autonomy is too much? Who should hold abort authority? What if public traffic command is wrong? She did not pretend governance made error vanish. She told them error needed witnesses.

"A private mistake becomes public physics," she said. "Design accordingly."

One student stayed after class and asked whether fear belonged in engineering. Sian packed her notes slowly.

"Fear belongs near engineering," she said. "Not driving. Not absent. Near enough to be consulted."

The student wrote that down. Sian hoped it would survive ambition.

Chapter 49: Shepherds' Memorial

The shepherds' memorial was informal because formal ceremonies invited speeches. Kira, Pavel, Noor, Malik, Joss, Sian, Ito, and Vera met on a maintenance deck six months after the shell event. Little Argument was clamped nearby, repaired but still mismatched in paint.

They chose the deck because it had no podium and because the port showed Shell C without making it pretty. The repaired tug wore panels in three shades of white and one unpainted patch Pavel said would be covered when he trusted the replacement skin. The new capture arm folded neatly, younger than the rest of the machine and already insulted by Kira's handling.

They marked lost machines first: Weather East Three, H-8, two maintenance drones, Little Argument's original arm. Then near-missed people: Anchor Nine ward, Patiently's passengers, coastal cities under degraded forecasts, crews who waited in closed corridors. Noor read East Three's log note. Malik read the medical count: transferred, treated, recovered, still recovering. Vera read no speech. She simply recorded the gathering into the traffic archive under lessons, human.

No one called machines dead as if they were people. No one called them nothing either. They had carried work.

Malik brought coffee. Ito brought a ferry patch with Patiently's name and a small line of yellow marks representing micro-pits. Noor brought the stitched weather image printed on flexible film. Pavel brought the broken joint two actuator despite claiming not to be sentimental.

Kira fixed the actuator to a rail with wire.

"That violates every display standard," Pavel said.

"Good."

"It will rattle."

"Also good."

Sian stood beside Joss and looked uncomfortable with tenderness. "Do operators always memorialize equipment?"

"Only when the equipment wins arguments," Joss said.

"The arm lost."

Kira touched the wire. "It let go after the work."

That settled it.

Vera looked through the deck port at the crowded shell. "We will be cleaning this for the rest of our careers."

"Careers are one way to pass debris," Joss said.

It was a bad joke. Everyone appreciated it properly.

Ito raised a coffee bulb. "To dirty windows we do not repeat."

"To public fuel tables," Noor said.

"To patients who complain," Malik said.

"To machines with replacement parts," Pavel said.

Kira looked at Little Argument, mismatched and ready. "To usable sky."

They drank. The coffee was bad. Nobody improved it by saying so.

After the memorial, they took turns walking through Little Argument. Pavel insisted on clean gloves. Ito bumped his shoulder on the hatch and accused the tug of hostility. Noor studied the weather image clipped near Kira's console. Malik found the old note, release before pride, still under its strap.

"Good prescription," he said.

"Terrible patient compliance," Pavel said.

Kira ignored him and showed Vera the new arm's range. Vera watched the mechanism unfold, smooth and quiet.

"You trust it?" Vera asked.

"Not yet."

"But you will fly it."

"Trust comes after work."

They left the actuator wired to the rail. It rattled softly when the deck fans cycled, a small unbeautiful sound that meant something had held until it could not.

Before they dispersed, Vera opened the maintenance deck channel to the traffic archive. Each of them stated one lesson, no speeches allowed. Noor said reserves are data. Malik said evacuation begins before motion. Ito said dirty windows require honest names. Edda said secret risk is not risk, it is trespass. Pavel said certification should include what frightened engineers already know.

Kira went last. "Small work matters before it becomes large work."

No one improved it. The archive saved their voices with the fan noise and the actuator rattle underneath.

When the archive light went off, the gathering became ordinary. Ito argued with Pavel about whether Patiently's new patch should include a small mark for every micro-pit. Noor asked Malik for the exact phrase he used to classify the paper flower. Sian stood at the port with Vera and watched a maintenance tug cross a far lane.

"Do you miss the room?" Sian asked.

"Command?"

"Yes."

Vera considered. "I miss believing there was one room."

Sian nodded. The event had proved the opposite. Command had been spread across a cockpit, a ward, a weather desk, an insurance office, a repair bay, and machines old enough to have their own bad habits.

Chapter 50: The Gravity Orchestra

Kira returned to orbit on Little Argument's next rotation with a new arm, reinforced frame, and a coffee warmer Pavel had upgraded beyond mission justification. Her first target was a dead relay no larger than a suitcase, drifting toward a lane used by weather repair drones.

Routine work.

She liked the phrase again, cautiously. Routine did not mean safe. It meant known risks, named margins, public tracks, honest fuel, and a plan dull enough to trust. Dullness had become beautiful to her in the way a clean checklist was beautiful after an alarm.

Joss counted her in. Noor's patched forecast scrolled on a side display: cloud fields, storm confidence, drag notes for the debris lane. Sian's revised limits sat in the mission file, required reading before the cleanup burn. Vera's traffic rules required public fuel declarations for every retired platform. Helix's surviving assets flew under supervision until they fell. Anchor Nine's shield scars remained visible on inspection feeds because Malik said paint could wait.

Pavel had added a diagnostic that made the new arm report mood states. He denied this and called them maintenance summaries. Joint two currently read: compliant with reservations.

"Your arm is editorializing," Kira said.

"Your flying inspires prose."

"Keep prose out of my hardware."

"You named the tug Little Argument."

"That is legal testimony."

The dead relay drifted ahead, rotating slowly. Its casing was dark blue, a municipal communications box that had outlived two budgets and one merger. It had no hidden fuel, no autonomy, no arrogance beyond inertia. Kira brought the tug close. The new arm extended smoothly. Too smoothly. She missed the old stutter for one shameful second, then forgave the new machine for being good at its job.

"Range two meters," Joss said.

"I see it."

"Relative spin?"

"Polite."

"That is not a number."

"It is a better category."

She captured the relay cleanly. The claw closed around the bracket, force bars green. No alarms. No red shell. No legal officer threatening compulsion. The quiet felt earned and suspicious.

Below, Earth turned with storms, cities, oceans, hospitals, ports, farms, and roads, every fragile system pretending ground was separate from sky. Orbit was not music. It was not destiny. It was work moving fast enough to punish vanity. It was also people: Noor arguing for storm fuel, Malik turning his worried face away from patients, Ito abusing a dirty window, Sian putting limits before equations, Joss refusing elegant lies, Vera improving signal quality by muting power, Pavel listening to machines suffer.

Kira armed the disposal burn. The relay would drop into a drag corridor over open ocean, small enough to vanish without witness. That was the best kind of cleanup. No fire someone had to explain. No headline. No memorial.

"Burn on your mark," Joss said.

Kira checked the public fuel declaration on the relay even though she already knew it was empty. Habit, rule, respect. "Verified."

"Mark."

She made the burn.

For one quiet second, the tracks on her display aligned: tug, debris, station, weather chain, planet. The geometry had no intention. Humans would be tempted to give it one anyway. She let herself see the shape and refused the myth.

Not an orchestra. A crew.

The relay began its slow fall. The weather drones' lane cleared. Little Argument's new arm folded home with reservations. Kira logged the disposal, sent the track to traffic, and watched the sky remain usable.

Joss cleared the weather repair drones through the lane three minutes later. They passed without incident, small service craft carrying panels and propellant to Noor's battered chain. Kira watched them go and felt the old satisfaction return in cautious form. Not triumph. Completion.

"Nice capture," Joss said.

"Routine."

"That word has history now."

"Most useful words do."

Pavel opened a maintenance note on the new arm. "Joint two reports compliant."

"With reservations?"

"Always."

Kira laughed, short and real. Below her, a storm moved toward a coast that would receive its warning on time. Above her, the catalog still held too many objects, too many old choices, too much work. Little Argument turned toward the next target, and Kira let it. The sky did not need saving once. It needed tending.

The next target appeared on the schedule before she finished logging the first. A paint-fleck cluster, small but drifting toward a sensor lane. Then a dead inspection lens. Then an old bolt bag whose owner had merged twice and denied memory. Routine work had a queue.

Kira accepted the next assignment and warmed Little Argument's thrusters.

"Already?" Pavel asked.

"The sky is untidy."

"The pilot is medically annoying."

"Log both."

Joss cleared her path. Noor's weather feed steadied on the side display. Somewhere below, people made plans under forecasts they did not have to think about. Kira liked that best: work done well enough to disappear.

Little Argument turned, small and persistent, and went to argue with the next piece of metal.

The paint-fleck cluster looked like nothing at first, a faint scatter in the lidar return, too small for drama and too sharp for neglect. Kira brought the tug in slow. The new arm stayed folded; this job needed only a shepherding burn, a minor nudge that would lower the flecks into atmosphere before they crossed the sensor lane.

"Range clean," Joss said.

"Weather drones clear?"

Noor answered, "Clear and grateful in their limited mechanical way."

"Pavel, arm mood?"

"Not involved and therefore smug."

Kira smiled. She made the small burn. No alarm answered. No hidden satellite woke. No one important asked for a private consultation. The flecks shifted onto the planned decay path, a tiny correction in a crowded sky.

Routine did not feel small anymore. It felt like catching trouble before it learned to speak loudly. Kira logged the burn, watched the track settle, and let the quiet be quiet.

Then she opened the maintenance queue and marked the paint-fleck cluster complete. The system asked for disposition. She chose decaying. It asked for confidence. She chose sufficient. The word looked modest and strong.

"Next object in twelve minutes," Joss said.

"Send it."

"You want the owner history?"

"Only if it affects the capture."

"It does not."

"Then spare me someone else's paperwork sins."

Noor's weather feed refreshed with a clean coastal warning, ordinary and on time. Kira watched it for one second longer than necessary, then closed the side display. Work done well enough to disappear, she reminded herself. That was the point. The sky stayed usable not because anyone declared it safe, but because people kept returning to the small corrections after the speeches ended.

Little Argument nosed toward the next fleck of work. Its new arm rested folded, its old scars still visible under paint that did not quite match. Kira liked that. Perfect repairs lied a little. Honest ones showed where the load had passed through and where the frame had learned to hold.

On the traffic loop, ordinary voices returned: a clearance request, a weather-drone handoff, a maintenance query about a stubborn latch. Kira listened to them with more affection than she would admit. Ordinary voices were the sound of a system still alive, still arguing, still worth the trouble of keeping clear.

She answered the next clearance with steady hands, and the usable sky answered by staying, for one more pass, ordinary.

That, she thought, was enough work for any miracle, and enough proof for morning, and repair afterward.
