Chapter 1:

The bell tolled twice before Lychard realized it was his own bones ringing.

He sat up in the narrow cot, the sound still vibrating through his ribs. Three months since the ritual, and still his skeleton announced itself at odd hours. He pressed a hand to his chest, feeling the faint hum beneath his skin. The marrow singing. The dead in the hill answering.

Outside his cottage, the village of Crow's Nest lay in its usual pre-dawn hush. Lychard dressed in the dark, pulling on the patched coat that had belonged to his father, and the father before him. The coat smelled of smoke and old earth. It smelled like duty.

The ossuary sat atop the hill, a low stone building with a roof of slate and bones set into the mortar. Every family in Crow's Nest had contributed to its walls. Lychard's own grandmother was in the eastern face, her skull grinning between two river stones. He touched it as he passed, a habit his mother had taught him.

Inside, the shelves were full. Femurs stacked like firewood. Rib cages nested one inside another. Skulls in neat rows, facing the door as if watching. Lychard lit three candles from the ember jar and set them in the iron holder. The light caught the carvings on the bones—names, dates, prayers to gods whose names had been forgotten.

He knelt.

"Bones of my blood," he whispered. "Bones of my neighbors. I ask only for the marrow you can spare."

He took up the bone needle and the hollow reed. On the workbench sat a clay bowl, already chipped from a hundred uses. He chose a femur from the recent shelf—Marta Henley, dead of the sweating sickness six weeks past. The bone was still warm. They always were, at first.

He positioned the needle at the joint and pressed. The bone resisted, then gave. The marrow came slow and dark, threading through the reed into the bowl. It smelled of copper and wet stone and something else, something that made Lychard's teeth ache.

When the bowl was half full, he sealed the puncture with wax and returned the femur to its shelf.

"Thank you, Marta."

He carried the bowl to the village square, where the first light was touching the rooftops. The marrow would be portioned into the morning meals—a spoonful for old Thomas whose joints had seized, a few drops for the Baker's newborn who coughed black phlegm, the rest for the winter stores. This was the compact. The dead gave, and the living endured.

Lychard had been the Marrow Thief for five years now, ever since his mother's bones had been added to the wall. It was not a title anyone wanted. It was not a title anyone refused.

The bell tolled again. Closer this time. Lychard looked toward the hill and saw a figure standing at the ossuary door—tall, hooded, still as a standing stone.

The Marrow Thief set down his bowl and began to walk.

Chapter 2:

The figure did not move as Lychard climbed the hill. The morning wind carried the smell of snow from the mountains, and the hooded shape shifted only to face him directly.

Lychard stopped ten paces away.

"You're not from Crow's Nest."

"No." The voice was a woman's, low and worn thin as old linen. She pushed back her hood, and Lychard saw a face that belonged to the road—weathered, sharp-boned, with eyes the color of ash. "I'm told you are the bone-worker here."

"I'm the Marrow Thief." He said it the way his mother had taught him, as a title, not a description. "I tend the ossuary and keep the compact. What's left of a dying trade."

"The trade is not dying," she said. "It is being killed."

She stepped past him into the ossuary, and Lychard followed. Inside, she ran her fingers along the shelves, touching the skulls, the stacked femurs, the smaller bones of children in their own neat row.

"Who has taught you?" she asked.

"My mother. Her mother before her. The tradition stays in the blood."

"Then your blood knows this." She turned and met his eyes. "The Throne Inquisition has burned three ossuaries in the eastern vales. They have hanged two Marrow Thieves and driven a third into the mountains. They call it a corruption. A perversion of the natural order."

Lychard felt the words settle into him like cold water. "The Inquisition has not come here. We are small. We are nothing."

"You are bone-workers." She said it flatly. "That is enough. They answer to a new Archprelate, a man named Harrows. He has declared all marrow-work an abomination. He has declared that the dead belong only to the ground, and that any who disturb them are blasphemers. He has declared war on every ossuary from here to the Salt Sea."

"Then we will hide," Lychard said. "We have hidden before."

"No." The woman reached into her coat and removed something wrapped in leather. She unwrapped it slowly, and Lychard saw the gleam of polished bone—a femur, but carved with symbols he did not recognize. Runic. Alive. The marrow inside it glowed faintly, pulsing like a heartbeat.

"This is the work of the first Marrow Thieves," she said. "Before the compacts. Before the kings. This is what the Inquisition truly fears. Not the village healers and their spoonfuls of milk-marrow. This."

Lychard felt his own bones vibrate in answer. "What is it?"

"It is a key," she said. "To the grave of the first bone-worker. A woman called Ember. She is buried beneath the city where the Archprelate now sits his throne. Harrows does not know she is there, or if he does, he has not found her. But if he does find her, if he claims her marrow—"

"He would not dare," Lychard said. "Even the Inquisition—"

"He would drink her like wine and become a god in flesh." The woman wrapped the femur again. "I am called Vesper. I have walked two hundred miles to find a Marrow Thief who still remembers the old songs. Your mother's mother's mother was among those who sealed Ember's grave. The knowledge is in your blood."

Lychard stared at the wrapped femur. His bones hummed.

"What do you want from me?"

"I want you to leave this place," Vesper said. "I want you to come with me. And I want you to help me open a grave that has been sealed for three hundred years."

Chapter 3:

Lychard did not sleep that night.

He sat at his kitchen table, the candle burned down to a nub, and listened to the wind rattle the windows. Vesper was staying at the inn, a thin thing at the edge of the square. He had told her he would give his answer by dawn.

The ossuary called to him. He could feel it in his molars, a low thrum that never quite went silent. His mother had described it as a second heartbeat, one that only bone-workers could hear. He had always thought she was being poetic. Now he understood she had been describing a fact.

He thought about the eastern vales. He had never traveled farther than the market town fifteen miles south. He knew the names of the mountains the way a child knows the names of distant uncles—familiar, but not family. And yet his bones were telling him to go.

He thought about Marta Henley's femur, still warm in his hands. He thought about the newborn with the black phlegm, and whether he would live to see spring. He thought about the Inquisition's fires and the hanged Marrow Thieves, and he thought about his grandmother's skull grinning from the ossuary wall.

By the time the first gray light touched the window, he had made his decision.

He found Vesper at the inn, already awake, already dressed. She was eating a heel of bread and drinking water, and she did not look surprised to see him.

"You're coming."

"Yes."

She nodded as if she had known it all along. "Good. We leave within the hour."

"I need to prepare the ossuary. Seal the stores. Tell someone—"

"I will wait." She bit into her bread. "But not long."

Lychard spent the morning in the ossuary, working faster than he ever had. He drained what marrow remained into sealed clay pots, stacked them in the cold cellar beneath the floor. He wrote a letter to the Baker's wife, explaining the dosages. He left the bone needle and the hollow reed on the workbench, clean and ready for whoever would take his place.

There was no one. He was the last of his line.

He walked through the village one last time. The Baker's wife was sweeping her stoop. Old Thomas was smoking his pipe on the bench outside his door. The children were running through the square, chasing a dog with a stolen shoe. They did not look at him. They never looked at him. The Marrow Thief was a necessary shadow, a thing that existed at the edges.

He met Vesper at the crossroads, a pack on his back and the wrapped femur in his coat.

"Where are we going?"

"The first ossuary that burned," she said. "In the eastern vales. There is something there I need to show you before we make for the city."

"How far?"

"Ten days. If we are fast and the weather holds."

Lychard looked back at Crow's Nest. The ossuary sat on its hill, solid and patient, a monument to two hundred years of bone-work. He could feel it in his teeth. He could feel it fading.

He turned and walked east.

The road was mud and stone and the bones of smaller animals, ground into the earth by cart wheels and hooves. Vesper walked with a long stride, her hand never far from the knife at her belt. She did not speak, and Lychard did not ask her to.

After six hours, the sun began to sink, and the shadows stretched long across the fields. Vesper stopped at a ruined mill, its roof collapsed, its wheel cracked and dry.

"We camp here," she said.

Lychard sat against the mill wall, watching the dark gather. He had never spent a night away from Crow's Nest. He had never slept without the sound of the ossuary's hum in his ears.

Now the silence was vast and empty, and his bones ached as if they missed something he had not known he was carrying.

Vesper built a small fire, its light barely pushing back the dark. She sat across from him, her face half in shadow.

"Do you know what the Inquisition calls us?" she said.

"Blasphemers."

"Worse. They call us thieves." She smiled, but there was no warmth in it. "As if the dead could own anything. As if the marrow in my bones is mine to keep or theirs to judge."

Lychard said nothing. He watched the fire and felt his bones hum their lonely song.

Chapter 4:

The first burned ossuary stood at the edge of a village called Thornwell, which had once been a thriving market town and was now a scar on the landscape.

Lychard had seen drawings of ash and ruin in old books, but they had not prepared him for the smell. Charred bone had a sweetness to it, like burnt sugar and pork left too long in the sun. He gagged before they were within a hundred yards.

The ossuary itself was nothing but a blackened foundation, the bones scattered across the field like pale stones. Some had been broken, their ends splintered where they had been thrown against the ground. Others bore the marks of fire, cracked and blackened, their marrow boiled away to nothing.

Vesper walked through the field with a quiet reverence, stepping over femurs and ribs and the tiny bones of hands. She stopped at the center, where a blackened post still stood upright, and touched it.

"This was the bone-worker," she said. "They tied her here and let the village watch."

Lychard forced himself to look. The post was stained dark at its base, and the ground around it was hard and barren, as if the soil itself had rejected what had happened there.

"Did she say anything?" he asked. "Before—"

"She sang." Vesper's voice was flat. "She sang the old bone-songs, the ones that call the marrow to dance. She sang until the smoke took her voice, and then she mouthed the words. The villagers say the bones in the field rattled in time. They say the Inquisition priest who lit the fire fled before it was finished."

"They were afraid of her."

"They were right to be." Vesper turned and walked toward the edge of the field, where a small grove of trees stood withered and bare. "This way. There is something you need to see."

The grove had been a place of burial, older than the ossuary, older than the village. The graves were marked with flat stones, and the stones were carved with symbols Lychard recognized from the femur Vesper carried.

"These are the old dead," Vesper said. "Before the compacts. Before the Marrow Thieves became healers and village servants. These people knew the true work."

She knelt at one of the stones and pressed her palm against it. She closed her eyes, and Lychard felt a shift in the air, a pressure behind his eyes. The stone began to glow—faintly, like embers in a dying fire.

"Place your hand here," she said.

Lychard knelt beside her and touched the stone. The cold of it was immediate, but beneath the cold, something else. Warmth. Movement. The stone was alive.

"Bone remembers," Vesper said. "Even when it has turned to stone. Even when it has turned to dust. The marrow leaves its trace in everything it touches."

She withdrew her hand and the glow faded. She stood, brushing the dirt from her knees.

"The Inquisition burned the ossuary and killed the bone-worker," she said. "But the old dead do not burn. They wait. They remember. And when the time comes, they will answer."

Lychard looked at his hand. The palm was tinged faintly red, as if he had been holding a coal.

"The marrow in the old dead," he said slowly. "It is still there?"

"It is. The Inquisition does not know this. They think the old graves are empty, that the power died with the first bone-workers. They are wrong."

Vesper turned and began walking back toward the ruined ossuary.

"The bone-worker who died here was trying to raise an army from the old dead," she said. "That is why they burned her. Not for heresy. Not for blasphemy. For fear."

"An army," Lychard repeated. "Against what?"

"Against what is coming." Vesper looked back at him, and her face was unreadable in the gray light. "The Archprelate Harrows is not a fool. He burns ossuaries not out of hatred, but out of strategy. He is clearing the field. Because when he finds Ember's grave, he will not need an army. He will be the army."

The wind picked up, carrying ash and the scent of old fire. Lychard felt his bones hum a warning.

He followed Vesper out of the field, and he did not look back.

Chapter 5:

They reached the city of Gravenmarch on the twelfth day, and Lychard understood for the first time what smallness meant.

The walls rose forty feet, built from stone the color of old teeth. Towers bristled along the ramparts, each one flying the black banner of the Inquisition—a skeletal hand clutching a flame. The gates were iron-bound oak, and they stood open, but the guards at the entrance wore the Inquisition's silver-and-black, and they looked at every traveler as if measuring them for a grave.

Vesper had stopped at a inn five miles out and shed her road-worn coat for the gray robes of a traveling scholar. Lychard had done the same, though his felt wrong against his skin. The wrapped femur was hidden in a hollowed book, a volume of agricultural treatises that Vesper had prepared in advance.

"Walk like you belong here," she said as they approached the gates. "Walk like you have seen a hundred cities and this one is no different."

Lychard tried. He kept his eyes forward, his pace steady. The guards glanced at them, saw the scholar's robes, and waved them through without a word.

Inside, Gravenmarch was a cacophony. The streets were packed with carts and peddlers and pilgrims in gray, the air thick with the smell of smoke and sewage and roasting meat. Vendors called out from every stall, selling charms and relics and indulgences. Lychard saw a man with a cage full of live pigeons, offering them for sacrifice at a copper each. He saw a woman selling strips of painted cloth that she claimed had touched the Archprelate's robe.

"What is this place?" he murmured.

"The heart of the Inquisition," Vesper said. "Every road leads here. Every prayer ends here. Harrows has built his throne on the bones of a hundred smaller faiths, and he has made the city in his image."

They pushed through the crowds, deeper into the maze of streets. The buildings grew taller, older, their foundations sunk deep into the earth. Lychard could feel them in his bones—old bones, buried beneath the stone and mortar. The city had been built on a graveyard. A vast one.

"The grave of Ember is beneath the cathedral," Vesper said, keeping her voice low. "There is a crypt. Older than the cathedral itself. The priests who built it did not know what they were building on. They thought the crypt was a saint's tomb."

"And you know this how?"

"I was one of them." Vesper's face did not change. "Ten years ago, I was a novice in the Inquisition archives. I found the records. I read the old maps. And when I understood what was beneath my feet, I left."

Lychard looked at her, trying to reconcile the woman beside him with the black-and-silver of the Inquisition. He could not.

"Where do we go now?"

"There is a safe house near the cathedral wall. A woman who keeps the old faith. We will rest there tonight, and tomorrow, we will find a way into the crypt."

They turned down a narrow alley, and the noise of the city faded. The buildings here leaned close overhead, blocking out the sky. Vesper stopped at a door painted red, the paint peeling and old, and knocked three times, then twice, then once.

The door opened a crack. A face peered out—old, female, sharp-eyed.

"You brought company," the woman said.

"I brought the blood," Vesper replied.

The door swung open, and they stepped inside.

Chapter 6:

The safe house was a single room above a tannery, the walls stained with smoke and the floor uneven from centuries of settlement. The old woman, who gave her name only as Etta, served them bowls of thin stew and watched Lychard with eyes that missed nothing.

"You're young for a Marrow Thief," she said.

"I turned twenty-two this winter."

"That's what I said. Young." She sat across from him, her hands wrapped around a cup of tea. "The last one they brought through here was fifty if he was a day. Didn't last the week. The Inquisition found him in the sewers, trying to dig his way to the crypt."

"How did they find him?"

"The walls have ears in this city. More than ears." She tapped her temple. "Bones, too. The cathedral is built on a mass grave, boy. Every foundation in this quarter sits on the dead. The Inquisition doesn't know the old work, but they know something is down there. They feel it in their sleep. They have nightmares they cannot name."

Lychard felt the truth of it in his own chest. The hum was louder here, deeper, a bass note that vibrated through the floor. He could feel the dead beneath him, stacked in layers, their marrow singing in a language he had only begun to learn.

"How do we get in?" Vesper asked.

Etta set down her cup. "There is a tunnel. It begins in the cellar of the butcher's shop three doors down and ends beneath the cathedral sacristy. The butcher is faithful to the old ways. He will not betray you."

"The Inquisition guards the sacristy?"

"Night and day. But the tunnel opens beneath a storage closet. If you are quiet, if you are careful, you can reach the crypt stairs before the guards make their rounds."

"And the crypt itself?"

Etta's face grew still. "I do not know what is in the crypt. I know that the priests who go down there do not come back smiling. I know that the Archprelate himself visits it once a month, alone, and emerges pale as milk. I know that the bones beneath this city are restless, and have been restless for three hundred years."

She looked at Lychard.

"You feel it, don't you, boy? The pull."

He nodded. He could not have denied it if he tried.

"Then you are the blood," Etta said. "And the blood will do what it must."

They slept that night on straw pallets, Lychard's hand never far from the hollowed book that held the femur. He dreamed of bones rising from the earth, marching in formation, their empty eye sockets fixed on a distant point beyond the horizon. He dreamed of a woman made of fire and ash, her voice cracking the sky.

He woke with the dawn, his palms bleeding from where his nails had dug in.

Vesper was already awake, her face grim.

"The Inquisition raided a house in the eastern quarter last night," she said. "They found a marrow-worker. They hanged him at sunrise."

Lychard looked at his bloody palms.

"How long until they find us?"

"Not long," Vesper said. "We go tonight."

Chapter 7:

The butcher's shop closed at sundown, and by the time the last customer had left, the street outside was empty. Etta had given them a lantern with a shuttered flame, a set of iron tools wrapped in cloth, and instructions that Lychard had memorized twice over.

The butcher himself was a broad man with arms like joints of meat and a face that had seen too much of the world's ugliness. He opened the cellar door without a word and pointed to a stack of barrels against the far wall.

"Behind the third barrel," he said. "There's a ring in the floor. Lift it and you'll find the tunnel. It's low—you'll have to crawl."

Vesper nodded. "How much time before the guards change?"

"Midnight. You have three hours until the sacristy patrol shifts. After that, new eyes, new schedule. I don't know it."

"Three hours is enough."

The butcher looked at Lychard. "You're the one who's going to open the grave."

It was not a question.

"I'm going to try."

"Try harder." The butcher turned and climbed the stairs. The door closed behind him, and the lock clicked.

Vesper moved the barrel aside. The ring was exactly where the butcher had said, brass and tarnished, embedded in the stone floor. She pulled, and a section of the floor lifted, revealing a dark hole and the smell of wet earth and something older.

Lychard went first.

The tunnel was barely three feet high, the ceiling rough stone that scraped his back as he crawled. The lantern cast a sickly yellow light that seemed to be swallowed by the dark ahead. He could feel the dead around him—not as presences, but as pressure, as if the earth itself was packed tight with bones.

"How far?" he whispered.

"A quarter mile. Maybe less."

They crawled in silence. The tunnel sloped downward, then leveled, then sloped again. Lychard's knees ached. His palms were raw. The hum in his bones grew louder with every yard, until it was not a hum but a song, a chorus of voices singing in a language that predated language itself.

He stopped.

"Do you hear that?"

Vesper's voice came from behind him. "I hear nothing."

"The bones. They're singing."

"Then we are close."

He crawled faster.

The tunnel ended at a stone wall, smooth and fitted, with a small iron grate set into its base. Lychard pushed the grate, and it swung open on oiled hinges. Beyond it, darkness and the smell of cold stone.

He pulled himself through and stood, his back popping, his legs unsteady. The room was small, lined with shelves that held jars of dark liquid and bundles of dried herbs. The sacristy storage closet. Etta's directions had been exact.

Vesper emerged behind him, brushing dirt from her robes.

"The crypt entrance is through that door, across the sacristy, and down the spiral stairs. If the guards are at their posts, we will see them before they see us."

Lychard pressed his ear to the door. Silence.

He opened it.

The sacristy was empty. Moonlight filtered through a high window, illuminating a stone floor polished by centuries of footsteps. Robes hung on hooks along the wall. Candles flickered before a small altar.

And in the center of the floor, a spiral stair descended into darkness.

Lychard felt the pull like a hand on his chest. The dead were calling.

He took the first step.

Behind him, Vesper drew her knife.

They descended together into the dark.

Chapter 8:

The stairs wound deeper than Lychard had expected. The air grew cold and still, carrying the smell of damp stone and something metallic, like blood dried on iron. His breath misted in front of his face. The lantern flame shrank to a blue bead, as if the dark itself was feeding on the light.

At the bottom, a corridor stretched ahead, its walls lined with niches. In each niche, a skeleton sat cross-legged, arms folded, skulls bowed. They had been arranged with care, their bones polished and clean, their hands positioned as if in prayer.

Vesper touched one of the skulls. "These are the Inquisition's dead. The priests who served before Harrows. They chose to be interred here, close to the old power, even if they did not understand it."

"They sat themselves like this?"

"No." Her voice was quiet. "Someone arranged them after death. Someone who knew the old positions. The old prayers."

Lychard looked at the skeletons. Their bones were warm. He could feel it from where he stood.

"Ember's grave is at the end of the corridor," Vesper said. "There will be a door. The door will be sealed."

They walked. The skeletons watched with empty eyes. Lychard's bones sang a note so low it was almost below hearing, a vibration that made his teeth ache and his vision blur at the edges.

The door at the end was not what he expected.

It was not stone or iron or wood. It was bone. A slab of fused femurs and ribs and vertebrae, polished smooth and fitted together like a puzzle. The surface was white and gleaming, and it was warm to the touch.

"It is made from the bones of Ember's disciples," Vesper said. "They gave themselves to seal her grave. Three hundred years ago, they lay down and let the marrow-worker of that age draw the life from their bodies to forge this door."

Lychard placed his palm flat against it.

The door sang.

The note was pure and clear, a single tone that passed through his hand and into his arm and chest and skull, resonating in every bone he had. He felt the marrow in his own body answer, rising like water in a well.

He understood.

"The key," he said. "The femur you carry. It is not the key to the door."

"No," Vesper said. "You are."

She unwrapped the femur and held it out to him. The glow from within pulsed in time with his heartbeat.

"The door will open only for the marrow of a true Marrow Thief. One who carries the bloodline of those who first sealed the grave. Your mother's mother's mother was among them. The door knows your bones. It has been waiting for you."

Lychard took the femur. The warmth of it spread through his fingers, up his wrist, through his arm. The marrow inside it was alive, and it recognized him.

"Three hundred years," he said.

"The door will open," Vesper said. "Or it will take everything you have trying."

He pressed the femur against the surface of the door. The glow spread from the bone into the door itself, tracing the lines where the femurs and ribs met, illuminating the seams like veins of fire.

The door began to pulse.

And somewhere above them, a bell began to ring.

Chapter 9:

The bell was not the cathedral's. Lychard knew that sound—it was the toll of the Inquisition's alarm, the same bell that had rung for the marrow-worker in the eastern quarter. It was ringing now, and it was close.

"They know," he said.

Vesper's knife was already in her hand. "Keep working. I will hold the stairs."

"The guards—"

"There are more than guards coming." She looked at the door, at the light spreading across its surface like veins through skin. "The Archprelate has felt what you are doing. He is coming himself."

She turned and ran back down the corridor, her footsteps echoing off the walls. Lychard heard her reach the stairs, heard the clash of metal and a cry that was not hers.

He pressed the femur harder against the door.

The light in the bones intensified. The door was warm now, hot, the surface beginning to soften as if the bones themselves were becoming pliable. But it was not fast enough. He could feel the resistance, the weight of three centuries pressing back against him.

He thought of his mother, showing him how to draw marrow from a fresh bone. He thought of his grandmother's skull in the ossuary wall. He thought of the burned field at Thornwell and the blackened post where the bone-worker had sung until the smoke took her voice.

He began to sing.

The old songs came without thought, words he had never been taught but had always known, a language that lived in the marrow of his bones. He sang of the earth and the dead and the long dark between. He sang of the first marrow-worker, Ember, who had learned the secrets from the bones of a fallen star. He sang of the compact and the cost and the love that bound the dead to the living.

The door groaned.

The light blazed white, and the bone surface began to crack.

From behind him, he heard Vesper's voice, sharp and desperate. "Lychard! Now!"

He threw his weight against the door.

It shattered.

Not outward, but inward, the bones falling away like curtains of ash, dissolving into white dust that rose in a cloud and settled on his shoulders like snow.

Beyond the door was a chamber. Small. Round. The walls were packed earth, and in the center, on a raised platform of stone, lay a skeleton.

It was smaller than he expected. The bones were dark, almost black, as if they had been burned. The skull was turned to the side, the jaw slightly open, and the hands were crossed over the chest.

In the space where the heart would have been, something gleamed.

Lychard stepped forward, and the dust of the door swirled around his feet.

"Ember," he whispered.

The bones did not answer. But the marrow inside them did. It called to him, a voice older than language, older than the city above, older than the Inquisition and its fires. It called to him like a mother calling a child home.

He reached out and touched the skull.

Chapter 10:

The world went white.

Lychard was no longer in the crypt. He was standing in a field beneath a sky the color of bruises, and the grass beneath his feet was made of bone-white stalks that crunched when he walked. The air was warm and still, and it smelled of rain on stone.

A woman stood a few yards away. She was young, her hair dark and long, her face unlined. She wore a dress of simple gray wool, and her feet were bare.

"You are early," she said.

"Ember."

She tilted her head. "You know my name. That means Vesper found you."

"She found me."

"Good." Ember walked toward him, and the bone-grass bent away from her feet. "I have been waiting a long time. Longer than I expected. The seal was strong."

"I broke it."

"I felt it." She stopped before him, close enough that he could see the flecks of gold in her dark eyes. "You sang the old songs. My songs. You have the blood."

"I am a Marrow Thief."

"A thief." She smiled, and there was sadness in it. "That is what they call us now. We were not always thieves. We were shepherds. We tended the dead and the dead tended us. But the world forgot, and the shepherds became thieves, and the thieves became hunted."

"The Inquisition—"

"Is afraid." She said it simply. "Harrows is afraid of what I can do, and he is right to be. But he has made a mistake. He has been sitting on my grave for ten years, drinking the fear of the city, growing fat on the power of the dead beneath him. And he has not realized that the dead beneath him are mine."

Lychard looked at his hands. They were the same hands he had always had, but now they seemed different. The lines on his palms glowed faintly, threads of silver light.

"What happens now?"

"Now you wake up." Ember touched his forehead with her fingertip. "And you finish what I started."

The field dissolved. The bone-grass scattered. The sky bled to darkness, and Lychard fell.

He woke on the stone floor of the crypt, his head in Vesper's lap. Her face was bloodied, her arm bound with a torn strip of cloth, but she was alive.

"You were gone for a long time," she said.

"How long?"

"Long enough for the Archprelate to reach the top of the stairs." She helped him sit up. "He is there now, with his guards and his priests and his fire. And he is waiting."

Lychard looked at Ember's skeleton. The bones were still dark, still still, but the glow in the chest cavity had spread. The entire skeleton was lit from within, a soft amber light that pulsed like a heartbeat.

He stood. His body ached, but his bones hummed with a new strength, a new song.

"Can you walk?" Vesper asked.

"I can do more than that."

He knelt beside Ember's skeleton and placed his hands on the rib cage. The marrow sang to him, and he sang back. The bones began to move, not as a body rising, but as pieces coming together, knitting, reforming.

A single rib detached itself and floated into his palm. It was warm, and it hummed with an age of power.

Lychard stood, holding Ember's rib like a sword.

"Let's go meet the Archprelate."

He climbed the stairs, Vesper at his side, the rib blazing in his hand. At the top, the sacristy was filled with black-and-silver figures, their torches casting dancing shadows across the walls.

And at their center, a man in white robes, his face thin and pale, his eyes burning with a hatred that had been fed on generations of fear.

Harrows.

The Archprelate looked at the bone in Lychard's hand, and for the first time, his composure cracked.

"You opened the grave," he said.

"I did."

"Then you have damned us all."

Lychard raised the rib. The light from it bathed the room in gold, and he felt the dead beneath the city stir in answer.

"No," he said. "I have set us free."
