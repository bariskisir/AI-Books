Songs My Mother Sold

A Novel

---

Arc I: The Wound

---

Chapter 1: The Jukebox at St. Sebastian's

The jukebox arrived on a Wednesday, which in Christo Palat's experience meant trouble from the start. Wednesdays were when the fish boats limped back early, when the monsoon gutters swelled fastest, when old women found new reasons to curse the government. Wednesdays brought broken things to his father's repair shop, and broken things always arrived in disproportionate numbers on days that already felt broken.

Christo stood in the doorway of Palat & Son Jukebox Repairs, wiping his hands on a rag that had once been white, watching the auto-rickshaw struggle up the mud track from the main road. The driver had strapped a Wurlitzer 1015 to the roof with coconut-fiber rope, and every pothole sent a shudder through the machine that made Christo wince. The wooden cabinet had been painted over twice—once in church-hall beige, once in someone's idea of gold leaf—but the bubble tubes along the sides still held their colored water, still and dead.

"From St. Sebastian's," the driver said, killing the engine. "Father Isaac's instructions. Fix it or bless it, he said. Either way."

Christo was twenty-seven years old. He had been fixing jukeboxes since he was fourteen, working alongside his father until the stroke took Appachen's left side and most of his words. Now Appachen sat in the back room, in the wicker chair by the window, staring at the Arabian Sea with an expression that could have been peace or could have been the stroke's final cruelty—no one could tell anymore. Christo did the work. Christo took the calls. Christo wrapped the broken machines in tarpaulins when the rain came through the roof.

He had never seen a Wurlitzer 1015 in person. The model was 1946, American, built in the year the world decided to stop killing itself and start dancing instead. The bubble tubes should have held colored oil and water in motion, creating a display that looked like champagne rising. This one's bubbles had settled into a gray sludge at the bottom. The chrome trim had rusted in the coastal salt air. The grille cloth was torn, showing the empty speaker cone beneath, and someone had carved a crucifix into the wood above the record mechanism—not a cross, but a crucifix, with the corpus and everything.

"Father Isaac said it hasn't played in thirty years," the driver added, handing Christo a folded note. "Said you'd understand when you read it."

Christo unfolded the note. The handwriting was trembling and precise, the ink brown with age:

*Christo—*
*This machine arrived in 1947, a gift from the American mission in Thiruvananthapuram. It played for one year. Then it stopped, though no mechanism failed. I have kept it because it holds something that belongs to this church, and to your family. Come when you can. There is a song you need to hear.*
*—Fr. Isaac, St. Sebastian's, Kattakada*

"Your family." The words sat in Christo's chest like a stone.

He helped the driver wrestle the Wurlitzer off the roof and through the workshop door. It weighed more than the two of them combined, a slab of oak and steel and dead circuitry designed for an America that had no business in coastal Kerala. But here it was, in Christo's shop, dripping condensation from its journey through the morning fog, smelling of incense and salt and something else—something Christo could not name but recognized immediately.

His father had smelled this way in the months before the stroke. Like old paper. Like waiting.

"You want me to take it to the back?" the driver asked.

"No," Christo said. "Leave it in the front. I'll start today."

He didn't start that day. He spent the morning staring at it, walking circles around it, touching the dead bubble tubes and the rusted chrome and the carved crucifix. The record magazine was empty. Every slot. No records at all. A jukebox without records was a body without a voice, a shell with nothing to say.

And yet, Father Isaac's note said the machine stopped playing though no mechanism failed. As if it had chosen silence. As if it were waiting for something.

Christo's mother had owned a voice. That's what everyone told him. She had sung in the church choir, at weddings, at the funeral of the old bishop—a voice like monsoon rain on a tin roof, they said, like the sea pulling back before a wave. Christo had no memory of it. She died when he was three, and his father never spoke of her, never played music in the house, never allowed a radio or a cassette player or anything that might produce a song.

When Christo asked, once, at fifteen, his father had turned away from the window—the same window he now stared through without seeing—and said: "Some songs are better left unsung."

That was all. The only words Appachen had ever spoken about his wife.

Now a jukebox from 1947 had arrived with a priest's promise: *There is a song you need to hear.*

Christo waited until the workshop was dark. He waited until the last auto-rickshaw had rattled past, until the fish-sellers had folded their stalls, until the only sound was the sea and the occasional scrape of a boat against the jetty. He waited until Appachen had been helped to bed by the neighbor who came each evening to cook and clean and pretend she did not pity them.

Then he plugged the Wurlitzer in.

The machine hummed. The bubble tubes flickered. The amp tubes glowed orange through the rusted grille. Everything worked—the mechanism, the amplifier, the selection panel with its forty-eight numbered buttons. Everything worked.

But there were no records.

Christo pressed button number one.

The turntable did not move. The tone arm did not rise. There was nothing to play.

But the speakers crackled. And then they sang.

Not a recording. Not a song Christo had ever heard. A voice emerged from the Wurlitzer's rusted grille, a woman's voice, raw and close, as if she stood in the room with her mouth inches from the microphone:

*Little one, little one, the sea is wide—*
*I crossed it once, on the other side—*
*I left my voice in a wooden box—*
*Sold it to a man who winds the clocks—*

The needle skipped. The voice stopped.

But the air in the room had changed. The humidity seemed heavier, charged with something electric and old. Christo stood frozen, his hand still on button number one, his breath shallow.

He pressed button number two.

The same voice returned, singing a different song. This one had no words—only a melody, high and keening, the kind of sound a mother might make to call a child home from a dangerous distance. It rose and fell, carrying something Christo could not name but felt in his chest like a pressure, an almost-bursting.

It was his mother's voice.

He knew this the way you know a language you heard in the womb—not by recognition but by residue, by the shape of the absence it leaves behind.

He pressed button three. Button four. Button five. Each one a different song. Each one sung by the same voice. Each one a memory he had never possessed, a moment from a life he had been too young to remember.

And then he pressed button seven.

The voice changed. This was not his mother. This was a different woman, younger, laughing between verses, her voice carrying something that felt like a warning wrapped in silk:

*Don't fix what's broken, boy—*
*The jukebox knows your name—*
*Every song your mother sold—*
*Is coming back again—*

Christo's hand dropped from the panel.

The Wurlitzer fell silent.

He stood in the dark workshop, the sea muttering beyond the walls, and understood for the first time that his father's refusal to speak of his mother was not grief. It was fear.

Appachen had known. Appachen had always known.

The jukebox did not play records. It played what people had buried. What they had sold. What they had promised never to remember.

And somewhere in coastal Kerala, in a church in Kattakada, an old priest was waiting to tell Christo why.

Chapter 2: The Song That Wasn't on the Record

Christo did not sleep. He sat in the workshop until the gray pre-dawn light seeped through the salt-crusted windows, watching the Wurlitzer as if it might move, might speak again, might offer some explanation for what had happened. The machine remained inert, silent, a dead thing that had briefly come alive.

At six, the neighbor arrived. Her name was Gracy, a widow in her sixties whose husband had died at sea twelve years ago. She came every morning to make Appachen's rice porridge, to change his clothes, to pretend she did not notice the way the old man's left hand curled into a permanent fist.

"You look like you saw a ghost," she said, setting a bag of vegetables on the counter.

"I think I did," Christo said.

Gracy paused. She was a practical woman, the kind who had buried a husband and three children—two to fever, one to the sea—and still found reasons to laugh at the evening soap operas. She looked at Christo with an expression that was not quite pity and not quite understanding.

"Your mother's ghost?"

Christo said nothing.

"Everyone in Kattakada knows," Gracy said quietly. "We knew before you were born. Before your father stopped talking. We knew there was something wrong about the way she left. Not wrong like a scandal. Wrong like a song that ends in the middle."

"What do you know?"

"Nothing she told me. Nothing anyone told me. But there are things you can learn from silence, Christo. Your father's silence said more than most men's words."

She went to the back room without waiting for a response, and Christo heard her begin her morning ritual—the clink of the stove grate, the running water, the soft murmur of the prayer she said before every meal.

He looked at the Wurlitzer.

He pressed button one again.

Nothing.

Button two. Button three. Nothing.

The silence was absolute, the kind of silence that felt like refusal. The jukebox had spoken once and would not speak again until it was ready—or until Christo was ready to hear what it had to say.

But Christo had not imagined the voice. He had not imagined the song. And he had not imagined the woman on button seven, the one who laughed and warned and seemed to know him.

He needed to see Father Isaac.

---

The road to Kattakada wound through hills that were too green to be real, the kind of green that only Kerala knew—coconut palms and rubber trees and jackfruit heavy enough to break a branch. Christo drove his father's old Royal Enfield, the one with the mismatched mirrors and the engine that coughed twice before catching, and the wind tasted of salt and wet earth and something floral he could not identify.

St. Sebastian's sat at the top of a hill, a whitewashed church built in the Portuguese style, its facade crumbling at the edges. The compound was empty except for a woman sweeping the steps with a coconut-frond broom. She was young, maybe thirty, with dark hair pulled back and a way of moving that suggested she was not a parishioner but someone with purpose.

"Father Isaac is expecting you," she said without looking up from her sweeping. "He said you would come today. He said you would look like you hadn't slept."

"Who are you?"

The woman stopped sweeping and looked at him. Her eyes were dark and direct, and they held something that made Christo feel, for a moment, that she was the one who had been waiting for him—not the priest.

"Meera Cherian," she said. "I'm a musician. I came to see the jukebox."

"The jukebox is in my shop."

"I know. Father Isaac told me. He said you would bring it back eventually. He said the jukebox would need to come home to finish what it started, but first you would need to understand what it is."

"And what is it?"

Meera set the broom against the wall. "That's what I'm here to find out."

She led him into the church. The interior was cool and dark, smelling of wax and incense and the particular mustiness of old stone. Father Isaac sat in the front pew, a man so old he seemed to have become part of the wood, his skin the color of aged paper, his eyes the pale blue of a sky that had been washed clean by decades of rain.

"Sit," he said, without turning around. "Both of you."

Christo sat in the pew behind him. Meera sat beside Christo, close enough that he could smell jasmine in her hair.

"The jukebox," Christo said. "It played songs without records. It played my mother's voice."

"Yes."

"How?"

Father Isaac was silent for a long moment. The only sound was the ceiling fan, clicking its slow rotation overhead.

"Your mother came to me in 1947," he said finally. "She was seventeen years old. She had a voice that could make the sea stand still. But she wanted to sell it."

"Sell her voice?"

"Sell her songs. She had written dozens—love songs, lullabies, songs about the monsoon and the fisherman and the girl who waited on the shore. She wanted to record them. She wanted to send them to Bombay, to the film industry. She wanted to be heard beyond this village, beyond these hills."

"What stopped her?"

"Your father. And the jukebox."

Father Isaac turned, slowly, his joints creaking. His eyes found Christo's face and held them.

"The Wurlitzer arrived at St. Sebastian's in early 1947. It was a gift from the American mission, meant to bring joy to the parish. But when they installed it, something strange happened. It played songs that were not in the record magazine. Songs no one had ever heard. Songs that seemed to come from nowhere."

"Songs from people's memories," Meera said quietly.

Father Isaac nodded. "We did not understand it then. We thought it was a miracle, or a trick. The bishop wanted to exorcise it. But I was young then, and curious, and I saw something the others did not. The machine did not create songs. It found them. It found the songs people had hidden in themselves—the songs they had forgotten, or suppressed, or promised never to sing again."

"Like my mother's songs."

"Your mother came to hear the jukebox. Everyone in the district came. But when she stood near it, the machine played her own compositions back to her. Songs she had written but never recorded. Songs no one else knew. And the machine would not stop playing them until she made a decision."

"What decision?"

Father Isaac's voice dropped, barely audible above the ceiling fan. "The machine offered her a bargain. It would keep her songs safe, locked in its memory, unable to be heard by anyone. In exchange, she would give up her voice. She would never sing again."

"Why would she agree to that?"

"Because your father asked her to."

The words hung in the air like smoke.

"She loved him," Father Isaac said. "And he was afraid. Afraid that if she went to Bombay, she would never return. Afraid that her voice would take her to a world he could not follow. So he asked her to stay. And the jukebox gave her a way to do that—to bury her songs so deep that even she could not retrieve them."

"But the jukebox still plays them," Christo said. "I heard them."

"The jukebox plays them when someone needs to hear them. It played them for you because you are her son. Because the bargain is not complete. Your mother sold her songs, but she never took the payment."

"What payment?"

Father Isaac closed his eyes. "That is what you must discover. The jukebox will guide you. But be warned, Christo—every song it plays comes from someone's suppressed memory. And some memories, once unearthed, cannot be buried again."

Chapter 3: The Unplayed Song

Christo returned to the workshop to find Appachen sitting upright in his wicker chair, his good hand gripping the armrest, his eyes fixed on the door as if he had been waiting for this conversation his entire silent life.

"Appachen."

The old man's jaw worked, the muscles of his ruined face straining. He had not spoken a word since the stroke. The doctors said the damage was complete, that the part of his brain responsible for language had been drowned in blood. But Appachen's eyes had never been silent. They watched and judged and remembered, and now they were watching Christo with an intensity that stopped him in the doorway.

"You knew," Christo said. "You knew about the jukebox. About the songs. About what she did."

Appachen's hand tightened on the armrest. He tried to speak, and what came out was a sound—not a word, but a shape, a vowel that might have been the beginning of a name.

"Amma," Christo said. "Mother."

The old man's eyes filled with tears. He nodded, once, with the effort of a man lifting a weight he had carried for decades.

"I heard her voice," Christo said. "From the Wurlitzer. It was her, Appachen. She was singing. The songs she never recorded. The songs she sold."

Appachen made the sound again. *Amma.* And then he raised his good hand and pointed at the workshop's back wall, at the cabinet where he kept the things he could no longer use—the old tools, the broken watches, the photographs he would not look at.

Christo opened the cabinet.

Behind a stack of yellowed service manuals, wrapped in oilcloth and tied with fishing twine, was a notebook. The pages were soft with age, the ink faded to brown, and the handwriting was a woman's—looping, confident, the handwriting of someone who had learned to write in English at a mission school.

*Songs of Anna Palat,* read the first page. *Transcribed at St. Sebastian's, 1947.*

Christo turned the pages slowly. Each one held lyrics and musical notation, songs written in Malayalam and English, love songs and lullabies and songs about the sea. The melodies were written in a simple hand, notes arranged on hand-drawn staffs, and as Christo looked at them, he realized he could hear them—not in his mind, but in the air.

The Wurlitzer had begun to play.

It was soft, barely audible, the kind of sound that might be mistaken for the wind. But it was there—a melody that matched the first page of the notebook, the lullaby Christo had heard the night before:

*Little one, little one, the sea is wide—*

Christo looked at Appachen. The old man had closed his eyes, and tears were running down the left side of his face, the side the stroke had frozen into something that was not quite a smile.

"Can you hear it?" Christo asked.

Appachen opened his eyes. And for the first time in three years, he spoke.

"I never stopped."

The words were slurred, barely intelligible, dragged from a mouth that had forgotten how to shape them. But they were words. Real words.

"I never stopped hearing her," Appachen said. "Every day. Every night. The jukebox plays her songs when I am near it. It has been playing them for thirty years. But I could not tell you. I could not tell anyone. Because if I spoke, the jukebox would take her away completely."

"Take her where?"

"To the song she never sang. The one she wrote for me. The one the jukebox would not let her sing."

Appachen's voice broke. He reached out with his good hand, and Christo took it, feeling the tremor that ran through his father's body like a current.

"On the night before our wedding," Appachen said, "she came to me. She had written a song—a song for me, only for me. She wanted to sing it at the ceremony. But the jukebox had already made its offer. If she sang the song aloud, the bargain would be broken. Every song she had sold would come flooding back, and she would have to leave. She would have to go to Bombay, to the world that wanted her voice."

"And she chose to stay."

"She chose to stay. She chose to keep the bargain. She never sang that song. Not once. She carried it inside her, unplayed, unvoiced, until the day she died."

"Why didn't the jukebox take it? If she sold all her songs—"

"Her songs were her compositions. The bargain covered work she had created. But the song she wrote for me was not a song she meant to sell. It was a gift. And the jukebox cannot take what is given freely."

Appachen's grip tightened.

"I want you to find it, Christo. I want you to find the song she wrote for me. The Wurlitzer knows where it is. But the machine will not play it until you understand what your mother truly sold. It was not her songs. It was not her voice. It was something else. Something I have never understood."

Christo looked at the notebook in his hands. The lyrics of a woman who had given up everything for a love that had not been strong enough to let her fly.

---

The notebook contained forty-seven songs. Christo counted them that night, sitting at the workbench with the Wurlitzer humming softly in the corner, its bubble tubes occasionally flickering as if the machine were dreaming. Each song was numbered in his mother's hand, and each number corresponded to a button on the jukebox's selection panel.

Button one: The lullaby he had heard.
Button two: The wordless melody.
Button three: A love song in Malayalam.
Button four: A song about a fisherman's daughter.
Button five: A protest song about the colonial hangovers.
Button six: A children's rhyme.

Button seven: The song of the woman who had warned him.

That song was not in the notebook. Christo checked twice. The notebook jumped from song six to song eight, with no entry for number seven. In the margin, in his mother's hand, was a note in Malayalam that read: *Not mine. Not for me. Do not play.*

Christo pressed button seven.

The voice of the other woman filled the room, laughing, warning, singing lines that made Christo's skin prickle:

*The jukebox has a heart, boy,*
*And it beats in double time—*
*Every song your mother sold*
*Is someone else's crime.*

Do not play, his mother had written. But Christo had played. And the woman in song seven knew things she should not know.

Chapter 4: Memories in the Grooves

The next morning, Meera Cherian arrived at Palat & Son with a harmonium balanced on her lap and a question in her eyes. She had come to see the jukebox, she said, but also to see Christo, because Father Isaac had told her something before she left.

"What did he tell you?"

"That you would need help understanding what the machine does," she said. "That you would need someone who knows music."

"And you know music."

"I know sound. I know silence. I know the difference between a song that comes from the throat and a song that comes from somewhere deeper."

She set the harmonium on the workbench and walked to the Wurlitzer, circling it the way Christo had the day before. She touched the rusted chrome, the dead bubble tubes, the carved crucifix above the record mechanism.

"Father Isaac told me about the bargain," she said. "About your mother. About the song she never sang."

"He told you everything?"

"He told me enough to know that the jukebox is not the only thing in this room that has been playing suppressed memories."

Christo felt the words land in his chest. "What do you mean?"

"I mean you have been fixing jukeboxes for thirteen years. You have worked on hundreds of machines. And you are telling me the Wurlitzer is the only one that plays songs from people's memories?"

Christo opened his mouth to respond, and then closed it.

She was right.

He had fixed jukeboxes for thirteen years. Rock-Olas and AMIs and Seeburgs, Wurlitzers of various models, Japanese knockoffs from the eighties, a few Soviet machines that had washed up on Kerala's shores through channels no one could trace. He had fixed them and tested them, and every single one had played something—something that was not on the records.

He had thought it was interference. Radio signals, he had told himself. Electrical noise. The sea air.

He had never pressed the buttons after the owners took them home.

"The jukeboxes," he said slowly. "All of them."

"Every jukebox you have ever fixed," Meera said. "Every one of them has been playing the songs people have buried. And you have been sending them out into the world, not knowing what they carry."

Christo sat down heavily on a crate of amplifier tubes.

"People bring me broken machines," he said. "I fix them. I send them home. I never—I never thought to ask what they play."

"Because the jukeboxes are empty when you test them. No records. No sound. But when the owners stand near them, the machines find what is hidden. They play it back. They make it heard."

"How do you know this?"

Meera was silent for a moment. Then she sat down across from him, her hands folded in her lap, her dark eyes holding something that looked like confession.

"Because I am one of them. I brought a jukebox to your father, years ago, when I was eighteen. A Seeburg V. It was broken—the amplifier was fried, the tone arm was bent. Your father fixed it. I took it home. And when I stood near it, it played a song I had never heard, sung in a language I did not know. But I understood every word."

"What did it say?"

"It said I was carrying a memory that did not belong to me. A memory of a woman who drowned herself in the river behind my grandmother's house. I had never met this woman. I had never heard her story. But the song told me everything—how she died, why she died, what she wanted me to do with the knowledge."

"What did she want you to do?"

"To tell her daughter the truth. That the drowning was not an accident. That she had been pushed."

The words hung between them, heavy and cold.

"Did you tell her?"

"I did. The daughter was my grandmother. She had believed her mother died by accident her entire life. She had carried the guilt of that belief—the suspicion that she could have prevented it, that she should have been there. When I told her the truth, she wept for three days. And then she died. As if the truth had been the only thing keeping her alive."

Meera looked at Christo with an expression that was equal parts defiance and sorrow.

"That is what the jukeboxes do. They find the songs people have buried. And they play them until someone hears. Until someone acts. Until the memory is no longer suppressed."

Chapter 5: What the Jukebox Knew

Christo spent the next three days traveling. He went to every customer he had served in the past five years, every home and shop and community hall that held a jukebox he had repaired. He asked the same question each time: "Has the machine played anything unusual? Anything you did not expect?"

The answers were always the same: "No. It plays fine. Just the records."

But Christo saw it in their eyes. The flicker. The hesitation. The way they glanced at the jukebox as if it were a witness to something they had not confessed.

They knew. They all knew. But they would not tell him.

On the third evening, he stopped at the house of a fisherman named Shaju, whose Rock-Ola had been sitting silent for two years before Christo had rebuilt the amplifier and replaced the tonearm mechanism. Shaju's wife, Lissy, opened the door. She was a thin woman with nervous hands and the kind of smile that seemed to be apologizing in advance.

"The jukebox," Christo said. "Does it play anything strange?"

Lissy's smile flickered. "Strange?"

"Songs that are not on the records. Voices you do not recognize."

For a long moment, Lissy said nothing. Then she stepped aside and let him in.

The Rock-Ola sat in the corner of the sitting room, its chrome gleaming, its selection panel glowing softly in the evening dimness. It was playing when Christo entered—a woman's voice, low and mournful, singing a song about a child who had been taken by the sea.

"That's not on the records," Christo said.

"No," Lissy said. "It's been playing that song for a month. Every time I walk past, it starts again."

"Whose voice is it?"

Lissy sat down heavily on a plastic chair. "Mine."

Christo stared at her.

"I sang that song once," she said. "When I was sixteen. I wrote it for a boy who drowned. I never told anyone about it. Not even my husband. I buried it so deep I forgot it myself. But the jukebox found it."

"And it plays it back to you?"

"Every time. Until I remember. Until I feel the grief I never let myself feel."

"Have you?"

"Felt it?" Lissy's eyes filled with tears. "Yes. Every night. Every time the song plays. I sit here and I cry for a boy I haven't thought of in thirty years. And I think—I think that is what the machine wants. Not to punish me. To make me remember. To make me finish the feeling."

Christo stood in the fisherman's house, watching the jukebox glow, listening to a song a sixteen-year-old girl had written for a dead boy, and understood for the first time what his mother had really sold.

She had not sold her songs. She had sold her grief.

Every song she had written was a container for a feeling she could not keep. And the jukebox had taken them, locked them away, kept them safe until someone needed to hear them.

But songs seven had not been hers. The woman in song seven had her own songs, her own grief, her own story.

And she was trying to tell Christo something.

Chapter 6: Meera

Meera Cherian had her own history with jukeboxes. She told Christo the full story on the fourth night, sitting on the seawall below his workshop, watching the fishing boats bob in the darkness.

"The Seeburg V," she said. "The one your father fixed. It belonged to my grandmother. She bought it in 1951, when she was a young bride, because her husband had seen one in Bombay and said it was the most beautiful thing he had ever seen."

"Your grandfather?"

"He died before I was born. The jukebox was all she had of him. She kept it in the front room, polished the chrome every Sunday, played the same records on a loop. But the records wore out. The needle scratched. Eventually, the machine stopped working altogether."

"And you brought it to my father."

"I was eighteen. I had just finished my music degree. I thought I was going to be a concert pianist—I had the technique, the training, the ambition. But when the jukebox started playing songs that were not on the records, I realized I had been training for the wrong thing."

"What do you mean?"

"The jukebox played my grandmother's memories. Songs she had never sung, never written, never told anyone about. Songs about a man she had loved before my grandfather. A man she had left because her family forbade the match. She had buried those songs for sixty years, and the jukebox found every single one."

Meera turned to face him. The moonlight caught her face, making her look older, wiser, more tired than her years.

"I listened to those songs for a month. And then I asked my grandmother about them. She wept. She told me everything. And when she finished, she said something I have never forgotten."

"What did she say?"

"She said: 'The jukebox does not play songs. It plays the space where a song used to be. It plays the absence.'"

---

The next morning, Christo woke to find the Wurlitzer playing. Not a song—a single note, sustained, unwavering. A note that seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere, vibrating through the floorboards and the walls and the bones of his skull.

He followed the sound to the workshop. The Wurlitzer's bubble tubes were glowing, pulsing with light that moved like blood through veins. The machine was playing a single note, one that had no source, no beginning, no end.

And standing in front of it, with his hand on the selection panel, was Appachen.

"I heard it," the old man said. His voice was weaker than the night before, as if the effort of speech had cost him something irreplaceable. "Last night. While you were out. The machine played a song I have not heard since I was a boy."

"What song?"

"A song my mother used to sing. Your grandmother. She died when I was seven, and I forgot her voice completely. I did not even remember that I had forgotten. But the jukebox found it. It played her voice, Christo. My mother's voice."

Appachen swayed, and Christo caught him, guided him to the wicker chair.

"The machine is waking up," Appachen said. "All the years I kept it silent, all the years I refused to let it play—the machine was waiting. Waiting for you. Waiting for someone who would listen."

"I'm listening."

"Then listen to this." Appachen reached into his shirt and pulled out a chain. On the end was a key, small and brass, the kind that might open a lockbox or a diary or a door that had been closed for a very long time.

"Your mother gave me this the night before she died. She said it would open the thing she valued most. I never used it. I was afraid of what I would find."

"Where is the lock?"

"In the jukebox. Inside the cabinet, behind the record mechanism, there is a panel. I have never opened it. I never had the courage."

Christo took the key. It was warm from his father's skin, and it seemed to hum with a vibration that matched the note still sounding from the Wurlitzer.

He walked to the jukebox. He reached behind the record mechanism, his fingers finding the edges of a panel he had never noticed, one that seemed to be part of the machine's original design. He pressed, and the panel clicked open, revealing a lock that looked as old as the jukebox itself.

He inserted the key. He turned.

The sustained note stopped. The bubble tubes went dark. The silence that followed was absolute, the kind of silence that came before something began.

And then the Wurlitzer played a song Christo had never heard, sung by a voice he had never known.

It was his mother's voice. But she was not singing one of her own songs.

She was singing a children's lullaby, the kind every mother in Kerala knew, the kind that had been passed down for generations without a composer's name attached. It was simple. It was familiar. It was devastating.

And as it played, Christo understood.

His mother had not sold her songs to the jukebox. She had traded them. She had given the jukebox her voice, her compositions, her grief, in exchange for the ability to hear the songs she had lost—the lullabies her own mother had sung to her, the melodies of her childhood, the sounds of a time before she had learned to be afraid.

The jukebox did not take. It gave back.

---

Chapter 7: The Sound of Absence

Meera moved into the workshop that week. There was no ceremony about it—she arrived with a bag of clothes and her harmonium, and Christo cleared a space on the workbench. They worked in silence, side by side, fixing the jukeboxes that arrived each day, testing them, listening to the songs they played.

Every machine held someone's buried memory.

A Rock-Ola from a tea shop in Neyyattinkara played a fisherman's song about a brother lost at sea—a brother the owner had never mentioned, never mourned, never acknowledged existed. When Christo asked the owner about it, the man broke down and wept for the first time in forty years.

An AMI from a bar in Kovalam played a woman's whispered confession—a love affair she had carried on in secret for a decade, a truth she had told no one. She came to collect the machine and heard the song playing, and she turned around and walked back to her husband and finally spoke the words she had buried.

A Seeburg from a church hall in Trivandrum played a choir that had never existed, voices singing in harmonies that should have been impossible, a song that seemed to be made of light and shadow and the sound of rain.

Every machine, every song, every memory unearthed.

And through it all, the Wurlitzer sat silent, waiting. It had played its note. It had given Christo the key. It had shown him the lullaby. But the song it held—the song his mother had written for his father, the one she had never sung—remained hidden.

"Because you're not ready," Meera said one evening. "The machine will not play it until you understand what your mother truly gave up."

"I understand. She gave up her voice. Her career. Her dreams."

"No. She gave up the freedom to regret. She made a choice, and she locked all the alternatives away so she would never have to wonder what might have been. The jukebox holds her what-ifs. And you need to face them before you can unlock the song."

"Face them how?"

Meera pointed to the Wurlitzer. "Press button seven."

"I pressed it before. It played the other woman. The one who warned me."

"Press it again."

Christo walked to the machine. He pressed button seven.

The woman's voice filled the room, but this time the song was different. Not a warning. A story.

*I was the girl who sold her grief,*
*The one who locked her songs away—*
*I stand beside you, shadow-thief,*
*And ask you now: what will you pay?*

The voice stopped. The machine fell silent.

And the workshop door opened.

A woman stood in the doorway. She was young, no older than Christo, with dark hair and dark eyes and a face that seemed to shimmer at the edges, as if she were not quite solid. She wore a white saree with a gold border, the kind a bride might wear, and her feet were bare and wet, as if she had walked through water to get here.

"Hello, Christo," she said. "I have been waiting for you to press that button."

The woman in song seven.

---

Chapter 8: Three Jukeboxes

The woman stepped into the workshop, leaving wet footprints on the concrete floor. She did not look at Meera. She did not look at Appachen, who had risen from his chair with a sound like an animal in pain. She looked only at Christo.

"You know who I am," she said.

"You are the woman in song seven."

"I am the woman your mother traded with. I am the keeper of the bargain. I am the one who holds what she gave up."

Meera stepped forward. "That's not possible. The jukebox is a machine. It cannot hold a soul."

The woman smiled. "The jukebox is not a machine. It is a cage. And I am the bird who was trapped inside."

She turned to Christo. "Your mother came to me in 1947, carrying a notebook full of songs and a heart full of fear. She wanted to bury her voice so she would never have to choose between love and music. I offered her a way. I offered to hold what she could not carry. But the bargain required a keeper—someone who would watch over the songs, who would ensure they were never forgotten. I was that keeper. I became part of the machine."

"Who were you before?"

"Before the jukebox, I was a singer. I came to Kerala in 1946 with a traveling theater company. I had a voice like your mother's—the kind that could stop a room, could make men weep, could change the weather. But I was careless with it. I sang songs that should not have been sung. I revealed secrets that should have remained buried. And the jukebox—the first jukebox, the one that came before the Wurlitzer—it found me and made me an offer. I could keep my voice, but I would be bound to the machine, forced to guard the songs it collected."

"How did you escape?"

"I did not escape. You let me out when you pressed button seven. You acknowledged my existence. You invited me into the world. And now I am here, and I cannot go back, and the songs your mother sold are no longer safe."

The woman—the keeper—looked around the workshop with something like wonder and something like grief.

"There are three jukeboxes, Christo. Three machines that are not machines. The Wurlitzer at St. Sebastian's is the first. The second is in your father's workshop, the one he has been hiding for thirty years. And the third—the third is the one you must find before the woman who built them finds it first."

"Who built them?"

The keeper's smile faded. "A man named Elias Varghese. A clockmaker. A collector of songs. He built the first jukebox in 1932, in a village north of here, and he has been building them ever since. He has been collecting voices. He has been trapping memories. He has been preparing for something—something that requires the songs your mother sold."

"And the third jukebox?"

"Elias built it to be the final cage. The one that will hold every song, every memory, every voice he has collected. Once it is activated, no one will ever be able to retrieve what has been lost. The songs will be sealed forever, and the people who sold them will never remember what they gave up."

The keeper stepped closer to Christo, close enough that he could see the water still dripping from her saree, could smell the salt of a sea that had touched her.

"Your mother knew this. She sold her songs to keep them from Elias. She trusted me to guard them until someone could stop him. And that someone is you."

"Why me?"

"Because you are the only one who can hear the songs without being trapped by them. Because you are her son. And because you have already pressed button seven."

---

Chapter 9: The Silence Between Songs

The keeper would not stay. She faded at dawn, returning to the Wurlitzer, to the cage she could not escape for long. But before she left, she gave Christo a name and a location: Elias Varghese, clockmaker, last seen in a village called Kuttanadu, in the backwaters south of Kochi.

"We will need a boat," Meera said.

"We will need more than a boat."

Christo looked at Appachen. The old man had not spoken since the keeper's appearance, but his eyes were alive with something Christo had not seen in years—purpose.

"I knew about Elias," Appachen said. "I knew about the three jukeboxes. Your mother told me everything before she died. She told me to keep you away from it, to never let you fix the Wurlitzer, to never let you press the seventh button."

"But you let me."

"I let you because I was a coward. I let you because I hoped you would find the answers I could not. I let you because the stroke took my words but not my guilt, and I have been carrying that guilt for thirty years."

Appachen reached into his shirt and pulled out a second key, identical to the first.

"This opens the second jukebox. The one I have been hiding. I kept it locked because I was afraid of what the keeper would tell you. But she has told you already, and now you know the truth."

"Where is it?"

"In the church. St. Sebastian's. In the basement, behind the altar where the old vestments are stored. Father Isaac has been guarding it for me."

Appachen pressed the key into Christo's palm.

"Go. Find the third jukebox. Stop Elias. And when you have finished, come back and play the song your mother wrote for me. I want to hear it before I die."

---

Chapter 10: A Mother's Voice

Christo and Meera left at dawn, taking the Royal Enfield as far as the road went, then hiring a boat to carry them deeper into the backwaters. The landscape changed as they traveled—the coastal salt flats giving way to emerald-green paddy fields, the coconut palms replaced by water hyacinths and mangroves, the air thickening with humidity and the smell of standing water.

Kuttanadu was a village built on water, a maze of canals and narrow strips of land, where houses sat on stilts and boats were the only transportation. The people here spoke a different dialect, moved at a different pace, lived lives that were measured by the rhythm of the water rather than the clock.

Elias Varghese's workshop was at the end of a canal so narrow the boat could barely pass. It was a two-story structure, wood and corrugated iron, with a porch that extended over the water. Jukebox parts littered the dock—tone arms, speakers, transformers, the guts of machines that had been torn apart and never reassembled.

"He's been expecting you."

The voice came from the porch. An old man sat in a rocking chair, his hands folded over a walking stick, his eyes the color of the backwater after a storm—dark, deep, unreadable. He was not what Christo had expected. He looked frail, ordinary, like a grandfather who spent his days watching the boats pass.

"You are Elias Varghese?"

"I am. And you are Anna's son. I have been waiting for you for a long time."

Elias rose slowly, using the walking stick to steady himself. He walked to the edge of the porch and looked down at Christo in the boat.

"You mother had the most beautiful voice I have ever heard," he said. "I offered her everything—fame, fortune, a life of music. She chose your father instead. She chose silence. I have never understood why."

"Because love was more important than her voice."

"Was it?" Elias's eyes narrowed. "She sold her songs to a jukebox. She trapped herself in a bargain that cost her everything she had created. And for what? For a man who could not even speak her name after she died?"

"Don't."

"Your father never told you about her because he could not bear the guilt. He knew what he had cost her. He knew she could have been one of the greatest singers India had ever produced. And he kept her small, kept her quiet, kept her in a village where no one would hear her."

"You do not know anything about my parents."

"I know everything about your mother. I know every song she wrote. I have spent thirty years trying to retrieve them from the Wurlitzer. And I would have succeeded, if not for the keeper."

"The keeper is free now. I pressed button seven."

Elias's face changed. The calm cracked, revealing something underneath—fear, rage, something Christo could not name.

"You did what?"

"I pressed button seven. The keeper is free. The songs are no longer safe."

Elias gripped his walking stick so hard his knuckles went white. "You have destroyed everything. Everything I have worked for. The third jukebox—the final cage—it is useless without the keeper. She was supposed to be the key. She was supposed to seal the bargain forever. And you have let her out."

"I have let her out so I can find my mother's song."

"Your mother's song." Elias laughed, a dry, hollow sound. "Your mother's song is not in the Wurlitzer. It was never in the Wurlitzer. Your mother's song is in you."

Christo stared at him.

"She did not write that song," Elias said. "She lived it. Every moment of her life with your father, every sacrifice, every silence—that was the song. She did not need to sing it because she was already living it. And the only way to hear it is to understand why she made the choices she made."

Elias stepped back from the edge of the porch.

"Go home, Christo. Go home and ask your father the questions you have never asked. And when you have the answers, come back. I will show you the third jukebox. I will show you what your mother's song truly is."

He turned and walked into the workshop, leaving Christo and Meera floating in the narrow canal, the water lapping against the boat, the silence of the backwater pressing in around them.

End of Arc I

Arc II: The Keeper

---

Chapter 11: The Weight of Water

The journey back from Kuttanadu took two days. The monsoon had arrived in earnest, turning the backwaters into a single gray sheet, the rain falling so heavily that Christo could not see three feet beyond the boat's bow. Meera sat across from him, her harmonium wrapped in oilcloth, her face a mask of concentration as she navigated through channels that had become rivers.

Christo had not spoken since leaving Elias's workshop. The words the old clockmaker had spoken circled in his mind like fish in a tank: *Your mother's song is in you.*

He did not understand. He had never understood. And now, with the keeper freed and the third jukebox still hidden, he felt the weight of his ignorance pressing down on him like the rain, like the water, like the distance between himself and the truth.

"Stop," he said suddenly.

Meera looked up. "What?"

"Stop the boat."

She guided the boat to the bank, where the roots of a banyan tree formed a natural dock. Christo climbed out, stood in the shallows, let the rain soak him completely.

"I don't know what I'm doing," he said. "I don't know what I'm supposed to find. Elias said the song is in me, but I don't feel it. I don't hear it. I don't know what he meant."

Meera stepped out of the boat, pulling her saree up to keep it from the water. She stood beside him in the rain, close enough that he could feel her presence without touching her.

"Maybe he meant that you are the song," she said. "Not your mother's song, but the song of her choice. The song of what she gave up. You are the living proof of her decision. Every time someone looks at you, they see what she chose."

"That's not a song. That's a consequence."

"It's the same thing. A song is a consequence of something felt. Your mother felt something so strongly that she gave up everything for it. That feeling became a song. And that song became you."

Christo turned to look at her. The rain had plastered her hair to her scalp, and water ran down her face like tears, but her eyes were steady.

"What if I don't want to be a song?"

"Too late. You have been a song since the moment you were conceived. The only question is whether you will let yourself be heard."

---

They reached Kattakada late that night. St. Sebastian's was dark, but a single light burned in the rectory window. Father Isaac was waiting for them, a pot of tea on the table, two cups already poured.

"I knew you would be back," he said. "I knew Elias would not give you the answers you needed."

"He told me the song is in me. That my mother lived it, not wrote it."

Father Isaac nodded slowly. "He is not wrong. But he is not entirely right either."

"What does that mean?"

"Sit. Drink your tea. And let me tell you the story of Anna Palat, as I knew her."

Christo sat. Meera sat beside him. Father Isaac picked up his own cup, holding it in both hands as if warming himself against a cold that had nothing to do with the weather.

"Your mother came to me in 1947," Father Isaac said. "She was seventeen, as I told you. But what I did not tell you was that she was already pregnant."

The words hit Christo like a wave.

"She was carrying your brother. A child she conceived before marriage, with a man who was not your father."

"I have a brother?"

"You had a brother. He was stillborn at seven months. Your mother nearly died herself. And when she recovered, she was different. The light in her voice had dimmed. The songs she wrote became darker, sadder, filled with grief she could not express any other way."

"Why did no one tell me?"

"Because your father asked us not to. He did not want you to know that you were not his first child. He did not want you to carry the weight of a brother you never met."

"Does Appachen know about the brother?"

"Your father knows everything. He was the one who stayed by her side through the stillbirth. He was the one who held her while she wept. He was the one who asked her to stay, to marry him, to build a life out of the wreckage. And when the jukebox offered to take her songs—to take the grief she could not carry—he did not stop her. He understood that she needed to put the weight somewhere."

Father Isaac set down his cup.

"Your mother's song—the one she wrote for your father—was not a love song in the way you imagine. It was a song of gratitude. A song of thanks for a man who loved her enough to let her bury her grief. And she never sang it because she was afraid that if she did, the grief would come back."

---

Chapter 12: The Second Jukebox

The basement of St. Sebastian's was older than the church itself, a stone chamber that had been part of an earlier structure built by Portuguese missionaries in the eighteenth century. The air was cold and damp, and the only light came from a single bare bulb that swung gently in some draft Christo could not feel.

The second jukebox sat in the center of the room, covered in a white cloth that had yellowed with age. It was smaller than the Wurlitzer, more modern—a Seeburg Model E, from the early 1950s—but Christo could feel the same energy emanating from it, the same hum that the Wurlitzer had produced.

Father Isaac pulled the cloth away.

The machine was pristine. The chrome gleamed. The selection panel was clean, the buttons numbered one through fifty. But the most striking feature was the glass panel at the top, which showed not records but a series of small brass cylinders, each one engraved with a name.

"Your father kept this in his workshop for thirty years," Father Isaac said. "He brought it here when he realized the Wurlitzer was not the only machine that could play. He was afraid that if the two machines were in the same place, they would amplify each other—that the songs would become too loud to bear."

"Who is inside this one?"

"No one. This machine was built by Elias in 1953, after he had perfected the technology. It was meant to be the successor to the Wurlitzer. But Elias made a mistake. He built this one to collect songs without a keeper, and the songs it collected did not stay. They passed through, played once, and vanished."

"What happened to the songs it collected?"

"Some went to the Wurlitzer. Some went elsewhere. And some—the ones that were too dangerous, too painful, too heavy—went to the third jukebox, the one Elias is still guarding."

Christo circled the machine. It was humming now, a low vibration that he could feel in his teeth.

"Can it play? Even without a keeper?"

"It can play. But it will not play songs from the past. It will play songs from the future."

"What?"

"The Seeburg does not retrieve memories. It retrieves possibilities. Songs that have not been written yet, sung by voices that have not yet existed. That is why Elias built it—to hear what was coming, to prepare for the moment when all the songs would converge."

"Converge for what?"

"For the final song," Father Isaac said. "The one that will end the bargain forever. Elias believes that if he can collect enough voices, he can create a song so powerful that it will rewrite the past. He will undo the bargain. He will free every voice that has been trapped. And he will be the one who owns them all."

---

Chapter 13: The Future Song

Christo sat in the basement for an hour, staring at the Seeburg, listening to the hum. Meera sat beside him, her harmonium across her knees, her fingers moving absently over the keys without pressing them.

"Play it," she said finally.

"Play what?"

"The Seeburg. Press a button. See what it shows you."

"I do not want to hear songs that have not been written yet."

"Neither do I. But we need to understand what Elias is planning."

Christo pressed button one.

The Seeburg's speakers crackled. A voice emerged—not a woman's voice, not a man's, but something in between, something that sounded like a child who had grown up too fast:

*The clockmaker winds his clocks,*
*But the clockmaker never hears—*
*The song he stole from yesterday*
*Will cost him fifty years.*

The speakers fell silent.

"That was a warning," Meera said.

"It was a riddle. 'Will cost him fifty years.' Fifty years from when?"

"From the future. From the song's future. The song was written about an event that has not happened yet."

"Or it was written about an event that happened fifty years after Elias started collecting."

Meera looked at him. "That is impossible. Elias built the Wurlitzer in 1932. The first machine played songs from the past. The Seeburg is supposed to play songs from the future."

"Unless the future is not the only direction time moves in. Unless the songs are not bound by chronology."

"You sound like a philosopher."

"I sound like a man who has spent the last week learning that jukeboxes can hold human souls. At this point, I will believe anything."

Meera pressed button two.

This time, the voice was different—a woman's, clear and strong, singing in Malayalam:

*When the rains come, the river rises—*
*When the river rises, the boats leave—*
*When the boats leave, the women weep—*
*When the women weep, the children dance—*

The song continued, a chain of cause and effect that seemed to have no end, each line leading to the next, each consequence spawning another. It was hypnotic, beautiful, and utterly meaningless.

But Christo noticed something. The song had a pattern. The pattern repeated every twelve lines, with a variation that changed the outcome. It was not a song at all. It was a formula.

"This isn't a song from the future," he said. "It's a prediction. A prophecy. A calculation."

"Of what?"

"Of what happens when the third jukebox is activated. The Seeburg is showing us what will happen if Elias succeeds."

---

Chapter 14: The Coast of Ghosts

They returned to the workshop to find Gracy standing in the doorway, her face pale. Behind her, the Wurlitzer was playing—not a song, but a noise, a static-filled roar that sounded like the sea during a storm.

"Your father asked me to fetch you," Gracy said. "The machine started an hour ago. It has not stopped."

Christo pushed past her into the workshop. Appachen was in his chair, his hands covering his ears, his eyes squeezed shut. The Wurlitzer was vibrating so violently that the bubble tubes were shaking, and the noise that came from the speakers was deafening—a wall of sound that seemed to contain every song Christo had ever heard, layered on top of each other, competing for space.

"Make it stop," Appachen said. "Please. Make it stop."

Christo ran to the machine. He pressed every button. He pulled the plug. He slammed his palm against the side of the cabinet. Nothing. The noise continued, growing louder, until Christo could feel it in his bones, in his teeth, in the spaces between his thoughts.

And then it stopped.

The silence was absolute.

Meera was standing in front of the Seeburg, her hand on button twelve. She had brought the second jukebox from the church, and the machine was humming, its brass cylinders glowing with a light that seemed to come from inside the metal.

"The Seeburg stopped it," she said. "They are connected. The two machines are linked."

"How did you know?"

"I didn't. I pressed a button, and the noise stopped. The Seeburg played a song that cancelled the Wurlitzer's song."

Christo looked at the Wurlitzer. The bubble tubes were dark. The machine was dead.

"It is not dead," the keeper's voice said. "It is waiting."

She was standing in the corner of the workshop, more solid than before, her white saree dry, her feet no longer wet. She looked at Christo with an expression that was almost affectionate.

"You have awakened the second jukebox," she said. "Now Elias knows you are close. He will move faster."

"Move faster toward what?"

"Toward the third jukebox. Toward the final song. He has been waiting for someone to activate the Seeburg. Now that you have, he can complete the circuit. The three machines are connected. When he activates the third, they will all play at once."

"And then?"

"And then every song that has ever been trapped in those machines will be released. Not into the air, not into the world. Into the person who activated them."

The keeper looked at Christo directly.

"Into you."

---

Chapter 15: The Circuit

Christo did not sleep that night. He sat in the workshop, the two jukeboxes facing each other like opponents in a boxing ring, and he tried to understand what the keeper had told him.

The three machines were connected. The Wurlitzer held the past. The Seeburg held the future. The third jukebox—Elias's final machine—held the present, the moment of convergence. When all three played together, the songs would pour into the person who had activated the last machine.

And Christo had activated two of them.

He had pressed button one on the Wurlitzer. He had pressed button one on the Seeburg. Elias would make sure he was the one who pressed the first button on the third machine, because Elias needed a vessel—a living person to hold every song that had ever been trapped.

"You cannot let him win," Meera said, sitting beside him. "You cannot touch the third jukebox."

"Then who will?"

"Someone who has not activated the first two. Someone who is not connected to the circuit."

"Who?"

"Me."

Christo shook his head. "No. I will not let you become a vessel for every trapped song."

"It is not your choice to make. The songs need to be freed. If Elias also activates the third jukebox, he will own them forever. But if I activate the third jukebox, the songs will still be released into a living person—and that person can choose to let them go."

"How?"

"I do not know. But the keeper said the songs can be released. She said they can be freed. If I hold them, I can release them."

"And if you cannot? If you are trapped forever, like the keeper was?"

"Then at least I will have tried."

---

Chapter 16: The Woman in Song Seven

The keeper appeared again at midnight, when the rain had stopped and the clouds had parted to reveal a moon so bright it cast shadows. She stood on the seawall, looking out at the sea, and she did not turn when Christo approached.

"I was a singer," she said. "Before the jukebox took me, I was a singer in a traveling theater. I performed in every village between here and Madras. I sang at weddings, at funerals, at festivals, at the bedsides of the dying. My voice was my gift, and I gave it freely."

"Until Elias built the jukebox."

"Until Elias heard me sing. He came to a performance in 1933, in a village north of here. I sang a song about a woman who had been drowned by her husband. It was an old folk song, one everyone knew. But when I sang it, something happened. The woman's ghost appeared."

"Ghost?"

"Not a ghost in the way you think. A memory. The song was so powerful that it brought the dead woman's story into the room. Everyone who heard the song saw what had happened to her. They felt her fear. They felt her drowning. They lived her death."

The keeper turned to face him.

"Elias was in the audience. He saw what the song did. And he knew that my voice was the key to his machines. Not because it was powerful, but because it was open. I could channel the memories of others. I could sing their unspoken songs."

"He trapped you in the Wurlitzer."

"He offered me a choice. I could continue singing and slowly lose myself to the voices I channeled. Or I could enter the machine, become the keeper, and preserve the songs without being consumed by them. I chose the machine."

"And my mother? Did she choose the machine too?"

"Your mother chose your father. She sold her songs to the jukebox because Elias told her that if she kept them, they would kill her. The grief she carried from her stillborn child was too heavy. The songs were the only way she could let it go."

"But the songs did not contain the grief. The songs were the grief."

"Yes. And the jukebox took them. But the jukebox also kept them alive. Your mother's grief is still in the Wurlitzer. It has been waiting for you to claim it."

---

Chapter 17: The Unfinished Melody

Christo returned to the workshop and opened his mother's notebook. He turned to the last page—the space between song six and song eight, where his mother had written *Not mine. Not for me. Do not play.*

The keeper had said the songs were the grief. But song seven was not his mother's. It was the keeper's. The keeper had used song seven as a doorway, a way to reach out to whoever pressed the button.

"Why song seven?" Christo asked.

The keeper appeared beside him, her reflection visible in the workshop window. "Because seven is the number of completion. Seven days of creation. Seven notes in a scale. Seven songs that will end the world."

"Seven songs?"

"Your mother wrote forty-seven songs. The Seeburg holds fifty brass cylinders. The third jukebox will hold one hundred and forty-four buttons, one for each thousand of the saved. The numbers are not random. Elias built his machines according to a pattern, a pattern he found in an old book—a book of songs that were written before the world began."

"Where is this book?"

"In the Sea of Certainty. That is what Elias calls it. A place beneath the sea where the songs of creation are stored. The third jukebox is not a machine. It is a door. And Elias will open it when the final song is played."

The keeper's reflection flickered.

"You have to stop him, Christo. Not because the songs should stay hidden, but because they should be sung. Every song in every jukebox was meant to be heard. Elias wants to seal them away forever. But you can set them free."

"How?"

"By singing the song your mother never sang."

---

Chapter 18: What the Sea Holds

The next morning, Christo found Appachen standing at the edge of the water, his bare feet in the surf, his walking stick planted in the sand. The old man had not left his chair in weeks, but here he was, facing the sea, his back straight, his eyes clear.

"Appachen."

"I heard her last night. The keeper. She spoke to me in my dreams."

"She told you about the Sea of Certainty?"

"She told me about the book. The book of songs. I have seen it, Christo. I saw it once, when I was a young man, before I met your mother."

"Where?"

"In a church. Not St. Sebastian's. A smaller church, in a village that no longer exists. It was washed away in the floods of 1924. The book was kept in a stone chest, and the chest was sealed with pitch and iron. I saw it when I was twelve years old, when my father took me to see the old priest who was dying. The priest showed me the chest. He said it contained the songs that were sung when the world was made."

"And you never told anyone?"

"I was twelve. I forgot. I forgot until the keeper reminded me."

Appachen turned to face his son.

"The chest is still there. It is buried beneath the water, in the ruins of the old church. Elias has been looking for it his entire life. He needs the songs in that chest to complete his final machine."

"Where is the church?"

"At the bottom of the Vembanad Lake. Near the island of Pathiramanal. The church was built on a sandbar that was washed away in the floods. The chest is still there, chained to the ruins."

Appachen reached into his pocket and pulled out a third key, identical to the first two.

"Your mother gave me this the night she died. She said it would open the chest. She said I would know when to use it."

---

Chapter 19: The Drowned Church

Christo and Meera took a boat to Pathiramanal, the island of midnight sand, named for a legend about a moon that fell from the sky and turned to gravel. The lake was calm that morning, the water flat and green, reflecting a sky that could not decide whether to rain.

The old church was visible from a kilometer away—a ghostly white shape beneath the water, its spire still pointing upward, its walls covered in algae and silt. The floods of 1924 had swallowed it whole, and it had never been excavated.

"You are going to dive for it?" Meera asked.

"I am going to find the chest."

"The lake is full of crocodiles."

"Crocodiles do not eat jukebox repairmen."

"They eat everything."

But Christo was already stripping off his shirt, tying a rope around his waist. He had tied the third key around his neck on a length of fishing line, and he could feel it bouncing against his chest like a second heartbeat.

"I will be back in ten minutes. If I am not back, pull the rope."

He dove.

The water was warm and murky, filled with suspended silt that turned the sunlight into golden shafts. Christo swam downward, following the spire, his lungs beginning to burn before he reached the roof. He found a hole in the stonework—a gap where the roof had collapsed—and squeezed through, entering the drowned nave.

The church was intact in a way that felt deliberate. The pews were still in rows, their wood preserved by the water, their surfaces covered in a fine layer of silt. The altar stood at the far end, and on the altar was a stone chest, chained to the structure with iron that had rusted into something that looked like coral.

Christo swam to the altar. He pulled the key from around his neck. He inserted it into the lock on the chest, and it turned with a resistance that felt like the pressure of years.

The lid opened.

Inside the chest, sealed in a series of brass tubes, were scrolls of palm leaf, each one covered in writing that Christo could not read. The writing was old—older than Malayalam, older than Sanskrit, older than any language he knew.

And as he touched the first scroll, he heard a song.

Not in his ears. In his chest. A song that was older than the sea, older than the land, older than the sky. A song that vibrated through his bones and spoke to something in him that had been waiting to hear it.

He grabbed the scrolls, tucked them into the bag he had tied to his waist, and kicked upward. He broke the surface gasping, and Meera pulled him into the boat.

"You found it," she said.

"I found something."

Christo opened the bag and looked at the scrolls. The writing was still unreadable. But the song was still playing in his chest, and he understood now what Elias had been looking for.

Not songs to be collected. Songs to be sung. Songs so old that they predated the idea of a singer.

The songs of creation itself.

---

Chapter 20: The Singer and the Song

The keeper was waiting on the dock when they returned. She was more solid than ever, her saree dry, her hair moving in a wind that did not touch the trees around her.

"You found them," she said.

"I found the songs. But I cannot read them."

"You do not need to read them. You need to sing them."

"I am not a singer."

"Everyone is a singer. The question is whether you have the courage to open your mouth and let the song out."

The keeper stepped closer and touched Christo's chest, where the bag of scrolls hung against his skin.

"These songs are the original songs. The ones that were sung when the universe was born. They are not in any language you know, because language had not been invented yet. But you can sing them because they are in a language that predates language—the language of vibration, of intention, of the space between thoughts."

"How?"

"By letting go of everything you think a song should be. Close your eyes. Feel the scrolls against your skin. And open your mouth."

Christo closed his eyes. He felt the scrolls, heavy and ancient, pressed against his chest. He felt the song that had started playing when he first touched them, the vibration that had not stopped.

He opened his mouth.

What came out was not a voice. It was a sound, a tone, a vibration that seemed to come from somewhere deeper than his throat. It was the sound of the sea. It was the sound of the wind. It was the sound of the earth turning.

And as he sang, the scrolls began to glow.

The keeper smiled.

"Now you understand. The songs are not on the scrolls. The scrolls are just containers. The songs are in you. They have always been in you. They are the songs your mother passed down when she carried you, the songs of the universe that every mother passes to every child, the songs that have been forgotten because no one had the courage to sing them."

Christo stopped singing. The vibration continued, resonating through the air, through the water, through the ground beneath his feet.

"What do I do now?"

"Now you go back to Elias. And you show him what you have found. Because Elias has been looking for these songs his entire life, but he has been looking in the wrong place. He has been looking outward, in the machines, in the chest, in the past. But the songs were never out there. They were always inside him."

The keeper's form began to fade.

"I am sorry," she said. "I cannot stay much longer. The song you sang has freed me. I am no longer bound to the machine."

"Where will you go?"

"Where all songs go when they are sung. Into the world. Into the ears of those who need to hear them."

She smiled, and for a moment she looked young, and free, and full of light.

"Thank you, Christo. For pressing button seven."

And then she was gone.

---

End of Arc II

---

Arc III: The Clockmaker's Bargain

---

Chapter 21: The Clockmaker's Museum

Elias Varghese was waiting for them. He stood on the porch of his workshop as Christo's boat approached, and his face was not angry. It was hungry.

"You found them," he said, before Christo could speak. "The songs of creation. I felt the moment you touched the chest. The water itself sang."

"You knew where they were all along."

"I knew where the chest was. I knew where the songs were supposed to be. But I could not retrieve them. The chest was sealed with a key that only your mother could make. She forged it from the metal of her wedding ring, melted down and recast into three pieces. One piece she gave to your father. One piece she gave to the priest. And one piece she kept for herself until she was ready to pass it on."

"She passed it to me."

"She passed you the key. But she also passed you something else. Something I did not anticipate."

Elias stepped down from the porch and walked to the edge of the dock. He was old, older than Christo had realized—his skin was like parchment, his eyes like stones that had been worn smooth by centuries of water.

"She passed you the ability to sing the songs. Not just to find them. To sing them. I can collect songs. I can store them. I can build machines that hold them. But I cannot sing them. My voice is dead. It died the day I built the first jukebox."

"Because you trapped the keeper inside it."

"Because I had to." Elias's voice cracked, and for a moment Christo saw something behind the hunger—a grief so old it had fossilized. "The keeper was my daughter."

---

Chapter 22: The Lost Daughter

Elias sat them in his workshop, surrounded by the guts of a hundred jukeboxes, and told them the story of a girl who sang the universe into being.

"Her name was Esther," he said. "She was born in 1915, in a village that no longer exists. Her mother died giving birth to her, and I raised her alone. I was a clockmaker then, not a collector of songs. I repaired timepieces, not memories. But when Esther began to sing, everything changed."

"How old was she?"

"Three. She was three years old when she sang her first song. It was a song I had never heard, in a language I did not know. And when she sang it, the clocks in my workshop stopped. Every single one. They stopped at the same moment, and they did not start again until she finished."

Elias paused, his hands trembling.

"I knew then that she was special. Not just talented—special. Her voice could affect time. Could affect matter. Could affect the boundaries between what was real and what was possible. I should have protected her. Instead, I studied her. I recorded her. I built machines to capture her voice."

"The jukeboxes."

"The first one. The Wurlitzer. I built it to hold her songs so that I could study them without needing her to sing them again and again. But the machine did more than capture her voice. It captured her. Every time she sang into it, a piece of her stayed behind. By the time she was eighteen, she was half-empty. A shell. A body without a soul."

"And you put her in the machine to save her."

"No. I put her in the machine because I was afraid of what she would become without it. I trapped her because I could not bear to lose her entirely. And then I built the Seeburg to find a way to bring her back."

"But you could not."

"I could not because I had forgotten the songs of creation. I had forgotten the source of her power. I had spent so much time studying the effects that I had lost sight of the cause. And by the time I realized my mistake, it was too late. She had been in the machine too long. She had become part of it."

Elias looked at Christo with eyes that held no hope.

"I have been trying to free her for sixty years. And you, by pressing button seven, have succeeded where I failed. You have freed her. But you have also destroyed the only reason I had to keep building."

---

Chapter 23: The Father's Guilt

"You let her out," Appachen said when Christo returned to Kattakada. "The keeper. You let her out of the machine."

"She was Elias's daughter. She was trapped there for sixty years."

"Elias trapped her there. He built the machine that ate her soul. He is not a victim, Christo. He is a monster who has been trying to undo his own monstrosity by creating more monsters."

"He is a father who lost his daughter."

"So am I."

The words stopped Christo cold.

"I lost your mother," Appachen said. "I lost her long before she died. I lost her the moment she sold her songs to the jukebox, because she was never whole again. She walked through life like a woman who had forgotten how to feel. And it was my fault."

"Why?"

"Because I asked her to stay. Because I could not bear to let her go to Bombay, to become the singer she was meant to be. I told myself I loved her. But love does not ask someone to shrink. Love does not ask someone to sell their voice."

Appachen's good hand gripped the arm of his chair.

"I asked her to stay because I was afraid that if she became someone else, she would no longer love me. I was afraid that her voice would take her to places I could not follow. And so I kept her small. I kept her quiet. I kept her here."

"Did she know?"

"She knew. And she forgave me. That was the worst part. She forgave me, and she stayed, and she sold her songs to the jukebox so that she would never have to wonder what she had given up. She locked her grief away so that she could love me without resentment."

Appachen began to cry.

"I have been sitting in that chair for three years, listening to her songs play from the machine I kept in the back room. Every night, when the house was quiet, I turned it on. I let her voice fill the room. And I wept. I wept for the life she could have had. I wept for the songs she never sang. I wept because I loved her, and love is not enough."

---

Chapter 24: The Bargain Revisited

Meera found Christo on the seawall at midnight, staring at the sea.

"Your father told you the truth."

"He told me his truth. But I still do not know my mother's."

"Your mother's truth is in the scrolls. The songs of creation. She knew about them. She knew where they were. And she made three keys—one for your father, one for the priest, one for you."

"Three keys. Three jukeboxes. Three parts of a story I still do not understand."

"The keys are not for the jukeboxes. The keys are for the songs. Your mother wanted you to find them, but she also wanted you to have the choice she never had. The choice to sing or to stay silent."

Christo looked at the scrolls, still wrapped in oilcloth, still pressed against his chest.

"I can feel them," he said. "The songs. They are not just sounds. They are memories. Memories of things that happened before I was born. Before anyone was born. They are the memory of the universe itself."

"And they are asking you to sing them."

"They are asking me to finish what my mother started."

Meera sat beside him. "What did she start?"

"She started a revolution. She found the songs of creation, and she made keys to unlock them. But she did not sing them herself because she was afraid. Afraid of what would happen if the songs were heard. Afraid that they would change the world too much. Afraid that she would lose the people she loved."

"And now?"

"Now I have to decide. Do I sing the songs and risk everything? Or do I lock them away and let the world continue as it is?"

"Your mother locked them away because she was afraid. Do you want to be the son who carries her fear? Or the son who carries her courage?"

---

Chapter 25: The Third Jukebox

They found it the next morning, in a cave beneath the clockmaker's workshop, hidden by a trapdoor that had been covered by a rug woven from coconut fiber. The third jukebox was enormous—twice the size of the Wurlitzer, built from dark wood and brass and glass that seemed to absorb light rather than reflect it.

It had one hundred and forty-four buttons, arranged in a circle around a central panel that glowed with a faint blue light. The panel was blank, but Christo knew it would display something when the machine was activated.

"One hundred and forty-four thousand," he said. "The keeper said the buttons represent the saved."

"Saved from what?" Meera asked.

"Saved from silence. Saved from the forgetfulness that comes when songs are not sung."

Elias stood in the doorway of the cave, his walking stick in hand, his eyes fixed on the machine.

"It is ready," he said. "The third jukebox is ready. But it cannot be activated without a vessel. Without someone to hold the songs."

"You want Christo to be the vessel."

"No. I want to be the vessel."

The words hung in the air.

"I built these machines," Elias said. "I trapped my daughter. I collected songs that were not mine to take. If anyone deserves to hold the weight of every song, it is me. Let me be the vessel. Let me carry the songs. Let me be the one who suffers for what I have done."

"And if you die?"

"Then I die having done one good thing."

Elias walked to the third jukebox and pressed his palm against the central panel. The blue light flickered. The machine hummed, a sound so deep it seemed to come from the center of the earth.

"These songs have been waiting for a voice," Elias said. "They have been waiting for someone willing to carry them. I am old. I am tired. I have spent my life chasing something I should never have touched. Let me end this."

"No."

Christo stepped forward.

"You built the cage. But you did not build the key. My mother built the key. She built it for me. And if I am going to sing the songs of creation, I am going to sing them myself."

---

Chapter 26: The First Note

Christo stood before the third jukebox. The one hundred and forty-four buttons glowed faintly, each one representing a song that had been waiting to be sung. He reached out and pressed button number one.

The machine played a note.

It was the same note the Wurlitzer had played in the workshop, the note that had sustained for hours, the note that had seemed to contain everything. But this time, Christo understood it. This time, he heard the song behind the note.

He opened his mouth and sang.

The note that came out of him was not his voice. It was the universe's voice. It was the sound of stars being born. It was the sound of galaxies spinning. It was the sound of the first cell dividing, of the first breath, of the first word.

And as he sang, the third jukebox began to absorb the song.

Elias gasped. "You are feeding the machine. You are giving it what it needs."

"What does it need?"

"Your voice. Your life. Your soul. Every song you sing will be stored in the machine, and the machine will use it to power the final door."

Meera grabbed Christo's arm. "Stop. If you keep singing, you will end up like the keeper. You will be trapped in the machine."

Christo stopped singing. The note faded. The machine's glow dimmed.

"It wants my voice," he said. "But it also wants my choice. My mother chose not to sing. She chose silence. That is what the machine cannot understand. It can take songs, but it cannot take the choice not to sing."

"Then do not sing," Meera said.

"But if I do not sing, the machine cannot be completed. And if the machine cannot be completed, the songs cannot be freed."

"Your freedom is worth more than the songs."

"Is it?"

---

Chapter 27: The Weight of Memory

That night, Christo dreamed of his mother.

She was sitting on the seawall, her feet dangling over the edge, her voice humming a melody he had never heard. She was young, younger than he was now, and her face held a light that seemed to come from somewhere inside her.

"You found the scrolls," she said, without turning around.

"You left them for me."

"I left them for you because I knew you would find them. I knew you would face the same choice I faced. And I wanted you to make it with your eyes open."

"What choice did you make?"

"I chose to stay. I chose your father. I chose the life I had, not the life I could have had. And I have regretted it every single day."

"Then why did you make that choice?"

"Because I was afraid. Afraid of failing. Afraid of success. Afraid of leaving the people I loved. Afraid of becoming someone they would not recognize. So I stayed small. I stayed quiet. I stayed safe."

"Was it worth it?"

His mother turned to face him. Her eyes were full of tears, but her smile was soft.

"Look at you," she said. "You are standing in front of the third jukebox, ready to sing the songs of creation. You are braver than I ever was. You are everything I hoped you would become."

"I am afraid."

"Good. Fear means you are about to do something that matters."

She reached out and touched his face. Her hand was warm, solid, real.

"I loved your father," she said. "I love him still. But love is not enough to make a life worth living. You need to sing, Christo. You need to let your voice be heard. Not because the songs demand it. Because you deserve to be heard."

---

Chapter 28: The River of Sound

Christo woke to find the third jukebox playing. No one had touched it. The machine had started on its own, cycling through the one hundred and forty-four buttons, each one producing a fragment of song that lasted only seconds before switching to the next.

It was the keeper's voice.

Esther was singing from inside the machine—not trapped, not bound, but present, her voice filling the cave with a sound that was both joyful and sorrowful, a sound that seemed to contain every emotion at once.

"You are still here," Christo said.

"I am always here. I was here before the machine existed. I will be here after it is gone."

"Your father thinks he trapped you."

"My father thinks he has control. But the songs do not belong to him. They never did. They belong to the universe, and the universe has been singing them through me since before I was born."

Esther's voice softened.

"You have the scrolls. You have the keys. You have the choice. But you also have something else—something my father never had. You have a reason to sing that is not about power."

"What is my reason?"

"You want to know your mother. Not through the songs she wrote, but through the songs she did not write. You want to understand the silence she chose. And the only way to understand silence is to fill it with sound."

Esther's voice began to fade.

"The machine will try to take your voice. But you can choose what to give it. You can choose what to hold back. You are not a vessel, Christo. You are a singer. And singers choose what they sing."

---

Chapter 29: The Gathering

Word spread through the backwaters. People arrived at Elias's workshop in boats, in auto-rickshaws, on foot. They came because they had heard the songs—the songs that the jukeboxes had been playing for years, the songs that had been buried in their memories and suddenly unearthed.

Lissy came, the fisherman's wife. She brought her husband, Shaju, and together they stood in the cave, listening to the third jukebox hum.

The tea-shop owner from Neyyattinkara came. The bartender from Kovalam. The priest from Trivandrum. The grandmother from the house by the river. They filled the cave and spilled out onto the dock, a crowd of people who had been touched by the songs.

"They are here to witness," Elias said. "They are here because the songs called them."

"Songs can call people?"

"Songs can do anything. They are older than language, older than thought, older than time itself. They are the original form of communication. And they have been speaking to these people for years, through the jukeboxes I built."

Elias looked at the crowd.

"I spent my life trying to control the songs. But the songs were never meant to be controlled. They were meant to be shared. They were meant to be sung. And now they are calling everyone who has ever heard them, calling them to witness the final song."

"Will they be in danger?"

"They will be in exactly as much danger as you are. No more. No less."

Meera stepped up beside Christo. "Then it is time."

"It is time."

---

Chapter 30: The First Hundred Forty-Four

Christo stood before the third jukebox, the scrolls pressed against his chest, the keys around his neck, the crowd silent behind him.

He pressed button one.

He sang.

The song was not his. It was the song of a star that had burned out before the Earth was formed. It was the song of a supernova that had scattered elements across the galaxy, elements that would become planets and oceans and life. It was the oldest song in the universe, and he sang it.

The third jukebox absorbed the song. Button one lit up with a golden light.

He pressed button two.

He sang the song of the first ocean. The song of water forming on a young planet, of tides pulled by a moon that had not yet stabilized, of the first molecules of life assembling in the depths.

Button two lit up.

Button three. The song of the first cell. Button four. The song of the first breath. Button five. The song of the first heartbeat.

Button after button, song after song. Christo sang the history of everything. He sang the formation of mountains and the erosion of continents. He sang the birth of languages and the death of civilizations. He sang love songs that predated the concept of love, lullabies that predated the concept of mothers, elegies that predated the concept of death.

The crowd wept. Meera wept. Even Elias wept.

And when Christo reached button one hundred and forty-four, when he sang the final song—the song of the present moment, the song of now—the third jukebox began to glow with a light so bright that it seemed to contain every color and no color at all.

"It is done," Elias whispered. "The door is open."

---

End of Arc III

---

Arc IV: The Sea of Certainty

---

Chapter 31: The Door

The third jukebox's central panel did not display a song title. It displayed a landscape: a beach made of white sand, a sea of impossible blue, a sky that shifted through colors that had no names. And standing at the edge of the water, her back to the viewer, was a woman.

"Your mother," Elias said.

Christo stared at the image. The woman's silhouette was familiar in a way that made his chest ache. She stood with her hands at her sides, her hair moving in a wind that came from inside the machine.

"What is this?"

"The Sea of Certainty. The place where all songs come from. The place where they return. Your mother has been waiting there since the day she died."

"She is not dead."

"No. She is not dead. She has been living in the Sea of Certainty, guarding the songs that were never sung."

Meera stepped forward. "How do we reach her?"

"You do not reach her. You sing to her. The third jukebox is a door, but it can only be opened from the other side. Your mother has to choose to come back."

"The scrolls," Christo said. "The songs of creation. She left them for me so that I could call her back."

"She left them so that you could finish what she started. Whether that means calling her back or letting her go—that is your choice."

---

Chapter 32: The Call

Christo pressed button one again. This time, the song he sang was not a song of creation. It was a song of longing. A song of a son calling to his mother across a distance that measured more than miles.

He sang without words, because the longing did not need words. It needed vibration, intention, the raw force of a heart opening itself to the unknown.

The image on the panel flickered. The woman at the water's edge turned, slowly, as if hearing something from very far away.

Christo pressed button two. He sang the lullaby his mother had written, the one she had sung from the Wurlitzer:

*Little one, little one, the sea is wide—*
*I crossed it once, on the other side—*
*I left my voice in a wooden box—*
*Sold it to a man who winds the clocks—*

The woman on the panel began to walk toward the shore. She moved slowly, her feet leaving no footprints on the sand, her saree trailing behind her like a bridal train.

Button three. The song of the fisherman's daughter.
Button four. The protest song.
Button five. The children's rhyme.

Each song brought her closer. Each song she recognized as her own.

When Christo reached button six, the woman stopped at the water's edge. She was close enough now that he could see her face—and she was young, as young as she had been when she wrote the songs, before the stillbirth, before the bargain, before the silence.

"Anna," Elias said.

But Christo was already pressing button seven.

He sang the song the keeper had left, the song that was not his mother's but had been entrusted to her: *The jukebox has a heart, boy, and it beats in double time.*

The woman on the panel—Anna—raised her hand and pressed it against the invisible barrier between her world and theirs.

And she spoke.

"Christo. My son. You have done what I could not."

---

Chapter 33: The Other Side

"I never died," Anna said, her voice coming through the third jukebox's speakers like a radio transmission from a distant star. "I crossed into the Sea of Certainty the day I gave birth to you. The pain was too much. The grief of your brother was too heavy. I could not carry it and raise you at the same time."

"You left me."

"I left you to save you. I carried the grief of a dead child, and I knew that if I stayed, that grief would poison your life. So I traded places with the keeper. I entered the Sea of Certainty. She entered the world, bound to the Wurlitzer, waiting for someone to press button seven."

"The keeper was you."

"The keeper was a part of me. A fragment of my voice that I left behind. She carried my memories, my songs, my love for you. She watched over you from inside the machine. And when you were ready, she showed you the way."

Christo fell to his knees.

"I have been searching for you my whole life."

"I have been waiting for you my whole life. But I cannot come back, Christo. The Sea of Certainty is not a prison. It is a duty. Someone must guard the songs that have not yet been sung. And that someone is me."

"Then I will come to you."

"No. You have your own songs to sing. You have your own life to live. But I can give you one thing, if you ask for it."

"What?"

"The song I wrote for your father. The one I never sang. I can sing it to you now, through the third jukebox. And you can carry it back to him."

---

Chapter 34: The Mother's Song

Anna began to sing.

The song was not in Malayalam. It was not in English. It was a language older than both, a language that predated the division of tongues, a language made of pure emotion shaped into sound.

But Christo understood every word.

She sang of a woman who had loved two things: a man and a voice. She sang of choosing between them, of choosing the man, of watching her voice fade into something she no longer recognized. She sang of a child born from that choice, a child who carried the echoes of the voice she had abandoned. She sang of regret that was not regret—a grief that was also gratitude—a love that had cost her everything and given her everything in return.

She sang the song she had written for Appachen, the song that was never meant to be sold, the song that was given freely and could never be taken.

And when she finished, the third jukebox went silent.

"Take that song to your father," Anna said. "Tell him I have loved him from the other side of the door. Tell him I have loved him longer than memory. Tell him I have loved him in every song I never sang."

---

Chapter 35: The Return

Christo left the cave. He left Elias and Meera and the crowd of listeners. He walked through the backwaters in the rain, the song his mother had sung echoing in his chest, and he did not stop until he reached Kattakada.

Appachen was in his chair, staring at the sea.

"I heard her," the old man said, before Christo could speak. "The song. It reached me here, carried on the wind, carried on the water."

"She sang it for you. She has been waiting to sing it for you her entire life."

Appachen began to cry. Not the quiet tears of the stroke's aftermath, but the full weeping of a man who had held back a flood for decades.

"I never deserved her."

"Neither did I. But she loved us anyway. She loved us enough to leave. She loved us enough to stay. She loved us enough to sing a song across the boundaries of life and death."

"Sit with me," Appachen said.

Christo sat. They sat together, father and son, as the sun set over the Arabian Sea, and the song of Anna Palat played on repeat in their chests, a song that needed no machine to be heard.

---

Chapter 36: The Sea of Regret

The next morning, Christo returned to the cave. The crowd was gone. Elias was sitting alone, staring at the dead panel of the third jukebox.

"Your mother spoke to me last night," Elias said. "She told me that the Sea of Certainty is not the only sea. There is another sea, deeper, darker, older. The Sea of Regret. It is the place where songs go when they are sung and then forgotten."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I am going there. I am going to the Sea of Regret to find the songs I stole. I am going to return them to the people they belong to."

"Can you do that?"

"I do not know. But I have to try. The third jukebox is not a door I can open. But the first jukebox—the Wurlitzer—it can take me where I need to go."

Elias pulled a key from his pocket. Not one of Anna's keys. His own key, made of brass, worn smooth by decades of use.

"This opens the Wurlitzer's heart. The mechanism that holds the keeper's—your mother's—songs. I am going to lock myself inside it. I am going to enter the Sea of Regret. And I am going to do what I should have done sixty years ago."

Elias stood. He looked at Christo with an expression that was not quite peace and not quite surrender.

"Thank you for freeing my daughter. I will spend eternity trying to deserve your mercy."

He walked to the Wurlitzer—the machine that had started everything—and he opened its heart.

---

Chapter 37: The Clockmaker's Last Song

Christo watched Elias step into the Wurlitzer. The old clockmaker did not hesitate. He climbed into the cabinet, settled himself where the records should have been, and pulled the door closed behind him.

The machine began to play.

Not a record. Not a memory. A song that was being written in real time, composed by the machine itself as it absorbed Elias's final act. The melody was strange, discordant in places, as if the universe was still deciding what key it wanted to be in.

Elias's voice emerged from the speakers, distorted by the mechanism but still recognizable:

*I built a cage to hold a voice,*
*And called it love, and called it choice—*
*But every cage I built was me,*
*A prison I could not foresee.*

The song continued, line after line, a confession that stretched for what felt like hours. Elias sang about every song he had taken, every voice he had silenced, every choice he had made in the name of art and ambition. He sang about his daughter, about Anna Palat, about the songs of creation that he had tried to own.

And when the song ended, the Wurlitzer went silent. The bubble tubes died. The chrome tarnished in seconds, as if decades had passed in the space of a single song.

Elias Varghese was gone. He had entered the Sea of Regret, and he was not coming back.

---

Chapter 38: The Keeper's Return

Esther appeared at the workshop door that evening. She was solid now, fully present, her eyes clear, her hands steady. She was free.

"Your mother sends her love," she said.

"You have spoken to her."

"I have been with her. In the Sea of Certainty. When my father entered the Sea of Regret, the balance shifted. The door between the seas opened, and I could finally go where I needed to go."

"Is she well?"

"She is tired. Guarding the unsung songs is not easy. But she is at peace. She knows you have heard her song. She knows your father has heard it too."

Esther stepped into the workshop and looked at the Wurlitzer. The machine was dead now, truly dead, its life spent to carry Elias on his final journey.

"He is gone," she said. "My father has gone to make amends. He will spend eternity returning what he stole. And when he is done, he will be free."

"Do you forgive him?"

Esther was silent for a long moment.

"Forgiveness is not a door that opens once. It is a door that must be opened every day. I have forgiven him today. I will have to forgive him again tomorrow. And the day after. And the day after that."

She turned to Christo.

"But I have also learned something. The songs are not meant to be held. They are meant to be passed on. My father tried to own them. Your mother tried to bury them. But the songs belong to the world, and the world has a right to hear them."

"What are you saying?"

"I am saying that the third jukebox does not have to be the final door. It can be the first door. It can be the door that opens the world to the songs that have been waiting."

"The songs of creation."

"The songs of creation. The songs of every voice that has ever been silenced. The songs of every memory that has been buried. The jukeboxes did not trap the songs. They preserved them. They kept them safe until the world was ready to hear them."

"And is the world ready?"

"The world has always been ready. It is the people who are afraid."

---

Chapter 39: The World's Song

Christo made a decision.

He returned to the cave with Esther and Meera. He opened the third jukebox's panel. And he began to sing.

Not the songs of creation. Not his mother's songs. Not the keeper's songs. He sang the songs that the Seeburg had predicted—the songs from the future, the songs that had not yet been written.

He sang them into existence.

He sang a song about a girl who would be born a hundred years from now, a girl who would sing the world into a new age. He sang a song about a reconciliation between the land and the sea, a healing of the wound that had been open since the beginning of time. He sang a song about peace, genuine peace, not the absence of conflict but the presence of understanding.

And as he sang, the third jukebox began to play the songs back, not into the cave but into the world. The songs spread across the backwaters, across Kerala, across India, across the planet. They were heard by everyone who was ready to hear them.

Meera picked up her harmonium and began to play along. Her notes wove with Christo's voice, creating harmonies that no one had ever heard, melodies that seemed to have been waiting for centuries to be expressed.

The workshop filled with sound. The walls seemed to dissolve. The cave became a cathedral. The sea itself listened.

"This is what the jukeboxes were meant to do," Esther said. "Not to trap songs. To release them."

---

Chapter 40: The Unlocking

Christo sang for three days.

He did not eat. He did not sleep. He stood before the third jukebox, and he sang every song that came to him—songs from the scrolls, songs from his mother's notebook, songs from the Seeburg's predictions, songs that seemed to come from nowhere and everywhere at once.

People came from across Kerala to listen. They brought food and water, but Christo did not touch them. He only sang.

On the third day, the Wurlitzer in Kattakada began to play. The dead machine, the one that had carried Elias into the Sea of Regret, suddenly came alive, playing the songs Christo was singing in perfect synchronization.

The Seeburg in the workshop joined next. The brass cylinders glowed, and the machine played the future songs, the ones that would be written when Christo's songs had seeded themselves in the hearts of listeners.

And finally, the third jukebox itself played the song of the present—the song of now, the song of this moment, the song of everything that was happening as it was happening.

The three machines played as one, and the world heard them.

Esther wept. Meera wept. Even the sky seemed to weep, releasing a rain that was warm and gentle and smelled of jasmine.

And then the song ended.

The jukeboxes fell silent. The rain stopped. The sun broke through the clouds, casting the backwaters in a light that seemed older than the sun itself.

And Christo collapsed.

---

End of Arc IV

---

Arc V: The Song That Was Always There

---

Chapter 41: The Silence After

Christo woke in a white room. The walls were white. The sheets were white. The light coming through the window was so bright it seemed to erase the edges of things. He was in the hospital in Alappuzha, and Meera was sitting in the chair beside his bed.

"You sang for three days," she said. "You did not stop. Your voice should have given out. Your body should have failed. But you kept going until the last note."

"Did it work?"

"The songs reached everyone. Everywhere. People told me they heard them in Chennai, in Mumbai, in Delhi. Someone called from London to say their grandmother had heard a song she had not remembered since childhood. The songs crossed oceans, Christo. They crossed borders. They crossed time."

"And the jukeboxes?"

"Dead. All three. They stopped when you collapsed. The Wurlitzer cracked down the middle. The Seeburg's brass cylinders crumbled to dust. The third jukebox—the panel went blank, and the wood began to rot as if it had been underwater for a century."

"Elias?"

"Gone. Esther says he is in the Sea of Regret, returning songs one by one. It will take him eternity. But he is at peace."

"And my mother?"

Meera was silent.

"She stayed," Christo said.

"She stayed. She said the Sea of Certainty needs a guardian, and she has chosen to be that guardian. She said to tell you that she loves you, and that she will be listening."

---

Chapter 42: The Wound That Heals

Christo left the hospital after three days. He went back to Kattakada. He went back to the workshop, where the pieces of the Wurlitzer lay in a pile, and he sat among the wreckage, trying to understand what he had become.

He was not the same person who had received a broken jukebox from St. Sebastian's. That person had been a repairman, a son, a man who carried his father's silence like a second skin. This new person was a singer. A vessel. A door that had been opened and could not be closed.

"Will the songs come back?" he asked, when Esther appeared in the doorway.

"Some will. Some will find new voices. Some will stay buried, because they were never meant to be sung. That is the nature of songs. They come and go as they please."

"And what am I now?"

"You are the person who heard the songs and sang them anyway. That is enough."

---

Chapter 43: Appachen's Last Words

Appachen died on a Thursday, the same day of the week the Wurlitzer had arrived. He died in his wicker chair, facing the sea, a smile on his face that Christo had never seen before.

"He heard the song," Meera said. "He heard your mother's song, and he carried it with him to the end."

Christo sat beside his father's body and held his hand. The hand was still warm, the fingers curled as if still reaching for something.

"He spent thirty years in silence," Christo said. "Thirty years unable to speak what he knew. And when he finally spoke again, it was to tell me the truth."

"Did you forgive him?"

"I do not know if there is anything to forgive. He was a man who loved a woman and was afraid of losing her. That is not a crime. That is a tragedy."

"And your mother?"

"She made her choice. She chose him. She chose me. She chose to guard the unsung songs so that no one else would have to carry that weight. I cannot forgive her because she did nothing wrong."

"What can you do?"

"I can sing. I can sing the songs she could not. I can be the voice she gave up."

---

Chapter 44: The Funeral

The funeral was small, as Appachen had requested. Father Isaac said the prayers. Gracy wept. The neighbors brought food and stood in respectful silence.

Meera played her harmonium. She played the song Anna had written for Appachen—the one Christo had carried back from the third jukebox. She had learned it by heart, hearing it from Christo's memory, translating it into notes that could be played on keys and bellows.

The music filled the church. It rose to the rafters. It slipped through the open windows and down the hill toward the sea.

And as it played, Christo saw his mother.

She stood at the back of the church, young and whole and luminous, her saree white, her hair loose, her eyes fixed on the coffin where Appachen lay.

She did not speak. She did not move. She simply stood, and listened, and when the song ended, she smiled.

And then she was gone.

---

Chapter 45: The House by the Sea

Christo did not leave Kattakada. He stayed in the workshop, sleeping in the back room where Appachen had sat for three years, eating the meals Gracy brought, watching the sea change color with the hours.

He did not fix jukeboxes anymore. There was no need. The machines that came to him now were ordinary machines, broken amplifiers and dead motors, things that could be fixed with a soldering iron and patience. But no one brought them anymore. The people had stopped needing jukeboxes. They had the songs in their heads, the songs Christo had sung, and they did not need machines to hear them.

Meera stayed too. She brought her harmonium, and in the evenings she played, and Christo sang, and the songs that came out of them were new—songs that had never been written, songs that belonged only to the two of them.

"Your mother would have liked this," Meera said one evening.

"She would have liked the music."

"She would have liked the silence between the notes. That is where the real songs live."

---

Chapter 46: The Last Jukebox

A letter arrived six months later, postmarked from a village Christo did not know. It was written on brown paper, in handwriting that was shaky but determined:

*Christo—*
*I have returned every song I stole. It took me six months, but I found each person, each memory, each buried grief. I gave them back. Some wept. Some cursed me. Some thanked me. One woman kissed my hands and said she had been waiting forty years to hear her mother's voice again.*
*I am free now. The Sea of Regret has released me. I am going to find Esther, to spend whatever time I have left making amends to her directly.*
*Thank you for letting me finish what I started.*
*—Elias Varghese*

Christo folded the letter and put it in the drawer where Appachen had kept his mother's notebook. He did not show it to anyone.

That night, he dreamed of the third jukebox. It was whole again, glowing with blue light, its one hundred and forty-four buttons arranged in a circle around a central panel. The panel displayed a single word:

*SING*

He woke with the word in his chest, a note that vibrated through every cell of his body.

"What is it?" Meera asked.

"It is starting again."

"The songs?"

"The songs. They are coming back. They want to be sung."

---

Chapter 47: The Second Singing

Christo did not resist. He stood on the seawall at dawn, facing the sea, and he began to sing.

The songs that came out of him were not the songs of creation. They were not his mother's songs. They were not the keeper's songs. They were the songs of ordinary life—a fisherman mending his net, a mother feeding her child, a bride walking to the altar, a child learning to read.

He sang them all. Every moment of beauty, every moment of grief, every moment that deserved to be remembered.

And as he sang, the sea began to glow.

Not with light. With sound. The waves themselves were singing, vibrating with a frequency that matched Christo's voice, creating harmonies that spanned the spectrum of human emotion.

The people of Kattakada came out of their houses. They stood on the beach, in the streets, on the rooftops. They listened. And then they began to sing.

Not the same songs. Their own songs. The songs of their own lives, the memories they had never expressed, the joys and sorrows they had never given voice to.

The entire village sang.

---

Chapter 48: The Harmony

Meera joined Christo on the seawall, her harmonium balanced on her knees, her fingers finding the chords that matched the song he was singing. They did not plan the harmonies. They did not write them down. They simply played, and the music took shape on its own.

The Seeburg had predicted this. Christo understood now. The future songs it had played were not warnings. They were glimpses. The machine had shown him what would happen if he chose to sing—not destruction, but creation. Not silence, but sound.

"You knew," he said to Meera. "You knew this would happen."

"I knew the songs would find a way. I did not know it would be you."

"I am not a singer."

"You are the singer. You have always been the singer. Your mother knew it. The keeper knew it. Elias knew it. The only one who did not know was you."

---

Chapter 49: The Song Without End

The singing did not stop. It continued through the day, through the night, through the next day. The people of Kattakada sang until their voices cracked, and then they sang in whispers, and then they sang in silence, their mouths forming words that no longer needed sound.

Christo sang with them.

He sang for his mother, for the voice she had given up. He sang for his father, for the silence he had carried. He sang for Elias, for the guilt he had tried to escape. He sang for Esther, for the freedom she had earned. He sang for Meera, for the music she had brought into his life.

And he sang for himself—for the boy who had fixed broken machines, for the man who had learned that some things cannot be fixed, only understood.

When the song ended, the sun was rising on the third day. The sea was calm. The sky was clear. The people of Kattakada were standing on the beach, their faces lifted to the light, their hearts full of the songs they had sung and the songs they had heard.

---

Chapter 50: Songs My Mother Sold

Christo walked into the workshop one last time. The pieces of the Wurlitzer were still there, scattered across the floor, covered in dust.

He knelt and picked up a fragment of the bubble tube. The colored water had long since evaporated, leaving behind a residue that shimmered in the morning light.

He heard his mother's voice, one last time. Not from a machine. From inside his own chest.

*You found it, Christo. You found the song that was always there.*

"Was it worth it?" he asked. "Giving up your voice?"

*I did not give up my voice. I gave up my silence. I gave up the fear that kept me from singing. And in giving it up, I passed the song to you.*

"What song?"

*The song of a mother who loved her son enough to leave. The song of a woman who loved a man enough to stay. The song of a singer who loved her voice enough to let it go.*

*You are that song, Christo. You have always been that song.*

The voice faded. The workshop was quiet. The sea murmured beyond the walls.

Christo stood. He walked to the door and looked out at the village, at the people, at Meera waiting for him on the seawall.

He did not know what came next. But for the first time in his life, he was not afraid.

Because the song was still playing. It had always been playing. It would always play. Not in a machine, not in a record, not in a voice.

In the space between what was lost and what was found.

In the place where silence became sound.

In the heart of the jukebox repairman who had learned that some songs cannot be sold.

They can only be sung.

THE END
