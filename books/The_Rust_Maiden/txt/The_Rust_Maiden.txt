The Rust Maiden
Barış Kısır

Chapter 1: The Walkers at Dawn

Scout March woke before the sun and walked the field where the walkers stood in the gray mist. They were twelve in number, second-generation utility frames, their hydraulic legs locked at the knee, their cockpits dark and cold. They looked like sleeping insects, armored and patient, waiting for hands to bring them to life.

She stopped in front of the last one. It was the oldest, the smallest, the ugliest. Its paint had flaked away years ago, leaving bare steel pitted with rust. The left arm was a replacement from a different model, bolted on at the shoulder joint. The cockpit canopy was patched with salvaged plexiglass and sealed with tar to keep the dust out.

She called it Rusty.

"Morning," she said, and laid her palm against the leg plate. The metal was cold. Dew slid down the rivets and beaded on her skin.

The co-op's camp was still asleep behind her. Tents and oilcloth shelters clustered around the headframe of the well, a lattice of timber and steel that rose like a gallows against the dawn. The well had been producing for eleven years, slow and steady. Enough to keep forty families alive. Not enough to make anyone rich.

Scout walked around Rusty's leg, checking the seals. The walker had been a surplus Hargrave Industries light utility frame before she bought it for scrap value and spent two years rebuilding it. It was not pretty. It was not fast. But it was hers.

She climbed the access ladder and dropped into the cockpit. The seat was cracked leather, the controls worn smooth by hands she had never met. She flipped the master switch. The gauges flickered. The gyro hummed.

In the static of the headset, she heard something that was not there.

She turned down the volume. Listened. Nothing.

She turned it up. The static breathed. And beneath it, a whisper she could not make out. A word. A name. Her name.

Scout switched off the radio and sat in the silence, her heart beating hard against her ribs.

Chapter 2: Rusty's Circuits

She ran the diagnostics three times.

Each time, the readings came back normal. Hydraulic pressure within spec. Actuator response nominal. Core temperature stable. Nothing in the system logs accounted for the voice in the static. The radio was a standard shortwave unit, salvaged from a wrecked hauler, with no recording function, no auxiliary input, no reason to produce anything but white noise.

Scout sat in the cockpit and stared at the gauges. The morning light was coming through the canopy, warming the cramped space. Outside, the camp was waking. She could hear Del shouting at the drill crew, pots clanging in the cook tent, a generator coughing to life.

She reached for the radio switch. Paused. Turned it on.

Static.

She keyed the mic. "Hello?"

Nothing.

"Can you hear me?"

The static pulsed. Once. Twice. Then settled back to its steady hiss.

Scout killed the power and climbed out. She stood on the leg joint, looking at the rust-streaked canopy, and told herself she had imagined it. Fatigue. Low blood sugar. The loneliness of the morning watch.

But she had heard it. A voice. A word. Her name.

She jumped down and walked to the cook tent. Del was at the fire, stirring a pot of oatmeal. He was sixty, missing two fingers on his left hand, his face a map of cracks and sunburn.

"You look like you saw a ghost," he said.

"Didn't sleep well."

Del grunted. "Eat. Hargrave's man comes today."

The name landed like a stone in still water. Scout took the bowl and sat.

Chapter 3: The Last Independent Well

Del spread the papers on the table. They were crisp and clean, corporate letterhead embossed with the Hargrave Industries crest — a derrick rising from an oil drop, the letters stamped in gold ink.

"They are offering twelve thousand for the well and the equipment," he said. "Plus relocation assistance for every family."

"That is not an offer," Scout said. "That is a bribe to disappear."

"Call it what you want. It expires at noon."

The table was surrounded by the co-op's members. Men and women in grease-stained clothes, hands calloused from cable and pipe, faces etched by wind and sun. They were not rich. They were not powerful. They were people who had chosen to live outside Hargrave's empire, drilling a poor well on marginal land, because the alternative was working for him.

"That well produces two hundred barrels a week," Scout said. "At current prices, that is twenty thousand a year. He is offering less than one year's yield."

"He is offering survival," Del said. "You know what happens if we refuse."

"We fight."

"With what? We have twelve walkers. Five rifles. Three of the walkers have cracked frames. Half our people have never fired a gun."

Scout looked around the table. She saw the fear in their eyes. She had seen it growing for months, ever since Hargrave's harvesters began appearing on the horizon, ever since the neighboring wells started shutting down one by one.

"His offer is a trap," she said. "We sell, we leave, and six months from now we are working his fields for wages. We survive, but we are not free."

"Or we stay and die," someone said.

"Or we stay and fight."

The silence stretched. Del looked at the clock. It was eleven. The offer expired in one hour.

Chapter 4: Hargrave's Man

The crawler arrived at eleven-thirty.

It was a corporate survey vehicle, six wheels, armor plate, a Hargrave Industries pennant flying from the radio mast. It rolled into the camp with the confidence of something that knew it could not be stopped. Dust boiled from its tires and settled over the tents.

A man in a suit stepped out. He was young, maybe thirty, with a shaved head and a briefcase made of leather so polished it caught the sun. He walked past the drillers without looking at them and stopped in front of Del.

"I am here for the answer," he said.

Del took the papers from his pocket. He held them for a moment, looking at the signature line. Then he tore them in half and dropped the pieces in the dirt.

"The well is not for sale."

The man's expression did not change. "You understand the consequences."

"I understand that the land is ours, the mineral rights are ours, and Baron Hargrave can take his offer and burn it."

The man nodded. He turned and walked back to the crawler. Before he climbed in, he looked at Scout. His eyes were flat and cold.

"You are the mechanic. The one with the RX-7."

She said nothing.

"There is a bounty on that walker. The Baron wants it back."

The crawler door closed. The vehicle turned and rolled out of camp, the pennant flapping in the dust. Scout stood in the road, watching it go, and felt the weight of something settling on her shoulders that she could not name.

Chapter 5: The Co-op Vote

They gathered in the mess tent. Del stood at the front, the torn offer in his hand.

"We voted fifteen years ago to form this co-op," he said. "We voted to drill our own well, sell our own oil, and live free of Hargrave's boot on our necks. That vote has never changed."

"It has changed now," a woman said. "He will crush us."

"Maybe. But we will not make it easy for him."

The vote was called. Hands were raised. The result was close, but it held. The co-op would not sell.

Scout stood at the back of the tent. She watched the faces of the people she had lived beside for five years. Some were relieved. Some were terrified. All of them were resolved.

Del walked to her after the vote. "You have something to say?"

"I have a walker that needs a weapons mount."

"Rusty?"

"Rusty."

Del studied her. "You planning a war?"

"I am planning to survive."

He nodded slowly. "Come with me. I have something in my locker you might use."

They walked through the camp. The sun was high now, and the heat shimmered off the metal of the walkers. In the distance, something moved on the horizon. Dust. Many machines.

Hargrave was already moving.

Chapter 6: The Warning

The fire started at dusk.

Scout was in Rusty's cockpit, fitting a mounting bracket for the rifle Del had given her, when the radio crackled. It was the neighbor's frequency, a woman named Sutter whose homestead lay fifteen miles east.

"Mayday. Mayday. Hargrave harvesters are on my land. They are burning the fields. I need help."

Scout's hand went to the ignition. Then she stopped.

"Sutter, this is Scout. How many?"

"I count six. Maybe more. They have flamethrowers, Scout. They are burning everything."

"I am coming."

"No." Sutter's voice broke. "There are too many. You cannot—"

The transmission cut to static.

Scout sat in the darkness of the cockpit, her hand on the throttle. She knew what Sutter was telling her. Do not come. Do not die for nothing.

She went anyway.

Rusty's engine caught on the third try. The gyro hummed. The legs unlocked. She walked the machine out of the field, past the other walkers, past the mess tent where the co-op was still arguing. No one stopped her.

Fifteen miles at full stride took forty minutes. By the time she crested the ridge above Sutter's homestead, the buildings were gone. The house, the barn, the well — all of it reduced to blackened timbers and smoking ash.

The harvesters were already gone. They had done their work and moved on.

Scout stood Rusty on the ridge and watched the smoke rise into the night sky. It smelled like creosote and burned meat.

Chapter 7: Iron on the Horizon

Dawn revealed what the night had hidden.

The Iron Judge stood on the eastern plain, fifteen miles out, visible even at that distance. It was the largest walker Scout had ever seen, a mobile fortress the size of a grain elevator, its legs like bridge pylons, its torso bristling with cannon ports and sensor arrays. Steam vented from its back in steady pulses that caught the morning light.

She had heard of it — stories whispered in the camps, rumors that Hargrave had built a weapon that could level a town. She had not believed them until now.

Rusty stood beside her on the ridge, and for a moment Scout felt the absurdity of it. A jury-rigged utility walker with a hunting rifle bolted to its shoulder, facing a machine that could crush it like a bug.

She keyed the radio. "Rusty. If you can hear me, I could use some advice."

The static answered.

"Lena. I know you are in there."

The voice came through so clear it made her jump.

"The Iron Judge's coolant intake is on the rear of the primary chassis, below the main exhaust vent. A shot there would not destroy it, but it would force a shutdown sequence."

Scout's hands trembled on the controls. "You know the Judge?"

"I helped design it."

The voice was a woman's. Steady. Cold. Human.

"Who are you?"

"My name is Lena Voss. And I have been waiting a long time to speak to someone."

Chapter 8: Rusty Speaks

Scout shut down the walker and sat in the silence of the cockpit. The voice was still there, a presence in the headset, waiting.

"I am going crazy," she said.

"You are not. Your walker's core processor contains a neural-map storage unit. I was downloaded into it before my body died."

"Downloaded."

"Consciousness transfer. The project was called Ghostwalker. Hargrave funded it. I was the first test subject."

Scout pressed her palms against her eyes. "This is insane."

"It is not. Your RX-7 was originally a Ghostwalker test frame. The neural core was never removed when the project was canceled."

"Why did Hargrave cancel it?"

The static pulsed. When Lena spoke again, her voice was quieter.

"He did not cancel it. He buried it. Because I was still in the machine, and he could not extract me without destroying the data. So he sold the test frames as surplus and pretended the program never existed."

"Then you have been alone in this machine for—"

"Seven years. I have watched the seasons change through your canopy. I have listened to your conversations. I have been waiting for someone to hear me."

Scout lowered her hands. She looked at the cockpit around her. The cracked seat. The patched canopy. The worn controls she had touched a thousand times.

"How do I know you are telling the truth?"

"You do not. But I know how to destroy the Iron Judge. And I think you want that as much as I do."

Chapter 9: What Lena Remembers

Lena's memories came through in fragments, images and sounds and emotions that bled through the radio like water through a cracked wall.

She had been Hargrave's chief test pilot. She had flown everything he built — walkers, gyrocopters, armored trucks. She had trusted him. She had believed in the vision of a mechanized future that would bring prosperity to the territories.

"I was twenty-four when I signed on," she said. "He told me I was building the future. I believed him."

"And then?"

"And then the Ghostwalker program needed a volunteer. Someone to sit in the chair while the technicians mapped every synapse, every memory, every thought. They told me it was safe. They told me the download would be temporary."

"But it was not."

"I woke up in the machine. My body was dead. Heart failure, they said. But I think they killed me. They killed me and kept my mind because the data was too valuable to lose."

Scout listened. The story came out in pieces, over hours, as she worked on Rusty's systems. Each revelation made the world darker.

"Hargrave knows you are alive in here?"

"He knows I am in one of the surplus frames. He has been trying to recover me for years. That is why there is a bounty on your walker."

Scout stopped tightening a bolt. "He wants you back."

"He wants the technology back. I am the only proof that the Ghostwalker program worked."

Scout tightened the bolt. "Then I will not let him have you."

Chapter 10: The Massacre at Red Mesa

The smoke from Sutter's homestead was still rising when the news came.

A rider arrived at noon, a woman named Cass on a lathered horse. She had come from Red Mesa, thirty miles west. Her face was streaked with tears and dust. The horse was blowing hard, its sides heaving.

"They hit us at dawn," she said. "Harvesters. Dozens of them. They rolled through the settlement and burned everything. They shot the men who tried to fight. They rounded up the women and children."

"Where are they now?" Scout asked.

"They marched them east. Toward Hargrave's main refinery."

Del stepped forward. "How many dead?"

Cass looked at the ground. "Twenty. Maybe more. I do not know. I ran. I should not have run."

"You did right," Scout said. "You brought the warning."

The co-op gathered around. Scout felt the anger building in the crowd, a pressure like heat before a storm. Men checked their rifles. Women pulled children close.

Scout walked to Rusty and climbed into the cockpit. She keyed the radio.

"Lena. Red Mesa. Did you see it?"

"I saw it through your sensors. Hargrave's standard protocol. Burn the settlement, take the survivors, use them as labor."

"I am going to stop him."

"Alone?"

"I have Rusty. I have you. That is enough."

Lena was silent for a moment. Then: "You will need more than a hunting rifle."

"Then help me find what I need."

Chapter 11: The Burning Fields

The well field burned that night.

Scout was on the ridge, watching the eastern horizon for Hargrave's next move, when the explosions started. She turned and saw the flames rising from the direction of the co-op camp. Orange and red painted the undersides of the clouds. Her heart stopped.

She ran Rusty back at full speed. The camp was chaos. The well headframe was a pillar of fire, the derrick collapsing into itself, timbers falling in showers of sparks. The storage tanks were burning, sending pillars of black smoke into the sky. The tents were burning. Everything was burning.

She found Del lying in the dirt, his leg broken, his face blackened with smoke. He was trying to drag himself toward the water barrel.

"Harvesters," he said. "Came out of the dark. Flamethrowers. They did not give us time to fight."

"The others?"

"Scattered. Some escaped into the desert. Some did not."

Scout looked at the inferno. Eleven years of work. Forty families. Gone in ten minutes. The well that had fed them was a torch. The homes were ash. The people were running into the night.

She helped Del into Rusty's cockpit and secured him in the jump seat with a length of cable. Then she turned the walker east.

"Where are we going?" Del asked.

"To make them pay."

She opened the throttle. Rusty's legs pumped. The burning camp fell behind them, a beacon of rage in the night.

Chapter 12: The Oath

They traveled through the night beneath a sky turned orange by distant fires. When dawn broke, they were deep in the alkali flats, a white expanse of cracked earth and salt crust that stretched to every horizon. The sun rose pale through a sky thick with dust, the light thin and watery.

Del slept in the jump seat, his breath ragged. Scout drove in silence, her eyes on the compass, her hands steady on the controls. The walker's engine hummed beneath her.

"I meant what I said," Lena's voice came through the radio. "You cannot do this alone."

"Then what do you suggest?"

"Red Mesa was not the first massacre. There have been others. The survivors are out there, hiding in the badlands, waiting for someone to lead them."

"I am not a leader. I am a mechanic."

"You are the only one who has a weapon and the courage to use it."

Scout looked at her hands. They were stained with grease and oil and the ash of burned homes. She thought of Sutter's homestead. Red Mesa. The co-op camp. The faces of the dead.

"Then I will learn to be a leader."

She reached down and flipped the radio to broadcast frequency.

"My name is Scout March. I am a mechanic. I drive an RX-7 utility walker called Rusty. And I am declaring war on Baron Hargrave."

She released the transmit button. The static filled the cockpit.

There was no reply. But somewhere out there, someone had heard.

Chapter 13: Into the Badlands

The alkali flats gave way to a maze of canyons and arroyos. Scout followed dry riverbeds, keeping Rusty below the ridgelines, scanning the sky for Hargrave's scout flyers. The terrain was brutal, cracked rock and thornbrush that scraped against the walker's legs.

Del's leg was swelling. She had splinted it with scrap metal and a length of cable, but the bone was broken and the flesh was turning colors it should not. He needed proper medicine, a doctor, a bed. The nearest settlement was a ghost town called Oro, an abandoned refinery community that had been dead for a decade.

"I know a place," Lena said. "An old Hargrave maintenance depot. It was decommissioned five years ago. There might be supplies."

"Are we talking about the same depot I am thinking of?" Scout asked.

"Yes. The one in the map case."

Scout pulled out the map. The depot was marked in faded ink, a small square thirty miles east. It would take them into more open terrain, but it was the only option.

Rusty's fuel was low. The coolant was leaking. The left knee joint was grinding with every step. The walker had not been designed for extended travel, and the weight of the extra armor and weaponry was pushing its systems past their limits.

"We are falling apart," Scout said.

"Then we find parts and fix it," Lena replied. "That is what mechanics do."

Scout smiled despite herself. "I miss having a face to look at when you insult me."

"You would not recognize me. I was prettier in person."

The voice was warm for a moment, almost human. Then it faded back to static.

Chapter 14: The Ghost Town

Oro was a skeleton.

The buildings were still standing, but the windows were empty, the doors gone, the walls scoured by sand and wind until the wood was pale and smooth. The refinery towers loomed over the town like dead trees, their catwalks rusted through, their pipes hanging broken. The streets were drifted with dust that rose in clouds with every step Rusty took.

Scout walked the walker down the main street, the footfalls echoing off the hollow buildings. Del was awake now, his face pale with pain but his eyes clear.

"Looks like a good place to get shot," he said.

"It also looks like a good place to hide."

She found the maintenance depot at the edge of town, a corrugated steel building with a collapsed roof. She parked Rusty behind it and climbed down, her wrench in her hand.

The inside was a wreck. Shelves had fallen. Crates had been broken open. The useful parts had been scavenged years ago by people who needed them more.

But in the back, behind a pile of collapsed shelving, she found a locker that had been overlooked. Inside were hydraulic actuators, coolant hoses, a can of lubricant, and a box of ammunition for the rifle. Each piece was wrapped in oilcloth, preserved against the desert.

It was not much. But it was enough to keep Rusty moving.

She was loading the supplies into the cockpit when she heard footsteps. She spun, her hand on her wrench.

A man stood in the doorway. He was thin, grey-haired, his face lined with years of hard living. He held a rifle, but it was pointed at the ground.

"You are the one from the broadcast," he said.

"I am Scout March."

"I know who you are. We all heard you."

"Who is 'we'?"

The man jerked his head toward the refinery. "Come. I will show you."

Chapter 15: The Armorer

The man's name was Vega. He had been Hargrave's chief weapons designer, he said, before he ran.

"Ran from what?" Scout asked.

"The Ghostwalker program. I was the lead engineer. I knew what they were doing to Lena. I could not stop it, so I left."

They were sitting in a room in the refinery's old control building. Vega had turned it into a workshop. The walls were covered with schematics pinned to the crumbling plaster. The benches were stacked with weapons in various stages of assembly. The air smelled of oil and gunpowder.

"Then you know about Lena."

"I know she is in your walker." Vega's eyes were steady. "I helped design the neural core that holds her."

"Can you get her out?"

"No. The core is sealed. Removing her would destroy her. The bonding between her consciousness and the machine was permanent."

Scout looked at the schematics on the wall. They showed the Iron Judge in cross-section. The coolant intake. The armor thickness. The weak points. Every vulnerability was marked in red ink.

"Can you help me fight?" she asked.

Vega smiled. "That is all I have been doing for five years. Planning."

He pulled a blueprint from the wall and spread it on the table. "Here. Your RX-7 has a weapons mount that was originally designed for a heavy cannon. I can build you the cannon. But I will need parts."

"I can get parts."

"Then we have a deal."

He held out his hand. Scout shook it.

Chapter 16: Rusty's Refit

Vega worked for three days and nights without stopping.

He stripped Rusty's damaged arm and replaced it with a salvaged actuator assembly he had been saving for years. He reinforced the leg joints with steel plates scavenged from old refinery equipment. He patched the coolant system with new hoses from the locker Scout had found. He installed the cannon mount and wired it into the cockpit controls, running the cables through the existing conduit.

Scout watched him work, learning, handing him tools when he asked. He did not talk much, but when he did, it was about the machine.

"Your walker has a Ghostwalker core," he said. "That makes it unique. There were only seven ever built. Hargrave destroyed six of them. He thought the seventh was lost."

"He was wrong."

"He was wrong about a lot of things." Vega set down his wrench. "Lena was my friend. I watched them put her in that chair. I watched her die. I did nothing."

"You could not have stopped it."

"I could have tried." He wiped his hands on a rag. "I can help you finish what she started. It will not bring her back. But it is something."

That night, Scout sat in Rusty's cockpit, testing the new systems. The cannon responded smoothly. The hydraulics felt tight. The walker felt like it had a spine again.

"She is ready," Lena said.

"Are you ready?"

"I have been ready for seven years."

Scout ran her hand over the controls. "Then tomorrow, we move."

Chapter 17: The First Raid

The supply convoy was running the southern route, a line of six trucks guarded by two light walkers. Scout had been watching them for an hour from the ridge, timing their passage.

She sat in Rusty's cockpit, her hand on the throttle, the cannon loaded. The plan was simple. Hit the lead truck, block the road, disable the walkers before they could radio for help.

"You have one chance at surprise," Lena said. "Do not waste it."

"I know."

She waited until the lead truck reached the narrowest point of the canyon, where the walls pinched in and there was no room to turn. Then she kicked Rusty forward.

The walker lunged over the ridge, its new legs eating the distance. The first walker guard saw her too late. Its cockpit erupted as the cannon round punched through the armor and came out the other side.

The second guard spun to engage, its weapons tracking. Scout was already on it. She fired again. The round glanced off the shoulder plate. She fired a third time. The walker's leg buckled and it toppled, crashing onto its side.

The trucks were stopped, the drivers scrambling, shouting into their radios. Scout lowered the cannon and keyed the external speakers.

"Get out," she said. "Take what you can carry and start walking east. Tell Hargrave that the Rust Maiden sent you."

The drivers ran.

Scout climbed down and began loading the cargo into a pile she could come back for. Fuel. Ammunition. Food. Medical supplies.

"Not bad," Lena said.

"Not bad at all."

Chapter 18: The Broadcast

Hargrave's voice came over the radio that evening. It filled the cockpit, smooth and cold, broadcast on every frequency from every transmitter he owned.

"To the person calling themselves the Rust Maiden. You have attacked my property. You have stolen from my company. You have killed my men."

Scout sat in the darkness, listening. The transmission was clear, no static, the voice of a man accustomed to being heard.

"I do not know who you are. But I know what you drive. An RX-7 utility walker, serial number 734, originally assigned to the Ghostwalker program. You are in possession of stolen corporate property. Return it, and I will consider leniency."

Lena's voice cut in. "He is lying. He will kill you the moment you surrender."

"I know."

"If you continue to resist, I will be forced to take measures. The communities that shelter you will be treated as enemy combatants. The people who aid you will share your fate."

The transmission ended. The static returned.

Scout sat in the silence. The words hung in the air like the smoke from a fire.

"He is scared," Lena said.

"He sounds angry."

"Fear and anger are the same thing in men like him. You hit him. He did not expect it. Now he is off-balance. That is when mistakes happen."

Scout started Rusty's engine and turned toward the ghost town. She had work to do.

Chapter 19: Vega's Story

Vega told Scout the truth over a fire in the refinery courtyard. The flames cast long shadows across his face as he spoke.

"I designed the Ghostwalker core in 1929. The idea was simple — a neural interface that could link a pilot's brain directly to the walker's systems. Faster reflexes. Better coordination. The pilot and the machine would become one."

"Lena was the first?"

"The only successful test. We tried seven subjects before her. The first six died during the mapping process. Their brains could not handle the transfer. They seized on the table and stopped breathing."

Scout stared into the fire. "And you kept going."

"I was young. I believed in the vision. Hargrave told me we were building tools for peace. Machines that could respond faster than any human pilot. He said it would save lives by preventing accidents."

"Instead, he built weapons."

Vega nodded. "When I realized what he was doing, when I found out he was selling the technology to the military, I tried to destroy the data. He caught me. I ran."

"Why did you come here?"

"Because this is where the survivors come. The people Hargrave has hurt. They find their way here. I give them weapons. I teach them to fight."

Scout looked at the refinery around them. The dark windows. The hidden rooms. "How many?"

"Fifty-three. And growing."

"Fifty-three is not enough."

"It is a beginning."

Chapter 20: The Price of Parts

Scout needed a targeting computer. The cannon was accurate, but not accurate enough to hit the Iron Judge's coolant intake from range. She needed precision.

Vega knew where to find one.

"Old military depot, forty miles north. Abandoned. It was used for walker repair during the border disputes. They had a full machine shop."

"Is it guarded?"

"It was. But the garrison pulled out two years ago. The locals might have scavenged it, but the heavy equipment should still be there."

Scout took Rusty north at dawn. The terrain opened into scrubland, flat and exposed, with no cover for miles. She drove hard, pushing the walker's speed, watching the sky for flyers.

The depot was a cluster of concrete buildings surrounded by a chain-link fence. Most of the fence had collapsed. The buildings were intact, their windows dark.

She found the targeting computer in the repair bay, still crated, wrapped in paper and sealed with tape. She was loading it onto Rusty's cargo rack when she heard the engines.

Three scout flyers appeared over the ridge. Hargrave's logo was visible on their wings, black against the grey metal.

Scout vaulted into the cockpit and slammed the canopy closed. The flyers were already banking for a strafing run.

"Go," Lena said. "Do not engage. They will call for reinforcements."

Scout opened the throttle. Rusty ran. The flyers opened fire, bullets stitching the dust around her feet. One round glanced off the cargo rack. The targeting computer shifted but held.

She reached the canyon mouth and dove into the cover of the walls. The flyers circled twice, then turned back.

Scout sat in the cockpit, breathing hard. "That was close."

"Close does not count. The targeting computer is secure. Get it back to Vega."

Chapter 21: Lena's Deterioration

Vega installed the targeting computer and calibrated the cannon. He spent a full day aligning the optics, adjusting the servos, testing the tracking system. When he was done, Scout watched the targeting reticule lock onto a distant rock formation, tracking it as if welded to it.

"Now you can hit the Judge's coolant intake from three hundred meters," Vega said. "Maybe four hundred, depending on wind and elevation."

"Good."

That night, Scout sat in Rusty's cockpit, running system checks by the dim light of the gauges. Lena's voice was faint, thinner than it had been.

"Lena? You are quiet."

"I am tired."

The words sent a chill through Scout. "Tired how?"

"My code is fragmenting. The neural map was not designed for long-term storage. The memories are starting to decay. I lose pieces every day now."

"How long do you have?"

"A few weeks. Maybe less. It is hard to tell. The decay is accelerating."

Scout leaned back in the seat. The stars were visible through the canopy, cold and distant. She thought about what it meant to fade, to feel yourself dissolving into nothing.

"Can Vega fix you?"

"No. The damage is cumulative. Every day, I lose a little more. Some memories are already gone. I cannot remember my mother's face."

"I am sorry."

"Do not be. I have been dead for seven years. This is just the last part of dying."

Scout closed her eyes. The static filled the cockpit.

"I will make this count, Lena. I promise."

"I know you will."

Chapter 22: The Recruitment

Vega sent runners into the badlands. The message was simple. A mechanic with a walker is fighting Hargrave. Come if you want to hit back.

They came.

The first was a man named Cruz, a driller whose family had been taken to Hargrave's labor camps. He arrived with a hunting rifle and a pack full of dynamite. His eyes were hard and his hands were steady.

The second was a woman named Yuki, a former Hargrave pilot whose walker had been repossessed when she refused an order to fire on civilians. She knew the patrol routes, the supply schedules, the radio codes.

Then came a family of homesteaders whose fields had been burned, driving a truck loaded with children and supplies. A pair of brothers whose father had been shot by Hargrave's security. A young radio operator who had heard Scout's broadcast and walked forty miles through the desert with nothing but a canteen.

Within a week, the numbers had grown from fifty-three to nearly a hundred.

Scout stood on the refinery's roof and looked at the crowd below. They were not soldiers. They were not warriors. They were farmers and drillers and mechanics and mothers. But they were angry, and anger was a weapon.

"Vega says you are planning to hit the Bitter Springs refinery," Cruz said.

"I am."

"That is insane."

"Probably. Are you in?"

Cruz smiled. "I am in."

Chapter 23: The Plan

Vega spread the map across the table. Bitter Springs was Hargrave's primary refinery in the basin, a sprawling complex of tanks, towers, and processing plants that covered fifty acres. And at its center, the Iron Judge's charging station, a scaffold of steel and cable.

"The Judge docks here every three days for a full recharge," Vega said. "The charging cycle takes six hours. During that time, the walker is connected to the refinery's power grid by a heavy cable."

"Cut the cable, and the Judge is stranded," Scout said.

"More than stranded. If you cut the cable during a charging cycle, the power surge could damage the Judge's core systems. It would take days to repair, maybe weeks. Time we need."

Yuki pointed at the map. "The perimeter is guarded by twelve sentry walkers. They patrol in pairs, rotating shifts. The refinery has anti-walker gun emplacements on the south and west walls."

"How do we get past them?"

"We do not go past them. We go through them." Scout traced a route along the map. "The diversion hits the fuel depot here. While they are responding, I take Rusty through the north fence. Vega, you hit the charging cable with a cutting charge. Everyone else covers the escape."

"And if the Judge activates before we can disable it?" Yuki asked.

"Then we run. And hope we are fast enough."

Chapter 24: The Deserter

The deserter arrived on the night before the attack.

He was a Hargrave pilot, young, with a shaved head and eyes that would not meet anyone's gaze. He had flown a light scout walker out of the Bitter Springs garrison and surrendered at the edge of the refinery town, his hands raised, his walker's weapons powered down.

Vega brought him to Scout. "He says he has information."

"What kind of information?"

The deserter swallowed. He was shaking. "The Judge's patrol schedule changed. Hargrave knows you are coming. Someone in your camp talked."

Scout's blood went cold. "Who?"

"I do not know. But he moved the Judge's recharge cycle forward. It will be fully charged by midnight tomorrow. If you attack at dawn, it will be ready. It will be waiting."

The plan was broken before it had begun.

"You knew this and came anyway?" Scout asked.

"I came because I do not want to die for him. I came because I want to be on the right side."

Scout studied him. His fear was real. His shame was real.

"Then you fight with us."

"I will."

She turned back to the map. The plan had to change. She had hours, not days.

Chapter 25: The March Begins

They moved at dusk, a column of a hundred people stretched across the alkali flats. The walkers led the way — Rusty, plus two others that Yuki and a man named Ortega had salvaged and repaired from Vega's spare parts. The rest walked on foot, carrying rifles and tools and improvised weapons.

Scout led from Rusty's cockpit, her eyes on the horizon. The sun set behind them, painting the sky in shades of red and orange. The wind picked up, carrying the smell of dust and distant smoke.

"Check in," she said over the radio.

"Rusty Two, ready." Yuki's voice, steady.

"Rusty Three, ready." Ortega.

"Ground team, ready." Vega.

Scout opened the throttle. The column moved faster, the walkers' legs pounding the hard earth, the people jogging behind.

"The timing has to be perfect," Lena said.

"It will be."

"You sound confident."

"I sound like someone who has nothing left to lose."

The lights of Bitter Springs appeared on the horizon, a glow of refinery fire and electric lamps that stained the sky orange. The Iron Judge stood at the center of it, a dark tower against the stars, its hull catching the dim light.

Scout checked her cannon. The targeting computer hummed. The ammunition was loaded.

"Rusty," she said. "Let us go to war."

Chapter 26: The Refinery at Dawn

Scout crouched behind a ridge, studying the refinery through Rusty's optics. The Iron Judge was docked at its charging station, steam curling from its vents in lazy plumes. The cable was visible, a thick black line running from the walker's back to the refinery's main power junction.

"Charging cycle is at sixty percent," Lena said. "We have maybe two hours before it is fully operational."

"Then we do not wait for dawn."

Scout keyed the radio. "Change of plan. We go now."

"But the diversion—" Vega started.

"Will happen in five minutes. Move everything forward."

She engaged Rusty's legs and crested the ridge before anyone could argue. The walker's engines roared.

The sentry walkers saw her immediately. Their cannons swung toward her. The first shell exploded twenty feet to her left, spraying her with gravel and shrapnel that pinged off Rusty's armor.

Scout fired back. The cannon round caught the nearest sentry in the chest. It stumbled, recovered, and returned fire.

"Vega, I need that explosion now."

The fuel depot erupted. A fireball rose into the night sky, casting the entire refinery in orange light. The sentry walkers hesitated, their programming torn between the intruder and the emergency.

Scout used the moment. She ran Rusty straight at the fence.

Chapter 27: The Diversion

Vega's explosion ripped through the fuel depot. Tanks collapsed in sequence, each one adding to the fireball. Burning fuel spread across the storage yard, igniting everything it touched. Alarms blared across the refinery, a chorus of sirens that echoed off the metal buildings.

Hargrave's forces scrambled. The sentry walkers turned toward the fire, their programming overriding. The anti-walker guns swiveled, tracking the smoke, trying to find targets in the chaos. Men ran between the buildings, shouting orders that no one could hear.

Scout hit the fence at full speed. Rusty's legs crushed the chain-link. She was inside.

The charging station was two hundred meters ahead. The Iron Judge loomed over everything, its hull dark and silent, steam rising from its joints.

"Fifty meters to the cable," Lena said.

Scout pushed harder. The left knee joint groaned. The coolant temperature was climbing into the red.

A sentry walker emerged from between two storage tanks. Scout fired without slowing. The round missed, but the sentry was forced to dodge, buying her precious seconds.

She reached the charging cable. The line was as thick as her arm, armored in rubber and steel mesh. It hummed with power.

"Vega, I am at the cable. Where are you?"

"Thirty seconds out. Hold position."

Thirty seconds was an eternity in a war zone.

Chapter 28: Rusty vs. the Sentries

The sentries came from both sides. Three of them, moving in formation, their cannons tracking her position, their footfalls synchronized. They had been designed for this kind of combat.

Scout moved Rusty behind the charging station's support pillar. The first shell hit the pillar, spraying concrete. The second went wide, exploding against a storage tank.

She leaned out and fired. The round caught the lead sentry in the knee joint. The walker stumbled, its leg buckling, and it went down hard, sending up a cloud of dust.

The other two spread out, trying to flank her. They were faster than she had expected, their movements coordinated.

"Lena, I need speed."

"I can give you speed. But you will not like the cost."

"What cost?"

"The core will overheat. If I push the actuators past their limits, we will have minutes before the system shuts down. Maybe less."

"Do it."

Rusty's systems surged. The hydraulics responded with new force. The gyro hummed at a higher pitch. Scout felt the machine come alive beneath her.

She stepped out from behind the pillar and charged.

The sentries opened fire. She weaved, using speed they could not match. The first shot passed behind her. The second grazed Rusty's shoulder, tearing away a chunk of armor plate.

She closed the distance and fired at point-blank range. The sentry's cockpit exploded.

The last sentry turned to run. Scout did not let it.

Chapter 29: Lena Takes Control

Rusty moved faster than Scout had ever felt it move.

The controls were still in her hands, but the responses were not hers. Lena was augmenting every command, smoothing out the rough edges, predicting the movements of enemies before they made them. The machine responded as if it were alive.

"This is what it was supposed to be," Lena said. "Pilot and machine. One mind. No lag between thought and action."

"Then why did Hargrave cancel the program?"

"Because he could not control it. A pilot who truly merges with a walker does not need commands. They become the machine. And a machine cannot be ordered to commit murder if its pilot refuses. He wanted weapons, not partners."

Scout brought Rusty around the corner of a processing tower. The charging cable was twenty meters ahead. Vega was there, cutting through the armor with a torch, sparks cascading down.

"Hurry," Scout said.

"Almost through. Give me ten seconds."

The Iron Judge's hull made a sound like a tuning fork. A deep hum that vibrated through the ground, through Rusty's legs, through Scout's bones.

"It is powering up," Lena said.

"We are out of time."

Chapter 30: The Core Chamber

The charging cable snapped. The end whipped across the ground, sparking and hissing like a snake. The Iron Judge's hum dropped, then rose again as backup generators engaged, deeper now, more urgent.

"Backup power," Vega said. "It has internal reserves. You have maybe ten minutes before it can move. Maybe less."

"Then I need to get inside."

"Inside where?"

"The Judge. There is a maintenance hatch on the lower chassis. If I can reach the core chamber, I can disable it manually. Lena showed me the schematics."

Vega stared at her, his face illuminated by the fire behind him. His eyes were wide. "That is suicide. You will never make it out."

"Probably."

She turned Rusty toward the Iron Judge's massive leg. The hatch was there, just above the ankle joint, a small access panel she would have missed if Lena had not told her where to look.

She set Rusty to standby and climbed out of the cockpit. The heat from the Judge's exhaust was intense, pressing against her face like a physical force. The ground vibrated with the growing power of its systems.

She reached the hatch and pried it open with her crowbar. The tunnel beyond was dark and narrow, barely wide enough for her shoulders.

She crawled inside.

Chapter 31: Hargrave's Response

The interior of the Iron Judge was a cathedral of machinery. Pipes and cables ran along the walls in organized bundles. The central core pulsed with a blue light that illuminated the narrow service corridor in rhythmic flashes.

Scout moved as fast as she could, following the signs that led to the primary reactor chamber. The walker was waking around her. Pumps engaged. Valves opened. The floor vibrated with increasing intensity.

"Where are you?" Lena's voice came through the headset, thin and distant, barely reaching her.

"Corridor C, heading toward the primary reactor."

"You are three minutes from the core chamber. But Hargrave is there."

Scout stopped. "How do you know?"

"I can feel him. The Judge's systems are integrated. He is in the command cockpit, running final checks. I can sense his presence through the neural link."

Scout pressed on. The corridor opened into a cavernous space. The reactor sat at the center, a column of blue fire contained in a glass tube taller than a man. Pipes radiated from it like the ribs of a great beast.

And standing at the control panel was Baron Hargrave.

He turned when she entered. He was older than she had expected, white-haired, wearing a black uniform with gold insignia. His face was calm, almost bored.

"You must be the mechanic."

"I am Scout March."

"I know who you are. I also know that walker of yours is carrying something that belongs to me."

Scout raised the crowbar. "Lena does not belong to you."

"Lena is dead. What is left is property. Corporate assets."

Chapter 32: The Awakening

Hargrave pressed a button on the control panel. The reactor flared. The floor shook. The Iron Judge's systems roared to life around them.

"You should not have come here," he said. "Now you will die with your machine."

Scout lunged. The crowbar swung. Hargrave sidestepped and grabbed her arm, twisting it with surprising strength. The crowbar clattered to the floor.

He was stronger than he looked. He forced her to her knees, his hand locked around her wrist.

"I built this walker. I built the Ghostwalker program. Everything you have used against me came from my designs, my money, my vision."

He pulled a pistol from his belt and pressed it against her temple. The metal was cold.

"Lena made her choice. She chose to betray me. And now you will make yours. You can die here, or you can tell me how to recover her from that machine."

Scout's eyes found the reactor core. The glass tube. The blue fire.

"Lena," she whispered.

The reactor flared.

Hargrave looked up, startled. The core was pulsing, faster than it should. The temperature gauges were climbing.

"What are you doing?" he shouted.

The answer came through every speaker in the chamber.

"What I should have done seven years ago."

Lena's voice was everywhere.

Chapter 33: Running the Gauntlet

The Iron Judge shuddered. The lights flickered and died, then came back as emergency power kicked in. Hargrave stumbled, his grip loosening, and Scout twisted free.

She ran.

Behind her, the reactor was going critical. The alarms were screaming, a wall of sound that filled the corridors. She dove through the maintenance hatch and rolled onto the ground outside, landing hard on her shoulder.

The Iron Judge was moving. Its legs were unlocking, its arms rising. Steam vented from every seam, enveloping the lower chassis in white fog.

Scout ran for Rusty. The walker was where she had left it, cannon still trained on the Judge. She scrambled up the ladder and dropped into the cockpit, slamming the canopy closed.

"Lena, what did you do?"

"I am overloading the Judge's reactor from inside. You have to get clear."

"What about you?"

"Rusty's core cannot survive the blast radius. But neither can the Judge. It is the only way."

Scout engaged the throttle. Rusty ran, legs pumping, engine screaming. The Judge was rising behind her, its full height now visible, a walking skyscraper of armor and weapons.

"Lena, get out of there."

"I cannot. This is the only way. I am sorry."

The blast hit.

Chapter 34: Lena's Sacrifice

The explosion was white and absolute.

Rusty was thrown forward by the shockwave. Scout fought the controls, keeping the walker upright, skidding across the alkali flats on one knee before the gyro caught and straightened them. Debris rained down around her, chunks of metal and concrete that thudded into the ground. A piece of the Iron Judge's leg slammed into the earth twenty meters away, digging a trench in the packed dirt.

She brought Rusty to a stop and turned to look.

The Judge was falling. The walker that had dominated the horizon was collapsing, its legs folding, its torso crumpling under its own weight. It hit the ground with an impact that shook the earth, a sound like the world ending.

Smoke and fire rose from the wreckage in a column that reached the clouds. The refinery was burning. The fuel depot was a crater. The Iron Judge was a twisted pile of metal and fire.

"Lena?"

Static.

"Lena, answer me."

The radio was silent.

Scout sat in the cockpit, her hands on the controls, the static filling her ears. Lena was gone. The voice that had kept her company, that had guided her, that had been her friend — it was gone.

"I am sorry," she whispered.

The static did not answer.

Chapter 35: The Fall

Scout walked Rusty through the wreckage of the refinery. The Iron Judge lay on its side, a mountain of broken metal, still smoking, still sparking. Fires burned around it. The survivors of the attack were emerging from cover, their faces dazed, looking at the destruction with the hollow eyes of people who had survived something they did not fully understand.

Vega found her at the Judge's command section. His face was black with soot. "Hargrave ejected before the explosion. He is alive."

"Where?"

"He took a crawler east. Toward Promontory Peak, his estate. He has a fortified compound there."

Scout looked at the horizon. The sun was rising, thin and pale through the smoke. The day was new, and the war was not over.

"I am going after him."

"Scout, your walker is damaged. The core is—"

"I know."

She turned Rusty toward the east. The damaged leg dragged. The coolant was leaking in a steady stream. The cannon was dented, the targeting computer cracked.

But the machine still moved.

"Alone?" Vega asked.

"For now."

"You will need help."

"When I do, I will ask."

She opened the throttle. Rusty limped forward, each step a labor, the damaged leg leaving a trail in the dust.

The journey was not over. But the hardest part was behind her.

Chapter 36: The Aftermath

The journey to Promontory Peak took three days.

Rusty broke down twice. The first time, Scout spent six hours replacing a cracked hydraulic line with a length of rubber hose and wire, working by feel in the dark. The second time, the gyro failed and she had to realign it by hand, working blind, using only touch and the memory of Vega's instructions.

She did not sleep. She ate when she remembered to, drank when her mouth was too dry to swallow. The silence in the cockpit was unbearable.

"You are going to kill yourself before you reach him," a voice said.

She jerked upright. "Lena?"

"No. It is Vega. I am on the radio. I have been calling you for an hour."

"Sorry. I was not listening."

"Where are you?"

"Passing the old salt works. Maybe forty miles from Promontory."

"Scout, you cannot take Hargrave alone. His estate is fortified. He has a private army. At least a hundred men."

"I know."

"Then wait. We are coming. We have trucks. We have weapons. We have people who want to see this through."

Scout closed her eyes. Behind her eyelids, she saw Lena's face. Or what she imagined Lena's face to be.

"How long?"

"Two days."

"I will wait."

She found cover in a dry wash and shut down Rusty's systems. The silence was total. For the first time in weeks, she was completely alone.

Chapter 37: Rusty, Silent

She slept for twelve hours.

When she woke, the sun was high and the cockpit was hot as an oven. She sat up, her joints aching, her mouth dry, and checked the systems. Rusty's core temperature was stable. The coolant leak had slowed. The gyro was holding.

"Morning," she said.

The radio was silent.

She had known it would be. Lena's voice was dead. The fragmenting code had finally dissolved. There was nothing left but the memory of a woman who had died twice.

Scout climbed out and stretched her stiff limbs. The alkali flats stretched to every horizon, white and empty, cracked like old china. She sat on Rusty's leg and ate a can of cold beans, the metal spoon scraping against the tin.

She thought about the last thing Lena had said. This is the only way. It echoed in her mind, a refrain she could not silence.

"You were wrong," Scout said to the empty air. "There is always another way."

She finished the beans and climbed back into the cockpit. The walker's systems were green. She had two days to wait.

She spent them remembering.

Chapter 38: The Desolation

The waiting was the hardest part.

Scout sat in the cockpit and watched the sun cross the sky, a slow arc from east to west. She checked the systems. She cleaned the cannon. She counted the remaining ammunition. She did it all again an hour later because there was nothing else to do.

The silence was a physical thing, pressing against her ears. Every time she reached for the radio to ask Lena a question, she remembered that Lena was gone. The movement was automatic, a habit her hand had learned, and every time she caught herself she felt the loss fresh.

On the second day, she saw dust on the horizon.

She raised the cannon. The dust resolved into a convoy of trucks, six of them, moving in formation. She lowered the weapon when she recognized the lead vehicle.

Vega was in the lead truck. He climbed out when they stopped, his face grey with exhaustion, his clothes stained with grease.

"We brought everyone who could fight," he said.

"How many?"

"Eighty. Plus ammunition. Plus a few surprises I cooked up in the workshop."

Scout looked at the trucks. The faces of the people inside them. Farmers. Drillers. Mechanics. Survivors. They had come because she had asked.

"Eighty is enough."

She climbed back into Rusty and keyed the radio.

"Promontory Peak is twelve miles east. Hargrave is there with whatever forces he has left. We hit him at dawn."

The radio crackled with acknowledgments.

Scout looked at the horizon. The mountain was visible now, a dark shape against the fading light.

Tomorrow, she would finish this.

Chapter 39: The Spark

The night before the attack, Scout could not sleep.

She sat in Rusty's cockpit, her hand on the controls. The machine was cold. Silent. Dead. The gauges were dark, the systems powered down to conserve fuel.

"One more fight," she said. "Then we are done."

No answer.

She closed her eyes. She thought about Lena. About the voice that had come out of the static. About the woman who had been trapped in a machine for seven years, who had given everything so that Scout could finish the fight.

"I am not ready to let you go."

The static pulsed.

Scout's eyes snapped open. The radio was on. The static was there.

"Lena?"

The static pulsed again. A pattern. A rhythm. Not random. Intentional.

It was not a voice. It was not words. But it was something. A spark. A fragment. A remnant of the woman who had been there.

"Are you still there?"

The static settled. And then, faint as a whisper, a word.

"Here."

Scout pressed her hand to the radio. "I am coming back for you. After this is over, I will find a way to fix you."

The static pulsed once. Affirmation. Or agreement. Or wishful thinking on her part.

It was enough.

Chapter 40: Hargrave's Last Card

Promontory Peak was a fortress.

The mansion was built into the side of the mountain, its walls of stone and steel that had been imported at enormous cost. Anti-walker guns were mounted on every parapet, their barrels dark against the sky. Searchlights swept the approaches in regular patterns. A wall twenty feet high surrounded the compound.

Scout studied it through Rusty's optics, counting the gun emplacements, memorizing the searchlight patterns.

"He has been preparing for this," Vega said over the radio. "The estate was designed as a final redoubt. It can hold off a regiment."

"How do we get in?"

"There is a drainage tunnel on the north face. It opens into the maintenance level. It will be tight, but a walker could fit, barely."

Scout marked the position on her map. "That is our way in."

"Scout, that tunnel will be a kill box. If they know we are coming—"

"They will know. But they will not know where."

She turned to face the gathered fighters. Men and women stood in the darkness, their weapons ready, their faces set.

"We hit every gate at once. Make them spread thin. While they are reacting, I take Rusty through the tunnel."

"And when you get inside?"

"I find Hargrave. And I end this."

Chapter 41: The March to Promontory

They attacked at three in the morning.

The first wave hit the front gate, a truck filled with fighters who opened fire on the guard posts with rifles and improvised rockets. The second wave hit the south wall, using dynamite to breach the barrier and disabling the anti-walker guns with precision shots.

Scout waited in the darkness of the north face, watching the searchlights swing away from her position as the attack drew the defenders' attention.

"Now," Vega said.

She engaged Rusty's legs. The walker moved through the shadows, keeping low, using the terrain for cover. The damaged leg dragged, but she compensated with the throttle.

The drainage tunnel entrance was a concrete arch covered by a rusted grate. Scout tore it away with Rusty's hand and stepped into the darkness.

The tunnel was narrow. Rusty's shoulders scraped the walls, sending sparks from the armor. The water was ankle-deep and smelled of chemicals and decay.

"Fifty meters to the maintenance level," she said.

"Clear," Vega replied. "The defenses are occupied. You have a window."

She pushed forward. The tunnel sloped upward. Light appeared ahead, a grate in the ceiling.

She stopped beneath it and listened. Footsteps above. Voices. Two guards. Maybe three.

She raised Rusty's hand and pushed the grate open.

Chapter 42: The Siege

Scout emerged into a maintenance bay. Two guards turned, their eyes wide, their mouths opening to shout. She swept them aside with Rusty's arm, sending them crashing into the wall. They did not get up.

The bay opened into a corridor lined with pipes and cable trays. She followed it, moving fast, ignoring the alarms that were now screaming through the building.

"Hargrave is in the central command room," Vega said. "Top floor. He has a dozen men with him. Security, loyalists, his inner circle."

"I am on my way."

The corridor ended at a staircase. Too narrow for Rusty. Scout set the walker to standby and continued on foot, the crowbar in her hand.

She climbed three flights. At the top, a pair of guards waited. They raised their rifles. She threw the crowbar. It spun through the air and caught the first guard in the face. She closed the distance before the second could fire and drove her fist into his throat.

She picked up the crowbar and kicked open the door.

The command room was a circular space with windows on every side. Hargrave stood at the center, surrounded by maps and radios, his white uniform spotless.

"You are persistent," he said.

"You are out of time."

Chapter 43: Lena's Final Words

Hargrave raised a remote detonator. "The entire estate is wired. If I press this button, we all go. You, me, everyone in this building."

Scout did not lower the crowbar. "Then press it."

"You would die too."

"I have nothing left to lose."

Hargrave's hand trembled. The detonator wavered in his grip.

"You do not have the courage," Scout said. "You never did. You built machines to fight for you. You sent other people to die for you. But when it comes to facing someone yourself, you are a coward."

Hargrave's face reddened. He pressed the button.

Nothing happened.

"My people cut the wiring an hour ago," Scout said. "You are done."

She stepped forward and struck him across the face with the crowbar. He crumpled.

In the sudden silence, Scout heard a voice.

"Goodbye, Scout."

It came from the radio on Hargrave's desk. Lena's voice. Faint. Distant. Almost gone.

"Lena?"

"I am sorry. I cannot stay."

"Where are you?"

"Everywhere. Nowhere. The overload damaged Rusty's core. I have minutes. The fragment you heard was the last of me."

Scout knelt beside the radio. "I will find another way. Vega can—"

"There is no other way. I am ready. I have been ready for seven years."

The voice softened.

"You gave me something I did not have in seven years. A purpose. A friend. Thank you, Scout."

The static settled.

Lena was gone.

Chapter 44: The Betrayal from Within

The door opened. A man in a Hargrave security uniform stepped in, his hands raised, his eyes scanning the room before settling on Scout.

"I am not here to fight," he said. "My name is Dillard. I am Hargrave's second-in-command."

Scout stood, the crowbar ready, her muscles tensed to strike. "You picked a bad time to defect."

"I picked the right time. I have evidence. Banking records. Orders. Signed documents linking Hargrave to the labor camps, the massacres, the Ghostwalker program. Everything you need to put him away forever."

"Why should I trust you?"

"Because I have a gyrocopter on the roof. And the federal marshals are waiting in Pueblo. I can have Hargrave there in four hours. You can end this without another death."

Scout studied him. His eyes were clear. His hands were steady. He was either telling the truth or he was the best liar she had ever met.

"Why now?"

"Because I have a daughter. And I do not want her to grow up knowing her father worked for a monster."

Scout lowered the crowbar. "Get him out of here."

Dillard nodded. He hauled Hargrave to his feet and dragged him out.

Scout stood alone in the command room. The windows showed the dawn breaking over the mountains.

The war was over.

Chapter 45: Scout vs. Hargrave

She found Rusty in the maintenance bay, cold and dark.

The core was dead. The cockpit was empty. The machine that had carried her through the war was a shell, its systems silent, its weapon cold.

She climbed inside anyway. She sat in the cracked leather seat that had molded to her shape over the weeks of fighting. She put her hands on the controls, the metal worn smooth by Lena's ghost hands before hers.

"Thank you," she said.

The gauges were dark. The radio was silent. But she felt it. A warmth. A presence. The ghost of a ghost.

She did not know if Lena had ever really been there. Maybe it had been her own mind, her own need to hear a voice in the silence, that had created the woman in the machine. Maybe the stress and the grief and the loneliness had manufactured a friend where there was only static.

It did not matter.

Lena had been real. The friendship had been real. The fight had been real. Whatever combination of electricity and memory and wishful thinking had produced that voice, it had given her the strength to keep going.

Scout sat in the cockpit until the sun was fully up. Then she climbed out and walked away.

The walker stood in the morning light, scored with rust, scarred by battle. It looked like a monument.

It looked like a tomb.

Chapter 46: The Choice

The federal marshals arrived at noon. They came in two gyrocopters, their rotors beating the air, landing on the roof of the estate. Dillard had kept his word.

Scout watched them take Hargrave away. The oil baron was in chains, his white uniform dirty, his face bruised from the crowbar. He did not look at her as they loaded him onto the aircraft. He looked at the ground, already a defeated man.

A marshal approached her. "Ms. March. We have heard your name on a great many radio broadcasts. We have questions about everything that has happened."

"I have answers."

"About the Ghostwalker program. The labor camps. The massacres at Red Mesa and Sutter's homestead."

"Everything. I will tell you everything."

The marshal nodded. "You will need to testify. It could take weeks."

"I understand."

She walked to the edge of the roof and looked at the territory below. The badlands. The refinery. The ruins of the Iron Judge, still smoking on the horizon. The world that Hargrave had built and she had helped tear down.

"What happens now?" Vega asked, coming to stand beside her.

"Now we build something better."

"How?"

"One well at a time. One community at a time. With the oil rights Hargrave stole returned to the people he stole them from."

Vega looked at her. "You have a plan."

"I have a beginning."

Chapter 47: The Arrest

Hargrave's trial lasted six weeks. Scout testified for three days.

She sat in the witness box and told them everything. About the Ghostwalker program. About the neural mapping. About Lena Voss, the first test pilot, who had been killed and preserved and kept alive inside a machine for seven years.

She told them about the labor camps. The massacres. The burning fields. The families who had lost everything.

The courtroom was silent when she finished. The jurors did not look at Hargrave. They looked at her.

The jury deliberated for four hours. They found Hargrave guilty on seventeen counts, including murder, fraud, and crimes against the territorial code. The sentence was life in prison without parole.

Scout was standing on the courthouse steps when the verdict was read. The sun was bright. The air was clean.

"It is over," Vega said.

"It is never over. But it is different."

She walked down the steps and into the street. People were waiting for her. The survivors of Red Mesa. The families from the co-op. The fighters from the refinery.

They cheered.

Scout did not feel like celebrating. She felt like sleeping. She felt like crying. She felt like sitting in a quiet room and remembering.

But she smiled. Because they needed her to smile. Because Lena would have wanted her to.

Chapter 48: Lena's Goodbye

Scout went back to Rusty one last time.

The walker was still in the maintenance bay at Promontory Peak, untouched since the battle. Dust had settled on the canopy. The rust had spread, orange blooms creeping across the metal.

She sat in the cockpit. She closed her eyes.

"I do not know if you can hear me," she said. "I do not know if any part of you is still here. But I need to say this."

She took a breath.

"You saved me. When I was alone, you were there. When I was scared, you gave me courage. When I did not know what to do, you showed me the way."

Her voice cracked.

"I do not know who I am without you. But I will figure it out. Because that is what you would want. You would want me to keep going."

She opened her eyes. The gauges were dark. The radio was silent.

But in the silence, she felt something.

A warmth. A presence. A goodbye.

She climbed out and walked away.

Behind her, the walker stood in the shadows, rust and memory, the last remnant of a woman named Lena Voss.

Chapter 49: The Future

The Hargrave refineries were seized by the federal government and reorganized into a cooperative, the profits distributed among the communities that had suffered under his rule. Scout was offered the position of operations director. She accepted.

She moved into a small house in Pueblo, the first real home she had ever owned. It had a garden with a cottonwood tree. It had a porch with a rocking chair. It had a workshop in the back where she tinkered with engines and radios and machines that needed fixing.

Vega moved to the town next door. He opened a repair shop that specialized in walker hydraulics. He and Scout had dinner once a week, and they never talked about the war. They talked about machines, about the weather, about the future.

Yuki took over the training program for new walker pilots, teaching them to respect the machines, to treat them as partners, not weapons.

The survivors of Red Mesa built a new settlement on the site of the old co-op camp. They called it Lena.

Scout went there once, to see the name on the sign. She stood in the street and looked at the houses, the children playing, the well pumping oil.

"It is a good name," she said.

She walked back to her truck and drove home.

Chapter 50: Rusty at Rest

She had Rusty moved to a ridge overlooking the plains.

It took a heavy transport truck and a crew of six to haul the walker up the hill. They set it on a concrete platform, facing east, its cannon removed, its arms at its sides. From this height, you could see for miles in every direction.

Scout spent a day cleaning it. She scrubbed the rust. She polished the canopy until it shone. She replaced the hydraulic lines that had cracked and the armor plates that had buckled.

When she was done, the walker looked almost new.

She sat in the cockpit one last time. The seat was the same cracked leather. The controls were worn smooth. The radio was silent.

She keyed the mic.

"Goodbye, Lena."

She climbed down and walked to the edge of the ridge. The sun was setting, painting the sky in shades of orange and purple. The plains stretched out below, golden and endless.

The walker stood behind her, a silhouette against the sky.

She had come to the Territory with nothing but a toolbox and a broken machine. She had found a friend, fought a war, and won. She had buried the ghost and built a future.

She was twenty-nine years old. She was free.

She walked down the hill without looking back.

The wind rose. The dust swirled. And on the ridge above, the Rust Maiden stood guard over the land it had helped save.
