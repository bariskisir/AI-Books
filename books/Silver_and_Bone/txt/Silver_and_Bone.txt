Chapter 1: The Photograph

The man in the viewfinder was cheating. Mira knew it before she pressed the shutter. His hand rested on the hostess's hip like it belonged there. Through the lens she caught the faint shimmer behind his face — something moving beneath the skin, like oil on water. She lowered the camera. The shimmer vanished. The man was just a man again, ordering a martini. Mira had learned to trust what the camera showed her. The naked eye lied. The lens did not.

She snapped three frames. Evidence for the client. Standard work. But the shimmer stayed in her mind as she packed the Nikon into her bag and slipped out of the steakhouse. It had been years since she saw anyone that deeply glamoured. Most fae she encountered were surface-level — a tweak to a waiter's attention, a nudge to a doorman's memory. This was different. This was architecture.

She walked east toward the lake. The November wind cut through her coat. Behind her, the reflection in a parked SUV's window shifted wrong. She stopped. Turned. Nothing. Just traffic and steam rising from a grate.

She kept walking. The feeling of being watched did not leave.

Chapter 2: The Body

Detective Sergeant Cole Hart did not believe in magic. He believed in motive, opportunity, and forensic accounting. But the body in the alley did not fit any category he knew.

Mira stepped under the tape. The uniform tried to stop her. Hart waved him off.

"You wanted me to see this," Mira said.

"Wanted someone who sees what I don't." Hart nodded at the body. "Female, mid-twenties. No ID. Her skin — look at her skin."

Mira knelt. The woman's face was relaxed, almost peaceful. Her arms bore a pattern of silver tracery, like filigree pressed into the flesh. The marks glowed faintly in the sodium light.

"Contact burn," Mira said.

"What contacts silver like that?"

Mira did not answer. She recognized the pattern. It was a warding glyph. Summer Court work. Someone had pressed an iron-and-silver seal into the woman's skin and held it there until the flesh gave way.

"Case is yours," Hart said. "Off the books. You find what I can't, and I pretend I didn't ask."

Mira stood. The woman's eyes were half-open. Blue, with a ring of gold around the pupil that no mortal eye possessed.

Chapter 3: Neutral Ground

The Tipsy Hag was a dive in Pilsen that changed locations every few weeks. The fae who ran it did not trust landlords. Mira found the door between a laundromat and a shuttered bakery. She knocked twice, paused, knocked once.

Fish-faced man opened it. "Private tonight."

"Tell Finn Mira's here."

The door closed. She waited. The wind carried the sound of a train passing. The door opened again. Finn stood there in a rumpled sport coat, the same one he always wore. His eyes were the color of wet slate.

"You look tired," he said.

"Dead woman in an alley. Silver burns. Summer Court pattern."

Finn's smile died. He pulled her inside.

The bar was half-empty. Fae at the tables did not look at her directly. The ones who did let their gazes slide off her like water. Mira had that effect on them — her sight made them uncomfortable.

"Tell me about the burns," Finn said, pouring whiskey he would not drink.

"Prisoners in the old world," Mira said. "They used silver brands to mark fae who broke the truce."

Finn set the bottle down. "That's not a Summer Court execution. That's a warning. Someone is sending a message to someone who isn't listening."

Chapter 4: The War

Finn leaned across the table. The bar's gas lamps cast shadows that moved independently of the flames.

"The Summer Court has ruled for a thousand years," he said. "Mab does not tolerate dissent. But her son, Rowan, has been gathering power in the mortal world. Half-fae, exiles, renegades. He calls it the Autumn Court."

"And the woman in the alley?"

"A message to the neutrals. Anyone who does not pick a side becomes a warning."

Mira turned the whiskey glass in her hands. "Why am I just hearing about this now?"

"Because you are useful to Mab exactly as long as you pretend not to know." Finn's voice dropped. "She has known about your sight since you were a child. The night you saw your first fae — in the hospital, after the accident."

Mira's jaw tightened. She had never told anyone what she saw that night.

"The accident was not an accident," Finn said. "Mab arranged it. She wanted to see if the sight could be induced in a mortal. You were her experiment."

The bar's lamps flickered. Mira felt something cold settle in her chest.

Chapter 5: The Second Murder

Hart called at 3 AM. "You need to see this."

The second body was in a Humboldt Park playground, slumped against the slide. Male, early thirties. Same silver tracery on the arms, but this time the pattern extended up the neck. The face was frozen mid-scream.

"The knots are different," Mira said, crouching.

"Means what?"

"This one's Autumn Court. See the weave? The Summer pattern is straight lines. This is spiral work."

Hart shone his flashlight on the symbols. "So both sides are killing their own people?"

"Or someone wants us to think they are."

Mira traced the air above the spiral pattern without touching it. The glyphs were not just decorative. They told a story. She recognized three of the bindings: silence, blindness, binding. The killer had forced this man to watch his own death.

"These are not executions," Mira said. "They are interrogations. The killer is looking for something."

Hart's radio crackled. He stepped away to answer it. When he came back, his face was gray.

"That was the deputy chief. Case is being reassigned to a task force from the Fifth District. We are off it."

"Since when does the Fifth District take homicides?"

"Since someone with money made a phone call." Hart holstered his radio. "They know we are looking."

Chapter 6: The Prince

Mira found him waiting on the stoop of her office building, a man in his late thirties with autumn-leaf hair and eyes that held the amber of old honey. He wore a wool coat that cost more than her monthly rent.

"Miss Black."

"I know who you are."

Prince Rowan smiled. It did not reach his eyes. "Then you know I did not kill those people."

"I know your court's symbols were on the second body."

"My mother's people left the symbols. They are framing me. She wants an excuse to bring the full Summer Court into the mortal world and purge every half-fae she can find." He stood. "I am offering you a job. Find the real killer. Prove my innocence."

"Why would I trust you?"

"Because I am the only one telling you that you have a choice." He pressed a card into her palm. It was warm, as if he had been holding it for hours. "Think about it."

He walked away. Mira looked at the card. No address, no number. Just a single word: Rowan, embossed in gold leaf that seemed to move when she blinked.

Chapter 7: The Embassy

The entrance to the Summer Court's Chicago embassy was a boiler room in a closed-down printing plant on the Chicago River's south branch. Mira had passed it a hundred times. She had never seen it.

A woman in a tailored gray suit met her at the door. She did not introduce herself. She led Mira down a flight of stairs that extended far deeper than the building's foundation should allow.

The air changed. Warmer. Sweeter. The concrete walls gave way to worked stone, then to living rock veined with quartz.

The throne room was a cavern the size of a cathedral. Roots hung from the ceiling, each one threaded with tiny lights. Queen Mab sat on a throne of black oak, her face ageless and her hair the color of winter moon.

"Mira Black." Her voice carried no warmth. "You have been avoiding me."

"I did not know I was supposed to come."

"You have always been supposed to come." Mab leaned forward. "I made you. That night in the hospital, when you were twelve, bleeding on the table — I was there. I opened your eyes. You belong to me."

Mira felt the words land like stones.

Chapter 8: The Claim

Mab rose from her throne. She was taller than any human, her proportions wrong in ways the eye tried to correct and could not.

"You saw what no mortal should see," Mab said. "Your mother was dying. The surgeon had given up. I reached into the room and I touched your forehead. I gave you the sight so you would never forget what you owed."

"I owe you nothing."

"You owe me everything. Every fae you have spotted, every deal you have avoided, every client who paid you to catch a cheating spouse because you saw the glamour on their skin. That was my gift."

Mira's hands were steady. Her voice was not. "You killed a surgeon to do it. He was a good man. He had children."

"He was a tool. As you are a tool." Mab stepped closer. "The murders are inconvenient. I want them solved. I want the killer found and the blade destroyed. Then you will be free of my debt."

"What blade?"

"The one your killer is using. A blade forged from unalloyed iron and fae bone. It can kill anything — mortal, fae, half-blood. It is the only thing in this world that can kill me."

Mab's eyes held Mira's. "Find it, Mira Black. Or I will take back every gift I ever gave you. Including your sight."

Chapter 9: The Brand

Finn was in the back of the bar, slumped against the wall. His shirt was open. On his chest, a fresh brand the size of a fist — a circle crossed with a line. Iron work. The edges were charred and weeping.

"Finn."

"Don't." His voice was a rasp. "Don't touch it. Iron spreads."

She knelt beside him. "Who did this?"

"My own people. Mab's spymaster. They found the records of my meetings with you." He coughed. "I am marked now. Every fae will see it. I am a traitor. I have no court."

"We need to get you to a doctor."

"No doctor can treat iron sickness. I need time. I need cold water and silence and darkness." His hand found her wrist. "They will come for you next. Mab has made her play. You are no longer useful to her as a secret — you are useful as a hostage."

"Then we run."

"Where?" Finn's laugh was wet. "You cannot run from a court that owns every shadow."

Mira helped him stand. He was lighter than he should be, his bones hollow like a bird's.

Chapter 10: The Message

Mira walked Finn to her apartment, three blocks from the bar. He collapsed on the couch. She covered him with blankets and turned off the lights.

She sat in the kitchen with the door cracked, listening to his breathing. The iron burn was poisoning him slowly. She did not know how to stop it.

Her phone buzzed. Rowan's card lay on the table. She picked it up. The gold leaf was still warm.

She pressed her thumb to the embossed lettering. The card unfolded itself into a thin sheet of paper covered in handwriting.

"Summer Court moves against the neutrals tomorrow night. The blade is in the hands of a mortal. His workshop is beneath the El tracks on Western Avenue. Come alone. — R."

Mira read the note three times. The letters were fading as she watched, the paper dissolving into dust.

She looked at the clock. 4:47 AM. She had until nightfall.

She was not a pawn. She was the board.

Chapter 11: The Terms

Dawn broke gray over the lake. Mira had not slept. She sat at the kitchen table, a cup of coffee cold in her hands, watching Finn's chest rise and fall.

He was worse. The skin around the brand had turned gray. She pressed a cold cloth to his forehead and he did not stir.

Her phone rang. Hart.

"Talk to me," she said.

"I did some digging on those symbols. Sent photos to a professor at UIC — archaeology department. She called me back in twenty minutes, scared. Said the symbols are pre-Christian Celtic binding marks. Used to seal oaths between tribes."

"I know what they are."

"Then you know the third symbol on the second body — the spiral — it's a death oath. Whoever carved it was swearing something. A promise."

"To kill?"

"To finish something. The professor said the spiral means 'until it is done.' Whoever the killer is, they are not stopping until their task is complete."

A third body, Mira thought. There would be a third body.

"I need you to find out where the El tracks intersect with old industrial infrastructure," she said. "Western Avenue, below the line. Anything abandoned."

"I am off the case, Mira."

"I know. Do it anyway."

Hart was quiet. Then: "One hour."

Chapter 12: The Bindings

The UIC professor's name was Dr. Elaine Vasquez. She met Mira at a coffee shop near campus, her hands shaking around a paper cup.

"I should not have called the detective back," she said. "I should have deleted the photos and forgotten them."

"But you did not."

"No." Vasquez leaned forward. "Those symbols — they are not just bindings. They are a language. The person who carved them knew the old grammar. That takes years of study. Decades."

"A fae could have done it."

"A fae would not need to carve them into flesh. They would simply speak them." Vasquez's eyes were dark. "The carver is mortal. A human who learned fae magic."

Mira processed this. A mortal weapon-smith. A human who knew the old ways.

"Who teaches a human fae magic?"

"Someone who wants to start a war." Vasquez set down her cup. "Miss Black, be careful. The symbols on that body were not just bindings. They were a recipe. Instructions for forging something. I think the killer is not the end goal. I think the killer is making something. And when it is finished, everyone will know."

Chapter 13: The Neutrals

Mira found the neutral faction's leader in a pet store on Belmont. She had expected a warehouse or a hidden estate. Instead, the woman was buying crickets.

"Greta." Mira said her name like a question.

The woman turned. She was unremarkable — brown hair, brown eyes, a coat from Target. But her shadow did not match her body. It was larger, winged, wrong.

"You see me," Greta said.

"I see everyone."

"Then you know I am trying very hard to be invisible."

"The murders are targeting half-fae who refuse to choose a side. You are the most prominent neutral in the city. I think you are next."

Greta set the cricket container on the counter. "I know. I have known for weeks." She paid the cashier and walked out. Mira followed.

"Why have not you run?"

"Because there is nowhere the blade cannot reach. It was forged to kill fae. It was forged to kill me." Greta stopped on the sidewalk. "The weapon-smith is not fae. He is a half-blood, like me. But he was raised by mortals who taught him to hate what he is. The blade is his suicide note. He is dying of iron poisoning. He wants to take as many of us with him as he can."

Mira felt the shape of the puzzle click into place.

Chapter 14: The Morrigan

The crow landed on Mira's windowsill at dusk. It was larger than any crow she had ever seen, its feathers the blue-black of oiled steel. It watched her with eyes that held no animal blankness.

"Finn is dying," Mira said.

The crow tilted its head.

"Can you help him?"

The crow hopped inside. It grew. The feathers folded into a cloak. The beak shortened, the eyes shifted, and a woman stood in Mira's living room — tall, gaunt, dressed in black that seemed to drink the light.

"You bargained well," the Morrigan said. "Most mortals offer me something first."

"I did not bargain. I asked."

"Same thing." The Morrigan walked past her to Finn's sleeping form. She touched the brand. Finn gasped but did not wake. "The iron is deep. I can slow it. I cannot stop it."

"Do it."

The Morrigan pressed her palm to the wound. Smoke rose. Finn's body arched and then relaxed. The gray edges of the brand receded to red.

"He will live," the Morrigan said. "But the brand remains. He will always be marked."

"Thank you."

"Do not thank me. I collect my debts." The Morrigan turned. "The blade's name is Silver and Bone. It was forged in the space between one breath and the next, in the moment a dying fae cursed the world that rejected them. It will kill anything. It will unmake anything. And when it is finished, it will unmake its wielder too."

She was gone. The crow sat on the sill, watching.

Chapter 15: The Offer

The Morrigan's voice came from the crow's throat. "You want to stop the war. I want to watch something interesting. We can help each other."

Mira closed the window. The crow did not move.

"I am not making a deal with you," Mira said.

"You already have. I slowed the iron in your friend's chest. That is a debt."

"Then name your price."

"Information. When you find the blade, tell me before you destroy it. I want to see its maker's face when it breaks."

"Why?"

"Because I am old. I have watched empires burn and courts rise and fall. The only thing I have not seen is a mortal who built a god-killing weapon out of spite. I want to witness that moment."

Mira studied the crow. "And if I do not tell you?"

"Then your friend's wound will reopen, and I will not be there to close it again."

The crow launched itself into the dark. Mira watched it dissolve against the sky. She had made a deal with a thing older than the courts. She was not sure which was worse.

Chapter 16: The Weapon

The blade was buried in an armory beneath the Western Avenue El tracks. Mira found the entrance through a storm drain grate that had been cut and rewelded. The workshop was a single room, concrete walls, a forge that still held heat.

The weapon-smith was not there. But the blade was.

It rested on a steel table, wrapped in leather. She did not touch it. Even through the wrapping, she felt its presence — a cold that was not temperature, a weight that was not physical.

She pulled back a corner of the leather. The blade was silver-white, the edge impossibly thin. The tang was carved from bone, etched with the same spiral bindings she had seen on the bodies.

"Silver and Bone." She spoke the name aloud. The blade seemed to hum in response.

She heard footsteps. She wrapped the blade and hid behind the forge.

A figure entered. Tall. Hooded. Carrying a fresh leather roll of tools.

The killer.

Mira held her breath. The figure walked to the table, paused, and turned directly toward her hiding place.

"I know you are there, Mira Black. I have been waiting for you."

Chapter 17: The Smith

The hood fell back. The face beneath was young — mid-twenties, pale, with the kind of bone structure that belonged on a Celtic cross. His eyes were the dead giveaway. They held the same ring of gold that the victim in the alley had.

"You are the son of a fae," Mira said.

"And a mortal woman who died giving birth to me." He set down his tool roll. "She told my father she was pregnant. He told her to drown me. She refused. He had her killed."

"The fae are not sentimental about half-bloods."

"No. They are not." He picked up the blade. "This is my inheritance. A thousand years of Summer Court cruelty forged into a single edge. It can kill anything. It will kill Queen Mab. And then it will kill me, and I will have made the world cleaner."

"You are the killer. You carved those symbols into those people."

"Interrogations. I needed to know where Mab's throne room was. How to reach her." He turned the blade in his hands. "I am sorry about the people I hurt. But they chose their sides. I did not choose mine."

Mira stepped out from behind the forge. "You can choose differently."

"I am past choosing." He raised the blade. "And so are you."

Chapter 18: The Chase

He swung. The blade cut the air with a sound like a bell. Mira threw herself sideways, knocking over a rack of half-finished tools.

She scrambled for the door. The smith was fast — faster than any mortal. The blade's edge passed close enough to shear strands of her hair.

She hit the door frame and ran up the stairs. The El train rattled overhead, drowning everything in noise.

She burst onto the street. Traffic. Pedestrians. None of them could see the nightmare chasing her.

The smith emerged from the storm drain. His face was calm. He sheathed the blade and walked the other way, disappearing into the crowd.

Mira leaned against a lamppost, breathing hard. She had found the killer. She knew his face. And she had nothing to prove it with.

Her phone buzzed. Hart.

"I found something. That industrial site you asked about — the steel mill in Gary. It has been bought by a shell company. The deed traces back to a fae bank."

Mira straightened. "I know who the killer is. I need you to meet me at the mill."

"I am off the case, Mira."

"Then come as a friend."

Silence. Then: "Two hours."

Chapter 19: The Spymaster

A black sedan pulled up beside Mira as she walked toward her car. The door opened. The woman from the Summer Court embassy sat inside, gray suit, gray face.

"Get in."

"I am busy."

"You are about to be dead." The woman's eyes were flat. "Mab sent me to retrieve you. The smith has left the workshop. He is moving toward the Summer Court embassy. He intends to kill the Queen tonight."

"How do you know that?"

"Because I am her spymaster. I know everything that moves in this city." She opened the door wider. "Get in. Or stay here and die when the Autumn Court purges every mortal who knows about us."

Mira got in.

The car pulled away from the curb. The windows were tinted so dark she could barely see outside.

"Where are we going?"

"Somewhere safe. Mab's orders."

Mira watched the streetlights slide past. The route did not match any direction she knew. "This is not the way to the embassy."

"No. It is not."

The spymaster's hand moved. Mira saw the glint of a hypo-spray a moment before the needle hit her neck.

The world went soft at the edges, then dark.

Chapter 20: The Cage

Mira woke in a cell. Concrete walls. A single light bulb in a wire cage. The floor was dirt, and the air smelled of rust and old blood.

She sat up. Her head throbbed. Her wrists were bound with zip ties.

Footsteps. The spymaster appeared on the other side of a barred door.

"Where am I?"

"Mab's private holding. Beneath the embassy. The Queen wants you contained until the smith is dealt with."

"She is using me as bait."

"Yes." The spymaster's face showed no emotion. "The smith knows you are here. He will come for you. And when he does, Mab's guards will take him."

Mira tested the zip ties. "And if he kills me first?"

"Then you were always going to die. The Queen just moved the date forward."

The spymaster walked away. The lights dimmed. Mira heard water dripping somewhere in the dark.

She had been played. From the first moment she took that photograph, she had been a piece on someone else's board.

She began working the zip ties against the edge of the concrete floor.

Chapter 21: The Market

Mira's wrists were raw by the time the zip ties gave way. She stood, steadied herself against the wall, and examined the cell door. Steel, deadbolt, electronic lock on the outside.

She was not getting out through the door.

She examined the walls. The concrete was old, cracked in places. The cell had been built fast, not well. One corner had a gap where the wall met the floor — not quite wide enough for her body, but wide enough to see through.

She lay flat and peered into the gap. A crawlspace. Dirt floor. Pipes overhead. It ran at least twenty feet before turning.

She began to dig.

The dirt was loose. She widened the gap by inches, scraping her shoulders against the concrete edge. The smell of wet earth filled her nose.

She squeezed through. The crawlspace was tight. She crawled on her belly, following the pipes.

She heard voices above her. Fae speech, the language that sounded like wind through leaves. She could not understand the words, but she understood the tone — alarm. They had found her gone.

She pushed forward. The crawlspace opened into a larger tunnel, brick-lined, clearly older than the embassy above. A maintenance tunnel. Probably pre-dated the fae occupation.

She followed it toward a dim light.

Chapter 22: Underground

The tunnel led to the underground market. Mira had heard of it — a bazaar beneath Wicker Park where fae and half-fae traded goods that belonged in neither world. She had never seen it.

The market was a series of chambers lit by cold blue lamps. Stalls sold things she could not name: fruit that pulsed with internal light, fabrics that shifted color as she watched, bottles of liquid that seemed to contain miniature storms.

The vendors watched her pass. A half-fae with a scarred face beckoned her closer.

"You are the sight-seer."

"I am looking for a way out."

"There is no way out of the market. Only in." He gestured to a tray of rings. "But there are ways to change what you are. Wear one of these, and the fae will not see you. They will look past you, around you, through you. You will be nothing."

"How much?"

"Two years of your life."

Mira looked at the rings. They were beautiful — silver bands etched with the same spirals the smith used.

"I do not have years to spare."

"Everyone has years to spare. The question is whether you value them more than your freedom."

Mira walked on.

Chapter 23: The Craftsman

The underground market ended in a cul-de-sac of abandoned shops. At the far end, a light burned in a window. Mira approached.

The shop was a weapons forge. Iron blades hung on the walls, each one etched with binding symbols. A man worked at the anvil, his back to her.

"Exiled," he said without turning. "That is what they call me. I refused to make weapons for the Summer Court. They took my hands."

He straightened and turned. His hands were prosthetics — iron and brass, articulated like clockwork.

"You are the smith who taught the half-blood."

"I taught him the craft. I did not teach him hate." The smith set down his hammer. "He came to me two years ago, broken and burning. I gave him a purpose. I did not know he would forge a god-killer."

"Where is he now?"

"Heading for the Summer Court embassy. He means to finish what he started." The smith's metal fingers clinked as he flexed them. "But he will fail. Mab's guards are ready for him. They have been ready since the first body was found."

"Then why did she let the murders happen?"

"Because she needed a reason to bring her full forces into the city. The murders are her justification. The smith is her excuse. When he attacks the embassy, she will slaughter him and use his attack to declare war on the Autumn Court."

Mira saw the pattern whole. The smith was not the plan. He was the trigger.

Chapter 24: Finn

Mira found Finn sitting on a crate outside the weapons forge, wrapped in a coat that was not his. The brand on his chest was livid red but no longer spreading.

"The Morrigan's work," he said. "I woke up and she was gone. You were gone. The crow told me where to find you."

"The crow talks to you now?"

"The crow talks to everyone. Most people do not listen." He stood, wincing. "Mab is going to kill the smith and use his death as a declaration of war. If she succeeds, the Autumn Court will retaliate. The mortal world will be caught between them."

"We need to get to the smith before Mab does."

"No. We need to get to Rowan. He is the only one who can call off the Autumn Court's counter-strike." Finn's hand found her arm. "The war does not start when Mab kills the smith. It starts when Rowan hears about it and moves his forces. We need to stop the news from reaching him."

"How?"

"I am a traitor. The Autumn Court will not believe a word I say. But you — you are mortal. You have no side. Rowan might listen."

Mira shook her head. "I met Rowan. He wants me to find the killer. If I tell him the killer is about to die at Mab's hands, he will not care. He will use it anyway."

"Then we need to give him something else to care about."

Finn's eyes held hers. She did not like what she saw in them.

Chapter 25: The Reckoning

Mira and Finn emerged from the market through a storm drain on Milwaukee Avenue. The street was quiet. A light rain had started to fall.

"We need to split up," Finn said. "You go to the embassy. Warn the smith that he is walking into a trap. I will go to Rowan. Tell him to hold his forces."

"If the smith dies, the Autumn Court loses its reason to fight."

"The smith is not the reason. He is the excuse. Rowan has been building toward this war for years. He will fight with or without an excuse."

Mira looked at him. "What changed? You were Mab's spy. Now you are trying to stop her."

"Her spymaster branded me. That changes things." Finn's voice was flat. "I was useful to Mab as long as I was invisible. Now I am marked. I am nothing to her. So I am something to myself."

He turned and walked into the rain. Mira watched him go. She did not know if she would see him again.

She turned toward the river. Toward the Summer Court embassy. Toward the smith who was walking into his own grave.

Chapter 26: The Trap

Mira approached the printing plant from the river side. The building was dark. No guards at the door. That was wrong.

She circled the block. The side alley showed fresh tire tracks, still wet. A delivery van was parked near the loading dock, engine warm.

She climbed the fire escape to the roof. The door at the top was unlocked.

She moved through the building floor by floor. Empty. No fae, no guards, no spymaster.

The trap was not for the smith. The trap was for her.

She heard the voices from the basement — the throne room. She followed the stairs down.

The cavern was lit with torches. Queen Mab sat on her throne, flanked by a dozen guards. The smith knelt before her, his hands bound, the blade Silver and Bone resting across Mab's lap.

"Ah, Mira Black." Mab's smile was cold. "You arrived just in time to witness the end."

Chapter 27: The Judgment

"Your experiment," Mab said, gesturing at the smith, "has failed. He walked into my embassy expecting to find you in a cage. Instead, he found my entire guard. He surrendered without a fight."

The smith did not look at Mira. His head was bowed, his shoulders slack.

"He is just a boy," Mira said.

"He is a weapon. And weapons that turn on their makers must be destroyed." Mab lifted the blade. "This is the famous Silver and Bone. Forged from the curse of a dying fae and the bones of a traitor. It can kill me. It can kill any fae. And I will see it unmade before I let it fall into the hands of my enemies."

She set the blade down. "But first, the boy dies. And then the Autumn Court learns what happens to those who send assassins against me."

"You are using him as an excuse for war."

"Of course I am. That is what queens do."

Mab nodded to the guards. Two of them seized the smith and forced his head back. A third drew a silver knife.

Mira stepped forward. "Wait."

The guards paused. Mab raised an eyebrow.

"Give me one hour. Let me prove he was not sent by Rowan. Let me prove he acted alone."

"And if you cannot?"

"Then you kill him. And you start your war." Mira met Mab's eyes. "But if I can prove he was acting alone, then you lose your excuse. And you have to find another reason to attack your son."

Mab considered. The cavern was silent.

"One hour." Mab's voice was ice. "Use it wisely."

Chapter 28: The Truth

Mira knelt beside the smith. The guards stepped back but did not leave.

"Who taught you the bindings?" she asked.

"My father. Before he died."

"Your fae father?"

"My mortal father. He found my mother's books after she was killed. He spent twenty years learning the old language. He wanted revenge on the fae who took her."

"Then why are you the one holding the blade?"

The smith's eyes finally met hers. "Because he died before he could finish it. Cancer. The iron poisoning got him before the blade was complete. I finished his work."

"So the revenge is yours now."

"It was never revenge. It was justice. My mother was killed for loving a mortal. My father was killed for trying to avenge her. The Summer Court has been executing half-fae for centuries. Someone had to stop them."

Mira looked at Mab. The Queen's face was unreadable.

"Your mother was executed?"

"By Mab's order. She was a handmaiden in the Summer Court. She fell in love with a mortal musician. When she became pregnant, Mab had her drowned in the river."

The cavern was silent. Mab's expression did not change.

"The boy lies."

"He does not," Mira said. "And you know it."

Chapter 29: The Breaking

Mab's hand tightened on the blade. The guards tensed.

"You cannot kill him for the truth," Mira said. "Not in front of witnesses."

"I can do anything I want. I am the Queen."

"Then you are no better than the killer you condemn."

The words hung in the air. Mab's eyes flickered — the first crack in her composure Mira had ever seen.

"Release him," Mab said.

The guards stepped back. The smith stayed on his knees, confused.

"Get out of my sight," Mab said. "All of you. Take the blade. Take the boy. Leave Chicago. If I see either of you again, I will not be merciful."

Mira helped the smith to his feet. He was shaking.

She picked up the blade. It was heavier than she remembered. It hummed against her palm.

She walked out of the throne room without looking back.

Chapter 30: The Escape

The printing plant stairs had never felt so long. Mira half-carried the smith, the blade tucked under her arm. The weight seemed to grow with every step.

They reached the ground floor. The rain had intensified, drumming on the roof.

"A car," the smith said. "Behind the building. My father's truck."

They found it — a rusted Ford pickup, decades old. The keys were in the visor. Mira helped the smith into the passenger seat and slid behind the wheel.

She drove. No destination. Just away.

The smith watched the city slide past. "Why did you save me?"

"Because you are the only one who can unmake that blade."

"I cannot. The forge is destroyed. The materials are gone."

"Then we find another way."

The truck's headlights cut through the rain. Mira drove south, toward the steel mill in Gary. She did not know what she would find there. She only knew she had to keep moving.

The smith's breathing evened out. He had fallen asleep. The blade rested between them, cold and patient.

Chapter 31: The Mill

The steel mill outside Gary was a ruin of rust and broken glass. The gates hung open. Weeds grew through cracks in the concrete.

Mira parked the truck in the shadow of a cooling tower. The smith stirred.

"This is where my father worked," he said. "Before he got sick. He said the iron in this place was the purest in the Midwest. That was why he chose it."

"For the forge?"

"For the ending. He wanted to destroy the blade in the same place he learned to hate."

Mira picked up the blade. "Then we finish what he started."

The smith led her through the mill. The interior was vast — conveyor belts rusted solid, furnaces cold for decades. At the center, a single crucible still stood, ringed by control panels that had been dead for years.

"The main furnace," the smith said. "If we can get it hot enough, the blade will melt. Iron and bone both. Nothing survives that heat."

"How do we light a furnace that has been dead for thirty years?"

The smith smiled. It was a thin, tired smile. "My father left instructions. He knew someone would have to finish the work."

He walked to a panel and pulled open a hatch. Inside, a gas line, pristine and new. Someone had kept it maintained.

"Propane. Enough to bring the crucible to three thousand degrees." He looked at Mira. "It will take thirty minutes to reach temperature. When it does, we drop the blade in and we are done."

Mira nodded. The smith turned a valve. The pilot light caught. The furnace began to hum.

Chapter 32: The Warning

Hart's call came as the furnace reached temperature.

"Where are you?"

"Gary. The steel mill."

"Mira, listen to me. Rowan knows Mab let you go. He thinks you made a deal with her. He is moving his forces toward the mill."

"How does he know where I am?"

"Because everyone knows where you are. You are not hiding. You are driving a stolen truck through open country with a god-killing blade in the passenger seat."

Mira closed her eyes. "How long do I have?"

"Twenty minutes. Maybe less."

The furnace roared behind her. The smith stood at the crucible, sweat streaming down his face.

"I need more time."

"You do not have it." Hart's voice was quiet. "I am coming. I will do what I can. But I am one cop against a fae army."

"Then do not come. Stay safe."

"I stopped being safe the day I met you."

The line went dead. Mira slipped the phone into her pocket and walked to the crucible.

The blade was warm. Almost hot. It knew what was coming.

Chapter 33: The Arrival

The first sign of Rowan's arrival was the temperature drop. The air in the mill went cold. The furnace flame guttered and steadied.

Rowan walked through the main door as if he owned the building. He wore a long coat and carried no visible weapon. Behind him, a dozen fae in dark clothes fanned out in a semicircle.

"Mira Black." His voice carried across the mill. "You have something that belongs to me."

"The blade belongs to no one."

"It was forged by my subject, using materials stolen from my court. I am claiming it as property of the Autumn Court."

The smith stepped forward. "I am no one's subject."

"You are half-fae. That makes you my responsibility." Rowan's eyes were cold. "Give me the blade. I will make your death painless."

Mira positioned herself between Rowan and the furnace. The blade was in her hand. The heat from the crucible pressed against her back.

"You do not want the blade, Rowan. You want the war. The blade is just an excuse."

"The blade is the only thing that can kill my mother. I would be a fool not to take it."

"You would be a monster to use it."

Rowan's smile was thin. "Monster. Queen. What is the difference?"

Chapter 34: The Standoff

The Autumn Court fae spread wider, encircling the furnace. Mira counted them. Thirteen. Too many.

"The blade goes into the crucible," Mira said. "That is not negotiable."

"I cannot let you do that." Rowan stepped closer. "You have one minute to hand it over."

"Or what? You kill me? Then you have to explain to every neutral fae in the city why you murdered the woman who stopped a war."

"You stopped nothing. You delayed."

"I bought time."

Rowan stopped. His head tilted, birdlike. "Time for what?"

The answer came from behind him. Headlights. A car engine. The screech of tires on gravel.

Hart's unmarked sedan slid through the mill doors, blocking Rowan's retreat. Hart got out, gun drawn.

"Police. Nobody move."

Rowan laughed. "A mortal gun. Against fae."

"Against iron." Hart's voice was steady. "I did my homework. These bullets are cold iron. They will not kill you. But they will hurt. A lot."

Rowan's smile faded. He looked at Mira. "Clever."

"I learn fast."

The standoff held. The furnace roared. The blade hummed in Mira's hand.

Chapter 35: The Option

"The blade goes into the crucible," Mira said again. "Then you and your mother negotiate. Or you and your mother fight without it. Either way, the blade is gone."

Rowan studied her. "You would destroy the only leverage you have."

"Leverage for what?"

"For peace. If I have the blade, my mother has to bargain. If she has the blade, I have to bargain. Without it, we have no reason to talk."

"You did not have a reason before the blade existed. You had every reason. You just did not take it."

Rowan said nothing.

"You have a choice," Mira said. "Let the blade burn. Sit down with Mab. Find a way to stop killing each other. Or take the blade, start a war, and watch Chicago burn for a throne that has never made anyone happy."

The furnace heat was unbearable now. The blade was singing — a high, thin note that vibrated through Mira's bones.

"Move aside," Rowan said.

"No."

Rowan's hand twitched. His guards shifted. Hart raised his gun.

Mira turned and threw the blade into the crucible.

Chapter 36: The Melt

The blade hit the molten steel and did not sink. It floated, glowing white, the silver surface bubbling and blackening. The bone handle charred and crumbled.

The singing rose to a scream. Everyone in the mill covered their ears. The furnace flame turned blue, then white, then transparent.

The blade twisted. For a moment, it held its shape against the heat. The bindings on the surface flared — every oath and curse that had been forged into the metal.

Then it melted.

The song cut off. The blade ran into the steel like water, losing its form, becoming nothing but metal and heat.

Mira stepped back from the crucible. Her hands were shaking.

Rowan stared at the furnace. His face was unreadable.

"It is done," the smith said. "Silver and Bone is gone."

Rowan turned without a word and walked out of the mill. His guards followed. The cold air left with them.

Hart holstered his gun. "Well. That was something."

Mira did not answer. She was watching the furnace, waiting to see if the blade would reform. It did not.

Chapter 37: The Aftermath

Dawn broke over the steel mill. The furnace had cooled. The blade was gone. The smith sat against a wall, pale and exhausted.

"What happens now?" Hart asked.

"Now I make sure the truce holds." Mira looked at the smith. "And I make sure he gets somewhere safe."

"He is a killer."

"He was dying. He thought he had nothing to lose." Mira crouched beside the smith. "The blade is gone. You released it. You can release the hate too."

The smith's laugh was hollow. "You do not understand. That blade was the only thing keeping me alive. The iron in my blood — it was part of the forging. Without the blade, the poison spreads faster."

"How long?"

"A month. Maybe two."

Mira looked at Hart. He shook his head slowly.

"I am sorry," she said.

"Me too." The smith closed his eyes. "Can I stay here? Just for a little while?"

Mira stood. "We will be outside."

She and Hart walked to the mill doors. The sun was rising over the lake, turning the rust-red mills gold.

"What happens to you now?" Hart asked.

"Same thing that always happens. I go back to my office. I take photographs of cheating spouses. I wait for the next thing that finds me."

"And if the fae come back?"

"They will. They always do." Mira looked at the sky. A crow circled overhead. "But maybe not today."

Chapter 38: The Negotiation

The Tipsy Hag had never been so crowded. Fae from both courts packed the bar, Summer in the booths, Autumn at the tables, neutrals lining the walls. The air was thick with tension.

Mira stood at the center, between Mab and Rowan. Finn leaned against the bar, watching. The Morrigan sat in a corner booth, visible only to those who had the sight.

"One hour," Mab said. "I am giving you one hour of my time. Use it."

Rowan sat across from his mother. Their eyes held the same cold fire.

"Silver and Bone is gone," Mira said. "The weapon that could kill either of you no longer exists. That means you have no reason to keep fighting — and no reason to stop. You need to decide which one it is."

"The boy is right," Mab said. "Without the blade, we return to the status quo. The Summer Court rules. The Autumn Court rebels. Half-fae die in the middle."

"That is not a status quo. That is a war that never ends."

"It has worked for a thousand years."

"It has worked because you made sure it worked. You killed anyone who challenged it." Mira looked at Rowan. "And you built an army to overthrow her. Neither of you has ever tried another way."

"What other way is there?" Rowan asked.

"You share power. You create a council. You give half-fae a voice."

"Impossible," Mab said.

"Impractical," Rowan said.

Mira waited. The bar was silent.

The Morrigan stood. Every fae in the room went still.

"I have not spoken in a century," the Morrigan said. "But I will speak now. The mortal is right. The war between you has gone on long enough that even the oldest of us are tired of watching. End it. Or I will end it for you."

Chapter 39: The Binding

The Morrigan's words had the weight of a curse. The room held its breath.

"On the new moon," the Morrigan said, "you will meet at the neutral ground between your worlds. You will agree to a truce. You will seal it with blood and breath. Or I will bind you both to silence and stillness until the world forgets your names."

Mab's face was pale. Rowan's hands were fists.

"You cannot bind a queen," Mab said.

"I can bind anything that has a name. And I know both of your names." The Morrigan's eyes swept the room. "The choice is yours."

She sat. The spell broke. Fae began to murmur.

Mira looked from Mab to Rowan. "The new moon is six days away. Use them wisely."

She walked to the bar. Finn handed her a glass of whiskey.

"You just threatened the most powerful fae in the world into a peace negotiation," he said.

"I did not threaten them. She did." Mira nodded at the Morrigan's booth. It was empty. The crow sat on the back of the chair, preening its feathers.

"Same thing."

"No. The threat was hers. The opportunity is theirs."

Finn raised his glass. "To opportunities."

"To endings."

They drank.

Chapter 40: The Watch

Six days. Mira spent them watching.

She watched the Summer Court embassy, saw the comings and goings of messengers. She watched Rowan's safe houses, saw his lieutenants preparing for the negotiation. She watched the neutral fae, saw them choosing sides in whispers.

No more bodies appeared. The killing had stopped.

Hart called every day with updates. The task force had been disbanded. The Fifth District had no memory of why they had been assigned. The murders had been filed as unsolved.

"Sometimes that is how justice works," Hart said. "We do not solve it. It just stops happening."

"It did not stop. I stopped it."

"Then you are the only one who knows that."

The crow appeared on her windowsill every evening. It did not speak. It watched. Mira left water in a bowl, and the crow drank.

On the fifth day, Finn appeared at her door. He looked better. The brand was still livid on his chest, but he moved without pain.

"Tomorrow," he said.

"Tomorrow."

"You should come. To the negotiation. They both trust you."

"They trust no one. They are afraid of the Morrigan. That is different."

"Same result." Finn sat at her kitchen table. "What will you do after this?"

"I do not know. What will you do?"

"I have been exiled from both courts. I am free." He said it like he was testing the word. "Free. I have not been free in a century."

"Freedom suits you."

"It is terrifying." He smiled. "I think I will stay terrified for a while."

Chapter 41: The Ground

The negotiation took place at dawn, in a warehouse on the border between Summer and Autumn territory. The neutral fae had chosen the location — a crumbling warehouse on Western Avenue where the El tracks passed overhead.

Mira arrived first. The crow was already there, perched on a rafter.

Mab came with three guards. Rowan came alone. The symbol of trust, or arrogance.

The room was empty except for a table and four chairs. The Morrigan had not appeared, but her presence was felt in the way the fae looked at the shadows.

"I will speak first," Mab said. "Since I am the one granting concessions."

"You are not granting anything," Rowan said. "You are being forced to bargain."

"If you interrupt me again, I will leave."

"Then leave. I will rule in your absence."

Mira stepped between them. "Enough. You are here because the alternative is worse. Act like it."

Mab sat. Rowan sat. The negotiation began.

It was slow. Painful. Every word was a concession, every silence a battle. But after four hours, they had the skeleton of an agreement.

The courts would share Chicago. The borders between worlds would remain sealed. Half-fae would have the right to choose their allegiance — or none at all.

It was not peace. But it was the closest thing to peace they had ever had.

Chapter 42: The Thin Place

A week after the negotiation, Mira drove to the South Side to see the thinning.

Finn had told her about it — a place where the wall between worlds had worn thin enough to see through. A vacant lot between two abandoned buildings where the air shimmered like heat rising off asphalt.

She stood at the edge of the lot and watched. Through the shimmer, she saw shapes moving — trees that grew upside down, sky that was green, figures that walked on hands instead of feet.

"The wall is thinnest here," Finn said. He had appeared beside her without a sound.

"Is it going to break?"

"Not if the truce holds. The wall is maintained by the courts' agreement. If they fight, the wall weakens. If they hold the truce, the wall strengthens."

"Then we rely on two people who hate each other to keep an agreement."

"We rely on a goddess to enforce it." Finn looked at the shimmer. "The Morrigan has not forgotten. She will not forget."

The shimmer pulsed. For a moment, Mira thought she saw a face looking back at her from the other side. Then it was gone.

"What happens if the truce fails?"

"Then every thin place in the city becomes a door. And everything that was kept out comes through."

Mira nodded. She had done what she could. The rest was up to people older and stranger than her.

Chapter 43: The Cost

The smith died on a Tuesday.

Mira found out from Hart, who found out from the coroner's office. Unidentified male, mid-twenties, cardiac arrest. No next of kin.

She drove to the county morgue. The body was on a steel table, covered with a sheet. She pulled it back.

The iron poisoning had turned his veins black. The patterns were visible under his skin, a map of the poison's progress. His face was peaceful.

"He asked for you," the coroner said. "Before he went under. He said to tell you the blade was not his revenge. It was his confession."

Mira touched the smith's cold hand. "He confessed to everything. And no one heard him."

"He confessed to you."

"Too late for a trial."

"Too late for a lot of things." The coroner pulled the sheet back up. "What do you want done with the body?"

Mira thought. "Cremate him. Scatter the ashes somewhere with running water. A river. A lake."

"You want me to handle that?"

"You are the only one who knows his name."

She left the morgue and stood in the parking lot. The November sun was pale and weak. The crow sat on a lamppost, watching.

"He confessed to me," Mira said. "And I heard him. That is more than most people get."

The crow said nothing. It did not need to.

Chapter 44: The Hollow

The days after the smith's death were quiet. Mira went back to her office. She developed the photographs from the cheating-husband job. The client paid in cash, no questions.

She sat at her desk and looked at the photographs. The husband was in every frame. No shimmer. No glamour. Just a man who could not keep his hands to himself.

Normal. She had forgotten what normal looked like.

Hart stopped by. He brought coffee and a file.

"Cold case," he said. "Woman went missing six months ago. No leads. I thought you might see something the rest of us missed."

Mira took the file. "You are putting me back on the payroll?"

"I am putting you on unpaid consultant status. Same as before."

"Same as before." She opened the file. A woman's face stared up at her. Late twenties. Brown hair. A smile that had not yet learned to be wary.

"Her name is Teresa Mendez. She disappeared from a bus stop at Western and Fullerton."

Mira studied the photograph. The woman was mortal. But the bus stop was two blocks from the Tipsy Hag.

"I will look into it."

Hart nodded. He did not say thank you. He did not need to.

Chapter 45: The Thread

The disappearance of Teresa Mendez had no obvious connection to the fae. That was what made Mira suspicious.

She walked the route Teresa had taken the night she vanished. Bus stop to bus stop, street to street. The November rain had turned to sleet.

She found nothing. No fae traces. No glamour residue. No shimmer in the air.

She went to the Tipsy Hag. The fish-faced man let her in without a word.

Finn was at the bar. He had a new coat, a new confidence. The brand was covered by a high-collared shirt.

"Teresa Mendez," Mira said.

Finn's expression flickered. "What about her?"

"She disappeared near here six months ago. You know something."

"It is not what you think."

"Then tell me what it is."

Finn set down his glass. "She was not taken by fae. She was taken by mortals. A trafficking ring that operates in the gap between Summer and Autumn territory. The courts turn a blind eye because the ring pays tribute to both sides."

Mira's hands went cold. "You knew."

"I know a lot of things I do not act on. I am not a hero, Mira. I am a survivor."

"Then start being something else."

She walked out. Finn called after her. She did not stop.

Chapter 46: The Raid

Hart did not ask questions when Mira told him there was a trafficking ring operating out of a warehouse near the interstate. He made calls. By midnight, he had a task force.

Mira rode in the back of an unmarked van, watching the warehouse through a night-vision scope. Heat signatures. Movement. At least four guards.

"We go in hot," Hart said. "Two minutes to clear the building. You stay in the van."

"I am the one who found it."

"Then you should have thought about what happens next." Hart checked his weapon. "If the fae are involved, I need you alive to testify. Dead witnesses do not testify."

The van stopped. The team moved.

Mira watched through the scope as Hart's people breached the doors. The operation was precise, professional, terrifying. In ninety seconds, they had the building.

In a hundred and twenty seconds, Hart came out holding the arm of a woman wrapped in a blanket.

Teresa Mendez was alive.

Mira sat back in the van and let herself breathe.

Chapter 47: The News

Teresa Mendez was reunited with her family. The trafficking ring was broken. The story made the local news for two days, then disappeared into the churn of other headlines.

Mira watched the press conference from her office. Hart stood at the podium, giving credit to the task force. He did not mention her.

That night, the crow brought something to her windowsill. A single feather, black as oil.

Mira opened the window. The crow hopped inside and transformed into the Morrigan.

"You interfered with mortal crimes," the Morrigan said. "That is not your role."

"The fae were involved. They were taking tribute."

"Tribute is tradition."

"Tradition is not law." Mira met the Morrigan's eyes. "I saved a woman's life tonight. If that is interference, I do not care."

The Morrigan studied her. "You are becoming dangerous, Mira Black."

"I have always been dangerous. You just did not notice."

A thin smile crossed the Morrigan's face. "Perhaps." She turned back to the window. "The courts have ratified the truce. It will hold for now. Your work is finished."

"Then why do I feel like it just started?"

The Morrigan did not answer. She became a crow and flew into the dark.

Chapter 48: The Threshold

The truce had held for three weeks when Mira received the letter. Parchment, sealed with wax that changed color as she held it.

She broke the seal. The letter was written in a hand she did not recognize.

"Mira Black — You are invited to serve as Mortal Liaison to the Conjoined Courts of Chicago. The position carries authority, immunity, and a seat at every negotiation. You will be the voice of the mortal world in fae matters. Accept, and you will never be ordinary again. Decline, and we will not ask again. — M & R."

Mira set the letter on her desk. The wax seal had settled to a deep gray, the color of compromise.

She read it three times. Then she folded it and put it in a drawer.

Finn called that evening. "I heard about the letter."

"Of course you did."

"Are you going to accept?"

The question hung in the air. Mira looked out her window at the city lights. Somewhere out there, a thin place shimmered. A crow watched from a rooftop. A trafficking ring had been broken, but there were others.

"I do not know," she said.

"Take your time. The offer will not expire."

"Nothing expires in the fae world. It just waits."

"Exactly."

She hung up and watched the city. The ordinary city, with its traffic and its streetlights and its people who did not know what walked beside them.

She had spent her whole life seeing things other people did not. She had never had to decide if she wanted to act on them.

Chapter 49: The Answer

Mira went to the lake at dawn. The water was gray, the sky gray, the city a line of shadows on the horizon.

The crow was there. It sat on a piling and watched her.

"I am not going to accept," Mira said.

The crow tilted its head.

"I spent my whole life trying to see things clearly. The fae offer me a seat at the table, but it is still their table. Their rules. Their games." She looked at the water. "I work better outside."

The crow hopped closer.

"If they need me, they know where to find me. If they want to negotiate, they can come to my office. But I am not becoming a liaison. I am not becoming a diplomat. I am not becoming anything except what I already am."

The crow opened its beak. The Morrigan's voice emerged, thin and distant. "You are wiser than I expected."

"I have had good teachers."

"You have had hard lessons. They are not the same thing."

The crow launched into the air. Mira watched it circle once, then fly west.

She walked back to her apartment. The city was waking up. Buses ran. Coffee shops opened. People went to work.

They did not know about the truce. They did not know about the courts. They did not know that an immortal goddess had spoken to a private detective on a lakeshore at dawn.

That was how it should be.

Chapter 50: The Dawn

Mira walked home through the waking city. The streets were wet with last night's rain. Steam rose from subway grates. A man sold newspapers on a corner, his face invisible behind the glare of a passing headlight.

She did not see shimmer behind his face. She did not feel the weight of watching eyes. For the first time in months, the city was just a city.

Her phone buzzed. A new case. A woman who thought her business partner was embezzling. Standard work. Mortal work.

She typed back: "Send me the details. I will take a look."

She passed the alley where the first body had been found. The tape was gone. The chalk marks had washed away. No trace remained of what had happened there.

That was how the city worked. It absorbed violence and moved on. The fae had taught her that.

She reached her building. The Tipsy Hag's door had moved again — she noticed it was now three doors down, disguised as a dry cleaner. She did not go in.

She climbed the stairs to her office. The coffee was cold in the pot. She made a fresh one.

The drawer with the letter sat closed. She did not open it.

She sat at her desk and looked at the photograph of the cheating husband. A mortal man with mortal problems. A case she could solve with a camera and patience.

Somewhere, a crow circled. Somewhere, a thin place shimmered. Somewhere, a queen and a prince sat at a table they had built on a broken foundation.

But here, in this office, the morning light fell on an ordinary desk. And an ordinary woman sat behind it, doing her work.

Mira poured her coffee. She opened her notebook. She began.

The city was full of secrets. She was not one of them anymore.
