Chapter 1: The Teddy Bear

The warehouse collapsed at 3:47 AM. Dominic Cross stood in the rain of ash and watched the roof cave in, one section at a time, like a deck of cards folded by a child. The other firefighters worked around him, hoses trained on the adjacent buildings, but this one was lost.

A woman screamed from the barricade. "My son's things. All his things."

Dominic walked closer to the heat. The flames had gutted the storage unit on the west end. He pulled his mask aside and let the temperature hit his face. Fifteen years since he had stood this close to a fire without gear between him and it.

"I'll check the perimeter," he told Captain Voss.

She looked at him. "Don't do anything stupid."

He went around the back where the wall had fallen outward. Debris still smoldered. His boot kicked something soft. A teddy bear, singed but intact, one eye missing. He picked it up and the smell hit him — burnt cloth, melted plastic, something sweet underneath.

The smell of the Hargrove Street fire.

He stood there holding the bear, the firelight painting him orange. The memory came up through his chest like bile. A boy's face in an upstairs window. The roar of a floor giving way. Screaming that stopped too fast.

He had told himself he saved everyone he could. But he knew which ones he dreamed about.

The teddy bear's remaining eye stared at nothing.

Dominic tucked it into his coat and walked back to the truck.

Chapter 2: Clockwork

Detective Elena Park arrived at Iron City's landmark clock tower at 6 AM, three hours after the flames had been extinguished. The tower stood like a blackened spear against the gray sky. All four clock faces had melted. The hands sagged downward, frozen at 2:17.

She ducked under the police tape. A uniform handed her a report. "No casualties. Night watchman smelled smoke and called it in."

Elena studied the base of the tower. The fire had been concentrated here, climbing evenly up the stone. That took accelerant and planning. She kneeled and found the pattern in the scorch marks — V-shaped, like a torch had been laid against the foundation.

"Is that a message?" the uniform asked.

"Look closer."

She traced the letters with her gloved finger. Each stroke was clean, made with a chisel or a similar tool. No hesitation marks. The person who carved this had training. Stonework or metalwork, maybe. Or a surgeon.

"Get photos. High resolution. I want the tool mark analyst to see every scratch."

Someone had carved words into the granite near the door. The letters were clean, deliberate, cut with a tool designed for the task.

"WHAT BURNS CANNOT BE UNBURNED. — CHAPTER 7:19"

Elena photographed it. Read the line six times. She called forensics and told them to treat the scene as arson, not accident.

Her phone rang before she could stand. Another fire. The old municipal theater three blocks away, already burning when the first trucks arrived.

She ran.

Chapter 3: Handwriting

Dominic sat in the firehouse common room with a cup of coffee he had not touched. The newspaper lay open to page three. The clock tower fire made the lead story, but the real news was the carving. A reporter had photographed the message before the police sealed the area.

"What burns cannot be unburned."

He read the line again. His hands were steady now. He had slept two hours.

"Chapter 7:19," Danny DeSantos said from the couch. "That's Bible stuff, right?"

Dominic did not answer. He was staring at the photograph of the carving. The curves of the letters. The way the 'B' leaned slightly to the left. The space between the 'n' and the 'b' in 'unburned.'

He knew that handwriting.

He had seen it a thousand times in his own letters. The same tilt, the same weight on the downstrokes. He had taught himself to write that way in his twenties, practicing the Palmer method until his hand cramped. No one else wrote a 'B' like that.

"What are you looking at?" Danny asked.

"Nothing."

Dominic folded the newspaper and went to his locker. He pulled out a steel box wrapped in a dirty coat. Inside were letters he had written to a boy at St. Catherine's Youth Home, returned unopened after the boy disappeared.

The handwriting on the envelopes matched the photograph.

He closed the box and sat in the dark for a long time.

Chapter 4: Broadcast

The video appeared on every social platform at noon. A man sat in a chair against a black backdrop. He wore a mask made of polished brass, shaped like a human face with eyeholes cut into dark glass. His voice was processed, a low mechanical hum.

"Citizens of Iron City. You know me as the one who lit the clock and the stage. But you do not know me as I truly am."

He leaned forward. The brass face caught the light.

"Seven pillars hold this city aloft. History. Memory. Justice. Transit. Commerce. Sanctuary. Power. I will burn each one until nothing remains but the foundation. Then we will rebuild."

The video cut to a shot of the clock tower in flames, then the theater. Then black screen with white text:

"Three down. Four to go."

Elena watched it three times in her office. The brass mask. The theatrical phrasing. This was not a vandal. This was a performance artist with a grudge and a chemistry degree.

Her phone buzzed. Her father's name on the screen. She let it ring.

The fourth pillar burned before sunset — a wing of the civic museum where the city charter was displayed under glass.

Chapter 5: Skeletons

Mayor Alice Goren sat behind a wide oak desk, her face tight from a morning of phone calls and damage control. Elena stood across from her with a notepad she had not opened.

"It's a coordinated campaign," Elena said. "The targets are symbolic. He's burning the city's identity."

"I know what symbols are, Detective." The mayor's voice was clipped. "What I need is a suspect. A profile. A direction."

"The carvings quote scripture. He's either religious or he's using religion to frame the message."

The mayor's eyes flickered to something on her desk. Elena followed her gaze. A framed photograph — the mayor shaking hands with a man in a red costume and a gold mask. Phoenix.

"You knew him," Elena said.

"Everyone knew Phoenix. He was here for eight years. Then he disappeared."

"Why?"

The mayor hesitated. "There was a fire. A bad one. Hargrove Street. He went in after a child. The building came down. He came out alone. After that, no one saw him again."

Elena wrote the name in her notebook. Hargrove Street.

"Could the person doing this be connected to Phoenix?"

The mayor's face went still. "I don't see how."

She was lying. Elena could tell by the way she folded her hands.

Chapter 6: Rooftop

Dominic took the service elevator to the top of the Meridian Building, the tallest structure in Iron City. The roof door was rusted shut. He put his shoulder into it and the frame gave with a groan.

The view hit him like a memory. Iron City spread out below, a grid of lights and shadows, smoke still rising from the museum's west wing. He had stood here every night for eight years, watching for the fires before they started.

The rooftop looked different now. Someone had been here recently. A circle of stones in the center, blackened with soot. A small fire had burned inside it. Around the stones, offerings: a melted clock face, a charred theater program, a singed page of the city charter.

He had left gifts for the Inferno to find.

Dominic kneeled and touched the ash. Still warm.

A piece of paper was wedged under one of the stones. He unfolded it. One sentence in that familiar handwriting.

"You remember now, don't you?"

He folded the paper and put it in his pocket. The wind picked up, carrying the smell of smoke from the museum. Down below, traffic had slowed. People gathered on sidewalks, looking at the sky. Iron City was scared.

He stayed on the roof until the sun went down.

Chapter 7: Sidekick

Marcus Webb lived in a renovated loft in the financial district. His apartment had white walls and expensive furniture and not a single photograph on display. He opened the door in a silk robe, holding a glass of wine.

"Detective Park. I was wondering when you'd show up."

"May I come in?"

"You're already in." He stepped aside.

Elena walked to the window. The museum was visible in the distance, the burned wing still smoking.

"You were Phoenix's partner," she said.

"I was his sidekick. There's a difference."

"Where is he now?"

Marcus laughed. "If I knew, I'd have better things to tell you than where he is. He walked away, Detective. No goodbye, no explanation. Just gone."

"When?"

"Hargrove Street. After the fire."

Elena watched his face. There was something underneath the casual tone. Old hurt.

"He never told you why?"

"He never told me anything. That was the deal. He taught me how to move in the shadows, how to read a fire scene, how to take a punch. But he never told me who he was." Marcus drained his glass. "And before you ask — no, I have no idea who's burning the city. But I know one thing."

"What?"

"If Phoenix is still alive, and if he knows who's behind this, he'll come back. He never could resist a fire."

Chapter 8: Library

The Iron City Public Library's east wing caught fire at 11 PM. Elena arrived to find the structure fully involved, flames punching through the roof in three places. Fire trucks lined the street, water arcing into the orange sky.

"The night custodian," a firefighter said. "He's still inside."

"How do you know?"

"His truck's in the lot. No one's seen him come out."

Elena watched the building. The east wing held the city archive — property records, census data, newspaper microfilm, the bones of Iron City's written history. And a man was somewhere in that maze, probably dead already.

Captain Voss was coordinating the attack. She was composed, issuing orders in a low voice that carried authority.

"Can we get in?" Elena asked.

"The roof's going. We have maybe ten minutes before the whole section comes down. My people aren't going in there."

Somebody else moved at the edge of the crowd. A tall man in civilian clothes. He walked straight toward the building, not running, not hesitating.

"Hey," Elena called. "You can't—"

The man ignored her. He pulled a bandana from his pocket and wrapped it around his face. Then he disappeared through a side door that had buckled from the heat.

Chapter 9: Rescue

The corridor was black with smoke. Dominic dropped to his knees where the air was marginally clearer and crawled forward, one hand on the wall, counting doors. The layout matched the blueprints he had studied years ago for a training exercise.

He had memorized every public building in Iron City during his years as Phoenix. It was what he did. He prepared for fire before it came.

The custodian was in the basement storage room, unconscious against a filing cabinet. Smoke inhalation. Dominic checked his pulse. Alive.

He dragged the man up the stairs one step at a time. The heat was building above them. The ceiling groaned. He moved faster, his legs burning, his lungs screaming.

They cleared the door as the east wing's roof collapsed.

Dominic laid the custodian on the grass and stepped back into the crowd. Hands gripped his shoulders. A paramedic tried to put an oxygen mask on him. He accepted it, panting, watching the building burn.

Captain Voss appeared beside him. "You're an idiot."

"He's alive."

"Next time, you wait for orders."

"Next time, there won't be time." He pulled off the mask and walked toward the firehouse, wiping soot from his face.

Behind him, Detective Elena Park watched him go, her eyes narrow and focused.

Chapter 10: Recognition

Elena followed the man from the library fire all the way to the Iron City Fire Department Station 4. She sat in her unmarked car across the street and watched him walk into the bay, exchange words with a young firefighter, and disappear into the locker room.

She pulled the man's name from the department roster: Dominic Cross. Fifteen years with ICFD. No disciplinary record. Promoted to captain six years ago. No photograph in the personnel file matched the face she had seen in the fire glow.

But something about his movement stayed with her. The way he had walked into the building — not charging, not rushing. Planting each foot with intention. The economy of motion that comes from years of training.

She had seen that walk before. In old news footage of a masked figure striding through smoke.

She searched "Iron City firefighter Dominic Cross Phoenix" and got no hits. Searched his birth date, his old addresses, his family records. Everything started fifteen years ago. No college. No high school records in the state. No mention of parents.

There was a gap between ages nineteen and thirty-four that no document filled.

She looked at the photograph of the man carrying the custodian. The way he held his shoulders. The angle of his jaw.

She made a note to visit Station 4 in the morning.

Chapter 11: Shake

Dominic sat in the locker room after his shift ended, his hands open on his knees. They were still trembling. Not from exertion. From the fire.

He had felt it tonight. The old pull. The heat reaching through his skin and finding something that should have been dead. His body remembered what it could do, even if his mind had buried it.

He made a fist. The trembling stopped.

He looked at his hands. These were the same hands that had once pulled a man from a car fire, that had carried a woman down twenty-seven flights of stairs. They had also held a dying child, years ago, in a hallway that smelled of gasoline.

Danny DeSantos appeared in the doorway. "Captain. You okay?"

"I'm fine."

"Detective Park is here. Wants to talk to you."

Dominic had expected this. He had seen her watching him at the library. He had seen her pull her phone out and type his name while he walked away.

"Tell her I'll be out in five."

"She's persistent. Been sitting there for twenty minutes."

"Then she can wait two more."

Danny left. Dominic sat for another minute, listening to the building settle around him. He had rehearsed this conversation a thousand times over fifteen years. Now that it was here, he had no idea what he would say.

Chapter 12: Gap

Elena sat on a plastic chair in the firehouse common room, a vending machine coffee growing cold beside her. The room smelled like diesel and laundry detergent. A calendar on the wall showed fire safety tips for elementary schools.

Dominic Cross walked in wearing civilian clothes. Jeans, a dark sweater, work boots. His face was weathered but not old. His eyes were the kind of quiet that came from watching things burn.

"Detective."

"Mr. Cross. Thanks for seeing me."

He sat across from her, one arm resting on the table. "You want to talk about the library."

"I want to talk about who you were before you became a firefighter."

"Everyone has a past."

"Everyone has records. You don't." She slid a folder across the table. "No birth certificate in state databases. No high school transcripts. No driver's license before age twenty-five. It's like you appeared from nowhere."

Dominic did not look at the folder. "Sometimes people start over."

"Most people don't start over by erasing every trace of who they were."

"Maybe they have reasons."

"Maybe they do." Elena leaned back. "I'm not here to expose you. I'm here because someone is burning my city, and I think you know something about it."

Dominic was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was low.

"Show me the carving again."

Chapter 13: Gazette

The old Iron City Gazette building had been empty for seven years, but its contents had never been moved. The basement held bound volumes of every newspaper printed since 1903. The third floor housed the photograph archive. The second floor was a morgue of filing cabinets, each drawer stuffed with decades of reporter notes and unpublished stories.

The fire started in the basement at 2 AM. By the time firefighters arrived, the building was a vertical torch, flames climbing the stairwells, smoke rolling from every window.

Elena stood at the perimeter, her face lit orange. "What's in there?"

"Everything," the fire chief said. "The city's written history. Birth records. Marriage licenses. The entire archive."

The Inferno's message had been clear: he was not just burning buildings. He was burning the proof that Iron City had ever existed.

Dominic arrived in his turnout gear, already suited up. He did not look at Elena. He walked to Captain Voss and had a brief conversation. She nodded once. He entered the building through a loading dock at the rear.

Elena watched the door swing shut behind him and felt something cold settle in her stomach.

She counted the seconds. Ten. Thirty. A minute. The building groaned but held. Smoke curled from every opening. She did not know this man beyond a folder of missing years. But she believed, without evidence, that he would come back out.

Chapter 14: Archive

The Gazette building's basement was a furnace. Dominic moved through the heat with a wet towel pressed over his mouth, his eyes stinging. He was not looking for a victim. He was looking for a message.

The Inferno had chosen this building for a reason. There was something here he wanted destroyed.

Dominic found the filing cabinets in the morgue section. The fire had not reached them yet, but the smoke was thick and the temperature was climbing. He pulled open a drawer at random. Old case files from the 1990s. Another drawer. Arrest records. Another. Court transcripts.

At the bottom of the third cabinet, a folder labeled "St. Catherine's Youth Home — Fire Incident — 2009."

He pulled it out. The paper was warm but intact.

He tucked it inside his coat and made his way back through the smoke, the building groaning around him. He emerged through the loading dock and walked to his truck without speaking to anyone.

Elena intercepted him in the parking lot.

"What did you find?"

He handed her the folder. "He's not just burning the city. He's burning evidence."

She opened the folder. The first page was a photograph of a burned building, paramedics loading a stretcher, a small hand reaching out from the blanket.

Chapter 15: Confrontation

Elena followed Dominic to a diner two blocks from the station. The place was empty at this hour, a single waitress reading a magazine at the counter. They took a booth in the back.

"Talk to me," she said.

"I was Phoenix." He said it flatly, like he was ordering coffee. "Eight years. I stopped fires before they spread. I caught the people who set them. Then something happened, and I walked away."

"The Hargrove Street fire."

He nodded. "I went in after a boy. Seven years old. I couldn't find him. The building came down. They found his body in the rubble three days later."

"And you quit."

"I didn't quit. I broke." He looked at his hands. "You can't be what people need you to be if you're empty inside."

Elena let the words settle. "The Inferno. You think it's connected to that boy."

"His name was Leo Marchetti. He was a patient at St. Catherine's Youth Home. I used to visit him. I wrote him letters. He never wrote back."

"Why would he be targeting you?"

"I don't know. But the handwriting in those carvings is mine. He's copying it. He's speaking in my voice."

Elena sat back. Outside, the sky was beginning to lighten. Another day, another fire waiting to be lit.

Chapter 16: Warning

Marcus Webb found Dominic at the firehouse the next morning. He walked in like he owned the place, past the fire trucks and the confused looks of the crew, straight to Dominic's locker.

"You need to stay out of it."

Dominic closed his locker. "Hello to you too, Marcus."

"I mean it. I saw you on the news walking into the library. What are you thinking?"

"I was thinking a man was going to die."

"That's what firefighters do. Not what Phoenix does. There's a difference, and you crossed it." Marcus stepped closer, lowering his voice. "People are going to connect the dots. They're going to figure out who you are. And when they do, everything you've built here is gone."

"Maybe that's a price I'm willing to pay."

"They'll come after you. The city. The press. Everyone you saved — they'll want to know why you left. They'll want to know why you stopped."

Dominic did not answer.

"There's another fire tonight," Marcus said. "I heard it on the scanner. The transit station. He's going for the subway."

"How do you know?"

"I still have contacts. People who remember what the costume looked like." Marcus turned to leave. "Be careful, Dom. Some fires don't go out."

Chapter 17: Bridge

The Iron City Bridge connected the mainland to the island district where the old industrial sector sat abandoned. It was a steel structure from the 1930s, rusted and reinforced but still carrying traffic. The Inferno had placed charges at three structural points along the span.

The explosion came at 5:47 PM, during rush hour.

Elena felt the shockwave through her car. She was three blocks away, heading to the transit station after a false alarm. The bridge section collapsed in slow motion, a thousand tons of steel folding into the river.

She slammed the brakes and ran to the waterfront.

Vehicles teetered at the edge of the break. People were screaming. A bus hung half off the gap, its rear wheels spinning over nothing. Fire crews were already arriving.

Elena looked for Dominic in the chaos and did not find him. But she found the Inferno's signature — words spray-painted on a concrete pylon near the bridge's entrance.

"FOUR PILLARS. FOUR FALLEN. THE FIRE FINDS ITS WAY HOME."

She photographed it with shaking hands.

The bridge had been part of the city's identity for ninety years. Now it was a wound. She wondered how many more wounds the city could take before it stopped being a city at all.

Chapter 18: Instinct

Dominic reached the bridge after the evacuation was underway. He had driven there without thinking, his body responding to the call before his mind had caught up. He stood at the barrier, watching the rescue crews work, and felt the old machinery in his chest start to turn.

A woman was trapped in a car wedged against the broken railing. Her door was jammed, the metal warped from the blast. A firefighter was trying to pry it open with a Halligan bar.

"Let me," Dominic said.

The firefighter hesitated. Dominic took the tool, found the leverage point, and pulled. The door came open with a screech of metal. He helped the woman out and handed her to a paramedic.

The firefighter stared at him. "How'd you know where to pull?"

Dominic did not answer. He walked along the broken edge of the bridge, reading the damage, tracing the Inferno's strategy in the placement of the charges. The man knew engineering. He knew where to hurt the city.

But more than that — he knew where to hurt Dominic.

The bridge was where Dominic had proposed to his wife, twenty years ago, before she left him for the life he could not give up.

The Inferno knew everything.

Chapter 19: Journal

Elena spent the night in the records room at the police station, surrounded by boxes of files from St. Catherine's Youth Home. She had requisitioned everything the department had on the facility, which was not much. The place had closed in 2012, its records scattered between state agencies and private hands.

Leo Marchetti's file was thin. Admitted at age six, withdrawn and nonverbal. Diagnosed with post-traumatic stress and behavioral disorder. No record of pyromania, despite what Dominic had said. No mention of fire setting at all.

But there was a journal. A therapy journal kept by one of the staff psychologists. Fifty-three pages of handwritten notes about a boy who drew pictures of flames. Who could describe the color and temperature of fire with precise scientific language. Who, according to one entry, had "an intimate relationship with combustion."

And in the margins of the journal, someone had written questions in a different hand. Questions that matched the phrasing of the Inferno's riddles.

"Who taught you to burn?"

"What happens when you burn the past?"

Elena photocopied every page and locked the originals in evidence.

She sat in her car in the parking lot, the photocopies spread across the passenger seat. Someone had been coaching Leo. Someone had been shaping him, directing his obsession. The questions in the margins were not therapy. They were instruction.

Chapter 20: Orphanage

The St. Catherine's Youth Home sat on a hill overlooking the industrial district, a three-story brick building with boarded windows and a chain-link fence around the yard. The structure had been condemned after the 2009 fire that killed three people and destroyed the east wing.

Dominic parked his truck at the bottom of the hill and walked up. The gate was padlocked, but the fence had been cut near the corner. Fresh cut. The wire gleamed in the moonlight.

He stepped through and crossed the yard. The building loomed above him, dark and hollow. He had not been here since 2009, the night of the fire that ended his career.

The front door was unlocked.

He pushed it open and stepped into the lobby. The smell hit him immediately — old smoke, wet wood, something organic decaying behind the walls. The floor was strewn with debris. A child's drawing still hung on the bulletin board, the paper yellowed and curled.

He walked to the stairwell and looked up. Light flickered on the third floor.

Someone was here.

He climbed the stairs, his footsteps careful in the dark.

Each stair creaked under his weight. The banister was loose in its brackets. He passed rooms that had held children, now empty and rotting. The fire on the third floor flickered through a doorway at the end of the hall. He approached it with his hands out, ready to summon a flame he was not sure would come.

Chapter 21: Pressure

Councilman Thomas Park sat in Elena's office at 8 AM, wearing a suit that cost more than her monthly rent. He was her father. She had not spoken to him in six months, not since he cut funding for the youth outreach program she had spent two years building.

"The mayor wants results," he said.

"Then the mayor should give me more resources."

"You have the entire detective bureau. You have a task force. You have a direct line to the chief."

"None of that helps me identify a suspect when every piece of evidence I have points to a ghost."

Thomas leaned forward. "You need someone to arrest. Someone visible. Someone the public can see being led into the station in handcuffs."

"I'm not going to arrest an innocent person."

"Then find the guilty one."

He stood and adjusted his cuffs. At the door, he paused. "Your mother wants you to come to dinner."

"When this is over."

He left. Elena sat in the silence, staring at the photographs of burned buildings spread across her desk. Her father had never visited her office before. He was nervous about something. But he would never tell her what.

She picked up the photo of the clock tower and held it to the light. Somewhere in these images was the answer. She just had to find it before the next fire started.

Chapter 22: Ember

Dominic locked himself in the station's equipment room after dark. He sat on the concrete floor with a small candle in a glass jar, lit the wick, and watched the flame.

He had not used his powers in fifteen years. He was not sure they still existed. The ability had come to him when he was seventeen, a sudden and terrifying gift that he had learned to control through years of practice. Fire answered to him. It bent to his will. He could extinguish a blaze with a thought or conjure a flame from nothing.

But he had let the gift atrophy. He had locked it away in the same vault where he kept his memories of the boy.

He held his hand over the candle. The flame flickered. He concentrated, reaching for the heat, trying to find the connection that had once been as natural as breathing.

Nothing.

He sat there for an hour, sweat beading on his forehead, the candle burning down to nothing. The flame sputtered and died.

Dominic sat in the dark, his hand still extended over the cold wick.

He had expected this. Powers did not return on command. They came when they were ready, or they did not come at all. He would try again tomorrow. And the day after. He would keep trying until fire answered him, or until the Inferno finished what he had started.

Chapter 23: Evidence

The police precinct's evidence locker was in the basement, behind a reinforced door with a keypad lock. The Inferno had bypassed the system without triggering an alarm. The fire had been small, targeted, and efficient — a chemical accelerant that burned hot and left little behind.

The only section affected was the row of shelves containing closed case files from 2008 to 2010.

Elena walked through the damage, her shoes crunching on ash. The fire had consumed six boxes completely and damaged a dozen more. The labels were gone. Whatever had been stored there was now indistinguishable from the debris.

She kneeled and sifted through the remnants. A corner of a manila folder survived, the edge of a label still legible: "— Needle —"

The Needle case. She had heard the name from Marcus Webb. Dominic had put away a serial arsonist known as the Needle, who had set fires across the city using a signature device that injected accelerant into walls.

The Inferno had destroyed those records. But why that case specifically? There were dozens of closed arson files in this room. The Needle was old news, a man already serving life.

She called Dominic. He answered on the first ring.

"The Needle," she said. "Tell me everything."

There was a long pause. "I'll meet you at the diner."

Chapter 24: Needle

The diner was nearly empty. Dominic sat in the same booth they had used before, a cup of black coffee steaming in front of him. Elena slid in across from him.

"The Needle was my last case," he said. "A serial arsonist who targeted old buildings. Churches, schools, community centers. He used a syringe-like device to inject accelerant into the walls, then lit a fuse. I caught him in 2008. He went to prison."

"What was his name?"

"Arthur Keene. He was a construction contractor who lost his business when the city rezoned his neighborhood. He blamed the old buildings — said they were symbols of urban decay."

Elena wrote it down. "But the Inferno burned the Needle's case file."

"Because something in that file doesn't match the official story." Dominic's jaw tightened. "I arrested Keene. But I never believed he acted alone. There were fires that didn't fit his pattern. Fires that were too clean, too precise. Someone else was working with him."

"And that someone is the Inferno."

"I think it's Leo. I think he was Keene's apprentice."

Elena looked at her notes. "Leo Marchetti. The boy from the fire."

"The boy I couldn't save." Dominic's voice cracked. "Except I'm starting to think I didn't fail to save him. I think someone else did."

Chapter 25: Partnership

The task force met in the precinct's conference room at 10 AM. Elena stood at the head of the table, flanked by a captain, two lieutenants, and six detectives. Dominic sat in the corner, out of uniform, wearing a dark jacket that did nothing to hide his size.

"Who's the civilian?" the captain asked.

"He's my consultant."

"I didn't authorize a consultant."

"I don't need authorization for an advisor." Elena turned to the board. "We have six confirmed attacks, seven declared targets, and no physical evidence linking to a single suspect. The accelerant is a custom mixture we haven't identified. The delivery mechanism varies per site. This is someone with chemistry training, engineering knowledge, and a personal grudge against the city."

"We know who he is," Dominic said.

The room went quiet.

"He's a former patient at St. Catherine's Youth Home. He survived a fire in 2009 that was ruled an accident but wasn't. He's been planning this for over a decade."

The captain stared at him. "And who the hell are you? You're not a cop, and you're not a consultant I signed off on."

Dominic met his eyes. "Someone who should have caught him a long time ago."

Elena stepped in. "He's with me. That's all you need to know."

Chapter 26: Training

Dominic started before sunrise, running the stairs at the old Meridian Building. He had not run in months. His legs burned by the fourth flight. His lungs screamed by the eighth. He kept going.

He reached the roof and stood in the cold air, hands on his knees, gasping. The city spread out below him, still dark except for the streetlights lining the avenues. Somewhere out there, Leo was planning the next attack.

He sat cross-legged on the concrete and closed his eyes. He tried to find the fire inside him, the heat that had once lived in his chest like a second heart. He breathed slowly. He imagined the flame growing, spreading, warming his veins.

A flicker. A single spark in the darkness of his ribcage.

He held onto it, feeding it with concentration, with will. The spark grew into a small flame, and he let it travel down his arm, into his hand. He opened his eyes. A thin column of fire rose from his palm, no bigger than a candle flame.

He almost laughed. It was pathetic. Fifteen years ago he could have melted steel.

But it was something.

He closed his hand and the flame died. He sat in the dark, breathing hard, and felt the old strength stirring like an animal waking from hibernation.

Chapter 27: Recording

The package arrived at Station 4 addressed to Captain Dominic Cross. It was a small cardboard box, wrapped in brown paper, no return address. Danny brought it to him during the lunch break.

"Looks personal."

Dominic took it to his locker and opened it away from the crew. Inside was a phone. An old flip model, the kind that had been popular fifteen years ago. A sticky note on the screen: "I found it in the ash. Listen."

He turned the phone on. The battery had one bar. There was a single audio file in the memory.

He put in earbuds and pressed play.

A boy's voice, thin and scared: "Where are you? I can't see. It's hot. Please. Where are you?"

The recording stopped.

Dominic sat on the bench, the phone in his hand, the boy's voice echoing in his head. That was Leo. That was the voice of the child he had not found in time. And someone had been recording it.

Someone had been in the building with the boy, watching him die.

He called Elena. His voice was steady but his hand was not. "He sent me a recording. From the Hargrove fire."

"Of what?"

"Of Leo. Calling for me. While the building burned."

Elena was quiet for a moment. "That means someone was there. Someone recorded it."

"I know."

"Dominic. That means the fire was staged."

He closed his eyes. "I know."

Chapter 28: Home

St. Catherine's Youth Home had kept therapy journals for every resident. The originals had been transferred to the county archives after the closure, but the county had digitized only the summaries, not the full text. Elena spent the afternoon in the county records office, going through boxes of paper.

She found Leo's journal in the third box. The cover was plain, the pages yellowed. The handwriting started neat and grew erratic over the months.

"I dreamed I was made of fire. I dreamed I burned the whole city and no one could stop me because I was already ash."

"He came to see me again. The man in red. He says fire can be controlled. He says I can learn."

"I want to go back to that night. The night of the first fire. I want to see it again."

The entries were dated 2008 to 2009. The last one was two weeks before the Hargrove Street fire.

"I met someone. He says he's like me. He says fire is the only honest thing because it doesn't pretend. He's going to help me."

Elena read it three times. The man in red. Dominic had visited Leo as Phoenix. He had tried to help a troubled boy who was drawn to fire. And someone else had found Leo first.

Someone who had been watching.

Chapter 29: Memory

Dominic sat in his apartment with the lights off, the recording of Leo's voice on repeat. He had listened to it twelve times. Each time, he tried to place the sounds in the background. The crackle of fire. The groan of metal. But there was something else, too faint to identify.

He called Elena. "The recording. There's background noise. Can your lab clean it up?"

"Maybe. I'll send someone to pick it up."

"It's evidence. You should have it anyway."

A pause. "Dominic. You know this isn't your fault."

"There was someone in that building with him. Someone who recorded his death and kept the recording for fifteen years. That's not the act of a witness. That's a collector."

"You think the Inferno was there."

"I think Leo didn't become what he is alone. Someone made him. Someone cultivated him. And I think that person has been waiting for this moment."

"Do you know who?"

Dominic was silent. An old suspicion was crystallizing in his mind. A figure from his past that he had refused to look at directly.

"I have a theory," he said. "But I need to check the evidence first."

"The Needle's partner."

"Yes. But not a partner. A puppeteer."

Chapter 30: Plume

The chemical plant on the edge of the city was the Inferno's most sophisticated attack. He had bypassed three security systems, disabled the sprinkler network, and ignited a storage tank of industrial solvents. The fire burned at temperatures that melted concrete.

The plume of black smoke rose three miles into the air and drifted over Iron City, a toxic cloud that carried the smell of burning plastic and sulfur. The evacuation order covered twelve blocks.

Elena coordinated from the command post, her phone pressed to her ear, listening to reports from hazmat teams. Dominic was on site in full turnout gear, working alongside the firefighters who were trying to contain the blaze.

"It's a diversion," Dominic said when he reached her. "The fire's big, but it's not the real target. He's pulling our resources here so he can hit something else."

"What else is there? He's hit everything."

Dominic looked at the smoke. "One pillar left. Power."

"The grid?"

"Not just the grid. The source." He pointed east, toward the reservoir where the city's main electrical substation sat. "If he takes that out, the entire city goes dark. Hospitals, traffic lights, water pumps. Everything."

Elena felt her blood go cold. "When?"

"He's already there." Dominic turned and ran for his truck. "Get everyone you can to the substation. Now."

Chapter 31: Records

Elena drove to St. Catherine's herself, alone, after the chemical plant fire was contained. The building looked different in daylight — smaller, shabbier, the neglect of a decade evident in every cracked window and peeling gutter.

She had a warrant for the administrative records that the county had not collected. The building's original filing system. Paper copies of intake forms and case notes and incident reports.

The main office was on the first floor, behind a door that had been locked but not well. She kicked it open.

Filing cabinets lined the wall, rusted but intact. She started with the "M" drawer. Marchetti, Leo. There were two files. One was the thin medical file she had already seen. The other was thicker, marked with a red stamp: "CONFIDENTIAL — DIRECTOR ONLY."

She opened it.

Inside was a signed agreement between St. Catherine's and a man named Alan Voss. The agreement granted Voss access to "particularly gifted or troubled residents" for "therapeutic mentorship." The signatures were dated 2007.

Alan Voss.

Captain Lorraine Voss's husband. A clinical psychologist who had lost his license in 2010 after allegations of misconduct with a minor. The allegations were never proven. He had left the state before the investigation concluded.

Elena stared at the name. Dominic's captain. The woman who had been protecting him for years.

Her husband had been the one who found Leo first.

Chapter 32: Certificate

Leo Marchetti's death certificate was filed in 2009, three days after the Hargrove Street fire. Cause of death: smoke inhalation and blunt-force trauma. The body was identified by dental records.

Elena pulled the original coroner's report from the county archives. The body had been recovered from the second floor of the building, near the rear stairwell. The dental records were a match.

But there was a problem. The coroner's report noted that the body had suffered "extensive thermal damage to the upper extremities and facial structure." The dental comparison had been done by a single forensic odontologist who had since retired and moved overseas.

No second opinion. No DNA confirmation.

And the name on the dental records? The records had come from St. Catherine's, not from an outside dentist.

Elena leaned back in her chair. Leo Marchetti had been declared dead by evidence provided by the institution that had been treating him. The institution that had signed an agreement with a psychologist who was later investigated for misconduct.

She called Dominic. "Leo's death was faked. I'm sure of it."

"How?"

"Someone in the system facilitated it. Someone with access to records and authority to sign off on an identification."

"Who?"

"I think it was your captain's husband."

The silence on the line stretched long enough that she checked if the call had dropped.

"Dominic?"

"Alan Voss," he said. "I knew him. He was at the fire that night. He told me Leo was already dead. That I couldn't save him. That's when I stopped."

Chapter 33: Phone

The burner phone rang at 11 PM. Dominic was in his apartment, staring at the wall, when the generic ringtone cut through the silence. He had given the number to no one except Elena.

He answered.

"Phoenix." The voice was processed, a mechanical hum, but underneath it was something human. Something young. "You've been looking for me."

"Leo."

"I don't go by that name anymore. You know what I've become."

"Tell me why."

"Because you left me." The voice cracked, the distortion briefly failing. "You came to visit me. You told me I could control it. The fire. You said I could learn to use it for good. And then you abandoned me."

"I didn't abandon you. The fire—"

"The fire was supposed to save me." Leo's voice rose. "Alan said it would. He said you would carry me out. He said the flames would make me new. But you didn't come. You let me burn."

Dominic closed his eyes. "Alan Voss did this to you."

"Alan Voss gave me purpose. He showed me that destruction is creation. He showed me that the only way to become something new is to burn away everything that came before."

"Where is he now?"

"Gone. But I don't need him anymore. I have the fire. And I have you."

The line went dead.

Chapter 34: Surge

A grease fire in the station's kitchen gave Dominic his opening. The flames jumped from the stove to the exhaust hood, spreading fast. Danny grabbed an extinguisher, but Dominic was closer.

He raised his hand and the fire stopped.

Not like a spray of water had hit it. Like someone had turned off a switch. The flames simply ceased to exist, as if they had never been there at all.

Danny stared. "What the hell—"

Dominic lowered his hand. "It must have starved for oxygen."

"That's not..." Danny shook his head. "That's not how fire works."

The other firefighters filed in, drawn by the commotion. Captain Voss arrived last. She looked at the stove, at Dominic's pale face, at Danny's stunned expression.

"Everyone out," she said. "Now."

The room emptied. Voss closed the door.

"Your powers are coming back."

"They never left. I just buried them."

"You need to be careful. Danny's smart. He'll figure it out."

Dominic met her eyes. "I know about Alan."

Voss went still. The color drained from her face.

"He was the one who took Leo. He was the one who faked the death certificate. He was the one who made the Inferno."

Voss did not deny it. She simply nodded, slow and defeated.

"I didn't know," she said. "Not until after. And by then, it was too late."

Chapter 35: Cover-Up

Elena confronted her father in his office at City Hall. She closed the door, locked it, and stood with her back to it.

"You helped cover up the Hargrove Street fire."

Councilman Park did not look up from his desk. "That's an accusation, not a question."

"Alan Voss was involved with a minor. He falsified records. He staged a fire. And someone in the city government made sure the investigation went nowhere."

"Allegations are not proof, Elena."

"Then help me find proof."

He looked up. His eyes were tired. "Some things are protected for reasons you don't understand."

"Then explain them."

"I can't."

"You won't."

He did not answer. Elena took a step forward, her voice dropping to a whisper.

"He's going to burn the whole city, Dad. He's going to kill people. And if you stand in the way of stopping him, you're as responsible as Alan Voss."

Thomas Park said nothing. His hands lay flat on the desk, palms down, fingers spread. A man bracing for impact.

She left the office and did not look back. The door clicked shut behind her. In the hallway, she leaned against the wall and pressed her palms to her eyes. Her father knew more than he was telling. She would find it without him.

Chapter 36: Riddle

The Inferno's final riddle appeared on the front page of the Iron City Chronicle, delivered to the editor in an unmarked envelope.

"I have burned the past. I have burned the present.
One pillar remains: the future.
On the night of the new moon, when Iron City is dark,
I will light the fire that ends all fires.
Find me if you can."

Elena read it at her desk, the newspaper spread open, the words glaring up at her. The new moon was in two days.

Dominic stood beside her, reading over her shoulder.

"The substation," he said. "If he takes out the power grid, the city goes dark. Then he can attack anywhere without anyone seeing him coming."

"Or he's already planted devices across the city and the blackout is the trigger."

"Either way, we have two days."

"Less than that." Dominic pointed at the poem's second line. "The new moon. That's forty-eight hours from tonight. He'll move at night, when the darkness helps him."

"You're not going to stop him by waiting for it." Elena turned to face him. "You need to find him first."

Dominic looked at the riddle again. "The fire that ends all fires." He read the line twice. "He wants to die. He wants to burn with the city."

"Then we need to get to him before he does."

Chapter 37: Suit

Dominic went to the storage unit he had kept for fifteen years, paying the rent every month even though he never opened the door. The lock was rusted. He cut it with bolt cutters and pulled the door up.

Inside, a single black duffel bag. He unzipped it and pulled out the suit.

It was not the costume he had worn as Phoenix. That was too recognizable, too theatrical. This was the backup — a fire-resistant suit of dark fabric with a phoenix emblem stitched over the left breast. Simple. Functional.

He held it up in the fluorescent light. It smelled like dust and old smoke.

He changed in the storage unit. The suit fit, but tighter than he remembered. He had put on muscle in the firehouse. The fabric pulled at his shoulders.

He stood in front of a cracked mirror and looked at himself. A man in his late forties, graying at the temples, wearing a uniform he had sworn he would never put on again.

He did not look like a hero.

But he looked like someone who was done running.

He locked the storage unit behind him and walked to the truck. The suit felt foreign and familiar at the same time, like a voice he had not heard in years but still recognized.

Chapter 38: Blackout

The city went dark at 8:17 PM on the night of the new moon. The substation explosion was simultaneous across three transformer banks, a coordinated attack that overwhelmed every backup system. Within thirty seconds, the grid was dead.

Dominic was driving when the lights went out. Streetlights flickered and died. Traffic signals went black. The buildings around him dissolved into silhouettes against a starless sky.

He pulled over and got out of the truck.

The silence was the worst part. No hum of electricity. No distant traffic. Iron City had become a ghost town in an instant.

His phone buzzed. Elena.

"The substation is destroyed. He's got teams hitting five neighborhoods simultaneously. Gas lines. Transformer boxes. Storage tanks. It's chaos."

"Where are you?"

"Central command. We have reports of fires spreading across the south end. We don't have enough trucks to cover them all."

"Give me the five locations."

She read them off. Dominic memorized them.

"I'm going dark," he said. "I'll find him."

"Dominic—"

"I know what I'm doing."

He hung up, pulled the mask over his face, and started running. The suit felt right. The fire inside him, the tiny spark that had grown in the past days, was burning brighter.

Chapter 39: Arson

The fires were everywhere. Dominic moved through the darkened streets, following the glow of flames. The Inferno's teams were fast and organized — small cells of three to four people, each carrying accelerant and igniters. They hit a gas station, a grocery store, an apartment building.

Dominic intercepted one team at a laundromat on 14th Street. Three figures in dark clothing, one holding a canister, the others spreading accelerant across the floor.

He stepped through the broken front window. "Put it down."

The one with the canister turned. He was young. Twenty, maybe. His eyes were wide, his breathing fast.

"Who the hell are you?"

"Someone who doesn't want to hurt you. But I will."

The young man laughed, but it was shaky. "You're just one guy."

"You're right." Dominic raised his hand. "But I'm not just a guy."

He opened his palm and a column of fire shot upward, illuminating the room with orange light. The three arsonists stumbled back. One dropped his igniter. The canister clattered to the floor.

"The Inferno," Dominic said. "Where is he?"

The young man pointed east. "The old school. St. Catherine's. He said we'd meet him there after."

Dominic extinguished the flame. "Call the police. Tell them where you are."

He left them standing in the dark, staring after him.

Chapter 40: Homecoming

St. Catherine's Youth Home sat dark at the top of the hill. The chain-link fence had been torn down. The front door stood open. Light flickered from within — not electric, but the warm, dancing light of flame.

Dominic walked up the hill slowly, his boots crunching on the gravel. The air smelled of gasoline and kerosene. The building was soaked in accelerant. One spark and it would go up like a bomb.

He stopped at the entrance. "Leo."

A voice came from inside. "Come in, Phoenix. I've been waiting."

He stepped through the door.

The lobby was transformed. Candles lined every surface, hundreds of them, filling the space with soft light and the scent of wax. The floor had been cleared of debris. In the center of the room, a man sat in a wooden chair.

He was young. Younger than Dominic had expected. Twenty-three, maybe twenty-four. His face was a map of scar tissue, the skin pulled tight and shiny across his cheekbones. His hands were bandaged to the wrists.

Leo Marchetti looked up and smiled. "You came."

"I always come."

"Not always." Leo stood. "But you're here now. That's what matters."

Dominic studied his face, searching for the boy he remembered. "Alan Voss did this to you."

"Alan Voss showed me the truth." Leo spread his arms. "Fire doesn't destroy, Phoenix. Fire transforms. And I'm going to transform this whole city."

Chapter 41: Lair

Dominic walked through the building behind Leo, past classrooms turned into workshops, dormitories converted into laboratories. The Inferno had been living here for months, building his arsenal, planning every attack.

"Iron City was founded by industry," Leo said. "Steel mills. Factories. The whole city was built on fire. But then they forgot. They turned it into offices and condos and coffee shops. They sanded off the edges."

"You're burning it because it's boring?"

"I'm burning it because it's false." Leo stopped at a doorway. "In here."

The room had been the chapel. Now it was a throne room of scorched metal and melted plastic. In the center, a ring of gasoline-soaked wood surrounded a metal pillar. On the pillar, a single object: the gold mask that Phoenix had worn fifteen years ago.

"I kept it," Leo said. "Alan gave it to me. After the fire."

Dominic felt something crack inside him. "He took my mask."

"He took everything from you. That's what he did. He took the boy you tried to save, and he turned him into this." Leo gestured at himself. "And then he waited for you to come back."

"Where is Alan now?"

"Dead." Leo smiled. "I burned him. Two years ago. It was the first fire I set for myself."

Dominic absorbed the information. Alan Voss was gone. There was no mastermind left except the one standing in front of him.

"It ends tonight, Leo."

"I know." Leo picked up a lighter. "One way or another."

Chapter 42: Revelation

They stood facing each other in the candlelit chapel, the gold mask between them on its pillar. Dominic heard the building settling around them, the wood expanding and contracting.

"Why the riddles?" Dominic asked. "Why the theatrics?"

"Because you taught me that symbols matter." Leo turned the lighter over in his bandaged fingers. "When you visited me, you always wore the mask. You never showed me your face. You were a symbol. I needed to become one too."

"I showed you my face once."

Leo's smile flickered. "Yes. The night of the fire. You came without the mask. You looked human. And then you left."

"I didn't leave. The building—"

"You chose the building over me." Leo's voice stayed level. "You could have found me. You could have pushed harder. But you stopped. You gave up. And Alan was there to pick up the pieces."

Dominic wanted to deny it. But the words lodged in his throat.

"You're right," he said. "I gave up. I should have found you. I should have tried harder. But I was young, and I was scared, and I failed you." He took a step forward. "I'm here now."

"You're too late."

"I know. But I'm here anyway."

Leo stared at him. The lighter trembled in his hand.

"Do you know what it's like," Leo whispered, "to be nothing? To have your entire life erased? To be declared dead and watch the world move on without you?"

"Yes." Dominic's voice was rough. "I do."

Leo's composure cracked. Something raw and young flickered in his scarred face.

"Why didn't you come back for me?"

Dominic had no answer that would be enough.

Chapter 43: Inferno

Leo dropped the lighter.

The flame caught the gasoline trail instantly, a blue-white line that raced across the floor and ignited the ring of wood around the pillar. The fire spread up the walls, finding the accelerant that had been soaked into every surface.

Dominic moved without thinking. He grabbed Leo by the arm and pulled him away from the flames.

"Let go of me!"

"The building's going to collapse."

"That's the point!"

Dominic dragged him toward the exit. The fire was spreading faster than it should, the accelerant turning the chapel into an oven. The gold mask on the pillar began to warp and melt.

Leo struggled, twisting in Dominic's grip. "You don't get to save me now. Not after all these years."

"I'm not saving you. I'm stopping you from dying like a fool."

The ceiling above them groaned. A section of burning plaster fell between them and the door. Dominic released Leo and raised his hand. The fire parted, a tunnel of clear air opening through the flames.

Leo stared. "Your powers."

"They came back."

Dominic pushed Leo through the gap and followed, the fire closing behind them like a curtain.

The corridor was fully involved. Smoke filled the space from ceiling to floor. Dominic dropped to his knees and pulled Leo down with him.

"Stay low. Breathe through your sleeve."

"Why are you helping me?"

"Because you're still the boy I visited." Dominic coughed. "You're still the one I failed. And I'm not going to fail again."

Chapter 44: Collapse

The building came apart around them. Dominic crawled through the smoke, one hand on Leo's collar, pulling him forward. The floor shuddered. A section of wall collapsed behind them, and the heat surged.

Elena's voice came through his earpiece, barely audible over the roar.

"Dominic. I'm at the base of the hill. The building is going. Get out."

"There's too much accelerant. We're not going to make it through the front."

"Then find another way."

Dominic's mind raced. He knew this building. He had memorized its layout years ago. The basement had a coal chute that opened onto the east side, where the ground sloped away from the structure.

"This way."

He changed direction, pulling Leo down a side corridor toward the stairwell. The stairs were intact but the basement hallway was filling with smoke. He found the coal chute door, rusted shut.

"We need to open it."

Leo stared at the door. "Together?"

"Together."

They braced their shoulders against the metal and pushed. The door groaned, resisted, then gave way with a screech of corroded hinges. Cold air rushed in.

Dominic pushed Leo through first, then followed, landing hard on the slope below. He scrambled down the hill, pulling Leo with him, as St. Catherine's Youth Home collapsed behind them in a column of flame and sparks.

They lay in the grass, coughing, covered in soot, watching the building burn.

Chapter 45: Rescue

Elena reached them first. She ran up the hill with a medical kit, her flashlight beam cutting through the smoke. Dominic was sitting up, one arm wrapped around his ribs. Leo lay on his back, staring at the sky, his bandaged hands pressed to his chest.

"Are you hurt?" Elena kneeled beside Dominic.

"Ribs. Maybe cracked. I'll live."

She turned to Leo. "And you?"

Leo did not answer. He was watching the fire consume St. Catherine's, the flames reflected in his scarred eyes. His expression was unreadable.

"Leo." Elena's voice was firm. "Do you need medical attention?"

"I don't need anything."

Dominic struggled to his feet. He stood over Leo, his shadow falling across the younger man's face.

"It's over."

"You stopped one fire." Leo's voice was hollow. "There will be others."

"Not from you."

Leo did not argue. He closed his eyes.

Elena called in the arrest over the radio. Two uniforms appeared at the base of the hill and began the climb up.

"Dominic." Elena's voice was quiet. "You saved him."

"I pulled him out of a building."

"That's saving."

Dominic watched the uniforms approach. He had done this dance before, fifteen years ago, handing criminals to the system and hoping it held. "It doesn't feel like it."

"Give it time."

He watched them take Leo into custody and felt nothing — no triumph, no relief. Just the hollow ache of a debt that could never be fully repaid.

Chapter 46: Firefight

The fight came after the arrest, in the ambulance bay where Leo was being treated for minor burns. He had been handcuffed to the gurney, but the cuffs were standard issue, and Leo had been planning for this moment for years.

He slipped them.

He grabbed a scalpel from the tray beside him and held it to the paramedic's throat.

"Everyone back."

The ambulance bay emptied. Dominic, who had been receiving treatment in the next bay, stepped out with his hands raised.

"Leo. Put it down."

"I'm not going to prison."

"You're not going to die here either."

"Those aren't the only options." Leo's eyes were wild, calculating. "I can disappear. I've done it before."

"That was Alan Voss's doing. You don't have him anymore."

"I never needed him."

Dominic took a slow step forward. "You told me fire transforms. But you're standing in a room full of people who are alive because you chose not to kill them. That means something."

"It means I was sloppy."

"It means you're not what Alan made you."

The paramedic was shaking, the scalpel dimpling the skin of her throat. Leo's hand was steady. Dominic watched his eyes and saw the war happening behind them.

Then Leo's arm lowered. The scalpel clattered to the floor.

"Take me," he said. "Just get me out of this room."

The paramedic stumbled away. Leo held out his wrists. Dominic closed the cuffs around them himself.

Chapter 47: Choice

The transport van took Leo to the county detention center. Dominic rode in the front passenger seat, watching the darkened streets slide past. The city was still without power, but emergency generators hummed in the buildings they passed.

"You could have let me die," Leo said from the back. "In the building. You could have walked away."

"I could have."

"Why didn't you?"

Dominic was quiet for a long moment. The question deserved an honest answer, and he had spent enough of his life running from honesty.

"Because I've spent fifteen years wishing I had done more. And I was tired of living with that wish."

"That's not a good reason."

"It's the only one I've got."

They drove in silence for a while. The detention center appeared ahead, a concrete block with floodlights and razor wire.

"Will you visit me?" Leo asked.

The question caught Dominic off guard. "In prison?"

"Alan visited me. Every week for two years. It's how he controlled me." Leo's voice was flat. "You don't have to visit. But I'd rather have you in my head than him."

Dominic did not answer. The van pulled through the gate.

He would think about the question later. He would think about it for a long time.

Chapter 48: Carried

The hearing took place the next morning, after the sun came up and the city began assessing the damage from the night. Leo was arraigned on thirty-seven counts, including arson, attempted murder, and terrorism. He did not speak during the proceedings.

Dominic sat in the back of the courtroom, out of uniform. Elena sat beside him.

"You look like hell," she said.

"I feel like hell."

"You should see a doctor about those ribs."

"I'll get to it."

The judge set bail at zero. Leo was led away in chains. As he passed Dominic's row, he stopped.

"The night of the Hargrove fire," Leo said. "I saw you. Through the window. You were reaching for me. And then the floor gave way. You didn't stop reaching."

"I couldn't reach you."

"You tried." Leo's voice cracked. "That's more than anyone else ever did."

He was led away. Dominic sat in the hard wooden seat, his ribs aching, his eyes burning.

"The system will process him," Elena said. "He'll get a trial. He'll get a sentence. That's how it works."

"And then what?"

"Then we see if he can become something else." She put a hand on his shoulder. "He's right."

"It doesn't feel like enough."

"It never does." She stood. "Come on. I'll buy you breakfast."

Chapter 49: Dawn

The power came back at 4:37 AM. Dominic was sitting on the steps of Station 4, a cup of coffee cooling in his hands, when the lights flickered on across the city. Block by block, Iron City emerged from the dark.

He had not slept. His ribs were wrapped, his face was bruised, and his hands still smelled of smoke. But he was alive, and the city was still standing.

Danny DeSantos sat down beside him. "Captain. You look like you went ten rounds with a building."

"Felt like it."

"The news is saying the Inferno got caught because an anonymous tip led the police to St. Catherine's."

"Anonymous tips are the best kind."

Danny was quiet for a moment. "I saw what happened in the kitchen. The fire. You raised your hand and it stopped."

Dominic did not deny it. He had known this conversation would come.

"I know who you were," Danny said. "Or who you are. I put it together after I saw the news footage from the library. The way you moved. The—" He stopped. "I'm not going to tell anyone."

"Thank you."

"I'm not doing it for you. I'm doing it because if people knew, they'd make you into something you don't want to be." Danny stood. "You're a good captain. That's enough for me."

He walked back inside. Dominic stayed on the steps and watched the sun rise over the city he had spent his life protecting.

Chapter 50: Rooftop

Dominic climbed the Meridian Building one last time. The roof door was open, the padlock broken. The circle of stones was still there, the ash scattered by the wind.

He stood at the edge and looked at Iron City.

The scars were visible even from here. The clock tower was a blackened skeleton. The library's east wing was a gap in the skyline. The bridge was under repair, cranes and lights clustered around the break. But the city was alive. People were moving. Traffic was flowing. Life was continuing.

He heard footsteps behind him. He did not turn.

"You came back," Elena said.

"I wanted to see it. All of it."

"It looks different in daylight."

"It always does."

She stood beside him. "Leo is being transferred to a state facility. Maximum security psychiatric unit. He'll be there for a long time."

"I know."

"He asked if you visited yet."

Dominic was quiet. "I will. Eventually."

"That's not a yes."

"It's not a no either."

Elena nodded. She did not push.

Below them, a siren started somewhere. An engine rumbled. Iron City was waking up, and there would be fires to fight. There would always be fires.

Dominic looked at the sunrise spreading across the sky, bands of red and orange and gold. The phoenix emblem on his chest caught the light.

"I think I'm going to stay," he said. "Here. In the city. Do the job."

"That sounds right."

"Yeah." He watched the sun clear the rooftops. "It does."

Below, on the street, Danny DeSantos looked up from the fire truck and saw the silhouette on the roof. He smiled to himself and climbed into the cab.

The day was just beginning.
