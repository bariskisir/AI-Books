# The Devil's Trombone — Outline

## Premise

1950s New Orleans. Blue Madere is a journeyman trombone player who can blow with anyone in the Quarter but never breaks through. Desperate and drunk, he walks to the crossroads at St. Claude and Urquhart one midnight and makes an offer. Mama Legba appears. She gives him the deal: his slide will sing like Gabriel's horn, but every note he plays costs him one year of life. Blue signs in blood. His playing becomes legendary — crowds weep, rivals quit, records sell. But each gig drains him. He grows thinner, older, stranger. His rival Dizzy figures out the price. Blue must decide how many years he is willing to burn before the trombone falls silent forever.

## Core Cast

- **Blue Madere** — Trombone player, late twenties, raw talent, a quiet desperation behind his eyes. Dark skin, long fingers, a laugh that sounds like rust.
- **Mama Legba** — The old woman at the crossroads. She appears as a Creole grandmother in a white dress. She does not lie. She does not bargain twice.
- **Dizzy Castain** — Trumpet player, Blue's rival and sometime-friend. Sharp dresser, sharper tongue. The first to notice something is wrong with Blue.

## 50 Chapter Plan

### Part I: The Crossroads (Chapters 1–10)

1. **The Hum** — Blue wakes in a boarding house on Rampart Street. He hears a low hum in his chest like a second heartbeat. It is the sound of his own life running out.
2. **Rampart Street** — Blue walks through the French Quarter at dawn. The neon is dying. He thinks about his father, who played cornet and died young.
3. **The Spotted Cat** — Blue sits in on a late set. His playing is competent but ordinary. The crowd barely claps. Dizzy watches from the bar.
4. **Dizzy's Mouth** — Dizzy corners Blue after the set. He needles him. "You sound like a man who already gave up." Blue walks out into the rain.
5. **The Offer** — A stranger at a bar tells Blue about the crossroads. "Mama Legba gives what you ask. She takes what she wants."
6. **St. Claude Midnight** — Blue walks to the crossroads at St. Claude and Urquhart. He waits. The streetlights buzz.
7. **Mama Legba** — She appears from the shadows. Old woman, white dress, eyes like wet stones. She names the terms: one year per note.
8. **The Signature** — Blue signs his name in a ledger. The ink is his blood. Mama Legba smiles and vanishes.
9. **First Note** — Blue plays a single B-flat on his trombone in an empty lot. The note rings like a bell through the city. He feels something leave his body.
10. **The Call** — The next morning, a club owner named Sully offers Blue a gig at the Dreamland. His life is about to change.

### Part II: The Rise (Chapters 11–20)

11. **Dreamland** — Blue's first set at the Dreamland. He plays "St. James Infirmary" and the room stops breathing. Sully signs him for a week.
12. **The Whisper** — Word moves through the Quarter. People say they heard something holy. Dizzy comes to listen.
13. **Dizzy Listens** — Dizzy stands in the back of Dreamland. He watches Blue's hands. He watches Blue's face. Something is wrong.
14. **The Recording** — A producer from Chicago offers Blue a recording contract. Blue signs without reading the fine print.
15. **Session One** — Blue cuts four sides at Cosimo's studio. The engineer says he has never heard a horn sound like that.
16. **The Weight** — Blue wakes tired. He is twenty-seven but his knees ache. He counts the notes from last night. Eighty-three. Eighty-three years.
17. **Mama Visits** — Mama Legba appears in his dressing room mirror. She reminds him the deal is still running. He tells her to leave.
18. **Dizzy's Question** — Dizzy buys Blue a drink. "You look like death, man. What did you do?" Blue says nothing.
19. **The Record Drops** — Blue's 78 hits the shops. It sells out in a day. Sully doubles his rate.
20. **Eighty-Fourth Note** — Blue plays a solo that lasts three minutes. Two hundred and ten notes. He coughs blood into his handkerchief.

### Part III: The Cost (Chapters 21–30)

21. **The Doctor** — A French Quarter doctor tells Blue his heart is aging fast. "You are forty-five years old on the inside."
22. **Dizzy Knows** — Dizzy corners Blue in the alley behind Dreamland. He has been counting. "You played four hundred notes last night. That's four hundred years."
23. **The Truth** — Blue tells Dizzy everything. The crossroads. Mama Legba. The ledger. Dizzy does not laugh.
24. **The Offer** — Dizzy offers to help. He knows an old hoodoo woman in the Treme. "She might know a way out."
25. **Treme** — Blue and Dizzy walk into the Treme. The old woman, Miss Cora, reads bones. She tells Blue the deal cannot be broken. But it can be transferred.
26. **The Price of Transfer** — Miss Cora explains: someone else must play the trombone in Blue's place. Take up the deal willingly. Blue refuses.
27. **The Tour** — Blue goes on the road. Houston, Dallas, Memphis. He plays bigger rooms. He loses more years.
28. **The Mirror** — Blue sees himself in a hotel mirror. He looks fifty. His hair is gray. He has been playing for six months.
29. **The Letter** — Blue writes a letter to his mother that he never sends. He tells her he is sorry.
30. **Counting the Cost** — Blue calculates he has played roughly eight thousand notes since the deal. He has perhaps two years left at this rate.

### Part IV: The Reckoning (Chapters 31–40)

31. **Dizzy's Plan** — Dizzy has an idea. What if Blue plays one final concert — the greatest set New Orleans has ever heard — and then never plays again?
32. **The Final Show** — Sully books the Municipal Auditorium. Two thousand seats. Blue will headline with a full band.
33. **Rehearsal** — Blue rehearses with the band. His playing is still transcendent. The pianist cries.
34. **Mama Legba Returns** — Mama Legba appears to Blue in his dressing room. She warns him that quitting does not break the deal. The notes already played are spent. The years are gone.
35. **The Night Before** — Blue walks through the Quarter alone. He stops at the cathedral on St. Louis. He does not go inside.
36. **Dizzy's Confession** — Dizzy admits he envied Blue. That he wanted the deal for himself once. Blue forgives him.
37. **The Crowd** — The auditorium fills. People line up around the block. Dizzy tunes his trumpet in the wings.
38. **First Set** — Blue plays. The music is fire. Every note is a year. He does not slow down.
39. **The Solo** — Thirty-two bars. Blue plays a solo that contains everything he has. The melody weeps. The crowd is silent.
40. **The Last Note** — Blue holds the final note. A long, pure tone. He holds it until his lungs give out. Then he lowers the slide.

### Part V: The Silence (Chapters 41–50)

41. **Curtain** — The crowd erupts. Blue walks off stage. Dizzy catches him as his knees buckle.
42. **The Aftermath** — Blue is alive. He collapses in the dressing room. Dizzy calls a doctor.
43. **Mama Legba's Last Visit** — Mama Legba appears in the hospital room. She tells him the ledger is closed. He played 11,843 notes. She takes her payment and leaves.
44. **The Long Sleep** — Blue is unconscious for three days. The city waits. The newspapers run headlines.
45. **Waking** — Blue wakes. He is weak, but he is alive. His hair is white. His hands shake.
46. **The Trombone** — Dizzy brings Blue's trombone to the hospital. Blue holds it. He does not raise it to his lips.
47. **Walking Out** — Blue leaves the hospital. He walks down Rampart Street in the sun. The hum in his chest is gone.
48. **The New Deal** — Dizzy tells Blue he has been offered a teaching job at a music school for kids. Blue does not know if he can teach what he cannot play.
49. **The Silence** — Blue sits on his porch. He does not play. He listens to the city. The traffic. The river. The birds. He is learning to hear differently.
50. **The Hum Returns** — Blue picks up the trombone. He does not play a note. He just holds the mouthpiece to his lips and breathes. The hum is not the deal. The hum was always his. He will teach. He will live.
