Chapter 1: The Hum

Blue Madere woke to a sound in his chest. It was low and constant, like a second heartbeat under the first. He pressed his palm flat against his sternum. The vibration hummed through his ribs.

He sat up on the narrow bed. The room was dark. A fan blade turned slow overhead. Outside, Rampart Street was waking up — a truck rattling past, a woman calling to someone from a window.

He had heard the hum before. It started three nights ago, after the rain. He thought it was his imagination. He thought it was the gin. But the gin wore off, and the hum stayed.

Blue reached for his trombone case beside the bed. He did not open it. He just put his hand on the worn leather and felt the shape inside.

The hum vibrated against his fingers.

He was twenty-seven years old. He had been playing since he was twelve. He had played in dives and dance halls and once on a street corner for nickels. He had never sounded the way he wanted to sound.

The hum said: you will.

He stood up and splashed water on his face from the basin. The mirror showed him a tired man with deep-set eyes and a mouth that did not smile much.

He dressed. He left the room. The trombone case swung at his side like a promise.

The morning was gray and humid. The Mississippi smell hung in the air. Blue walked toward the Quarter, past the shuttered shops and the men sleeping in doorways. The hum in his chest kept time with his footsteps.

He did not know what it was yet. He only knew it was growing louder.

Chapter 2: Rampart Street

Blue walked the length of Rampart before the sun burned through. He passed the pawn shops and the funeral parlor and the bar where he had spent most of his money last month.

The neon signs were dark. A cat picked through a trash can. A man in a straw hat swept the sidewalk in front of a shoe repair shop.

Blue stopped at the coffee stand on Ursulines. The woman behind the counter knew him. She poured black coffee into a tin cup without asking.

"You look thin," she said.

"I'm fine, Celie."

"You look like you haven't slept in a week."

He paid her and drank the coffee standing up. It was bitter and hot. He let it burn his tongue.

His father had played cornet. That was what people said, anyway. Blue did not remember. His father died when Blue was three. Pneumonia. Twenty-nine years old.

The only thing Blue had of his father was a photograph, creased down the middle, and the understanding that music ran in the blood like a fever.

His mother told him once: "He played until he couldn't breathe. Then he kept playing."

Blue finished the coffee and handed back the cup. He walked toward the river.

The hum in his chest had settled into a steady drone. It did not bother him anymore. It felt like company.

He passed St. Louis Cathedral. The bells were ringing. He stopped and listened.

The bells sounded like something calling his name.

Chapter 3: The Spotted Cat

The Spotted Cat was a narrow club on Bourbon with a cracked sign and a piano that had not been tuned since Truman was in office. Blue walked in at ten in the evening. The place was half full.

The house band was playing a slow blues. The trombonist was a fat man named Willie who played everything a half-step behind the beat. It should have sounded wrong, but it worked. Willie made the music drag its feet like a tired man walking home.

Blue found a stool at the bar. The bartender slid him a glass of rye without asking.

"Willie's off at midnight," the bartender said. "You sitting in?"

"Yeah."

Blue nursed the drink. He watched Willie's slide move slow and easy. The notes came out fat and round. The crowd nodded along. They were not listening. They were just existing in the same room as the music.

That was the difference. Blue wanted them to listen.

When Willie finished his set, Blue stepped up. He nodded at the piano player. "Slow B-flat."

The piano player raised an eyebrow. "You sure? Willie just—"

"I'm sure."

They counted in. Blue raised the horn.

The notes came out clean but thin. He played what he knew: the standard lines, the tried phrases, the things he had played a thousand times. It was competent. It was forgettable.

The crowd talked over him.

He finished the set and stepped down. Dizzy Castain was leaning against the wall near the bathrooms, a trumpet case at his feet, a cigarette burning between his fingers.

"Solid," Dizzy said. His voice was flat.

Blue did not answer.

"You sound like a man who already gave up," Dizzy said. He dropped the cigarette and crushed it with his shoe. "I heard you were better than that."

"I am," Blue said.

"Then play better."

Dizzy walked out. The door swung shut behind him.

Blue stood alone in the narrow hallway. The hum in his chest was louder than it had ever been.

Chapter 4: Dizzy's Mouth

Blue found Dizzy at the corner of Bourbon and Iberville. Dizzy was leaning against a lamppost, lighting another cigarette. His suit was sharp. His shoes were polished. He looked like a man who belonged in a better city.

"You follow me out?" Dizzy asked.

"You wanted to say something in there. Say it."

Dizzy took a long drag. The smoke curled around his face. "I been watching you since you got here, Blue. You got something. But you keep it locked up."

"I play what I can play."

"That's the problem. You play what you can play. You don't play what you hear."

Blue felt the words land like a punch. "What do you hear?"

Dizzy tapped his temple. "Inside my head, I hear something so beautiful it scares me. But I don't got the horn to get it out. You got the horn. But you ain't got the courage."

The street was empty. The music from the clubs bled through the walls, muted and distant.

"You don't know me," Blue said.

"I know enough. I know you're scared of what you could be."

Dizzy threw the cigarette into the gutter. "There's a price for being great, Blue. Most people ain't willing to pay it."

"What's the price?"

Dizzy laughed. It was not a kind laugh. "Everything."

He turned and walked away. His footsteps echoed down the empty street.

Blue stood under the lamppost. The hum in his chest was so loud now he could barely hear the music from the clubs.

He walked back to his room. He did not sleep.

Chapter 5: The Offer

Two nights later, Blue sat alone at a bar on Conti Street. The place was called the Half Note. It was the kind of bar where men went to drink in peace and no one asked questions.

A man sat down next to him. Old. White hair, dark skin, a scar running from his eyebrow to his jaw. He ordered a glass of milk.

"You the trombonist," the old man said. It was not a question.

"Who wants to know?"

"I know people. I hear things. You been sounding different lately."

"I sound the same."

"No. You sound hungry. That's different."

Blue waited. The old man drank his milk.

"There's a woman in the Treme," the old man said. "They call her Mama Legba. She helps people who want things bad enough."

"What kind of things?"

"The kind you can't get on your own."

The old man finished his milk and stood up. He put a folded piece of paper on the bar. "That's where to find her. Don't go unless you mean it."

He walked out.

Blue unfolded the paper. An address was written in pencil: St. Claude and Urquhart. Midnight.

He folded the paper and put it in his pocket. He finished his drink. His hand did not shake when he paid.

He walked home through the dark streets. The paper burned against his thigh like a brand.

Chapter 6: St. Claude Midnight

Blue stood at the corner of St. Claude and Urquhart. The streetlight above him buzzed and flickered. The intersection was empty. No cars. No people. Just the hum of the light and the distant sound of a dog barking.

He had come alone. He had left the trombone in his room.

The air was warm and wet. He checked his watch. Midnight.

Nothing happened.

He waited. The minutes passed. He started to laugh at himself. A grown man standing at a crossroads in the middle of the night, waiting for a ghost story.

He turned to leave.

"There you are."

The voice came from behind him. He turned.

The woman was old. Her hair was white and braided. She wore a white dress that did not seem to touch the ground. Her eyes were dark and wet, like stones at the bottom of a river.

"You are Blue Madere," she said.

"How do you know my name?"

"I know many things. That is not the most interesting of them."

She stepped closer. She was small, barely up to his shoulder. But the air around her felt heavy, like the pressure before a storm.

"You came for a deal," she said. "I can give you what you want. But there is always a cost."

"I don't have much money."

She smiled. Her teeth were white. "I do not want money."

"Then what do you want?"

"Time."

She reached out and touched his chest. Her fingers were cold. The hum in his chest rose to meet her touch.

"Every note you play will take one year from your life," she said. "That is the price. You will play like no one has ever played. But each note costs you."

"How many notes in a song?"

"That depends on how long you want to live."

Blue looked at her. The streetlight flickered above them.

"I'll take the deal," he said.

Chapter 7: Mama Legba

Mama Legba produced a ledger from the folds of her dress. It was bound in leather so dark it looked like dried blood. She opened it to a blank page.

"Your name," she said.

Blue took the pen she offered. The nib was sharp. He wrote his name. The ink came out dark red.

"Blood writes the contract," Mama Legba said. "Yours is already in the ink."

She closed the ledger and tucked it away. She reached up and touched Blue's forehead with her thumb. Her touch was cold.

"It is done. You will feel the change when you play your first note."

"Can I undo it?"

"No one has ever found a way."

She began to walk away. Her white dress moved like fog.

"Wait," Blue said. "How long do I have?"

She stopped but did not turn. "That depends on how many notes you play. I suggest you make each one count."

She faded into the darkness. One moment she was there. The next, the street was empty.

Blue stood alone at the crossroads. The streetlight hummed overhead. He felt different. He felt lighter, somehow, as if something had been removed from inside him.

He walked home. His footsteps were steady.

He did not sleep. He sat in the dark and waited for morning.

Chapter 8: The Signature

Dawn came gray and damp. Blue walked to the lot behind the old parish church. It was empty — weeds and broken glass and the skeleton of a burned-out car.

He had brought the trombone.

He raised the horn to his lips. His hands were steady. His breath was deep.

He pressed the mouthpiece to his lips. The metal was cold.

He played a single note. B-flat. The note rang out across the empty lot, clear and pure and so full of sound that the air itself seemed to vibrate.

The note hung in the air for a long time. It bounced off the buildings. It seemed to travel for miles before it faded.

Blue lowered the horn. His ears rang.

He felt it then — a pull inside his chest, as if something had been unhooked. A thread had been cut.

One year, he thought. That was one year.

He raised the horn again. He played a scale. Each note was a separate, perfect thing. Each note cost him.

He played until tears ran down his face. He played until his arms ached. He played until he could not breathe.

And when he stopped, the silence was the loudest thing he had ever heard.

He walked back to the Quarter. The street was coming alive. He passed people who looked at him differently. They did not know why. But they looked.

He looked different too. He could feel it in his bones.

Chapter 9: First Note

The lot was quiet now. Blue stood alone with the horn in his hands. The note he had played still echoed in his memory.

He played again. A single C. The sound split the morning air. It was stronger than the B-flat. Fuller. It came from somewhere deeper.

He felt the pull again. Another thread snapped inside him.

He played a melody. Just eight bars. Something he had been working on for months but could never quite get right. It came out perfect. Every note in its place. Every note singing.

He stopped. His hands were shaking.

He looked down at his fingers. They looked the same. He looked at his reflection in a puddle. He looked the same.

But something was different. Something underneath.

He packed the trombone and walked back toward Rampart. The sun was fully up now. The Quarter was waking. He passed a bar where a man was hosing down the sidewalk. The man looked up.

"You all right, brother?"

"Yeah," Blue said. "I'm fine."

The man watched him pass. Blue could feel the man's eyes on his back.

He went up to his room. He sat on the bed. He opened the case and looked at the trombone.

The brass gleamed. It had never gleamed like that before.

He did not play again that day. He was afraid of what would happen if he did.

Chapter 10: The Call

The knock came at noon. Blue opened the door to find a short white man in a cheap suit, sweating through his collar.

"Blue Madere?"

"Yeah."

"I'm Sully. I run the Dreamland over on Franklin."

Blue knew the Dreamland. Everybody did. It was the best jazz club in the Quarter. Bigger than the Spotted Cat. Better crowd.

"I heard you play last night," Sully said. "At the Cat."

"You were there?"

"Standing in the back. You were nothing special."

Blue waited.

"Then I heard you this morning. In that lot behind the church. I don't know what happened between last night and this morning, but something happened."

Sully wiped his forehead with a handkerchief. "I'm offering you a gig. Friday night. Opening slot. You bring your horn and you play like you played this morning, and I'll pay you triple scale."

"Friday," Blue repeated.

"You want it or not?"

Blue looked at his trombone case. He thought about the threads inside him, snapping one by one.

"Yeah," he said. "I want it."

Sully nodded and left.

Blue closed the door. He stood in the dark room and listened to his heart beat.

The hum in his chest was completely silent now. It had been replaced by something else.

Something that sounded like a countdown.

Chapter 11: Dreamland

Friday came fast. Blue stood in the wings of the Dreamland stage, the trombone in his hands. The club was full. Cigarette smoke hung in the air like fog. The piano player was finishing his solo.

Sully appeared beside him. "You're on after this. Five minutes."

Blue nodded. He could not speak.

The piano player hit his final chord. The crowd clapped. Sully stepped to the microphone.

"Ladies and gentlemen. You know him from around the Quarter. He's been playing in this city for years. But I promise you, you have never heard him like this. Mr. Blue Madere."

Blue walked onto the stage. The lights were hot. The crowd was a dark blur.

He raised the trombone.

The first note came out like nothing he had ever heard. It was not just sound. It was presence. It filled the room. It filled the people. It filled every gap and crack.

He played "St. James Infirmary." Slow. The notes were long and fat and full of things that had no name.

The crowd went silent. Not the way crowds go silent for good music. The way they go silent when they are afraid to breathe.

Blue played through the song. Each note cost him. He did not care.

When he finished, no one clapped. They just sat there, stunned.

Then a woman in the front row started to cry.

The applause came after. It was louder than anything Blue had ever heard.

He walked off the stage. His legs were shaking. His heart was pounding.

He had played two hundred and forty notes.

Chapter 12: The Whisper

The next morning, the Quarter was buzzing. People who had been at the Dreamland told people who had not. The story grew with each telling.

They said the trombonist played like an angel.

They said the trombonist played like a man possessed.

They said the trombonist played like he was dying.

Blue heard the whispers from his window. He sat on the bed with the trombone across his knees. He had not played since the show.

Two hundred and forty years, he thought. That is what I burned last night.

He did the math. Four minutes of music. Two hundred and forty years off his life. He could not afford many more sets like that.

He thought about his father, dead at twenty-nine. Blue was now older than his father had ever been. And yet he felt old. Not tired. Ancient.

A knock at the door.

It was Celie, from the coffee stand. She held a newspaper.

"You're in the paper," she said.

She handed it to him. The Times-Picayune. Page four. A short column about the show at the Dreamland. They called him "a revelation."

Blue handed the paper back.

"You okay?" Celie asked.

"I'm fine."

"You don't look fine."

"I look like a man who played a good show."

She studied his face. She did not believe him. But she let it go.

The hum in his chest was back. Low and steady. It was counting.

Chapter 13: Dizzy Listens

Dizzy Castain stood at the back of the Dreamland on Saturday night. He had come to hear Blue again. He had to know.

The set started. Blue played a slow blues in F. The notes came out like velvet smoke. The crowd swayed.

Dizzy watched. He watched Blue's hands. He watched Blue's breath. He watched the way Blue held the horn, as if it were the only thing keeping him upright.

The playing was beyond anything Dizzy had ever heard. It was not technique. It was not skill. It was something else. Something that came from a place music did not usually come from.

Dizzy counted the notes. One hundred and sixty in the first song alone.

After the set, Dizzy found Blue in the alley behind the club. Blue was leaning against the wall, breathing hard.

"You look like hell," Dizzy said.

"Thanks."

"That was the most beautiful thing I ever heard. And you look like hell."

Blue did not answer.

"What happened to you, Blue? Between the Spotted Cat and now?"

"Nothing."

"Bull."

Blue's eyes met Dizzy's. For a moment, Dizzy saw something in them. Fear. Deep and old.

Then Blue looked away.

"Stay out of it, Dizzy."

Dizzy lit a cigarette. He watched the smoke rise into the night.

"I can't stay out of it," he said. "Not anymore."

Chapter 14: The Recording

The producer from Chicago found Blue at the Dreamland. His name was Reese. He wore a Panama hat and a pinkie ring.

"I want to put you in a studio," Reese said. "Four sides. I'll press five hundred copies. If they sell, we do more."

Blue signed the contract without reading it. Reese slid a pen across the bar. Blue signed. The ink was black. His blood stayed where it belonged.

The session was set for Tuesday at Cosimo's Studio on Camp Street.

Tuesday morning, Blue arrived early. The studio was small. Microphones hung from the ceiling. The engineer was a thin man named Earl with a perpetual frown.

"You ever cut a record before?" Earl asked.

"No."

"Then do what I say and we'll get through this."

The band assembled. Piano, bass, drums, trumpet. Dizzy had agreed to play.

They ran through the first tune. "Delta Morning." Blue's original.

Earl listened from the booth. His frown deepened. Then it disappeared.

"That's the take," he said. "Next."

They cut four sides in three hours. Each one perfect. Blue played sparingly, choosing each note like a man choosing which coins to spend.

When they were done, Earl played the tape back. The sound filled the small room. Everyone was quiet.

"That's the best thing I ever recorded," Earl said.

Blue packed his trombone. He felt the weight of each note he had played, pressing down on him like years.

Chapter 15: Session One

The acetate disc spun on the turntable. Blue listened to his own playing coming out of the speakers. It did not sound like him. It sounded like someone else.

"You okay?" Dizzy was standing in the doorway.

"Just listening."

"You sound good."

"I sound like someone else."

Dizzy walked in and sat down. He put his trumpet case on the floor. "You ever think about how many notes you played today?"

"About eight hundred."

"That's a lot of anything."

Blue turned off the turntable. The silence was sudden and complete.

"I know what I'm doing," Blue said.

"Do you?"

Blue looked at Dizzy. The question hung in the air.

"I made a choice," Blue said. "I stand by it."

"A choice about what?"

Blue did not answer. He stood up and put the trombone in its case.

"I got to go."

He walked out into the afternoon sun. The street was bright. The air was hot. He felt hollowed out.

He walked to Celie's coffee stand and bought a cup. He sat on a crate and watched the people pass.

A woman walked by with a child. The child was laughing. Blue watched them until they turned the corner.

He thought about his mother. He thought about his father. He thought about the years he did not have.

He finished his coffee and walked home.

Chapter 16: The Weight

Blue woke before dawn. His knees ached. His back ached. He had slept ten hours but felt like he had not slept at all.

He sat up slowly. His joints creaked.

He counted the notes from the session. Eight hundred fourteen. Eight hundred fourteen years.

The number sat in his stomach like a stone.

He was twenty-seven. But his body felt fifty.

He went to the basin and looked in the mirror. His face was the same. But there was something in his eyes that had not been there before. Something old.

He touched his hair. A gray strand above his left ear.

He dressed and went out. The morning was cool. He walked to the pharmacy on Decatur and bought a bottle of aspirin.

The pharmacist looked at him. "You all right, son?"

"Fine. Just a long week."

"You look like you need more than aspirin."

Blue paid and left. He walked to the river and sat on a bench. The water was brown and slow. A barge moved past, heavy with cargo.

He thought about his father. Twenty-nine years old. Pneumonia.

Blue wondered if his father had known he was dying. If he had counted the days.

He opened the aspirin and shook two into his palm. He swallowed them dry.

The river kept moving. The barge disappeared around the bend.

Blue stood up and walked back to the Quarter. His joints still ached.

He had a show tonight.

Chapter 17: Mama Visits

Blue was in his dressing room at the Dreamland. He was tuning the trombone slide when he saw her reflection in the mirror.

Mama Legba stood behind him.

He spun around. The room was empty.

He looked back at the mirror. She was still there. Sitting in the chair he had not noticed before.

"You," he said.

"Me." She smiled. "You have been busy, Blue Madere."

"What do you want?"

"I came to check on my investment."

"I'm fine."

"You are not fine. You have played nearly three thousand notes. You have lost three thousand years."

"I didn't think it was literal."

"It is always literal." She leaned forward. Her eyes were dark stones. "I gave you a gift. You are using it. But you should know the ledger is adding up."

"I know."

"Do you? You have perhaps eight years left if you keep going this way. Maybe less."

Blue looked at his hands. "I can stop playing."

"You can. But you cannot un-play what you have already played."

She stood up. Her dress moved like water.

"I will come back when the ledger is full," she said. "Until then, keep playing. It is beautiful."

She faded from the mirror. The room was empty again.

Blue sat alone. The trombone rested across his knees.

He picked it up. He pressed the mouthpiece to his lips.

He did not play.

He sat there in the silence until the stage manager knocked. "Five minutes, Blue."

He went on stage. He played.

Each note was a year. Each note was a choice.

Chapter 18: Dizzy's Question

After the show, Dizzy caught Blue outside. The street was wet from a sudden rain. The neon signs reflected in the puddles.

"You been avoiding me," Dizzy said.

"I been busy."

"Busy dying."

Blue stopped walking. He turned to face Dizzy. "What did you say?"

"I said you look like you're dying. And I think I know why."

Blue did not answer.

"I counted tonight. Four hundred thirty-one notes. You played four hundred thirty-one notes. And I saw you before the show. You look worse every time."

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine. You look like a man who's burning through something he can't get back."

Dizzy stepped closer. "What did you do, Blue?"

The rain started again. A light mist.

"I made a deal," Blue said.

"What kind of deal?"

"The kind you can't undo."

Dizzy waited. The mist settled on both of them.

"Every note I play," Blue said. "One year off my life."

Dizzy stared at him. The words did not make sense at first. Then they did.

"Jesus," Dizzy said.

"Yeah."

"How many notes have you played?"

"Close to four thousand."

Dizzy did the math in his head. His face went pale.

"Blue..."

"I know."

They stood in the rain. The neon lights flickered.

"There has to be a way out," Dizzy said.

"There isn't."

"We'll find one."

Blue shook his head. But he did not argue.

Chapter 19: The Record Drops

The 78 arrived at the music shops on Wednesday. Blue's face stared out from the sleeve. He looked younger in the photograph. He looked like someone who had not yet made a deal.

The record sold out by Friday.

Reese called long distance from Chicago. "We need more sides. I got labels calling me from New York."

"Give me a week," Blue said.

"We don't have a week. This is happening now."

Blue hung up.

The record played on radios across the city. People hummed the melody. Someone had put it on the jukebox at the Half Note.

Blue heard it when he walked in for a drink. His own playing filled the room. He stood in the doorway and listened.

"Hey." The bartender nodded at him. "That's you."

"I know."

"Sounds like nothing else on earth."

Blue ordered a drink. He stood at the bar and listened to the record. It was good. It was better than good.

He had played that song three times in the studio. Eight minutes of music. Four hundred and eighty notes.

Four hundred and eighty years.

He finished his drink in one swallow. He left the bar while the record was still playing.

Outside, a man recognized him. "Hey! You're that trombonist!"

Blue kept walking.

"Hey! Play us something!"

Blue turned the corner. He broke into a run.

He ran until he could not hear the man's voice anymore.

Chapter 20: Eighty-Fourth Note

Sully booked Blue for a two-week run. Every night. Two sets.

Blue played the first set conservatively. Short solos. Long rests. He was learning to count.

Dizzy sat in the wings. He had a notebook. He was writing down the numbers.

Second set came. The crowd was loud. They wanted more.

Blue gave them more.

The solo came in the fourth number. A slow blues in E-flat. The piano player laid down the changes. Blue stepped forward.

He played eight bars. Sixteen. Thirty-two.

The notes poured out. He could not stop them. The music took over.

The crowd rose to their feet.

Blue kept playing. The slide moved like it had a mind of its own. The notes came faster and faster.

When he finally stopped, he was panting. The room was spinning.

He walked off stage. Dizzy caught him in the wings.

"Two hundred and ten notes," Dizzy said. "That solo was two hundred and ten notes."

Blue nodded. He could not speak.

He walked to the dressing room. He sat down. He coughed into his handkerchief.

It came away red.

He stared at the blood. It looked black in the dim light.

He folded the handkerchief and put it in his pocket.

He had another set in an hour.

Chapter 21: The Doctor

The doctor's office was a narrow room above a barbershop on Magazine Street. Dr. Boudreaux was old and tired. He had been delivering babies and sewing up knife wounds in the Quarter for forty years.

He listened to Blue's heart. He listened for a long time.

"Take off your shirt," he said.

Blue did. The doctor examined him. His hands were cold.

"When did your hair start going gray?"

"I don't know. A few weeks ago."

"You're twenty-seven?"

"Yes."

The doctor sat back. His face was unreadable.

"I've been doing this a long time," he said. "I've seen all kinds of things. But I've never seen a man your age with a heart like this."

"What's wrong with it?"

"It's worn. Like a man twice your age. Maybe older."

Blue put his shirt back on. His hands were steady.

"Is there anything you can do?"

"Rest. Eat well. Don't push yourself."

"That's it?"

"That's it."

Blue paid and left. He stood on the street. The sun was bright. People moved past him.

He was forty-five years old on the inside.

He walked to the river. He sat on the same bench as before.

He thought about Mama Legba. He thought about the ledger. He thought about the notes he had played and the years they had cost him.

He did not cry. He just sat there as the sun moved across the sky and the river kept flowing.

Chapter 22: Dizzy Knows

Dizzy found Blue on the bench by the river. He sat down without a word.

For a long time, neither of them spoke.

"Four hundred and thirty-one," Dizzy finally said. "Last night. Four hundred and thirty-one."

Blue did not answer.

"I've been counting since you told me. Every gig. Every note. Last night was the most you ever played in one set."

"It was a long set."

"Four hundred and thirty-one years, Blue. You burned four hundred and thirty-one years."

Blue looked at the river. "I know."

"You can't keep doing this."

"I don't know how to stop."

Dizzy turned to face him. His eyes were hard. "Then let me help."

"How?"

"There's a woman in the Treme. Miss Cora. She knows things."

"Hoodoo."

"Hoodoo, rootwork, whatever you want to call it. She helped my aunt when the doctors couldn't."

Blue shook his head. "The deal is the deal."

"The deal is a trap. And you're walking right into it."

Dizzy stood up. "Tomorrow. Noon. I'll take you."

He walked away.

Blue stayed on the bench. The sun was setting. The river was turning orange.

He watched the light fade.

He thought about his mother. He thought about the letter he should write.

He thought about how he would spend his last years.

Chapter 23: The Truth

They met at noon in front of St. Augustine Church. Dizzy was waiting. He had a bag of groceries in one arm.

"You brought food?" Blue said.

"Miss Cora likes visitors who bring things."

They walked into the Treme. The houses were painted in bright colors. Children played in the street. An old man sat on a porch, whittling a piece of wood.

Miss Cora's house was at the end of a narrow lane. Purple shutters. A blue door. A wind chime made of bones.

Dizzy knocked.

The door opened. The woman inside was older than Mama Legba. Her face was a map of wrinkles. Her eyes were milky with cataracts.

"Mr. Castain," she said. "You brought a friend."

"Miss Cora. This is Blue."

"Blue Madere." She said his name like she was tasting it. "I know who you are. I heard you play."

She stepped aside. "Come in."

The house smelled of herbs and candle wax. Bottles lined the shelves. Bundles of dried plants hung from the ceiling.

Miss Cora sat in a rocking chair. She gestured for them to sit on the floor.

"You made a deal," she said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"With Mama Legba."

"Yes."

Miss Cora nodded. She picked up a handful of bones from a bowl and let them fall on the table. She studied them for a long time.

"The deal cannot be broken," she said. "The notes are spent. The years are gone."

"I know."

"But the deal can be passed."

Blue looked up. "What do you mean?"

"Someone else can take it from you. Take up the trombone. Accept the terms."

"Who would do that?"

"Someone who wants what you have badly enough."

Blue shook his head. "I won't let anyone else—"

"That is your choice." Miss Cora's blind eyes found his. "But the offer stands."

Chapter 24: The Offer

Blue walked out of Miss Cora's house. The afternoon sun hit him like a wall. He stood on the porch, breathing.

Dizzy came out behind him. "She gave you a way."

"It's not a way. It's passing the poison."

"It's a chance."

"I'm not giving this to anyone else."

They walked in silence. The streets of the Treme were alive with music. Someone was playing piano through an open window.

"You might not have a choice," Dizzy said.

"Everyone has a choice."

"You chose to make the deal."

"I know."

"And now you're choosing to die."

Blue stopped. He turned to face Dizzy. "What do you want me to say? That I regret it? I don't. I played like no one has ever played. That was worth something."

"Was it worth your life?"

Blue did not answer. He could not.

They reached the edge of the Quarter. The Dreamland sign was visible down the street.

"I have a show tonight," Blue said.

"You can't—"

"I have a show."

He walked away.

The heat was thick. The air smelled of frying food and exhaust.

He went to his room. He opened the trombone case. The brass gleamed.

He picked up the horn. The weight was familiar.

He played a single note in the empty room. It filled the space like a living thing.

One more year gone.

He put the horn down.

He dressed for the show.

Chapter 25: Treme

Blue went back to Miss Cora's alone. He stood at the blue door until she opened it.

"I knew you would come back," she said.

She let him in. The room was dim. Candles burned on every surface.

"I need to know more," Blue said. "About passing the deal."

"Sit."

He sat. She poured tea into two cups. The tea was dark and bitter.

"The transfer requires a willing soul," she said. "Someone who takes the trombone and accepts the terms. They play one note. The ledger transfers."

"How many years would they get?"

"However many are left. The years you have not yet played."

Blue sipped the tea. It burned his throat.

"I can't ask anyone to do that."

"You do not have to ask. You only have to accept when they offer."

"Who would offer?"

Miss Cora smiled. It was not a kind smile. "Someone who wants to be great."

Blue put down the cup. He thought about Dizzy. He thought about all the trumpet players in the Quarter who would kill for the sound Blue had.

"No," he said.

"Then you will die."

"I know."

He stood up. He left a dollar on the table.

"Thank you, Miss Cora."

"Thank me when you change your mind."

He walked out into the night. The Treme was dark and quiet.

He thought about the ledger. He thought about the years he had left.

He thought about Dizzy, who had been counting notes.

He walked back to his room. He did not sleep.

Chapter 26: The Price of Transfer

Blue sat in his room. The morning light came through the thin curtains. He had not moved since he got home.

The transfer. Someone else takes the deal.

He could not ask. But what if someone offered? What if Dizzy offered?

He knew Dizzy wanted it. He had seen the hunger in Dizzy's eyes when Blue played. The same hunger Blue had felt before the crossroads.

He could not let Dizzy do that.

He stood up. He splashed water on his face. He looked in the mirror.

The gray in his hair was spreading. The lines around his eyes were deeper.

He was twenty-seven. He looked forty.

He thought about his father. Twenty-nine years old. Dead of pneumonia.

Blue was older than his father now. He had lived longer. He had played better. He had made a mark.

Maybe that was enough.

He dressed and went out. The day was hot. He walked to the Dreamland.

Sully was at the bar, counting receipts.

"Blue. You look like hell."

"I know."

"You got a show tonight."

"I know."

"Can you play?"

Blue looked at his hands. They were shaking slightly.

"Yeah," he said. "I can play."

Sully nodded. "Good. Because the whole city is coming to hear you."

Blue walked to the stage. He stood in the empty room and imagined the crowd.

He raised the trombone to his lips.

He did not play. He just stood there, the mouthpiece touching his lips, breathing through the horn.

The breath was its own kind of music.

Chapter 27: The Tour

Reese booked a tour. Houston, Dallas, Memphis, Chicago. Blue did not want to go. But the contract said he had to.

He packed a bag. He packed the trombone. He told Dizzy goodbye.

"Don't die out there," Dizzy said.

"I'll try."

The bus left at dawn. Blue sat by the window. The city slipped past.

Houston was loud. The club was packed. Blue played one set. His playing was flawless. The crowd went crazy.

He counted the notes. Three hundred twelve.

Dallas was worse. The room was bigger. The crowd was louder. He played two sets. Seven hundred notes.

He woke up in his hotel room and did not know where he was. The ceiling was unfamiliar. The bed was too soft.

He looked in the bathroom mirror. His reflection shocked him. His hair was mostly gray now. His face was gaunt.

He was twenty-seven.

He went back to bed. He did not play that day. He told the promoter he was sick.

The promoter said fine. But the next night, Blue played again.

Four hundred twenty notes.

He was burning through faster now. He could not stop. The music demanded it.

He called Dizzy from a payphone in Memphis.

"I'm running out," Blue said.

"Come home."

"I can't. I got Chicago after this."

"Forget Chicago. Come home."

Blue listened to the hum of the phone line.

"I can't," he said. And he hung up.

Chapter 28: The Mirror

The Chicago hotel room was narrow and cold. Blue sat on the edge of the bed. The radiator clanked.

He had played two sets at the Regal Theater. The crowd had been huge. The critics had come. They called him "the greatest living trombonist."

He had played eight hundred notes.

He looked in the mirror above the dresser. The man looking back was not him.

The hair was white now. Completely white. The face was thin, the cheeks hollow. The eyes were tired beyond tired.

He looked sixty.

He walked to the window and looked down at the street. Snow was falling. He had never seen snow before. It was beautiful and cold.

He thought about New Orleans. The heat. The river. The coffee at Celie's stand.

He thought about Dizzy.

He thought about his mother, who he had not called in months.

He sat down on the bed. He picked up the trombone.

He played a single note. Long and low. The note filled the room.

One more year.

He put the horn down. He lay back on the bed. He closed his eyes.

The hum in his chest was weak now. Like a battery running down.

He wondered how many notes he had left.

He fell asleep with his clothes on. The snow kept falling.

Chapter 29: The Letter

Blue sat at a small desk in the hotel room. The paper was cheap. The pen leaked.

He wrote:

"Dear Mama,

I know I haven't written. I'm sorry. I been busy. I been playing.

You probably heard the records. They're playing them on the radio now. People say I'm the best. They say I changed the music.

I don't know about that. I just play what I hear.

I think about Daddy a lot. I wonder if he knew. I wonder if he felt it coming.

I'm tired, Mama. Not the regular kind of tired. The deep kind.

I want you to know I did what I set out to do. I played the way I wanted to play. I heard the sound in my head and I got it out.

That's all I ever wanted.

I love you. I'm sorry I didn't come home.

Blue"

He folded the letter. He did not put it in an envelope. He did not have a stamp.

He put it in his coat pocket.

He had a show that night. He played it like it was his last.

Maybe it was.

He counted the notes. Six hundred.

He felt something crack inside him. Not a bone. Something deeper.

He finished the set. He bowed. The crowd roared.

He walked off stage and collapsed.

Chapter 30: Counting the Cost

Blue woke in a hospital room. The lights were too bright. The sheets were too white.

Dizzy was sitting in a chair by the window.

"You're in Chicago," Dizzy said. "You passed out on stage."

"How did you get here?"

"I flew."

Blue tried to sit up. His body would not cooperate.

"How long have I been out?"

"A day."

Blue looked at his hands. They were pale. The skin was thin.

"I did the math," Blue said. "Eleven thousand, eight hundred forty-three notes."

Dizzy did not answer.

"That's eleven thousand, eight hundred forty-three years. I'm not that old. But the numbers don't work like that."

"They don't work at all."

"I have maybe two years left. If I stop playing now."

"Then stop."

Blue looked at the ceiling. "I don't know how."

"You learn."

Blue closed his eyes. The hum in his chest was a whisper now.

"I want to go home," he said.

"I'll take you."

"New Orleans."

"I know."

Dizzy stood up. He put a hand on Blue's shoulder.

"We leave tomorrow."

Blue nodded. He did not open his eyes.

He dreamed of the crossroads. Mama Legba was there. She was holding the ledger.

It was full.

Chapter 31: Dizzy's Plan

They sat in Blue's room on Rampart Street. It had been a week since Chicago. Blue was thinner. His hands shook. But he was alive.

Dizzy had a plan.

"One more show," Dizzy said. "One final concert. The biggest New Orleans has ever seen. And then you stop."

"Stop playing?"

"Forever."

Blue looked at the trombone case in the corner. "I don't know if I can stop."

"You can. You have to."

Dizzy leaned forward. "Listen to me. You play one last set. The best set of your life. Put everything into it. And then you walk away. You never play again."

"And the notes I already played?"

"Already spent. But you stop spending."

Blue thought about it. No more playing. No more horn. No more sound.

"It would kill me," he said.

"Playing is killing you faster."

Blue looked at Dizzy. His friend's face was serious.

"How many notes would the last show be?"

"However many you want. Make them count."

Blue stood up. He walked to the window. The Quarter was quiet in the afternoon heat.

"One more show," Blue said.

"One more."

"Book it."

Dizzy nodded. He left without another word.

Blue stood at the window. The sun was low.

He picked up the trombone. He held it in his hands.

He did not play.

Chapter 32: The Final Show

Sully booked the Municipal Auditorium. Two thousand seats. He sold them all in a day.

The newspapers ran the story: "Blue Madere's Final Performance."

People came from everywhere. From the Quarter. From Baton Rouge. From Houston. From Chicago.

They came to hear the legend one last time.

Blue sat in his room on the day of the show. He had not played in three days. He was saving his notes.

Dizzy came by at four in the afternoon.

"It's time."

"I know."

Blue picked up the trombone case. He followed Dizzy out the door.

The streets were full. People recognized him. They called his name. He did not respond.

At the auditorium, the band was already setting up. The piano player was running scales. The drummer was tuning his kit.

Blue walked to the dressing room. He put the case on the table. He opened it.

The brass gleamed in the light.

He picked up the horn. It felt heavier than he remembered.

He sat down. He waited.

The minutes passed. The crowd filled the hall.

Dizzy appeared at the door. "They're ready for you."

Blue stood up. He walked to the stage.

The lights hit his face. The crowd roared.

He raised the trombone.

Chapter 33: Rehearsal

The rehearsal had been two days before. The band ran through the arrangements. The piano player, an old man named George, had tears in his eyes.

"I never heard anything like this," George said. "Not in forty years."

Blue nodded. He did not know what to say.

They ran through "Delta Morning." Blue played it soft. He saved his strength.

They ran through "St. James Infirmary." The notes hung in the empty hall like smoke.

Dizzy played trumpet on the third number. His playing was sharp and clear. It was the best Blue had ever heard him sound.

"You been practicing," Blue said.

"I been inspired."

After the run-through, George took Blue aside.

"Are you really quitting?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

Blue did not have an answer. He could not tell the truth.

"I got my reasons."

George nodded slowly. "I seen a lot of players come and go. Most of them were never as good as they thought they were. You're better than you think. That's rare."

He walked away.

Blue stood alone on the stage. The empty seats stretched out before him.

He played a single note. Just one.

It rang through the hall like a bell.

He stopped. He counted.

That was one.

Chapter 34: Mama Legba Returns

She came to him in the dressing room, an hour before the show. He was alone. The mirror fogged, then cleared, and she was there.

"Blue Madere."

He did not turn around. "I know."

"You are planning to stop."

"Yes."

"It will not undo what you have already done."

"I know."

She stepped out of the mirror. She was solid now. Real. Her white dress brushed the floor.

"I came to warn you. Quitting does not break the contract. The notes are spent. The years are gone."

"I know that too."

"Then why stop?"

Blue looked at her. Her face was calm. Her eyes had no pity.

"Because if I keep playing, I die. And I'm not ready to die."

"Then you should have thought of that before you signed."

"I was desperate."

"I know." She tilted her head. "Desperation is why I have work."

She walked toward the door. She paused with her hand on the knob.

"One more thing. The transfer offer still stands. If someone else takes the horn... but you already know that."

She opened the door. She was gone.

Blue sat alone. The mirror showed only his face now.

He looked at the trombone.

He thought about the transfer.

He would not ask Dizzy. He would die first.

Chapter 35: The Night Before

Blue walked the Quarter alone. The streets were quiet. The neon signs were dark. The clubs were closed.

He passed the Spotted Cat. The sign creaked in the breeze. He had played there a lifetime ago.

He passed the Dreamland. It was locked up tight. Tomorrow, he would play his last show there. No. At the auditorium. Bigger.

He walked to the cathedral on St. Louis. The doors were closed. He stood outside and looked up at the spire.

His mother had taken him here once. When he was a boy. She had lit a candle for his father.

He had not been inside a church since.

He stood on the steps. The moon was bright. The street was empty.

He did not go inside. He did not pray. He just stood there, letting the night wrap around him.

A car passed. The driver honked. Blue did not move.

He thought about the crossroads. He thought about the note he had played in the empty lot. The first note. The purest note.

He thought about all the notes that followed. Thousands of them. Each one a year.

He thought about the silence that would come after tomorrow.

He walked back to his room. He slept without dreaming.

Chapter 36: Dizzy's Confession

They sat backstage. The auditorium was filling. The sound of the crowd was a low rumble through the walls.

Dizzy was quiet. He had his trumpet in his lap, but he was not polishing it. He was just holding it.

"I got something to tell you," Dizzy said.

"Go ahead."

"I wanted the deal."

Blue looked at him.

"I thought about it," Dizzy said. "After you told me. I thought about going to the crossroads myself. Making my own deal."

"Why didn't you?"

Dizzy laughed. It was a hollow sound. "Because I'm a coward. I saw what it did to you. I didn't want that."

"Then you're smart."

"I'm not smart. I'm scared." Dizzy looked at him. "I been playing trumpet since I was ten. I'm good. But I'm not great. And I wanted to be great."

"You are great."

"Not like you."

Blue did not know what to say. He put his hand on Dizzy's shoulder.

"Don't make the deal," Blue said. "Ever. No matter how bad you want it."

"I know."

Blue sat back. The crowd was louder now.

"Thank you," Blue said.

"For what?"

"For being here. For counting the notes."

Dizzy nodded. He could not speak.

The stage manager knocked. "Five minutes."

They stood up. They walked to the wings together.

Chapter 37: The Crowd

The Municipal Auditorium was full. Two thousand people. They filled the seats and the aisles. They stood in the back.

The band was on stage. George at the piano. The bass player. The drummer. Dizzy with his trumpet.

Blue stood in the wings. He watched the crowd.

They were all ages. Old men in suits. Young women in dresses. White people from the Garden District. Black people from the Treme. They had all come to hear him.

He thought about what it meant. All those people, all those lives. And here he was, about to play for them. About to give them something they would never forget.

He thought about his father. He thought about his mother. He thought about the crossroads.

Sully appeared beside him. "You ready?"

"Yeah."

"Then get out there."

Blue stepped onto the stage.

The crowd erupted. They stood up. They cheered. They stamped their feet.

Blue raised the trombone. The crowd went silent.

He played a single note. A low B-flat. It filled the hall like nothing else.

The crowd stayed silent.

Blue counted.

One.

Chapter 38: First Set

The set opened with "Delta Morning." Blue played the melody straight, no frills. The notes were clean and pure. Each one a year.

The band followed him closely. They knew this was special. They played like men handling glass.

The second number was a slow blues. Blue stepped forward for his solo. He played sixteen bars. He held back. He was counting.

The third number was "St. James Infirmary." The crowd recognized the opening notes. A woman in the front row started crying.

Blue played the song like a prayer. Every note was a word. Every phrase was a sentence. He was telling them something. They were listening.

When he finished, the silence lasted five full seconds. Then the applause hit like a wave.

Blue stood still. He was breathing hard. His hands were shaking.

He looked at Dizzy. Dizzy nodded.

They moved to the fourth number. An original. Blue had written it the week before. He called it "The Devil's Trombone."

The opening notes were dark. The slide moved slow. The sound was deep and mournful.

The crowd leaned forward.

Blue played. He played like a man with nothing left to lose.

Chapter 39: The Solo

The band dropped out. It was just Blue and the trombone.

Thirty-two bars. The piano player had laid down the changes. Now it was Blue's turn.

He took a breath. He raised the horn.

The first note came out like dawn. Soft and gray. The second note was brighter. The third had a bite.

He played through the changes. Each note built on the last. He climbed and climbed. The melody twisted and turned. It was sad and joyful at the same time.

The crowd was silent. You could hear breathing.

Blue closed his eyes. He stopped counting. He let the music take him.

The notes came from somewhere deep. From the place where the deal lived. From the place where the years were stored.

He played thirty-two bars. Then he played thirty-two more.

The piano player came back in. The drums. The bass. The band lifted him up.

Blue played the final eight bars. Each note higher than the last. Each note more desperate.

He hit the final note. A high G. He held it.

He held it until his lungs burned. He held it until his vision blurred. He held it until he had nothing left.

Then he lowered the slide.

The note faded.

The silence was absolute.

Then the crowd rose to their feet.

Blue stood in the middle of the stage. He had stopped counting.

He did not know how many years he had left.

He did not care.

Chapter 40: The Last Note

The final song. Blue had chosen it weeks ago. "Just a Closer Walk With Thee." An old spiritual. His mother used to sing it.

He played it slow. He played it like a goodbye.

The first verse was soft. The second verse grew. The third verse soared.

The band played behind him. Dizzy's trumpet wove through the melody like a second voice.

Blue reached the final phrase. The last four notes.

He played them one at a time. Each one hung in the air like a held breath.

The last note. A low B-flat. The same note he had played in the empty lot.

He held it.

He held it for a long time.

He thought about the crossroads. He thought about Mama Legba. He thought about every note he had ever played.

He thought about Dizzy. He thought about the doctor. He thought about the letter in his coat pocket.

He held the note.

Then he let it go.

The sound faded. The room was silent.

Blue lowered the trombone.

The crowd exploded. They cheered. They cried. They called his name.

Blue turned and walked off stage.

His legs were shaking. His heart was pounding.

He made it to the dressing room. He closed the door. He sat down.

He did not cry. He just sat there, the trombone across his knees, the sound of the crowd muffled through the walls.

Chapter 41: Curtain

Blue sat in the dressing room. The applause continued. People were stamping their feet.

The door opened. Dizzy came in.

"You did it."

"Yeah."

"You okay?"

Blue looked at his hands. They were still shaking.

"I don't know."

Dizzy sat down. "You played three hundred and fourteen notes tonight."

Blue nodded.

"Three hundred and fourteen years."

"I know."

The applause was fading. The crowd was starting to leave. You could hear the shuffle of feet, the murmur of voices.

"You want to go out there?" Dizzy asked.

"No."

They sat in silence. The room smelled of sweat and brass polish.

Blue laid the trombone in its case. He closed the lid.

"Thank you," Blue said.

"For what?"

"For everything."

Dizzy nodded. He stood up. "I'll get the car."

He left.

Blue sat alone. He touched the trombone case. The leather was warm.

He had not played his last note. He had played his last note for tonight. But he had not played his last note forever.

He stood up. He walked out.

The hallway was empty. The stage was dark.

He walked through the back door into the alley. Dizzy was waiting in the car.

Blue got in. They drove away.

The city passed by. The lights blurred.

Blue did not look back.

Chapter 42: The Aftermath

Blue collapsed in the dressing room. His knees buckled. Dizzy caught him before he hit the floor.

"Blue!"

"I'm fine."

"You're not fine."

Dizzy eased him into a chair. Blue's face was gray. His breathing was shallow.

Dizzy called for a doctor. The same doctor who had listened to Blue's heart a month ago. He came quickly.

The doctor listened. He took Blue's blood pressure. He shone a light in his eyes.

"He needs to rest," the doctor said. "Real rest. Days. Maybe weeks."

"I got shows—"

"No more shows. Not for a while."

Blue tried to argue. The doctor cut him off.

"You want to die on a stage? Because that's where you're heading."

Dizzy drove Blue home. The streets were empty. The sun was coming up.

Dizzy helped Blue up the stairs. Blue's room was cold and dark.

"Sleep," Dizzy said.

Blue lay down. He closed his eyes.

The hum in his chest was barely audible now. A whisper. A memory.

He slept.

He dreamed of the crossroads. But in the dream, he did not stop. He kept walking past it.

He woke up hours later. The room was dark. He was alone.

He reached for the trombone case. It was there.

He did not open it.

Chapter 43: Mama Legba's Last Visit

She came to him in the hospital. Not the big hospital. The little clinic on Rampart where Dr. Boudreaux had his office.

Blue was sitting up in bed. The room was white and bare. A single window looked out on a brick wall.

Mama Legba was sitting in the chair by the door.

"The ledger is closed," she said.

Blue looked at her. "What does that mean?"

"Eleven thousand, eight hundred forty-three notes. Eleven thousand, eight hundred forty-three years. You cannot play them again. They are spent."

"I know."

"You are still alive."

"I know that too."

She stood up. Her white dress moved like water.

"The contract is complete. You owe nothing more."

"Then I'm free?"

She smiled. It was the first time he had seen her smile. It was not warm.

"You were always free, Blue Madere. You just forgot."

She walked to the window. She looked out at the brick wall.

"You played well," she said. "I have seen many contracts. Yours was one of the best."

She turned. She looked at him.

"Live the rest of your years. They are yours now."

She walked to the door.

"Mama Legba."

She stopped.

"Thank you."

She did not turn around. She opened the door and walked through it.

The door closed. The room was empty.

Blue sat in the bed. He listened to his own breathing.

The hum in his chest was quiet.

For the first time in months, he felt peace.

Chapter 44: The Long Sleep

Blue slept for three days. The doctor gave him something for the pain in his chest. The nurse checked on him every few hours.

Dizzy came every day. He sat in the chair by the door. He read the newspaper. He waited.

The city waited too. The newspapers ran headlines. "Madere Collapses After Historic Show." "Trombonist in Recovery."

People left flowers at the clinic door. Someone left a drawing of a trombone.

On the third day, Blue opened his eyes.

Dizzy was there. He was reading the paper.

"You look terrible," Blue said.

Dizzy looked up. The paper dropped.

"You're awake."

"Apparently."

Dizzy stood up. He walked to the bed. He looked at Blue for a long time.

"Don't do that again," Dizzy said.

"I'll try."

The doctor came in. He checked Blue's heart. He listened to his lungs.

"You're lucky," the doctor said. "Whatever was wearing you out seems to have stopped."

"It stopped."

"Can you tell me what it was?"

"No."

The doctor did not push. He wrote something on a chart.

"Another day of rest. Then you can go home. But no playing for at least a month."

Blue nodded.

The doctor left.

Dizzy sat back down. He picked up the paper.

"What does it say?" Blue asked.

"It says you're the greatest trombonist who ever lived."

Blue closed his eyes.

"That's nice," he said.

Chapter 45: Waking

Blue walked out of the clinic on a Thursday morning. The sun was bright. The air was thick.

He was thinner than when he went in. His clothes hung loose. His hair was completely white.

But he was alive.

Dizzy was waiting in the car. Blue got in.

"Where to?" Dizzy asked.

"Home."

Dizzy drove. The Quarter looked the same. The same streets. The same buildings. The same people.

They stopped in front of Blue's building. Dizzy helped him up the stairs.

The room was exactly as Blue had left it. The bed unmade. The trombone case in the corner.

Blue sat on the bed. He looked at the case.

"I'm going to sleep," he said.

"I'll be back tonight."

Dizzy left.

Blue lay down. The mattress was hard. The pillow was flat.

It felt like home.

He slept. No dreams.

When he woke, the light through the window was golden. Evening.

He sat up. He felt different. Lighter.

He walked to the trombone case. He opened it.

The brass gleamed. The slide was smooth.

He picked up the horn. He held it in his hands.

He did not raise it to his lips.

He put it back in the case. He closed the lid.

He went to the window and watched the sun set over the Quarter.

Chapter 46: The Trombone

Dizzy brought the trombone to the hospital the next day. Blue was sitting up, eating soup.

"You want your horn?"

Blue looked at the case. "Put it on the bed."

Dizzy set it down. Blue finished his soup. He wiped his mouth.

He reached for the case. He opened it.

The trombone lay inside. The brass was warm in the afternoon light.

Blue picked it up. It was lighter than he remembered. Or maybe he was heavier.

He held it in his hands. He felt the weight of it. The familiar curves.

He did not raise it to his lips.

Dizzy watched. He did not say anything.

Blue ran his fingers along the slide. The metal was smooth.

He thought about playing. He thought about the sound. He thought about the years.

"I don't know if I can play again," Blue said.

"You don't have to."

"I don't know if I can not play."

"That's a different problem."

Blue nodded. He put the trombone back in the case.

He closed the lid.

"Keep it for me," Blue said.

"What?"

"For a while. I don't want to look at it."

Dizzy picked up the case. "You sure?"

"Yeah."

Dizzy left. The door closed.

Blue lay back. The ceiling was white. The room was quiet.

No hum in his chest. No music in his head.

Just silence.

It was the loudest silence he had ever heard.

Chapter 47: Walking Out

Blue left the clinic on foot. The doctor had said he could go. No more bed rest. Just... live.

He walked down Rampart Street. The sun was warm. The street was alive.

He passed Celie's coffee stand. She saw him and smiled.

"Blue! You're out!"

"I'm out."

She poured him a cup without asking. He took it. The coffee was bitter and hot.

"Thanks, Celie."

"You look better."

"I feel better."

He walked on. He passed the Spotted Cat. The sign was being repainted. He passed the Dreamland. It was closed.

He walked to the river. He sat on the bench where he had sat with Dizzy.

The water was brown and slow. A barge moved past.

He thought about the notes. All of them. Eleven thousand, eight hundred forty-three.

He thought about Mama Legba. He thought about the ledger. He thought about the fact that he was still here.

The hum in his chest was gone. Completely gone.

He sat for a long time. The sun moved across the sky.

He finished his coffee. He stood up.

He walked back to his room.

The trombone case was not in the corner anymore. Dizzy had it.

The room felt empty. But it also felt free.

He sat on the bed. He listened to the sounds of the street. A car horn. A dog barking. A child laughing.

He was learning to hear differently.

Chapter 48: The New Deal

Dizzy came by with news. A music school in the Treme was looking for a teacher. They wanted someone who knew the music. Someone who could teach the kids.

"I told them about you," Dizzy said.

"Teaching?"

"Yeah. Teaching."

Blue shook his head. "I don't know how to teach."

"Sure you do. You just tell them what you know."

"Half of what I know I can't explain."

Dizzy sat down. "Look. You can't play anymore. Not the way you did. But you can still pass it on. The stuff you learned. The stuff you figured out."

Blue thought about it. He thought about the kids in the Treme. The ones who hung around the clubs, listening through the windows.

"What would I tell them?"

"Tell them not to make a deal with Mama Legba."

Blue laughed. It was the first time he had laughed in months.

"That's good advice."

"The best."

Dizzy stood up. "Think about it. The job is yours if you want it."

Dizzy left. Blue sat in the quiet room.

He thought about the kids. He thought about the music.

He thought about passing it on without the cost.

Maybe that was the way.

Chapter 49: The Silence

Blue spent a week doing nothing. He woke when the sun came up. He walked to Celie's for coffee. He sat by the river. He walked home. He slept.

No music. No practice. No notes.

The silence was strange at first. He kept expecting the hum. He kept reaching for the trombone.

But the trombone was with Dizzy. And the hum was gone.

On the fifth day, he walked past a club. A piano player was practicing inside. The notes drifted through the open door.

Blue stopped. He listened.

The playing was good. Not great. But good. The piano player hit a wrong note and started again.

Blue smiled. He walked on.

On the sixth day, he went to the Treme. He found the music school. It was a small building with a sign that said "Second Line Music."

He stood outside. He could hear children playing scales inside. They were terrible. They were wonderful.

He did not go in.

On the seventh day, he woke up and the silence felt like company instead of absence.

He was learning.

He got dressed. He walked to Celie's. He drank his coffee.

The city hummed around him. The traffic. The voices. The river.

He was part of it now. Not above it. Not separate.

Just part of it.

Chapter 50: The Hum Returns

Blue sat on his porch. The morning was cool. The street was quiet.

Dizzy brought the trombone case. He set it on the steps.

"You asked me to keep it. I kept it long enough."

Blue looked at the case. "I don't know if I'm ready."

"You don't have to be ready. You just have to decide."

Dizzy walked away.

Blue sat with the case at his feet. He did not touch it for a long time.

Then he bent down. He opened the latches.

The brass gleamed. The slide was smooth.

He picked up the trombone. It felt familiar. It felt like an old friend.

He raised it to his lips. He did not blow.

He just held the mouthpiece against his lips. He breathed through the horn.

The breath made a sound. Not a note. Just air moving through brass.

He lowered the horn.

The hum was back. But it was different. It was not the deal.

It was the music itself. The music that had always been there, before Mama Legba, before the crossroads. The music that was his.

He stood up. He put the trombone back in the case.

He walked inside. He got dressed.

He walked to the Treme. To the Second Line Music School.

The children were playing scales. They were terrible. They were wonderful.

Blue walked through the door.

"All right," he said. "Let me show you how it's done."

He picked up a student trombone from the rack. He raised it to his lips.

He played a single note. A B-flat.

It was just a note. Clean. Simple. Human.

It cost him nothing.
