Chapter 1: The Herbarium

The herbarium smelled of old paper and older leaves. Alekos Kappas worked at a table near the tall windows, where the April light fell in pale sheets across pressed specimens mounted on cream-colored card. In his hand, a fig leaf collected in 1921 by a German botanist whose handwriting he had come to know better than his own. The leaf was brown now, fragile as old lace, but its veins still traced the same map they had traced when it was green and breathing on a hillside above Ephesus.

He turned the specimen over. On the back, in fading ink: *Ficus carica var. smyrniaca. Collected near Kuşadası, April 1921. Tree age estimated 250 years.*

His father had known that tree. His father had known every fig tree in the Meander Valley before the exchange, before the burning, before the world broke along lines that no tree recognized.

A knock at the door. The department secretary, a young woman named Maria, held out an envelope with a Swiss stamp.

"From Geneva, Professor."

He opened it with a paper knife. The letter inside was brief and formal: the Montreux Botanical Foundation was pleased to inform Dr. Alekos Kappas that his application for a research travel grant had been approved. Funding for three months. Purpose: to document the heritage fig varieties of the eastern Mediterranean before they were lost to agricultural modernization and political instability.

Three months. He had applied almost a year ago, in a fit of conviction he had not felt since his youth. Now the money was here, the permission was here, and he would have to go.

He looked at the fig leaf on the table. It had waited twenty-seven years in the dark of the herbarium cabinet. He would not make it wait longer.

Chapter 2: The House in Kypseli

Eleni was in the kitchen when he came home, rolling out filo dough on the marble counter. She did not look up, but the rhythm of the rolling pin paused for half a beat — her way of acknowledging his presence without interrupting her work.

"There's a letter from Geneva," he said.

"I saw it on the hall table." She laid a sheet of filo across the baking dish and began brushing it with melted butter. "They approved the grant."

"Yes."

"Three months."

"Yes."

She worked the butter into the edges of the filo, her movements precise and unhurried. She had been rolling dough since she was a girl in Piraeus, helping her mother in the taverna kitchen. She had told Alekos once that she found cooking easier than talking — in the kitchen, things either worked or they didn't, and there was always a reason.

"You've been waiting to go back for fifteen years," she said. "Since before Nikos was born."

"Since before we married."

"I know." She set down the brush and turned to face him. Her hair was grayer than he remembered, or perhaps he was only noticing now. "I'm not going to stop you. I never have. But I need to know — is this about the figs, or is it about Smyrna?"

He considered the question. "Both," he said. "I don't think I can separate them."

"I don't think you've ever tried." She turned back to the filo. "Dinner will be ready at eight. Nikos is coming."

Chapter 3: The Son

Nikos arrived with a newspaper under his arm and an argument already forming on his lips. He was twenty-four and certain, the way only the young and the politically engaged can be certain. The newspaper was his own — he wrote for *Proia*, a left-wing daily that the Metaxas regime tolerated with narrowed eyes.

"So you're going," he said, not quite a question. They sat in the courtyard under the fig tree, drinking coffee Eleni had brought out on a brass tray.

"The grant is approved. I leave in two weeks."

"For Turkey." Nikos said the word as if it were a personal accusation. "The country that expelled us. That burned Smyrna. And my father is going there to look at trees."

"The trees didn't burn anyone."

"Neither did the people. The politicians did. But the people are still there, and we are still here, and Metaxas is still in power, and the Italians are arming in Albania, and the Germans are marching into Austria. And you're going to look at trees."

Alekos set down his coffee cup. The anger in his son's voice was familiar — he had felt it himself, once. The anger of having lost something you never possessed. The anger of being defined by an absence.

"When I was your age," Alekos said, "I was angry at my father for spending his life on plants. Politics, I told him. The world is burning. And he said—"

"'The world is always burning. The plants are always growing.'" Nikos finished the sentence. "You've told me this before."

"Because it's still true." Alekos looked up at the fig tree's branches, the first small fruits beginning to form among the leaves. "I'm not running away from politics. I'm doing the one thing I know how to do. When this war you fear comes — and it may — someone should have a record of what was here before."

Nikos was quiet for a moment. Then he picked up his coffee. "Write it well, then. So the rest of us know what we lost."

Chapter 4: The Fig in the Courtyard

The morning before he left, Alekos went into the courtyard with his notebook and a measuring tape. The fig tree — his mother's tree, the Smyrna purple — stood against the south wall, its trunk thick as a man's thigh, its leaves spreading wide in the spring warmth.

He had been meaning to document this tree for years. It was, in its way, the first specimen of his collection — the cutting his mother had wrapped in wet cloth and carried in her apron pocket when they boarded the refugee ship at Smyrna in September 1923. She had pressed it into his hands as they waited on the quay: "Take this. Your father's tree. Do not let it die."

He was seventeen. The cutting was a stick, twelve centimeters long, with two wilted leaves. He had planted it in a tin can filled with soil from the garden they were leaving behind — a small theft, but it mattered. The stick had survived the voyage, survived the refugee camp outside Athens, survived being transplanted three times before they found this house in Kypseli. Now it was a tree — four meters tall, bearing sixty kilos of fruit in a good year.

He measured the leaves: average length 18.2 centimeters, five-lobed, the characteristic deep green with paler undersides. He noted the date of first fruiting, the color of the fig when ripe — purple so dark it was nearly black, with a pale green interior and a pink center. The local name, his mother's name for it: *Smyrniá mávri*, the Smyrna black.

He pressed a leaf between two sheets of blotting paper and placed it in his specimen folder. The first entry. The tree that had crossed the sea.

Chapter 5: The Map

The map had belonged to his father. Alekos unfolded it on his study desk, weighting the corners with books. It was a German survey map from 1905, printed in Leipzig, showing the western Anatolian coast from Çanakkale to Marmaris at a scale of 1:250,000. His father had annotated it in pencil — red circles for documented fig trees, blue for varieties he intended to return to, green for specimens already collected.

Some of the marks were forty years old. Some of the trees they marked were certainly gone — cut down, burned, or simply no longer tended. But figs were resilient. A fig tree could survive neglect, drought, the cutting of its branches. It could grow from a stump. It could grow from a crack in a stone wall.

Alekos took out his own pencil and began to mark. Kuşadası, where his father had documented the 250-year-old İzmir black — if the tree survived, it would be nearly 300 now. Aydın, where a variety called the Sultan's Honey grew in the garden of an old Greek house. The monasteries of Lesbos, where monks had kept orchards since Byzantine times. The Bekaa Valley in Lebanon, where his former student Samia had written of varieties mentioned in medieval Arabic texts.

He worked until the light failed, marking the map with new circles. The old red and the new blue. Father and son, reaching toward each other across thirty years of silence.

Chapter 6: The Station

Eleni did not come to the station. She had said goodbye the night before, in the darkness of their bedroom, her hand resting on his chest.

"You'll come back," she said. It was not a question.

"I will."

"I know. You always do." She had turned away then, and he had lain awake listening to her breathing until the first light grayed the window.

Now he stood on the platform at Larissa Station, his bag heavy with notebooks and a camera and a folding knife for taking cuttings. The train to Thessaloniki would leave in twenty minutes, and from there he would continue to Istanbul, and from Istanbul by ferry to İzmir — the city that had been Smyrna.

Around him, families embraced, soldiers in uniform embraced their mothers, a priest in black robes read a newspaper on a bench. The ordinary chaos of departure.

He found his compartment and stowed his bag. Through the window, the familiar silhouette of Athens — the Acropolis on its rock, the white buildings climbing the slopes. The city that had taken him in when he had nothing. The city that was his home, but not his homeland.

The whistle blew. The train shuddered and began to move.

He was going back.

Chapter 7: The Border

At the Edirne border station, the Turkish customs officer examined Alekos's Greek passport with the careful suspicion of a man trained to find problems. The passport photograph showed a thinner, younger Alekos — taken in 1932, before the gray had fully claimed his hair.

"Purpose of visit?"

"Botanical research. Fig trees."

The officer looked up. "Figs?"

"I'm documenting heritage varieties for a Swiss foundation. The grant letter is in my bag."

The officer waved away the offer. He stamped the passport and handed it back. "My grandfather had a fig tree," he said, in accented French, the common ground of officials who did not share a language. "In the courtyard. Every summer, the children would climb it."

"Is it still there?"

"They cut it down. For an apartment building." The officer shrugged, the universal gesture of small losses accepted. "Welcome to Turkey."

Alekos walked across the border. The olive trees on the Turkish side were identical to the ones on the Greek side — same silver leaves, same twisted trunks, same roots reaching down into the same limestone. The trees did not know there was a border. They had been here before the Ottomans, before the Greeks, before anyone thought to draw a line on a map and call one side home and the other exile.

Chapter 8: Istanbul in Passing

Istanbul was a layover — twelve hours before the ferry to İzmir. Alekos left his bag at the port and walked into the city.

He had been here twice before: once as a boy with his father, visiting the great mosque gardens for botanical specimens, and once as a refugee in 1923, passing through on his way to Greece. The city had changed between those visits and changed again since. The new republic had made it Turkish in a way it had not been under the Ottomans. The Greek signs were fewer. The Greek voices were quieter.

He walked to Fener, the old Greek quarter on the Golden Horn. The Patriarchate still stood, its red brick walls unchanged, but the streets around it were emptier than he remembered. The population exchange of 1923 had taken most of the Greeks. Those who remained were old, or stubborn, or both.

He found a small church — St. George of the Gardens — and went inside. The interior was dark, smelling of incense and beeswax. An old woman in black was lighting a candle before an icon of the Virgin. Alekos lit a candle too, though he was not sure he believed in prayer anymore.

He thought of his mother lighting candles in this same church, perhaps, before the ship that took them away. She had never spoken of it. She had never spoken of much, after Smyrna.

He left the candle burning and walked back to the port.

Chapter 9: The Aegean Crossing

The ferry from Istanbul to İzmir was a French steamer, the *Ville d'Alger*, carrying cargo and passengers through the Sea of Marmara and down the Aegean coast. Alekos stood at the rail and watched the islands appear and disappear through the morning haze. Marmara Island. Lesbos, a gray-green shape on the horizon to starboard. Chios, closer, its white villages visible as dots on the hills.

An elderly man came to stand beside him. He was Turkish, with a weathered face and a wool cap pulled low. He offered Alekos a cigarette; Alekos declined.

"You are Greek?" the man asked in Turkish.

"I was born here. In Smyrna."

The man nodded slowly. "I was born in Crete. In Hania. Before the exchange, we spoke Greek. My mother still speaks it in her sleep." He looked at the water. "The islands — they are the same. The sea is the same. Only the flags change."

They shared bread and olives as the ferry steamed south. The man's name was İsmail. He was returning to İzmir after visiting his son in Istanbul. He asked Alekos about his research.

"Figs," İsmail said. "My father had figs in Crete. When we left, he tried to bring a cutting. The soldiers on the ship threw it overboard. He cried — my father, who never cried. He said, 'Now we have truly lost everything.'"

Alekos knew that grief. It was the grief of a specific thing, not the general loss of homeland but the particular loss of a tree, a fruit, a taste that would never be tasted again.

"Did he plant another?" Alekos asked.

"Yes. In İzmir. A different variety, but it grew." İsmail smiled. "Figs grow anywhere. That's their gift."

Chapter 10: Smyrna

İzmir rose from the sea in a haze of morning light, its minarets and clock towers and modern apartment buildings crowding the shoreline. Alekos stood at the bow as the ferry approached, and for a moment — just a moment — he was seventeen again, standing on a different ship, watching the city burn.

The fire of 1922 had destroyed the Greek and Armenian quarters. They said the flames were visible from Lesbos, seventy miles away. They said the screams carried across the water. Alekos did not remember the fire — his family had fled before the worst of it — but he remembered the glow on the horizon, the red reflection on the sea, his mother's hand gripping his shoulder hard enough to bruise.

The ferry docked. He walked down the gangplank and into the city that was no longer his.

He found the old Greek quarter, or what remained of it. His family's street was gone — replaced by municipal buildings. The house where he was born was now a parking lot for government automobiles. He stood at the edge of it and felt nothing at all, which was worse than grief.

Then he turned a corner and saw it: a fig tree, growing in the courtyard of what had once been the church of St. Photini. The church was a carpet warehouse now, but the tree remained. Its branches were heavy with developing fruit. It was a Smyrna purple, like his mother's tree in Athens.

He took out his notebook and began to write.

Chapter 11: The Old Quarter

The old woman who lived beside the carpet warehouse was named Fatma. She had been born in the house in 1890, and she had never left. When Alekos knocked on her door and explained in halting Turkish what he was doing, she stared at him for a long moment.

"You are Greek," she said.

"I was born here. In this neighborhood."

"I know. I remember your family. The botanist." She pointed at the fig tree. "Your father planted that tree. In 1910, the year my son was born. He said it was a gift — for the baby, and for the church."

Alekos looked at the tree with new eyes. His father had planted it. His father's hands had set the cutting in the earth, had watered it through its first summer, had watched it bear its first fruit. He had never known this. His father had never told him.

"Would you like some tea?" the old woman asked.

He accepted. They sat in her courtyard, in the shade of the fig tree his father had planted, and Fatma talked. She remembered his mother — a kind woman, she said, who always brought figs to the neighbors at harvest time. She remembered his father riding a bicycle with a plant press strapped to the back. She remembered the day they left, in September 1923, walking to the port with what they could carry.

"The cutting," Alekos said. "The fig cutting my mother took with her — do you know where it came from?"

Fatma looked at him. "From this tree," she said. "Your mother took a cutting from this tree. She said, 'I need something that will remember.'"

Chapter 12: The First Specimen

He spent three hours documenting the tree his father had planted. He measured the trunk circumference — 87 centimeters at chest height. He photographed the leaves from multiple angles, noting the five-lobed structure, the slightly serrated edges, the deep green of the upper surface and the paler underside with its fine hairs. He collected three leaves for pressing, two immature fruits for the spirit collection, and a small cutting wrapped in damp sphagnum moss.

Fatma brought him more tea and watched him work.

"You are like your father," she said. "He did the same thing. Measuring, writing, looking at the leaves as if they held secrets."

"Did he find what he was looking for?"

"I don't know. But he kept looking. Every year, he came back to this tree. He said it was his favorite — not the oldest, not the rarest, but the one he had planted with his own hands." She paused. "When he came in 1921, he said the tree would outlive all of us. He said that was the point."

Alekos closed his notebook. "Thank you," he said. "For remembering."

"Who else will remember?" The old woman shrugged. "The young people don't care about old trees and old neighbors. They want to go to Istanbul, to Ankara, to be modern. Someone has to remember."

She gave him dried figs from last year's harvest, wrapped in a cloth. He carried them in his bag, and for the rest of the journey they would remind him of her, and of his father, and of a tree that had survived everything.

Chapter 13: The Road to Kuşadası

The bus from İzmir to Kuşadası was a converted lorry with wooden benches and a canvas top that flapped in the wind. Alekos sat among farmers and traders, chickens in baskets and sacks of grain, a young woman reading a French novel, an old man smoking a pipe that smelled of cherry wood.

The road followed the coast for a while, then turned inland through hills covered with olive groves and the occasional cypress. Alekos watched the landscape and recognized it — not from his own memory but from his father's descriptions. The valley of the Meander River. The hills of ancient Lydia. The soil here was deep and alluvial, perfect for figs.

His father had brought him here in 1918, when Alekos was twelve. They had taken a donkey cart from the station and visited seven villages in ten days, documenting figs. His father had been tireless — up at dawn, walking the orchards, interviewing farmers in a mix of Greek and Turkish and the universal language of botanists, which was a kind of pointing and nodding and careful observation.

Alekos remembered the figs. They had eaten fresh figs at every stop — black and green and purple, some so ripe they burst at the touch. His father had named each variety for him: Sultan's Honey, Bride's Blush, the Sailor's Return. The names were poetry. The fruit was something beyond poetry — sweetness and texture and the particular taste of a place concentrated into a single bite.

He had not thought about those days in years. He had trained himself not to. But the bus was moving through the same landscape, and the memories rose unbidden, like water finding old channels.

Chapter 14: Mustafa's Tree

The village was called Kirazlı — "place of cherries" — though its real wealth was figs. Alekos walked from the bus stop along a dirt road, past fields of cotton and tobacco, until he reached a house with blue shutters and a courtyard dominated by a tree so large it seemed to hold up the sky.

The trunk was wide as a doorway. Its branches, supported by stone pillars, spread thirty meters in every direction. The leaves were a canopy of green so dense the ground beneath was in permanent shade. And the figs — still small, still green, but promising the heavy purple fruit that would come in August.

He had seen a photograph of this tree. His father had taken it in 1898, standing beside the trunk with a Turkish farmer whose name was written on the back: Yusuf Yılmaz. The same photograph, presumably, that Mustafa now possessed.

A man emerged from the house. He was sixty, lean and weathered, with the same broad cheekbones as the farmer in the photograph. He looked at Alekos without surprise.

"You are the Greek," he said in Turkish. "The son of the botanist."

"Yes."

"My father said you would come one day. He said, 'The son will return. The trees always bring them back.'"

Mustafa Yılmaz smiled, and Alekos felt something shift — some wall he had built inside himself, brick by careful brick, begin to crumble.

Chapter 15: The Photograph

They sat in the shade of the great tree, drinking tea that Mustafa's wife brought on a copper tray. The tea was sweet, flavored with sage from the garden. Mustafa smoked a cigarette and talked about the tree.

"It was already old when my great-grandfather was born. They say a dervish planted it — a holy man who traveled through here in the time of Sultan Ahmed III. He planted a fig at every village that gave him shelter. This one survived."

Mustafa went into the house and returned with a tin box. Inside were family papers — Ottoman title deeds, letters in Arabic script, and a photograph wrapped in yellowed paper.

"This is what you came for," he said.

The photograph showed two men standing beside the great fig tree. One was Yusuf Yılmaz, Mustafa's father — a younger man than Alekos had expected, perhaps thirty, with a thick mustache and a serious expression. The other man was Alekos's father.

He was forty-three in the photograph, the age Alekos was now when Nikos was born. He wore a European suit with a waistcoat, slightly rumpled from travel. One hand rested on the trunk of the tree. His expression was not serious — he was smiling, the slight, private smile Alekos remembered from childhood, the smile of a man who found the world more interesting than threatening.

"Your father came here six times," Mustafa said. "My father told me. He came to measure the tree, to photograph the leaves, to talk about figs. But also — my father said — he came because he liked it here. Because the tea was good and the shade was cool and no one asked him to be anything other than what he was."

Alekos held the photograph and felt his eyes sting. He had not cried since his father's funeral, twenty years ago. He had not allowed himself. But here, under the tree his father had known, holding the photograph of a man he had loved and never fully understood, the tears came without permission.

Mustafa looked away, toward the tree. "Fig trees weep a white sap," he said. "It's not grief. It's just what they do."

Chapter 16: Lale's Translation

Mustafa's daughter arrived in the late afternoon, riding a bicycle with a basket of school papers on the back. She was twenty-eight, with her father's cheekbones and her mother's dark eyes, and she spoke Greek with an accent that reminded Alekos of the old women in his Smyrna neighborhood.

"I learned from books," she said, apologetically. "And from a Greek woman who stayed — an old woman who refused to leave in the exchange. She died five years ago. I was the only one who would speak Greek with her."

They sat in the courtyard and Lale translated her father's stories. The great tree had survived the great fire of 1894, which burned half the village but stopped at the edge of the courtyard — "the dervish's blessing," the villagers said. It had survived the war, survived the occupation, survived the exchange. Soldiers had camped under it. Refugees had slept under it. Lovers had met under it. A generation of village children had learned to climb on its lowest branches.

"My father says the tree belongs to both families now," Lale said. "Your father documented it. Our family protected it. That makes it shared."

Alekos considered this. "In botany, there is a concept called the type specimen — the single individual that defines a species. It's the reference point for all future identifications. This tree"—he looked up at the canopy—"this tree is the type specimen of my childhood. Everything I know about figs began here."

Lale translated this for her father. Mustafa nodded slowly, then said something in Turkish.

"He says: 'The tree does not care about species. It only knows how to grow.'"

Chapter 17: The Village Figs

Over three days, Alekos documented the village's heritage figs. Lale had arranged for him to meet the families who kept the old trees, and they received him with the particular hospitality of rural people — curious, slightly suspicious, eventually warm.

The Wedding Fig grew in the courtyard of a woman named Ayşe, whose grandmother had planted it on her wedding day in 1877. The tree was sixty years old now, still bearing the pale green figs that the grandmother had brought as a cutting from her own mother's garden. "Every bride in our family takes a cutting from this tree," Ayşe said. "It's our dowry."

The Sailor's Fig grew near the waterfront, a solitary tree with fruit so dark it was almost black. An old fisherman named Mehmet told the story: his great-uncle had brought the cutting from Alexandria in 1850, wrapped in a wet cloth, carrying it across three seas. The man had planned to sail again — he was a sailor, always restless — but he died of fever before he could leave. The tree was his only voyage home.

The Thief's Fig grew at the edge of the village cemetery, its branches hanging over the wall. The fruit was small and intensely sweet, and for generations, children had stolen it on their way home from school. "The dead don't mind," the village imam told Alekos. "Figs are sadaqah — charity. The tree gives what it gives."

Alekos wrote everything down: the names, the stories, the leaf measurements, the fruit descriptions. But the stories mattered more than the measurements. A fig variety was not just a set of botanical characteristics. It was a family. A history. A way of remembering.

Chapter 18: The Shared Meal

The last night in Kirazlı, Mustafa and his wife prepared a dinner in Alekos's honor. Lamb roasted with dried figs and almonds. Pilaf with pine nuts. Fresh cheese and olives and warm bread. And figs — fresh figs, dried figs, figs preserved in syrup, figs baked into small pastries with honey and cinnamon.

They ate in the courtyard under the great tree, with oil lamps hung from the lowest branches. Lale sat beside Alekos and translated the conversation. Her younger brother, a farmer named Orhan, asked about Greece — the crops, the climate, the politics. Mustafa's wife, whose name was Zeynep, said nothing but kept refilling Alekos's plate, which was its own kind of welcome.

After the meal, Mustafa brought out a bottle of rakı. They drank slowly, the anise-scented spirit clouding with water, and the conversation shifted from agriculture to memory.

"My father used to say," Mustafa said through Lale, "that your father was the only man he ever met who truly understood a tree. Not the wood, not the fruit, but the tree itself — the living thing, the roots, the seasons. He said most men look at a tree and see what they can take from it. Your father looked at a tree and saw what it had to give."

Alekos raised his glass. "To the men who saw the trees."

They drank. The oil lamps flickered. Above them, the great fig spread its branches against the stars.

Chapter 19: The Departure from Kuşadası

Morning came with the call to prayer from the village mosque, the sound carrying across the fields and through the open window of the room where Alekos had slept. He packed his bag carefully: the notebooks, the pressed leaves, the spirit specimens, the camera. And the cutting Mustafa had given him from the great tree, wrapped in damp cloth and tied with twine.

Mustafa was waiting in the courtyard with tea. They sat in silence for a while, the way men of a certain age and disposition could sit, not needing to fill the space with words.

"Plant it somewhere it will be remembered," Mustafa said finally. Lale was not there to translate, but Alekos understood.

"I will," he said. "At the university botanical garden. With a label that tells the story."

"Stories don't grow in labels." Mustafa tapped his chest. "They grow here. But the tree — the tree helps people remember."

Lale arrived with her bicycle. She gave Alekos a notebook, school-sized, with a blue cover.

"I wrote the Turkish names," she said. "For all the varieties you documented. The Greek names too, where I knew them. And my father's stories, translated. It's not scientific. But I thought — you should have both. The measurements and the memories."

Alekos took the notebook. He opened it and saw her careful handwriting, Greek and Turkish side by side, the scientific names in Latin letters bridging the two.

"This is the most valuable thing I've been given on this journey," he said. "Thank you."

Lale looked away, embarrassed. "Come back," she said. "The trees will still be here."

Chapter 20: Aydın and Beyond

The bus to Aydın was dusty and slow, stopping at every village and every crossroads where a passenger might be waiting. Alekos sat with Lale's notebook open on his lap, reading the stories she had written.

In Aydın, he found what he had feared. The old Greek orchards were being replaced by commercial plantations — long rows of a single variety, efficient and uniform and utterly without history. The Sultan's Honey fig, which his father had documented in the garden of a Greek doctor in 1904, was gone. The garden was a cotton field now. The doctor's family had left in 1923, and no one had tended the tree.

He walked through the town, asking in cafés and barbershops: did anyone know of old fig trees? He was directed to a mosque courtyard where a single tree stood, its trunk scarred by age. The imam told him it had been planted by a Greek woman in 1890. She had converted to Islam for marriage, and her fig tree had stayed when the rest of her family left.

"The tree is Muslim now?" Alekos asked, half joking.

The imam smiled. "The tree is a tree. It doesn't care about religion. But the fruit is good, and the shade is welcome, and the children still steal figs on their way to school. Some things don't change."

Alekos documented the tree — Ficus carica var. aydinensis, Sultan's Honey type, single surviving specimen — and took a cutting. He moved on, through Nazilli and Sultanhisar, finding isolated survivor trees in cemeteries and mosque courtyards and the edges of new tobacco fields. Each tree a refugee of sorts. Each tree still bearing fruit.

Chapter 21: The Ferry to Mytilene

From Ayvalık, a small port town on the Turkish coast, Alekos boarded a ferry for Lesbos. The crossing took four hours through calm water, the island's shape gradually resolving from a gray-green smudge on the horizon into a landscape of olive groves and pine forests and white villages stacked on hillsides.

Lesbos was Greek now — had always been Greek in population, despite centuries of Ottoman rule. But it had its own refugees: Greeks from Anatolia who had crossed the water in 1923, bringing what they could carry. Some had brought fig cuttings. The island's orchards still held varieties that originated on the mainland, varieties that no longer existed in the places where they were born.

The ferry docked at Mytilene in the late afternoon. Alekos found a small hotel near the harbor, ate a plate of grilled sardines at a waterfront taverna, and went to bed early. Tomorrow he would climb to the monastery of Limonos, where the monk Gregorios kept his orchard of ancient figs.

He dreamed of his father, walking through an orchard with a notebook, calling out the names of trees.

Chapter 22: Father Gregorios

The monastery of Limonos stood on a hillside among cypresses, its stone walls pale gold in the morning light. Alekos walked up the path, past terraces of olive trees and vegetable gardens, until he reached the monastery gate.

A monk in a worn black cassock was working in the garden beside the gate. He was perhaps sixty-five, with a gray beard and hands stained dark from soil and sap. He looked up as Alekos approached.

"You must be the fig collector," he said. "The bishop sent word from Mytilene that a professor from Athens was coming."

"Father Gregorios?"

"The same." The monk straightened, wiping his hands on his cassock. "Come. I'll show you the orchard."

The orchard was unlike anything Alekos had seen. Twelve varieties of fig grew in a terraced garden behind the monastery church, each tree carefully pruned and mulched, each with a small wooden sign bearing a name: Christ's Thirst, the Virgin's Sweetness, the Apostle's Shade, St. Nicholas's Gift. The trees were old — some clearly centuries old — but well tended.

"This one"—Gregorios touched the trunk of a massive tree with pale green fruit—"came from a cutting brought by monks fleeing Constantinople in 1453. They carried fig cuttings along with the icons. They said both were holy."

"And this one?" Alekos pointed to a smaller tree with deeply lobed leaves.

"From your part of the world. A refugee brought it from Smyrna in 1923. She gave it to the monastery because she had no garden of her own. She said, 'Pray for the tree, and the tree will pray for me.'"

Chapter 23: The Monastery Orchard

Alekos spent two days in the monastery orchard, documenting each variety with the care he had learned from his father. Gregorios worked beside him, holding the measuring tape, recording leaf counts, bringing water and olives and bread when the work ran through lunch.

The names were the monk's own invention — the trees had no botanical variety names, only the old local words. But Gregorios had given them liturgical names as a form of devotion. "The fig appears in the Bible more than any other fruit," he explained. "Adam and Eve sewed fig leaves. The promised land was a land of fig trees. Jesus cursed a fig tree — the only destructive miracle. It's a complicated fruit, theologically speaking."

"Have you ever thought of writing this down? The history of the orchard, the varieties?"

Gregorios shook his head. "I'm a monk, not a scholar. I tend the trees. That's my writing."

"It's important work."

"It's the only work." The monk looked at the orchard. "These trees — they were planted by men who knew they would never eat the fruit. Most of these varieties take ten, fifteen years to produce a good crop. The monks who planted them did it for the monks who would come after. That's what a monastery is — a bet on the future."

Alekos thought about his own work. He had always thought of it as documentation, preservation, a defense against loss. But perhaps it was also what Gregorios described: a bet on the future. A belief that someone, someday, would want to know what he had recorded.

Chapter 24: The Beekeeper's Knowledge

Gregorios kept bees. Eight hives stood at the edge of the orchard, and the monk tended them with the same calm attention he gave his trees.

"The bees are part of the orchard," he explained. "Figs are pollinated by a specific wasp — you know this, you're a botanist. But the wasps need the bees, and the bees need the flowering herbs, and the herbs need the shade of the trees. Nothing exists alone."

He showed Alekos how he moved the hives through the orchard as different trees came into bloom, how he could taste the difference in the honey depending on which trees the bees had visited. The Virgin's Sweetness honey was pale and floral. The Christ's Thirst honey was darker, almost smoky.

"When I was young," Gregorios said, "I thought prayer was the most important thing a monk could do. Now I think it's attention. Paying attention to the bees, to the trees, to the way the light falls on a particular leaf at a particular hour. That is prayer. The rest is just words."

Alekos wrote this down in his notebook. Not as a botanical observation, but as something he wanted to remember.

Chapter 25: The Lost Varieties

On the third evening at the monastery, Gregorios brought out a bottle of sweet wine and they sat in the orchard as the light faded over the Aegean.

"I've kept records," the monk said. "Every fig variety known on Lesbos since the eighteenth century. Not scientific records — just notes from old agricultural reports, monastery ledgers, travelers' accounts. There were thirty-one varieties on this island in 1800. There are eighteen now. The others are gone."

"Gone completely?"

"Some may survive in a courtyard somewhere, an old woman's garden, a tree no one has bothered to cut down. But I haven't found them." He poured more wine. "The Smyrna white — that was lost in the exchange. The Aivali black — burned in 1922. The Pontic gold — abandoned when the Pontic Greeks were driven out. The fig varieties follow the people. When the people are displaced, the trees die."

Alekos listened. He thought of the Sultan's Honey in Aydın, the one surviving tree in the mosque courtyard. He thought of the Thief's Fig at the cemetery wall. He thought of his mother's cutting, carried across the sea in an apron pocket.

"Can I see your records?" he asked.

Gregorios nodded. "I thought you might want to. I copied them out for you — the list of lost varieties, with whatever descriptions I could find." He handed Alekos a folded paper. "Maybe you can find some of them. Or maybe the list itself is a kind of preservation. 'These existed. These were known. These were lost.' Even that is worth recording."

Chapter 26: The Letters Home

The monastery had a desk in the guest cell — a small wooden table facing a window that looked out over the orchard. Alekos sat there in the evenings and wrote to Eleni.

*My dear Eleni,*

*I am writing from a monastery on Lesbos, where a monk keeps bees and talks about trees as if they were saints. He has given me a list of fig varieties that no longer exist, and I have spent the evening reading it as if it were a book of prayers for the dead.*

*I think about you more often than I expected. Not with guilt — I know you did not want me to feel guilt — but with the particular loneliness of having no one to turn to and say, "Look at this. Look at what I found." You would like Father Gregorios. He has the same practicality you have, the same calm certainty that work is the answer to most questions.*

*The figs are what I came for, and I am finding them. But I am also finding something I did not expect: my father. He is everywhere here. In the trees he documented, in the people who remember him, in the particular way he held a leaf between thumb and forefinger when he was measuring. I have not thought about him this much since he died. I am not sure if it is healing or merely reopening.*

*Tell Nikos I am well. Tell him the trees are political after all — they are archives of everything the politicians tried to erase.*

*Your Alekos*

He sealed the envelope and set it aside. In the morning, one of the monks would take it down to Mytilene and post it. By the end of the week, Eleni would be reading it in their courtyard in Kypseli, under the Smyrna purple, the tree his mother had carried.

Chapter 27: The Confession

"Father, I have a confession."

Gregorios looked up from the beehive he was inspecting. "I'm not that kind of priest. I gave up hearing confessions years ago. Too many sins I didn't need to know about."

"Then as a friend."

The monk set down his smoker and sat on the low stone wall that bordered the orchard. "As a friend, then."

Alekos gathered his thoughts. The afternoon light fell through the fig leaves in patterns that shifted with the breeze. Somewhere, a bee was working its way through the clover.

"I came here to document figs," he said. "That's what I told the foundation, what I told my wife, what I told myself. And I am documenting them. But that's not the only reason."

"I know."

"I'm trying to find something I lost. Not a tree — something in myself. The person I was before 1923. Before we fled Smyrna." He paused. "I was seventeen when we left. I had never lived anywhere else. The smell of the sea in the morning, the sound of the muezzin and the church bells overlapping, the taste of the first figs in August — that was my whole world. And then it was gone."

"And you want it back."

"I know I can't have it back. But I wanted to see if any of it remained. If anything of that world survived."

Gregorios was quiet for a moment. Then he picked up a fallen fig leaf from the ground and held it out.

"The leaf is dead," he said. "It fell from the tree. It will decay into soil. But the tree is still alive, and next spring it will produce new leaves. Not the same leaves — the old leaves are gone — but leaves from the same roots, the same wood."

"You're saying I should let go of the old leaves."

"I'm saying the tree does not mourn its leaves. It makes new ones. But it does not forget the old ones either — they become the soil that feeds the roots." He handed Alekos the leaf. "You are still the same tree. Even if the leaves you remember have fallen."

Chapter 28: The Old Woman of Molyvos

The hill town of Molyvos climbed the northern slope of Lesbos in a cascade of red-tiled roofs and narrow alleys. Alekos had been told about a woman here — a certain Kyria Maro, eighty-three years old, who still spoke the old Lesbos dialect and kept a fig variety that existed nowhere else.

He found her in a small house near the top of the town, its courtyard overlooking the sea. She was small and stooped, with hands that had worked for eight decades and showed no sign of stopping. A fig tree grew against the south wall — old, gnarled, its trunk twisted by decades of wind.

"The Bride's Blush," she said, touching the trunk. "My grandmother's grandmother planted it. Seven generations of women in my family have been married under this tree."

The figs were pale green with a pink blush at the stem end — hence the name. The fruit was small but intensely sweet, with a flavor Alekos had never encountered. He documented the tree carefully: leaf shape, fruit characteristics, estimated age (she thought it was planted around 1770, which would make it nearly 170 years old).

"I'm the last," Kyria Maro said. "My daughters married men from Athens and Thessaloniki. They don't want an old house in a hill town. When I die, the house will be sold and the tree will be cut down."

"Not if I can help it." Alekos took a cutting, wrapping it carefully. "I'll plant this in Athens. The variety will survive."

"The variety." She shook her head. "The variety is not the tree. The tree is this tree, in this courtyard, where my grandmother stood at her wedding and my mother stood at hers and I stood at mine. You can plant a cutting in Athens, but it won't be the same tree."

"No," Alekos agreed. "It won't. But it will be a daughter of this tree. And that is not nothing."

The old woman considered this. Then she smiled, a smile that rearranged the wrinkles on her face into something like approval.

"Take your cutting, botanist. And when you plant it, tell it where it came from."

Chapter 29: The Storm

The storm came without warning — a spring squall that swept down from the north, turning the sea white and rattling the monastery windows. The ferry service was suspended. The roads to Mytilene were washed out in two places. Alekos was stranded on Lesbos for three extra days.

He spent them usefully. The monastery library held agricultural records going back to the eighteenth century — ledgers and correspondence, mostly, but also notebooks kept by monks with an interest in the land. Gregorios brought him candles and coffee and left him alone.

The records were revelatory. A monk named Father Anthimos had kept a detailed orchard journal from 1820 to 1860, recording planting dates, harvest yields, and the comings and goings of grafted varieties. Another monk, Father Iakovos, had sketched the leaves of thirty-one fig varieties in 1785, with notes on their origins — the villages, the families, the particular qualities of each fruit.

Alekos copied everything. The handwriting was difficult, the ink faded, but the information was gold. Here was proof that heritage varieties had been documented for centuries, that the monks had understood fig diversity as something worth preserving long before modern botany gave it a name.

"The Church has its faults," Gregorios said when Alekos showed him what he had found, "but patience is not one of them. A monastery thinks in centuries. The world outside thinks in election cycles. That's why the trees are still here."

Chapter 30: Departure from Lesbos

The storm passed. The roads were repaired. Alekos packed his bag — now considerably heavier with notebooks, pressed specimens, and the bundle of cuttings that included the Bride's Blush, a cutting from the Apostle's Shade fig at the monastery, and a dozen others.

Gregorios walked him down to the harbor at Mytilene. The monk carried a jar of honey from the monastery hives — a gift for Alekos's wife. "Tell her the bees sent it," he said.

At the dock, they shook hands.

"I have your address in Athens," Gregorios said. "I'll write. The orchard will be here when you want to return."

"I'll send you seeds," Alekos said. "From Lebanon. There's a variety there — the Bekaa gold — you would like."

"I would like that." The monk hesitated, then reached into his cassock and pulled out a small wooden cross, plain and unvarnished. "This is not for prayer. It's just something from the monastery. To remember."

The ferry whistle blew. Alekos boarded, and as the boat pulled away from the dock, he stood at the rail and watched the island recede. The monastery was invisible from here, hidden among the cypresses on its hillside. But he knew its orchard was there — the twelve varieties, the bees, the monk who tended them. A small, steady light in a world that seemed determined to go dark.

Chapter 31: The Crossing to Beirut

From Rhodes, Alekos caught a Greek merchant steamer heading east. The *Agios Nikolaos* was carrying olive oil and wine to Beirut, and his passage was arranged through a university connection. The captain, a grizzled man from Kalymnos named Vassilis, offered him a cabin and warned him about the weather.

"It's unsettled this time of year. Could be smooth, could be rough. The Mediterranean doesn't like to be predictable."

The sea was calm for the first two days. Alekos spent them on deck, going through his notes, organizing his specimens. He had documented twenty-three varieties so far — more than he had expected, fewer than he had hoped. Each one came with a story. Each story was, in some way, about loss: a family displaced, a village abandoned, a tree cut down or forgotten or simply no longer tended.

But the stories were also about survival. The great tree at Kirazlı, still bearing fruit three hundred years after a dervish planted it. The Bride's Blush in Molyvos, seven generations of weddings in its shade. The monastery orchard, twelve varieties tended by a monk who thought of it as prayer. These things endured. Against all odds, they endured.

The third day brought rough seas. Alekos stayed in his cabin, writing a preliminary report for the Swiss foundation. Scientific language, careful qualifications, no mention of dervishes or monks or the particular taste of a fig picked at dawn. But between the lines, if you knew how to read them, the stories were still there.

Chapter 32: Beirut

Beirut rose from the sea like a white crown — new buildings, French colonial offices, the minarets of mosques and the spires of churches competing for the skyline. The port was crowded with ships from Marseille, Alexandria, Istanbul, Trieste. The smell of coffee and diesel and salt water mixed in the warm spring air.

Samia Khoury was waiting at the dock. She was forty-five now, with silver streaks in her dark hair and the same sharp, curious eyes Alekos remembered from her student days. She had been his best student in the year he taught at the American University — not the most obedient, but the one who asked questions that made him reconsider his own answers.

"Professor Kappas." She embraced him formally, then less formally. "You look older."

"You look the same."

"Liar." She laughed. "Come. I have a car. We'll go to the university first, then I'll take you to the Bekaa. How was your journey?"

He told her about the ferry, the storm, the specimens. About Mustafa and Lale, Gregorios and the monastery. About the list of lost varieties, the photograph of his father, the old woman in Molyvos who had married under the same tree as her grandmother. Samia listened as she drove through Beirut's crowded streets, navigating between trams and donkeys and French soldiers in uniform.

"You've been on a pilgrimage," she said. "Not a research trip. A pilgrimage."

"Is there a difference?"

"For you? I don't think so."

Chapter 33: The Botanical Garden

The American University of Beirut sat on a hill overlooking the Mediterranean, its red-tiled roofs and stone buildings a piece of New England transplanted to the Levant. Samia led Alekos through the campus to the botanical garden, which she had designed and planted herself.

"The university let me have this corner," she said. "They thought I was going to plant roses. Instead I planted figs."

The garden held nineteen Lebanese fig varieties, each with a small brass label in Arabic, French, and Latin. Samia walked Alekos through the collection, pointing out the characteristics of each: the Bekaa gold, with its pale amber fruit and three-lobed leaves; the Tripoli black, small but intensely sweet; the Mountain Pearl, which grew only above a thousand meters and produced fruit so delicate it couldn't survive the journey to the coast.

"This one"—she stopped at a small tree with distinctive five-lobed leaves—"I found in a village in the Bekaa. A single tree, growing out of a stone wall. The farmer said it had been there since his grandfather's time. No one knew the variety name. I called it the Survivor."

"It's a good name."

"It's the only name that made sense." She touched a leaf. "Lebanon has been conquered by Egyptians, Assyrians, Babylonians, Persians, Greeks, Romans, Arabs, Crusaders, Mamluks, Ottomans, and now the French. The fig trees don't care. They were here before all of them, and they'll be here after."

Alekos recognized the sentiment. It was what Gregorios had said. What Mustafa had said. What his father had said, quietly, every time he knelt to measure a leaf.

Chapter 34: The Road to the Bekaa

The road climbed through the Lebanon Mountains, winding past villages of stone houses and terraced fields. Samia drove with the confidence of someone who had made this journey many times, pointing out landmarks as they went.

"The Bekaa Valley was the breadbasket of the Roman Empire. They grew wheat here, olives, grapes. But before the Romans, before anyone, there were figs. The Phoenicians traded dried figs across the Mediterranean. Some of the varieties in Italy and Greece probably originated here."

The valley opened before them — a broad, fertile plain between the Lebanon and Anti-Lebanon ranges, patchworked with fields and orchards. The light was different from the Aegean: harsher, more golden, the shadows sharper against the mountains.

"In the medieval period," Samia continued, "Arab agricultural writers described the figs of the Bekaa in detail. Ibn al-Awwam, in the twelfth century, listed fourteen varieties. I've found eleven. The other three are either lost or renamed."

"The story of my trip," Alekos said. "Finding some things, missing others."

"That's the nature of documentation. You never find everything. But you find something."

She turned off the main road onto a dirt track that wound between fields of wheat and vegetables. A village appeared ahead — small, with stone houses and a single minaret.

"This is where I found the Survivor," Samia said. "The people here — they're mostly farmers. Poor, but they've been on this land for centuries. They know things about figs that no university ever taught."

Chapter 35: The Village of the Figs

The village was called Deir al-Tin — "the monastery of the fig" — and it deserved its name. Fig trees grew everywhere: in courtyards, along walls, in the corners of fields, in the cemetery. Some were young, planted within living memory. Some were ancient, their trunks hollowed by age but still sending out new branches each spring.

The village mukhtar, a dignified man named Abu Khalil, received them with coffee and a lengthy welcome. He had known Samia for years and treated her with the respect accorded to a learned person — and with the slight wariness that rural people everywhere reserved for visitors from the city.

"The professor is from Greece," Samia explained. "He is documenting fig varieties. The old ones, the ones with stories."

"Stories." Abu Khalil nodded. "We have many stories. But the oldest one — you must see the Roman fig."

He led them to the edge of the village, where a fig tree of immense size grew beside the ruins of a stone building. The trunk was composed of multiple fused stems — the original central trunk had died centuries ago, but the tree had put out new shoots that had grown into trunks of their own. The canopy spread thirty meters in every direction.

"The old people say this tree was planted by a Roman centurion who retired here. They say his name was Marcus, and he was from Spain, and he brought the cutting from his family's farm near Cordoba."

"The claim is unlikely," Alekos murmured to Samia.

"Of course it's unlikely. But the tree is certainly very old. And the story — the story tells you something true, even if the facts aren't accurate. It tells you that figs travel. That a man far from home will plant something that reminds him of where he came from."

Alekos looked at the tree. It did not matter whether the centurion was real. What mattered was that for generations, the people of this village had believed in him, had told his story, had tended his tree.

Chapter 36: The Healer

An old woman named Umm Yusuf lived at the edge of the village in a house surrounded by herbs and fig trees. She was the village healer — the person people came to for burns, stomach ailments, skin problems, the kinds of complaints that didn't merit a trip to the clinic in Zahle.

Samia had told Alekos about her. "She knows things about figs that aren't in any book. Traditional medicine, folk knowledge. You should talk to her."

Umm Yusuf received them in her courtyard, surrounded by drying herbs. She was perhaps seventy, with the deeply lined face of someone who had spent her life outdoors. Her hands were stained with plant dyes. She spoke only Arabic, so Samia translated.

"The fig tree is a pharmacy," she said. "The latex, from the stem — it removes warts. You apply it three times, at the new moon, and the wart falls away." She demonstrated on a broken leaf, the white sap beading at the wound. "The leaves, boiled in water — they help with stomach pain. My mother used them for all of us when we were children. The dried fruit is good for the digestion, everyone knows that. But the fresh fruit — the fresh fruit is for the soul."

Alekos documented everything she told him. The medicinal uses, the preparations, the dosages. These things had been passed down orally for generations — mothers to daughters, grandmothers to grandchildren. They had never been written down because the people who knew them did not write. They tended their trees and healed their families and asked nothing from the world beyond rain and sun and the annual miracle of fruit.

When he finished his notes, Umm Yusuf gave him a bundle of dried fig leaves. "For tea," she said through Samia. "For your stomach. And for remembering."

Chapter 37: Samia's Question

They ate that evening at a small restaurant in Zahle, on a terrace overlooking the Litani River. The food was simple — grilled lamb, tabbouleh, fresh bread, and a plate of figs that Samia had brought from the university garden. They drank arak, the anise spirit clouding with water and ice.

"Can I ask you something?" Samia said.

"Anything."

"Why did you never come back? After the year you taught here. You were offered a permanent position — I know you were, I read the university minutes years later. You turned it down. Why?"

Alekos swirled the arak in his glass. "I was afraid."

"Of what?"

"Of putting down roots again, and being pulled up." He set the glass down. "When I was seventeen, I lost everything. My home, my city, my country — everything I knew. I rebuilt my life in Athens. A wife, a son, a position at the university. But I never — I never trusted it. I never believed it was permanent. I was always waiting for the next disaster, the next expulsion, the next ship waiting at the dock."

"And Beirut felt like the same thing? Another temporary home?"

"Everywhere felt temporary." He looked at her. "You were the best student I ever had. You asked the hardest questions. I'm sorry I wasn't a better teacher."

"You were the teacher I needed." She reached across the table and touched his hand. "You taught me to look at plants and see history. You taught me that botany is not just classification — it's attention. It's love, in a particular form. I've tried to teach my own students the same thing."

"Does it work?"

"Sometimes. With the ones who are paying attention." She smiled. "The ones who aren't — they become administrators."

Chapter 38: The Mountain Monastery

The Maronite monastery of Deir Mar Antonios stood on a mountainside above the Bekaa, accessible only by a road that was more of a goat path. Samia left the car at the bottom and they walked up, through terraces of olive trees and grapevines, until they reached the monastery gate.

A young monk received them. He had been told they were coming — Samia had arranged it through the bishop in Zahle — and he led them directly to the orchard.

The Maronite orchard was smaller than the one on Lesbos, but older. The monks had been here since the fifteenth century, and they had planted figs as soon as they arrived. The varieties were different from the Aegean — adapted to the drier climate, the colder winters, the limestone soil. But the devotion to the trees was identical.

"Our founder, Mar Antonios, planted the first fig tree himself," the monk said. "He was from a village near Tripoli. He brought a cutting from his family's tree and planted it here. He said that a monastery without a fig tree was not a monastery — it was just a building."

"And the trees since then?" Alekos asked.

"Every abbot plants a new variety during his tenure. It's a tradition. The current abbot — Abbot Boulos — planted the one over there." He pointed to a young tree near the wall. "It's a Syrian variety, from near Homs. He said the monastery should have figs from all the places where Christians live."

Samia and Alekos exchanged a look. The same impulse — to collect, to preserve, to bring something from home and plant it in new soil. It crossed borders and religions and centuries. It was, perhaps, the most human instinct of all.

Chapter 39: The Damascus Pearl

Near the Syrian border, in a village whose name Alekos never learned to pronounce correctly, Samia led him to a fig variety she had been trying to document for years.

The tree grew in the courtyard of a family who had been in the village since before anyone could remember. The figs were unlike any Alekos had seen — pale green, almost translucent, with a skin so thin you could see the pink flesh through it. The fruit was the size of a small plum, and when Samia picked one and handed it to him, it seemed to glow in his palm.

"The Damascus Pearl," she said. "It was mentioned by Ibn al-Awwam in the twelfth century. He called it 'the jewel of the garden, which cannot travel.'"

"Why can't it travel?"

"Taste it."

He bit into the fig. The flesh dissolved on his tongue — sweet, floral, with a texture like ripe melon. It was the finest fig he had ever eaten. And he understood immediately why it could not travel: the fruit was so delicate that it would bruise if you looked at it too hard. By the time you carried it to the next village, it would be mush.

"Some things can't be collected," Samia said. "They can only be experienced. In the place where they belong."

Alekos thought about his cuttings, his specimens, his carefully pressed leaves. He could document the Damascus Pearl — he would, in fact, write down everything he could about it — but he could not take it with him. It would always be here, in this courtyard, in this village, ripening each summer for the family that tended it.

"That's humbling," he said.

"That's the point." Samia picked another fig and ate it. "Not everything is for us to keep. Some things are just for us to notice."

Chapter 40: The Last Night in Beirut

His last evening in Lebanon, Alekos had dinner at Samia's house — a modest apartment near the university, filled with books and plants and the noise of her two daughters. The girls were sixteen and thirteen, curious and direct, and they treated Alekos with the particular candor of children who had grown up around academics and were not impressed by titles.

"Mother says you've been collecting figs," the younger one, Nadia, said. "Why?"

It was the simplest question and the hardest. Alekos considered it.

"Because no one else is doing it," he said. "And because once a variety is gone, it's gone forever. Like a language. Like a story."

"You make it sound so sad."

"It is sad. But it's also hopeful. Every cutting I take — every seed I save — has a chance to become a new tree. And a new tree means the variety continues. It's a small bet against loss."

Nadia thought about this. "Like planting a memory."

"Yes. Exactly like planting a memory."

After dinner, Samia walked him to his hotel. The streets of Beirut were lively even at night — cafés still serving coffee, families strolling, the sound of Arabic music from a radio somewhere.

"Will you come back?" she asked.

"I hope so. The foundation may fund a second trip."

"Not for the foundation. For yourself. For the figs."

He looked at her. "Yes. I think I will."

Chapter 41: The Return Voyage

The steamer from Beirut to Piraeus took four days, stopping at Rhodes and Crete. Alekos spent the voyage organizing his collection: forty-seven heritage varieties documented across three countries, eighty-six pressed leaf specimens, twenty-three spirit-preserved fruits, thirty-one cuttings wrapped in damp moss, and three notebooks filled with measurements and stories.

He also had Lale's blue notebook, Gregorios's list of lost varieties, Samia's notes on the Bekaa figs, and a bundle of dried leaves from Umm Yusuf. His bag was heavier than when he left Athens, but he felt lighter. Something had shifted in him during the weeks of travel — some tightness he had carried since he was seventeen had begun to loosen.

The ship's steward, a Greek from Corfu, asked about his work. Alekos told him about the journey: Turkey, Lesbos, Lebanon. The trees. The people. The figs.

"That's a lot of effort for a fruit," the steward said.

"It's not about the fruit." Alekos was surprised by his own certainty. "It's about what the fruit remembers."

The steward looked puzzled but didn't press. He brought Alekos a glass of ouzo and left him to his notes.

The Mediterranean was calm. The islands slid past, familiar now. Alekos stood at the rail and watched the sea and thought about his father, who had made this same crossing in reverse thirty-five years ago, carrying a plant press and a map and a faith that the work mattered.

He understood that faith now.

Chapter 42: Athens

Piraeus was gray under a morning haze when the steamer docked. Alekos carried his bags down the gangplank and into the customs shed, where a bored official glanced at his passport and waved him through.

And there was Eleni, waiting at the barrier. She was wearing the blue dress he liked, the one with the white collar, and she was not smiling — she was never the kind of wife who smiled on railway platforms — but her eyes met his and held them, and that was more than enough.

"You came back," she said.

"I said I would."

"I know. But you came back different."

He didn't deny it. He couldn't. He was different — thinner, darker from the sun, his hands roughened from handling branches and soil, his eyes carrying the memory of trees and faces and stories. He was a man who had gone looking for his childhood and found instead his purpose.

They took a taxi through the streets of Piraeus and Athens, past the refugee neighborhoods that had grown into permanent quarters, past the Acropolis shining on its rock. The city was the same. Alekos was not.

At the house in Kypseli, Eleni made coffee and they sat in the courtyard under the Smyrna purple. The tree was heavy with developing fruit.

"It's going to be a good year," Eleni said.

"Yes," Alekos said. "It is."

Chapter 43: The University Report

The report for the Swiss foundation took three weeks to write. Alekos worked in his university office, surrounded by specimens and notebooks, translating his field observations into the formal language of botanical science.

He described forty-seven heritage fig varieties, seventeen of them previously undocumented in the scientific literature. He noted the threats to each: agricultural modernization, political instability, the simple neglect that came when the old people died and the young people moved to cities. He recommended conservation measures — seed banks, germplasm collections, a network of local custodians who could be paid small stipends to maintain the old trees.

But between the formal sections, in the appendices and footnotes, he included things that were not strictly scientific. The story of the dervish who planted the great tree at Kirazlı. The names Gregorios had given the monastery figs. Lale's translations of her father's stories. Samia's observations about what figs remembered and what they forgot.

The foundation would publish the scientific parts. The stories would remain in his personal archive. But Alekos knew which would last longer. The science would be updated, revised, eventually superseded. The stories — the stories would endure as long as someone was willing to tell them.

He sent the report to Geneva and began the harder work: figuring out what to do with everything he had learned.

Chapter 44: The Cuttings

The university botanical garden gave him a corner plot, fifty square meters with good soil and southern exposure. Alekos spent a week preparing the ground — digging, amending with compost, laying out the planting grid. Then he began to plant.

Mustafa's cutting from the great tree went in first. He had carried it across two seas, kept it alive with water and care and the particular attention that plants demand. Now he set it in the earth, firmed the soil around its roots, and watered it.

Then the others: the Bride's Blush from Molyvos. The Apostle's Shade from Gregorios's orchard. The Survivor from Samia's collection. Cuttings from Aydın, from Lesbos, from the Bekaa. Each one labeled with its name and origin and the name of the person who had given it to him.

The young gardener who had been assigned to help him, a botany student named Dimitris, watched the planting with curiosity.

"Will they all survive?"

"Probably not. Some of them are adapted to different climates, different soils. But some of them will. And the ones that survive — they'll be a living collection. A library of trees."

"A library of trees." Dimitris tested the phrase. "I like that."

"So do I." Alekos pressed down the soil around the last cutting and stood. "Now we wait. And water. And watch."

Chapter 45: The Son Returns

Nikos came to the garden on a Saturday afternoon, carrying his newspaper under his arm as always. He stood at the edge of the plot and looked at the tiny cuttings, the carefully lettered labels, the irrigation channels Alekos had dug by hand.

"This is what you brought back," he said.

"This is part of what I brought back."

"I read your field notebooks. Mother left them on the desk." Nikos paused. "You wrote about Grandfather. Things I didn't know."

"The photograph," Alekos said. "The one from Mustafa's family. It shows them together — Grandfather and Mustafa's father, at the great tree near Kuşadası. 1905. He was smiling, Nikos. In all the photographs we have of him, he looks serious. In this one, he's smiling."

"Why was he smiling?"

"I think because he was doing work he loved. With people who didn't ask him to be anything else." Alekos looked at his son. "I never understood that about him until this trip. He was happy there. In the orchards. With the trees. He was happy in a way he never was in Athens, after the exchange. And I never knew."

Nikos was quiet for a long moment. "When the war comes — the one I keep writing about — what happens to all this? The cuttings, the garden, the library of trees?"

"Some of it survives. Some of it doesn't. That's always the answer."

"That's not very reassuring."

"It's not meant to be reassuring. It's just the truth." Alekos picked up a handful of soil and let it fall. "But here's what I learned, Nikos. The trees have survived worse than anything we're likely to see. They survived the fall of Rome. They survived the Crusades. They survived the burning of Smyrna. They'll survive whatever's coming. And if we do our work — if we document, if we preserve, if we plant — then something of us will survive too."

Nikos looked at the cuttings again. This time, his expression was different. Not skeptical. Just young, and afraid, and trying to find a way to be brave.

"Will you teach me?" he asked. "The names. The varieties. What to look for."

"Yes," Alekos said. "I will teach you."

Chapter 46: The Letter from Lale

The letter arrived in August, written on pale blue paper, the handwriting careful and precise.

*Dear Professor Kappas,*

*I am writing to tell you about the project I started after you left. With my students at the school, we have begun documenting the old trees of our village. Each student has adopted a tree — the oldest ones, the ones with stories — and is keeping a journal of its seasons. We measure the leaves, record the fruit, and interview the grandparents about what the trees have seen.*

*It is not very scientific, I know. The children draw pictures of the trees and write poems about them. But already, they are noticing things they never noticed before. The way the figs ripen from the inside out. The way the wasps arrive in May. The way the old people smile when they talk about the trees of their childhoods.*

*I think this is what you meant when you said that documentation is a form of love. The children love their trees now. They will not forget them, even if they move to Istanbul or Ankara or anywhere else. The trees are part of them.*

*My father sends his greetings. The great tree is heavy with fruit this year. He says it is a sign. I don't know of what, but he says it anyway.*

*With respect and gratitude,*
*Lale Yılmaz*

Alekos read the letter twice. Then he put it in the folder where he kept his most important things: his father's photograph, his mother's fig leaf, the list of lost varieties from Gregorios, and the cutting knife he had carried across three countries.

The work was continuing. Not because of him, but because he had stopped long enough to notice, to listen, to show someone else what was already there. That was enough. It was more than enough.

Chapter 47: The Monastery Correspondence

Father Gregorios wrote in September, his letter carried by a monk traveling to Athens for a church council.

*Brother Alekos,*

*The orchard is well. The bees produced forty kilos of honey this summer — the best yield in ten years. I have planted a new fig tree, a variety I think you will not know, from a cutting an old woman brought from Crete. She says it was planted by her grandfather's grandfather, a Venetian sailor who settled in Chania and married a Greek woman. The tree is a hybrid — the fruit has the color of the Venetian green figs but the sweetness of the Cretan black. I am calling it the Sailor's Return.*

*Your cuttings are growing. The Apostle's Shade is the strongest — it has put out new leaves already. The Bride's Blush is slower, more cautious. I tell it that patience is a virtue.*

*Do you remember when you asked me if I had ever wanted to write down what I know about the orchard? I have started. Nothing formal — just a journal, the way you keep your field notebooks. The varieties, the seasons, the stories. It will not be published. It will stay at the monastery. But it will be here, when the next monk comes, and the next one after that. A bet on the future, as I told you.*

*The gate is always open.*

*Gregorios*

Alekos wrote back the same day, enclosing seeds from the Damascus Pearl and the Bekaa gold. He told Gregorios about the university garden, about Nikos's new interest in the cuttings, about Lale's project with the schoolchildren. He wrote that he was planning a second trip — to Crete this time, and perhaps to Calabria, where Greek-speaking villages still kept fig varieties that might date back to the Byzantine Empire.

"It never ends," he wrote. "The collecting. The documentation. The planting. But I think that's the point. It's not supposed to end. It's supposed to continue."

Chapter 48: The Exhibition

The university gave him a room for an exhibition — a small gallery off the main hall, with white walls and good light. Alekos spent two weeks preparing it.

He mounted the pressed leaf specimens on archival card, each with a label in Greek and Latin. He hung large-format photographs of the great trees: Mustafa's tree at Kirazlı, the monastery orchard on Lesbos, the Roman fig in Deir al-Tin. He displayed Lale's blue notebook, open to a page where she had written the Turkish and Greek names side by side. He put out the jar of Gregorios's honey, the bundle of Umm Yusuf's dried leaves, the photograph of his father and Mustafa's father standing together in 1905.

The exhibition was called "The Heritage Figs of the Eastern Mediterranean: A Botanical and Human Record." The title was formal, suitable for a university. But the subtitle, which Alekos added at the last moment, was more honest: "What the Trees Remember."

Nikos wrote a long article about it for his newspaper — not the usual political polemic, but a careful, almost tender piece about his father's journey and what he had found. The article began: "My father went looking for fig trees and found a country that no longer exists. He brought back leaves and stories. The leaves are pressed on card. The stories are still breathing."

The exhibition was modestly attended. But among the visitors was a young botanist from Thessaloniki who asked Alekos detailed questions about the varieties of the Meander Valley. Another was an elderly woman who recognized the Bride's Blush fig from her childhood in Molyvos and wept.

Alekos stood in the corner of the room and watched people interact with his collection. He understood, finally, what Gregorios had meant. This was not a scientific display. It was a bet on the future.

Chapter 49: The Courtyard Fig

Autumn came to Athens in a wash of golden light. The Smyrna purple in the courtyard was heavy with fruit — the heaviest crop in years, as if the tree had been saving itself for this season.

Alekos and Eleni harvested together, as they had done every September since they bought the house. He climbed the ladder and picked the high fruit. She stood below with the basket, directing him to figs he had missed. It was an old rhythm, comfortable and wordless.

When the basket was full, they sat at the wooden table under the tree and ate the first figs of the harvest. The fruit was perfect — deep purple skin, pale green flesh, the pink center that gave the variety its character. The taste was the taste of Alekos's childhood, and his mother's childhood, and the tree in the courtyard in Smyrna that his father had planted in 1910.

"It's a good year," Eleni said.

"Every year is a good year if the tree still bears."

She looked at him with the expression she had worn since his return — still assessing, still measuring the change in him. "You're different," she said again.

"Different how?"

"You used to carry Smyrna like a wound. You still do, but now you also carry it like a harvest. Like something that gives rather than takes."

He thought about this. "I found my father," he said. "In Turkey. In Lesbos. In the stories people told about him. I found a version of him I never knew — the man who was happy in the orchards, who smiled, who had friends among people who were supposed to be enemies. And I found myself. The person I was before."

"And?"

"And I like him. That person. The one who was curious and hopeful and not yet afraid." He took her hand. "I've been afraid for so long, Eleni. Afraid of losing everything again. But the trees — the trees taught me something. They lose their leaves every winter and grow them back every spring. Loss is not the end. It's just the season turning."

Eleni squeezed his hand. "I've been waiting for you to understand that for twenty years."

"I know. I'm sorry it took so long."

"Don't be sorry. Just stay."

He stayed. The figs ripened. The tree bore its fruit, and they gathered it, and the season turned.

Chapter 50: The Seed

In November, when the last figs had fallen and the leaves were turning, Alekos went to the university seed bank. It was a small room in the basement of the botany building, climate-controlled and sterile, lined with cabinets of labeled envelopes.

He carried a box containing forty-seven envelopes, each filled with seeds from the varieties he had collected. Each envelope was labeled with the botanical name, the collection location, the date, and the name of the person who had shown him the tree.

Mustafa Yılmaz — Kuşadası İzmir Black.
Father Gregorios — Apostle's Shade.
Kyria Maro — Bride's Blush.
Samia Khoury — Bekaa Gold.
Umm Yusuf — Survivor.
Lale Yılmaz — Thief's Fig.
Fatma Hanım — St. Photini Smyrna Purple.

He placed each envelope in the cabinet, filing them by region and variety. The seeds would remain viable for decades, perhaps longer. If the living trees died — from war, from development, from simple neglect — their genetic heritage would survive here, waiting.

He closed the cabinet and latched it. Then he stood for a moment in the quiet of the seed bank, surrounded by thousands of envelopes, thousands of futures held in suspension.

Outside, the November sun was pale on the university lawns. Students hurried past in coats and scarves, heading to lectures, thinking about exams and romances and the ordinary urgencies of youth. They did not know about the seeds in the basement, the trees in the garden, the cuttings that were putting down roots in the corner plot. They did not need to know. That was the point of preservation — it continued whether or not anyone noticed.

He walked home through the streets of Athens, past the Acropolis, past the refugee neighborhoods that had become permanent quarters. In Kypseli, Eleni was in the kitchen, cooking something that smelled of cinnamon. Nikos was in the courtyard, reading a book under the bare branches of the Smyrna purple.

Alekos sat down beside his son. The branches were gray now, their leaves fallen, but the buds for next spring were already visible at the tips — small green nodes wrapped in protective scales, waiting for the warmth to return.

"So," Nikos said, closing his book. "What now?"

"Now we wait for spring. And then we plant."

"What do we plant?"

"Everything." Alekos looked at the tree, which had crossed the sea in his mother's apron and survived everything the century had thrown at it. "Everything we want to survive us."

Nikos followed his father's gaze. Above them, the branches stirred in the winter wind, and somewhere in the wood, the sap was already preparing its slow ascent toward the light.
