# The Fig Collector — Outline

## Premise

Spring 1938. Alekos Kappas is a Greek botanist of Anatolian birth — born in Smyrna before the population exchange tore his family from their homeland. He has spent his adult life in Athens, teaching at the university, raising a son, trying not to look back. But a grant from a Swiss botanical foundation gives him a mission he cannot refuse: document the heritage fig varieties of the eastern Mediterranean before modern agriculture and political upheaval erase them forever.

Traveling through villages in Turkey, the Greek islands, and Lebanon, Alekos collects more than botanical specimens. Each tree has a story — a family that planted it, a wedding it was meant to commemorate, a war it survived. Each village offers a different version of the past. And the figs themselves — Smyrna purple, Lesbos black, Bekaa gold, Calabrian green — become a map of memory, of displacement, of what it means to belong to a place that no longer exists.

The Fig Collector is a quiet, meditative novel about loss and preservation, about the stories embedded in the land, and about a man who discovers that the act of collecting is also an act of return.

## Core Cast

- **Alekos Kappas**, 52 — Botanist and professor at the University of Athens. Born in Smyrna in 1886, he was 36 when his family was forced to flee. He carries the grief of displacement in a body that has learned to be still. His wife Eleni is the anchor he returns to.

- **Eleni Kappas**, 48 — Alekos's wife, a secondary school teacher of Greek literature. Daughter of a Piraeus fisherman, she has never understood her husband's attachment to a homeland she never saw. Their marriage is steady but carries the quiet distance of those who love across an unspoken gap.

- **Nikos Kappas**, 24 — Their son, a journalist for a left-wing Athens newspaper. Impatient with nostalgia, hungry for a modern Greece, he sees his father's obsession with old trees as a refusal to live in the present.

- **Mustafa Yılmaz**, 60 — Turkish farmer near Kuşadası, custodian of a 300-year-old fig tree that Alekos's father once studied. Mustafa's own father was the Greek botanist's Turkish neighbor. The two old men discover they share more history than either expected.

- **Lale Yılmaz**, 28 — Mustafa's daughter, a schoolteacher who speaks some Greek learned from the old families before they left. She serves as translator and bridge between her father's world and Alekos's.

- **Father Gregorios**, 65 — Greek Orthodox priest and beekeeper on Lesbos, who maintains a monastery orchard with twelve heritage fig varieties. A mystic of the earth, he sees tending trees as a form of prayer.

- **Samia Khoury**, 45 — Lebanese botanist and Alekos's former student from his years teaching in Beirut. Now a professor herself, she helps him navigate the Bekaa Valley and challenges his melancholic view of loss.

## 50-Chapter Plan

### Part I: The Tree (Chapters 1–10) — Athens and Departure

1. **The Herbarium** — Alekos Kappas works in the university herbarium in Athens, pressing a fig leaf collected decades ago. A letter arrives from Geneva: his grant application has been approved. He will document the heritage figs of the eastern Mediterranean.

2. **The House in Kypseli** — Evening at home with Eleni. Over a simple meal of bread, olives, and figs from their own small tree, he tells her about the grant. She listens without surprise. "You've been waiting to go back for fifteen years," she says.

3. **The Son** — Nikos visits for Sunday lunch. He argues with his father about politics — the Metaxas dictatorship, the coming war in Europe, the irrelevance of old trees when fascism is rising. Alekos listens. He remembers being young and certain.

4. **The Fig in the Courtyard** — Alekos examines the fig tree in his own courtyard — a Smyrna purple, brought as a cutting by his mother when they fled in 1923. It is the only thing from his childhood that survived. He takes a leaf sample for his collection.

5. **The Map** — In his study, Alekos unfolds a map of the eastern Mediterranean. He marks the villages he must visit: Kuşadası, Aydın, the hills above Ephesus, the monasteries of Lesbos, the Bekaa Valley. Each mark is a memory. Each memory has a fig.

6. **The Station** — Alekos boards the train at Athens station, heading for the Turkish coast via Thessaloniki and Istanbul. Eleni does not come to see him off — she said her goodbye the night before, quietly, in the dark. His bag holds notebooks, a camera, and a cutting knife.

7. **The Border** — Crossing from Greece into Turkey at the Edirne border. The passport control, the different uniform, the same olive trees on both sides. Alekos feels the familiar dislocation of being between countries.

8. **Istanbul in Passing** — A night in Istanbul, waiting for the ferry to İzmir. He walks through the old Greek quarter of Fener, past the Patriarchate. The city has changed. The Greeks are fewer now. He lights a candle in a small church and remembers his mother doing the same.

9. **The Aegean Crossing** — On the ferry from Istanbul to İzmir, Alekos watches the islands slide past. Lesbos, Chios, Samos — his future stops. An elderly Turkish man shares bread and olives. They speak in the old mixed language of the coast, Greek and Turkish woven together.

10. **Smyrna** — Alekos arrives in İzmir — the city that was once Smyrna, the city of his birth. He stands at the waterfront where, in 1922, refugees crowded onto ships as the city burned. He has not been here in fifteen years. The first thing he does is look for a fig tree.

### Part II: The Root (Chapters 11–20) — Turkey

11. **The Old Quarter** — Alekos walks through the old Greek quarter of İzmir. His family's house is gone — replaced by a modern apartment building. But in a courtyard nearby, he finds a fig tree he remembers. It is still bearing fruit.

12. **The First Specimen** — Documenting the İzmir fig: leaf shape, fruit color, tree age, location, local name. He interviews an old Turkish woman who remembers the Greek family that planted it. She offers him dried figs from last year's harvest.

13. **The Road to Kuşadası** — Alekos takes a bus south along the coast. The landscape is familiar — the same hills, the same sea, the same quality of light. He was seven when he first came with his father, a botanist himself, who was cataloguing the fig varieties of the Meander Valley.

14. **Mustafa's Tree** — In a village near Kuşadası, Alekos finds the tree his father documented in 1898: a 300-year-old İzmir black fig, its trunk wide as a room, its branches supported by stone pillars. The farmer who owns it is Mustafa Yılmaz, son of the man who once shared tea with Alekos's father.

15. **The Photograph** — Mustafa brings out an old photograph: his father and Alekos's father standing beside the great fig tree, 1905. Neither man had ever seen it before. Alekos holds the photograph and feels the past shift beneath him.

16. **Lale's Translation** — Mustafa's daughter Lale arrives from the school where she teaches. She speaks careful Greek, learned from books. She translates her father's stories: how the tree survived the great fire of 1894, how it gave fruit through famines and wars. "My father says the tree belongs to both families now."

17. **The Village Figs** — Alekos spends three days in Mustafa's village, documenting eight heritage varieties. Each tree has a name, a story. The Wedding Fig, planted for a bride who died in childbirth. The Sailor's Fig, brought from Alexandria by a man who never returned. The Thief's Fig, so sweet that children stole from it for generations.

18. **The Shared Meal** — Lale invites Alekos to dinner with her family. Over lamb and pilaf and fresh figs with yogurt, they talk about the old days — not the politics, not the exchange, but the ordinary life. The meals shared. The songs sung in both languages. Alekos realizes how much he has forgotten.

19. **The Departure from Kuşadası** — Alekos says goodbye to Mustafa and Lale. Mustafa gives him a cutting from the great tree. "Plant it somewhere it will be remembered," he says. Lale gives him a notebook in which she has written the Turkish names for all the varieties he documented.

20. **Aydın and Beyond** — Alekos travels inland to Aydın, documenting more varieties. But the landscape is changing — commercial orchards are replacing the old mixed gardens. The heritage trees are being cut down. His work feels more urgent now, and more fragile.

### Part III: The Branch (Chapters 21–30) — Lesbos

21. **The Ferry to Mytilene** — Alekos sails from Ayvalık to Lesbos. The island rises from the sea like a memory — he visited once as a boy with his father. The monastery of Limonos, they said, had figs that dated back to Byzantine times.

22. **Father Gregorios** — At the monastery, Alekos meets Father Gregorios, a priest who has spent forty years tending the monastery's orchard. Twelve varieties of fig, some from cuttings brought by monks in the 14th century. Gregorios speaks of trees as if they were parishioners.

23. **The Monastery Orchard** — Alekos documents the monastery figs. Each has a liturgical name: Christ's Thirst, the Virgin's Sweetness, the Apostle's Shade. Gregorios explains: "The monks named them for what they understood. A fig is as holy as any prayer."

24. **The Beekeeper's Knowledge** — Gregorios also keeps bees. He shows Alekos how the bees move between fig and flower, how the orchard is a single living thing rather than separate trees. "You're not collecting figs," he says. "You're collecting relationships."

25. **The Lost Varieties** — Gregorios tells Alekos about figs that have already disappeared: the Smyrna white, lost when the Greeks were expelled; the Aivali black, burned in the 1922 fire; the Pontic gold, abandoned when the Pontic Greeks fled to Russia. Each variety a casualty of history.

26. **The Letters Home** — Alekos writes to Eleni from the monastery. He describes the orchard, the priest, the bees. For the first time in his letters, he writes about his father — about the botanical expeditions they took together, about the things he learned and the things he never asked.

27. **The Confession** — Walking with Gregorios through the orchard at dusk, Alekos confesses: he is not just documenting figs. He is trying to find something that was taken from him. The homeland, the childhood, the person he was before 1922. Gregorios listens. "The trees don't care about borders," he says. "They only care about soil."

28. **The Old Woman of Molyvos** — In the hill town of Molyvos, Alekos meets an elderly woman who still speaks the old Lesbos dialect of Greek. Her family has kept a fig variety called the Bride's Blush for seven generations. She gives him a cutting wrapped in damp cloth.

29. **The Storm** — A spring storm traps Alekos on Lesbos for three extra days. He spends them in the monastery library, reading old agricultural records. The documentation is comforting — proof that someone before him cared enough to write things down.

30. **Departure from Lesbos** — Alekos leaves the island with a bag full of cuttings, a notebook full of observations, and something he hasn't felt in years: a sense that he is doing exactly the work he was meant to do.

### Part IV: The Fruit (Chapters 31–40) — Lebanon

31. **The Crossing to Beirut** — Alekos sails from Rhodes to Beirut. The Mediterranean is calm, the light Levantine. He thinks about his former student Samia Khoury, who has offered to guide him through the Bekaa Valley. He taught her in 1925, when he briefly held a position at the American University.

32. **Beirut** — Samia meets him at the port. She is forty-five now, a professor herself, married with two daughters. She embraces him. "You look older," she says. "You look the same," he replies. Beirut is a city of new buildings and old secrets.

33. **The Botanical Garden** — Samia takes Alekos to the university botanical garden, where she has been cultivating Lebanese fig varieties. She shows him the Bekaa gold — a variety mentioned in medieval Arabic agricultural texts, thought lost until she found a single tree in a mountain village.

34. **The Road to the Bekaa** — Driving into the Bekaa Valley with Samia. The landscape is different from the Aegean — drier, more rugged, with the Anti-Lebanon mountains rising to the east. But the figs are the same species. The same genus. Ficus carica. One family, many faces.

35. **The Village of the Figs** — In a small village in the Bekaa, they find a community that has cultivated figs since Phoenician times. The oldest tree, they claim, was planted by a Roman centurion who retired here in 120 AD. Alekos examines the tree — the claim is unlikely, but the tree is certainly ancient.

36. **The Healer** — An old woman in the Bekaa village practices folk medicine using fig latex and leaves. She shows Alekos treatments for warts, burns, and stomach ailments that have been passed down for centuries. He documents them carefully — the uses of the fig beyond the fruit.

37. **Samia's Question** — Over coffee in a Bekaa café, Samia asks Alekos why he never returned to Beirut after his year of teaching. He hesitates. "I was afraid," he says. "Afraid of putting down roots again, only to be pulled up." Samia nods. "That's what displacement does. It teaches you that love is temporary."

38. **The Mountain Monastery** — Samia takes Alekos to a Maronite monastery in the mountains above the Bekaa. The monks have kept a fig orchard since the 15th century. The varieties are different from Lesbos — the fruit is drier, the leaves smaller — but the devotion is the same.

39. **The Damascus Fig** — Near the Syrian border, Alekos documents a variety called the Damascus Pearl — pale green, almost translucent, so delicate it cannot be transported. It must be eaten within hours of picking. "Some things can't be collected," Samia says. "They can only be experienced."

40. **The Last Night in Beirut** — Alekos has dinner with Samia and her family. Her daughters ask about Greece, about his travels, about the old trees. He tells them about Mustafa, about Gregorios, about the figs that carry the names of the dead. Samia's younger daughter says, "You're not a collector. You're a witness."

### Part V: The Seed (Chapters 41–50) — Return

41. **The Return Voyage** — Sailing back across the Mediterranean, Alekos organizes his specimens and notes. He has documented 47 heritage fig varieties across three countries. Some are thriving. Some are down to a single tree. All are worth preserving.

42. **Athens** — Alekos arrives home to find Eleni waiting at the port. She looks at his bags of cuttings, his sun-darkened face, his notebooks. "You found what you were looking for," she says. It is not a question.

43. **The University Report** — Alekos submits his report to the Swiss foundation. His description of the heritage figs is meticulous, scientific, complete. But between the lines, in the asides and annotations, a different document emerges — a record of loss, of survival, of the people who tend what remains.

44. **The Cuttings** — Alekos plants Mustafa's cutting in the university botanical garden. It will take years to bear fruit, if it survives at all. But the planting itself is an act of faith. He waters it and waits.

45. **The Son Returns** — Nikos reads his father's report and comes to the house in Kypseli with questions. For the first time, he listens without arguing. "You were documenting more than trees," he says. Alekos nods. "I was documenting what trees remember."

46. **The Letter from Lale** — Lale Yılmaz writes from Kuşadası. She has started a small project with her students: documenting the old trees of their village, recording the stories that accompany them. She thanks Alekos for showing her what was already there.

47. **The Monastery Correspondence** — Father Gregorios sends Alekos a letter describing the spring flowering. His bees have produced the best honey in years. He has grafted a new fig from one of Alekos's Lesbos cuttings. "The orchard grows," he writes. "As all living things do, given the chance."

48. **The Exhibition** — Alekos arranges an exhibition at the university: photographs of heritage fig trees, specimens of leaves and fruit, maps of the varieties' distribution. The exhibition is modest but well-attended. Young students ask questions. Nikos writes an article about it for his newspaper.

49. **The Courtyard Fig** — Alekos and Eleni sit in their courtyard under the Smyrna purple fig, the one his mother brought as a cutting in 1923. The tree is heavy with fruit this year. Eleni reaches up and picks one. "It's a good year," she says. Alekos takes her hand. "Every year is a good year if the tree still bears."

50. **The Seed** — Autumn. Alekos packages fig seeds for the university seed bank — a precaution against loss, a bet on the future. He labels each envelope with the variety name and the name of the person who showed it to him. Mustafa. Lale. Gregorios. Samia. His father. His mother. The seeds will remain viable for decades. Someone, someday, may plant them. He seals the envelopes and places them in the archive, which is its own kind of planting.
