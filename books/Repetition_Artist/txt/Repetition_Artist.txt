Chapter 1: The Tell

Ava Chen froze the frame at 00:14:23.

On her monitor, Raymond Harlow's face hung mid-sentence — a man who had strangled seven women across three states, now speaking calmly to a FBI videographer about his favorite brand of instant coffee. His expression was placid. Cooperative. Almost bored.

Ava zoomed in on his left eye. She went frame by frame.

00:14:23 — neutral.
00:14:24 — micro-frown, left lip corner. 120 milliseconds.
00:14:25 — eyebrows rise, asymmetrical. Right brow higher. 80 milliseconds.
00:14:26 — nostrils flare. 60 milliseconds.
00:14:27 — head tilt, one degree left.
00:14:28 — back to neutral.

Three seconds. A wave that passed through his face and vanished, leaving no trace anyone would notice without training.

Ava leaned back. The量化 analyst cave — Room 417 of the J. Edgar Hoover Building — hummed around her: the drone of servers, the click of her colleague Karen's mechanical keyboard three desks over, the distant thrum of Washington DC traffic eight floors below. Fluorescent lights. No windows. The air tasted like recycled desperation and old coffee.

She pulled up her case archive.

Darius Webb. Confession video, 2019. He'd eaten at a Waffle House two hours before his arrest, discussed the weather with the arresting officer. In the tape, he described dismembering four victims with the same flat affect a man might use to describe assembling furniture.

Ava isolated the segment where Webb described the first kill. She ran it at quarter speed.

00:08:52 — neutral.
00:08:53 — micro-frown, left lip corner.
00:08:54 — asymmetric brow rise.
00:08:55 — nostril flare.
00:08:56 — head tilt left.
00:08:57 — neutral.

She stared at the screen.

It could be coincidence. Humans shared universal facial expressions. Ekman's work had proven that. A micro-frown followed by surprise markers was a known pattern — cognitive dissonance, perhaps. Guilt. The brain registering the weight of what the mouth was saying.

She pulled up a third file.

Elena Vasquez. The "Angel of Mercy" — twelve patients, twelve insulin overdoses. Her confession was three years old, shot in a stark interview room in El Paso. Vasquez had cried during the interview, but not when describing the deaths. She'd cried when the agent asked about her cat.

Ava found the moment Vasquez described the first injection.

00:31:17 — neutral.
00:31:18 — micro-frown, left lip corner.
00:31:19 — asymmetric brow rise.
00:31:20 — nostril flare.
00:31:21 — head tilt left.
00:31:22 — neutral.

Ava's hand moved to her mouse, but she didn't click anything. She just sat there, watching the three timelines side by side on her monitors.

Six faces. Six confessions. Six identical three-second sequences.

Karen's keyboard stopped clicking. "You still here, Chen? It's nine-thirty."

"Yeah," Ava said. "Looking at something."

"Look at it tomorrow." Karen gathered her bag — a massive canvas tote covered in cat hair and Smithsonian logos. "The files aren't going anywhere."

Ava nodded but didn't move. Karen shrugged and left, the door hissing shut behind her.

Alone, Ava pulled up more files. She had access to every confession video the Bureau had digitized — over two hundred, spanning fifteen years. She started running them through the facial analysis software, searching for the same sequence.

The micro-frown. The asymmetric brow rise. The nostril flare. The head tilt.

The tell.

At midnight, the software had processed thirty-seven videos. Thirty-two contained at least one instance of the full sequence. Twenty-seven contained it at the exact moment the killer described their first murder.

Ava turned off her monitor and sat in the dark room for a long time, listening to the servers breathe.

Chapter 2: The Pattern Library

Special Agent Marcus Pike found her still at her desk the next morning, slumped in her chair, staring at a montage of frozen faces.

"You look like hell," he said.

"Thanks. You look like a man who didn't find three bodies in a storage unit yesterday, so congratulations."

Marcus pulled up a chair. He was a big man, broad-shouldered, with the weary face of someone who'd seen too much and learned to carry it quietly. He'd been a field agent for eighteen years — violent crimes, then behavioral analysis. He and Ava had worked four cases together. She respected him because he didn't treat her like a lab tech who'd wandered into the wrong building.

"What are you looking at?" he asked.

"Patterns."

"Good. That's your job."

"My job is timeline analysis. I reconstruct sequences of events. I don't usually do facial coding."

"And yet."

She turned the monitor toward him. She'd lined up four confessions, the frames synchronized. She played them side by side.

"Watch their faces," she said. "Right at the three-second mark."

Marcus leaned forward. The four killers spoke, their words mismatched, but their faces moved in perfect sync. Micro-frown. Brow rise. Nostril flare. Tilt.

"What the hell," he said quietly.

"Thirty-two out of thirty-seven processed files. All serial homicide. All at the moment they describe the first kill." She paused. "And I found it in non-homicide confessions too. Armed robbery. Arson. Not as consistent, but it's there."

Marcus sat back. His chair creaked. "You're saying these people all have the same tell."

"I'm saying their faces do the same thing at the same narrative moment. The exact same sequence of micro-expressions, in the exact same order, with the exact same timing. That's not human variation. That's a signature."

"What kind of signature?"

Ava had been asking herself that question all night. The answer that kept surfacing was the one she didn't want to say out loud.

"Training," she said. "Someone taught them to do this."

"That doesn't make sense. You can't teach someone a micro-expression. They're involuntary."

"Most are. But you can train someone to produce them on cue. Actors do it. So do liars who've been coached to look sincere." She pulled up another window. "And there's another possibility."

She showed him a graph she'd built overnight. A comparison of the timing deltas.

"Look at the intervals. Between each micro-expression. They're not just in the same order — they're the same duration. The same rhythm. Like a song."

Marcus stared at the graph. His jaw tightened.

"How many cases have you reviewed?"

"Two hundred and thirty-seven confession videos in the Bureau database. I've processed sixty-one. The pattern appears in fifty-three."

"Which cases don't have it?"

She pulled up a list. "Non-violent offenses. White-collar crime. Witness statements." She paused. "Also, three of the five interrogations I analyzed from before 2014 don't show it."

"Before 2014?"

"The pattern starts appearing consistently around 2014. Becomes nearly universal in confessions from 2016 onward."

Marcus was quiet for a long moment. Then he said, "Who else have you told?"

"No one. I wanted a second pair of eyes first."

"Good. Keep it that way." He stood up, walked to the door, and turned back. "I'm going to make some calls. Don't share this with anyone until I tell you otherwise."

"Marcus. What are you thinking?"

He didn't answer right away. His hand rested on the doorframe.

"I'm thinking seventy-three serial killers have confessed on camera in the last decade," he said. "And if you're right — if every single one of them learned the same choreography — then somewhere out there, somebody taught them. Which means someone talked to all of them before we did."

He left.

Ava sat alone, the frozen faces staring back at her from the monitors.

Chapter 3: The Archive

Dr. Julian Cross lived in a Victorian townhouse in Georgetown, surrounded by books he'd never read and paintings he'd never look at again. He was seventy-one years old, a former professor of forensic psychiatry at Harvard, and the author of four books on criminal confessions. He had also, in his younger years, consulted for the FBI on twenty-seven serial killer interrogations.

Ava found his name in the literature — one of his papers from 2012 referenced "anomalous synchronous facial behavior" in a study of six confession videos. She'd called him that morning, caught him before his third coffee, and now she stood in his foyer while an elderly golden retriever sniffed her shoes.

"You're early," Cross said. "I like that."

He led her to a study that looked like a museum of a mind: floor-to-ceiling shelves, filing cabinets with rusted drawers, a desk buried under papers. Sunlight fell through tall windows onto dust motes that drifted like sediment.

"Show me what you found," he said.

Ava set up her laptop on a clear corner of his desk. She ran the comparison video — four faces, synchronized, performing the same sequence.

Cross watched without speaking. When it ended, he said, "Play it again."

She did.

He sat back, his fingers steepled. "I first noticed this in 2011. I was reviewing the confession of a man named Troy Kellerman. Killed five people in Kansas City. During the interview, his face did something I couldn't explain." Cross reached into a drawer and pulled out a yellowed manila folder. "I flagged it. I thought I'd found a new kind of deception marker."

He opened the folder. Inside were photographs — freeze-frames of faces, marked with handwritten annotations.

"I collected a dozen more cases over the next two years. Found the same pattern in eleven of them. I started writing a paper."

"What happened?"

Cross looked out the window. The dog had settled at his feet, head on its paws.

"Two men from the Bureau visited me. Behavioral Analysis Unit. They said my research touched on classified material. They asked me to stop."

"Asked you?"

"Told me. Politely, but firmly. They said the pattern was a known phenomenon under active investigation, and that my paper could compromise operational security." He shrugged. "I was a consultant, not an employee. I had no clearance. I withdrew the paper."

"You just gave up?"

"I was sixty years old. I didn't have the energy to fight a classified designation I couldn't see." He met her eyes. "But I kept the folder. I kept watching. And I noticed something else."

He pulled out a graph — hand-drawn, axes labeled in fountain pen. "The pattern becomes more refined over time. Earlier cases — 2009, 2010 — it's sloppier. The timing is less precise. By 2016, it's surgical. Someone improved the technique."

Ava studied the graph. "You think someone's been iterating."

"I think someone's been teaching. Iterating implies they're learning from mistakes. And they are. Whatever this is, it's not static. It's evolving."

"Who would have access to that many killers?"

Cross was quiet. The dog shifted in its sleep.

"That's the question I asked myself for ten years," he said. "And I never found an answer. But I'll tell you what I told myself to sleep at night: whoever it is, they're inside the system. They have access to interrogation rooms. They have access to suspects before we do. They're law enforcement, or they're posing as it."

He leaned forward.

"And they're still working. Which means they're still training. Which means there are killers out there right now who have been given this gift — and we'll never know it when we're looking at their faces."

Chapter 4: The Thread

Marcus called her that evening.

"I talked to a friend at Quantico. Retired BAU chief named Tom Forrest. He wouldn't say much, but he told me to look up something called the Ingram Project."

"Ingram?"

"Codename. Apparently it was a research initiative in the early 2000s — studying whether certain facial expressions could be reliably linked to deception. It got shut down around 2008. Forrest said most of the records were destroyed."

"Why would they destroy research data?"

"Because the research went somewhere they didn't like, and someone decided it was better if it didn't exist." Marcus's voice was tired. "He gave me a name. A researcher who ran the early coding work on Ingram. Dr. Lydia Grant."

"Is she still alive?"

"That's the thing. She died in 2009. Car accident. But before she died, she published a paper — single-author, in a small journal — about something called 'expression choreography.' The paper got almost no citations. But Forrest said it's the closest thing to a public record of what Ingram was actually studying."

"I want to read it."

"I already sent it to your email. Ava — be careful who you share this with. Forrest made it very clear that people who dug too deep into Ingram had a habit of disappearing from the Bureau."

"Disappearing how?"

"Transferred. Fired. Retired early. One died in a boating accident." A pause. "Forrest himself was pushed out in 2012. He told me he got a call from someone in the Deputy Director's office, telling him to drop an investigation. He refused. Two weeks later, he was reassigned to field training in Oklahoma."

"He's telling you all this on the phone?"

"He's telling me because he's seventy-three years old and he has stage four pancreatic cancer. He doesn't care anymore." Marcus exhaled. "Read the paper. I'll call you tomorrow."

Ava opened her email. The attachment was a PDF — scanned, OCR'd badly. The title: Expression Choreography: The Teachability of Deceptive Micro-Expression Sequences in High-Risk Populations.

Author: Dr. Lydia Grant. Journal of Forensic Behavioral Studies, 2008.

She started reading.

The paper was extraordinary. Grant had taken sixteen subjects — all convicted violent offenders — and trained them to produce a specific sequence of micro-expressions on command. The sequence was exactly what Ava had found in the confession videos. The training took an average of fourteen sessions. After training, the subjects could reproduce the sequence with 94% accuracy under controlled conditions.

But the paper's conclusion was what made Ava's blood run cold.

Grant wrote: "The successful training of this sequence suggests that the boundary between voluntary and involuntary facial expression may be more permeable than previously understood. Subjects report that, after sufficient practice, the sequence begins to feel automatic — occurring without conscious intent in high-stress situations. This raises the possibility of embedding such sequences as unconscious 'triggers' in populations that anticipate future high-stress interviews."

Embedding triggers. For populations anticipating future interviews.

Grant wasn't just teaching killers a tell. She was teaching them a tell that would make them look deceptive — that would make their confessions suspect, their credibility questionable, their statements easier to dismiss.

Or she was teaching them the opposite.

Ava pulled up the confession footage again. She watched Raymond Harlow's face cycle through the sequence.

If the tell was designed to look like deception... what if the real purpose was the opposite? What if it was designed to make the speaker look honest? To create a sympathetic response in the viewer? To make the confession feel authentic, final, unappealable?

She thought about the cases she'd worked. How many times had a clean confession closed a case early? How many investigations had stopped the moment the killer started talking?

How many times had they stopped looking because they thought they already had the answer?

Chapter 5: Lydia Grant

Lydia Grant's obituary was brief. Born 1968, PhD in cognitive psychology from MIT, joined the FBI as a research psychologist in 1995. Died in a single-car collision on a rural road in Maryland, November 2009. Survived by no immediate family.

Ava found her grave the next day. A simple headstone in a cemetery outside Rockville, set among older graves, slightly neglected. Moss had grown over the inscription.

She knelt and cleared the grass from the base.

"You found something," she said to the stone. "And someone didn't want it found."

She spent the afternoon at the Rockville Public Library, going through microfilm archives of local newspapers from 2009. The accident was reported on page six of the Montgomery County Sentinel, three paragraphs. Dr. Lydia Grant, 41, died when her vehicle left the roadway on Old Georgetown Road at approximately 11:45 PM. No other vehicles involved. Investigation ongoing.

Ava found the police report in the county records office. It was thin. No skid marks. No mechanical failure. No signs of impairment. Cause of death: blunt force trauma. Case status: closed.

She copied every page.

That night, she called Marcus. "The accident report says no skid marks."

"So?"

"So if she drove off the road at 11:45 at night, no alcohol in her system, no mechanical issues, no weather problems — why didn't she brake? Why didn't she turn?"

"You think she was forced off."

"I think she was going somewhere, or she was coming from somewhere, and someone didn't want her to arrive. The report says the investigation was 'inconclusive.' But they didn't investigate. They closed it in three days."

Marcus was silent.

"There's something else," Ava said. "Grant's paper was published in November 2008. The Ingram Project was shut down in 2008. If she knew something — if she was about to publish something that contradicted the official story — a year is a long time to wait."

"Maybe she wasn't planning to publish."

"What do you mean?"

"Maybe she was planning to talk to someone. Maybe she found out who was really behind Ingram. And maybe that person found out she was asking questions."

Ava stared at her notes. The pattern on her screen. The synchronized faces.

"I need to find out who worked with her," she said. "Who funded Ingram. Who ordered the shutdown."

"Be careful, Ava."

"I'm always careful."

"That's not what Karen says. She says you slept in your office last night."

"Karen talks too much."

"She also says you haven't eaten a real meal in two days."

"I'll eat when I understand what I'm looking at."

"That's not how bodies work." A long pause. "I'm going to come by tomorrow. We need to talk about something else."

"What?"

"Not on the phone. But I pulled a file this afternoon. A case from 2017. Unsub, multiple homicides, never caught. And the only witness who got a look at him? She said something strange. She said he smiled at her. Not a normal smile. She said it was like he'd practiced it."

Chapter 6: The Witness

The witness's name was Diane Porter. She lived in a stone cottage in the Blue Ridge Mountains of Virginia, seventy miles from the nearest FBI field office. She kept dogs — three of them, large, alert — and a shotgun in the mudroom. She had not given an interview about the 2017 case since the trial of a different suspect collapsed for lack of evidence.

But Diane Porter remembered everything.

She met Ava and Marcus on her porch, a cup of tea in her hand, her eyes scanning the tree line as she spoke. The dogs lay at her feet, watchful.

"He came through the back door," she said. "I was in the kitchen. It was three in the morning. I'd gotten up for water."

She described it flatly, the way trauma survivors describe things they've told themselves too many times. Her husband had been killed in the living room. She'd hidden in a crawlspace under the stairs. She heard the intruder moving through the house, opening drawers, taking things.

"And then he stopped," she said. "Right outside the crawlspace. I could see his shoes. Work boots, brown. He stood there for maybe thirty seconds. I thought he knew I was there. I thought I was going to die."

She took a sip of tea.

"But he didn't open the door. He just... stood there. And then I heard him say something."

"What did he say?" Marcus asked.

"I couldn't hear it clearly. It was quiet. Like he was talking to himself. But I heard the end of it. Three words. 'All things align.'"

"All things align," Ava repeated.

"That's what it sounded like. I didn't remember it for months. It came back to me in pieces, like a dream. But I'm sure about the words."

"And the smile?" Marcus said.

Diane Porter's hand tightened on her cup.

"After he said it. He smiled. I could hear it in his voice, and then I saw it — there was a crack of light under the door, and I saw his face. Just for a second. The smile was... wrong. Too wide. Too long. Like he was showing someone how to smile."

"Like a demonstration," Ava said.

"Like an instruction manual."

On the drive back to Washington, Ava was quiet.

"She described the same sequence," she finally said. "The micro-expressions. Even if she didn't know what she was seeing."

"She described a man who practiced his expressions," Marcus said. "That's unusual, but it's not proof of a conspiracy."

"It is when combined with a decade of synchronized confessions, a dead researcher, and a classified project that doesn't exist."

Marcus said nothing.

Ava's phone buzzed. An email from Julian Cross. The subject line: I found something.

She opened it.

Cross had been digging through Lydia Grant's old correspondence — letters she'd written to colleagues before her death, preserved in university archives. One letter, dated October 2009, mentioned a meeting Grant was planning. A meeting with someone she called "the curator."

Cross had found the recipient of the letter. A former DARPA program manager named Thomas Rourke.

Rourke was still alive. And he was willing to talk.

Chapter 7: The Curator

Thomas Rourke lived in a retirement community in Annapolis, a sprawling complex of beige buildings and trimmed hedges where old people went to wait. He was eighty-two, used a cane, and had the sharp, appraising eyes of a man who had spent his life in rooms where secrets were traded.

He received Ava in the community's library, a sterile room with large-print books and armchairs covered in plastic.

"You're Ava Chen," he said. "The timeline analyst."

"That's right."

"Lydia mentioned you. Not by name, but she said the Bureau had hired someone with a gift for pattern recognition. She was worried you'd find what she found."

"When did she mention me?"

"2008. I was consulting on a DARPA project about predictive behavior modeling. She called me, asked if I knew anyone who'd worked on what she called 'expression architecture.' She was cagey. She knew she was being watched."

Rourke shifted in his chair, his cane resting across his knees.

"She told me about the pattern. About the training protocol she'd developed. About how someone had taken her work and weaponized it."

"Who?"

"I don't know. She never said a name. But she told me it was someone inside the Bureau. Someone with access to the confession archive, the interrogation rooms, the suspects. She said they'd been running their own program — parallel to Ingram but separate. They called it the Conservatory."

"The Conservatory."

"It's a metaphor, I think. A place where something is cultivated. Grown. Refined."

"What happened to Lydia?"

Rourke's eyes didn't waver. "She was killed. Make no mistake about that. She was supposed to meet me the night she died. She had documents she wanted to show me. She never arrived."

"Did you tell the police?"

"I told the Bureau. Two agents came to my house the next day. They told me Lydia had been in a car accident. They asked what she'd told me. I said nothing. I could see in their faces that they already knew."

He leaned forward.

"I've been waiting twelve years for someone to ask these questions. I thought it would be Marcus Pike — he has the instincts. But it's you. The analyst. That's interesting."

"Why?"

"Because the person who built the Conservatory knows the Bureau. They know how agents think. They know how to hide inside procedures and paperwork. But they might not know how to hide from someone who reads the patterns in the margins."

Rourke reached into his jacket and pulled out a folded piece of paper.

"The night before she died, Lydia mailed me this. No note, no explanation. Just this address."

Ava took the paper. An address in Baltimore. 1427 Holliday Street.

"What's there?"

"I don't know. I never went. I was afraid. And I was old, even then. I told myself it was better to let it go." He smiled, a thin, sad expression. "My last chance to be brave, and I chose cowardice."

Ava folded the paper into her pocket.

"Thank you, Mr. Rourke."

"Be careful, Ms. Chen. The people who built the Conservatory are still there. They've had twelve years to improve their methods. And they know what questions to ask. They taught that to every killer they trained."

Chapter 8: 1427 Holliday Street

The address was a condemned building in a neighborhood that had been abandoned decades ago. Boarded windows. Graffiti. A chain-link fence with a sign warning of toxic materials.

Marcus parked two blocks away. They approached on foot, Ava carrying a flashlight, Marcus with his hand resting near his service weapon.

"You sure about this?" he asked.

"No."

"That's not reassuring."

"Good. You should be nervous."

They found a gap in the fence and slipped through. The building's front door was padlocked, but a side door had been pried open. Inside, the air was thick with mold and decay. The floor was littered with debris — broken glass, discarded clothing, the remains of a fire.

Ava swept the flashlight across the walls. They were covered in markings. Diagrams. Charts. Photographs.

She stopped breathing.

The walls were filled with faces.

Hundreds of photographs, arranged in a grid. Each one a mugshot or a surveillance still. Each one annotated with dates, locations, and a code she didn't recognize. Lines connected them — threads of association, timelines of contact.

At the center of the wall was a photograph of a man she didn't recognize. He was middle-aged, nondescript, with short brown hair and a face that seemed designed to be forgotten. Beneath his photograph, in handwritten block letters, was a single word:

CURATOR

"This is their archive," Ava whispered. "This is the Conservatory."

Marcus was studying the photographs. "These are killers. All of them. I recognize twenty of them off the top of my head."

"There are hundreds."

"How did they get these photographs? Some of these are from sealed case files."

Ava followed the lines. They formed a network — a map of influence. Each killer connected to a date, a location, and a method. The codes were consistent. She started decoding them mentally.

"These are training dates," she said. "Each code is a session number. The locations are where the training happened. They took them to specific places — these are all marked with coordinates — and they trained them."

"Trained them how?"

"I don't know. But I know what they trained them to do."

She pointed to a corner of the wall where a series of photographs showed the same sequence Ava had identified. Micro-frown. Brow rise. Nostril flare. Head tilt. Each frame annotated with timing instructions.

"It's a curriculum," she said. "They teach the sequence in stages. First the individual expressions. Then the combination. Then the timing. Then they wire it into a trigger phrase."

"'All things align,'" Marcus said.

Ava nodded slowly. "They train them to say it to themselves at the moment of the confession. The phrase triggers the sequence. The sequence makes them look authentic — or deceptive, depending on what the Conservatory needs."

"Why would they want killers to look deceptive?"

"Maybe they don't. Maybe some of them are meant to look authentic. To close the case. To make the confession stick." She turned to face him. "Maybe they're not teaching killers how to get away with it. Maybe they're teaching them how to confess perfectly. How to give the Bureau exactly what it needs so the case closes cleanly and no one looks any further."

Marcus's face went pale.

"You're saying they're manufacturing confessions."

"I'm saying someone has been inside the interrogation process for fifteen years. They've been shaping what we see, what we believe, what we close. And no one noticed because the confessions worked. Cases closed. Killers went to prison. Everyone felt good about the system."

"Until you looked at the faces."

"Until I looked at the faces."

Ava's phone buzzed. A text from Julian Cross.

She read it and felt the blood drain from her own face.

"It's Cross," she said. "He says someone broke into his house. They took his files. His computer. Everything."

"Is he okay?"

"He's in the hospital. He was unconscious when they found him."

The room felt smaller. The faces on the wall seemed to turn toward her.

"Marcus. They know we're looking."

---

Chapter 9: The Conservatory

The FBI placed Julian Cross under guard at George Washington University Hospital. He'd been beaten — not fatally, but thoroughly. His hands were broken. His laptop was gone. His files, his research notes, his correspondence — everything taken.

Ava sat by his bedside. His face was swollen, but his eyes were open.

"They knew exactly what they were looking for," he said, his voice a rasp. "They didn't take anything else. Not my wallet. Not my watch. Just my work."

"What did you find? Your email said you found something."

Cross tried to smile. The effort clearly hurt.

"Lydia Grant wasn't the only researcher studying the pattern. She had a collaborator. A graduate student named Michael Tran. He helped her code the early data. After she died, he went quiet. I found him living in Philadelphia, teaching at a community college."

"Did you contact him?"

"No. I didn't get the chance." Cross closed his eyes. "But I found something else. In Lydia's old files — the ones she'd archived before she died. She kept a journal. Digital. Password-protected. I managed to crack it."

"What did it say?"

"It said the Conservatory wasn't her idea. She was approached. Recruited. Someone senior in the Bureau saw her early research and offered her resources — funding, access, protection — to develop the training protocol. She didn't ask questions at first. She was young. Ambitious. She thought she was advancing the science."

"But something changed."

"She found out where the training was being applied. She found out they were testing it on real suspects, not just volunteers. She confronted her handler. And that's when she realized the Conservatory wasn't a research project. It was an operational program."

Cross struggled to sit up. Ava helped him.

"They were running experiments on people in custody," he said. "Prisoners. Suspects. People who couldn't say no. They taught them the sequence, then they put them in front of cameras and watched what happened. They documented everything. They built a taxonomy of tells."

"For what purpose?"

"Control. If you know exactly what a truthful person looks like, you can make anyone look truthful. If you know exactly what a liar looks like, you can make anyone look like a liar." His voice dropped to a whisper. "The Conservatory isn't about catching killers. It's about manufacturing credibility."

Ava sat back. The implications spiraled outward.

"It's not just confessions," she said. "It's witnesses. It's testimony. It's anyone who goes in front of a camera."

Cross nodded, a shallow movement.

"Who runs it?"

"Lydia's journal mentions a name. Not clearly — she uses a code. 'The Architect.' She describes meeting him once, in a basement room at Quantico. She says he had perfect posture and never blinked."

"Never blinked?"

"It's a metaphor. She meant he was unreadable. A man who had trained himself so thoroughly that he showed nothing."

Ava looked out the window. The lights of Washington glittered in the distance.

"I need to find Michael Tran," she said. "I need to know what Lydia taught him."

"Be careful," Cross said. "If they beat an old man for a laptop, imagine what they'll do to someone who has the actual knowledge."

Chapter 10: The Student

Michael Tran taught introduction to psychology at a community college in Northeast Philadelphia. He was forty-three years old, married, with two children and a house in the suburbs. He had not published a paper since 2009.

Ava found him in his office, grading papers. He looked up when she knocked, and she saw recognition flicker in his eyes — not of her, but of what she was.

"You're from the Bureau," he said.

"I'm a forensic analyst. My name is Ava Chen."

"I know who you are." He set down his pen. "I've been expecting someone to find me for twelve years. I thought it would be a man in a suit. I'm glad it's you."

"Why?"

"Because a man in a suit would have come to arrest me. You came to ask questions."

He closed the door. His office was small, cluttered with textbooks and student art. He offered her a chair.

"I was Lydia's graduate assistant," he said. "I coded the video data. I built the first version of the pattern recognition software. I know everything she knew."

"Then you know about the Conservatory."

"I know it exists. I know what it does. I don't know who runs it." He paused. "But I know how to find them."

"How?"

"Lydia built a back door. Before she died, she embedded a tracking code in the training protocol — a second sequence that she never told her handlers about. Every trained subject carries it. Every confession video that uses the primary sequence also contains the secondary sequence. It's a signature. A tracer."

"Why would she do that?"

"Insurance. She knew she was working for people who would kill her if she became a liability. She wanted a way to prove what they'd done, if it ever came to that."

"Where's the back door?"

Tran reached into his desk drawer and pulled out a USB drive. "It's all here. The secondary sequence, the decoding software, and a partial list of trained subjects that Lydia compiled before she died. I've never used it. I've been too afraid."

He handed her the drive.

"But now you're here. And Dr. Cross is in the hospital. And I think the time for being afraid is over."

Ava took the drive. It felt heavier than it should.

"One more thing," Tran said. "Lydia's journal mentioned a location. A place where the original training sessions were held. Somewhere in West Virginia. She called it the Studio."

"Do you have an address?"

"No. But I have coordinates."

He wrote them on a sticky note and handed it to her.

Ava looked at the numbers. A point in the Appalachian Mountains, miles from any town.

The Studio.

She thanked Michael Tran and left. On the steps of the community college, she called Marcus.

"I have the back door," she said. "And I know where they started."

"Where?"

"A place called the Studio. West Virginia. I need to go there."

"Alone?"

"I don't have a choice. If I bring a team, someone will tip them off. The Conservatory is inside the Bureau. I don't know who to trust."

Marcus was silent. Then: "I know someone. A private investigator. Former Marine. He owes me a favor. His name is Cole. Call him if you need backup."

"Thank you, Marcus."

"Don't thank me. Just come back in one piece."

Ava ended the call and looked at the sky. The clouds were gathering. A storm was coming.

And she was walking straight into it.

Arc II: The Studio

Chapter 11: The Road

Ava drove south at dawn. The highway unrolled through Maryland, then Pennsylvania, then into the folded green hills of West Virginia. The coordinates Michael Tran had given her led to a narrow valley deep in Monongahela National Forest, miles from the nearest paved road.

She'd told no one except Marcus where she was going. She'd left her Bureau phone in her apartment. She carried a burner, a flashlight, a knife, and a SIG Sauer she'd qualified with but never fired outside a range.

The last five miles were unpaved. Her rental car scraped against exposed rock, branches dragging along the doors. The forest pressed in on both sides, dense and dark.

She found the Studio at the end of the road.

It looked abandoned. A two-story concrete building, windows boarded, graffiti spray-painted across the front wall. Weeds grew through cracks in the parking lot. A chain-link fence surrounded the property, topped with rusted barbed wire.

But the padlock on the gate was new.

Ava parked, got out, and examined the lock. No rust. Clean oil. Someone had been here recently.

She climbed over the fence, dropped into the overgrowth on the other side, and approached the building. The front door was steel, bolted. But a side window had been covered with plywood that was rotted at the edges. She pried it loose with her knife and climbed through.

Inside, the air was cold and still. Dust covered everything. But the floor showed footprints — recent ones, leading from the window she'd entered through to a stairwell at the back of the building.

She followed them.

The basement was a different world.

It was clean. Fluorescent lights hummed overhead. The floor was polished concrete. The walls were white, covered in the same kind of annotated photographs she'd seen in Baltimore — but more. Hundreds more. And in the center of the room, arranged in a semicircle, were chairs.

Eight chairs. Facing a single larger chair. Like a classroom.

She walked to the wall and studied the photographs. New faces. Cases she didn't recognize. Some looked recent — within the last year. The Conservatory was still active. Still training.

At the far end of the room, a door stood slightly ajar.

She approached it slowly, her hand on the SIG Sauer.

The room beyond was an office. Desk, computer, filing cabinets. On the desk, a photograph faced away from her. She picked it up.

It was her. Ava Chen. Standing outside FBI headquarters, talking on her phone. The photograph had been taken from across the street. The date stamp was three days ago.

She set the photograph down, her hands steady despite the cold that had settled in her chest.

On the computer monitor, a file was open. A document titled:

SUBJECT: AVA CHEN
STATUS: INVESTIGATING
RISK LEVEL: HIGH
RECOMMENDATION: CONTAINMENT

She heard a sound behind her. The door swinging closed.

"I was wondering when you'd find this place," a voice said. "You're faster than I expected."

Ava turned.

A woman stood in the doorway. Tall, gray-haired, dressed in a suit that cost more than Ava's monthly rent. Her face was familiar in the way that faces seen in crowds are familiar — generic, trained, forgettable.

But her eyes were sharp. And she wasn't blinking.

"My name is Deputy Director Katherine Wells," the woman said. "And I think we need to talk."

Chapter 12: The Deputy Director

Katherine Wells had been the FBI's Deputy Director of Criminal Investigations for six years. Before that, she'd run the Behavioral Analysis Unit. Before that, she'd been a field agent in Detroit, rising through the ranks on a reputation for closing cases that other agents couldn't solve.

She was also, Ava realized, the person who had been beating Julian Cross in his home.

"I didn't hurt Dr. Cross," Wells said, as if reading her mind. "I ordered the retrieval of his materials. The physical confrontation was... regrettable. An overzealous team."

"You broke his hands."

"Like I said. Regrettable." Wells pulled out the chair from behind the desk and sat down. "Close the door. We have things to discuss."

Ava didn't move. "I'm not closing the door."

"Suit yourself." Wells folded her hands on the desk. "You've been investigating the Conservatory for a week. In that time, you've identified the pattern, located two of our safe houses, and spoken to three people with direct knowledge of the program. I need you to understand how impressive that is — and how dangerous."

"Are you threatening me?"

"I'm warning you. There are people in this organization who would kill you for what you know. I am not one of them. But I can't protect you if you keep digging."

"I don't need your protection."

"You do, actually. Because the person who runs the Conservatory — the Architect — is not me. I'm his deputy. His administrator. I handle logistics. I keep the program running. But I don't control it."

"And who controls it?"

Wells was silent for a long moment. When she spoke, her voice was quieter.

"I don't know his real name. I've never seen his face. I communicate with him through encrypted channels. He's been running the Conservatory since before I joined the Bureau. He recruited Lydia Grant. He designed the protocol. And he's been watching you since the moment you pulled that first confession file."

"Where is he?"

"He's everywhere. The Conservatory has resources you can't imagine — access to every database, every case file, every interrogation. We have agents in every field office. We have judges. Prosecutors. Defense attorneys. The Architect built a system that doesn't depend on any single person. It's a machine now. It runs itself."

Ava's mind raced. "Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I want out."

The words hung in the air.

"I joined this program believing it was a counterintelligence operation," Wells said. "I was told we were identifying threat actors — people who might be turned by foreign powers. That's what the training protocol was supposed to do. Create reliable markers for deception."

"And then you found out the truth."

"I found out the markers were being used to manufacture testimony. To close cases that should have stayed open. To protect people who should have been prosecuted." She met Ava's eyes. "And I found out the training was being applied to people who were not suspects. Witnesses. Jurors. Even agents."

Ava felt the floor drop beneath her. "Agents?"

"Field agents. Analysts. People who work in the Bureau every day. Some of them don't know they've been trained. The protocol can be administered without the subject's knowledge — through repeated exposure to certain stimuli, certain phrases, certain facial cues. It's a conditioning process."

"That's impossible."

"It's not. Lydia Grant was a genius. And the Architect took her research and turned it into a weapon."

Wells stood up.

"I can't tell you the Architect's name. I don't know it. But I can tell you how to find him."

"How?"

"The Conservatory has a central server. It contains the complete training history, the subject database, and the administrative records. If you can access that server, you can identify every person who's been trained, every case that's been compromised, and every agent who's part of the program."

"Where is it?"

"Quantico. In a server room that doesn't officially exist. Behind the firearms training range. Access requires two forms of authentication — one biometric, one cryptographic. I can give you the biometric key."

"Why would you do that?"

Wells looked at her with something that might have been regret.

"Because the Architect is planning to expand the program. He's not satisfied with the criminal justice system. He wants to move into politics. Media. National security. The Conservatory is going to become something much bigger than a confession-manufacturing operation."

"When?"

"Soon. Months, not years." She reached into her jacket and pulled out a small device — a USB drive with a fingerprint scanner embedded in it. "This contains my biometric authorization. The cryptographic key is held by a man named Edward Porter. He's the lead engineer for the Conservatory's IT infrastructure. He doesn't know what the program really does. He thinks he's maintaining a training simulation database."

"You want me to find this engineer and get his key?"

"I want you to stop the Architect before he expands the program. Before it's too late."

Ava took the USB drive. It felt like a grenade.

"Why now?" she asked. "Why help me now?"

Wells looked away. "The Architect ordered me to have you killed. Two weeks from now, a team will be dispatched. I'm supposed to sign the authorization tomorrow."

"And you're refusing."

"I'm buying time. Use it well."

Chapter 13: The Engineer

Edward Porter lived in a townhouse in Alexandria, Virginia, with his wife and two golden retrievers. He commuted to Quantico every morning at 6:47 AM and returned at 6:12 PM, six days a week. He had worked for the FBI for twenty-two years and had never fired a weapon in the line of duty.

Ava watched his house from across the street for three hours before approaching. She'd told Marcus she needed a favor from his PI contact — a man named Cole Vance, who had served with Marcus in Afghanistan. Cole had provided the surveillance on Porter, confirming the routine.

At 6:47 PM, Porter was in his backyard, throwing a tennis ball for his dogs. His wife was inside, visible through the kitchen window, preparing dinner. Normal. Suburban. Innocent.

Ava walked up the side path and called his name.

Porter turned. He was a small man, soft around the edges, with the startled expression of someone who didn't expect surprises.

"Edward Porter? My name is Ava Chen. I work for the Bureau. I need to talk to you."

"I know who you are. You're the timeline analyst. I've seen your work on VICAP." He hesitated. "What's this about?"

"It's about the server you maintain. The one behind the firearms range. I need to access it."

Porter's face went pale.

"I don't know what you're talking about."

"Yes, you do. You think you're maintaining a training simulation database. But it's not. It's a classified operational program called the Conservatory. And I need to get inside it."

Porter looked toward his house. His wife had turned from the stove, watching them through the window.

"You need to leave," he said.

"I can't. People are going to die if I don't get into that server."

"Please. I have a family. I can't be involved in whatever this is."

"Mr. Porter. You're already involved. You've been maintaining the server for years. You probably signed NDAs. You probably wondered why the security protocols were so strict. You probably told yourself it was for national security."

He said nothing. His hands were shaking.

"The program is not for national security. It's being used to manufacture evidence. To manipulate confessions. To train people without their knowledge. And the people running it are planning to expand — to move into media, politics, every institution that shapes what we believe."

Porter's dogs had stopped playing. They sat at his feet, watching him with concern.

"If I help you," he said, "they'll know it was me."

"Not if we do it right."

"How?"

"You're going to take me there. Tonight. You're going to use your credentials to get me inside. And I'm going to copy the server's contents."

"They'll kill me."

"Mr. Porter. If we don't do this, they're going to destroy what's left of the integrity of the justice system. And eventually, they'll find out you maintained the server, and they'll kill you anyway. Because that's what they do to people who know too much."

Porter looked at his house. His wife was still watching. She couldn't hear them, but she could see his face.

"Wait here," he said.

He went inside. Through the window, Ava saw him talking to his wife. She saw her face change. She saw him put his hands on her shoulders.

He came back out wearing a jacket.

"Let's go."

Chapter 14: The Server Room

Quantico at night was a different place. The buildings were dark, the parking lots empty, the forest that surrounded the base pressing in like a held breath.

Edward Porter drove them through the main gate, flashing his badge. The guard waved them through without a second look. They parked behind the firearms training range, a long concrete building that echoed with the memory of gunfire.

"The entrance is in the maintenance shed," Porter said. "Behind the boiler."

He led her through a door marked AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY, past rows of heating equipment, to a wall that looked like concrete. He pressed his palm against a section of it, and a panel slid back, revealing a retinal scanner and a keypad.

"I need your finger for the biometric key," he said.

Ava inserted the USB drive Wells had given her. A light turned green.

Porter placed his eye against the scanner. There was a click. The wall slid open.

Beyond it was a room that didn't exist in any official floor plan.

Servers lined the walls, their cooling fans humming in a synchronized chorus. Monitors displayed scrolling data. The room was cold — colder than the maintenance shed — and the air smelled of ozone and metal.

"This is it," Porter said. "The Conservatory's brain."

Ava walked to the central console. She plugged in the portable drive she'd brought — a high-capacity SSD she'd purchased with cash that morning.

The system asked for a password.

"Porter. I need your cryptographic key."

He hesitated. Then he entered a long string of characters on the keyboard.

The screen changed. A directory structure appeared. Ava started copying.

"How long will this take?" Porter asked.

"Twenty minutes. Maybe thirty."

"We don't have thirty minutes."

"We don't have a choice."

The files began transferring. Ava watched the progress bar crawl across the screen.

It was at 12% when the door behind them opened.

Chapter 15: The Trap

Katherine Wells stood in the doorway. Behind her, four armed agents in tactical gear.

Ava's hand moved toward her weapon.

"I wouldn't," Wells said. "You're good, but you're not that good."

Porter had gone pale. "You said this was sanctioned. You said you had authorization."

"I lied." Wells stepped into the room. Her eyes were on the server console, where the file transfer was still running. "I needed you to bring her here. I needed her to attempt the access."

"Why?" Ava asked.

"Because the Architect wanted to see what you would do. How far you would go. Who you would bring with you." She smiled, a thin, cold expression. "You've been very instructive."

The progress bar hit 23%.

"The biometric key you gave me," Ava said. "It was a trap."

"It was a tracker. Everywhere you've been, every person you've talked to — we've been recording. We know about Michael Tran. We know about Thomas Rourke. We know about Marcus Pike's involvement."

The progress bar stopped. The system had frozen.

"What did you do?" Porter said.

"I killed the connection. The copy failed." Wells stepped closer. "I'm sorry, Ava. I genuinely am. You're very good at your job. But you're not the first person to find the Conservatory, and you won't be the last. We have protocols for this."

"Then why didn't you just kill me?"

"Because the Architect wants to meet you."

The agents moved forward.

Ava looked at Porter. His face was a mask of terror. He hadn't known. He'd been used, just like she had.

"Don't fight them," Wells said. "You'll only make it worse."

The agents took her arms. She didn't resist.

As they led her past Wells, the Deputy Director leaned close and whispered:

"You should have stayed with your spreadsheets, Ava. The timeline was safer."

Chapter 16: The Architect

They took her to a room she didn't recognize. No windows. White walls. A single table and two chairs. It could have been anywhere — Quantico, another facility, underground.

She sat for two hours. No one came. No one spoke through the speakers.

Then the door opened.

He was not what she expected.

The Architect was a thin man in his sixties, dressed in an expensive suit that hung loosely on his frame. His hair was white, perfectly combed. His face was smooth — unlined in a way that suggested cosmetic intervention. But his eyes were what held her attention.

He blinked exactly once every seven seconds. She counted.

"Ms. Chen," he said, taking the seat across from her. "I've been watching your career with great interest."

"And you are?"

"Call me whatever you like. Names are not important. What's important is understanding."

He set a tablet on the table between them. On the screen, her face appeared — a video of her, taken from somewhere above and behind. Her visit to the Baltimore safe house.

"You found our archive. You decoded the system. You traced the training network." He tapped the screen. "In nine days. Without authorization. Without a team. Do you know how remarkable that is?"

"I don't need flattery. Tell me why I'm here."

He smiled. It was a practiced smile — every muscle moving in precisely the right sequence. She recognized the training in it.

"You're here because I have an offer."

"I'm not interested."

"You haven't heard it yet."

"I don't need to. You run an illegal program that has corrupted every case it's touched. You've trained killers to lie to the FBI. You've manipulated the justice system. There is nothing you can offer me."

The Architect tilted his head. The gesture was familiar — she'd seen it in the confession videos. The head tilt. The micro-sequence.

"You see the pattern everywhere now, don't you? That's the problem with pattern recognition. Once you see one, you see them all. But not all patterns are what they appear to be."

He leaned forward.

"The Conservatory is not about manipulation, Ms. Chen. It's about calibration. The criminal justice system is broken — not because people lie, but because people can't tell when they're being told the truth. We've created a world where everyone is suspicious of everyone, where no confession is trusted, where no witness is believed. The Conservatory was designed to fix that."

"By teaching killers how to look deceptive?"

"By teaching everyone how to signal truth. The training protocol is neutral. It's a tool. It can be used to make someone look honest, or it can be used to make someone look guilty. The question is who controls the tool."

"And you control it."

"I did. But I'm old. I'm tired. And I've been looking for a successor for a long time."

He let the words settle.

"You want me to run the Conservatory."

"I want you to take over the program. Reform it. Use it the way you think it should be used. You have the skills. You have the vision. And you have the one quality that none of my other candidates possessed: you cannot be bought."

"I can't be bought because I'm not for sale."

"Precisely. That's why you're perfect."

Ava stared at him. The room was silent except for the hum of ventilation.

"Let me think about it," she said.

"Take all the time you need." The Architect stood. "But remember: you're not the only person I've made this offer to. And the other candidates are less principled than you."

He walked to the door.

"If you accept, you'll have access to everything. The full history. The complete training protocol. The identity of every trained subject. And the means to dismantle it, if that's what you choose."

The door closed behind him.

Ava sat alone in the white room, the ghost of his offer hanging in the air.

Chapter 17: The Cage

They moved her to a different room. Smaller. A cot, a toilet, a sink. No windows. A single light fixture she couldn't reach.

She was there for three days.

No one spoke to her. Food appeared through a slot in the door. She tried to track time by the meals — three per day, approximately eight hours apart. She slept in fragments.

On the third day, the door opened.

It wasn't Wells. It wasn't the Architect.

It was Marcus.

He looked different — tired, unshaven, his clothes rumpled. But his eyes were sharp.

"Can you walk?"

"Yes."

"Then let's go."

He pulled her to her feet. She followed him through a hallway, up a flight of stairs, into a parking garage. A black SUV sat idling, Cole Vance behind the wheel.

"How?" she asked.

"Wells left a message. Encrypted. She said you were being held in the subbasement of the Quantico admin building. She gave me the access codes."

"She's still playing both sides."

"Maybe. Or maybe she's finally picking one."

They got in the SUV. Cole drove them out of the garage, past the guard station, onto the highway.

Ava looked back at Quantico receding in the rear window.

"They have everything," she said. "The server. The database. Everything I copied."

"Not everything." Marcus reached into his jacket and pulled out the SSD she'd used. "This was still in the drive bay when I found you. I grabbed it before anyone noticed."

"The file transfer was stopped at 23%."

"23% of something is better than nothing."

Ava took the drive. It was warm from being against his body.

"We need to find someone who can read this," she said. "Someone outside the Bureau."

"I know a guy. Ex-NSA. Lives in a cabin in the Shenandoah. He owes me."

"Everyone owes you."

"That's what happens when you've been an agent for eighteen years." Marcus smiled — a tired, genuine smile. "You collect debts."

Chapter 18: The Hermit

The ex-NSA analyst's name was Frank Mercer. He lived in a converted fire lookout tower in the George Washington National Forest, surrounded by antennae and solar panels. He was sixty-four years old, bearded, and suspicious of everyone.

He was also, according to Marcus, the best data recovery specialist on the East Coast.

"Twenty-three percent," he said, examining the SSD under a magnifying lamp. "That's not much."

"It's all we have."

"I can work with it. But it'll take time. The data is encrypted, probably with a proprietary algorithm. I need to reconstruct the directory structure, then break the encryption on individual files." He looked up. "A week, maybe two."

"We don't have two weeks."

"You got a better option?"

Ava looked at Marcus. He shook his head.

"One week," she said.

"Fine. Seven days. I'll send you whatever I find." Mercer paused. "But I need you to understand something. If this data is what you think it is, my life is going to be in danger. I'm going to need protection."

"You'll have it," Marcus said.

"No, I won't. You're an FBI agent. You can't protect me from the FBI." Mercer turned to Ava. "But you can. You're off the grid now. No Bureau phone. No Bureau credit cards. No Bureau anything. You're a ghost."

"Okay."

"Don't contact me. Don't send anyone. I'll contact you when I have something. And if I don't contact you within two weeks, assume I'm dead."

He shook her hand. His grip was firm, his eyes steady.

"Good luck, Ms. Chen. You're going to need it."

Chapter 19: The Hunted

Ava and Marcus drove south through the night.

They stopped at a motel outside Charlottesville, paying cash. Two rooms, adjoining. Marcus took the side with the door to the parking lot.

"We can't stay long," he said. "They'll have tracking algorithms. License plate readers. Facial recognition. We need to switch vehicles, get off the main roads."

"We also need to find the other candidates."

"The other candidates?"

"The Architect said he'd made his offer to other people. Other potential successors. If we can find them, we can find out more about the program. Maybe find a way to shut it down."

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

"There's a woman I worked with in Detroit. Former FBI analyst named Sandra Oka. She left the Bureau in 2019, suddenly. No explanation. I always wondered why."

"Do you know where she is?"

"She lives in Ann Arbor. Teaches at the university. I can get you her address."

"Not me. Us."

"You need to understand something, Ava. I'm still an agent. If I disappear, they'll know. They'll put out a BOLO. They'll issue a warrant. I'm more useful to you inside the Bureau than outside it."

"Marcus—"

"I'm going back. I'm going to act like I never helped you. I'm going to keep my head down and try to figure out what they're planning."

"You'll be in danger."

"I've been in danger before." He stood. "I'll contact Frank Mercer for updates. I'll keep Wells off your trail as long as I can. But you need to work alone from here."

She wanted to argue. But she knew he was right.

"Be careful, Marcus."

"Always."

He left. The door clicked shut. Ava sat on the edge of the bed, looking at the cracked SSD on the nightstand, the weight of everything she'd learned pressing down on her.

She was alone now. No badge. No backup. No one to call except people who might be dead or compromised.

The Conservatory had been running for fifteen years. It had hundreds of trained subjects. It had the resources of the FBI.

And she had a partial data recovery and a list of potential successors.

It would have to be enough.

Chapter 20: Sandra Oka

Ann Arbor was a different world from the one Ava had left behind. College students on bicycles. Coffee shops with chalkboard menus. Trees lining quiet streets.

Sandra Oka lived in a craftsman bungalow with a garden full of native plants. She answered the door in gardening gloves, her gray hair pulled back in a bandana.

"Can I help you?"

"Dr. Oka? My name is Ava Chen. I used to work for the Bureau."

Oka's expression didn't change, but her hands stilled. "Used to?"

"I'm trying to stop something. A program called the Conservatory. I was told you might know about it."

Oka was silent for a long moment. Then she stepped aside.

"You'd better come in."

The house was filled with books. Shelves lined every wall, stacked two rows deep in places. Oka led her to a sunroom at the back, where a cat slept on a windowsill.

"I was approached in 2017," Oka said. "A man in a suit. No name. He told me I'd been selected for a special training program. Advanced interrogation techniques."

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him I wasn't interested. He left. A week later, my security clearance was suspended. A month after that, I was pushed out."

"Did he say anything else? Anything about the program?"

"He said I had a gift for reading people. That the program needed people like me. He said it was about truth — about making the system work better." She paused. "He used a phrase. 'The right face for the right moment.'"

Ava felt a chill. "Did he mention anyone else? Other candidates?"

"He mentioned a man in Chicago. A forensic psychologist named David Park. He said Park had been recruited but hadn't accepted."

"Do you know how to find him?"

Oka wrote an address on a slip of paper. "I don't know if he'll talk to you. He left the field a few years ago. I heard he was writing a book."

Ava took the paper. "Thank you, Dr. Oka."

"Be careful, Ms. Chen. The people who run this program — they don't give up. They're patient. They're methodical. They're everything I'm not."

On the train to Chicago, Ava read Sandra Oka's file — a copy of the personnel record Marcus had sent her before they parted ways. Oka had been a gifted analyst. Her clearance suspension was flagged as "security concern — undisclosed." The official record gave no details.

But there was a note at the bottom of the file, added three years after her departure:

SUBJECT REMAINS UNDER OBSERVATION. DO NOT REHIRE.

The Conservatory didn't let people go. It just moved them to a different part of the cage.

Arc III: The Network

Chapter 21: The Candidate

David Park lived in a high-rise apartment overlooking Lake Michigan, with floor-to-ceiling windows that caught the afternoon light and turned it into gold. He was forty-seven years old, handsome in a forgettable way, and he answered the door in a cashmere sweater that cost more than Ava's rent.

"I was wondering when someone would show up," he said, stepping aside. "Come in."

His apartment was sparse — modern furniture, white walls, a single painting that was probably worth more than Ava's car. He offered her coffee, which she declined.

"You were approached by the Conservatory," she said.

"Approached isn't the right word. I was recruited. Aggressively."

"In 2018?"

"2017. A woman called me. She didn't give her name. She said she represented an organization that was building something important and that my skills were needed."

"What did she offer you?"

"Money. Access. Resources. The chance to work on the cutting edge of forensic psychology." He smiled, a thin expression. "I turned her down."

"Why?"

"Because I could hear the coercion in her voice. Not directed at me — she was too polished for that. But she described the work in a way that suggested the subjects wouldn't be volunteers."

"You understood what they were doing."

"I suspected. I did some research. Found an old paper by a woman named Lydia Grant. Tried to contact her. Found out she was dead." He shrugged. "That was enough."

"Do you know who else they approached?"

"A few names. I kept notes." He walked to a desk and pulled out a leather-bound notebook. "I was planning to write a book about it someday. Expose the program. But I didn't have enough proof."

He handed her a page torn from the notebook. A list of names, dates, and locations.

"These are the people I know of who were recruited. Some accepted. Some didn't. The ones who accepted... I haven't been able to find any of them since."

"Thank you, Dr. Park."

"You're not going to stop them, are you?"

"I don't know."

"Then let me give you some advice." His eyes were serious. "The people who run this program — they're not evil. They're not even corrupt. They believe they're doing the right thing. That's what makes them dangerous. You can't reason with someone who thinks they're saving the world."

Ava folded the paper into her pocket. "I'll keep that in mind."

Chapter 22: The Journalist

The first name on David Park's list was a woman named Rebecca Torres, an investigative journalist who had won a Pulitzer for her coverage of wrongful convictions. She lived in Brooklyn, and she had been approached by the Conservatory in 2019.

The sun was fully up now. The city was stirring. And Ava Chen, forensic timeline analyst, sat on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial and watched the day begin.
