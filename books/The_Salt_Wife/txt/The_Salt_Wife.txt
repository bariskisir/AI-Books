Chapter 1: The Crossing

The ferry cut through a sea the color of iron. Dr. Elara Morrow stood at the rail, her coat pulled tight against the wind. The mainland had dissolved an hour ago into a haze of grey and memory.

The ferryman, Iain MacKenzie, worked the wheel with the ease of a man who had done the same thing for thirty years. He had not spoken since they left Ullapool.

"The island," he said finally.

She looked. Stiorra rose from the water like a dark tooth. Black cliffs. Green slopes. A scatter of white houses at the edge of a bay.

"The crossing can be rough," he added. Not a warning. A fact.

She had studied the maps. Seven miles long. Three miles wide. Population one hundred and twelve, according to the last census. A single road. A single pub. A single church.

"What do people do here?" she asked.

He did not answer for a long time. The engine chugged. The water slapped the hull.

"They endure," he said.

The pier came into view. A concrete slab worn by weather. A small crowd of figures waiting. They were all old. They stood still as stones.

Morrow checked her bag. Recording equipment. Notebooks. A change of clothes. She had come for the folklore. The songs. The stories. The old ways that still breathed on islands like this one.

The ferry bumped the pier. Iain threw a rope to a man onshore.

"Welcome to Stiorra," Iain said. He did not sound like he meant it.

She stepped onto the concrete. The crowd did not move to greet her. They watched. The wind carried the smell of salt and something else. Something rotting.

She turned to ask Iain a question. But he was already untying the rope. The ferry would leave again tonight. And she would stay.


Chapter 2: The Cottage

The cottage was small. Whitewashed stone. A roof of corrugated iron. It sat at the edge of the village, facing the sea.

Morrow pushed open the door. The air inside was cold and damp. A single room. A bed. A table. A fireplace. Salt film covered the windows. She could taste it on her lips.

She lit a fire with the peat and driftwood stacked by the hearth. The smoke was thick and bitter. It smelled of earth and decay.

The previous occupant had left things. A tin cup. A worn Bible. A woman's shoe, salt-stained, in the corner of a cupboard. She put it outside.

She unpacked her equipment. The recording device tested. The notebooks laid flat. She had grant money for six weeks. Time to collect, observe, and return with material sufficient for publication.

She sat at the table and looked out the window. The sea was grey. The sky was grey. The line between them barely existed.

A knock at the door.

An old woman stood in the doorway. White hair. Dark eyes. Hands that looked like they had been shaped by weather.

"Dr. Morrow," she said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"I am Morag MacLeod. I have been sent to welcome you."

Sent. Not volunteered. Morrow noted the word choice.

"Thank you. Would you like to come in?"

Morag stepped inside. She did not look at the room. She looked at Morrow. Her gaze was steady and old.

"You are here for the stories," Morag said.

"Yes. The oral traditions. The songs."

"There are songs." Morag paused. "There are stories you will not find in books."

She reached into her coat and produced a bundle wrapped in cloth. She placed it on the table.

"Salt bread," she said. "Eat it. You will need your strength."

She left without another word.

Morrow unwrapped the bread. It was dense and dark. Crystals of salt glittered on its crust. She bit into it. The salt was sharp. Almost painful.

She ate it anyway.


Chapter 3: First Night

Sleep did not come easily. The wind worked around the cottage like something searching for a way in. The iron roof creaked and groaned.

Morrow lay in the narrow bed, her coat pulled over the blanket. She had banked the fire, but the cold crept back. It came through the floor. Through the walls. Through the gaps in the window frame.

Then she heard it.

A sound beneath the wind. Low. Rhythmic. Human.

She sat up. The room was dark. The fire had died to embers.

The sound continued. A voice. Singing. The words were not English, not Gaelic as she knew it. Old words. The melody rose and fell like the tide.

She went to the window. The glass was frosted with salt. She wiped it with her sleeve.

Outside, the beach was empty. The sea was black. No moon. No lights.

The singing stopped.

She waited. Her breath fogged the glass. The cold bit through her clothes.

Nothing moved. The sea was flat. The village was silent.

She told herself it was the wind in the rocks. A natural phenomenon. The island had strange acoustics. She had read about such things.

She went back to bed.

But she did not sleep.

In the morning, she found a line of salt on the windowsill. A straight line. As if drawn by a finger.

She could not explain it. The salt on the windows had been undisturbed. She would have noticed.

She wiped it away and made tea.

She did not mention it when Morag came at noon.


Chapter 4: Morag

Morag arrived at noon with a bowl of fish stew. She set it on the table without speaking and sat in the cottage's only chair.

Morrow ate. The stew was good. Rich. The fish tasted of the deep sea.

"You want the stories," Morag said. "I will tell you one."

Morrow reached for her recorder.

"No." Morag raised a hand. "Listen first. Record later."

Morrow put the recorder down.

"There was a girl," Morag said. "Mhairi. The year was 1743. She was seventeen years old. She walked into the water and did not come back."

"A drowning," Morrow said.

"No." Morag's eyes were flat. "A wedding."

Morrow kept her face neutral. She had heard similar stories. Every coastal community had its version. The bride of the sea. The mermaid's bargain. Folklore was full of such things.

"She married the sea," Morag continued. "He came to her in dreams. He brought her gifts. Shells. Fish. A ring made of kelp. She accepted him."

"And then she walked into the water."

"She walked into the water. And the sea took her. And the village prospered."

Morag stood. She smoothed her apron.

"That is the story," she said.

"What happened to her family?"

"They prospered too." Morag paused at the door. "The sea pays his debts. He always pays."

She left.

Morrow sat for a long time. The stew cooled. The wind pressed against the windows.

She wrote in her notebook: "First recorded instance of sea-bride legend. Informant: Morag MacLeod. Note: informant treated narrative as literal truth."

She underlined literal truth.

Then she crossed out the underlining.


Chapter 5: The Story

Morrow found Morag the next day at the village well. The old woman was drawing water. Her hands moved with practiced ease.

"I would like to hear more," Morrow said. "About Mhairi."

Morag did not look up. "There is not much more. She was chosen. She went. That is the shape of it."

"Chosen by what?"

Morag straightened. She looked at the sea. The water was calm today. Grey silk.

"The Sea Husband," she said. "That is what we call him. He comes for a bride every sixty years."

"Every sixty years."

"Yes."

Morrow calculated. "So there have been others. After Mhairi."

"Aye."

"Can I speak with their families?"

Morag laughed. It was not a warm sound. "Their families are dead. The brides are gone. There is only the remembering."

"But you remember."

"I remember." Morag picked up her bucket. "I remember because I was chosen too."

Morrow's breath caught. "You were—"

"Chosen. Aye. In 1963." Morag held out her hands. The palms were crossed with white scars. "I refused. The sea did not like my blood. He let me go."

"Let you go."

"He threw me back. Like a fish too small to keep." She smiled, and there was nothing happy in it. "I have been here ever since. Watching. Waiting for the next one."

"The next one is due."

"Soon." Morag's voice dropped. "You should finish your collecting and leave before midsummer."

"Why midsummer?"

But Morag was already walking away. The bucket swung at her side. Water splashed onto the path.

Morrow watched her go. The wind picked up. The sea darkened.

She told herself it was just a story.


Chapter 6: The Shore

Morrow walked to the strand in the afternoon. The beach was black sand mixed with crushed shell. White salt crusted the rocks at the high tide line.

The sea breathed. In and out. The sound was everywhere.

She walked along the water's edge. The cold seeped through her boots. The salt air stung her eyes.

She found the dress on a flat rock above the tide line.

It was white linen. Simple. Hand-sewn. Barnacles encrusted the fabric. Tiny white shells glued to the cloth as if the dress had been underwater for months.

But the fabric beneath the barnacles was dry. The dress had been placed here recently.

She touched it. The linen was stiff with salt.

She looked up the beach. The village was visible in the distance. White houses against the green slope. No one was watching. Or if they were, she could not see them.

She considered taking the dress. Evidence. An artifact. But something stopped her.

This was not hers to take.

She left it on the rock.

That night, she dreamed of a woman in white walking into grey water. The woman did not look back.

Morrow woke with her hands wet. She had been crying in her sleep. Her tears tasted like the sea.

She made tea. The salt had returned to the windowsill. Not a line this time. A handprint. Small. As if a child had pressed their palm to the glass.

She did not wipe it away.

She stared at it until dawn.


Chapter 7: Priest's Warning

Father Thomas found her outside the village shop. He was a thick man in a black coat. His face was weathered like the island itself.

"Dr. Morrow," he said. "A word."

They walked together along the road. The sea was on one side. The hills on the other.

"You have met Morag," he said.

"Yes."

"She tells stories."

"I am a folklorist. Stories are my work."

He stopped. Turned to face her. His eyes were pale blue. Water-colored.

"There are stories and there are truths," he said. "On this island, the line between them is thin."

"I can handle thin lines."

"Can you?" He looked past her, at the sea. "The old women here... they keep things alive that should have died. Superstitions. Pagan nonsense."

"Is that what the salt wife legend is? Nonsense?"

He flinched. Just slightly. She saw it.

"You know about it," she said.

"Everyone on Stiorra knows about it." He resumed walking. "The sea bride. The sixty-year bargain. The girl who walks into the waves."

"You do not believe it."

"I believe in God," he said. "And I believe the devil has many masks."

They reached the church. It was small. Stone. A simple cross above the door. But beside the cross, set into the wall, was a clock face. Not telling time. Showing the tide.

"Why is there a tide clock on a church?" Morrow asked.

Father Thomas touched it. "Because the sea is the only god the old people trust. I meet them where they are."

He opened the door. Inside, the church was dark and cold. The altar was bare. No crucifix.

"The cross is outside," she said.

"The cross is everywhere." He spoke carefully. "But here, I keep it where the sea cannot wash it away."

He turned to her. "Leave before midsummer, Dr. Morrow. There is nothing for you here but sorrow."

He went inside. The door closed behind him.

Morrow stood alone in the churchyard. The tide clock ticked.


Chapter 8: The Tapestry

The village hall was a long stone building with a corrugated roof. Inside, it smelled of damp wool and old tea.

The tapestry hung on the far wall. Seven feet wide. Four feet high. Woven in wool dyed in colors of sea and shore.

Morrow studied it.

Seven panels. Each showed a procession of women walking toward the sea. The first panel was faded, the colors thin with age. The most recent was bright, the wool still rich.

In each panel, one woman led the procession. She wore white. The others wore dark. The leading woman's face was featureless. A blank oval of cream wool.

Seven women. Seven processions. Seven walks into the water.

She counted the years in her head. 1743. 1793. 1843. 1893. 1943. 1993.

Sixty years between each.

But the tapestry had seven panels. The last panel showed a procession in progress. The women were mid-stride. The leading woman was at the water's edge.

The scene was incomplete. The weaver had stopped before the woman entered the sea.

The seventh panel was waiting.

Morrow stepped closer. The wool in the seventh panel was newer. The dye still sharp. This panel had been woven recently.

She found the label stitched into the corner. Tiny letters in brown thread.

"2026."

This year. This midsummer.

Someone was already weaving the next panel before the event had happened.

She looked for a name. No weaver's mark. No signature.

"Beautiful, isn't it?"

She turned. An old woman stood in the doorway. Not Morag. Someone else. Smaller. Bent.

"Who made this?" Morrow asked.

"The island," the woman said. "We all add our stitches."

"When was the last panel started?"

The woman smiled. Or bared her teeth. It was hard to tell.

"When the last bride was taken. It is always being woven. Always waiting."

She left.

Morrow looked back at the tapestry. The faceless bride in white. The waiting sea.

She took a photograph. The camera flash made the wool glow.

The seventh panel looked almost wet.


Chapter 9: Catriona

The girl was fourteen. Maybe fifteen. She stood at the edge of the road as Morrow walked past. Her dress was white linen. The same fabric as the dress on the shore.

Morrow stopped.

"Hello," she said.

The girl looked at her. Her eyes were grey. Sea-colored.

"You are the folklorist," the girl said.

"I am. My name is Elara."

"I know." The girl's voice was flat. "You came to write down our stories."

"I did. Would you like to tell me one?"

The girl shook her head. "I do not have stories. I have a dress."

Morrow felt cold. "The dress on the beach. Is it yours?"

The girl nodded. "My mother made it. She cried the whole time. The salt from her tears got into the stitches."

"Why did she make it if it made her cry?"

The girl looked at the sea. "Because that is what mothers do. They make the dress. They cry. They let their daughters go."

A woman appeared in the doorway of the nearest house. She called out. The girl's name.

"Catriona. Come inside."

The girl went. She did not look back.

The woman watched Morrow with hard eyes. Then she closed the door.

Morrow stood on the road. The wind blew. The sea growled.

She walked back to her cottage and wrote everything down. When she looked up, it was dark. She had been writing for hours. Her hand ached.

She looked at the window. The handprint was gone. Fresh salt had covered it.

She touched the glass. Her fingertip came away wet.

She did not lick it. She knew what it would taste like.


Chapter 10: Archives

The parish archives were kept in a room behind the church. Father Thomas unlocked the door and left her alone with the records.

The books were leather-bound. Salt-warped. The pages brittle.

She started with the earliest. Baptisms. Marriages. Deaths.

1743. A death record for Mhairi MacLeod. Cause: "drowned."

1793. A death record for Iona MacNeil. Age sixteen. Cause: "drowned."

1843. Kirsty MacAulay. Age nineteen. "Drowned."

1893. Flora MacKenzie. Age seventeen. "Drowned."

1943. Sarah MacLeod. Age eighteen. "Drowned."

1993. Not one but two records. A death: Margaret MacLeod. Age sixteen. "Drowned." And a marriage: Margaret MacLeod to... the space was blank. The name of the groom had been scratched out.

Morrow traced the scratching with her finger. Someone had used a knife. The paper was thin where the blade had cut.

She turned the page. There was no record for 2026. Not yet.

She checked the dates of birth. Every bride had been born on Stiorra. Every bride had turned sixteen in the year of her drowning.

She checked the dates against the island's population records. There was one girl on the island who would turn sixteen this year.

Catriona MacLeod.

She closed the book. Her hands were shaking.

She stepped out of the archives. Father Thomas was waiting.

"You found what you were looking for?" he asked.

"I found a pattern."

"Yes." He nodded slowly. "Patterns are the devil's favorite language."

"The records have been altered. The groom's name was removed."

"Maybe it was." He looked at the sea. "Or maybe there was never a name to write."

"There must have been."

"Why?" He turned to her. "If the groom is not a man, what name would you write?"


Chapter 11: The Cairn

Morag took her to the cairn. It stood on the headland above the main beach. A pile of stones. Each one was smooth, sea-worn, chosen.

Morag touched the top stone. "This one is Mhairi. She is the oldest."

Morrow counted. The cairn was wider than it was tall. Dozens of stones.

"Each stone is a bride?" she asked.

"Each stone is a woman who went to the sea." Morag knelt. Her fingers found the stones without looking. "Not all of them were brides. Some were given. Some were taken. The sea is not particular about the form of the gift."

"Given?"

"Sacrifice." Morag said the word plainly. "When the bride price is not paid, the sea collects in other ways. A boat. A child. A husband who falls from the cliffs."

"And the brides are the price."

"The brides are the bargain. One woman every sixty years. The island pays and the island lives."

Morrow looked at the stones. There were more than seven. Many more.

"I thought it was one every sixty years."

"It is." Morag's voice was patient. "But the sea accepts other payments too. We have not always been able to give a bride."

"When?"

"1943." Morag touched a stone near the center. "There was no bride that year. The war had taken all our young women. They were in Glasgow, in the factories. There was no one to choose."

"So the sea took something else."

"The fishing fleet. Three boats. Eighteen men." She touched a cluster of smaller stones. "These are theirs."

Morrow stared at the stones. Eighteen small stones. Worn smooth.

"The sea always collects," Morag said. "One way or another."

She stood. Her knees cracked. She looked old. Older than she had looked before.

"You should understand this, Dr. Morrow. You are not a visitor here. You are a witness. And witnesses are not released."


Chapter 12: Village Silence

The village had changed. Not visibly. The houses were the same. The faces were the same. But something in the air had shifted.

Morrow walked to the shop. Two women stood outside. They stopped talking when she approached. One of them nodded. The other looked at the ground.

Inside the shop, the man behind the counter took her money without meeting her eyes.

"Lovely weather," she said.

He grunted.

She bought milk and bread. The milk was fresh. The bread was salt bread. Dense and dark.

"Morag's been telling you things," the man said. Not a question.

"She has been helpful."

"Aye." He wiped the counter. "She tells things. Whether they need telling or not."

"Should she not have told me?"

He looked at her then. Direct. Unblinking.

"Some things are not meant to leave the island. They live here because they cannot live anywhere else. You take them away in your notebooks, and they die."

"Or they live somewhere else."

"Or they follow you home."

She paid and left. The bread was heavy in her bag.

As she walked back, she saw Catriona at the window of her house. The girl pressed her palm to the glass. She held it there.

Morrow raised her hand. She did not know why.

The girl did not wave back. She just kept her palm against the glass.

When Morrow reached her cottage, she found a stone on the doorstep. Smooth. Sea-worn. Warm, as if it had been held.

She took it inside.

She did not tell anyone.


Chapter 13: Dreams

The dreams came every night now.

In the dreams, she stood on the beach at midnight. The water was black. The sky was black. There was no horizon.

A voice called her name. Not from the water. From beneath it.

Elara.

She walked forward. The water reached her ankles. It was warm. Warmer than air.

Elara.

She looked down. Something white moved below the surface. A hand. Open. Reaching up.

She woke with her heart pounding. The sheets were damp. The fire had died.

She lit a candle. The flame shook in the draft.

The handprint was back on the window. Larger now. Adult-sized. Five clear fingers.

She put her hand against it from the inside. The print was larger than her hand.

She pulled back.

In the morning, the print was gone. Melted salt had run down the glass in streaks.

She did not sleep the next night. She sat by the fire and read. The recorded tales of the Hebrides. Academic texts. Dry. Safe.

But the wind sang outside. And beneath it, always beneath it, she heard the voice.

Elara.

She closed the book.

The candle went out.

She sat in the dark and listened.

The sea was patient. It could wait.


Chapter 14: The Offering

She found the figure at dawn.

She had not slept. She had walked to the beach when the sky began to lighten. The grey of night giving way to the grey of morning.

The figure sat on a rock at the tide line. Small. Clay. A woman.

She picked it up. It was crudely made. Pinched into shape by fingers. The head was a smooth oval. The body was a cylinder wrapped in the suggestion of a dress.

It was half-dissolved. The salt water had softened one side. The face was gone. The left arm had melted into the chest.

She turned it over. On the base, someone had scratched letters.

E.L.A.R.A.

She almost dropped it.

She looked around. The beach was empty. The village was quiet. No one was watching.

She put the figure in her coat pocket. She did not tell anyone.

Back in the cottage, she set it on the table. It dripped salt water onto the wood.

She touched the letters. They had been carved with something sharp. A nail. A knife. A fingernail.

She took a photograph. Then another.

She did not know what to do with it. Evidence of a prank. A threat. An invitation.

She wrapped it in cloth and put it in her drawer.

Then she sat at the table and tried to think.

Her name. On a clay figure. On a beach where the sea left gifts for brides.

She was not a bride. She was a researcher. She was an observer.

She repeated this to herself.

The figure wept salt water into the cloth.

She did not check the drawer again.


Chapter 15: Iain's Truth

The ferry returned on Thursday. Morrow walked to the pier to meet it. She needed supplies. She needed contact with the mainland. She needed to hear a voice that did not belong to this island.

Iain tied off the boat. He looked at her.

"You are still here," he said.

"I have work to do."

"Aye." He lit a cigarette. "That is what they all say."

"Who?"

"The ones who stay. They always have work to do. Research. Writing. Recording." He exhaled smoke. "They never leave."

"I am leaving in six weeks."

He laughed. Short. Bitter.

"You are not," he said. "You have the mark."

She touched her wrist. The salt line was still there. Faint. But there.

"Anyone can get salt on their skin," she said.

"This is not salt on the skin." He tapped his own wrist. "This is salt in the skin. Under it. In the blood."

She looked at her wrist. The line was pale. Like a scar. Like a scar that had always been there.

"You should not have come," Iain said. "The sea noticed you."

"I do not believe in—"

"The sea does not care what you believe." He dropped his cigarette and crushed it under his boot. "You are here. You are marked. That is all that matters."

He walked away.

Morrow stood on the pier. The boat rocked. The water slapped the concrete.

She looked at her wrist. The line was not fading.

She rubbed it. It did not hurt. It did not change.

She considered taking the boat back. Just getting on and letting it carry her away.

But her equipment was in the cottage. Her notebooks. Her research. She could not abandon her work.

She told herself that was the reason.

She walked back to the cottage.

The sea followed her. Not with water. With sound. The low pulse of waves. A heartbeat she could feel in her teeth.


Chapter 16: Morag's Confession

Morrow found Morag in her house. The old woman's home was smaller than the cottage. Darker. The windows were filmed with salt so thick the light came through grey.

Morag sat at her table. A cup of tea steamed in front of her.

"Sit," she said.

Morrow sat.

"I was chosen in 1963. I was fifteen years old." Morag's voice was flat. Reciting. "The sea came to me in dreams. He showed me his house. Coral walls. Light like green glass. He said I would want for nothing."

"You refused him."

"I refused him." Morag held out her hands. The scars were pale against her dark skin. "I took a fish knife and I cut my palms. I bled into the water. The sea cannot take a bride who bleeds of her own will. It is the one rule."

"And he let you go."

"He let me go. But he did not forget." She picked up her cup. "Every night since, I dream of his house. I see the coral. I hear his voice. He tells me I owe him still."

"How do you live with that?"

"I live." Morag drank her tea. "That is all. I live and I remember and I watch the girls grow up and I know which one he will choose."

"Catriona."

"Catriona." Morag set the cup down. "She was marked from birth. A caul over her face when she was born. Sea-changed."

"Is there no way to stop it?"

"Stop it?" Morag looked at her. "Why would we stop it? The sea keeps us alive. The fish. The warmth of the gulf stream. The rain that waters our crops. He gives and we give back."

"A girl's life."

"A girl's life for everyone else's. That is the bargain. That has always been the bargain."

Morrow sat in the dark room. The salt filmed the windows. The tea steamed.

She had no answer.


Chapter 17: The Marks

Morag showed her the crystals.

They grew from the scars on her palms. Tiny. Transparent. Salt crystals. They caught the light and threw it back in splinters.

"They form when the moon is full," Morag said. "He is reminding me."

Morrow wanted to touch them. She held back.

"Does it hurt?"

"Not anymore. At first, yes. Like needles under the skin. Now it is just a feeling. Like cold. Like something waiting."

"How do you remove them?"

"I do not." Morag closed her hands. "They are part of me now. Part of the debt."

They sat in silence. The cottage was cold. The fire had burned low.

"Show me yours," Morag said.

"I do not have—"

"The line on your wrist. Show me."

Morrow hesitated. Then she rolled up her sleeve.

The line was darker today. It ran across her wrist like a bracelet. Thin. Perfect.

"It glows," Morrow whispered.

It was true. In the dim light, the line gave off a faint luminescence. Pale blue.

"Green, for me," Morag said. "Blue, for you. He is still deciding."

"Deciding what?"

"Your color." Morag reached out and touched Morrow's wrist. Her fingers were cold. Dry. "Blue means bride. Green means rejected. You will know when it settles."

"It cannot settle. I am not staying."

"You are not leaving."

"I can leave. The ferry—"

"The ferry will not take you." Morag let go. "Not now. Not until after."

"After what?"

"Midsummer."

Morag stood. She walked to the door.

"Wear long sleeves," she said. "Do not let the village see. Some of them would rather give a stranger than their own."

She left.

Morrow sat alone. She stared at the blue line on her wrist. It pulsed. Faint. Steady.

Like a heartbeat.

Like the tide.


Chapter 18: The Pattern

Morrow spent the day mapping.

She spread her notes across the table. The dates. The names. The ages. The circumstances.

1743 — Mhairi MacLeod — 17 — June 21
1793 — Iona MacNeil — 16 — June 21
1843 — Kirsty MacAulay — 19 — June 21
1893 — Flora MacKenzie — 17 — June 21
1943 — No bride — Fishing fleet lost — June 21
1993 — Margaret MacLeod — 16 — June 21

Every sixty years. Midsummer. The summer solstice.

She checked the calendar. Midsummer was twelve days away.

She checked the names. MacLeod appeared twice. The family name ran through the pattern like a thread.

She found the genealogy records. Morag's full name was Morag MacLeod. Her mother had been Margaret MacLeod. The bride of 1993.

Morag's daughter.

Morrow sat back.

Morag had been chosen in 1963. She had refused. And then, thirty years later, her own daughter had been taken.

The debt collected.

She thought of Catriona MacLeod. Another MacLeod. Another generation.

She looked at the dates again. 1993 to 2026. Thirty-three years. Not sixty.

The pattern had shifted. The interval had shortened.

She went to find Morag.

"Why is it earlier?" she asked.

Morag was in her garden. Pulling weeds. She did not look up.

"What do you mean?"

"The intervals. They were sixty years. Every sixty years exactly. But 1993 to 2026 is only thirty-three years."

Morag stopped pulling. She straightened slowly.

"You see more than you should," she said.

"Why is it shorter?"

"Because the debt is compounding." She threw a weed onto the pile. "The sea waits patiently. But he also remembers. Each refusal adds to the interest."

"He refused you."

"I refused him. And then my daughter paid. And now..." She looked at the horizon. "Now the debt is growing faster than we can pay."

"What happens if you do not pay?"

"The island dies." She said it simply. "The fish leave. The water turns sour. The land stops bearing. We have seen it before. In the old stories."

"Is that what the tapestry is for? To track the debt?"

"The tapestry is to remind us." Morag turned back to her weeds. "We are not the first island. We will not be the last. The sea has many brides."


Chapter 19: Catriona's Fear

Morrow found Catriona on the beach. The girl sat on a rock at the water's edge. Her bare feet were in the water.

Morrow sat beside her.

"You should not be here," Catriona said.

"Neither should you."

The girl almost smiled. "I have to be here. He calls me."

"Who calls you?"

"The sea." She looked at the water. "He sings to me at night. I hear him in my dreams. He shows me things."

"What things?"

"His house. The place where I will live." Her voice was small. "It is beautiful. All green light and white sand. He says I will never be cold again."

"You do not have to go."

"No." Catriona pulled her feet out of the water. They were red with cold. "I do not have to. But the island does. And my mother. And my sisters. If I do not go, the sea will take something else."

"Maybe that is not true. Maybe it is just a story."

"It is not a story." Catriona looked at her. Her eyes were old. "I know. I have seen his face. In the water. In my dreams. I know what he wants."

She stood.

"I am scared," she said. "But I am more scared of what happens if I do not go."

She walked away. Her footprints filled with water behind her.

Morrow sat on the rock. The sea licked at her boots.

She did not follow.

She sat until the tide came in and the rock was surrounded by water. Then she waded back to shore.

Her boots were wet. Her socks were soaked.

She did not care.


Chapter 20: The Bride Clothes

The dress was gone from the rock. Catriona wore it now.

Morrow saw her through the window of her house. The girl stood in the middle of the room. Her mother circled her, adjusting the fabric. The white linen hung to her ankles. The sleeves were long. The neck was high.

It was a wedding dress.

Catriona's mother saw Morrow watching. She pulled the curtain closed.

Morrow walked away. But the image stayed with her. The girl in white. The mother's hands on the fabric. The silence of it.

She went to the shop. The man behind the counter was stacking tins.

"The bride clothes," she said. "What is the tradition?"

He did not look at her. "The dress is sewn by the mother. Worn for the first time on the day of the wedding. The bride walks into the water in it."

"And then what?"

"And then the sea takes her. And the dress. And everything."

"The dress is not recovered?"

"Sometimes." He set a tin on the shelf. "Sometimes the sea returns it. As a sign. To show he remembers."

"The dress on the beach. That was Catriona's."

"Aye. He returned it. To mark her."

"Mark her?"

"As his." The man finally looked at her. "The dress on the shore means the bride is claimed. The ceremony is just the collection."

Morrow thought of the barnacles. The salt crust. The dress had been returned to show ownership.

Like a flag on a map.

"Can she refuse after the dress is returned?"

He did not answer. He went back to stacking tins.

Morrow bought nothing. She left the shop.

The salt line on her wrist was darker. She pulled her sleeve down.


Chapter 21: The Gifts

The gifts appeared on Catriona's windowsill.

Morrow saw them when she walked past the house. She stopped. Counted.

A spiral shell. Perfectly formed. Unbroken.

A seal's tooth. White. Polished.

A piece of sea glass. Blue. Smooth as silk.

The girl's mother came out. She did not invite Morrow inside.

"For Catriona," Morrow said.

"Aye." The mother's face was hard. "They appear each morning. The sea sends them."

"What do they mean?"

"They mean he is courting her." The mother's voice cracked. "Shells. Gifts. He is showing her what she will have."

"And the ring?"

The mother's eyes went sharp. "What ring?"

But Morrow had seen it. On the windowsill, among the shells. A ring. Braided kelp. Green-black. Woven into a circle.

"A kelp ring," Morrow said.

The mother crossed herself. The gesture was quick. Automatic.

"That is not a gift," she said. "That is the claiming."

She went inside. The door closed.

Morrow stood at the window. The kelp ring lay on the sill. It moved slightly. As if something had touched it.

She walked on.

That night, she dreamed of a hand reaching through green water. Holding a ring made of seaweed. Offering it.

In the dream, she reached for it.

She woke with her hand extended. Reaching for nothing.

The salt line on her wrist burned.

She held it under cold water until the burning stopped.


Chapter 22: Morrow's Research

Morrow found the poems in an old book. Gaelic. Handwritten. The ink was brown with age.

She translated them slowly. Her Gaelic was not fluent. But she knew enough.

The poems were about the Sea Husband. He was called Fear na Mara. The Man of the Sea.

He lived in a house of coral. His hair was kelp. His eyes were empty shells. His voice was the sound of waves in a cave.

He chose his bride by tasting her tears.

She read the line again.

"He tastes her tears and knows her. He tastes her tears and claims her."

She remembered Catriona's tears. The girl had cried on the beach. Her tears had run into the water.

She looked at her own hands. She had cried in her sleep. Her tears had wet the pillow. Not the sea. But the salt line on her wrist had appeared the morning after.

She went to find Morag.

"The poems say the sea tastes tears. Is that how he chooses?"

Morag was mending a net. Her fingers moved automatically.

"Aye. The sea tastes the grief of the island. The tears of the mothers. The tears of the girls. He tastes them all. He chooses the one that tastes most like home."

"Home?"

"The sea." Morag pulled a thread tight. "He is looking for a bride who belongs to him already. A girl whose tears taste of salt water. Who has the sea in her blood."

"And he finds her."

"He always finds her." She bit the thread. "He found Catriona. He found you."

"I am not—"

"You cried on this island. Your tears touched the ground. The earth here is full of salt. He tasted you through it."

Morrow felt cold.

"That is not how science works," she said.

"Science." Morag laughed. "You think the sea cares about science? The sea is older than science. Older than God. Older than the land itself."

She held up the net. Repaired.

"The sea will always get what it wants, Dr. Morrow. That is the only truth that matters."


Chapter 23: The Choosing

Morag explained the choosing.

It happened over weeks. The sea sent signs. A gift on the windowsill. A dream. A touch in the water.

The girl knew. Before anyone told her, she knew.

"She will resist at first," Morag said. "They always do. They cry. They pray. They hide."

"And then?"

"And then he comes to them in the dreams. He shows them his house. He shows them peace. He shows them that the water is not cold. That it is warm. That it is home."

"They accept."

"They accept." Morag nodded. "Because the alternative is to watch the island die. And no girl on Stiorra can live with that."

Catriona had accepted. Morrow could see it in the girl's eyes. The fear had faded. Something else had taken its place. Resignation. Or peace. Or both.

"I want to talk to her again," Morrow said.

"She will not tell you anything new. She is already gone. Her body is here. Her mind is with him."

"Then I will talk to her body."

Morag shrugged. "You will do what you will do."

Morrow found Catriona at the well. The girl was drawing water. Her movements were slow. Deliberate.

"Catriona."

The girl looked up. Her eyes were distant.

"I have accepted him," she said. Before Morrow could ask. "I have said yes."

"You do not have to."

"I know." Catriona smiled. It was a sad smile. "But I want to."

"You want to die?"

"I want to live." She touched her chest. "In his house. Under the water. I want to live where I will never be hungry or cold or afraid."

"That is not living. That is drowning."

Catriona shook her head. "That is what you think. You do not understand."

She picked up her bucket and walked away.

Morrow stood at the well. The water inside was dark. She looked down at her own reflection. Pale. Blurred.

Behind her reflection, something moved in the water.

She stepped back.

The water was still.

She walked away quickly. Her heart pounded.

She did not look back.


Chapter 24: Storm

The storm came without warning.

One moment the sky was grey. The next it was black. The wind rose from nothing. The sea turned white.

Morrow watched from her window. The waves were enormous. They crashed against the cliffs. The spray reached her cottage.

The power went out at midday. The phone lines followed. The radio produced only static.

She was alone.

The wind screamed. The cottage shook. The iron roof rattled like it might tear away.

She lit candles. They flickered. Shadows moved across the walls.

She thought of Catriona. Of Morag. Of the village.

She thought of the sea.

It was not a storm. It was a message.

She knew that was irrational. She knew storms happened. She knew islands in the Hebrides were subject to extreme weather.

But she knew.

The sea was telling her something.

She sat by the fire and waited. There was nothing else to do.

The storm lasted three days.

When it ended, the ferry was gone. Iain had taken it to the mainland for shelter and had not returned.

The island was cut off.

Morrow walked to the beach. The strand was covered in debris. Kelp. Shells. Plastic. A dead seal.

And a dress.

White linen. Barnacle-encrusted. Spread on a rock.

Catriona's dress.

Or another one like it.

She touched it. The fabric was wet. The barnacles were sharp.

She left it there.

She walked back to the village. The houses were shuttered. The streets were empty.

The storm had not been weather.

The storm had been a door closing.


Chapter 25: The Phone Lines

The phone lines remained dead.

Morrow checked her mobile. No signal. She climbed the hill behind the village. The highest point on the island. She held the phone above her head.

Nothing.

She tried the landline in the cottage. Dead.

She went to the shop. The phone there was dead too.

"No ferry, no phone, no radio," she said to the shopkeeper.

"Aye."

"The isolation. Does this happen often?"

He looked at her. "It happens when the sea wants it to happen."

"When was the last time?"

He thought. "1943. The summer of the lost boats. The phones went dead. The ferry could not land. We were alone for three weeks."

"Three weeks."

"Aye." He wiped the counter. "Long enough."

"Long enough for what?"

He did not answer.

She left the shop. The village was quiet. The sea was calm now. Flat. Grey. Waiting.

She walked to the church. Father Thomas was inside. Lighting candles.

"The phones are dead," she said.

"I know."

"The ferry is gone."

"I know."

"Are we trapped?"

He looked at her. "We are on an island, Dr. Morrow. We have always been trapped."

He lit another candle.

"When the sea wants to keep people here, it keeps them. You can fight it or you can accept it."

"I do not accept it."

He smiled. It was not a happy smile.

"You are still here. That is a form of acceptance."

She left the church. The wind was picking up again.

She went to her cottage. She packed her bag. Notebooks. Recorder. Clothes.

She walked to the pier. The sea was empty. No boats. No ferries.

She stood on the concrete and watched the horizon.

Nothing came.

She waited until dark.

Nothing came.


Chapter 26: Father Thomas's Ritual

Father Thomas performed the ritual at midnight. Morrow heard about it from Morag.

"He goes to the beach," Morag said. "He reads from his book. He throws holy water at the waves."

"Does it work?"

"It makes him feel better." Morag's voice was dry. "The sea does not care about holy water."

Morrow went to watch. She stood at the edge of the beach, hidden in the shadow of a rock.

Father Thomas walked to the water's edge. He wore his black coat. He carried a book. A Bible, she assumed.

He read. The words were Latin. She caught fragments. Exorcizo te. I command you.

He threw water at the sea. It glittered in the moonlight.

The sea did nothing. The waves lapped. The tide rose.

He read again. His voice was steady. He did not falter.

He threw more water.

The sea rose higher. A wave came further up the beach than the others. It soaked his shoes.

He stepped back. Read louder.

Another wave. Higher. It reached his knees.

He kept reading.

The sea kept rising.

She wanted to call out to him. To tell him to stop. But she could not move.

The water reached his waist. He stumbled. Dropped the book.

Morrow ran forward.

She grabbed his arm. He was cold. Shaking.

"Father. Come away."

He looked at her. His eyes were wild.

"I cannot stop it," he said. "I have tried for forty years. I cannot stop it."

She pulled him up the beach. He came. He was heavy. His coat was soaked.

They reached the dry sand. The sea stopped advancing. It withdrew. Slowly. Reluctantly.

Father Thomas fell to his knees.

"It is older than my God," he whispered. "Older than all of it."

Morrow helped him stand.

She walked him back to the church.

She did not sleep that night.

She listened to the sea.

The sea was quiet.

It had made its point.


Chapter 27: The Beach at Night

She could not stay inside.

The walls pressed in. The salt filmed everything. She needed air. She needed to see.

She walked to the beach at midnight. The moon was full. The water was silver.

She stood at the tide line. The waves reached for her feet. She stepped back.

The waves reached further.

She stepped back again.

The waves followed.

She stopped. Let them come. The water touched her boots. Cold. She felt it through the leather.

She looked out at the sea.

There was light beneath the surface.

Green. Faint. Moving.

She watched it. It moved in circles. Pacing. Like something in a room.

The light grew brighter. Closer.

The water was shallow here. She could see the bottom. White sand. Dark rocks.

The light came from something. A shape. A body.

She could not make it out. It was too deep. Too dark. The light was just a glow. A presence.

It stopped moving.

It was looking at her.

She could feel it. Something beneath the water. Watching. Waiting.

She took a step back.

The light moved toward her.

She turned and walked. Fast. Not running. She would not run.

The water splashed behind her. A wave. Higher than the rest.

She reached the dry sand and kept walking.

Behind her, the sea sighed. A sound like disappointment.

She did not look back.

She went to her cottage and locked the door.

The lock was rusted. It barely held.

She pushed a chair against it.

She sat in the dark and listened.

The sea was patient.

It could wait.


Chapter 28: Catriona's Poem

Catriona gave her the poem on a scrap of paper. She pressed it into Morrow's hand outside the shop.

"For your collection," she said. And walked away.

Morrow unfolded it. The paper was damp. The ink was smudged.

It read:

*My husband lives in a house of water*
*His voice is the tide*
*His hands are the waves*
*He dresses me in white*
*He feeds me salt*
*I am becoming his*

*My mother cries*
*But I am not lost*
*I am found*
*Under the green light*
*In the coral room*
*I am found*

Morrow read it twice. Three times.

The handwriting was young. Unformed. The lines were crooked.

But the words were old. Older than the girl who wrote them.

Morrow folded the paper carefully. She put it in her notebook.

She went to find Catriona's mother.

"What do the poems mean?" she asked.

The mother was at the stove. Cooking. She did not turn.

"They mean she is ready."

"She wrote about a coral room."

"She has seen his house." The mother's voice was steady. Too steady. "In her dreams. He shows it to her. So she will not be afraid."

"Is she afraid?"

"She was." The mother turned. Her eyes were red. "Now she is not."

"Can I see her?"

"She is asleep. The dreams exhaust her."

Morrow nodded. She understood.

She walked back to her cottage. The poem burned in her pocket.

She copied it into her notebook. Word for word.

Then she burned the original. In the fire.

She did not know why.

But it felt right.

The paper curled. Blackened. The words disappeared.

The smoke smelled of salt.


Chapter 29: The Mark

It was darker now. The line on her wrist had changed from pale blue to deep blue. Almost black.

Morag examined it in the light.

"You are being claimed," she said.

"I am not a bride."

"You are marked as one."

Morrow looked at her wrist. The line was a bracelet. Perfect. Unbroken.

"The sea does not mark people by accident," Morag said. "He has chosen you."

"He cannot choose me. I am a folklorist. I am a visitor."

"You are a woman of childbearing age on an island that owes a debt. You are a bride."

Morag let go of her wrist.

"The other brides were island-born. But the sea is expanding his reach. He is looking further."

"Because the debt is growing."

"Because the island is running out of daughters." Morag's voice was flat. "We are a small community. The young leave. The old stay. There will not always be a sixteen-year-old girl to give."

"So he takes visitors."

"He takes what is offered." Morag looked at her. "And you have been offered."

"By whom?"

"By yourself." Morag touched her own wrist. "You came here. You listened. You cried. You dreamed of him. You offered yourself without knowing."

"I did not offer—"

"You stayed." Morag's voice was gentle. "That is the only offering the sea requires."

Morrow sat down. Her legs had gone weak.

"I can leave," she said. "When the ferry returns."

"When the ferry returns." Morag nodded. "But will it? And will you?"

She left.

Morrow stared at her wrist. The blue line. The mark.

She thought of the ferry. Of Iain's face when he said she would not leave.

She thought of the storm. The dead phones.

She thought of the dress on the beach.

She touched the mark.

It was warm.

She could feel it pulsing.

Like a heartbeat.

Like the tide.


Chapter 30: Morag's History

Morag told her everything.

They sat in Morag's cottage. The fire was low. The salt filmed the windows. The room was dim.

"I was fifteen when he chose me. I saw him in a tide pool. His face. Made of water. Made of light."

"What did he look like?"

"Beautiful." Morag's voice was distant. "He looked like everything I had ever wanted. Safety. Warmth. Love."

"And you refused him."

"I refused him because my mother told me to." She smiled. Bitter. "She had lost her sister to the sea. She did not want to lose me."

"So you cut your hands."

"I cut my hands. I bled into the water. He recoiled. The sea cannot abide blood that is given freely. It spoils the taste."

"You said he let you go."

"He let me go. But he did not forget. He waited. He watched." She touched her scarred hands. "And thirty years later, he took my daughter."

"Margaret."

"Margaret." The name came out like a wound. "She was sixteen. She looked like me. He took her because he could not have me."

Morrow was quiet.

"And now he is taking Catriona."

"Catriona is Margaret's daughter. My granddaughter."

Morrow felt the world shift.

"Your granddaughter."

"Aye." Morag stared at the fire. "The sea takes what he is owed. He takes it from the bloodline that owes him. My refusal created a debt that has passed through three generations."

"Catriona is paying for your choice."

"She is paying for my freedom." Morag's voice broke. "Every day I live, she pays. Every breath I take, she pays."

Morrow reached for Morag's hand. The old woman let her take it.

"I am sorry," Morrow said.

"It is not your sorrow to carry." Morag pulled her hand back. "It is mine. And soon, it will be Catriona's mother's. And then it will be no one's, because Catriona will be gone."

The fire popped. The salt on the windows glittered.

Morrow looked at her wrist. The blue line.

She was being claimed.

She was not a MacLeod.

But the sea did not care.


Chapter 31: The Debt

Morag explained the debt.

It was not metaphorical. It was not symbolic. It was real. Measurable. The island's prosperity was tied to the sea's favor.

"The fishing has been good for sixty years," Morag said. "Since your daughter—" Morrow stopped.

"Since my daughter went into the water. Yes. The boats have been full. The winter has been mild. The crops have grown."

"And now?"

"The fish are leaving. The spring was cold. The lambs were stillborn." Morag's hands were folded. "The debt is due. And the sea is reminding us."

"Is that why the village accepted Catriona's fate? Because the island needs it?"

"The village accepts it because the village has no choice." Morag's eyes were flat. "There is no democracy with the sea. There is no negotiation. There is only payment."

"And Catriona?"

"She knows. She has always known. The island raises its daughters knowing they may be asked. It is not spoken of. But it is known."

Morrow thought of the girls on the island. The young ones. The ones who would grow up knowing they might be chosen.

"How do they live with that?"

"They live." Morag stood. "What else is there?"

She walked to the door. Held it open.

"You should go, Dr. Morrow. You have heard enough for one day."

Morrow stood. She walked to the door.

"Midsummer," she said. "That is when she goes."

"That is when she goes."

"Three days."

"Three days."

Morrow stepped outside. The air was cold. The sea was grey.

She walked to her cottage.

The salt line on her wrist glowed in the dark.

Three days.


Chapter 32: Morrow's Guilt

She should have left. She should have taken the ferry that first week. She should have stayed in Glasgow with her books and her theories.

She sat at her table. The notebooks were spread in front of her. The recordings. The photographs. The evidence.

She had come to observe. To record. To understand.

She had not come to be part of it.

But she was part of it. Her presence had accelerated the timeline. Morag had said so. The sea had noticed her. The mark on her wrist proved it.

She had made things worse.

She thought of Catriona. The girl who would walk into the water in three days. The girl who had written a poem about her own death.

She could not stop it. She knew that. The village would not let her. The island's survival depended on it.

But she could witness.

That was her role. The chronicler. The recorder. The one who would tell the story after.

She opened her notebook. She began to write.

She wrote everything. The legend. The pattern. The debt. The dress. The mark on her wrist.

She wrote until her hand cramped. She wrote until the words blurred.

When she finished, she had thirty pages. A complete account.

She sealed them in an envelope. She addressed it to her department at the university.

She would give it to Iain when the ferry returned.

She did not know if the ferry would return.

But if it did, the story would leave the island.

She could not save Catriona. But she could make sure the world knew.

It was not enough.

It was all she had.


Chapter 33: The Proposal

She was on the beach when the wave brought it.

She walked the strand at dusk. The light was low. The water was grey. The line of white foam marked where the tide had been.

A wave came further than the others. It curled. It broke. It left something on the sand.

She walked to it.

A ring. Braided kelp. Green-black. Woven into a circle.

Like the one on Catriona's windowsill.

She stood over it. The kelp glistened. Wet. Fresh.

She did not pick it up.

The sea had brought her a ring. The sea was proposing.

She stepped back.

The next wave came. It covered the ring. It withdrew.

The ring was gone.

She breathed out.

She turned to walk away.

The ring was at her feet.

She had not seen it arrive. It was just there. On the sand. In front of her.

She looked at the sea. The water was flat. The surface was still.

She looked down at the ring.

She picked it up.

It was cold. It was smooth. It smelled of the deep.

She held it in her palm.

The salt line on her wrist burned.

She closed her fingers around the ring.

She did not put it on.

But she did not throw it away.

She carried it back to the cottage.

She set it on the table.

It lay there. Coiled. Waiting.

She sat in the chair. Staring at it.

She did not sleep.

The ring watched her from the table.

She watched it back.

At dawn, she wrapped it in cloth and put it in the drawer.

The same drawer where the clay figure lay.

She closed the drawer.

She felt the ring's presence through the wood.

She would not wear it.

She would not.


Chapter 34: The Weeping

Catriona wept in her mother's arms.

Morrow saw them through the window. The girl's shoulders shook. Her mother held her. Rocking. The mother's face was dry. She had no tears left.

Morrow turned away. She should not have been watching.

She walked to the beach. The ring was in her pocket. She had taken it from the drawer. She did not know why.

She took it out. Held it in her palm.

It was beautiful. She could admit that. The kelp was woven into a perfect circle. The strands were tight. Even. The work of something with patience. Something with hands.

Or something that had learned to imitate hands.

She thought of Catriona's tears. The salt. The sea tasting them.

She thought of her own tears. The morning after her first dream. The salt on her pillow.

The sea had tasted her too.

She looked at the ring.

She thought of the coral house. The green light. The voice that spoke in dreams.

She thought of Catriona walking into the water.

She thought of herself.

No.

She threw the ring into the sea. It arced. It splashed. It sank.

She watched the water close over it.

She felt nothing. Relief. Grief. She did not know.

She walked back to the cottage.

The ring was on her table.

She stopped in the doorway.

It lay coiled. Wet. Fresh. As if it had never left.

She had thrown it into the water. The water had given it back.

She approached slowly. She touched it.

It was cold.

She picked it up.

The salt line on her wrist pulsed.

She put the ring in her pocket.

She did not try to throw it away again.


Chapter 35: The Mothers

The island mothers gathered in the village hall. Morrow heard about it from Morag.

"They meet to prepare," Morag said. "The food. The clothes. The procession."

"Can I attend?"

"No." Morag's voice was firm. "You are not a mother. You are not of the island."

"I am a woman."

"You are a woman who has been marked." Morag looked at her. "The mothers would see you as a threat."

"A threat to what?"

"To their daughters. To the ceremony." Morag paused. "To the bride."

Morrow felt the weight of it. She was not welcome. She was not trusted.

She was an outsider who had been touched by the sea.

She sat in her cottage. The ring was in her pocket. She took it out. Held it.

She thought of the mothers. Meeting. Planning. Preparing for a death they called a wedding.

She thought of Catriona's mother. Holding her daughter. Dry-eyed.

She thought of her own mother. Far away. In Edinburgh. Who did not know where her daughter was.

She put the ring back in her pocket.

She walked to the village hall. The door was closed. The windows were lit. She could hear voices. Low. Steady.

She stood outside. She did not go in.

But she listened.

She heard words she recognized. Salt. Bride. Sea. Payment.

She heard weeping.

She heard silence.

She left before the door opened.

Back in her cottage, she took out her notebook.

She wrote: "The mothers meet. The island prepares. The bride accepts."

She underlined accepts.

Then she crossed it out.

She wrote: "The bride has no choice."

That was the truth.

She closed the notebook.

The ring was warm in her pocket.

She did not take it out.


Chapter 36: The Decision

The elders met at dawn. Morag told her.

"They have decided. The ceremony will be at midnight. Midsummer. The full moon."

"Tomorrow."

"Tomorrow."

Morrow felt the time collapsing. The days had blurred. She had lost track.

"Can I be there?"

"You can watch." Morag's voice was careful. "From a distance. You are not part of the procession."

"But I can watch."

"You can watch. And you can remember. That is your role now."

Morrow nodded.

She spent the day walking. The island was quiet. The houses were shuttered. The streets were empty.

She walked to the cairn. Counted the stones. Ran her fingers over the names.

She walked to the church. The tide clock ticked.

She walked to the beach. The water was calm. Flat. Waiting.

She walked to Catriona's house. The windows were dark. The curtains were drawn.

She stood outside for a long time.

Then she walked back to her cottage.

She packed her notebooks. Her recordings. Her evidence.

She sealed the envelope. She addressed it to Glasgow.

She set it by the door.

If the ferry came tomorrow, Iain would take it.

If.

She sat by the fire. The ring was in her pocket. She took it out. Looked at it.

It was beautiful. She hated it.

She put it back.

She waited for midnight.

The sea sang outside.

She listened.

The song was for Catriona.

But some part of it, she knew, was for her too.


Chapter 37: Morrow's Plan

She had a plan.

It was not a good plan. It was desperate. It was foolish. But it was a plan.

She would document the ceremony. She would record everything. And when the ferry returned, she would take the evidence to the authorities. The police. The press. Someone who could stop it.

She told Morag.

"The authorities will not come," Morag said. "They never do."

"They will if I have evidence."

"What evidence? A girl walking into the water? That is not a crime. That is a tragedy. They will call it suicide. They will call it drowning. They will call it anything except what it is."

"Then I will stay. I will protect her."

"You cannot protect her from the sea." Morag's voice was gentle. "The sea is patient. The sea is strong. The sea will take her whether you watch or not."

"Then I will go in after her."

"You will drown."

"Maybe. But I will try."

Morag was quiet for a long time.

"You are brave," she said finally. "Bravery is not the same as wisdom."

"I know."

She walked to the beach. The tide was low. The sand was wet.

She practiced. Walking into the water. The cold on her ankles. Her knees. Her waist.

She could do it. She could follow Catriona in.

She did not know what she would do once she was in the water. Fight something she could not see. Save someone who did not want to be saved.

But she would try.

She walked back to the cottage.

The ring was warm in her pocket.

She did not sleep.

She waited for midnight.


Chapter 38: The Dress

Catriona wore the dress.

Morrow saw her at dusk. The girl stood at the window of her house. White linen. Long sleeves. Her hair was loose. Her face was calm.

She was beautiful.

Morrow raised her hand. A wave. A goodbye.

Catriona did not wave back. She just stood. Watching. Waiting.

Morrow walked to the church. Father Thomas was there. Lighting candles.

"Tomorrow," she said.

"Tomorrow." He did not turn. "I have performed this ceremony five times."

"Five?"

"I came here in 1965. I have seen the brides of 1993 and 2026. And the ones before me saw the others."

"You have watched them die."

"Girls die every day, Dr. Morrow. Everywhere. Some of them die quietly. Some of them die screaming. These girls die believing they are loved." He turned. "Is that so terrible?"

"It is still death."

"It is still death." He nodded. "But it is death with meaning."

"Meaning does not bring them back."

"No. It does not." He lit another candle. "But it keeps the island alive."

She left him there. The candles flickered.

She walked to the beach. The ring was in her pocket. She took it out.

She looked at the water. Grey. Endless.

She thought of Catriona in the white dress. Walking into the waves.

She thought of the coral house. The green light. The voice.

She put the ring back in her pocket.

She would follow.

She would try.


Chapter 39: The Procession

They came at midnight.

The village walked to the strand. Every house. Every person. Old and young. They carried torches. The flames threw shadows across the sand.

Catriona led them. She walked in front. Her white dress glowed in the firelight. Her hair was braided with bladderwrack. Dark green against the white linen.

Behind her came her mother. Then the other mothers. Then the fathers. The children. The old.

They sang.

The words were Gaelic. Morrow did not understand them all. But she caught the meaning. It was a wedding song. A song of sending. A song of goodbye.

Morrow stood apart. At the edge of the beach. Hidden in shadow.

She watched.

The procession reached the water's edge. The torches formed a circle. The singing stopped.

Catriona walked forward. Alone.

The water reached her feet. White linen touching black water.

She did not stop.

The water reached her knees.

She did not stop.

The water reached her waist.

She did not stop.

The water reached her chest. Her neck.

Morrow stepped forward. Her legs moved without her permission.

"No," she whispered.

But Catriona kept walking.

The water closed over her head.

The white dress disappeared.

The sea was still.

The torches flickered.

The village waited.

And Catriona did not surface.

Morrow stood at the edge of the circle. Her hands were shaking. Her eyes were wet.

She had watched a girl die.

She had done nothing.

The village began to walk back. The torches moved away. The darkness returned.

Morrow stood alone on the beach.

The sea was calm.

The ceremony was complete.


Chapter 40: The Witness

She stayed on the beach.

The village had returned to their houses. The torches were gone. The darkness was complete. No moon. No stars. Only the sound of the sea.

Morrow stood where Catriona had entered the water. The waves lapped at her boots.

She had watched. She had done nothing.

She had planned to follow. To try. To save.

But she had not moved. She had stood frozen. A witness. Not a rescuer.

The word echoed in her mind.

Witness.

She sank to her knees. The sand was cold. Wet.

She had failed.

She had come to this island to collect stories. To record folklore. To advance her career.

She had watched a girl die.

She had watched and done nothing.

The sea sighed. A wave touched her knees.

She did not move.

She stayed on the beach until dawn.

When the light came, grey and thin, she was still there. Her clothes were wet. Her body was cold. Her eyes were dry.

She had no tears left.

She stood. Her legs were stiff. Her joints ached.

She looked at the sea.

It was calm. Flat. Innocent.

It had taken a girl.

It was satisfied.

She turned and walked back to the village.

The houses were quiet. The curtains were drawn.

She went to her cottage.

She sat at the table.

She opened her notebook.

She wrote: "Catriona MacLeod. Age fifteen. Drowned. June 21, 2026."

She closed the notebook.

She sat in the silence.

The ring was in her pocket.

She took it out.

She looked at it for a long time.

Then she put it back.

She had earned it now.

She had earned the mark.


Chapter 41: Aftermath

The village resumed its rhythm.

Morning came. The shop opened. The men went to the boats. The women hung laundry. Children played in the streets.

Life continued.

But something had shifted.

The air was lighter. The sky was brighter. The sea was calm.

The debt was paid.

Morrow walked through the village. People nodded at her now. Met her eyes. They had not done that before.

She was part of something now. She had witnessed. She had not interfered.

She was no longer an outsider.

She went to see Morag.

"She is gone," Morrow said.

"She is gone." Morag was at her table. Drinking tea. "The sea has her."

"Does it hurt? That she was your granddaughter?"

Morag was quiet. "It hurts. But it is also a relief. The waiting is over. The debt is paid."

"For now."

"For now." Morag set down her cup. "The next one will come in sixty years. Or sooner. Depending on how the sea feels."

"I will not be here."

"No." Morag looked at her. "You will not."

They sat in silence.

Morrow touched her wrist. The mark was still there. Blue. Pulsing.

"When does it fade?"

"It does not." Morag's voice was soft. "You are marked for life, Dr. Morrow. You witnessed the bridal. You accepted the ring."

She knew about the ring. Of course she knew.

"I did not accept it."

"You kept it." Morag's eyes were knowing. "You kept it. You did not throw it away. You did not give it back."

"I tried. It came back."

"Because you are meant to have it." Morag reached across the table. Took Morrow's hand. "You are not a bride. But you are something. A witness. A keeper of the story."

"Is that what I am?"

"That is what you have become." Morag squeezed her hand. "You will carry the story of Stiorra with you. Wherever you go. You will tell it."

"Is that my purpose?"

"That is your burden." Morag let go. "And your gift."


Chapter 42: The Shift

Morrow felt the change before she understood it.

The island no longer resisted her. The doors were open. The faces were friendly. The shopkeeper smiled.

She was part of the community now. She had seen the ceremony. She had kept the secret. She had not tried to stop it.

She was trusted.

But the trust came with a price.

Morag explained.

"You saw," Morag said. "Now you are part of it. The island has a new witness. The next salt wife in sixty years."

"Catriona was the salt wife."

"Catriona was the bride. You are the witness." Morag touched her own chest. "I was the witness after my daughter went. Now you are the witness after Catriona."

"What does that mean?"

"It means you remember. You watch. You tell." Morag's eyes were steady. "When the next bride comes, you will know. You will feel it. The sea will call you back."

"I will not be here. I am leaving."

"You will leave." Morag nodded. "But you will return. The sea does not release its witnesses."

Morrow wanted to argue. But she could not.

The ring was in her pocket. The mark was on her wrist.

She was bound.

She walked to the beach. The water was calm. The sky was clear.

She touched the mark on her wrist. It pulsed. Steady.

Like a heartbeat.

Like the tide.

She would leave this island. She would go back to Glasgow. She would publish her research.

But she would come back.

She knew it.

The sea knew it.

The ring in her pocket was warm.


Chapter 43: The Fever

She fell ill that night.

The fever came suddenly. One moment she was sitting by the fire. The next she was shaking. Cold. Burning.

She crawled to the bed. Pulled the blanket over herself.

Her skin was covered in salt. It crystallized on her arms. Her legs. Her face.

She dreamed.

In the dream, she was underwater. The light was green. The sand was white.

Catriona was there. The girl wore her white dress. Her hair floated around her face. She was smiling.

"You should not have come," Catriona said. Her voice was clear. Not muffled by water.

"You are dead," Morrow said.

"I am married." Catriona touched her chest. "I am his wife. I live here now."

"Is it cold?"

"No." Catriona laughed. It was a young sound. "It is warm. It is beautiful. He is kind to me."

"If he is kind, let me go."

"I cannot." Catriona's face was sad. "He has chosen you too."

"I am not a bride."

"You are a witness." Catriona reached out. Touched Morrow's face. "But a witness is still bound. You saw me go. You accepted the ring. You are his now. In a different way."

Morrow tried to speak. But the water filled her mouth.

She woke.

Morag was there. Sitting by her bed. A damp cloth on Morrow's forehead.

"The fever will pass," Morag said. "It is the sea's way of settling into you."

"Into me?"

"The mark. The ring. The memory." Morag wrung out the cloth. "You are changed now. Your body is adjusting."

"How long?"

"Three days. Maybe four. Then you will be yourself again."

"Will I?"

Morag did not answer.

She just placed the cloth back on Morrow's forehead.

Morrow closed her eyes.

She dreamed of green light.

She dreamed of a voice.

She dreamed of the ring on her finger.

She woke in the morning. The fever was gone.

The mark on her wrist had settled to a permanent blue.

She touched it.

It was cool.

She was changed.

She did not know if she was herself anymore.


Chapter 44: Morag's Vigil

Morag stayed with her through the fever.

The old woman sat in the chair. She dozed. She made tea. She changed the cloth on Morrow's forehead. She spoke in Gaelic. Words Morrow did not understand. Words that sounded like prayers.

On the third day, Morrow was strong enough to sit up.

"Thank you," she said.

"You do not need to thank me." Morag was at the fire. Stirring something in a pot. "It is my duty."

"Your duty?"

"To the witness." Morag brought her a bowl. Broth. Thin. Salty. "Drink."

Morrow drank. The broth was warm. It tasted of the sea.

"What happens now?" she asked.

"You recover. You leave. You tell the story."

"And then?"

"And then you live your life. You marry, perhaps. Have children. Grow old."

"And when the next bride comes?"

Morag was quiet.

"You will feel it," she said. "The sea will call you. You will return."

"I do not want to return."

"You will." Morag took the empty bowl. "The sea does not ask what you want."

Morrow lay back.

"What if I do not come?"

"You will." Morag stood. "Because you are a witness. And witnesses always return to the thing they have seen."

She walked to the door.

"Rest," she said. "You will need your strength."

"For what?"

"For the leaving." Morag opened the door. "The leaving is harder than the staying."

She left.

Morrow stared at the ceiling.

The mark on her wrist pulsed.

The ring was in her pocket.

She was not free.

She would never be free.


Chapter 45: The Return of Iain

The ferry returned on the fifth day.

Morrow heard the horn from her cottage. A low sound. Familiar. The sound of the outside world.

She walked to the pier. Iain was tying off the boat. He saw her and stopped.

"You are still alive," he said.

"Barely."

He nodded. Looked at the island. "The ceremony happened."

"You know about it."

"Everyone knows about it." He lit a cigarette. "I felt it. The sea went calm three days ago. That is what happens when the debt is paid."

He looked at her. His eyes stopped on her wrist. The mark was visible. She had forgotten to cover it.

"You are marked," he said.

"Yes."

He drew on his cigarette. Exhaled.

"You can still leave," he said. "The mark will not stop you from leaving."

"But I will come back."

He nodded. "That is what they all say. The witnesses. They leave. They come back. They leave again. They always come back."

"I have an envelope. For Glasgow."

"Give it to me. I will post it."

She walked to her cottage. The envelope was on the table. She picked it up.

The ring was in her pocket.

She took it out. Looked at it.

She could leave it here. In the cottage. On the table. A gift for the next occupant.

But she knew she would not.

She put it back in her pocket.

She took the envelope to Iain.

He took it. "Anything else?"

She looked at the island. The green slopes. The black cliffs. The white houses.

"No," she said.

She stepped onto the ferry.

The engine chugged.

The island receded.

She stood at the rail and watched Stiorra grow small.

She touched her wrist.

The mark was still there.

It would always be there.


Chapter 46: The Choice

She could not leave.

The ferry was at the pier. Iain was waiting. The mainland was two hours away.

She stood on the concrete with her bag. The envelope was already on the boat. She had given it to Iain. Her research. Her evidence. Her story.

She could step on board. She could go home.

She did not move.

"Dr. Morrow," Iain called. "Are you coming?"

She looked at the boat. The familiar shape. The escape.

She looked at the island. The cottages. The church. The cairn.

She could feel it. The pull. The sea calling her.

Not as a bride. As a witness.

She had to stay.

"Not yet," she said.

Iain nodded. He had expected this.

"The boat will return in two weeks," he said. "If you want to leave then."

"If."

He almost smiled. "If."

He untied the rope. The ferry pulled away.

Morrow stood on the pier and watched it go.

The mark on her wrist was warm.

She walked back to the village.

She had made her choice.

She was not a visitor anymore.

She was not a researcher.

She was not a bride.

She was a witness.

She would stay.

She would watch.

She would remember.

When the next bride came, she would be here.

She walked to her cottage. The door was open. She had left it that way.

Inside, everything was the same. The table. The chair. The fireplace.

She sat down.

The ring was in her pocket. She took it out. She looked at it.

She did not put it on.

But she did not put it away.

She held it.

The sea sang outside.

She listened.

She was home.


Chapter 47: The Manuscript

She wrote the manuscript in three weeks.

The words came easily. She did not struggle. She did not revise. She wrote as if someone was dictating. The sentences arrived fully formed.

She wrote about the crossing. The cottage. The salt on the windows.

She wrote about Morag. Her scarred hands. Her stories.

She wrote about Catriona. The dress. The poem. The procession.

She wrote about the ring. The mark. The dreams.

She wrote about the debt.

She wrote everything.

When she finished, she had two hundred pages. A complete account. Academic in structure. Personal in voice.

She sent it to Glasgow with Iain. A thick envelope. The manuscript. The recordings. The photographs.

She did not know if anyone would believe it.

But she had done her duty.

She was a folklorist. She had collected the story.

The fact that the story had collected her was not something she needed to include in the publication.

She sat on the beach and watched the ferry disappear.

The mark on her wrist glowed faintly.

She touched it.

She was still here.

She would always be here.

The island had claimed her.

Not as a bride.

As something else.

As the one who remembered.


Chapter 48: The Last Chapter of the Book

She realized she was not writing a folklore collection.

She was writing a witness statement.

Every page was evidence. Every sentence was testimony. Every chapter was a record of a crime that no law could punish.

She sat in her cottage. The completed manuscript was gone. Sent to Glasgow.

But there was more to write.

She opened a new notebook.

She wrote her own story. The personal one. The one that had no academic value.

She wrote about the fever. The dreams. The ring that would not stay thrown.

She wrote about the moment Catriona disappeared beneath the water.

She wrote about standing on the pier and choosing to stay.

She wrote about the mark on her wrist.

She wrote about the sea.

She wrote for three days without stopping.

When she finished, she had a second manuscript. Personal. Private. True.

She did not send this one to Glasgow.

She kept it in her drawer.

Next to the clay figure.

Next to the ring.

Her witness statement.

Her confession.

Her prayer.

She closed the drawer.

She walked to the beach.

The sea was calm.

But she knew it was listening.

It always listened.

It always waited.


Chapter 49: The New Vigil

She stayed on Stiorra.

The weeks passed. The summer turned to autumn. The days grew shorter. The nights grew colder.

She became part of the village. Not fully. Never fully. But enough.

She helped Morag with her garden. She learned to bake salt bread. She sat in the pub and listened to the old men sing.

She did not record them. She did not take notes.

She just listened.

The mark on her wrist remained. Permanent. Blue. A bracelet of salt.

She did not cover it anymore.

It was part of her.

She had accepted it.

Not the ring. Not the sea.

But the mark. The witness.

She was the chronicler of the salt wives. The keeper of the memory.

When she died, someone else would take her place.

The island would always have a witness.

She thought of Catriona sometimes. Under the water. In the coral house.

She hoped she was happy.

She thought of the next bride. Sixty years from now. Or less.

She hoped she would be brave.

She walked the beach at dawn. The sea was grey. The sky was grey.

She touched her wrist.

The mark was warm.

She was still here.

She would stay.


Chapter 50: The Tide Turns

She walked the beach at dawn. Six months since midsummer. The winter had passed. The spring was coming.

The sea was calm. The tide was low.

She stood at the water's edge. The mark on her wrist was pale now. Faded. But still there.

She touched it.

It was cool.

She looked at the horizon. The line where sea met sky.

She thought of Catriona. Of Morag. Of all the brides who had walked into this water.

She thought of herself.

She was not a bride. She was not a victim. She was not a hero.

She was a witness.

She would remember.

She would tell the story.

She would be here when the next bride came.

Sixty years.

She would wait.

The tide turned. The water rose. It touched her boots.

She did not step back.

She stood and let it come.

The water reached her ankles. Cold. Familiar.

It stopped.

The sea had acknowledged her.

She had acknowledged it.

They understood each other.

She turned and walked back up the beach.

The cottage was waiting. The fire was lit. Morag would come soon with tea.

She had work to do.

She had stories to write.

She had a vigil to keep.

She touched her wrist one last time.

The mark was still there.

It would always be there.

She was the Salt Wife's witness.

She was the keeper of the tide.

She was home.
