Chapter 1: The Lion

The ship was called the Lion. She was a hundred tons of damp oak and salt-crusted rope, and she smelled of bilge water and the vomit of sixty souls. Eleanor Sutton stood at the rail and watched Plymouth shrink to a thumbprint on the horizon. The wind cut through her wool dress. She did not shiver.

Below deck, the married couples had curtained off corners with blankets. The single women slept in a row along the starboard side, packed like herrings in a cask. Eleanor had chosen a spot near the ladder. She wanted to be first up when the air turned bad.

The voyage was supposed to take two months. The investors had promised fresh water, dried beef, and hard cheese for the duration. What they got was ship's biscuit crawling with weevils and a cask of water that tasted of iron before the first week was done.

Her father had wept when she told him she was leaving. She had not wept. The marriage contract was signed, the groom was a wool merchant with bad teeth and a dead wife, and Eleanor had looked at the ink on the parchment and seen a cell door closing. The colony was an open door. She took it.

The Lion rose on a swell and plunged. Water sheeted across the deck. Eleanor gripped the rail and tasted salt on her lips. Behind her, England was gone. Ahead was nothing but gray water and a name on a map she had never seen.

She had never been more than twelve miles from her father's house in Southwark. She was twenty years old and she was sailing into blank space.

Chapter 2: Ship's Biscuit

The rats came out at night. Eleanor could hear them scratching between the casks, fighting over spilled grain. The darkness was complete. She learned to sleep with her hand on her boot, ready to swing.

Margaret Lawrence slept two berths down. She was fifty-six, with gray hair twisted into a knot and fingers like gnarled roots. The colony needed a midwife, and the investors had found her in a parish poorhouse. She had not been asked if she wanted to come.

"Hold out your hands," Margaret said one morning.

Eleanor obeyed. Margaret turned them palm-up, examined the calluses.

"Tanner's daughter?"

"Yes."

"You know hides. You know what rot looks like. That will serve." She pressed a dried leaf into Eleanor's palm. "Yarrow. Stops bleeding. Learn the look of it."

The sailors cursed the women for taking up space. The settlers cursed the sailors for short rations. By the third week, the fresh water was rationed to a cup per person per day. Eleanor's lips cracked. Her gums bled when she chewed the biscuit.

A man named George Howe caught a rat and ate it. He was proud of this. He told everyone at supper, laughing with his mouth full. The laughter did not spread.

Eleanor wrote in her journal by candlelight. She had bought the book in Plymouth, a dozen pages of rag paper bound in soft leather. She wrote the date and the direction of the wind and the number of weevils in her biscuit. She did not write about the wool merchant or the look on her father's face.

She kept the journal inside her bodice. It stayed dry there.

Chapter 3: The Mapmaker

Governor John White stood apart from the other men. He had a long face and careful hands, and he spent most of his daylight hours on the quarterdeck with a piece of charcoal and a roll of parchment. He was drawing the coast. Every headland, every inlet, every sandbar. He worked as if the map could hold the sea still.

Eleanor brought him his supper on the third Tuesday. Hard cheese, ship's biscuit, a cup of vinegar water. He thanked her without looking up.

"Are you drawing where we are going?"

He lifted his head. His eyes were pale blue, like winter ice. "I am drawing where we have been. The map ahead does not exist yet."

She asked him about the previous colony. The one Sir Walter Raleigh had sent in 1585. The fort on Roanoke Island with seventy-five men.

"Fifteen of them are still alive," White said. "Ralph Lane brought them home on a privateer. The rest are buried there."

"What happened to them?"

He looked at the charcoal in his hand. "The same thing that happens to Englishmen who do not know how to feed themselves in a foreign land."

He did not tell her the rest. Not then. Not that the men had killed the Secotan king and burned his village. Not that the colony had been a failure of violence, not of weather. Eleanor did not know to ask.

She left him on the quarterdeck with his charcoal and his secrets. The Lion sailed on.

Chapter 4: A Cry in the Night

The storm hit without warning. The sky went green, then black, and the Lion climbed a wall of water and fell down the other side. Eleanor was thrown from her berth. She landed on her shoulder and felt something twist.

Then Eleanor Dare screamed.

The sound cut through the storm. Margaret Lawrence was already moving, her herb satchel in her hand. She pushed Eleanor toward the ladder.

"Hot water. Find the cook. Tell him I need clean cloths."

Eleanor stumbled across the pitching deck. The cook was a Welshman named Owens, and he was on his knees praying. She grabbed his collar and shouted Margaret's words into his ear. He stared at her like she was speaking a foreign language. She filled a kettle herself.

Eleanor Dare lay on a blanket in the captain's cabin. Her face was gray. Her husband Ananias held her hand and looked like a man watching his house burn.

The baby came at midnight, during the worst of the storm. Margaret caught her with steady hands. A girl, pink and squalling, her fists clenched against the cold air.

Margaret wrapped her in a clean cloth and placed her on Eleanor Dare's chest. The mother wept. The father wept. The storm raged on.

Eleanor Sutton stood in the corner with the kettle and watched a new world receive its first English child. The baby's name would be Virginia. Named for the land they had not yet reached.

Chapter 5: Landfall

The Lion scraped sand on July 22, 1587. The settlers poured onto the beach like cargo finally unloaded. Eleanor stepped into the surf and felt solid ground beneath her feet for the first time in eight weeks. Her legs did not believe it.

Roanoke was not what she had expected. The forest pressed against the shore, dense and dark. The air smelled of pine and salt and something green she could not name. The heat was thick, wetter than any English summer.

The fort was a ruin. The palisade had fallen in three places. The houses inside were roofless, their timbers silver with weather. Grass grew through the floorboards.

White walked through the empty structures with his map in his hand. He did not speak. The soldiers who should have been garrisoned here were gone — dead or fled, no one could say.

Ellis kicked at a pile of ash. "They left us nothing."

"They left us the land," Margaret said.

Ellis spat on the ground. "The land does not feed you."

Eleanor walked to the shoreline. The water was flat and pale under the summer sky. Across the sound, she could see the dark line of the mainland. It went on forever, no roads, no church towers, no boundary markers. An ocean of trees.

She felt something that was not fear. It came from her chest and spread to her fingers. She did not have a word for it.

She helped unload the Lion. The work kept the feeling at bay.

Chapter 6: The Carving

The settlers worked for a week before anyone saw him. He stood at the edge of the forest where the pines gave way to marsh grass, and he did not move. A man, copper-skinned, bare-chested, watching.

Eleanor was the first to notice. She was carrying water from the creek and she looked up and there he was. Her hands went still. The bucket dripped.

He wore a single feather in his hair. His chest was painted with a spiral design. He held no weapon.

She did not run. She did not call out. She stood in the mud and looked back at him.

He raised his right hand, palm outward. She understood the gesture. It needed no translation.

Then White came around the corner of the ruined fort, and the man stepped forward, and they met in the open space between the palisade and the trees. White spoke in awkward Algonquian. The man answered in English.

"My name is Manteo. I am of the Croatan."

Eleanor learned later that Manteo had been taken to England two years earlier by the previous expedition. He had learned the language. He had met the Queen. He had been brought back to serve as interpreter and guide.

She asked him once what he thought of England. He looked at her for a long time before answering.

"Everything smells of stone."

Chapter 7: Repair

Ellis organized the work crews. He was a carpenter by trade and a tyrant by nature, and he drove the settlers through the daylight hours without rest. The palisade went up. The roofs were patched. The well was dug.

Eleanor carried timber until her palms bled through the rags she wrapped around them. She ate her supper standing up because sitting down hurt too much to rise again.

Manteo came and went. He brought fish. He showed the men where to set crab traps. He pointed out the plants that would blister the skin and the ones that would heal. Ellis did not trust him but ate his fish.

Governor White began his map of the settlement. He drew the fort, the creek, the sound, the curve of the coast. Eleanor watched him work when she could spare a moment. His hand was steady. His lines were sure.

"He will draw us into existence," Margaret said, standing beside Eleanor. "As long as he draws, we are real."

"Do you think it works that way?"

Margaret looked at the forest. "It has to work some way."

At night, Eleanor wrote in her journal. She recorded the measurements of the fort: ninety feet on each side. The number of nails used: three hundred. The depth of the well before they struck water: fourteen feet.

She did not write about the way the forest pressed in at the edges of her vision, patient and dark, waiting to see what the English would do.

Chapter 8: The Welcome

Manteo came into the fort with five Croatan men. They carried woven baskets filled with fish — silver drum and speckled trout, still gleaming with moisture. The scales caught the afternoon light.

White met them in the central square. He spoke slowly, picking his way through Algonquian words like a man crossing a stream on uncertain stones. Manteo answered in English, translating for his companions.

The Croatan men sat cross-legged on the packed earth. They examined the English tools with frank curiosity. One of them picked up a carpenter's plane and turned it over in his hands, testing the weight. Ellis snatched it back. The man's face did not change.

Eleanor brought water. Manteo took the cup from her and drank. His eyes met hers. She looked away first.

"Your governor draws everything," he said. "Why does he draw the roofs?"

"He is making a record," Eleanor said. "So people in England will know what we built."

"You build houses. Then you draw them. Then you leave."

"We are not leaving."

Manteo said nothing. He stood and walked to the open gate and looked out at the sound. The Croatan men followed. They left the fish on the ground.

Eleanor picked up a basket. The fish were still warm. She carried them to Margaret for cleaning and did not look at the gate again.

Chapter 9: Translation

White held a council in the long house they had repaired as a meeting hall. Manteo sat at the table with the English men. Eleanor stood at the back with the other women.

Manteo spoke of the mainland. The tribes there — the Secotan, the Weapemeoc, the Chowanoke — were many. Some were friendly. Some were not. The Roanoke people who had lived on this island were gone now, decimated by disease and war.

"Your soldiers killed their king," Manteo said. "Ralph Lane. The one who came before. The Roanoke remember."

Ellis slammed his hand on the table. "That was not us. That was Lane. We are not Lane."

"The dead do not distinguish between Englishmen."

Wanchese sat at the end of the table, silent. He had also been to England. He had also learned the language. But where Manteo moved between worlds, Wanchese had chosen his side. His face was stone. His eyes moved from speaker to speaker like a man counting his enemies.

When the council ended, Eleanor stepped outside. The night air was cool. The stars were different here, unfamiliar constellations wheeling overhead.

Manteo found her by the well.

"The governor trusts me," he said. "But he does not listen."

"Does Wanchese trust you?"

He did not answer. He walked into the darkness and was gone.

Chapter 10: The Map

White spread his map across the table. The sound was drawn in blue wash. The coastline curved east and west. Islands dotted the shallows. In the empty space beyond the tree line, he had written in his fine italic hand: The sea of China.

Eleanor traced the shore with her finger. She had never seen a map like this. The maps she knew showed London, the Thames, the roads that connected the market towns. They were maps of known things. This was a map of guesses.

"What is this?" she asked, touching the mainland.

"The continent. It goes to the Pacific. Sir Walter believes there is a passage — a water route to Cathay."

"Does the passage exist?"

White looked at her. His eyes were tired. "I do not know. I have not been far enough inland to find out."

"Will you go?"

"I will draw what I can see. I will leave the passage for the men who come after."

He rolled the map and tied it with a leather thong. The gesture was final. The map was finished. The colony was begun. He had done what the investors paid him for.

Eleanor left the long house. Manteo was standing by the palisade, looking across the sound. She stood beside him.

"Your governor draws a line between water and land," Manteo said. "He thinks the line is real."

"Is it not?"

Manteo was silent for a long time. Then he said, "The water remembers where it used to be."

Chapter 11: Summer Fever

August brought heat that pressed down like a wet blanket. The air was thick with mosquitoes. The well water turned tepid and brackish. By the second week, half the colony was sick.

Margaret moved from bed to bed with her herb satchel. Eleanor followed her, carrying clean water and clean cloths and the names of plants she was learning. Feverfew for the headaches. Willow bark for the aches. Sassafras root for the stomach.

"The body in a new place does not know itself," Margaret said. "It fights the air, the water, the food. It takes time to learn."

"Will we have time?"

Margaret did not answer. She pressed a cool cloth to a woman's forehead and moved on.

The first death came on a Tuesday. A boy named Thomas, nine years old, whose father had brought him to the colony for a better life. The fever took him in three days. His father did not speak for a week after.

Eleanor helped dig the grave. The soil was sandy and easy to move. They buried Thomas on a rise above the sound, facing east. Toward England.

The settlers gathered. White read from the prayer book. His voice was steady but his hands shook.

Eleanor looked at the fresh mound of earth and counted the days since they landed. Thirty-one. And one grave.

She wrote it in her journal. She wrote the boy's name, the date, the cause of death. She wrote it carefully, as if the act of recording could make sense of it.

Chapter 12: The Garden

The ground was poor. Sandy, thin, quick to dry. Eleanor had grown up around tanners, not farmers, but she knew what rotted and what did not. She turned the soil with a shovel borrowed from the carpenter's stores and planted turnip seeds in shallow rows.

Manteo found her on her knees in the dirt. He watched for a moment, then crouched beside her.

"Your seeds will not grow here."

"The investors sent them. They must grow."

He took a seed from her palm and held it up to the light. "English seed. English soil. You brought your dirt across the ocean."

"I brought what I had."

He stood and walked to the edge of the clearing. He came back with a handful of dried beans, black and hard. He pressed them into her hand.

"Plant these with the corn. The bean climbs the corn. The corn feeds the bean. They help each other."

"The Croatan way."

"The way that works."

She planted the beans. She planted the corn. She dug the holes deep enough, covered them carefully, and poured the water she had carried from the creek.

The English seeds did not grow. The Croatan beans pushed through the soil in nine days.

Eleanor watched the green shoots rise and felt something shift in her chest. She had planted foreign seeds in foreign dirt, and the dirt had accepted them.

Chapter 13: Council

White called a council in the long house. The settlers crowded in. The air was hot and stale. A baby cried and was shushed.

"Summer is almost gone," White said. "We have food for three months if we ration. We have no word from England. The supply ships were supposed to follow us. They have not come."

Ellis stood. "You want to go back."

"I want to secure supplies. If I sail with the Lion now, I can reach England by October. I can be back before spring."

"Spring is eight months away."

"Eight months is better than starvation."

The room divided. Some agreed with White. The colony needed supplies, and no supplies were coming. Others sided with Ellis. White was the governor. His duty was here. If he left, the colony might not hold.

Eleanor Dare stood with her child in her arms. "If you go, you will see her grandfather. You can tell him she is beautiful."

White's face broke. He crossed the room and took Virginia Dare in his arms. He held her for a long time.

"I will come back," he said. "I promise."

Eleanor Sutton watched the promise being made and said nothing. She had learned in London that promises were paper-thin. They tore in the first wind.

Chapter 14: The Last Boat

The Lion sailed on August 27. White stood at the stern, watching the colony shrink behind him. The settlers lined the shore. The women wept. The men stood rigid.

Eleanor did not weep. She stood with her arms at her sides and watched the ship become smaller and smaller until it was a speck, then a shadow, then nothing but a line of wake dissolving into the gray water.

"Spring," Margaret said beside her. "He said spring."

"He said what he had to say."

The ship was gone. The settlement fell silent. The wind moved through the pines. The waves lapped at the shore. The colony was alone.

Ellis walked back to the fort without a word. The other men followed. The women picked up their children and went back to the houses.

Eleanor stayed on the beach. Manteo stood at the edge of the trees.

"He will not come back," Manteo said.

"You do not know that."

"I know the ocean. I know the English. I know that men who leave are not the same as men who stay."

Eleanor had no answer. She walked back to the fort. The gate was open. She closed it behind her.

That night, she wrote in her journal: August 27. The governor sailed. He promised to return by spring. I am writing this so I will remember what a promise sounds like.

Chapter 15: First Frost

October brought the cold. It came not gradually but all at once, as if the air had turned against them overnight. The leaves fell. The creek ran shallow. The garden died.

Ellis took inventory. He announced the results in the long house, his voice flat.

"Corn: two barrels. Salt pork: one barrel. Dried peas: half a barrel. Biscuit: three sacks."

"What about the livestock?" someone asked.

"There is no livestock. The chickens died. The pig we ate last week."

The room was silent. They had been on the island for three months. They had rations for maybe two more.

Manteo came through the gate carrying a string of fish. He laid them on the ground. Ellis looked at the fish. He looked at Manteo.

"How many more?"

"Enough for today."

"Tomorrow?"

"I cannot promise tomorrow."

Ellis took the fish. He did not thank Manteo. He carried them to the cook fire and dropped them in the pot.

Eleanor watched from the well. Manteo walked toward the forest. He stopped at the tree line and turned.

"The fish will stop coming when the water freezes," he said. "In my village, we store food for winter. Your people do not."

"We have stores."

"Not enough."

He disappeared into the trees. Eleanor stood in the cold and felt the truth of his words settle on her shoulders like frost.

Chapter 16: The Hunt

Manteo organized a hunt. He chose four Englishmen — the strongest, the youngest. George Howe was among them. He was eager, too eager, slapping the men on the back and talking about the venison they would bring home.

Eleanor watched them leave at dawn. Manteo walked at the front, his bow unstrung, his eyes on the tree line. Howe followed with a musket that had never been fired at anything larger than a seagull.

They returned at dusk, exhausted. They carried a deer between them on a pole. The men were grinning despite their fatigue.

"Howe got him," one of them said. "Clean shot through the neck."

But the grin on Howe's face was wrong. Too wide. Too bright. There was more to the story.

Later, Eleanor heard the rest from Manteo. The hunt had gone well until Howe's second shot. He had fired at a rabbit and hit a Croatan hunting dog instead. The dog belonged to a man from Wanchese's village.

"It was an accident," Manteo said. "But accidents have teeth."

"How bad?"

"The dog will live. The hunter will not forget."

Eleanor looked across the compound. Howe was still laughing, showing the men where he had placed his shot. He did not know what he had done.

She thought: He will never know. He will think it was nothing. And that is the worst part.

Chapter 17: Bad Blood

Wanchese came to the fort the next morning. He did not enter. He stood at the gate with two Secotan warriors behind him and called for Manteo.

Manteo came. They spoke in Algonquian, fast and low. Eleanor could not follow the words, but she understood the tone. Wanchese was angry. Manteo was calm. The calm was the more dangerous of the two.

When Wanchese left, Manteo stood at the gate for a long time. Eleanor approached him.

"What did he say?"

"He says the English are children with thunder-sticks. He says we should drive you into the sea before you burn our villages the way your soldiers burned the Roanoke villages."

"Are you going to?"

Manteo turned to face her. His eyes were flat. "I am the one who brought you here. I told my people you came in peace. If you bring war, that is my shame."

"We did not come to make war."

"Then tell your hunter to stop shooting at dogs."

He walked away. Eleanor watched him go. She did not follow.

That evening, she found Howe by the cook fire. She asked him about the dog. He laughed.

"It was an accident. One dog. They have plenty."

"The dog belonged to a man who owes us nothing."

Howe's smile faded. "You sound like you agree with them."

"I sound like I can count."

She left him by the fire. The night was cold. The stars were sharp. She looked at the dark wall of the forest and wondered how many eyes were looking back.

Chapter 18: The Journal

November. The cold deepened. The days shortened. The colony had retreated into the repaired houses, huddling around fires that burned more wood than the timber supply could sustain.

Eleanor wrote in her journal every night. The entries were short.

November 3. Rained all day. The roof leaks over the southeast corner. We moved the flour barrel. Too late.

November 7. Ellis shot a wild pig near the marsh. Six pounds of meat for sixty people. The division was fair. The portions were small.

November 11. Margaret's cough has not improved. She says it is nothing. I have seen nothing kill men before.

November 16. Manteo did not come today. His shadow did not cross the gate. I do not know whether to fear his absence or welcome it.

November 19. We are living in the white space between the map's edge and the unknown. Every morning I wake and remember where I am. Every morning it surprises me.

She wrote these things in her leather journal, in ink she made from oak gall and vinegar. The pages were soft from the salt air. The binding was loosening.

She kept the journal inside her bodice, close to her skin. It was the only English thing that still felt like hers.

She did not show it to anyone. Not Margaret. Not Manteo. The words were for her alone, a record that she had existed here, in this place, at the edge of everything.

Chapter 19: The Signal

December brought a ship.

It was a gray morning, the sky heavy with clouds that did not break. The lookout on the palisade shouted first. Eleanor heard the cry and ran to the beach.

The ship was small, a pinnace maybe, riding low in the water. It was coming from the north, making for the channel between the barrier islands.

Ellis ordered the beacon lit. Men piled brush and dry wood on the highest dune. Smoke rose in a dark column against the gray sky.

The settlers gathered on the beach. Women held their children up so they could see. Men waved their arms. Someone had a flag — a scrap of red cloth tied to a pole — and he waved it until his shoulder ached.

The ship held its course. It did not turn.

It passed the southern point of the island and continued into the sound. The settlers watched it go. The wind carried their voices across the water. The ship did not answer.

By noon, it was gone. The smoke from the beacon drifted sideways and dissolved.

No one spoke on the walk back to the fort. Eleanor looked at the empty horizon and felt the absence of the ship like a physical weight.

That night, Margaret prayed. She prayed aloud, her voice thin in the darkness. Eleanor did not join her.

She wrote in her journal: There was a ship today. It did not stop. I do not know if it was English or Spanish or a ghost. We lit a fire. It sailed past. I do not know if the captain saw us or saw nothing at all.

Chapter 20: Spring

April came slowly, reluctantly, as if the winter did not want to release its grip. The ice on the creek melted. The first green shoots appeared in the marsh.

Governor White had been gone for eight months. The colony was thin. Three more graves had been added to the rise above the sound. The rations were almost gone. The fish had not returned with the warm weather.

Eleanor was in the garden, trying to coax life from the dead soil, when she heard footsteps behind her. She did not turn.

"Your English spring," Manteo said.

"Our English spring. It is not arriving."

He crouched beside her. He had a basket made of reeds, and inside the basket were clams, dark and wet, packed in seaweed.

"The tide gives them," he said. "The tide will give more."

"How long will the tide give enough?"

He looked at her. The question hung between them. He did not answer it.

He helped her dig the clams from the basket into a pot of water. His hands moved with practiced ease. She watched his fingers work.

"Manteo. How many of us will see next winter?"

He did not pretend to misunderstand. "Some. Not all."

"Which ones?"

"I do not know which ones. But I know this: the ones who learn to live here will stay. The ones who only wait to leave will die."

Eleanor looked at the clams in the pot, the garden that refused to grow, the dark line of the forest beyond the palisade.

She decided, in that moment, to learn.

Chapter 21: The Killing

George Howe was found dead on the northern beach. He had been collecting clams. The tide had gone out and left him facedown in the wet sand.

The arrows were still in him. Three of them. They had been pulled from a different quiver than English arrows, fletched with feathers that did not match.

Ellis found him first. He carried the body back to the fort himself, his face expressionless. He laid Howe on the table in the long house and stood over him while the settlers gathered.

They looked at the arrows. They looked at each other.

"Secotan," Ellis said. "They killed him."

"We do not know that," Margaret said.

"What else do we need to know? He is dead. He has their arrows in him."

Ellis called for the men. They armed themselves with muskets and swords and axes. They formed up in the square, waiting for his command.

Eleanor found Manteo at the edge of the forest. His face was unreadable.

"Tell me the truth," she said. "Who killed him?"

"I do not know."

"Do you have a guess?"

"I have many guesses." He paused. "The arrows are Secotan. But the feathers are from a bird that flies inland, not on the coast. Someone brought these arrows from far away."

"To make it look like the Secotan?"

He did not answer. He walked into the forest, and Eleanor let him go.

Chapter 22: Retaliation

Ellis led the raid at dawn. Twenty men, armed with muskets and fire. Eleanor watched them march into the forest and felt her stomach turn to stone.

They found the Secotan village three miles inland. Manteo had drawn a map on the ground, showing Ellis where the village was. He had not led the raid himself. He had stood aside.

The Englishmen burned three houses. They killed two men who tried to fight. They found no women or children — the village had warning, and the families had fled into the trees.

Eleanor stayed at the fort with the women and the sick. She heard the shots from miles away, muffled by the forest. Each one was a door closing.

The men returned at noon. They were dirty and silent. One of them carried a burn on his arm. Another was limping.

Ellis walked through the gate and looked at the women who had gathered.

"We showed them," he said. "They will not touch us again."

"Did you find Howe's killers?"

He did not answer. He walked to the well and poured water over his head. The water ran down his face like tears.

Eleanor looked at Manteo. He was sitting against the palisade, his eyes closed.

"You drew the map," she said.

"He asked me. I drew it."

"They will come back. The Secotan. They will come back for blood."

"I know."

"Then why did you draw the map?"

He opened his eyes. "Because if I did not draw it, he would have burned the wrong village."

Chapter 23: The Reckoning

Manteo asked for a council. He stood before the settlers in the long house and told them what he knew.

"The Roanoke people are dead," he said. "Most of them. The disease that your ships brought killed them. The ones who survived fled to the mainland and were taken in by other tribes."

Ellis sat with his arms crossed. "This is not news."

"The warriors who killed your hunter were not Secotan. They were from the Chowanoke, on the mainland. The Secotan you burned were innocent."

"How do you know this?"

"Because my people have eyes. Because the Secotan sent a messenger this morning. They want peace. You burned their houses instead."

The room was silent. Ellis stood up. His face was red.

"Your people," he said. "You say 'your people' like you are one of us."

"I am the man who speaks for both sides. And both sides are fools."

He walked out. Eleanor followed him into the yard.

"Manteo."

He stopped but did not turn.

"I believe you," she said.

He turned slowly. His face was tired in a way that had nothing to do with sleep.

"Believing me is not enough. Your people will not stop. They will fight the forest until the forest wins."

He walked to the gate. He did not look back.

Chapter 24: Burial

They buried George Howe beside the boy Thomas and the three others who had died of fever. The ground was hard. The digging took most of the morning.

Margaret spoke a prayer. Her voice was weak — her cough had worsened over the winter, and she could not stand for long without leaning on a staff.

"We commend our brother George to the earth. The earth that is not his home. The earth he came to build a new home in."

Eleanor helped lower the body. She had wrapped him in a sheet herself. The sheet was the last clean one the colony had.

After the burial, she stood alone at the graveside. The wind came off the sound, cold and salt. She looked at the row of graves and counted the names.

Thomas Bell, aged 9. Fever.

Rose Payne, aged 34. Childbed fever.

John Sampson, aged 52. Fever.

Catherine Sampson, aged 48. Fever.

George Howe, aged 27. Killed.

Five graves. Five reasons the colony might not survive.

She thought of Governor White, somewhere in England, trying to find a ship. She thought of the supply ships that never came. She thought of the beans she had planted with Manteo's help, and how the Croatan way had worked when the English way had failed.

She knelt and placed a flat stone on Howe's grave, the way the Croatan marked their dead. It was the only marker she could give him.

Chapter 25: Desertion

Two men stole the skiff on a moonless night. They took what food they could carry and a musket and a compass that belonged to White's personal stores. They rowed south, toward the sound that led to the open sea.

Ellis discovered the theft at dawn. He stood on the beach and stared at the empty spot where the boat had been tied. He did not shout. He did not curse. He stared.

"They will die out there," someone said.

"Then they will die."

Eleanor thought of the two men. She knew their faces but not their names. They had been quiet men, the kind who did not speak in councils. They had probably been afraid for months.

The skiff was found three days later, overturned on a sandbar. The bodies washed up on different beaches. One of them had been in the water long enough that the crabs had been at him.

Ellis ordered the remains buried where they were found. He did not want the bodies brought back to the fort.

Eleanor walked to the southern beach with Margaret. They found one of the men half-buried in the sand. Margaret performed the burial herself, her hands steady, her face calm.

"They wanted to live," Margaret said. "That is all. They wanted to live, and they chose the wrong way."

"Is there a right way?"

Margaret looked at the sky. "I have not found it yet."

Chapter 26: The Teacher

Manteo returned the day after the burial. He did not mention the raid or the deaths. He found Eleanor in the garden and sat down beside her.

"Learn the names," he said. "The names of what lives here. Then you will not be a ghost."

He taught her the words. The names of the trees that grew along the creek: persimmon, hickory, sweet bay. The names of the clouds that brought rain and the clouds that brought wind. The names of the fish in the sound and the birds in the marsh.

She practiced the words aloud. Her tongue tripped over the unfamiliar sounds. Manteo corrected her without impatience.

"Say it from the throat. Not the teeth."

She tried again. He nodded.

"Better."

She learned the word for star and the word for path and the word for home. She learned that in his language, the word for home was also the word for the place where your mother was born.

"What is the word for England?" she asked.

"There is no word for England. It is not a place that has a name in our language."

"How do you speak of it?"

He thought for a moment. "We say the land where the cold people live."

She laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in months. The sound startled her.

Manteo almost smiled.

"You are learning," he said.

Chapter 27: Drought

June came dry. The sky stayed blue day after day, cloudless, pitiless. The creek shrank to a trickle. The wells began to fail.

Ellis ordered water rationing. One cup per person per day. The allocation was strict. The arguments were immediate.

Eleanor went to the marsh at low tide with a bucket. She dug a hole in the sand and waited for the water to seep in. It was brackish and gritty, but it was wet. She carried it back to the fort and distributed it to the women who could not walk that far.

Manteo showed her the wild grapevines that still held moisture in their stems. He showed her how to cut the vine and catch the dripping water in a gourd. She spent her mornings in the forest, collecting water one vine at a time.

The garden died. The cornstalks withered. The beans shriveled on the vine. Eleanor looked at the brown collapse of her work and felt nothing. There was no water for tears.

Ellis ordered a digging crew to deepen the well. The men worked in shifts, lowering each other into the dark hole on frayed ropes. They dug through sand and clay and found nothing but dry dirt.

"We need rain," Margaret said.

"It will not rain," Eleanor said.

"I know. But we still need it."

One cup per day. Sixty people. The numbers did not add up. The numbers had never added up.

Eleanor went to the marsh again, but the holes she dug came up dry.

Chapter 28: The Split

Ellis called the council. His face was thin. His eyes were hollow.

"We cannot stay here," he said. "The water is gone. The food is gone. If we stay, we die."

He looked around the room. No one argued.

"There are two choices. We go inland and find the tribes that Manteo speaks of. Or we build boats and try to reach the mainland coast, where English ships might find us."

Ananias Dare stood. His wife Eleanor Dare sat beside him, Virginia in her arms. The child was sickly now, pale and thin.

"We should go inland. The Croatan have offered to take us. They have food. They have water. They know this land."

"Some of us will not go," Ellis said. "Some of us will stay and hold the fort. If ships come, someone must be here to meet them."

"To hold the fort for what? For who?"

"For England."

The room divided. The families with young children wanted to go inland. The men who still believed in rescue wanted to stay. The arguments went on for hours.

Eleanor sat in the corner and listened. She did not speak. She was not sure which side she belonged to.

She thought of Manteo's word for home. The place where your mother was born. Her mother was buried in a churchyard in Southwark. That was home, and she could never go back.

Chapter 29: The Vote

Ellis called for a vote. Every adult in the colony would decide. Stay at the fort, or go inland with the Croatan.

The settlers gathered in the long house. The heat was suffocating. The children cried. The men stood in clusters, whispering.

Eleanor's name was called. She stood.

"The vote is not about where we will be safer," she said. "It is about who we will be. If we stay here, we stay as English subjects waiting for rescue. If we go inland, we become something else. Something that has no name yet."

"What are you saying?" Ellis demanded.

"I am saying that I do not know which is right. But I know that staying here has killed five of us already. And I am not ready to be the sixth."

She sat down. The room was silent.

The vote was called. The result was close. Twenty-three for going inland. Twenty-two for staying. Ananias Dare's faction had won by a single vote.

Ellis accepted the result without comment. His face was stone. He would stay with the twenty-two who voted with him.

Eleason had voted to go. The decision settled in her chest like a stone dropped in deep water.

She packed her journal in her bodice and waited for dawn.

Chapter 30: Departure

They left at sunrise. Twenty-three settlers, walking into the forest with what they could carry. Ananias led the way. Manteo walked at the front, his bow in his hand, his eyes scanning the trees.

Eleanor walked behind the children. There were seven of them, including Virginia Dare, who was two years old now and could barely stand. Her mother carried her on her back, wrapped in a shawl.

Margaret walked beside Eleanor. Her cough was worse. Her face was gray. But she carried her herb satchel and she did not complain.

"The fort is already gone," Margaret said. "We just left the shell behind."

"Will we make it?"

"The Croatan village is three days' walk. If the children hold, we hold."

They walked through the morning. The forest was cool under the canopy. The path was narrow, barely visible. Manteo cut branches away as they went.

At noon, they stopped by a stream. The water was clean and cold. Eleanor drank until her stomach hurt and then filled her flask.

She looked back the way they had come. The trees had closed behind them. The fort was gone. The sound was gone. England was an idea that grew fainter with every step.

She turned forward and kept walking.

Chapter 31: The Empty Fort

Ellis watched the last of the settlers disappear into the trees. He stood at the gate for a long time after they were gone.

The fort was quiet. Twenty-two people in a space built for a hundred. The empty houses gaped like missing teeth.

He ordered the gate closed. The men obeyed. The women went back to their work. The children played in the dust.

The well was still dry. The garden was dead. The food stores were enough for perhaps a month, if they were careful.

Without Manteo, the crab traps gave nothing. The fishing lines caught only the smallest fish, barely worth the eating.

Ellis sat in White's chair in the long house. He looked at the map White had drawn, the careful lines of coast and sound. The map was a lie now. The colony it described no longer existed.

"We hold," he said to the empty room. "We hold for England."

No one answered. The wind moved through the empty houses. The fort creaked like a ship settling into its grave.

Twenty-two people. Four weeks of food. A dry well. And a promise from a governor who had been gone for two years.

Chapter 32: The Gift

The Croatan village rose out of the forest without warning. One moment, Eleanor was walking through trees. The next, she was standing at the edge of a clearing filled with longhouses, smoke rising from cooking fires, children running between the houses.

The children stopped when they saw her. They stared.

Manteo spoke to them in his language. A woman came forward. She was older than Margaret, her face lined with years, her eyes sharp. She looked at Eleanor without curiosity. She looked at her as if she had already known she was coming.

Manteo introduced her. Her name was Kitchi. She was the village's medicine woman.

Kitchi reached out and took Eleanor's hand. She turned it over, examining the calluses, the scars, the English pallor of the skin.

"She will learn," Kitchi said. In English. The words were slow but clear.

"How do you know?" Eleanor asked.

"Because your hands have worked. A person who works can learn anything."

Kitchi let go of her hand and walked away. Eleanor stood in the middle of the Croatan village, surrounded by strangers, twenty-two days from the nearest English settlement.

She felt no fear. What she felt, rising in her chest like a tide, was the beginning of something she had no word for.

Chapter 33: The Dance

The harvest ceremony began at dusk. The Croatan built a fire in the center of the village, high enough to touch the darkening sky. The drums started low, a heartbeat rhythm that vibrated through the ground.

Manteo found Eleanor at the edge of the clearing. He was painted, his chest bare, the spiral design she had seen on their first meeting.

"You should watch," he said. "Or join."

"I do not know the steps."

"Watch first. Then join."

She watched. The dancers moved in a circle, following the drums. Their feet stamped the earth. Their voices rose in a melody that had no English equivalent. It was not a song she could take apart into notes. It was a single sound, older than language.

She watched for a long time. Then, when the drums shifted rhythm, she stepped into the circle.

She did not know the steps. She moved as the others moved, following the current of bodies. She stumbled. Someone caught her elbow and set her right. She kept moving.

The fire was hot on her face. The drums were loud in her chest. She did not feel like Eleanor Sutton, tanner's daughter from Southwark. She felt like something the drum was beating into existence.

The dance went on for hours. When it ended, Eleanor sat on the ground, breathless, her heart hammering.

Manteo sat beside her. "You are not a ghost here."

She did not answer. She watched the fire burn down to coals and felt the darkness settle around her like a blanket.

Chapter 34: The Fever Returns

Margaret collapsed on the third day in the village. She was gathering herbs by the stream when her legs gave out. Eleanor found her on the ground, her satchel open, the herbs scattered across the moss.

She carried Margaret to the longhouse. Kitchi came and felt Margaret's forehead. Her face told Eleanor everything she needed to know.

"The same fever?"

"The same. It never left her. She kept it hidden."

Kitchi prepared a tea from roots Eleanor did not recognize. She fed it to Margaret by the spoonful. Margaret's eyes were open but unseeing, focused on something far away.

Eleanor sat beside her through the night. She changed the cloth on her forehead. She held her hand. She listened to her breathe.

Margaret spoke once, near dawn. Her voice was barely a whisper.

"I did not want to come here. They told me I had to. The parish. They said England had no use for a poor widow of fifty-six."

"I know."

"But I am glad now. I saw the green of it. The green of this place. I saw the green."

She closed her eyes. Her breathing slowed.

Eleanor held her hand until it stopped moving.

She did not weep. She wrapped Margaret in a deerskin and went to find a place to dig.

Chapter 35: The Letter

Eleanor sat by the stream with her journal open on her knees. The pages were soft from handling, the ink faded. She had two blank pages left.

She wrote:

Governor White. If you ever read this, know that we waited. We waited through the first winter and the second. We buried our dead on the rise above the sound. We lit a beacon when a ship passed and it did not stop.

Your granddaughter is alive. She is thin but she lives. Your daughter Eleanor carries her through the forest and does not complain.

I am writing this because I need someone to know that we tried. We built your fort. We planted your seeds. We followed your map. The map was wrong, Governor. The map did not show the drought or the fever or the graves.

I do not know if you are alive. I do not know if England is still there or if the war with Spain swallowed it whole. I do not know if you ever meant to come back or if telling us you would was easier than telling us the truth.

I am not angry. I am not anything anymore. I am just writing this down because the words exist and they need to go somewhere.

She closed the journal. She tucked it inside her bodice. The paper pressed against her heart.

She never showed the letter to anyone.

Chapter 36: The Offering

Manteo came to her at dawn. He carried a deerskin cloak, soft from tanning, the fur still on the inside. He held it out to her.

"For winter."

She took it. The leather was warm in her hands. She had not owned anything made by hand in almost three years. Everything she had brought from England was worn through or lost.

"Why?"

"Because you will need it. And because you have stopped asking why."

She put on the cloak. It was too large, but it was warm. It smelled of smoke and leather and the forest.

She walked through the village in the cloak. The Croatan women looked at her and nodded. The children stopped staring.

She went to the well to draw water. Two of the women who had come with her from the fort were there. They looked at her cloak. They looked at each other.

"You are wearing their clothes now," one of them said.

"I am wearing warm clothes."

"You are becoming them."

"Is that a bad thing?"

The woman did not answer. She picked up her bucket and walked away.

Eleanor stood at the well in her deerskin cloak and felt the divide widening beneath her feet. She was no longer fully English. She was not yet Croatan. She was something in between, a creature of the margin between two worlds.

She drew her water and carried it back to the longhouse.

Chapter 37: The Englishman's Grave

A runner came from the coast. He brought news that the survivors at the fort had dwindled. Fever had taken more. Ellis himself was dead.

The runner did not know the details. He knew only that the English fort was nearly empty, that the palisade had been torn down for firewood, that the men who remained were too weak to fish.

Eleanor listened to the news without surprise. She had known, somehow. The fort had been dying since the day White sailed.

She walked to the edge of the village and stood alone. The forest was quiet. The sky was gray.

Ellis was dead. She had not liked him. He had been rigid and suspicious and wrong about many things. But he had believed in the colony. He had believed in England. He had held the fort long after holding it made sense.

She found a piece of flat stone and carried it to the stream. She washed it clean and carried it back to the village.

She did not carve his name. She did not know where his body was. She placed the stone at the foot of a large oak, a marker for a man who had died in a place that did not have his name on its map.

It was the only grave she could give him.

Chapter 38: The Last Winter

The snow came early and stayed late. The sound froze solid. The deer retreated deep into the forest. The world went white and still.

The Croatan village had stored food for winter — dried fish, smoked venison, corn that had been ground and packed in baskets. But the winter was long, and the village had taken in twenty-three extra mouths.

Rations were cut. The children cried from hunger. The old people gave their portions to the young and said they were not hungry.

Eleanor learned to set snares. Manteo showed her where the rabbits ran, how to read the tracks in the snow, how to set the loop at the right height. She caught two rabbits in the first week. She brought them to the communal pot.

She learned to patch the longhouse walls with mud and moss, sealing the gaps against the wind. She learned to start a fire with a bow drill, her hands blistering until the calluses formed.

She learned to live without England.

At night, she lay in the longhouse with the other women and children, packed together for warmth. The wind howled outside. The fire crackled. The babies cried.

She thought of Southwark. The cobblestones. The bell of St. Saviour's. The smell of the tannery. It felt like a memory from another life, another person.

She touched her journal, still pressed against her chest. She had not written in it for months. There was no more blank paper.

Chapter 39: The Decision

Spring came again. The ice broke. The fish returned. The Croatan village emerged from winter thin and tired but alive.

The English survivors gathered at the edge of the village. They had changed. Their clothes were rags. Their skin was brown from the sun. The children spoke Algonquian as easily as English.

Ananias Dare called them together. He looked older than his years. His wife stood beside him, Virginia in her arms. The child was four now, dark-haired and quiet.

"We cannot go back," Ananias said. "The fort is gone. The colony is gone. We are what is left."

"What do we do?" someone asked.

"We stay. We build new lives here. We become part of this village, or we find another. But we do not go back."

The group was silent. Some of them wept. Some of them nodded. Some of them looked at the forest and said nothing.

Eleanor listened. She had known this moment was coming. She had been preparing for it since the day Manteo taught her the word for home.

She stood up.

"I will stay," she said. "I will learn their language. I will learn their ways. I will not pretend that England is coming to save us, because England is not coming. England forgot us the day we sailed."

No one argued. There was nothing to argue.

The colony of Roanoke was over. What came next would be something else.

Chapter 40: CROATOAN

Eleanor walked back to the coast alone. It took her four days. She followed the streams and the deer paths, sleeping under the trees, eating what she could find.

The fort was a ruin. The palisade was gone, burned in the winter. The roofs had collapsed. The long house where White had held his councils was open to the sky.

She walked through the ruins. She found a rusted nail, a shard of pottery, a piece of cloth too rotted to identify. The graves on the rise were overgrown.

She stood on the beach where she had watched White sail away. The sound was gray and flat. The horizon was empty.

She found a post still standing at the gate. The wood was weathered, but the surface was smooth enough to carve.

She took her knife — the one she had brought from England, the one her father had given her — and she carved the word.

Seven letters. C-R-O-A-T-O-A-N.

It was the only message she could leave. A sign that they had not died, that they had gone south, that they had found a way.

She stepped back. The word looked back at her, raw and pale in the weathered wood.

She walked away. She did not look back.

Behind her, the word stood witness. Ahead, the forest waited.

Chapter 41: The March

Fifteen settlers walked into the forest. They had chosen to leave. The Croatan village was crowded, and the food was scarce, and some of the English wanted their own place.

Eleanor was among them. Manteo guided them south, toward the villages of the Croatan mainland, where the hunting was better and the land was not yet claimed.

They walked for a week. The path was rough. The children tired easily. The old people slowed the march. But no one complained. There was no point.

Eleanor carried Margaret's satchel. She had kept it after Margaret died. The dried herbs rustled inside. She did not know what all of them were. She was learning.

They crossed rivers on rafts of lashed logs. They climbed ridges through fields of stone. They saw deer and bear and snakes that rattled in the dry leaves.

The Croatan gave them food. They slept in temporary shelters built from bark and branches. They moved on at dawn.

On the seventh day, Manteo stopped at the top of a ridge.

"There," he said.

Below them, a valley opened. A river curved through it, silver in the sun. The trees were thick and green. The soil was dark.

Eleanor looked down at the valley. She saw no houses, no fields, no smoke. She saw empty land, waiting.

"We build here," Manteo said.

And they did.

Chapter 42: The Village

They built where the river bent. The trees came down. The longhouses went up. The women planted corn and beans and squash in the dark river soil.

Eleanor worked with her hands from dawn to dusk. She learned to weave mats from river reeds. She learned to make pots from the clay bank and fire them in a pit. She learned to cure hides and sew them into clothing and shoes.

The English among her learned too. Some learned faster than others. Some refused to learn at all. Those ones sat by the river and stared at the water and waited for ships that would never come.

Eleanor slept in a longhouse with three Croatan women and their children. She learned their names. She learned their stories. She learned the name of every plant within a day's walk.

At night, she lay on her deerskin and listened to the river moving past. The sound was steady, constant, indifferent. It did not care that she had been born in Southwark. It did not care that she was English. It only moved.

She fell asleep to the sound of the river. She woke to the sound of the birds. She worked through the daylight and ate what the village grew and hunted and caught.

She was not happy. She was not sad. She was alive, and the world around her was alive, and that was enough.

Chapter 43: The Healer

Kitchi came to the new village when the summer fevers started. She walked out of the forest one morning, her satchel over her shoulder, her eyes sharp as ever.

"I hear you need a healer," she said.

"We need many things."

"Start with a healer."

Eleanor became Kitchi's apprentice. She learned the roots that cured the fever and the leaves that stopped the bleeding and the bark that eased the pain of childbirth. She learned to set a broken bone with splints of cedar. She learned to sew a wound with sinew thread.

Kitchi was not patient. She did not explain twice. If Eleanor made a mistake, Kitchi made her start over from the beginning.

"Healing is not kindness," Kitchi said. "It is work. The work of knowing what is wrong and knowing what to do about it. Kindness is for after."

Eleanor learned. Her hands learned the shapes of roots and leaves. Her nose learned the smells of sickness and health. Her ears learned to hear the difference between a dry cough and a wet one.

She treated the children who fell ill. She treated the men who cut themselves building. She treated the women who bled too long.

She was not a midwife like Margaret. She was something else. A gatherer of knowledge. A keeper of roots and leaves and the stories of how they worked.

She did not think of England anymore. England had no medicine for this place.

Chapter 44: The Stranger

A fishing party found him on the beach. He was wearing the remains of an English soldier's uniform, salt-bleached and torn. His leg was broken. His lips were cracked. He had been in the water.

They carried him to the village. Eleanor treated his leg, setting the bone, binding it with splints and strips of cloth. He screamed when she moved the bone. She did not stop.

When he woke, he looked at her with confusion.

"You are English," he said.

"Yes."

"You are living with the savages."

"Yes."

He tried to sit up. She pushed him back down.

"Your leg is broken. If you move, you will never walk again."

He lay still. His name was Thomas. He had been on a supply ship bound for the Roanoke colony. Spanish privateers had attacked them off the coast of Cuba. He had been adrift for two weeks.

"The war," he said. "It is still going. England and Spain. The Queen has no ships to spare for a colony."

"I know."

"You know?"

"We have known for a long time."

He looked at her deerskin cloak, her brown skin, her hands rough with work. "You are not going back."

"No."

He closed his eyes. He did not speak again for two days.

When he could walk, he stayed. He had nowhere else to go.

Chapter 45: The Message

Eleanor found Thomas by the river. He was carving a piece of driftwood with a knife. His leg was healing, but he still walked with a limp.

"I need you to do something," she said.

"What?"

"Remember. Remember that we are here. Remember the name of this place. If you ever reach England again, you must find Governor White. You must tell him we are alive."

"I do not know if I will ever reach England."

"Neither do I. But if you do."

She gave him a letter. It was written on a scrap of rag paper, the ink made from soot and water. She had folded it small enough to fit in a pouch around his neck.

He took it. He tucked it inside his shirt.

"I will try," he said.

"Trying is all anyone can do."

She left him by the river. She went back to the village. She had patients to tend, herbs to gather, a life to live.

She did not think about the letter again. It was a seed thrown into the wind. It might grow. It might not. Either way, she had done what she could.

Thomas left the village in the autumn. He took a canoe south, following the coast. He promised to send word if he found a ship.

Eleanor did not watch him go.

Chapter 46: The Second Winter

The village was ready for the cold. The longhouses were sealed. The food stores were full. The hunting had been good.

Eleanor spent the winter learning. Kitchi taught her the winter medicines, the roots that could only be gathered after the frost, the barks that had to be stripped from the north side of the tree.

She learned to tell stories. In the longhouse, by the fire, the Croatan elders told the old stories. The story of the first man and the first woman. The story of the great flood. The story of the trickster who stole the sun.

She listened. She learned the words. She learned the rhythms of the telling.

One night, an elder asked her to tell a story from her people.

She thought for a long time. Then she told them about London. The river full of boats. The crowds in the streets. The bells that rang from every church.

"Bells?" the elder said.

"Metal that makes sound. Hung in tall houses."

"Like drums."

"Yes. Like drums, but made of metal. And we rang them to tell the time."

The elder considered this. "That seems like a lot of work."

Eleanor smiled. "It was."

She did not tell them about the wool merchant or the bad teeth or the cell door. Some stories were not stories yet.

Chapter 47: The English Return

Governor John White stepped onto the beach at Roanoke in August of 1590. Three years had passed since he sailed away. The war with Spain had delayed him. The investors had refused to fund another ship. He had begged, borrowed, and finally chartered a private vessel at his own expense.

The fort was empty.

He walked through the ruins. The roofs were gone. The palisade was scattered. Grass grew through the floorboards of the house where his daughter had lived.

He found the word carved on the post at the gate. CROATOAN. No cross carved beside it. No sign of distress.

He called out. No answer. The wind moved through the empty houses. The waves lapped at the shore.

He searched for three days. He walked the beaches. He explored the forest edge. He found nothing. No bones. No scraps of clothing. No sign of conflict.

The colony had simply vanished.

On the third day, a storm forced his ship to weigh anchor. He tried to reach Croatoan Island, but the wind drove him back. He stood on the deck and watched the coast disappear into the rain.

He wrote in his journal that night. He wrote that the colony was lost. He wrote that he had failed his daughter, his granddaughter, the people who had trusted him.

He sailed for England the next morning. He never returned.

Chapter 48: The Search

White's ship anchored off Croatoan. He sent a landing party to the island. They found a village, but the village was empty. The inhabitants had moved inland, warned by runners who had seen the English sails.

White went ashore himself. He walked through the abandoned village. He found a cooking pot that might have been English. He found a scrap of cloth that might have been a dress.

He could not be sure.

He interviewed the few Croatan who had stayed. They told him that a group of English had come south, that they had lived with the Croatan for a time, that they had moved on. But where? The answers were unclear. The language barrier was too wide. The witnesses were too few.

White fired a cannon into the forest. The boom rolled across the water and faded into the trees. He waited.

No answer came.

He fired again. Silence.

He could not stay. The ship's captain was anxious to leave. The season was late. The storms were coming. Every day he stayed was another day he might die.

White wrote a final entry in his journal. He recorded the date, the location, the fact of the cannon shot. He wrote that he had heard no answer.

He ordered the ship to weigh anchor.

He stood on the deck as the coast receded. He was sixty years old. He had lost everything. He would die in England, drawing maps from memory.

He never stopped wondering what happened to them.

Chapter 49: The Report

White wrote his report in London, in a small room near the river. The winter light was gray. The ink was thin. His hand shook.

He wrote that the colony had been established. He wrote that he had left them with sufficient provisions. He wrote that the war with Spain had prevented his return. He wrote that he had found the word CROATOAN and nothing else.

The investors read the report. They shook their heads. They called the colony a failure. They moved on to other ventures.

White published his account. It was read by men who had never crossed the ocean. They debated what might have happened. They proposed theories. They argued about the intentions of the natives.

White did not argue. He had no theories. He had only what he had seen: an empty fort, a carved word, a silence where voices should have been.

He lived another sixteen years. He drew maps. He painted watercolors of the coast and the people he had met. He kept a portrait of his granddaughter on his desk, painted from memory.

The portrait showed a child of three months. He had not seen her grow. He did not know if she had grown at all.

The report became a document in a file. The colony became a mystery. The word CROATOAN became a riddle that no one ever solved.

Chapter 50: The Name

Eleanor heard the cannon shot from the ridge. The sound rolled across the valley, distant and hollow, like thunder from a clear sky.

She stopped. The basket of roots hung from her hand. She listened.

The sound did not come again.

Kitchi was beside her. She had also stopped. Her eyes were on the eastern horizon.

"That is your people," Kitchi said.

"I know."

"They are looking for you."

Eleanor stood on the ridge and felt the weight of the question. The path to the coast was three days' walk. She could be there by the end of the week. She could stand on the beach and wave and go home.

She did not move.

"Who are you going to be?" Kitchi asked.

The question was simple. The answer was not.

Eleanor thought of the journal in her bodice, the last page empty. She thought of Margaret's grave. She thought of the word she had carved in the post.

She was not Eleanor Sutton anymore. Eleanor Sutton had sailed from Plymouth in 1587 and never returned. She was a woman who knew the name of every plant in the valley. She was a woman who could set a bone and deliver a baby and speak the language of the river.

She turned away from the shore.

"My name is Anoke," she said. It was the word Manteo had given her. It meant she who walks beside.

She walked back to the village. The cannon did not fire again.

Behind her, the forest closed. Ahead, the smoke of the cooking fires rose through the trees. She walked toward it, and she did not look back.
