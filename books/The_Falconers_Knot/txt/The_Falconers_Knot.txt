Chapter 1: The Road to San Colombano

The rain came sideways off the Arno valley, cold as January though the calendar still read October. Matteo Corvaro pressed himself against the cliff face and waited for the gust to pass. Below, the river was a brown rope threading through fields already stripped of wheat. Above, invisible in the cloud, the monastery of San Colombano clung to its limestone shelf like a barnacle on a hull.

He carried a leather bag with one change of clothes, a psalter his mother had given him, and his father's falconry glove. The glove was old calfskin, scarred by talons, stained with mutes. It smelled of the bird — the sharp mineral scent of a peregrine's feathers. He should have left it behind. A man entering a monastery had no use for such things. But his hand had closed around it at the last moment, the way a drowning man grabs driftwood.

The path narrowed. Cut steps in the rock, worn smooth by centuries of sandaled feet. Matteo's boots slipped on the wet stone. He caught himself, knees barking against the cliff, and looked down. The drop was two hundred feet. A shepherd's hut on the valley floor looked no bigger than a walnut shell.

He climbed.

The accusation had come three weeks ago. Piero Saltini, dead in an alley behind the wool merchants' hall on the Via dei Calzaiuoli. Knife wound under the ribs. Two witnesses — both Saltini's men — who swore they'd seen Matteo standing over the body. The podestà had issued a warrant. Matteo's father, old Corvaro, had woken him before dawn and pressed coins into his hand.

"San Colombano," his father had said. "The abbot owes me a debt. Go."

Matteo had not asked what debt. His father was not a man who explained himself.

The path leveled. A stone wall appeared through the rain, stained dark with water. An iron gate, closed. A bell rope hanging beside it, frayed at the end. Matteo pulled it three times and heard the clang echo inside the walls, hollow and flat.

He waited. Rain dripped from his hood. His fingers were numb. He tucked his father's glove deeper into the bag and tried to look like a man who had chosen God rather than a man who was running from the law.

The gate opened.

---

Chapter 2: The Gate

Brother Filippo was thick-necked and suspicious. He stood in the gateway with his arms folded across a chest like a wine barrel, blocking the entrance with his body.

"Your name."

"Matteo Corvaro. Of Florence."

"Florence." Filippo said the word as though it were a species of vermin. "We have had trouble from Florence before."

"I seek admittance as a novice. I have a letter for the abbot."

Matteo held out the folded paper his father had given him. Filippo took it without unfolding it and studied the seal — red wax, pressed with a signet Matteo did not recognize. Whatever it was, it softened the porter's expression by a single degree.

"Wait in the gate room."

The gate room was a stone cell with a bench, a crucifix, and nothing else. Matteo sat and dripped. Through the narrow window he could see a cloister garden, gray in the rain, and the arched walkway beyond it. A monk crossed the garden carrying a basket of turnips. Another stood under the arch reading a book, turning pages with one hand while the other held his cowl against the wind.

An hour passed. The rain stopped. The clouds thinned enough to let a weak afternoon light through, the color of old linen.

The door opened and Father Silvestro entered.

The abbot was sixty and lean as a greyhound. His face was all planes and angles — high cheekbones, sharp jaw, deep-set eyes the color of wet slate. He wore the Benedictine black with a wooden cross on a leather cord. He moved with the deliberate economy of a man who had learned to conserve energy.

"Corvaro," he said, sitting on the bench opposite Matteo. "Your father's letter asks me to accept you as a novice. He reminds me of a service he performed for this house many years ago. He does not specify the nature of the service."

"I do not know what service he means, Father."

"No. He would not have told you." Silvestro studied Matteo for a long moment. "You are young. Twenty-two?"

"Yes, Father."

"And you wish to take vows."

"I wish to serve God."

Silvestro's mouth twitched — not quite a smile. "That is what they all say. Some of them even mean it." He stood. "You will be assigned to Brother Anselmo in the scriptorium. You will attend all offices. You will observe the Rule of Silence from Compline to Lauds. You will not leave the monastery grounds without my permission."

"Yes, Father."

"And Corvaro — whatever you are running from, leave it at the gate."

He left before Matteo could answer.

---

Chapter 3: The Rule

The bells woke him at two in the morning. Matins. Matteo stumbled from his cell in the novices' dormitory, following the sound of shuffling feet down the dark corridor to the church. The air was cold enough to see his breath. The monks filed into the choir stalls without speaking, their faces waxen in the candlelight.

The chanting began. Latin, of course. The psalms rose and fell like breathing, the voices blending into a single sound that filled the stone vault above them. Matteo mouthed the words, guessing at half of them. He had learned his letters from his mother and his Latin from a parish priest who drank, and both educations had been incomplete.

After Matins, he slept for two hours. Then Lauds at dawn. Then Prime. Then breakfast — bread, oil, a cup of watered wine — eaten in silence in the refectory while a young monk read aloud from the Rule of Saint Benedict. The reader had a stammer that turned the Latin into a kind of music, each phrase arriving after a small, surprised pause.

Brother Anselmo came for him after breakfast.

The librarian was seventy-six years old and moved as though his joints were filled with sand. His eyes were milky with cataracts, the left worse than the right. He walked with one hand on the wall, feeling his way through corridors he had traveled for fifty years.

"You are the falconer's boy," Anselmo said. It was not a question.

"My father trained birds, yes."

"I knew him. Long ago." Anselmo stopped at a heavy oak door and produced a key from the ring at his belt. "This is the scriptorium. You will help me here. Cataloging, mostly. Fetching volumes I can no longer see well enough to find."

The scriptorium was a long room with high windows on the south side, designed to catch the best light. Writing desks lined the walls, each with an angled surface, an inkwell, and a candle holder. Shelves filled the spaces between the windows, holding perhaps three hundred volumes — more books than Matteo had seen in his life.

"Can you read?" Anselmo asked.

"After a fashion."

"Then we will improve your fashion."

---

Chapter 4: The Scriptorium

Over the next three days, Anselmo taught Matteo the library's system. Each volume had a number painted on its spine in white lead. A master catalog — a thick ledger bound in pigskin — listed every book by number, title, and author. Most were devotional: psalters, gospels, commentaries on the Church Fathers. But there were also works of natural philosophy, medical treatises, a copy of Boethius, and a surprisingly beautiful bestiary with illuminations in gold and ultramarine.

"Brother Luca painted those," Anselmo said, running his fingertips over the page as though he could feel the color. "He has the gift. The rest of us merely copy."

On the third morning, Matteo noticed the locked cabinet. It stood in the far corner of the scriptorium, built into the wall — a heavy wooden case with iron straps and a lock the size of a man's fist. Unlike the open shelves, it had no catalog number visible.

"What is kept there?" Matteo asked.

Anselmo's hand, which had been tracing a line of text, stopped moving. "That cabinet is not your concern."

"I only asked —"

"And I have answered. Some things in a library are meant to be read. Others are meant to be kept."

Matteo let the matter rest. But that afternoon, when Anselmo dozed at his desk — the old man slept often, brief collapses into unconsciousness — Matteo crossed the room and examined the cabinet. The lock was old but well-oiled. Someone had been opening it recently. And on the floor beneath it, so faint he almost missed it, a scuff mark in the dust. The shape of a sandal, pointed toward the cabinet. Then away.

Someone had knelt here. Recently. And whatever they had come for, they had left in a hurry.

---

Chapter 5: Vespers

The church of San Colombano was Romanesque, built two centuries before in honey-colored stone. The apse was painted with a Christ Pantocrator whose enormous eyes followed Matteo wherever he stood. The acoustics were strange — whispers carried from wall to wall, while the chanting seemed to pool beneath the dome like water collecting in a basin.

Matteo stood in the back row of the choir stalls for Vespers, watching.

This was what his father had taught him. Not the prayers, not the Latin — the watching. A falconer's first skill was observation. You studied the bird before you flew it. You learned its habits, its moods, the way it held its wings when it was hungry versus when it was afraid. You read the sky the way a monk read Scripture.

Twenty-three monks lived at San Colombano. Matteo had counted them. He was beginning to learn their names.

Father Silvestro stood at the front, his face unreadable. Giacomo the Cellarer sang loudly and off-key, his big voice filling spaces the other voices left empty. Brother Tommaso, who kept the accounts, stood with his eyes closed and his lips barely moving. Brother Marco, the herbalist, coughed throughout the service — a wet, rattling cough that made the monks on either side of him edge away.

Brother Luca caught Matteo's eye during the final psalm and smiled. After the service, he waited for Matteo in the cloister.

"You look like a man who has swallowed a stone," Luca said.

"Is it that obvious?"

"You are the first new face in this place in two years. Everyone is watching you, which means you feel watched, which makes you look guilty. It is a circle." Luca was twenty-five, dark-haired, with paint stains on his fingers that soap could not remove. "Where are you from?"

"Florence."

"I am from Arezzo. Also fleeing something, though in my case it was boredom." He grinned. "Come. I will show you the workshop."

Luca's workshop adjoined the scriptorium — a small room that smelled of linseed oil and ground minerals. Jars of pigment lined the shelves: lapis lazuli for blue, malachite for green, vermillion for red, gold leaf pressed between sheets of vellum. On the worktable lay an unfinished illumination — the letter Q, its tail curling into a dragon that devoured its own tongue.

"Beautiful," Matteo said, and meant it.

"It will be, when I finish. If Brother Anselmo ever gives me the time." Luca lowered his voice. "The old man keeps me copying psalters when I could be making something that matters. He is afraid of beauty, I think. Afraid of anything that draws too much attention."

---

Chapter 6: The Falconer's Eye

By the end of his first week, Matteo had mapped the monastery in his mind the way his father had mapped a hunting ground. He knew the layout: church, cloister, refectory, scriptorium, chapter house, dormitory, kitchen, cellars, the women's house across the courtyard where the small community of Benedictine nuns kept the infirmary and the garden. He knew the routines: the seven offices that divided the day, the meals eaten in silence, the hour of manual labor in the afternoon, the brief recreation before Compline.

And he knew the people.

Father Silvestro moved through the monastery like a chess piece — deliberate, strategic, always appearing where he was least expected. He took his meals apart from the community. He spoke to individual monks in private, rarely in groups.

Giacomo the Cellarer controlled the keys to the storehouse, the wine cellar, and the pantry. He was the only monk who was fat. He laughed too much. He touched people — a hand on the shoulder, a grip on the arm — in a way that felt less like fellowship than possession.

Brother Tommaso was nervous. He flinched at sudden sounds. He ate quickly, hunched over his bowl. Twice Matteo saw him arguing with Giacomo in the cloister, their voices low but their gestures sharp.

Brother Anselmo rarely left the scriptorium. He ate there, slept there, lived among the books like a hermit in his cave. At night Matteo heard him talking in his sleep — fragments of Latin, a name repeated: "Girolamo. Girolamo."

Brother Marco kept to his herb garden and his medicines. His cough was getting worse.

And then there was the locked cabinet, which no one mentioned and everyone avoided. Matteo watched the scriptorium during his shifts and noticed that the dust around the cabinet was disturbed every two or three days. Someone was visiting it at night.

He did not yet know why it mattered. But his father's voice was in his head, steady as a heartbeat: Watch first. Understand later.

---

Chapter 7: The Body

Matteo found him.

He rose before Lauds, unable to sleep, and walked to the scriptorium to light the candles — a task Anselmo had given him because the old man's hands shook too badly to manage flint and steel. The corridor was dark. His sandals whispered on the stone.

The scriptorium door was ajar. That was wrong. Anselmo locked it every night — the key lived on his belt even while he slept. Matteo pushed the door open and smelled something sharp and metallic underneath the familiar scent of ink and parchment.

Brother Tommaso was slumped over the third desk from the window. His head rested on the writing surface as though he had fallen asleep while working. But his eyes were open, fixed on nothing, and the front of his habit was dark with blood. A wound beneath the sternum, hidden by the folds of cloth. Matteo might have missed it entirely if not for the blood that had pooled on the desk and dripped, slowly, onto the floor.

In Tommaso's right hand, clenched so tightly that the knuckles had gone white, was a torn piece of parchment. Half a page, ragged at the edge where it had been ripped from a larger sheet.

Matteo did not touch the body. He stood in the doorway and looked. The candles on the desk were cold — they had burned out hours ago, stubs of tallow in their iron holders. The ink pot was open, a quill lying beside it. Tommaso had been working. Or pretending to work. Or waiting for someone.

No sign of struggle. The other desks were undisturbed. The shelves were in order. Whoever had done this had come and gone cleanly, leaving only the body and the torn page.

Matteo went to find the abbot.

---

Chapter 8: The Page

Father Silvestro came to the scriptorium with Brother Filippo, who crossed himself three times and would not enter the room. Silvestro walked to the body, placed two fingers against Tommaso's neck — a gesture that seemed more habitual than hopeful — and straightened.

"When did you find him?"

"A quarter hour ago. Before Lauds."

"Did you touch anything?"

"No."

Silvestro looked at Matteo with those flat gray eyes. "Good." He bent and carefully opened Tommaso's fist, extracting the torn parchment. He read it. His face did not change, but his hand tightened on the page, crinkling the edge.

"Do you know what this is?" he asked.

"May I see it?"

Silvestro handed it over. The text was Latin, written in a careful hand — not Tommaso's, which Matteo had seen in the account books. The script was older, the ink faded to brown. Matteo's Latin was imperfect, but he recognized enough words to piece together the meaning: a passage about the poverty of Christ and the apostles, arguing that the Church had no right to own property.

"This is from the Franciscan controversy," Matteo said. "The dispute about apostolic poverty."

"You are better educated than you pretend."

"My mother read. She taught me what she could."

Silvestro took the page back. "This text is from a treatise called De Paupertate Christi. Written by a Spiritual Franciscan named Girolamo of Cortona. It was declared heretical in 1323 and ordered burned. Every copy."

"Yet here is a copy."

"Yes," Silvestro said. "Here is a copy. In a dead man's hand."

---

Chapter 9: The Abbot's Commission

Silvestro summoned Matteo to his study after the funeral Mass. The room was spare — a desk, a chair, a prie-dieu, a wooden chest. No books, no ornament. A single window looked out over the valley, where smoke rose from a village Matteo could not name.

"The roads are closed," Silvestro said without preamble. "Plague has reached Empoli. The podestà of the valley has forbidden travel. No magistrate will come here to investigate a dead monk."

"And so?"

"And so the matter falls to us. To me." Silvestro paused. "To you."

Matteo stared. "I am a novice of ten days. I know nothing of —"

"You know how to watch. Your father trained you. I have seen it — the way you study a room before you enter it, the way you mark who stands where. You have a hunter's eye."

"My father trained birds, not investigators."

"The skills are the same. Patience. Observation. The willingness to wait for the quarry to reveal itself." Silvestro leaned forward. "I cannot do this myself. The abbot cannot interrogate his own monks without tearing the community apart. But a novice — a newcomer who asks questions out of curiosity — that is different."

"You want me to spy."

"I want you to find the truth. Someone in this monastery killed Brother Tommaso. I want to know who and why." He held up the torn page. "And I want to know where this came from."

Matteo thought about it. He thought about the locked cabinet, the scuff marks in the dust, the arguments between Tommaso and Giacomo. He thought about his father's voice: Watch first.

"I will try," he said.

"That is all I ask."

---

Chapter 10: The First Feather

Matteo returned to the scriptorium that evening with Anselmo's key. The body had been removed, the desk scrubbed, but the ghost of the bloodstain lingered in the grain of the wood. He lit a candle and got on his hands and knees.

The floor was stone — limestone flags, uneven and pitted. Dust collected in the cracks. He moved the candle slowly across the area around Tommaso's desk, looking for anything the others might have missed.

Under the desk, caught between two flagstones, he found it. A feather. Small — no longer than his thumb — curved and soft. He held it up to the candlelight and turned it.

He knew feathers. His father had taught him to read a bird's plumage the way a scholar reads a manuscript. This was a covert feather, from the upper surface of a wing. And the barring — fine dark lines on a pale gray ground — was unmistakable.

Peregrine falcon.

Matteo sat back on his heels. A peregrine's feather in a monastery scriptorium. It could be coincidence. Birds nested in the eaves; feathers drifted. But this feather was clean, unweathered. It had not blown in through a window. Someone had brought it here.

Or dropped it.

He tucked the feather into his sleeve and stood. The candle threw his shadow long against the wall — a tall, thin shape that looked, for a moment, like a hooded bird on a perch.

His father would have said: The hunt has begun. Know your quarry.

Matteo blew out the candle and went to find Brother Anselmo.

---

Chapter 11: Questions in the Cloister

The morning after Tommaso's funeral, Matteo walked the cloister arcade and waited for the monks to come to him. A hunter does not chase — he positions himself and lets the game come within range.

Brother Paolo, the baker, was the first. A small man who kneaded anxiety along with his dough.

"Terrible business," Paolo said, falling into step beside Matteo. "Nothing like this has ever happened. Not in all my years."

"How many years?"

"Eighteen come Michaelmas." Paolo lowered his voice. "I will tell you what I think. Tommaso was mixed up in something. He and Giacomo — arguing every other day. About grain. About money. About things that go missing."

"Missing?"

"Stores. Flour, oil, salt pork. Giacomo keeps the keys, keeps the ledger. But the numbers do not add up. Tommaso was the one who noticed. He kept the accounts for the abbot."

This was useful. Matteo let Paolo talk and stored every word.

By noon he had spoken to six monks. The picture that emerged was consistent: Tommaso had been asking uncomfortable questions about the cellarer's management. Giacomo had responded with bluster and threats. Two monks had heard Giacomo say, in the refectory, that Tommaso should mind his prayers and leave the counting to those who could count.

But no one had seen anything on the night of the murder. The silence of the Rule, the darkness of the corridors, the closed cells — the monastery at night was a place of blindness and isolation. A man could walk from one end to the other and be seen by no one.

---

Chapter 12: The Cellarer's Ledger

Giacomo's storeroom occupied the ground floor of the west range, built into the cliff itself so that the back wall was living rock. It was cool and dark, smelling of wine and smoked meat. Hams hung from hooks in the ceiling. Barrels of flour and oil stood in rows. Against the far wall, a table where Giacomo kept his accounts.

"You want to see my ledger." Giacomo stood in the doorway with his arms folded. "Why?"

"Father Silvestro asked me to understand the monastery's affairs. As part of my education."

A lie, but a useful one. Giacomo scowled but could not refuse a request that invoked the abbot's name. He pulled the ledger from a drawer and dropped it on the table.

"There. Look all you like."

The entries were in Giacomo's hand — large, uneven letters, blotted with ink. Matteo turned the pages slowly. Receipts and expenditures, grain in and grain out, purchases from the valley merchants. The arithmetic was clumsy but mostly correct.

Then he found it. A column of entries marked only with a small cross instead of a description. Five entries over three months, each recording an expenditure of flour, oil, and wine. Significant quantities — enough to feed ten men for a week.

"What do these mean?" Matteo pointed.

Giacomo glanced at the page. "Charitable distributions. We give to the poor."

"Which poor?"

"The poor are the poor. Does it matter which ones?"

"It matters to the ledger."

Giacomo stepped close. He was a head shorter than Matteo but twice as wide, and his hands were the hands of a man who had spent his youth hauling barrels. "Listen to me, boy. You have been here a fortnight. You know nothing about this place, nothing about how we live, nothing about what keeps these walls standing. Do not push your nose into matters that will bite it off."

He took the ledger and left.

---

Chapter 13: The Women's House

The women's house stood across the main courtyard, separated from the monks' quarters by a wall with a single gate. Six Benedictine nuns lived there, tending the infirmary and the kitchen garden. They attended services in the church but entered through a separate door and sat behind a screen.

Matteo crossed the courtyard in the weak afternoon light and knocked on the infirmary door.

Sister Beatrice opened it. She was perhaps forty, with a square, practical face and hands that were raw from lye soap. Her habit was plain, her wimple starched to the consistency of parchment. She regarded Matteo with the unsentimental gaze of a woman who had spent twenty years treating sickness, injury, and the general stupidity of men.

"You are the new novice."

"Yes, Sister."

"What do you want?"

"I want to ask about Brother Tommaso."

She let him in. The infirmary was a long room with six beds, all empty. Dried herbs hung from the rafters — lavender, rosemary, sage. A table held mortar and pestle, jars of salve, rolls of linen bandage. Everything was clean. Everything was in its place.

"Tommaso came to me eight days before he died," Beatrice said, measuring dried chamomile into a pot with the precision of an apothecary. "He had bruises on his arms and shoulders. He said he had fallen on the stairs."

"Had he?"

"The bruises were from gripping. Someone had held him. Hard." She poured hot water over the herbs. "I told him so. He did not argue. He asked me not to tell the abbot."

"And did you?"

"I told the abbot. It is my duty." She looked at Matteo directly. "Silvestro thanked me and did nothing. That is his way. He collects information the way some men collect coins. He spends it only when it profits him."

---

Chapter 14: Night Walk

Matteo waited until the monastery was asleep. After Compline, the Rule of Silence descended like a blanket. Doors closed. Candles were extinguished. By ten o'clock the corridors were black.

He left his cell barefoot, carrying his sandals. The stone floor was ice. He moved by memory, one hand on the wall, counting doorways. Past the dormitory. Past the refectory. Down the stairs to the cloister.

Moonlight came through the arches, painting silver rectangles on the flagstones. The garden was a dark tangle of herbs and shadows. Matteo crossed it and entered the passage that led to the chapter house.

He heard them before he saw them. Two voices, low and urgent, echoing faintly off the vaulted ceiling. The chapter house door was closed but not latched. Through the crack, Matteo could see candlelight — a single flame, moving.

The words were indistinct. He pressed his ear to the wood.

"— cannot continue. He will find —"

"— dealt with. As Tommaso was dealt with —"

"— different. The boy asks questions. He watches —"

"— a novice. He knows nothing —"

The candle moved. Footsteps. Matteo pulled back from the door and flattened himself against the wall. The chapter house door opened and a figure emerged, face hidden by a cowl, moving quickly toward the east corridor. A second figure followed moments later, going west.

Matteo stood in the dark, breathing carefully. He had not seen their faces. But one of them — the second voice — had spoken with the cadence of a man accustomed to giving orders. And the phrase echoed in his mind: As Tommaso was dealt with.

---

Chapter 15: The Wanderer

He arrived at the gate two days later, in the gray hour before dawn. Brother Filippo found him collapsed against the wall outside — a tall man in a filthy Franciscan habit, his feet bare and bleeding, his face flushed with fever.

"I am Elia," the man said when they brought him inside. "Elia of Fiesole. A brother of the Friars Minor. I have walked from Siena."

"Siena is five days' walk," Filippo said. "In good weather."

"The weather was not good. And I walked faster than the pestilence, which is all that matters."

Father Silvestro came to examine the newcomer. Elia was perhaps thirty-five, with a lean, angular face and intelligent dark eyes that moved constantly, cataloging everything they saw. Despite the fever and the exhaustion, there was a controlled energy about him — the alertness of a man who has learned to sleep with one eye open.

"You claim to be a Franciscan," Silvestro said.

"I do not claim it. I am one. Though my order has seen better days." Elia smiled. It was a disarming smile. "The plague has scattered us. I seek only shelter until the roads are safe."

"And what were you doing in Siena?"

"Preaching. Teaching. The usual business of a mendicant friar." The smile widened. "Unless you fear I will corrupt your monks with radical notions about poverty. In which case I will keep my sermons to myself."

Silvestro studied him for a long moment, then nodded to Beatrice. "See to his feet. Feed him. He may stay in the guest house."

After the abbot left, Matteo lingered. Elia's eyes found him.

"You are not a monk," Elia said. "Not yet. I can tell by the way you stand. Monks learn to fold in on themselves. You still stand like a man in the open air."

"I am a novice."

"Ah. Running from something or running toward something?"

"Does it matter?"

"It always matters."

---

Chapter 16: Brother Anselmo's Warning

That evening, Anselmo summoned Matteo to the scriptorium after Compline. The old man sat in his usual chair, wrapped in a blanket against the cold, a single candle burning on the desk beside him. His clouded eyes caught the flame and held it.

"Close the door," Anselmo said.

Matteo closed it.

"You found the torn page in Tommaso's hand. You showed it to the abbot."

"The abbot took it from Tommaso's hand himself. I only watched."

"Do not split hairs with me, boy. You know what that page was."

"A passage from De Paupertate Christi. A banned text."

Anselmo nodded slowly. "What you do not know is where it came from. That text was ordered destroyed twenty years ago. Every copy in Christendom was to be burned. Pope John himself issued the decree."

"Yet a copy survived here."

"Not a copy." Anselmo's voice dropped. "The original. Written in Girolamo of Cortona's own hand. It was brought here for safekeeping by men who believed the Church was wrong to condemn it. Men who believed that the truth about Christ's poverty was worth preserving, whatever the Pope might say."

"Who brought it?"

"That is a question I will not answer. Not tonight." Anselmo reached out and gripped Matteo's wrist with surprising strength. "Listen to me carefully. The text in Tommaso's hand was torn from that original manuscript. Someone removed it from where it was kept. And now Tommaso is dead." He released Matteo's wrist. "Be very careful which doors you open in this place. Some of them close behind you."

---

Chapter 17: The Locked Cabinet

Matteo waited three nights.

On the fourth night after Anselmo's warning, he stole the librarian's key. Anselmo kept his key ring on his belt even in sleep, but the old man slept deeply after Compline, and his hearing was as poor as his sight. Matteo lifted the ring with the practiced fingers of a boy who had grown up in the crowded streets of Florence, where quick hands were a survival skill.

The scriptorium was cold and dark. He lit no candle — moonlight through the high windows gave enough light to navigate. The locked cabinet stood in its corner, solid and silent.

The key turned smoothly. The door swung open.

Inside, arranged on three shelves, were perhaps twenty volumes. Not the fine bindings of the library's official collection — these were rougher, some unbound, some wrapped in cloth. Matteo took one down and carried it to the window.

Franciscan writings. Treatises on poverty, on the Church's corruption, on the original rule of Saint Francis. Authors he had never heard of — Ubertino da Casale, Angelo Clareno, Girolamo of Cortona. The texts were dense, passionate, furious. They accused the papacy of betraying Christ's message. They called for a return to apostolic poverty — no property, no wealth, no power.

These were the writings of the Spiritual Franciscans, the radical wing of the order that had been crushed by the Pope in the 1320s. Their leaders had been excommunicated, imprisoned, burned. Their books had been destroyed.

Except here. Hidden in a monastery that should have had nothing to do with Franciscan disputes.

Matteo replaced the volumes, locked the cabinet, and returned the key to Anselmo's belt. The old man did not stir.

In his cell, lying on his narrow bed, Matteo stared at the ceiling and thought. A banned library. A torn page in a dead man's hand. A locked cabinet that someone visited at night. The questions multiplied, but the answers did not follow.

---

Chapter 18: The Second Death

Brother Marco's body was floating face-down in the rainwater cistern behind the kitchen garden.

Sister Beatrice found him at dawn. She had come to draw water for the infirmary and saw the dark shape in the water, turning slowly in the morning breeze. She did not scream. She set down her bucket, crossed herself, and went to find the abbot.

Matteo arrived with Silvestro. Two monks pulled Marco from the cistern and laid him on the flagstones. He was still in his habit, his rosary tangled around his wrist. His face was swollen, his lips blue. There was no wound visible.

"Drowned," Silvestro said.

"Perhaps." Matteo knelt beside the body. "May I?"

Silvestro nodded. Matteo examined Marco's hands. No defensive marks, no broken nails. He checked the back of the head — no bruise, no contusion. If Marco had been held under water, the killer had been strong enough or fast enough that Marco had not struggled.

Or Marco had been unconscious when he entered the water.

"He coughed," Matteo said. "He was ill. Was he taking any medicine?"

Beatrice answered from behind him. "I gave him syrup of horehound for the cough. And a sleeping draught — valerian and poppy — because the coughing kept him awake."

"A sleeping draught strong enough to make him insensible?"

"Strong enough to make him sleep. Not strong enough to make him drown." Beatrice's voice was steady, but her hands were clasped tightly at her waist. "Someone would have had to carry him here. Or lead him."

Matteo stood. The cistern was waist-high, built of stone. A conscious man would not simply fall in. But a man drugged or half-asleep, led to the edge in the dark—

Then he saw it. Floating on the surface of the water, spinning slowly among the debris of leaves and dust. A feather. Larger than the first — a tail feather this time — and the barring was different. Not peregrine.

He fished it from the water and held it up.

"Hobby," he said. "A Eurasian hobby."

Silvestro looked at him. "A second feather."

"A second bird. A second death."

---

Chapter 19: The Herbalist's Garden

Marco's workshop was a lean-to built against the south wall of the kitchen garden, where the sun was strongest. It smelled of drying herbs — mint, thyme, chamomile, feverfew — hanging in bunches from the rafters. Shelves held earthenware jars labeled in Marco's cramped handwriting: SALVIA, MELISSA, CALENDULA, ACONITUM.

Matteo searched methodically. He started with the worktable, then the shelves, then the drawers of a battered cabinet that held Marco's tools — mortar and pestle, scales, a small copper still for making tinctures.

In the third drawer, hidden beneath a stack of linen sachets, he found a leather pouch. Inside: seventeen gold florins. More money than a monk should possess. More money than most merchants carried.

He held a florin up to the light. Florentine. The lily on one side, Saint John on the other. Recently minted — the edges were still sharp.

Matteo pocketed the pouch. He continued searching and found nothing else of note — only the herbs, the tools, the quiet order of a man who had lived a careful, solitary life among his plants.

But the coins troubled him. Where had Marco gotten Florentine gold? And what had he been doing that was worth that much?

He went to the jar labeled ACONITUM. Monkshood. Wolfsbane. One of the most poisonous plants known. He opened the lid and found it half-full of dried purple flowers and finely chopped root.

A healer kept poison because poison, in small doses, was medicine. But Matteo noted the amount and filed it away.

---

Chapter 20: Suspicion Falls

It happened at supper. The monks were eating in silence, as always, while the reader droned through a passage from the Lives of the Saints. Matteo was spooning lentil soup when Giacomo stood.

The cellarer's face was red. His hands were shaking. He pointed at Matteo across the refectory.

"This man," Giacomo said, his voice filling the room, "arrived at our gate a fortnight ago. One week later, Brother Tommaso was murdered. Another week, and Brother Marco is dead. The feathers — falcon feathers — what does a falconer know better than feathers?"

The silence that followed was absolute. Every face in the room turned to Matteo.

"He brought death with him," Giacomo continued. "He came from Florence with blood already on his hands — yes, I know about the warrant. A man wanted for murder in the city comes to our monastery, and we are surprised when men start dying?"

Matteo set down his spoon. He met Giacomo's eyes and held them.

"I did not kill Brother Tommaso," he said. "I did not kill Brother Marco."

"Then who did?"

"That is what I am trying to discover."

Giacomo laughed — a short, harsh sound. "The fox investigating the henhouse. How convenient."

Father Silvestro rapped his knuckle on the table. "Enough. Both of you. This is the refectory, not a court of law." He looked at Giacomo. "Sit down."

Giacomo sat. But the damage was done. Matteo could feel it — the shift in the room, the way the monks angled their bodies away from him, the way eyes that had been curious were now afraid.

He finished his soup in silence and left.

---

Chapter 21: Confined

The next morning, Silvestro summoned Matteo to his study.

"I am confining you to the scriptorium," the abbot said. "For your own protection. Giacomo's speech last night has turned sentiment against you. If another death occurs, I cannot guarantee your safety."

"If you confine me, I cannot investigate."

"You will investigate through Brother Anselmo. He can move freely. He can ask questions that would seem natural from a seventy-six-year-old librarian." Silvestro's mouth thinned. "I need answers, Corvaro. Quickly. The community is frightened, and frightened men do foolish things."

Matteo spent the day in the scriptorium with Anselmo. The old man listened to everything Matteo had learned — the missing stores, the bruises on Tommaso's arms, the coins in Marco's workshop, the two feathers, the voices in the chapter house — and said nothing for a long time.

Then he said: "You are asking the wrong questions."

"What are the right questions?"

"You are asking who killed Tommaso and Marco. But the answer to that begins with why those texts are here. Everything in this monastery — every secret, every lie — begins with that cabinet."

---

Chapter 22: Anselmo's Story

Anselmo told the story in pieces, over the course of two days, sitting in his chair by the window while the autumn light faded and the wind rattled the shutters.

"In 1318," he began, "a group of Spiritual Franciscans fled the papal court at Avignon. They had been summoned by Pope John to answer charges of heresy. They knew what awaited them — prison, or worse. Four of them came here."

"Why here?"

"Because San Colombano was remote, because the abbot at the time was sympathetic, and because a man in Florence paid for their passage. A wealthy man. He believed in their cause — the radical poverty of Christ, the corruption of the institutional Church. He gave money to shelter them, and when they arrived, they brought their writings with them."

"The texts in the locked cabinet."

"Yes. The most dangerous library in Christendom, if you measure danger by what the Pope would do to anyone caught possessing it. The Franciscans stayed for two years, then moved on — south, to the Kingdom of Naples, where they eventually disappeared. But their manuscripts remained."

"And no one destroyed them?"

"Several abbots considered it. But the texts had become — how shall I put it — an insurance policy. As long as San Colombano possessed them, the monastery had leverage. Against the papacy, against the local bishops, against anyone who might threaten its independence." Anselmo paused. "It was a foolish bargain. Power corrupts, and secret power corrupts secretly."

"And Tommaso found out about the texts."

"Tommaso found a page. Whether he understood what he had found, I do not know. But someone decided he knew too much."

---

Chapter 23: The Tunnel

Anselmo told him about the tunnel on the second day.

"Beneath the chapter house, there is a passage cut into the limestone. It was made centuries ago — perhaps by the monks who first built this place, perhaps by someone older. It leads to a chamber deep in the cliff."

"And the manuscripts?"

"Not all of them are in the cabinet. The most dangerous — the originals, the correspondence, the ledger — are kept below."

That night, after Compline, Matteo took a candle and went to the chapter house. The room was simple — rows of stone benches arranged in a rectangle, facing inward. At the eastern end, a carved stone seat for the abbot. The floor was flagstone, like everywhere else in the monastery.

Matteo got on his hands and knees and searched. For an hour he ran his fingers along the cracks between the stones, pressing, testing. Near the abbot's chair, his thumbnail caught on something — a groove cut into the edge of a flagstone, invisible unless you were touching it. He dug his fingers in and pulled.

The stone lifted on a hidden hinge, revealing a narrow stairway descending into darkness. Air came up from below — cold, dry, carrying the faint scent of ancient stone.

Matteo descended. The stairs were steep and uneven, carved directly from the cliff. Twenty steps down, the passage leveled and opened into a vaulted chamber the size of a small chapel. The ceiling was low — Matteo could touch it with his outstretched hand — and the walls were rough limestone, sweating with moisture.

In the center of the chamber stood a stone table. On the table, wrapped in oilcloth, were the documents.

---

Chapter 24: The Archive

Matteo unwrapped the bundles carefully. Three oilcloth packages, each containing multiple documents.

The first held letters. Correspondence between the Spiritual Franciscans and their supporters, dating from 1318 to 1320. Names, dates, safe houses. A map of the route from Avignon to Tuscany. A letter from a cardinal — Matteo could not make out the name — warning that papal agents were closing in.

The second held manuscripts. The original De Paupertate Christi, the text from which Tommaso's torn page had come. Other treatises — on poverty, on the nature of the Church, on the apocalyptic prophecies of Joachim of Fiore. The handwriting varied — different authors, different decades, all bound by a common fury.

The third held a ledger. This was what stopped Matteo's breath. A record of payments — dates, amounts, and a name. The patron who had funded the Franciscans' escape, who had paid for their shelter, who had maintained the hidden archive for twenty years.

The name was Corvaro.

Not Matteo's father. His grandfather. Giovanni Corvaro, wool merchant of Florence, dead these fifteen years. The man who had built the family's modest fortune and whose portrait hung in the hall of the house on the Via dei Servi.

Matteo sat on the cold stone floor and stared at the ledger. His grandfather had funded heretics. His father had known — had sent him here, to the one place where the Corvaro name still carried weight. The debt the abbot owed was not to Matteo's father. It was to his grandfather. And it was a debt built on dangerous secrets.

---

Chapter 25: Elia's Confession

Elia found Matteo in the cloister garden the next morning. Or rather, Matteo was waiting for him.

"You are not what you seem," Matteo said.

"No one in this monastery is what they seem. It is the first thing one notices." Elia sat on the stone bench beside the dormant rosemary bushes. "But you have a specific accusation. Make it."

"You did not come from Siena. You came from the north — Avignon, or somewhere close. Your accent slips when you are tired. And your hands are not the hands of a man who has been walking for five days. They are soft. Scholar's hands."

Elia examined his fingers and smiled. "Very good. Your father taught you well."

"Who sent you?"

Elia was quiet for a moment. Then he sighed. "Cardinal Guillaume de Court. Of the papal curia in Avignon. He received intelligence that this monastery harbors texts that were ordered destroyed — texts that, if made public, could embarrass the Holy See. He sent me to find them and destroy them."

"A cardinal sent a single friar to destroy a library?"

"A cardinal sent a man who knows how to move quietly, ask the right questions, and leave no trace. I was not meant to announce myself. The fever complicated things."

"And Tommaso? Marco?"

Elia's face hardened. "I did not kill them. I am a thief, if you like — a thief of documents. Not a murderer. Whoever is killing monks in this place has their own reasons, and they are not mine."

Matteo believed him. Not because Elia was trustworthy — he wasn't — but because the timeline was wrong. Tommaso had died before Elia arrived. Whatever had set the killings in motion, Elia was not the cause.

---

Chapter 26: The Third Death

Brother Filippo was found at dawn, slumped against the gatehouse wall with his keys still in his hand. His face was purple. A leather cord — the kind used to bind manuscripts — was wound around his throat, buried so deeply in the flesh that Matteo had to cut it free.

In his mouth, placed with deliberate care on the tongue, was a feather. Smaller than the others. Matteo extracted it with two fingers and held it to the light.

"Sparrowhawk," he said. "Accipiter nisus."

Beatrice stood beside him, her face pale but her voice steady. "Three deaths. Three feathers. Three different birds."

"Someone is counting," Matteo said. "Or ranking."

The community gathered in the chapter house. Father Silvestro addressed them from his stone chair, his face grave.

"Brother Filippo served this monastery for thirty-one years. He was faithful, diligent, and kind. His death diminishes us all." He paused. "I have decided to seal the monastery. No one will enter or leave until this matter is resolved. The plague makes travel dangerous in any case. We are safer behind our walls."

Matteo caught Silvestro's eye. The abbot's face was composed, unreadable. But in that moment Matteo thought he saw something flicker behind the gray eyes — not grief, not fear, but calculation.

---

Chapter 27: The Quarantine

The great gate was barred with an oak beam. The postern door was locked. The walls of San Colombano, which had once been a comfort, became a cage.

The monastery turned inward. Monks who had walked the cloister freely now moved in clusters, watching over their shoulders. The refectory meals, once silent by rule, were now silent by fear. Even the chanting at the offices grew ragged, voices dropping out as attention wandered.

Matteo felt the change in the air. The quality of the silence shifted — from the contemplative quiet of prayer to the charged stillness of prey animals listening for the predator.

Elia made it worse. He began holding informal sermons in the cloister after supper, speaking in a low, persuasive voice about God's judgment on corrupt institutions. He never mentioned the murders directly, but the implication hung in the air like smoke: these deaths were divine punishment. Something rotten lived in San Colombano, and God was cutting it out.

The monks listened. Some crossed themselves. Others wept.

Matteo watched Elia's performance with reluctant admiration. The man was skilled. He knew exactly how much fear to feed and when to offer comfort. He was building a following — and in a sealed monastery with a killer at large, a following was power.

---

Chapter 28: Sister Beatrice's Discovery

Beatrice summoned Matteo to the infirmary on the third day of the quarantine. She had laid out three feathers on a linen cloth — the peregrine covert from Tommaso, the hobby tail feather from Marco, the sparrowhawk plume from Filippo.

"I am not a falconer," she said. "But I have treated enough hunting injuries to know birds of prey. These are not random feathers."

"No."

"Tell me what you see."

Matteo touched each feather in turn. "Peregrine falcon. Eurasian hobby. Sparrowhawk. Three different species, yes. But more than that — three different levels of the falconry hierarchy."

"Hierarchy?"

"In falconry, each bird is assigned to a social rank. The peregrine is the bird of the prince. The hobby is the bird of the young gentleman. The sparrowhawk is the bird of the priest." He paused. "Tommaso, the accountant — a man of some importance. Marco, the herbalist — younger, less senior. Filippo, the porter — a man in minor orders, practically a lay brother."

"So the killer is working down the scale?"

"Or sending a message. Each feather tells you who the victim was — their place in the order. Prince, gentleman, priest."

Beatrice was quiet for a moment. "Then the next feather will be lower still."

"If there is a next time."

"There will be," she said. "You know it as well as I do."

---

Chapter 29: The Falconer's Code

That night, Matteo sat in the scriptorium and wrote down everything his father had taught him about the Boke of St. Albans — the medieval treatise that assigned birds of prey to social ranks.

Emperor: eagle. King: gyrfalcon. Prince: peregrine. Duke: rock falcon. Earl: tiercel peregrine. Baron: buzzard. Knight: saker. Squire: lanner. Lady: merlin. Young gentleman: hobby. Priest: sparrowhawk. Knave: kestrel.

He stared at the list. Peregrine — prince. Hobby — young gentleman. Sparrowhawk — priest.

But the victims did not match their ranks. Tommaso was no prince. Marco was no young gentleman. Filippo was a priest only in the loosest sense.

Unless the ranking was not about the victims.

Matteo turned the idea over. What if the feathers were not describing the dead — but the living? What if each feather was a message from the killer, revealing something about themselves? A confession in code — I am a prince, I am a gentleman, I am a priest — disguised as a calling card.

Or — simpler — what if the ranking was descending? Prince, gentleman, priest. The killer was working through a list, and the next rank down was merlin. The bird of the lady.

The bird of a woman.

He felt cold.

---

Chapter 30: Accusations

Elia struck first.

At the community meeting in the chapter house the following morning, Elia rose from the visitors' bench and addressed the room. His voice was calm, reasonable, devastating.

"Brothers, I have held my peace because I am a guest in your house. But three of your brothers are dead, and the truth must be spoken. The feathers left on each body are the feathers of hunting birds. There is one person among us who was trained in falconry, who knows the names and habits of these birds, who carries a falconer's glove in his possessions."

Every head turned to Matteo.

"I do not accuse," Elia continued. "I observe. The murders began when this novice arrived. The feathers speak of a falconer's knowledge. And there is the matter of the warrant in Florence — a charge of murder that was never resolved."

Brother Paolo stood. "I say we lock him up. Until the roads open and a magistrate can come."

Others murmured agreement. The fear that had been building for weeks found its target. Matteo saw it on their faces — the relief of having someone to blame, the primal comfort of identifying the predator.

"Let us vote," Silvestro said. His voice was neutral. Too neutral.

The vote was fifteen to six. Matteo would be confined to the wine cellar until the quarantine ended.

Brother Luca was the last to vote no. His hand shook as he raised it.

Two monks led Matteo to the cellar. They gave him a blanket, a jug of water, and a candle. The door closed. The lock turned. Darkness settled around him like a hood pulled over a falcon's eyes.

---

Chapter 31: The Cellar

The wine cellar was cut from the cliff itself, the walls sweating limestone in the candlelight. Oak barrels lined both sides, resting on timber cradles. The air was cool and still, heavy with the smell of fermentation.

Matteo sat on the stone floor with his back against a barrel and his blanket pulled around his shoulders. The candle burned on the floor beside him, casting a circle of light that reached perhaps six feet in every direction. Beyond that, darkness.

He closed his eyes and thought.

Three dead. Three feathers. Peregrine, hobby, sparrowhawk. Descending ranks. Someone was working through a system — but what system, and why?

He had been asking who had opportunity. Now he asked a different question: who had knowledge? The falconry code was obscure. His father had taught it to him from an old book — the Boke of St. Albans, English, rare. Few people in Tuscany would know the ranking system. A nobleman, perhaps. A scholar. Someone who had read widely.

Someone like Brother Anselmo.

Or someone like Father Silvestro, who had recognized Matteo's skills immediately, who had spoken of "a hunter's eye" as though he understood what that meant.

The candle guttered. Matteo shielded it with his hand and let his mind work. He reviewed every conversation, every glance, every moment that had seemed wrong. He thought about the voices in the chapter house — the second voice, the one that spoke with authority. As Tommaso was dealt with.

Dealt with. Not killed. Not murdered. Dealt with. The word of a man who considered murder an administrative task.

---

Chapter 32: A Friend in the Dark

The scraping came at midnight — a soft sound at the base of the cellar door. Then a whisper.

"Matteo."

Luca's voice. Matteo moved to the door and knelt.

"I brought bread. And a candle." A cloth bundle slid under the gap at the bottom of the door. "And news."

"Tell me."

"Elia has taken control. He holds prayer meetings every evening in the cloister. Half the monks attend. He speaks about purification — about how the plague is God's broom, sweeping clean the corrupt. The others stay in their cells with the doors barred."

"And the abbot?"

"Silvestro watches. He attends the prayer meetings but says nothing. I think he is afraid, though he hides it well."

"Has anyone searched my cell?"

"Giacomo did. He found your father's falconry glove and showed it around the refectory. He called it proof."

Matteo pressed his forehead against the cold wood of the door. The glove. He had been a fool to keep it.

"Luca. I need you to carry a message to Brother Anselmo."

"What message?"

"Ask him about the ledger in the hidden archive. The one that records payments from Florence. Ask him who the patron was."

A pause. "Hidden archive?"

"Beneath the chapter house. There is a trapdoor near the abbot's chair. Anselmo knows. Tell him I sent you."

"I will try. But Matteo — be careful. Giacomo talks of trial. He wants to judge you himself."

"Giacomo is not a judge."

"He does not need to be. In a sealed monastery with no magistrate, power belongs to whoever takes it."

---

Chapter 33: The Ledger's Secret

Luca came back the next night. His voice through the door was different — hushed, almost frightened.

"I spoke to Anselmo. He did not want to answer. I had to tell him you had already seen the documents."

"And?"

"The patron. The man who funded the Spiritual Franciscans, who paid for their shelter here. His name was Giovanni Corvaro."

Matteo exhaled. He had known, of course. He had read the ledger himself. But hearing it spoken aloud by another voice made it real in a way that seeing it on parchment had not.

"Corvaro," Luca repeated. "That is your family. Your grandfather."

"Yes."

"Then you — your family is connected to this. The heretical texts, the secret library, the murders. Everything leads back to your name."

"That does not make me a murderer."

"No. But it makes you something worse, in the eyes of this community. It makes you a reason. If your grandfather hid those texts here, and someone is killing to protect or expose them, then your arrival did not cause the murders — but it may have triggered them. Someone saw you come and knew what your name meant."

Matteo was quiet. He thought about his father sending him here. Had the old man known? Had he understood that by sending his son to San Colombano, he was lighting a fuse?

"Luca. One more thing."

"Yes?"

"On the night Tommaso died, where was Father Silvestro?"

A long pause. "I do not know. But I will find out."

---

Chapter 34: Blood Debt

Alone in the cellar, Matteo reconstructed the history.

His grandfather, Giovanni Corvaro, had been a man of modest wealth and radical sympathies. He had funded the Spiritual Franciscans' escape from Avignon — an act of conscience, or perhaps of defiance. He had paid for their shelter at San Colombano and arranged for their manuscripts to be hidden in the cliff chamber.

Then he had died, and the secret had passed to Matteo's father, who had kept it for fifteen years without acting on it. The monastery continued to hold the manuscripts. The abbot — first Silvestro's predecessor, then Silvestro himself — maintained the bargain: silence in exchange for patronage.

But something had changed. The balance had shifted. Someone had decided that the manuscripts were no longer merely a secret to be kept — they were a weapon to be used. Or a threat to be eliminated.

Tommaso had found a page. Had he been looking for it, or had he stumbled on it? The accounts — the missing stores, the unmarked column in Giacomo's ledger — suggested something else was happening alongside the manuscript conspiracy. Money was moving through San Colombano in ways that the official records did not capture.

Two threads, then. The manuscripts and the money. Were they connected? Or were they separate problems that had collided in Tommaso's death?

Matteo pulled his blanket tighter and watched his candle burn. Somewhere above him, in the corridors and cloisters of San Colombano, a killer moved freely while he sat in the dark.

---

Chapter 35: Beatrice's Gambit

Sister Beatrice demanded an audience with the abbot. This was unusual — the nuns communicated with Father Silvestro through the prioress, a formal process that could take days. Beatrice bypassed it entirely, walking across the courtyard and pounding on Silvestro's study door with her fist.

Luca heard the argument through the wall. He reported it to Matteo that night.

"She told him Matteo must be released. She said — and this is nearly word for word — 'You sealed this monastery to catch a killer. Instead, you have caged the one man who might find him. And while he sits in your cellar, the killer is free to act again.'"

"What did Silvestro say?"

"He said the community voted. She said the community voted from fear, not reason. She said — this is the part that mattered — 'No one has died since the boy was locked up. But if someone does die while he is confined, you will have proved his innocence and you will have proved your own failure.' Then she left."

It was a gambit. Beatrice was betting that the killer would strike again, and that when he did, Matteo's imprisonment would become proof of his innocence.

It was a bet on another death.

Matteo did not know whether to admire her logic or be horrified by it.

---

Chapter 36: Released

Four days after Beatrice's gambit, Silvestro unlocked the cellar door himself.

"Come out," the abbot said. "You are free to move within the monastery."

Matteo stepped into the corridor and blinked at the light. Even the dim torches seemed blinding after the cellar's darkness. Silvestro watched him with an expression that was difficult to read — part relief, part calculation.

"What changed?" Matteo asked.

"Nothing has changed. That is the problem. Sister Beatrice pointed out that your confinement has accomplished nothing except to remove the one person with both the skill and the mandate to investigate."

"So I am reinstated."

"You were never dismissed. You were protected." Silvestro turned to go, then stopped. "The monastery is different now. Prepare yourself."

It was different. Matteo walked the corridors and barely recognized the place he had left ten days ago. Monks moved in pairs, never alone. The chapter house meetings had become nightly affairs, with Elia presiding from the abbot's chair — a presumption that Silvestro tolerated without comment. The refectory meals were served in near-darkness, candles rationed because no one would go to the storeroom alone.

Fear had become the dominant presence in San Colombano, more powerful than the abbot, more pervasive than the Rule.

And Elia fed it. His sermons — Matteo attended one that evening — were masterful. He spoke of Revelation, of the seven plagues, of the angel with the flaming sword. He drew parallels between the pestilence outside the walls and the corruption within. He never named the corruption. He did not need to. Every monk supplied his own guilt.

---

Chapter 37: The Fourth Death

Giacomo the Cellarer did not come to Matins.

This was unusual. Giacomo never missed an office — his loud, off-key singing was as reliable as the bells. Brother Paolo went to find him and came back white-faced.

The wine cellar — the same cellar where Matteo had been imprisoned — was where they found him. Giacomo sat slumped against a barrel, his legs stretched out on the stone floor, his hands folded in his lap as though he had simply fallen asleep. His face was peaceful. His lips were slightly parted. There was no mark of violence.

On his chest, placed precisely over his folded hands, lay a feather. Matteo identified it at once: merlin. The bird of the lady.

"Poison," Beatrice said after examining the body. She lifted Giacomo's hand and pointed to the fingertips, which were faintly blue. "The heart stopped. Something that acts on the heart."

"Aconitum," Matteo said. "Monkshood."

"Yes. Fast-acting in sufficient quantity. And nearly tasteless in wine."

Matteo looked at the barrel against which Giacomo was leaning. The tap was open, a thin stream of red wine still dripping into a clay cup on the floor beside the body. Someone had drawn a cup of wine, laced it with monkshood extract, and left it for the cellarer to find. Or offered it to him directly.

"He came here to drink," Matteo said. "As he always did after Compline. The killer knew his habits."

"Everyone knew his habits."

---

Chapter 38: The Poison Trail

Matteo and Beatrice went to Marco's workshop together. The herbalist's lean-to had been sealed since Marco's death, the door roped shut on Silvestro's orders. Matteo cut the rope and they went inside.

The jar labeled ACONITUM was half-empty. The last time Matteo had seen it, it had been half-full. Someone had taken a significant quantity of dried monkshood root — enough to make a lethal tincture several times over.

"How long would it take to prepare?" Matteo asked.

"The root must be soaked, then boiled to concentrate the extract. Several hours. But the preparation could be done in any kitchen with a pot and a fire."

"When was the last time this door was opened before tonight?"

"I do not know. But the rope was cleanly cut, not worn. Someone has been here recently."

Matteo examined the floor. Dust, dried leaves, a scatter of chamomile flowers near the overturned jar where he had found Marco's coins. And footprints — faint, partial, but visible in the thin layer of dirt that had accumulated since Marco's death.

Sandal prints. The same pattern as every other monk in the monastery. But the stride was distinctive — long, deliberate, the print of the left foot slightly deeper than the right, as though the wearer favored one side.

He had noticed that gait before. In the cloister, crossing the garden, moving with the deliberate economy of a man who conserved energy.

Father Silvestro walked with a slight limp on his left side. An old injury, barely visible. But enough to leave a mark in dust.

---

Chapter 39: Elia Unmasked

Matteo found Elia in the guest house, packing.

"Going somewhere?" Matteo asked from the doorway. "The gate is sealed."

Elia looked up. He had the grace to look embarrassed. "I was... organizing my belongings."

"You were preparing to leave. Through the postern gate, which you have been studying for the past week. You know where the key is kept — in the abbot's study, second drawer of his desk." Matteo stepped inside and closed the door. "But before you go, you will tell me the truth."

"I have told you the truth."

"You told me you were sent by a cardinal in Avignon. That was a lie." Matteo held up a letter he had found in the hidden archive — a letter addressed to the abbot of San Colombano, dated six months earlier, warning of an agent sent by the Albizzi banking family of Florence to locate and steal certain documents. "You came from Florence. Not Siena, not Avignon. And the people who sent you are not churchmen. They are bankers."

Elia sat down on his cot. The charm fell away from his face like a mask removed, and what remained was tired and wary.

"The Albizzi," he said. "Yes. They learned of the manuscripts through — I do not know how. An informant, perhaps. Someone in this monastery who offered to sell the information. The Albizzi want the texts not to destroy them but to use them. Leverage against the Pope. Bargaining chips in their political games."

"Who is the informant?"

"I do not know. I was sent to find the texts and remove them. The informant was to make contact with me, but after the murders began, no one came forward."

"You are a thief."

"I am a tool. The Albizzi use many tools. I am not proud of it."

Matteo studied him. Elia looked defeated — a man whose plan had been overtaken by events he could not control. Not a killer. A thief, as he said. A messenger. A pawn.

---

Chapter 40: The True Mission

"Then who is killing them?" Matteo asked.

Elia shook his head. "I have thought about it. Endlessly. At first I assumed it was connected to the manuscripts — someone protecting the secret, eliminating anyone who got too close. But the pattern does not fit. Tommaso found a page, yes. But Marco? Filippo? Giacomo? What did they know?"

"Marco had Florentine gold hidden in his workshop. Seventeen florins."

Elia's eyebrows rose. "That is interesting. Marco was not a man I would have associated with gold."

"And Giacomo's ledger showed supplies disappearing — goods unaccounted for, marked only with a cross."

"Someone was skimming. Moving resources out of the monastery for private gain. Marco received payment for his role in it — whatever that role was. And Tommaso discovered the discrepancy."

"Which gives Giacomo a motive for killing Tommaso. But Giacomo is now dead himself."

"Which means the motive is larger than theft." Elia leaned forward. "Someone in this monastery is running a scheme — embezzlement, smuggling, I do not know what — and the manuscripts are cover. The real crime is financial. The deaths are not about theology. They are about money."

Matteo stood. He had been looking at the problem from the wrong angle. The manuscripts had been a distraction — dramatic, dangerous, compelling. But the murders were practical. Someone was stealing from San Colombano and killing anyone who noticed.

And the feathers? The falconry code? A red herring — designed to point suspicion at Matteo, the falconer's son. Planted by someone who knew exactly who he was and why he had come.

Someone who had known his grandfather. Someone who had maintained the archive. Someone who controlled the monastery's finances.

---

Chapter 41: Process of Elimination

Matteo spent two days constructing a chart. He borrowed ink and parchment from the scriptorium and, in the privacy of his cell, mapped every monk's movements on the four nights of the murders.

He used what he had observed, what Luca had gathered by listening at doors, what Beatrice had noted in her infirmary records, and what Anselmo remembered from his nightly wanderings through the scriptorium.

For each murder, he listed who had opportunity — who was unaccounted for during the relevant hours. Then he cross-referenced. Who had opportunity for all four deaths?

The first murder, Brother Tommaso, narrowed the field to eleven monks who could not confirm their whereabouts. The second, Brother Marco, narrowed it further to seven. The third, Brother Filippo, to five. The fourth, Giacomo, to three.

Brother Anselmo. Brother Luca. Father Silvestro.

Matteo stared at the three names. One was his mentor. One was his friend. One was his patron. And one of them was a murderer.

---

Chapter 42: The Illuminator's Secret

Matteo went to Luca first. He found the illuminator in his workshop, bent over a page with a brush so fine it was nearly invisible, touching gold leaf onto the tongue of a painted dragon.

"Luca."

The brush stopped. Luca did not look up.

"I have been copying the banned texts," Luca said. "For Elia. He asked me — he said they were needed for scholarly purposes, that a cardinal in Avignon wanted them studied. I believed him. I am a fool."

"When did you start?"

"Three weeks after Elia arrived. He came to the workshop at night and showed me a page. He said the texts were important — historically, theologically. He offered me money. I refused the money but I copied the texts because—" Luca set down his brush and finally looked at Matteo. His eyes were red. "Because they are beautiful. The handwriting, the illuminations. Girolamo of Cortona was an artist as well as a theologian. I wanted to preserve his work."

"How did you access the texts?"

"Elia showed me where the hidden chamber was. He had found it himself — or someone had told him. I went down at night, brought up one manuscript at a time, copied it in the workshop, and returned the original before dawn."

"Did anyone see you?"

"I was careful. But—" He hesitated. "Once, I thought I saw someone in the chapter house. A shadow. I could not tell who."

"On which night?"

"The night Filippo died."

---

Chapter 43: Anselmo's Silence

Matteo sat with Anselmo in the scriptorium that evening. The old man was reading — or pretending to read, his milky eyes straining at a page held two inches from his face.

"You have been evasive," Matteo said, "about the night Brother Tommaso died."

Anselmo's hand tightened on the page. "I have told you what I know."

"You have told me what you chose to tell me. There is a difference." Matteo kept his voice gentle. He owed this man kindness — Anselmo had been his guide, his teacher, the closest thing to a friend in this place. But kindness did not change the mathematics of the chart in his cell. "On the night Tommaso was killed, you were not in your quarters. I checked. Your blanket was cold when I came to light the candles the next morning — you had not slept in it."

"I often work late in the scriptorium."

"The scriptorium was locked. You told me so yourself. And the key was on your belt."

"Then I was walking. Old men walk when they cannot sleep."

"Anselmo." Matteo leaned forward. "I am trying to save your life. If the killer follows the falconry code, the next death will use a kestrel feather — the bird of the knave, the lowest rank. That could be anyone. It could be you. Tell me what you saw."

The silence stretched. Anselmo's clouded eyes glistened in the candlelight.

"I saw him," the old man whispered. "Leaving the scriptorium. That night."

---

Chapter 44: The Librarian's Burden

"I could not sleep," Anselmo said. "The wind was bad — it howls through the cloister arches on nights like that, sounds almost human. I got up and walked to the scriptorium. I wanted to check the locks. I have always checked the locks."

"What time?"

"After Matins. Perhaps three in the morning. The corridor was dark. I moved by feel, as I always do. I came around the corner and I heard the scriptorium door close. Softly. The latch clicking home."

"And you saw someone."

"A shape. Moving away from the door. Toward the east corridor." Anselmo's voice was barely audible. "I am nearly blind, Matteo. My right eye sees shapes and shadows. My left sees nothing. But I knew the shape. The way it moved — that deliberate, measured step. I have seen it every day for twenty years."

"Who?"

Anselmo closed his eyes. "I said nothing because I believed — God forgive me — I believed the deaths were deserved. Tommaso was a meddler. Marco was corrupt. Filippo was a spy. I told myself that God had sent an instrument of justice, and it was not my place to interfere."

"Anselmo. Who did you see?"

"Father Silvestro."

The words fell into the silence like stones into still water. Matteo felt the ripples spread through everything he thought he knew.

"The abbot," Matteo said.

"The abbot. Who asked you to investigate. Who confined you when suspicion grew. Who sealed the monastery so that no one could bring in outside authority." Anselmo opened his eyes. "He has been playing you, boy. From the moment you walked through the gate."

---

Chapter 45: The Name

Matteo spent the night in his cell, assembling the pieces.

Father Silvestro. The abbot who had accepted him because of his grandfather's name. Who had recognized his skills and put them to use. Who had directed his investigation — subtly, always subtly — away from the financial irregularities and toward the manuscript conspiracy.

The manuscript conspiracy was real. The texts existed. Elia's mission was genuine. But the murders were not about the texts. They were about money.

Giacomo had been skimming — that much was true. But Giacomo was not working alone. The column of unmarked expenditures in his ledger, the goods that vanished without explanation — someone above Giacomo was directing the theft. Someone with the authority to approve expenditures and the power to silence questions.

Tommaso had found the discrepancy. He had gone to the abbot with his concerns. And the abbot had killed him.

Marco had been receiving payments — the Florentine gold — for his role in the scheme. Perhaps he prepared the medicines that were sold outside the monastery for profit. Perhaps he knew too much. Either way, he had become a liability.

Filippo controlled the gate. He saw who came and went, what was carried in and out. He had been silenced.

And Giacomo — the cellarer, the front man, the one whose ledger held the evidence — had been the last loose end.

The feathers were misdirection. Silvestro had learned the falconry code from the texts in the archive — the same archive built by Matteo's grandfather. He had used Matteo's own heritage as a weapon against him.

The elegance of it was sickening.

---

Chapter 46: The Motive

The next morning, Matteo told Beatrice and Luca. They met in the infirmary, the one place where the three of them could speak without being observed — the nuns' quarters were separate from the monks', and Silvestro rarely entered.

"He has been selling the monastery's treasures for years," Matteo said. "The silver chalice that disappeared two years ago — blamed on a traveling merchant. The reliquary of Saint Colombano — reported damaged, replaced with a copy. The gold leaf from the scriptorium — Luca, you told me yourself that supplies were always short."

Luca's face was ashen. "I thought it was poor management. Giacomo's carelessness."

"Giacomo was the instrument, not the author. Silvestro directed everything. The monastery's legitimate income goes to the diocese. But the abbot has expenses the diocese does not know about — or perhaps debts. I do not yet know what he does with the money. But I know where the evidence is."

"The hidden archive," Beatrice said.

"Not the Franciscan texts. The other ledger — the one recording the Corvaro payments. Silvestro has been keeping his own accounts in the same chamber, hidden among the manuscripts. I saw entries I did not understand at the time. Dates, amounts, initials. I thought they were part of the old records. They are not. They are current."

"So what do we do?" Luca asked.

Matteo looked at Beatrice. She looked back at him with those clear, unsentimental eyes.

"We set a trap," she said.

---

Chapter 47: The Trap

The plan was simple. Simple plans were hardest to derail — another of his father's lessons, learned in the field with hawks.

Matteo would let it be known — through Luca, who would tell Elia, who could be relied on to tell everyone — that he had found the final piece of evidence in the hidden archive. A document that proved who was responsible for the thefts and the murders. He would announce that he intended to present this evidence to the full community at the morning chapter meeting.

The killer — Silvestro — would have to act before the meeting. He would have to go to the archive to destroy the evidence, or to the scriptorium to destroy Matteo. Either way, he would have to move.

And when he did, witnesses would be waiting.

"It is dangerous," Beatrice said.

"Everything in this monastery is dangerous. At least this way, we choose the ground."

Luca delivered the message at supper. Matteo watched Silvestro's face across the refectory. The abbot ate calmly, methodically, showing nothing. But his left hand — the hand below the table — gripped the edge of the bench with white-knuckled force.

That night, Matteo went to the hidden archive alone. He lit a candle, spread documents on the stone table, and waited. Beatrice and Luca took their positions in the chapter house above, hidden behind the stone benches, within earshot of the trapdoor.

The candle burned. The silence pressed in.

Matteo waited the way his father had taught him to wait — motionless, patient, his breathing slow and even. The way you wait for a falcon to return to the fist. The way you wait for the quarry to break cover.

---

Chapter 48: The Confrontation

He came at midnight.

Matteo heard the stone slab above scrape open, then careful footsteps on the stairs. The candlelight wavered as a draft entered the chamber. A shadow appeared at the bottom of the stairs, and then Father Silvestro stepped into the light.

He carried a knife. Not a large blade — a fruit knife, the kind kept in the refectory — but sharp enough. In his other hand he held a candle of his own, the flame reflected in his gray eyes.

"I knew you would come," Matteo said. He was standing behind the stone table, the documents spread before him. His voice was steady, though his heart was hammering.

"You are clever," Silvestro said. He did not seem surprised. He set his candle on the table beside Matteo's, and the two flames threw doubled shadows on the limestone walls. "Too clever for a falconer's son. I should have sent you away when you arrived."

"Why didn't you?"

"Because your grandfather's name still carried weight. And because I needed a scapegoat. A young man with a murder charge, arriving just before the killing began — it was perfect. I even planted the feathers to point at you."

"The falconry code."

"I found it in these very manuscripts. Your grandfather's legacy provided the murder weapon and the frame." Silvestro moved around the table. Matteo moved with him, keeping the stone between them. "I underestimated you. I thought you were your father — competent but incurious. Instead, you are your grandfather. Giovanni was a man who could not leave a secret alone."

"The money, Silvestro. Where does it go?"

The abbot's face tightened. "This monastery is dying. The diocese takes ninety percent of our income. The remaining ten percent does not feed twenty-three mouths, repair a roof, replace a wall. I have been supplementing our income for fifteen years. Selling what we can spare. It is not theft — it is survival."

"Tommaso did not agree."

"Tommaso was a pedant who could not see past his ledger. He would have reported me to the bishop, and the bishop would have closed San Colombano. Every monk here would have been cast out. In a plague year, that is a death sentence."

"So you killed him."

"I made a decision." Silvestro's voice was flat. "An abbot's decision. The needs of the community against the life of one man. And then another. And another. Each one necessary. Each one a wound I will carry before God."

He lunged.

Matteo had been expecting it. He stepped back, caught his heel on the uneven floor, and stumbled. The knife passed close enough to slice the fabric of his habit. Silvestro came forward again, faster than his age suggested, and Matteo grabbed the only weapon available — the stone candlestick from the table.

He swung. The candlestick caught Silvestro's wrist and the knife clattered to the floor. The abbot cried out, clutching his hand, and Matteo kicked the knife into the darkness.

"Beatrice! Luca! Now!"

Footsteps above. The trapdoor opened. Light poured down the stairs.

---

Chapter 49: The Reckoning

They brought Silvestro up from the archive in the gray light before dawn. His wrist was broken — Beatrice bound it with linen strips, her face carefully neutral. Matteo carried the documents.

The community assembled in the chapter house. Every monk, every nun. They sat in the stone benches and watched as Matteo laid the evidence on the abbot's chair — the ledger of stolen goods, the records of sales, the correspondence with dealers in Florence who had purchased the monastery's silver and gold.

Silvestro stood in the center of the room. He held his broken wrist against his chest and looked at no one.

"Tell them," Matteo said.

The chapter house was built for sound. The vaulted ceiling, the curved walls, the stone benches — they carried every whisper, amplified every word. It was, Matteo realized, a whispering gallery. The monks in the back rows heard as clearly as those in the front.

Silvestro spoke. His voice was quiet but it reached every corner of the room.

"I sold the chalice of Saint Colombano. I sold the silver reliquary. I sold gold leaf, pigments, and two manuscripts that I judged to be duplicates. I used the money to repair the west wall, to buy grain when the harvest failed, to purchase medicine when the fever came three winters ago. Everything I took, I returned to this community in another form."

"And the murders," Beatrice said.

Silvestro closed his eyes. "Brother Tommaso discovered the discrepancy in Giacomo's ledger. He came to me. I asked him to keep silent. He refused. I— that night, in the scriptorium, I used a knife. It was quick. He did not suffer."

A sound went through the room — a collective inhalation, the sound of twenty men breathing in at once.

"Brother Marco was receiving payments from me for his silence. He grew greedy. He threatened to reveal everything unless I doubled the amount. I led him to the cistern after giving him a sleeping draught."

"Brother Filippo saw me entering the cellarer's storeroom at night. He said nothing, but I could not risk it. The porter sees everything. I strangled him with a binding cord."

"Giacomo — the cellarer — had been my partner in the scheme. But after the other deaths, he panicked. He wanted to confess to the bishop. I could not allow it."

The chapter house was silent. The monks sat frozen on their benches. Some wept. Others stared at their hands.

"I do not ask forgiveness," Silvestro said. "I ask only that you understand. This monastery would not have survived without what I did. The theft, yes. But the killing — each one was a choice I made to protect all of you. Every one of you is alive because I was willing to bear the sin."

"That was not your choice to make," Anselmo said from his bench. His voice was thin but clear. "You are an abbot, not God."

Silvestro said nothing. There was nothing left to say.

---

Chapter 50: The Open Gate

Spring came late to the Arno valley. The almond trees bloomed in the third week of March, their white flowers opening against a sky the color of washed linen. The river dropped, revealing sandbars where herons stood on one leg, patient as monks at prayer.

The plague had passed. News came from the valley — carried by a shepherd who shouted it from the cliff path — that the pestilence had moved south, toward Rome, leaving the Tuscan countryside exhausted but alive. The roads were open.

Matteo stood at the great gate of San Colombano in the early morning light. He had been awake since before Lauds, unable to sleep. The monastery was quiet behind him — a different kind of quiet now. Not the silence of fear, but the silence of exhaustion. Of recovery.

Father Silvestro was locked in the cellar where Matteo himself had been imprisoned. The monks had elected Brother Anselmo as acting abbot — a title the old man accepted with a sigh and a muttered complaint about his eyesight. Elia of Fiesole had been permitted to leave with his belongings and a stern warning never to return. The manuscripts in the hidden archive remained where they were. Anselmo had decided — and the community had agreed — that the texts would stay. They were part of San Colombano's history, even the dangerous parts.

"You are leaving," Beatrice said behind him.

"Yes."

"Where will you go?"

Matteo looked out over the valley. The morning mist was lifting, revealing the patchwork of fields and orchards below. Smoke rose from a farmhouse. A dog barked, the sound carrying clear and sharp in the still air.

"Florence," he said. "The warrant is old, and the witnesses were Saltini's men. With Saltini dead of the plague, there is no one to press the charge." He paused. "My father will want to know what happened here."

"And the monastery?"

"The monastery will stand. Anselmo is wiser than Silvestro, if less ambitious. And Brother Luca — Luca will make this place beautiful again."

Beatrice stood beside him. She smelled of lavender and lye soap — the scents of the infirmary, of healing.

"You could stay," she said. "Take your vows. Become what you pretended to be."

Matteo reached into his bag and took out his father's falconry glove. He pulled it on — the old calfskin settling over his hand like a second skin, scarred and stained, shaped by years of use.

"I was never meant for walls," he said.

He lifted the oak beam from the gate and pushed it open. The hinges groaned. Morning air flooded in — cold, clean, carrying the smell of wet earth and new grass.

He walked out. The cliff path descended before him, the stone steps worn smooth by centuries of feet. Below, the valley spread wide and green and alive.

Above him, riding the updraft from the warming cliff face, a peregrine falcon turned in a slow, perfect circle. Its wings were steady, its flight effortless — a creature made entirely of purpose and air.

Matteo watched it until it disappeared into the light. Then he shouldered his bag and began to walk down.
