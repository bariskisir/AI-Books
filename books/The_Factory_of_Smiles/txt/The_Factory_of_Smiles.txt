Chapter 1: The Alarm

Joy woke to the chime. It was bright and melodic, designed to produce a positive emotional response. Three ascending notes repeated until she touched the snooze bar. She lay in the dark and counted to ten. This was her ritual. The only thing she still controlled.

Her apartment was small and white. A bed, a table, a mirror. The mirror was mandatory. The Ministry recommended starting each day with a smile. Joy looked at her reflection and did not smile. She brushed her teeth, dressed in her gray uniform, and stepped into the hallway.

The corridor lights flickered on automatically. Motion sensors. Everything in Optima was efficient. Everything was monitored. She walked past the neighbors' doors. No one spoke. Talking before seven was against the community harmony guidelines.

The elevator played the same jingle as her alarm. Three ascending notes. Joy pressed the ground-floor button and watched the numbers change. She had worked at the Factory of Smiles for three years. She had never missed a shift. She had never questioned a protocol.

Today was different. She did not know it yet. But something in the air was wrong. A thickness. A weight. The smell of something sweet that should have been sour.

The elevator doors opened. The street was yellow. Yellow banners, yellow awnings, yellow uniforms. Optima was the happiest city in the world. Everyone said so. The Ministry said so.

Joy stepped into the light and began her walk to the factory.

Chapter 2: The Commute

The smile-check kiosks stood on every corner. White booths with glass screens and a camera lens shaped like a happy face. Citizens approached them one by one. They placed their chins on the rest. They smiled. The screen flashed green or red.

Green meant you passed. Red meant you were summoned for a mood evaluation.

Joy watched a man in a gray coat approach a kiosk ahead of her. He was middle-aged, tired. His shoulders sagged. He placed his chin on the rest. He tried to smile. His mouth moved but his eyes did not. The screen flashed red.

Two officers emerged from a van. They took the man by the elbows, gently. They smiled as they did it. The man did not resist. Resistance was a mark of non-compliance. Non-compliance meant a longer evaluation.

The van doors closed. The kiosk reset. The next citizen stepped forward.

Joy's turn came. She placed her chin on the warm plastic rest. She looked into the smiling camera lens. She remembered how to smile. Three years of factory work had taught her the mechanics. Corners of the mouth up. Cheek muscles tightened. Eyes crinkled just enough.

The screen flashed green.

She walked on. Behind her, the kiosk chimed its approval. Ahead of her, the factory rose against the pale sky. Tall, windowless, painted the color of weak tea. Smoke rose from its stacks. The smoke was the same color as the clouds. Everything in Optima matched.

Chapter 3: Assembly Line Seven

The factory floor was vast and white. Fluorescent lights hummed overhead. The air was cool and dry and smelled of disinfectant. Assembly Line Seven stretched from the wall of hoppers to the packaging bay, a hundred meters of gleaming metal rollers.

Joy took her position at Station Fourteen. Her job was simple: inspect each smile-cube for defects, polish it with a soft cloth, and place it in the approved bin. The cubes were small, palm-sized, made of a translucent material that caught the light. They looked like candy. They smelled of nothing, officially. But Joy had always detected something underneath the chemical cleaner. A sweetness. A warmth. Like the memory of a summer afternoon you could not quite recall.

The conveyor belt started. The cubes moved past her. She picked one up. It was warm. Warmer than it should have been. She turned it over in her fingers. The light inside it shifted, pale yellow to pale pink and back again.

She polished it. She placed it in the bin. She reached for the next one.

The woman beside her, Mari, worked faster. Mari always worked faster. Her hands were a blur of motion. She never looked at the cubes. She never paused. Mari had been on Line Seven for eight years. Mari smiled while she worked. Mari always smiled.

"Do you ever wonder where they go?" Joy asked.

Mari's smile did not waver. "The outer districts. Everyone needs happiness."

"But where do they come from?"

Mari's hand paused. Just for a second. "The hoppers."

Joy looked at the wall of hoppers at the head of the line. Silver cylinders, floor to ceiling. Pipes ran from them to the conveyor. The source. The beginning.

She had never seen what went into the hoppers.

Chapter 4: Morning Inspection

Supervisor Voss arrived at exactly 8:03. He walked the length of the assembly line with measured steps, his black shoes clicking on the white floor. He was tall and thin, with pale skin and darker hollows under his eyes. His uniform was pressed. His expression was flat.

He carried a handheld compliance meter. A silver wand with a blinking blue light. He stopped at each station and pointed the wand at the worker. The wand measured smile-output. Measured enthusiasm. Measured the quality of the emotional labor being performed.

He stopped at Mari. The wand blinked green. "Excellent, Mari. Your numbers are exemplary."

Mari beamed.

He moved to Joy. The wand hovered near her chest. She kept her face neutral. She did not smile. She did not frown. She waited.

The wand blinked yellow.

Voss lowered it. He looked at her. His eyes were the color of slate. "Your enthusiasm metrics are declining, Mendes. Three consecutive days of yellow readings."

"I'm focused on quality," Joy said.

"Focus on your face. The face is the product. The cubes are secondary." He tapped the wand against his palm. "I expect green tomorrow."

He moved on. Joy watched him go. Mari shot her a sympathetic look. Joy picked up another cube. It was warm. Warmer than before.

She polished it. She placed it in the bin. She thought about the hoppers and the pipes and the sweet smell that came from the vents when the machines were running at full capacity.

Chapter 5: The Smell

It came at mid-morning. A shift in the air. Joy caught it between one breath and the next. A scent that did not belong. Metallic, but not like iron. Organic, but not like anything alive. It drifted from the ventilation grate near her feet.

She looked down. The grate was square, about thirty centimeters across, bolted to the floor. The smell was strongest there. She bent to tie her shoe and let her face hover near the metal.

It hit her. Sweet. Thick. Alive.

She straightened. No one had noticed. Mari was in her rhythm. The other workers were focused on their stations. The supervisors were clustered at the far end of the line.

Joy worked until the first break. The bell rang. The conveyor stopped. She walked to the lunchroom and sat alone.

The food was gray and bland and nutritious. She ate without tasting. She thought about the smell. She had noticed it before. Everyone had. But no one talked about it. The Ministry discouraged speculation. Speculation was a form of negative thinking.

A worker from Line Three sat down across from her. His name was Aldric. He had been at the factory for five years. He looked tired.

"You smell it too?" he asked.

Joy nodded.

"It's getting stronger. Every week, stronger."

"What is it?"

Aldric looked around. The lunchroom was full. Workers ate in silence or spoke in low murmurs. The supervisors' table was in the corner.

"I don't know," he said. "But I think it's coming from below."

Chapter 6: The Mimic

After lunch, Joy watched herself smile. It was strange to observe her own face from the inside, to feel the muscles move without the emotion behind them. She practiced as she returned to her station. Corners up. Cheeks tight. Eyes crinkled.

She passed a mirror in the hallway. The woman looking back at her was smiling. Joy felt nothing.

The afternoon shift was harder than the morning. The fluorescent lights seemed brighter. The conveyor belt seemed faster. The cubes moved past her in an endless stream. She polished and placed. Polished and placed.

Mari talked through the work. She talked about the weather, the new cafeteria menu, the upcoming Day of Gratitude celebration. Her voice was bright and steady. She used words like "wonderful" and "delightful" and "incredible." She never said anything negative. She never paused for breath.

"Yes," Joy said. "Wonderful."

She heard the word leave her mouth and did not recognize her own voice. It was too high. Too bright. She was mimicking. She was doing what Mari did. What everyone did.

She stopped talking. Mari did not notice.

The cube in Joy's hand was warm. Almost hot. She turned it over. The light inside swirled, color shifting from yellow to a deep, bruised purple. She had never seen that before.

She put it in the reject bin. The reject bin went back to the hoppers.

She wondered what happened to the defective cubes. Whether they were melted down. Whether the warmth in them was something that could be unmade and made again.

Chapter 7: The Audit

At four o'clock, Joy was called to the compliance office. Voss was there, sitting behind a metal desk. A file folder lay open in front of him. Her file.

"Close the door."

She closed it. The office was small. No windows. A single light panel in the ceiling. Voss's face was half in shadow.

"Your quarterly smile audit is due," he said. "We'll conduct it now."

He produced the compliance meter and pointed it at her. The blue light blinked. She smiled. The meter blinked green. He wrote something in the file.

"Your volume is passing, but your authenticity score is low." He looked at her. "People can tell the difference, Mendes. The outer districts have buyers who test for authenticity. They can taste whether a cube is real or manufactured."

"The cubes are manufactured," Joy said.

"The emotion inside them is real. The vessel is not. We need the vessel to feel authentic." He closed the file. "Stay after your shift tonight. We'll do remedial training."

"The night shift?"

"You'll work the training during the night shift. Report to Room Seven at seven o'clock."

Joy nodded. She left the office. Her hands were cold.

The night shift was different. Fewer supervisors. Fewer workers. The building was quieter. The machines hummed at a lower frequency. She had never worked the night shift before.

She wondered what Room Seven was. She wondered what remedial training involved.

She walked back to her station and picked up the next cube. It was cold.

Chapter 8: The Collapse

The news came at five-thirty. A worker on Line Three had collapsed. Cardiac event, they said. The emergency team carried her out on a stretcher. Her face was blue. She was not smiling.

The line stopped for seven minutes. Seven minutes of silence. The supervisors stood at attention. The workers stared at the empty space where the woman had been.

Then the line started again. The cubes moved. The workers worked.

Aldric leaned toward Joy during the restart. "That's the third one this month."

"Third what?"

"Collapse. Always on Line Three. Always near the hoppers."

Joy looked at the wall of silver cylinders at the head of Line Three. They were identical to the hoppers on Line Seven. But the pipes that ran from them were thicker. Darker. There were warning labels she had never noticed.

"What's in the hoppers?" she asked.

Aldric shook his head. "Nobody who knows is talking. Nobody who talks is still here."

The bell rang. Shift change. Joy gathered her things. She was supposed to report to Room Seven at seven. She had two hours. She walked toward the exit, then stopped.

She turned and walked back toward Line Three.

The hoppers were unattended. The night crew had not arrived yet. She stood in front of the nearest cylinder and touched the metal. It was warm. The same warmth as the cubes. She pressed her ear to the surface.

She heard something. A hum. A pulse. A heartbeat.

She stepped back. Her own heart was pounding. She walked away quickly, without looking back.

Chapter 9: The Lock

Room Seven was in the administrative wing. A white door with a number plate. Joy arrived at seven o'clock exactly. The door was unlocked.

Inside was a classroom. Chairs in rows. A screen at the front. Voss stood by the screen, holding a remote.

"Sit," he said.

She sat in the front row. He clicked the remote. The screen lit up with a diagram of a human face. Muscles and tendons. The mechanics of a smile.

"Authenticity is produced by the zygomaticus major and the orbicularis oculi," Voss said. "The mouth and the eyes. Most people can fake the mouth. The eyes are harder."

He clicked. The diagram changed. A cross-section of a face, smiling.

"To produce an authentic smile, you need to engage both muscle groups simultaneously. The mind follows the body. If you make the correct facial expression long enough, the corresponding emotion will follow."

Joy watched the screen. The smiling face was glowing green. She thought of the blue-faced woman on Line Three.

"Your authenticity score is low because your eyes are not participating," Voss said. "You need to think of something that makes you genuinely happy."

"I can't."

"Then invent something. Memory is malleable. Create a happy memory and hold it in your mind while you smile."

He looked at her. She looked at him. Neither of them smiled.

"There's a reason you were assigned to my line, Mendes. You're more observant than the others. I've seen you watching the hoppers. I've seen you smelling the vents."

Joy said nothing.

"Curiosity is valuable. But it is also dangerous. Keep it contained." He turned off the screen. "Practice the smile. Report back tomorrow."

She left Room Seven. The corridor was empty. The night shift was in full operation somewhere in the building. She could feel the vibration through the floor.

She walked toward the exit. Halfway there, she stopped. The ventilation grate was near her feet. The smell was stronger at night. She crouched and looked through the slats.

She saw nothing but darkness.

She kept walking.

Chapter 10: The Night Shift

Joy did not go home. She stood outside the factory and watched the building breathe. Lights flickered in the upper windows. Smoke rose from the stacks. The smoke was thick and dark against the night sky.

She had two hours before her remedial training session. She walked around the perimeter of the factory. The walls were smooth and windowless at ground level. No doors except the main entrance. No access points.

She found a delivery bay at the rear. Roll-up doors. A loading dock. Trucks parked in a row, their engines silent. The bay doors were locked.

She circled back to the front. A guard stood at the entrance, arms crossed, face blank. She approached.

"I forgot something inside," she said.

The guard looked at her. "Shift is over. Come back in the morning."

"I have night training. Room Seven."

He checked a clipboard. "Name?"

"Joy Mendes."

He found her name and nodded. "You're cleared for the admin wing only. Stay out of the production floor."

She entered. The lobby was empty. The lights were dim. The air was cooler than outside. She walked past the elevator and took the stairs.

The stairwell was concrete and bare bulbs. Her footsteps echoed. She climbed to the second floor, where the training rooms were. But she stopped on the landing between floors.

A door. Not marked. AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL ONLY. And below it, a small sign she had never seen before: MAINTENANCE ACCESS — LEVEL B1.

She tried the handle. Locked.

She looked around. No cameras in the stairwell. No one watching. She pulled her hairpin from her uniform pocket. Her father had taught her when she was twelve. A useless skill, he'd said. You never know when a useless skill becomes useful.

She bent the pin and worked it into the lock. The mechanism was old. Simple. It clicked open.

She pushed the door and stepped into the dark.

Chapter 11: The Descent

The stairs went down. Concrete steps, narrow, without railings. The light dimmed with each landing. The air grew warmer. The smell grew stronger.

Joy counted the steps. Twenty. Forty. Sixty. At the bottom, another door. No sign. Just metal, gray, streaked with condensation.

She pushed it open.

The room was large. Low ceiling. Fluorescent tubes flickered overhead. The floor was concrete, stained dark in places. The walls were lined with pipes. Thousands of pipes, bundling together and separating again, running from floor to ceiling.

And along the walls, reclining chairs.

She counted them. Twenty on the left wall. Twenty on the right. Forty chairs, each holding a person. Their eyes were open. Their faces were slack. Electrodes were attached to their temples, thin wires running from the chairs to the pipes.

The pipes were full of light. A soft, pulsing glow, moving through the tubes like blood.

Joy walked forward. Her feet made no sound. The people in the chairs did not move. They breathed. Shallow. Regular. In and out.

She stopped at the first chair. A man, maybe fifty. His eyes were pale blue. They did not track her. They stared at nothing. She touched his hand. It was warm. The same warmth as the cubes.

The pipes pulsed. The glow brightened.

She pulled her hand back. The pulse slowed.

She walked down the row. Woman. Man. Woman. Young, old. Different faces, same emptiness. Same slack expression. Same open eyes seeing nothing.

At the far end of the room, a terminal. A screen. A keyboard. She sat and touched the keyboard. The screen lit up.

WELCOME, ADMINISTRATOR. PLEASE ENTER CREDENTIALS.

She typed: GUEST.

ACCESS DENIED.

She typed: MAINTENANCE.

ACCESS DENIED.

She tried the most obvious password she could think of. HAPPINESS.

ACCESS GRANTED.

The screen filled with data.

Chapter 12: The Protocol

The technical logs scrolled in green text on a black screen. Joy read them with a growing coldness in her chest.

EMOTIONAL HARVESTING PROTOCOL — PHASE 7.
SUBJECTS: 47 ACTIVE, 12 DECEASED, 8 RETIRED.
DAILY OUTPUT: 3,400 UNITS.
CONVERSION RATE: 92%.
NOTES: Subjects in quadrant B show declining yield. Recommend rotation.

She scrolled further. Diagrams. Flow charts. The process was simple. Electrodes on the temporal lobes stimulated the amygdala. Emotional responses were triggered by targeted memory cues. The resulting neural signals were amplified, converted, and piped through the tubes to the hoppers on the production floor.

The hoppers did not contain raw materials. They contained people.

Joy stared at the screen. The cursor blinked. The pipes hummed. Behind her, the chairs held their silent occupants.

She opened a file labeled SUBJECT 47.

Name: Elara Voss.
Age: 34.
Admission date: 14 months ago.
Status: Active. High yield.
Notes: Subject demonstrates exceptional emotional range. Memories of family trauma produce reliable despair response. Recommend continued use without modification.

Joy closed the file. She sat in the dark with her hands on her knees.

Voss. The same last name as Supervisor Voss.

She thought of his flat face. His slate-colored eyes. His absolute loyalty to the Ministry.

She thought of the woman in the corner chair. The one whose tears ran ceaselessly down her face.

She stood up. She walked to the corner chair. The woman there was thin, pale, her hair cropped short. Her eyes were open. Her cheeks were wet. Her lips were slightly parted.

Joy knelt beside the chair. She took the woman's hand.

"I'm sorry," she said.

The woman did not respond. But the electrodes pulsed. Joy felt something pass through her. A wave of grief. Not her own. A stranger's grief, borrowed and amplified.

She let go. She stood up. She walked to the door and climbed the stairs and did not look back.

She had a training session in Room Seven in forty minutes. She would attend it. She would smile.

And then she would decide what to do.

Chapter 13: The Morning After

Joy did not sleep. She lay in her bed and stared at the white ceiling and thought about the forty-seven chairs. She thought about the pipes full of light and the terminal full of lies. She thought about Elara Voss and her ceaseless tears.

The alarm chimed. Three ascending notes. She touched the snooze bar and counted to ten. The ritual felt obscene now.

She dressed. She walked to the factory. The smile-check kiosk flashed green. She did not remember smiling.

On Assembly Line Seven, the cubes moved past her. She picked one up. It was warm. She knew what the warmth was now. She knew why the defective cubes turned purple.

She put it in the bin. She picked up the next one.

Mari was talking. "The Day of Gratitude is next week. I heard Minister Harcourt himself will be at the factory. Can you believe it?"

"No," Joy said.

Mari kept talking. Joy worked. The hours passed. The smell from the vents was thicker now. She breathed through her mouth.

At lunch, she found Aldric in the cafeteria. She sat across from him.

"I went downstairs," she said.

His fork stopped halfway to his mouth. He put it down. "Downstairs where?"

"The basement. B1."

Aldric's face went pale. He looked around. The room was full, but no one was watching them. "You're lying."

"I'm not. I saw the chairs. I saw the pipes. I saw the terminal."

Aldric leaned forward. His voice was a whisper. "How far did you get?"

"Forty-seven chairs. Operating logs. Phase Seven of the Emotional Harvesting Protocol."

"Jesus." Aldric pressed his palms against the table. "You need to leave. Right now. Go home, pack nothing, and walk out of the city."

"Why?"

"Because they know when someone has been in the basement. There are sensors. There are logs."

Joy felt her stomach tighten. "Then why am I still here?"

"Because Voss hasn't decided what to do with you yet."

She looked toward the supervisors' table. Voss was not there. His chair was empty.

Chapter 14: The Proposal

The afternoon shift ended. Joy gathered her things. She expected a summons. She expected guards. But no one stopped her at the door.

She walked home through the yellow streets. The banners fluttered in the breeze. HAPPINESS IS PRODUCTIVITY. GRATITUDE IS DUTY. SMILE IS THE LAW.

She closed her apartment door and leaned against it. The room was small. White. Quiet.

A knock.

She opened it. Voss stood in the hallway. He was not in uniform. He wore a gray coat and carried no compliance meter.

"May I come in?"

She stepped aside. He entered. He stood in the center of her small room and looked at the bare walls, the single window, the mandatory mirror.

"You read the logs," he said.

"Yes."

"You saw the subject files."

"Yes."

He turned to face her. His face was the same as always. Flat. Unreadable. "I know you went to the basement. The system flagged your access."

"Then why am I not in a chair?"

"Because I did not report it."

Joy stared at him. "Why?"

Voss sat on the edge of her bed. He looked tired. Older than he did under the factory lights. "Because I have been waiting for someone to find it. Someone who would not break."

"The woman in the corner chair. Elara."

His jaw tightened. "She is my sister."

"I know."

"She was a journalist. She found evidence of the harvesting program and brought it to the Ministry. They offered her a choice: silence in the basement or silence in a grave." He paused. "I made the call. I had her committed. I told myself it was mercy."

"It was not mercy."

"No. It was cowardice." He looked at her. "You have two days. The Day of Gratitude broadcast is the only time the Ministry turns its attention outward. If you want to expose them, that is your window."

"What about you?"

"I will not help you. I will not stop you. That is the best I can offer."

He stood and walked to the door. He paused. "Elara's memories are in the system. The ones they use for harvesting. She remembers a lake. A yellow house. A dog named Beano. That is who she was."

He left.

Joy sat on the floor and did not move for a long time.

Chapter 15: The Contact

The next morning, Joy skipped the smile-check kiosk. She walked past it without slowing. The camera tracked her. She did not care.

She went to the data archives instead of the factory floor. A narrow building attached to the main complex, filled with filing cabinets and humming servers. The archivist was a woman named Mira, thin and sharp-eyed, with ink stains on her fingers.

Mira looked up when Joy entered. "You're not supposed to be here."

"I know."

"You're supposed to be on Line Seven."

"I know."

Mira studied her. Then she motioned to a chair. "Sit."

Joy sat. She told Mira about the basement. The chairs. The pipes. The terminal. The protocol. The subjects.

Mira listened without interruption. When Joy finished, Mira folded her hands.

"I know," Mira said.

"You know?"

"I've known for two years. The numbers don't add up. Output exceeds input. The factory produces more happiness than the population can generate. The math only works if there's another source."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"Because I wanted to find the right person to tell." Mira opened a drawer and pulled out a folder. "There are others. People who know. People who want to act. We meet tomorrow night. I'll send you the address."

"What do I do until then?"

Mira smiled. It was not a happy smile. "Go back to Line Seven. Work your shift. Smile when they tell you to. And do not go into the basement again. They will be watching now."

Joy returned to the factory floor. The cubes moved past her. She polished and placed. She thought about the meeting. She thought about Elara's yellow house and her dog named Beano.

That afternoon, a new worker replaced the woman who had collapsed on Line Three. She was young. She smiled too wide. She looked at the hoppers and did not seem to see them at all.

Chapter 16: The Numbers

That night, Joy could not sleep. She sat at her table with a piece of paper and wrote down everything she remembered from the terminal log.

Forty-seven subjects. Twelve deceased. Eight retired. Daily output of 3,400 units. Ninety-two percent conversion rate.

She did the math. Each subject produced roughly seventy-two units per day. The factory employed six hundred workers on three shifts. Each worker processed approximately two hundred cubes per shift.

If every cube contained emotion harvested from the basement subjects, then the numbers did not work. There were not enough subjects. There was not enough output.

Which meant the harvesting was not the only source. It was a supplement. A boost. A way to make the factory's happiness stronger, more addictive, more effective.

The cubes on the line were inauthentic. But they still worked. The chemically harvested emotion enhanced them. Made the consumer dependent. Made them need more.

She wrote: The smile-cubes are a delivery system. For what?

She did not have the answer. But Mira would.

She folded the paper and put it in her pocket. The alarm was set for five. She lay down and closed her eyes.

She dreamed of a yellow house. A dog named Beano. A woman whose tears ran ceaselessly down her face.

She woke before the alarm.

Chapter 17: The Garage

The address was in the old district, where the buildings were brick and the streets were cobbled and the Ministry's cameras were fewer. Joy found it at dusk: a garage behind a boarded-up bakery. No sign. No light.

The door was unlocked. She pushed it open.

Inside, twelve people sat in a circle on wooden crates. A single bulb hung from the ceiling. The walls were lined with shelves full of rusted tools.

Mira was there. She nodded at Joy. "Sit."

Joy sat. The others looked at her. A maintenance engineer in a stained coverall. A woman with a scar on her cheek. A young man with quick eyes. A delivery driver in a company jacket. They looked tired and hard and afraid.

Mira introduced them. Casper, the maintenance engineer. Wren, who drove trucks between districts. The others gave only first names or none at all.

"We are the Underground," Mira said. "We have been watching the factory for eighteen months. We know about the basement. We know about the protocol. We know about Subject 47 and the other forty-six."

"Why haven't you acted?" Joy asked.

Casper spoke. "Because the Ministry controls the broadcast. The newspapers. The public address system. We could shout the truth from every corner of Optima and no one would hear. The speakers would play music. The screens would show cartoons. The truth would disappear."

Wren leaned forward. "But the Day of Gratitude broadcast is different. It's live. The whole city watches. Minister Harcourt himself speaks. If we could interrupt that feed — even for thirty seconds — people would see."

"See what?"

"The basement. The chairs. The electrodes. The truth."

Joy looked at Mira. "You have footage?"

"We have plans. We have access. We have the knowledge. What we don't have is someone inside the broadcast center."

The room went quiet. Twelve faces turned toward her.

"You work the stage crew for the Day of Gratitude," Mira said. "I checked the assignments. You're on the list."

Joy had not known. Had not volunteered. But Voss had known. Voss had put her there.

She met Mira's eyes. "What do I need to do?"

Chapter 18: The Plan

The plan was simple in theory. Wren would smuggle a data chip into the broadcast center. Casper would disable the backup generator. Mira would be at a terminal in the archives, ready to patch the feed. And Joy would be behind Camera Two, ready to switch the signal.

"Thirty seconds," Mira said. "That's all we get before they cut us. Thirty seconds of the basement. Thirty seconds of the chairs. Thirty seconds of Elara Voss."

"That's not enough time," the young man said.

"It's enough to plant a seed. People will talk. The guards will not be able to arrest everyone who saw it. The truth will spread."

Joy nodded. "When do we start?"

"Tomorrow. The broadcast is in four days. We have one chance to rehearse."

The meeting ended. The twelve people dispersed into the night. Joy walked home through the old district, past the boarded-up shops and the quiet houses. The cameras here were fewer but not absent. She kept her head down.

At her apartment, she sat on the bed and looked at the mandatory mirror. The woman looking back at her was not smiling. She looked like someone who had not slept in a week. She looked like someone who had seen the basement.

She touched the glass. "Four days," she said.

The mirror gave no answer.

Chapter 19: The Rehearsal

The broadcast center was a glass building in the center of Optima, shaped like a sunburst. Joy reported for stage crew rehearsal at eight in the morning. The lobby was polished marble. The wall behind the reception desk read THE MINISTRY OF HAPPINESS — TODAY IS THE HAPPIEST DAY.

A production assistant led her to the studio. The set was a stage with velvet curtains and a podium shaped like a smiling face. Cameras stood in three positions. Technicians adjusted lights and tested microphones.

"You'll be on Camera Two," the assistant said. "You'll move it during Harcourt's speech, following him as he walks to the center of the stage. Simple."

"Simple," Joy repeated.

She took her position behind Camera Two. The camera was heavy, on a wheeled dolly. She practiced the movement. Left, then forward, then stop. Left, then forward, then stop. Over and over.

Minister Harcourt arrived at noon. He was shorter than he appeared on screens. His hair was perfect. His teeth were white. His smile never wavered.

He walked through the studio, shaking hands, patting shoulders, laughing at jokes that were not funny. The technicians smiled back. The production team smiled. Everyone smiled.

When he reached Joy, he took her hand. "And what is your name?"

"Joy Mendes."

"Perfect," he said. "A smiling face for a happy city."

She smiled. He moved on. She watched him work the room. Every gesture was calculated. Every laugh was timed. He was the most miserable man she had ever seen.

Rehearsal continued until six. Joy pushed Camera Two through its paces. She memorized the angles. She noted the cable runs. She spotted the panel where the main video feed was routed.

She left at six-thirty. Wren was waiting outside, leaning against a delivery van.

"How was it?" Wren asked.

"He touched my hand."

"Which one?"

"The right. I'll burn it later."

Wren laughed. It was a genuine laugh. Joy had not heard one in days. "Come. I have something for you."

The back of the van contained a crate labeled SMALL PARTS — MINISTRY OF HAPPINESS. Inside was a data chip, wrapped in foam.

"Everything Mira has on the basement," Wren said. "Edited to thirty seconds. Clear. Raw. Unforgettable."

Joy took the chip. It was small and light. It felt heavier than it should.

"Day after tomorrow," Wren said. "You know what to do."

Joy nodded. She put the chip in her pocket and walked home through the darkening streets.

Chapter 20: The Duty

The next day, Joy went to work as if nothing had changed. She walked past the smile-check kiosk. Green. She took her position on Assembly Line Seven. The cubes moved past her. She polished and placed.

Mari talked about the Day of Gratitude. She had made a banner. She was going to wave it during the broadcast. She had sewn it herself.

"That's wonderful," Joy said.

"You should make one too! We can wave them together."

"Maybe."

The supervisor on duty was not Voss. It was a woman named Tess, thick-armed and humorless. She carried a compliance meter and used it often. The wand blinked green for everyone except Joy. For Joy, it blinked yellow.

"Focus, Mendes," Tess said.

"Sorry."

"She's been distracted lately," Mari said. "Probably just pre-broadcast nerves."

Tess moved on. Joy picked up the next cube. It was hot. She dropped it. It shattered on the floor.

The light inside it bled out, purple and viscous, pooling on the white tiles.

Everyone stared.

Tess walked over. She looked at the broken cube. She looked at Joy. "Clean it up. Report to Voss's office at end of shift."

Joy knelt and wiped the purple liquid with a rag. It was warm. It smelled of nothing. It smelled of everything.

At end of shift, she walked to Voss's office. She knocked. He opened the door.

"Come in."

She sat. He closed the door.

"You broke a cube," he said.

"It was defective. It was hot."

"You've been on that line for three years. You know defective cubes go in the reject bin."

"I dropped it."

Voss sat behind his desk. He looked at her for a long moment. "The broadcast is in two days."

"I know."

"Is there anything you want to tell me?"

Joy met his eyes. "No."

He held her gaze. Then he nodded. "Go home. Get some rest."

She left. She could feel his eyes on her back.

Chapter 21: The Underground

The second meeting was in a basement on Spinner Street. Fourteen people now. Two new faces. The word was spreading.

Mira stood at the front. "We have the chip. We have the access. We have the timing. Tomorrow night, we do a final walkthrough. The night after, the broadcast."

Casper raised his hand. "There's a problem."

Everyone turned.

"The backup generator. I was wrong about disabling it. It's not a standalone unit. It's tied to the main grid. If I cut it, the entire district goes dark. The broadcast goes dark. We lose our window."

"Then we don't cut it," Wren said.

"Then when they cut us off, they cut us off. No second chance."

Mira considered. "We only need thirty seconds. If the chip plays, the damage is done. They can cut the feed, but people will have seen."

"Some people," the young man said. "The ones watching live. What about the rest?"

"Word of mouth. The Ministry can't arrest everyone."

The discussion continued. Joy listened. Her mind was elsewhere. She kept thinking about the broken cube. The purple light. The warmth. She kept thinking about Voss's question: Is there anything you want to tell me?

She wondered if Voss would try to stop her. She wondered if she would stop herself.

After the meeting, Mira pulled her aside. "You're quiet."

"I'm thinking."

"Don't think too much. Thinking leads to doubt. Doubt leads to hesitation. Hesitation gets people killed."

"I know."

Mira put a hand on her shoulder. "Tomorrow night. Final run-through. Be there."

Joy walked home. The streets were empty. The yellow banners hung limp in the still air. HAPPINESS IS PRODUCTIVITY. She had seen those words every day for three years. She had never questioned them until now.

She stopped at the corner. A camera on a pole watched her. She looked directly into it. She did not smile.

Let them see.

Chapter 22: The Dressing

The day before the broadcast. Joy woke to the alarm. Three ascending notes. She touched the snooze bar. She did not count to ten.

She showered. She dressed. She looked at the mandatory mirror. She practiced her smile. Corners up. Cheeks tight. Eyes crinkled. It looked real. It felt like nothing.

She walked to the broadcast center. The streets were already decorated. Yellow bunting. Yellow balloons. Yellow streamers. The Day of Gratitude was the most important day of the year. A celebration of happiness. A celebration of compliance.

The studio was busy. Technicians ran cables. Designers adjusted the set. The podium had been polished. The velvet curtains had been steamed.

Joy took her position behind Camera Two. She checked the cable runs. She checked the access panel. She noted the locations of the security guards. There were more today. Ministry security, not factory guards. They wore black uniforms and carried visible weapons.

The production assistant approached. "Full dress rehearsal in thirty minutes. The Minister will be here at eleven."

"I'll be ready."

The assistant left. Joy moved Camera Two into position. As she did, she slipped the data chip from her pocket into the access panel. A tiny click. It was in place.

She rehearsed the movement. Left, forward, stop. Left, forward, stop. When she reached the stop position, her hand would be inches from the panel. A flick of a switch. A swapped signal. Thirty seconds.

Minister Harcourt arrived at eleven. The dress rehearsal began. Joy moved Camera Two through the choreography. Harcourt spoke. His words were hollow. His gestures were perfect.

During a break, Joy excused herself to the restroom. She locked the stall door. She took out her phone. She typed a message: Ready.

She deleted it. She put the phone away. No evidence.

She walked back to Camera Two. The rehearsal resumed. She did not look at the access panel. She did not think about the chip. She focused on the movement. Left, forward, stop.

One more day.

Chapter 23: The Confrontation

That evening, Joy did not go to the Underground meeting. She went to the factory instead. She did not know why. She walked through the empty lobby, past the guard, into the stairwell.

The door to B1 was still unlocked. She descended. The air was warm. The smell was thick.

The basement was unchanged. Forty-seven chairs. Forty-seven silent citizens. The pipes pulsed with light.

Elara was in the corner. Her face was wet. Her eyes were open.

Joy knelt beside her. She took her hand.

"Tomorrow," Joy said. "Tomorrow, people will see you. They will know what happened to you. They will know your name."

The electrodes pulsed. A wave of something passed through Joy. Not grief this time. Something else. Something like recognition.

"Your brother let me find you," Joy said. "I don't know if that makes him brave or broken. But he did it."

She stayed there, holding Elara's hand, for a long time. The pipes hummed. The lights pulsed. The other forty-six subjects breathed their shallow breaths.

When she finally stood, her legs were stiff. She climbed the stairs. At the top, Voss was waiting.

"I thought you might be here," he said.

"I had to see her."

"You need to stop coming. The guards check the logs. I can only hide so much."

"Tomorrow."

He nodded. "Tomorrow."

"Will you try to stop me?"

He was silent for a moment. Then he said, "I have stopped people I loved from speaking the truth. I have not stopped anyone I respected."

He turned and walked away. Joy watched him go. She had never seen him walk with his shoulders down before.

Chapter 24: The Morning

The Day of Gratitude. Joy woke before the alarm. She lay in bed and watched the ceiling grow light through the window. The city was already humming with activity. Music played from the street speakers. Children's laughter from somewhere distant. Recorded, probably.

She dressed slowly. Gray uniform. Comfortable shoes. The same clothes she wore every day.

She did not practice her smile.

The streets were crowded. Families in yellow. Children with balloons. Couples holding hands. Everyone smiling. The smile-check kiosks were showing green for everyone today. Of course they were. The Ministry had adjusted the thresholds. Everyone passed on Gratitude Day.

Joy walked through the crowd like a ghost. No one noticed her. No one looked at her face and saw the absence there.

The broadcast center was a fortress. Guards at every entrance. Metal detectors. Bag checks. Joy submitted to the search. Her bag contained nothing but her lunch and a water bottle. No chip. The chip was already in the access panel.

She was cleared. She walked to the studio.

The set was ready. The podium gleamed. The velvet curtains were perfect. Camera One was in place. Camera Three was in place. Camera Two was waiting.

Joy took her position. She touched the access panel. The chip was still there. She could feel it through the metal.

The production assistant's voice came through her earpiece. "Thirty minutes to air. All positions, final check."

Joy adjusted her camera. She adjusted her face. She waited.

Chapter 25: The Broadcast Begins

The countdown began. Ten. Nine. Eight. The studio lights brightened. Seven. Six. Five. The music swelled. Four. Three. Two. One.

Minister Harcourt walked onto the stage. His smile was radiant. His arms were open. The cameras caught every angle.

"Citizens of Optima!" His voice filled the studio and the city. "Today is the happiest day of the year!"

Joy watched through the viewfinder of Camera Two. Harcourt moved to the center of the stage. She pushed the dolly forward. Left. Forward. Stop.

She was in position. Her hand was inches from the access panel.

"Our city is built on happiness," Harcourt said. "Every smile, every laugh, every moment of joy is a brick in the foundation of our great society."

Joy's fingers touched the panel. The latch was smooth. She could feel the chip inside.

"But happiness does not come from nowhere. It must be created. Nurtured. Harvested."

The word hit her like a slap. Harvested. He had used the real word. In public. On live broadcast. Because he knew no one would understand. Because he knew the truth was hidden behind layers of pretty language.

"There is no greater gift than the gift of happiness," Harcourt said. "And there is no greater worker than the one who provides it."

Joy's hand moved. She opened the access panel. Her fingers found the switch.

She looked at Harcourt. He was still smiling. He would keep smiling until the last citizen stopped watching.

She thought of Elara. Of the yellow house. Of Beano the dog.

She flipped the switch.

Chapter 26: The Feed

The screen went dark. For a moment, nothing. Then the image resolved.

The basement. The chairs. The pipes.

The camera angle was wide. It showed the full room. Twenty chairs on the left wall. Twenty on the right. Forty-seven silent figures with electrodes on their temples.

The view zoomed in on the corner chair. Elara's face. Slack. Pale. Tears running down her cheeks.

The audio was raw. The hum of the pipes. The distant drone of the factory. A woman's breathing, shallow and regular.

Joy watched through the viewfinder. She had seen the basement before. But seeing it through the broadcast felt different. It was no longer a secret. It was real. It was public. It was undeniable.

She heard the first sounds from outside. The crowd in the street had gone quiet. The recorded laughter had stopped. The music had cut out.

Thirty seconds.

A guard grabbed her from behind. Strong hands on her arms. The earpiece was ripped from her ear.

"Secure the feed!" someone shouted.

Another guard hit the access panel. The chip was ejected. The screen went black. The broadcast cut to the Ministry logo. A smiling sun.

The guards dragged Joy across the studio. She did not resist. She looked at the set as she passed. Minister Harcourt was standing at the podium. His smile was gone. His face was white. His hands were shaking.

"You," he said.

She did not answer.

They took her to a room. No windows. A metal table. Two chairs. They sat her down and closed the door.

She was alone.

She waited.

Chapter 27: The Echo

The city was silent. Joy could feel it through the walls. No music. No recorded laughter. No cheerful announcements. The silence was louder than anything she had ever heard.

She sat at the metal table. The room had no clock. Time passed. She did not know how much.

The door opened. A woman in a black uniform entered. She was not a guard. Her insignia was different. Ministry Internal Affairs.

"Joy Mendes."

"Yes."

"You committed an act of broadcast interference. You understand the penalty."

"You mean the harvesting chair."

The woman's face did not change. "You will be processed. Your emotional output will be evaluated. You will contribute to the happiness of the city."

"How long?"

The woman did not answer. She placed a tablet on the table. A document. Joy's name. A declaration of non-compliance. A box to sign.

"If you sign, the processing is quick. A few hours. You will not suffer."

"And if I don't sign?"

The woman picked up the tablet. "Then it takes longer."

She left. The door closed. Joy was alone again.

She touched her face. It was dry. She had not cried. She had not smiled. She had not felt anything since the broadcast cut.

She wondered if the Underground had seen it. If Mira had watched. If Wren had heard. If Casper had managed to disable the backup generator after all.

She wondered if Elara knew. If somewhere in the dark of the basement, Elara had felt the shift in the air. The momentary silence. The thirty seconds of being seen.

It did not matter. The broadcast had happened. The truth was out. Even if the Ministry played it as a technical error. Even if they executed her before dawn. Thirty seconds had been enough to plant a seed.

She leaned back in the chair. She closed her eyes. She waited.

Chapter 28: The Visitor

Hours passed. Joy lost track of time. The windowless room offered no cues. She was fed once. A tray of gray food pushed through a slot in the door. She ate without tasting.

Then the lock clicked. The door opened. It was not the Internal Affairs woman. It was Voss.

He was in uniform. His face was the same as always. Unreadable. But his hands were trembling.

"Come," he said.

She stood. He led her down a corridor, past empty offices, through a fire door, into the stairwell.

"The Ministry is in chaos," he said. "The broadcast was seen by sixty percent of the city before they cut the feed. People are gathering in the streets. They are not celebrating."

"What happened to the Underground?"

"Underground?"

"The people who helped me."

Voss walked faster. "Mira is in custody. Casper ran. Wren is missing. The others scattered."

"And you?"

"I am still here. Which means I am still useful to you."

He pushed open a door. The night air hit her. Cold and clean. They were at the back of the broadcast center, in a service alley. A van was parked with its engine running.

"Get in," Voss said.

"Where are you taking me?"

"Away. The Underground has a safe house. Wren contacted me before she disappeared."

"Wren contacted you?"

"We have the same source. Now get in."

Joy climbed into the van. Voss got in beside her. The driver pulled away.

She looked back. The broadcast center was dark. The sunburst shape was just a silhouette against the night sky.

"The city is changing," Voss said. "The question is whether it changes fast enough."

Chapter 29: The Safe House

The safe house was in the industrial district, above a shuttered foundry. The staircase was iron and creaked with every step. The apartment was three rooms: a living space, a kitchen, a bedroom. The windows faced the foundry wall.

Mira was already there. She sat at the kitchen table, her hands wrapped around a cup of tea. She looked up when Joy entered.

"You did it."

"We did it."

Mira shook her head. "I watched the feed. Thirty seconds. The basement. Elara's face. You did what none of us could do."

"What happens now?"

"The Ministry has shut down the factory. All operations suspended. The workers have been sent home. The basement is sealed."

Joy sat across from her. "And the subjects?"

Mira's face tightened. "Still inside. The Ministry guards won't let anyone near them. They're claiming the building is structurally unsound."

"They're going to destroy the evidence."

"Probably."

Voss entered the apartment. He stood by the door, arms crossed. "The Ministry is holding an emergency meeting. Harcourt is expected to resign by morning. They'll blame him. They'll call the basement a rogue operation."

"But it wasn't rogue," Joy said. "The whole Ministry knew."

"The Ministry knows a lot of things. Acknowledging them is different." Voss looked at her. "You have a choice. You can stay here and wait for the Ministry to find you. Or you can leave Optima and start somewhere else."

"There is nowhere else. The outer districts are dependent on the factory's output."

"Then you stay and fight."

The word hung in the air. Fight. Joy had never thought of herself as a fighter. She had been a worker. An observer. A woman who polished smile-cubes and kept her head down.

She looked at Mira. At Voss. At the cracked cup of tea in her hands.

"Show me the plan," she said.

Chapter 30: The Numbers

Mira spread papers across the kitchen table. Production logs. Distribution routes. Financial records. The Underground had been gathering data for months.

"The factory is shut down," Mira said. "But the distribution network is still active. The Ministry has warehouses full of smile-cubes. They can keep the outer districts supplied for weeks."

"Then shutting down the factory isn't enough," Joy said.

"No. We need to shut down the distribution network. We need to empty the warehouses. We need to show people what they've been consuming."

"How?"

"We leak the production records. Every batch number. Every destination. Every buyer. We show them that the happiness in their water, their food, their medicine — it all came from the basement."

Joy studied the papers. The numbers were staggering. The factory had been operating for seven years. Thousands of subjects. Millions of units of harvested emotion. The entire city was built on stolen happiness.

"There's a warehouse on Dock Street," Casper said. He had arrived while they were talking, covered in grease, his eyes red from lack of sleep. "It's full. The guards are Ministry loyalists. But I know the ventilation system."

"You want to break in?"

"I want to open the doors and let the city see."

Joy looked at the map. Dock Street was in the port district. The warehouse was large, with multiple entrances. A water approach. A road approach. A roof access.

"When?" she asked.

"Tomorrow night. While the Ministry is still in disarray."

Mira nodded. "We have twelve people. Maybe fifteen if Wren shows up."

"Wren will show up," Joy said.

She did not know why she was confident. But something had shifted inside her. The fear was still there. But it was no longer in control.

She looked at the papers. The map. The plan.

"Let's do it."

Chapter 31: The Warehouse

The Dock Street warehouse loomed against the harbor. Brick and iron, three stories tall. A faded sign read MINISTRY OF HAPPINESS — DISTRIBUTION DIVISION 4.

Joy arrived with Casper at midnight. They came through the water, in a small boat, silent on the dark surface. The dock was slick with rain. The warehouse wall was wet and cold.

Casper found the ventilation intake. He removed the grate. "Narrow crawl. Twenty meters. Comes out near the main floor."

"Ladies first?" Joy said.

Casper almost smiled. "After you."

She crawled. The duct was tight. Her shoulders scraped the sides. Dust filled her lungs. She kept her eyes forward and moved.

Twenty meters. It felt like twenty kilometers.

The duct opened into a drop. She lowered herself to the floor. The warehouse was vast. Pallets of smile-cubes rose to the ceiling. Thousands of them. Millions of units. The light from her flashlight bounced off the translucent cubes, making the whole room glow.

Casper dropped beside her. "This way."

They moved between the pallets. The floor was concrete. The air was cold and dry. No smell. The cubes here were sealed in plastic.

At the front of the warehouse, a row of loading bays. Roll-up doors. Casper found the manual release.

"Once I open this, we have about sixty seconds before the alarm sounds."

"Sixty seconds is enough."

Casper pulled the release. The door rolled up with a rattle. The alarm did not sound.

"System hasn't been activated," Casper said. "They're still in chaos."

They opened the other doors. One by one. The night air poured in. Lights from the city reflected on the water.

Joy stood at the open bay and looked out. The city was quiet. But not peaceful. A different kind of quiet. The quiet of waiting.

She took out her phone. She typed a message to Mira: Doors open.

The reply came: Starting the broadcast.

Chapter 32: The Truth

Mira had been busy. While Joy and Casper were breaking into the warehouse, Mira had been at a terminal in the archives, uploading files. Production logs. Harvesting protocols. Subject records. Financial statements.

She uploaded them to every public server in the city. The Ministry's own network. The public information kiosks. The school computers. The hospital terminals.

By the time the Ministry realized what was happening, the data was everywhere.

Joy watched through a window as people on the street stopped to look at their phones. They read the logs. They saw the names of the subjects. They saw the production numbers. They saw the truth.

A woman looked up from her phone. Her face was pale. She looked at the warehouse. At the open doors. At the pallets of smile-cubes inside.

She walked toward them. Slowly at first. Then faster.

Others followed.

Within an hour, a crowd had gathered. They stood at the loading bay, staring at the cubes. No one spoke. The silence was heavier than shouting.

A man stepped forward. He picked up a cube from an open pallet. He turned it over in his hands. It was warm.

"My daughter worked on Line Three," he said. "She collapsed last month. They said it was a heart condition."

No one answered. He did not seem to expect an answer. He put the cube down and walked away.

The crowd began to disperse. Not because they were leaving. Because they were going to tell someone. Spouses. Neighbors. Friends. The truth spread faster than the Ministry could contain it.

Joy watched them go. She stood in the open bay of the warehouse, the cold air on her face, the faint glow of the cubes at her back.

She had spent three years packaging happiness. Now she knew what it cost.

Chapter 33: The Response

The Ministry responded at dawn. Black vans. Armed guards. A cordon around the warehouse. The crowd had grown to hundreds by then. They did not resist. They did not disperse. They stood in silence and watched.

Joy was not in the warehouse when the vans arrived. She was two blocks away, in a coffee shop that had not opened yet. Mira sat across from her. They watched the news on a small screen behind the counter.

"The Ministry has declared a state of emergency," the broadcaster said. "Citizens are advised to remain indoors. The source of the data breach is under investigation."

"They're not denying it," Joy said.

"They can't. The records are timestamped and authenticated. They'd need to admit the whole system was falsified."

On the screen, the camera showed the warehouse. A Ministry spokesperson stood in front of the cordon. "The Ministry of Happiness is committed to transparency. An independent investigation will be launched into the allegations."

"Independent," Joy said. "Appointed by the Ministry."

"Let them play that game," Mira said. "The truth is out. The city knows. The underground press in the outer districts is picking it up."

Her phone buzzed. She read the message. "Wren is safe. She's at the safe house."

"And the others?"

"Casper made it out. The rest scattered. No arrests from our group."

Joy looked at the screen. The camera was showing the open loading bay now. Pallets of cubes. The glow was visible even in daylight.

"How many people saw the broadcast yesterday?"

"Sixty percent of the city."

"And today?"

"Everyone."

Joy leaned back. Her body was tired. Her mind was racing. She had not slept in thirty hours. She felt like she was floating.

"What happens next?" she asked.

Mira looked at her. "That's up to you. You're the one who flipped the switch. The Underground will follow whatever direction you give."

"I never asked to lead."

"No one does. But you're the one who did it."

Joy stared at the screen. The Ministry spokesperson was still talking. His mouth moved. His words were meaningless. Behind him, the warehouse glowed with stolen happiness.

Chapter 34: The Offer

At noon, Joy's phone rang. The number was blocked. She answered anyway.

"Joy Mendes."

"Joy." The voice was familiar. Minister Harcourt. His smile was gone from his voice. He sounded old. Tired. "I want to meet."

"Why?"

"Because I can give you what you want. The subjects in the basement. Their release. Their rehabilitation. A full inquiry."

"On what condition?"

Silence. Then: "You stop. You and your people. No more broadcasts. No more data leaks. No more open warehouses."

"You want me to trade forty-seven lives for the silence of the city."

"I want to prevent a massacre. The hardliners in the Ministry are advocating for a purge. They want to arrest everyone who saw the broadcast. They want to expand the harvesting program. I can stop them. But I need leverage."

"And I'm your leverage."

"You're the symbol. If you disappear or die, you become a martyr. The movement becomes unstoppable. The hardliners panic. People die." He paused. "If you stay visible, if you negotiate, we can do this without violence."

Joy looked at Mira. Mira was watching her, waiting.

"Give me an hour," Joy said.

"We don't have an hour."

"You have an hour."

She hung up. She told Mira what Harcourt had said.

"It's a trap," Mira said.

"Probably."

"Are you going?"

"I don't know."

Mira leaned forward. "If you go, you need protection. You need witnesses. You need the world watching."

"How do I arrange that?"

Mira smiled. It was not a happy smile. "You call Wren."

Chapter 35: The Meeting

The meeting was in the Ministry building. The same building where Joy had rehearsed the broadcast. The same studio where she had flipped the switch.

She arrived with Wren and two others from the Underground. Wren had spent the morning arranging something Joy did not ask about.

Ministry guards searched them. They took phones. They took bags. They let them through.

Harcourt was in his office. The room was large and bright, with windows overlooking the square. The square was full of people. Silent. Watching.

He was standing at the window when they entered. He turned.

"Joy Mendes."

"Minister."

He looked smaller than he had on stage. His suit was still perfect. His hair was still precise. But something behind his eyes was different. Something like fear.

"You want the subjects released," he said.

"Yes."

"I can arrange it. Medical care. Rehabilitation. A home in the outer districts."

"And the harvesting program?"

"It ends. Today. I have already signed the order."

Joy studied him. "That was fast."

"I had the order prepared. I was waiting for the right moment to use it."

"You knew this day was coming."

Harcourt sat behind his desk. He looked at his hands. "I built the system. I also built the exit. Every tyrant keeps a door. Most are too proud to use it."

"The hardliners won't let you."

"I am the Minister. They cannot stop me."

"The guards outside the warehouse. The black vans. They don't report to you."

Harcourt's jaw tightened. "No. They report to someone else. Which is why I need you to stay visible. As long as you are alive and free, the hardliners cannot act. You are the deterrent."

"What about the Underground?"

"They are covered by the same agreement. No arrests. No reprisals. In exchange, you cease all operations."

Joy looked at Wren. Wren gave a small nod.

"There's one more thing," Joy said.

"What?"

"Subject 47. Elara Voss. I want her released first. Today."

Harcourt nodded. "It will be done."

Chapter 36: The Release

Joy watched from the square as the factory doors opened. It was late afternoon. The light was golden. The crowd that had gathered was silent.

A medical team emerged. They carried a stretcher. On it was a woman. Thin. Pale. Her eyes were open.

Elara Voss.

Joy pushed through the crowd. She reached the stretcher as the medics loaded it into an ambulance. Elara's hand was resting on the blanket. Joy touched it.

"Elara."

No response. The eyes did not track. The face did not move.

"She's heavily sedated," a medic said. "The neural stimulation caused extensive damage. She may not recover full function."

"How long?"

"Months. Years. We don't know."

Joy held the hand. It was warm. The same warmth as the cubes.

"She was a journalist," Joy said. "She tried to expose the truth."

The medic did not answer. He closed the ambulance doors. The vehicle pulled away.

Joy stood in the street and watched it go. The factory loomed behind her. The yellow banners hung limp. HAPPINESS IS PRODUCTIVITY. The letters were peeling.

She turned and walked back to the square. The crowd parted for her. She did not know where she was going. She just walked.

Voss was standing at the edge of the square. He wore civilian clothes. No uniform. No compliance meter. His face was the same. Flat. Unreadable.

"She's out," Joy said.

"She's out."

"What will you do now?"

Voss looked at the factory. "I don't know. I have spent fourteen years serving a system that hurt the only person I ever loved."

"You kept her alive."

"I kept her in a basement."

Joy had no answer for that. She stood beside him and looked at the factory. The sun was setting behind it. The smoke from the stacks was thinning.

"The hardliners will try to take control," Voss said. "Harcourt's truce will not last."

"Then we make it last. One day at a time."

Voss said nothing. They stood together in silence until the sun went down.

Chapter 37: The Aftermath

Days passed. The city did not return to normal. The smile-check kiosks were still active, but fewer people used them. The yellow banners still hung, but no one replaced the ones that fell.

The factory remained closed. The basement was empty now. The chairs had been removed. The subjects had been transferred to a medical facility in the outer districts. Elara was there, in a private room, with a window that faced a garden.

Joy visited her every day. She sat beside the bed and talked. She talked about the weather, the news, the slow changes in the city. Elara did not respond. But sometimes her fingers twitched. Sometimes her eyes moved toward the window.

"She can hear you," the doctors said. "The processing centers for language may still be active."

"Then I'll keep talking."

The Underground had become something larger. The fourteen people in the garage had grown to hundreds. They met in public now. In the square. In the old district. They discussed the future of Optima. The future of the factory. The future of happiness.

Mira had become a spokesperson. Casper had become an organizer. Wren had become a legend.

Joy had become something else. A symbol. A face. A woman who had flipped a switch.

She did not feel like a symbol. She felt like a factory worker. She felt tired. She felt hopeful. She did not know if the two could exist together.

On the seventh day after the broadcast, a package arrived at the safe house. No return address. Inside was a single smile-cube. Cold. Inert.

And a note: "The last one. They are all gone."

Joy held the cube. It did not glow. It did not warm her hand. It was just a piece of plastic. Empty.

She put it on the windowsill. It caught the light. It did nothing.

That was progress.

Chapter 38: The Rebuilding

The Ministry of Happiness was dissolved by executive order. Harcourt announced it on live broadcast. His face was gray. His voice was steady.

"The Ministry of Happiness has been found to have engaged in activities contrary to the principles of genuine well-being. Effective immediately, the Ministry is dissolved. All assets will be transferred to the newly formed Office of Emotional Health, which will operate under independent oversight."

Joy watched the broadcast in the safe house. Mira was beside her. Voss was at the table.

"They're renaming the same thing," Voss said.

"They're admitting fault," Mira said. "That's something."

"Admitting fault is not the same as fixing it."

Joy said nothing. She watched Harcourt's face on the screen. He looked like a man who had been holding his breath for years and had finally exhaled.

The Office of Emotional Health. A new name. A new logo. The same building. Some of the same people.

But not all. The hardliners had been purged. Arrested. Disappeared. No one knew where they had gone. No one asked.

The factory building was being converted into a community center. Casper was leading the project. The hoppers had been removed. The pipes had been cut. The floor where Line Seven had stood was being turned into a garden.

Joy visited the construction site. She walked through the empty space. The fluorescent lights were off. Sunlight came through new windows cut into the walls.

"Is it strange?" Casper asked.

"Yes."

"Does it help?"

"I don't know."

He nodded. "The soil for the garden is coming from the old district. They say it's good soil. Nothing was ever harvested from it."

Joy touched the wall where her station had been. The wall was marked with a faint line where the conveyor belt had run. A ghost of the old world.

"Keep the garden," she said. "Make it green."

She left the building and walked into the square. The yellow banners were gone. The new banners were white. No slogans. Just blank fabric.

No one had decided what the slogans should be yet. They were taking suggestions.

Chapter 39: The Trial

The trial of Minister Harcourt began six weeks after the broadcast. The courtroom was packed. Journalists from the outer districts. Citizens who had lost family members. Former workers from the factory.

Joy was called as a witness.

She stood in the witness box and looked at the room. Harcourt sat at the defendant's table. He looked smaller than she remembered. His suit was still perfect. But his face was different. The smile was gone. It had been gone since the broadcast. It was never coming back.

"Ms. Mendes," the prosecutor said. "You were employed at the Factory of Smiles for three years."

"Yes."

"You discovered the harvesting protocol in the basement."

"Yes."

"You interrupted the Day of Gratitude broadcast to expose it."

"Yes."

The prosecutor paused. "Do you regret your actions?"

Joy looked at Harcourt. He was watching her. His eyes were flat. Not angry. Not pleading. Just watching.

"No," she said. "I regret that it took me three years to look under the floor."

A murmur in the courtroom. The prosecutor nodded.

"What would you say to the citizens of Optima, if you could address them now?"

Joy thought about it. She thought about the cubes. The chairs. The pipes. The forty-seven silent people.

"I would say that happiness is not a product. It is not a duty. It is not something you can manufacture or harvest or sell." She paused. "It is something you feel when you are free."

The courtroom was silent. The prosecutor did not ask another question.

The trial lasted three weeks. Harcourt was found guilty on twelve counts. He was sentenced to twenty years in a rehabilitation facility.

The sentence was controversial. Some wanted him executed. Some wanted him released. Joy did not know what she wanted.

She did not attend the sentencing.

She visited Elara instead.

Chapter 40: The Garden

The garden at the factory took shape slowly. Soil was brought in. Seeds were planted. Water was piped from the roof. Casper had designed an irrigation system from old factory pipes.

Joy helped plant the first row of vegetables. She knelt in the dirt and pressed seeds into the soil with her fingers. The earth was cool and dark. It smelled of nothing but itself.

"Is this weird?" Mira asked, kneeling beside her.

"Yes."

"Good. Weird is better than normal."

Normal. Joy did not know what normal meant anymore. She had spent three years in a white room, polishing smile-cubes. That had been normal. Now she was planting tomatoes where the hoppers used to be.

"There's a meeting tonight," Mira said. "The Office of Emotional Health wants to appoint a citizen advisory board. They asked for you."

"Tell them I'll consider it."

"You should do it. You have credibility."

"I have dirt under my fingernails."

Mira laughed. It was a real laugh. Joy had not heard many of those lately.

The sun was warm on their shoulders. The garden was small, but it was growing. Joy looked at the green shoots pushing through the soil. They were small. Fragile. Alive.

She thought about Elara. About the garden outside her window. About the possibility that Elara might one day see it.

"Is it hard?" Mira asked. "Not knowing if she'll recover?"

"Yes."

"I'm sorry."

Joy pressed another seed into the soil. "She tried to tell the truth. She paid for it. The least I can do is water the garden she can see from her window."

They worked until the sun went down. The garden was planted. The soil was dark. The air was clean.

Chapter 41: The Meeting

The citizen advisory board met in the old Ministry building. The room was the same one where Harcourt had made his offer. The windows still faced the square. The square was still full of people.

Joy sat at the table with eleven others. Former factory workers. Teachers. Doctors. A man who had lost his wife to the harvesting program. A woman who had been a non-compliant citizen but had escaped the basement through a clerical error.

"Issue one," the chairperson said. "The smile-check kiosks. Should they remain?"

A debate followed. Some argued that the kiosks were tools of oppression. Others argued that removing them would create chaos, that people needed structure to heal.

Joy listened. She had opinions, but she was not sure they mattered. She was here because of what she had done, not because she knew what to do next.

"Joy?" The chairperson looked at her. "What do you think?"

She thought about the kiosks. The red lights. The green lights. The man in the gray coat being taken away.

"The kiosks were never about measuring happiness," she said. "They were about control. Remove them."

The room was silent for a moment. Then someone nodded. Someone else disagreed. The debate continued.

But the kiosks were removed the following week.

Joy watched from her window as workers unbolted the last machine from the corner. They loaded it onto a truck. The truck drove away.

The corner was empty. A man stood there, waiting for a bus. He was not smiling. He was not frowning. He was just waiting.

She considered that a victory.

Chapter 42: The Anniversary

One year after the broadcast. The city held a memorial for the subjects of the harvesting program. Forty-seven names read aloud. Forty-seven candles lit. Forty-seven seconds of silence.

Joy stood at the front of the crowd. Beside her, in a wheelchair, was Elara.

Elara had improved. She could sit upright now. She could turn her head toward sounds. She could hold a spoon. She could not speak. But the doctors said her brain activity was increasing. The damaged pathways were slowly rewiring.

Joy held her hand during the silence. Elara's fingers moved slightly. A squeeze. Maybe intentional. Maybe reflex.

"Elara Voss," the speaker read. "Journalist. Age thirty-five."

The crowd murmured. Journalist. The word had been redacted from the original file. But the truth had come out during the trial. Elara had been investigating the factory. She had found the evidence. She had been silenced.

After the ceremony, Voss approached. He was thinner now. His hair was gray. He had left Optima and was living in the outer districts, working as a clerk in a legal aid office.

"She looks better," he said.

"She is better."

"I'm glad."

They stood in silence. The candles flickered. The crowd dispersed.

"Will you come back?" Joy asked.

"No. There's nothing for me here."

"There's Elara."

Voss looked at his sister. His face, usually flat, cracked slightly. "She wouldn't want me here. She would want me to remember what I did."

"She would want you to forgive yourself."

"You don't know that."

"No. But I know what it is to live with guilt. It's heavier than any work I did in the factory."

Voss said nothing. He touched Elara's shoulder. Then he turned and walked away.

Joy watched him go. Elara's hand moved in hers. A squeeze. Softer this time.

"Come on," Joy said. "Let's go home."

She pushed the wheelchair through the quiet streets. The city was different now. Quieter. Slower. More real.

Chapter 43: The Letter

A letter arrived at the safe house. No return address. Handwritten. Joy opened it in the kitchen while the kettle boiled.

Dear Joy,


I do not expect you to forgive me. I do not forgive myself. But I wanted you to know that the harvesting program was not my idea. It was in place before I became Minister. I inherited it. I kept it running. I told myself I had no choice.

That was a lie. I had choices. I made the wrong ones.

I do not expect a reply. But I wanted to say thank you. For stopping it. For making me stop.

Harcourt

Joy read the letter twice. Then she folded it and put it in a drawer.

She did not reply. She did not need to.

The kettle boiled. She made tea. She sat at the table and watched the rain streak the window.

A year ago, she had been polishing smile-cubes on Assembly Line Seven. Now she was receiving letters from the former Minister of Happiness. The world had turned. She was still standing.

She drank her tea. The rain continued. The city continued. She continued.

That was enough.

Chapter 44: The Offer

Six months later, the Office of Emotional Health offered Joy a job. Director of Community Outreach. A salary. An office. A title.

She turned it down.

"Why?" Mira asked. "You'd be good at it."

"I don't want an office. I want to stay in the garden."

"You can't grow vegetables forever."

"Watch me."

Mira shook her head. But she was smiling. "You're impossible."

"I'm a factory worker. We're stubborn."

The garden had expanded. It now covered the entire ground floor of the old factory. Volunteers came every weekend to plant and weed and harvest. The vegetables were distributed to the outer districts. The soil was dark and rich. The plants were green and strong.

Joy worked in the garden every day. She dug. She planted. She watered. She harvested. The work was hard and simple and honest.

She thought about the cubes sometimes. The warmth. The light. The weight of them in her hands. But the thought was distant now. Like a dream that fades after waking.

She thought about Elara. She visited her twice a week. The speech therapy was working. Elara could say a few words now. "Yes." "No." "Water." "Joy."

That last one had made Joy cry.

She did not cry often. But that day, sitting beside Elara's bed, with the afternoon light coming through the window and Elara's voice saying her name, she had cried.

She had not apologized for it.

Chapter 45: The Awakening

It happened on a Tuesday. Joy was in the garden, pulling weeds, when her phone rang. The medical facility.

"Ms. Mendes. Elara is speaking."

Joy dropped the phone. She picked it up. "Full sentences?"

"Short ones. But yes. She asked for you by name."

Joy was at the facility in twenty minutes. She ran through the corridors. She burst into Elara's room.

Elara was sitting up. Her eyes were clear. Her face was thin but present.

"Joy," she said.

Joy sat on the bed. She took Elara's hands. "I'm here."

"I saw you." Elara's voice was rough. Unused. "In the broadcast. I saw you through the camera."

"You remember?"

"The electrodes. They recorded everything. The harvesters used my memories. But I saw yours too. In the pipe. The electric light."

Joy did not understand. It did not matter. Elara was speaking. The words were coming.

"Elara, you're going to be okay."

Elara looked at her. Her eyes were wet. "I know."

They sat together as the afternoon light shifted across the floor. Joy did not let go of Elara's hand.

She thought about the basement. The chairs. The terminal. The moment she had decided to act.

She thought about everything that had happened since.

The alarm chime. The smile-check kiosk. The warmth of the cubes. The smell from the vents. The lock that clicked open. The forty-seven chairs.

All of it had led to this. A woman who was no longer silent. A city that was no longer blind. A garden that was growing where the factory had stood.

Chapter 46: The Speech

The Office of Emotional Health invited Joy to speak at the unveiling of a new memorial. The memorial was a sculpture in the square, where the largest smile-check kiosk had once stood. It was a tree. Bronze. Its branches reached upward.

Joy stood at the podium. The square was full. Thousands of people. Some had been her coworkers. Some had been Ministry employees. Some had been subjects. Some had been bystanders.

She had prepared a speech. She had written it and rewritten it. In the end, she held the paper in her hands and did not look at it.

"I worked on Assembly Line Seven for three years," she said. "I polished thirty thousand smile-cubes. I thought I was making happiness. I was wrong."

The crowd was silent.

"I learned that happiness cannot be manufactured. It cannot be harvested. It cannot be forced. It is not a product. It is not a duty. It is not a quota."

She paused.

"It is a choice. A daily choice. To see the world as it is and still find reasons to keep going."

She looked at the crowd. She saw Mira, standing in the front row. Casper. Wren. Faces from the Underground. Faces from the factory.

"The factory is gone. The basement is empty. The truth is out. What happens next is up to all of us."

She stepped back from the podium. The applause started slowly. Then it grew.

She did not smile. She did not frown. She just stood there, in the square, under the bronze tree, and let the sound wash over her.

Chapter 47: The Open Door

The factory building was fully converted now. The community center had classrooms, a clinic, a kitchen, and a garden. The garden was Joy's domain.

She was planting carrots when a young woman approached. She was maybe twenty. Her uniform was gray. Factory issue.

"Are you Joy Mendes?"

"Yes."

"I work at the new distribution center. The one that replaced the old warehouse."

Joy straightened. "How can I help you?"

The woman looked at her hands. She was twisting a ring on her finger. Nervous.

"I found something. In the old records. The ones that were supposed to be destroyed."

She held out a data chip.

Joy looked at it. Her heart did not stop. Her hands did not shake. She had seen too much to be surprised by one more truth.

"What's on it?"

"Distribution lists. Names. Dates. The program was bigger than the basement. It was in other cities. Other factories."

Joy took the chip. It was small. Light.

"Does anyone else know?"

"No. I came to you first."

Joy looked at the chip. She looked at the garden. She looked at the young woman, who was afraid and brave in equal measure.

The work was not finished.

She had known that. Deep down. A system that big does not disappear because one door is opened. It just moves. Rebrands. Hides.

"Come inside," Joy said. "I'll make tea. We'll talk."

The young woman followed her into the community center. The door closed behind them.

But it was not locked.

Chapter 48: The Network

The data on the chip was more extensive than Joy had expected. Three other cities. Five other factories. Thousands of subjects. The same protocol. The same harvest. The same lie.

Joy sat in the community center with Mira, Casper, and Wren. The chip lay in the middle of the table.

"We thought we stopped it," Mira said.

"We stopped it in one city. That's not the same."

"Can we expose the others?"

Joy picked up the chip. "The broadcast method worked once. The Ministry was caught off guard. They won't make the same mistake twice."

"So we need a different approach."

Casper leaned forward. "The distribution network is the same. If we can map the supply chains in the other cities, we can target the warehouses simultaneously. Hit all of them at once."

"That requires coordination," Wren said. "People in each city. People we can trust."

"We have people in the outer districts. The recovery program has volunteers everywhere."

Joy looked at the chip. The light from the window caught it. It gleamed.

"Then we build a network," she said. "Quiet. Careful. Connected. We find the people in each city who already know the truth. We give them the tools to expose it."

"And then?"

"And then we flip the switch again. All of them. At the same time."

The table was silent. Then Mira nodded. "I'll start mapping the contacts."

"I'll map the supply chains," Casper said.

"I'll move the information," Wren said.

They looked at Joy.

"I'll hold the chip," she said. "Until the moment is right."

She put the chip in her pocket. It was warm.

The work continued.

Chapter 49: The Threshold

Two years after the broadcast, Joy stood at the door of Elara's apartment. Elara was living independently now. Her speech was almost normal. She walked with a cane but did not need the wheelchair. She had started writing again. Short pieces. Observations. Memories.

Joy knocked.

Elara opened the door. She smiled. A real smile. One that reached her eyes.

"Come in. The tea is ready."

Joy stepped inside. The apartment was small and bright. Books stacked on the floor. A computer on the desk. A vase of flowers from the garden.

They sat at the kitchen table. The tea was hot. The window was open. The city hummed outside.

"I'm leaving," Joy said.

Elara's hand stopped on her cup. "Where?"

"The other cities. The network Mira has been building. It's ready."

"How long?"

"Months. Maybe a year."

Elara looked at her tea. The steam rose. The window curtains moved in the breeze.

"Will you come back?"

"Yes."

"And the factory here? The garden?"

"The volunteers will keep it going. The community center is self-sufficient now."

Elara was quiet for a long time. Then she reached across the table and took Joy's hand.

"I remember the broadcast," she said. "Not the details. The feeling. I was in the dark for so long. And then there was light. Your light."

"It was the camera light."

"It was you."

Joy squeezed her hand. "I'll come back."

"I know. But I'll still miss you."

They drank their tea. The sun moved across the floor. The city continued. But something had shifted. A threshold had been crossed. The world was larger now. The work was larger.

Joy was ready.

Chapter 50: The Garden

The morning Joy left Optima, she walked through the garden one last time. The carrots were ready. The tomatoes were ripe. The sunflowers were taller than she was.

She knelt and touched the soil. It was dark and cool. It smelled of earth and water and green things growing.

She thought about the first time she had noticed the smell from the vents. The metallic sweetness. The wrongness. She thought about the lock that had clicked open. The stairs down. The forty-seven chairs.

She thought about Elara. About Harcourt's letter. About Voss walking away with his shoulders down.

She thought about the chip in her pocket. The other cities. The other factories. The work still to come.

She stood up. She brushed the dirt from her hands.

Mira was waiting at the gate. Casper was with her. Wren was already in the van.

"Ready?" Mira asked.

Joy looked back at the garden. The school. The clinic. The kitchen. All of it growing where the factory had stood.

"Ready."

She walked to the van. She got in. The engine started.

The city passed by the windows. The streets were still yellow in places, but the banners were gone. The smile-check kiosks were gone. The people on the sidewalks were not smiling on command. Some of them were not smiling at all.

That was progress. Real progress.

The van reached the city gate. The guard checked their papers. He looked at Joy. He recognized her.

"Going somewhere?"

"There are other factories," Joy said. "Other basements. Other chairs."

The guard nodded. He waved them through.

The van rolled past the gate. The city shrank in the side mirror. The factory — the community center now — was just a shape against the sky.

Joy touched the pocket where the chip rested.

She did not smile. She did not have to.

She was free.
