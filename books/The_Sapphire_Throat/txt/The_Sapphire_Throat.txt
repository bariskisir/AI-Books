Chapter 1: The Sapphire Lounge

The heat hit first. Then the smoke. Then the sound.

Della Monroe closed her eyes and let the music carry her. The piano walked a slow blues. The trumpet answered. She opened her mouth and the room went still.

"I got the blues," she sang, "but I ain't gonna let 'em win."

The Sapphire Lounge was packed. Men in fedoras. Women in beads. Everyone drinking from teacups that held whiskey, not tea. It was 1926 and Prohibition was a joke in Chicago. Everyone knew it.

Della held the last note. The crowd erupted. She smiled and stepped back from the microphone.

Lena Cross winked from the piano bench. Her fingers danced across the keys.

Della moved through the tables. Hands reached for her. She touched shoulders, shook hands. The men looked at her like she was made of gold.

At the back of the room, Sal Capri watched. He stood in the shadows near the curtain. He did not clap. He just watched, his face unreadable.

Della felt his eyes on her. She always did.

She made her way to the bar. The bartender slid her a glass of water. She drank it slow.

"That was beautiful," a man said beside her.

She turned. He was young. Clean-shaven. Not a regular.

"Thank you," she said.

"You got a record?"

"Soon," she said. "Maybe."

Sal appeared at her elbow. "Della. My office. Now."

His voice was soft. That was when he was most dangerous.

She followed him through the curtain.

Chapter 2: The Manager's Smile

Sal's office was small. A desk. Two chairs. A photograph of his mother on the wall.

He sat behind the desk and lit a cigar. The smoke curled toward the ceiling.

"You were good tonight," he said.

"Thank you, Sal."

"Sit."

She sat.

He leaned back. "I got a call today. From a man at Okeh Records."

Della's heart jumped. "Okeh?"

"He heard you sing at the Coliseum last month. He wants to talk."

"That's wonderful."

"It is." Sal smiled. It did not reach his eyes. "But we need to discuss your contract."

"My contract?"

"The one you signed when I started managing you. Remember?"

She remembered. Six months ago. She had been desperate. Broke. Sleeping in a boarding house with three other girls.

"I remember," she said.

"The terms are generous. For both of us."

"I know."

"Good." He stood and came around the desk. He put a hand on her shoulder. "I take care of my people, Della. You remember that."

She nodded.

"Go get some sleep. Tomorrow we celebrate."

She left his office. The club was emptying. The lights were coming up.

Lena was packing her sheet music at the piano.

"What did he want?" Lena asked.

"He said Okeh Records called."

"That's big, Della. That's real big."

"I know."

Lena looked toward Sal's office. "Just be careful."

"Of what?"

"The price of his help."

Della did not answer. She grabbed her coat and walked out into the cold Chicago night.

Chapter 3: Midnight Rehearsal

The club was dark. Della stood alone on the stage.

She had a key. Sal had given it to her. "In case you need to practice," he had said.

Tonight she needed to think.

She sat at the piano and picked out a melody. Something new. Something she had been working on for weeks.

The notes hung in the empty room. The piano was out of tune. She did not care.

She was halfway through the second verse when she heard voices.

They came from Sal's office. The door was closed. Light bled under it.

Della stopped playing. She listened.

A man's voice. Loud. Angry.

"You promised me the South Side," the voice said.

Another voice. Calm. Sal's.

"The South Side is complicated."

"A man is dead, Sal."

"Accidents happen."

"That was no accident."

Della crept closer. Her bare feet made no sound on the wood floor.

She pressed her ear to the door.

"You will make it right," the angry voice said. "Or I will."

"Threats?" Sal's voice was soft. "In my own club?"

"I'm not threatening. I'm promising."

Della heard a chair scrape. She backed away. Her heel hit a loose board.

The voices stopped.

Chapter 4: The Argument

Della froze. Her heart pounded.

The office door stayed closed.

She counted to ten. Then twenty. The voices started again, lower now.

She crept back to the piano and sat down. Her fingers found the keys. She played loudly. Let them think she had been playing the whole time.

The office door opened. A man came out. Tall. Broad-shouldered. A scar ran from his eyebrow to his jaw.

He did not look at her. He walked to the back door and went out into the alley.

Sal appeared in the doorway of his office. He lit a cigar.

"Still here, Della?"

"Couldn't sleep. Thought I'd work on that new song."

"What song?"

"The one I was just playing."

He nodded. "I heard you. Pretty melody."

She kept playing. Her hands were shaking.

"You okay?" he asked.

"Fine. Just tired."

He walked toward her. His footsteps echoed in the empty room.

"That man," he said. "Business partner. We had a disagreement."

"It's not my business."

"No. It isn't."

He stood beside the piano. His fingers traced the wood.

"You should go home, Della. It's late."

"I'll finish this verse."

He waited. She played. When she looked up, he was still watching her.

"Good night, Della."

"Good night, Sal."

He grabbed his coat and left. The door locked behind him.

She sat alone in the dark club. She did not play anymore.

Chapter 5: Alley After Dark

The air was cold and sharp. Della stepped out the side door of the club for a cigarette.

She did not smoke often. Only when she was nervous.

The alley was narrow. Brick walls rose on both sides. A single bulb burned above the door.

She lit the cigarette. The smoke tasted like relief.

Then she heard them.

Voices. In the alley. Closer than she expected.

She stepped back into the doorway. The light did not reach her.

Three men stood near the far end of the alley. Sal. The scarred man from earlier. And a third man she did not recognize.

The third man was on his knees.

"Please," he said. "I didn't tell anyone."

"You sure about that?" Sal asked.

"I swear. On my mother's life."

Sal looked at the scarred man. Frankie. She knew him. Frankie DeMarco. Sal's shadow.

Frankie pulled a gun.

Della's mouth opened. No sound came out.

The man on his knees started crying. "Sal, please. We grew up together. Please."

"I know," Sal said. "That's what makes it hard."

He nodded.

Frankie raised the gun.

Chapter 6: The Shot

The gunshot bounced off the brick walls.

The man on his knees fell forward. His face hit the ground.

Silence.

Then Sal spoke. "Clean it up."

Frankie grabbed the body by the ankles. He dragged it deeper into the alley, toward the river side.

Sal turned.

He saw Della.

She stood in the doorway. The cigarette had fallen from her fingers. She did not remember dropping it.

Sal's face changed. For one moment, he looked surprised. Then the mask came back.

"Della," he said. "You should be home."

She could not move. Could not speak.

He walked toward her. His steps were slow. Deliberate.

"You didn't see anything," he said.

She shook her head. "I saw everything."

He stopped in front of her. She could smell his cologne. Could see the gray in his hair.

"That was a bad man, Della. He was going to hurt people."

"You shot him. In the back of the head."

"He stole from me. He was going to testify against me."

"Testify?"

Sal grabbed her arm. His grip was firm.

"You listen to me. You say nothing. You go home. You sing tomorrow. Life goes on."

"He's dead."

"The police will find him. They'll call it a robbery. No one will ask questions."

She looked into his eyes. He was calm. Completely calm.

"Can you do that for me, Della? Can you keep quiet?"

Chapter 7: The Promise

"Yes," Della whispered. "I can keep quiet."

Sal let go of her arm. He smiled. The warm smile. The manager's smile.

"Good girl. Now go home."

She walked. She did not run. Her legs felt like they belonged to someone else.

The cold air hit her face. She turned the corner. Her apartment was six blocks away.

She walked past the boarded-up buildings. Past the all-night diner where the cabbies drank coffee. Past the stoop where the old man sat every night, rain or shine.

She reached her building. Three stories. Brown brick. A light in the landlady's window.

She climbed the stairs. Her room was at the back. Small. A bed. A dresser. A window that looked out at the alley.

She locked the door. She leaned against it.

Then she vomited into the sink.

She stayed on the floor for a long time. The floorboards were cold. The ceiling was stained.

She had seen a man die.

She had watched her manager give the order.

And she had promised to say nothing.

She curled into a ball. The night stretched on. She did not sleep.

When the sun finally came up, she was still on the floor.

She looked at her hands. They were steady now.

She had a show tonight.

She got up. She washed her face. She put on her dress.

Life goes on.

Chapter 8: Detective Malone

Detective Michael Malone arrived at The Sapphire Lounge at noon.

The club was empty. The chairs were upside down on the tables. A man was mopping the floor.

"I'm looking for Sal Capri," Malone said.

"Upstairs," the man said. "Back office."

Malone walked through the club. He had been here before. Twice. Both times on complaints about noise.

This time was different. A body had been found in the river. The name was Tommy Russo. A low-level bootlegger with a bullet in his skull.

Malone climbed the stairs. He knocked on the office door.

"Come in."

Sal sat behind his desk. He was reading a newspaper. He looked up and smiled.

"Detective. To what do I owe the pleasure?"

"A body. Tommy Russo."

"Never heard of him."

"We found him in the river. Shot once in the head."

"Terrible. These are dangerous times."

"His widow says he was coming to see you. The night he died."

Sal folded the newspaper. "I don't know his widow. She's mistaken."

"I think you're lying."

"That's your right, Detective."

Malone sat down across from Sal. "I'm going to find out who did this."

"I hope you do. Chicago needs good men like you."

"I need to talk to your staff. Your singers."

"Why?"

"Maybe one of them saw something."

Sal's smile did not waver. "They're all busy. But I'll tell them to expect you."

"Thank you."

Malone stood. He looked around the office. The photograph of Sal's mother. The ledgers on the shelf.

"I'll be back," he said.

"I'm sure you will."

Malone walked out. He stopped at the top of the stairs.

A woman was coming up. Young. Pretty. Dark circles under her eyes.

"You Della Monroe?" he asked.

She stopped. "Yes."

"I'm Detective Malone. I'd like to ask you a few questions."

Chapter 9: The Piano Girl's Warning

Malone questioned Della in the empty club.

He sat across from her at a small table. He had kind eyes. A face that had seen too much.

"Did you know Tommy Russo?" he asked.

"No."

"Were you working the night he died?"

"I finished my set at midnight. I went home."

"Did you see anything unusual?"

She looked at her hands. "No. Nothing."

Malone watched her. His eyes were steady.

"Miss Monroe, if you're scared of someone, I can protect you."

"I'm not scared of anyone."

"I've been working this precinct for twelve years. I know how things work around here. Sal Capri owns half the cops on the force. He owns the alderman. He owns this neighborhood."

"I don't know what you're talking about."

Malone leaned forward. "He killed Tommy Russo. I know it. You know it. The only question is whether you'll help me prove it."

Della did not answer.

"Think about it," he said. He stood and put his card on the table. "When you're ready."

He left.

Della sat alone. The card was white. Simple. "Detective Michael Malone, Chicago Police Department."

She picked it up. She put it in her pocket.

Lena came out from the back room. She sat down beside Della.

"He's trouble," Lena said.

"The detective?"

"Sal. The detective. All of them."

"I know."

"Sal killed that man, didn't he?"

Della did not answer.

Lena took her hand. "Listen to me. You stay quiet. You smile. You sing. You don't trust anyone."

"Not even you?"

"Especially not me. I got my own secrets."

She walked back to the piano and started playing.

Della watched her. The notes filled the empty room.

Everyone had secrets.

Chapter 10: Corinne's Whisper

Corinne Webb had been watching.

She stood in the hallway behind the stage. She had heard everything.

She was twenty-six. Pretty. Blonde. She was the second singer at The Sapphire Lounge, which meant she sang the early sets and Della sang the late ones.

She hated Della.

It was not personal. It was survival. The Sapphire Lounge only needed one star. And Della was that star.

Corinne watched Della leave. She watched Lena go back to the piano.

She walked to Sal's office. She knocked.

"It's Corinne."

"Come in."

Sal was at his desk. He was counting money.

"What is it?"

"I saw the detective talking to Della."

"I know."

"I think she knows something."

Sal looked up. "What makes you say that?"

"Look at her. She's scared of her own shadow. Something happened."

Sal put down the money. "Thank you, Corinne. That's useful."

"I'm just looking out for you, Sal."

"I know you are."

He opened a drawer. He pulled out a roll of bills. He peeled off three twenties and held them out.

"Keep your eyes open."

She took the money. "Always."

She left his office. She felt good. The money was nice. But the power was better.

She walked past Della's dressing room. The door was cracked.

Della was sitting in front of the mirror. She was staring at her own reflection.

Corinne smiled and walked on.

Della had no idea what was coming.

Chapter 11: A Second Body

Malone was at his desk when the call came in.

Another body. This one in an alley behind the Fulton Street Market. A newsboy. Seventeen years old. Shot in the chest.

The name was Arthur Bell.

Malone drove to the scene. The body was still there, covered with a sheet. The coroner was taking notes.

"Same gun?" Malone asked.

"Different caliber," the coroner said. "But same shooter, maybe. Execution style."

Malone looked at the body. The boy was small. Thin. He still had his newspaper bag.

"Any witnesses?"

"No one saw nothing. Shocker."

Malone knelt beside the body. He lifted the sheet. The boy's face was peaceful. He looked like he was sleeping.

"Arthur Bell," Malone said. "Age seventeen. Sold papers on the corner of Clark and Van Buren."

"How do you know that?"

"I buy a paper from him every morning."

The coroner was quiet.

Malone stood. His knees popped. He was getting old for this.

"Run the ballistics. Check for connections to Tommy Russo."

"You think they're related?"

"I don't believe in coincidences."

He walked back to his car. The sun was setting. The streetlights were coming on.

He thought about Della Monroe. The way she would not look at him. The way her hands shook.

She knew something.

He lit a cigarette. He watched the coroner load the body into the van.

Somewhere in this city, someone was going to talk.

He just had to find them before Sal did.

Chapter 12: The Alderman's Visit

Alderman P.J. Mulroney was a large man with a large smile and a larger appetite.

He came to The Sapphire Lounge once a week. He never paid for anything.

Tonight he sat in Sal's office. A bottle of imported whiskey on the desk between them.

"The detective is getting close," Mulroney said.

"Malone?"

"Him and a few others. They're pushing for a task force. Federal involvement."

"That would be bad."

"Very bad." Mulroney poured himself another drink. "You need to give me something to bury this."

"What do you need?"

"A scapegoat. Someone low-level. Someone expendable."

Sal nodded. "I have a man. Frankie."

"Frankie's your enforcer. That's too high."

"Then who?"

Mulroney drank. "The girl. The singer."

"Della?"

"She was there. She saw something. Put the fear in her. Make her disappear for a while. Say she left town. Case goes cold."

Sal was quiet. His fingers tapped the desk.

"She's valuable to me."

"So is your freedom."

Sal looked at the photograph of his mother. He thought about the electric chair.

"All right," he said. "I'll handle it."

"Good man."

Mulroney finished his drink and stood. He clapped Sal on the shoulder.

"You're doing the right thing."

"I know."

But as Mulroney left, Sal felt something he had not felt in years.

Doubt.

Chapter 13: Della's Doubt

Della did not go home after her set.

She waited until the club was empty. She waited until Sal left.

Then she used her key.

The club was dark. She walked past the stage. Past the piano. Down the hall to Sal's office.

The door was locked.

She pulled out a hairpin. She had learned this trick in Memphis. A boy named Johnny Cole had taught her. He was dead now too.

The lock clicked.

She pushed the door open.

The office was neat. Everything in its place. She started with the desk.

The top drawer held pens, paper, a photograph of a woman.

The second drawer held money. Stacks of it.

The third drawer was locked.

She used the hairpin again.

Inside was a ledger. Black leather. Thick.

She opened it.

Names. Addresses. Dates. Amounts.

Payments to police officers. Payments to judges. Payments to the alderman.

And at the bottom of each page, a signature.

Sal Capri.

She turned the pages. Her hands were shaking.

Then she found the name she was looking for.

Tommy Russo. $5,000. Payment for silence.

The date was two days before he died.

She closed the ledger. She put it back. She locked the drawer.

She sat in Sal's chair and stared at the wall.

She had come looking for proof.

She had found more than she wanted.

Chapter 14: The Invitation

Sal found her in the dressing room the next night.

"Big night tomorrow," he said. "I need you to sing at a party."

"Where?"

"Gold Coast. A private residence. Important people."

"Who?"

"People who can make your career. Record executives. Theater owners. The mayor."

"The mayor?"

Sal smiled. "He loves the blues. Says it's the only honest music left."

Della looked at herself in the mirror. She looked tired.

"I'll do it."

"Good. Wear something nice. I'll send a car."

He left.

Lena came in. She closed the door.

"He's setting you up," she said.

"I know."

"Then why go?"

"Because I want to see who he knows. What he's connected to."

"That's dangerous, Della."

"Everything is dangerous now."

Lena sat down. She lit a cigarette. "You got a plan?"

"Not yet. But I'm working on it."

"What do you need?"

"A way out. When the time comes."

Lena nodded. "I know people. On the South Side. People who can hide you."

"They have to be people Sal doesn't know."

"Sal doesn't know the South Side. Not really."

Della took Lena's hand. "Thank you."

"Don't thank me yet. Thank me when we're both alive."

The door opened. Corinne walked in.

"Am I interrupting?"

"No," Della said. "We're done."

Corinne smiled. It did not reach her eyes.

"Big party tomorrow. I heard."

"Word travels fast."

"In this place, it travels faster than anything."

Corinne walked to her own mirror and sat down. She started applying lipstick.

Della watched her in the reflection.

She wondered how much Corinne knew.

Chapter 15: The Party

The house was a mansion on Lake Shore Drive.

Marble floors. Crystal chandeliers. A grand staircase that curved like a woman's hip.

Della stood in the ballroom. She wore a silver dress. Her hair was pinned up. She looked like she belonged.

She did not belong.

The men wore tuxedos. The women wore jewels. Everyone drank champagne from crystal flutes.

The champagne was illegal. Everyone knew. No one cared.

Sal stood near the fireplace. He was talking to a group of men. One of them was the mayor.

Della recognized another man. She had seen his photograph in the newspaper. Judge Harold Finch. He had sent twenty men to prison last year for possession of alcohol.

He was drinking Sal's whiskey.

She sang. Three songs. The crowd applauded. The mayor shook her hand.

She moved through the room. She smiled. She nodded. She listened.

She heard a man mention "the South Side operation." She heard another mention "the new shipments from Canada."

She saw Judge Finch shake Sal's hand.

She saw Alderman Mulroney count a stack of bills.

And she understood.

She was not just Sal's singer.

She was his alibi. His cover. His pretty face.

Every time she sang at his parties, she made him look legitimate.

She made him look like a businessman.

She sang one more song. Her voice was steady.

But inside, she was screaming.

Chapter 16: The Decision

Della did not sleep that night.

She lay in her bed and stared at the ceiling. The ledger. The bodies. The party. The judge drinking illegal whiskey.

She had to do something.

She got up at dawn. She washed her face. She put on her plainest dress.

She took Malone's card from her pocket.

She walked to the diner on the corner. She ordered coffee. She stared at the card.

The phone booth was in the back.

She walked to it. She closed the door. The receiver was heavy.

She dropped a nickel. She dialed the number.

"Sixth Precinct."

"Detective Malone, please."

"He's not in yet. Can I take a message?"

"I'll call back."

She hung up.

She stood in the booth. Her hand was shaking.

She had almost done it. She had almost called.

She stepped out of the booth. The coffee was still on the counter. Cold now.

She sat down. She stared at the window.

A black sedan pulled up outside.

Frankie got out.

He walked into the diner. He sat down across from her.

"Morning, Della."

"Frankie."

"Sal wants to see you."

"I have a show tonight."

"He wants to see you now."

He smiled. It was not a friendly smile.

Della felt the card in her pocket. She did not reach for it.

"All right," she said. "Let's go."

Chapter 17: The Tail

Frankie drove her to the club. He did not speak.

The club was empty. The chairs were up. The floor was wet.

Sal was in his office. He was eating breakfast. Eggs. Toast. Coffee.

"Sit," he said.

She sat.

"You look tired."

"I didn't sleep well."

"Me neither." He took a bite of toast. "I've been thinking about you, Della."

"Good things, I hope."

"Mixed things."

He put down his fork. He looked at her.

"I know you went through my desk."

Her heart stopped.

"I know you found the ledger."

She did not speak.

"I was hoping you would come to me. Talk to me about what you found."

"I was going to."

"When?"

"Today."

He nodded. He seemed to believe her.

"That ledger is a dangerous thing to have seen."

"I know."

"It could put a lot of people in prison."

She said nothing.

"I don't want to hurt you, Della. I like you. You're talented. You have a future."

"I know."

"But I need to know I can trust you."

"You can."

"Can I?"

She met his eyes. "Yes."

He studied her. His eyes were dark. Flat.

"All right. I believe you."

She exhaled. She had not realized she was holding her breath.

"But I'm going to have Frankie watch you for a while. Just to be sure."

"Okay."

"Good. Now get some rest. You have a show tonight."

She stood. She walked to the door.

"Della?"

She turned.

"If you try to run, Frankie will find you."

"I know."

"And if he finds you, he won't bring you back to me."

She nodded.

She walked out.

The tail had begun.

Chapter 18: The Note

She went to her dressing room. She closed the door.

She sat in front of the mirror. She looked at her face.

She was scared. But she was also angry.

She was not going to let Sal Capri control her life.

There was a knock.

"Come in."

Lena slipped inside. She closed the door behind her.

She pulled a folded piece of paper from her sleeve.

"Read this."

Della unfolded it.

"They know you were in the office. Corinne told him. She's been watching you for weeks."

Della read it twice. She looked at Lena.

"How do you know?"

"I got a friend in the kitchen. She heard Corinne and Sal talking."

Della crumpled the note. She dropped it in the trash.

"She's been following me too."

"She's been following everyone. She wants your spot."

"She can have my spot. She can have everything."

"Not everything. She doesn't have your voice."

Della smiled. It was the first time she had smiled in days.

"What do I do?"

"You run. Tonight. After the show."

"And Frankie?"

"I'll create a distraction. You go out the back. Through the old coal chute."

"The coal chute?"

"It leads to the alley behind the laundry. Frankie watches the front and the side. He doesn't know about the chute."

"How do you know about it?"

"I used to work at the laundry. Before I played piano."

Della looked at Lena. Her hard face. Her kind eyes.

"Why are you helping me?"

"Because someone helped me once. When I needed it."

She took Della's hand.

"Be ready."

Chapter 19: The Chase

The show ended at midnight.

Della sang her last note. The crowd cheered. She bowed.

She walked off stage. Her heart was pounding.

She went to her dressing room. She changed into dark clothes. She put on flat shoes.

She waited.

In the club, someone screamed.

Then another scream. Then laughter.

Lena's distraction. A staged fight. Two women pulling hair. Everyone watching.

Della opened her door. The hallway was empty.

She ran to the back. Past the kitchen. Past the storage room.

The coal chute was a small door near the floor. She pulled it open.

It was dark. Tight. She crawled inside.

The coal dust covered her hands, her knees, her face.

She pushed forward. The chute sloped down. She slid.

She hit the bottom hard. Her shoulder screamed.

She was in a narrow passage. Dark. Damp.

She crawled toward the light.

The exit was a grate. She pushed it open.

She was in the alley behind the laundry.

She stood. She brushed herself off.

Then she saw him.

Frankie stood at the end of the alley. He was not looking at her.

He was looking at the club.

She moved. Quiet. Fast.

She turned the corner. She ran.

Behind her, a shout.

She did not look back.

Chapter 20: Safe House

Della ran until her lungs burned.

She ran through alleys. Through streets. Through the empty market stalls.

She reached the South Side. The buildings changed. The streets got darker.

She found the address Lena had given her. A brownstone on Wabash Avenue.

She knocked.

The door opened. A woman stood there. Older. Gray hair. Kind eyes.

"You must be Della."

"Yes."

"Come in. Quickly."

Della stepped inside. The woman closed the door. She locked it.

"I'm Mamie Rose. Lena's friend."

"She told me."

"She told me everything."

Mamie led her to a small room in the back. A bed. A chair. A lamp.

"You'll stay here. No one will find you."

"Thank you."

"You rest now. We'll talk in the morning."

Mamie left. The door closed.

Della sat on the bed. The mattress was soft. The room was warm.

She was safe. For now.

She lay down. She closed her eyes.

For the first time in days, she slept.

When she woke, the sun was shining through the thin curtains.

She was alive.

Chapter 21: Malone's Offer

Three days passed.

Della stayed in the room. Mamie brought food. They talked.

Mamie had been a singer too, forty years ago. She had sung with King Oliver. With Ma Rainey. With the greats.

"I know what you're going through," Mamie said. "I had a man like Sal once. He owned me. Told me what to sing. Who to be."

"How did you get out?"

"I left. One night. I just walked away."

"And he let you?"

"He didn't have a choice. I knew where the bodies were buried."

Della looked at her hands. "I know where the bodies are buried too."

"Then you got leverage."

"Or a target on my back."

"Same thing, child. Same thing."

On the fourth day, there was a knock at the door.

Mamie answered. She came back with a man.

Detective Malone.

"You look better," he said.

"How did you find me?"

"Lena called me. She said you were ready."

"I didn't say I was ready."

"You ran. That means you're ready."

Malone sat down across from her. "I can protect you. I can put you in front of a grand jury. I can put Sal Capri away for life."

"And if he finds me first?"

"He won't."

"You don't know him."

"I know him better than you think. I've been watching him for five years."

Della looked at Malone. His face was tired. His suit was cheap.

But his eyes were honest.

"What do you need from me?"

"Your testimony. Everything you saw. Everything you know."

"And then what?"

"And then you walk away. Free. Clean. Start over."

She thought about it.

"All right," she said. "I'll do it."

Chapter 22: The Statement

Malone took her to the precinct at dawn.

The station was quiet. The night shift was still on. The day shift had not started.

Malone led her to a small room. A table. Two chairs. A recording machine.

"You ready?" he asked.

"No."

"That's normal."

He turned on the machine. He asked her name. She told him.

Then she told him everything.

The argument in the office. The alley. The gunshot. Tommy Russo's body hitting the ground.

She told him about the ledger. The names. The payments.

She told him about the party. The judge. The alderman. The mayor.

She told him about the newsboy. Arthur Bell. The boy she saw in the papers.

"I think Sal had him killed too," she said. "Because he saw something."

"Did you see that?"

"No. But the ledger. There's a payment. The same day."

Malone wrote it down.

When she was done, he turned off the machine.

"That's good," he said. "That's real good."

"What happens now?"

"I take this to the state's attorney. We get a warrant. We bring Sal in."

"How long?"

"A day. Maybe two."

"And until then?"

"You stay somewhere safe."

"Where?"

Malone hesitated. "I got a place. My sister's cabin. Outside the city."

"You think that's safe?"

"Safer than here. Someone in this station already tipped Sal off once. I can't trust my own people."

Della felt the fear return.

"Then let's go. Now."

Chapter 23: The Raid

They did not make it to the cabin.

Malone drove. Della sat in the passenger seat. The city fell away behind them.

They were ten miles out when the headlights appeared behind them.

Two cars. Moving fast.

Malone saw them. "Hold on."

He floored the gas. The engine roared.

The cars behind them sped up.

"Who is it?" Della asked.

"Guess."

The first car pulled alongside. A window rolled down. A gun appeared.

Malone swerved. The shot missed.

Della screamed.

Malone pushed the gas harder. The cabin was three miles ahead.

The second car pulled up on the other side.

Two guns now.

Malone slammed the brakes.

The cars flew past. They skidded. Turned around.

Malone reversed. Tires screaming. He spun the car around.

He drove back toward the city.

"They'll expect us to go to the precinct," he said.

"Then where?"

"My place. It's the last place they'll look."

The cars were behind them again. Gaining.

Malone turned hard. Into an alley. Through a yard. Onto another street.

The cars followed.

"Dammit," Malone said.

He turned again. The wheels caught gravel. The car fishtailed.

A shot. The back window exploded.

Della ducked. Glass rained down on her.

Malone turned again. The cars were still behind them.

"This is not good," he said.

Chapter 24: Underground

Malone drove through a fence.

Wood splintered. The car bounced. They were in a field now. Dark. Empty.

The headlights behind them disappeared.

Malone kept driving. No lights. Just the moon.

He stopped behind an abandoned barn.

"We walk from here."

"Where are we?"

"Nowhere. That's the point."

They got out. Malone grabbed a bag from the trunk. He led her through the field.

They walked for an hour. Through cornfields. Through dry creek beds.

They reached a small house. Wooden. A single light in the window.

"My sister's place," Malone said. "The one I was taking you to."

"The cars—"

"Took the long way. They won't find us here."

They went inside. The house was small. Clean. A kitchen. A bedroom. A wood stove.

"You'll stay here. I'll go back to the city. Get the warrant."

"Don't leave me alone."

"I have to. It's the only way to end this."

He looked at her. His face was tired. Kind.

"I'll be back by tomorrow night. Don't open the door for anyone."

"Anyone?"

"Anyone."

He left.

Della locked the door. She sat on the floor. The house was silent.

She was alone.

Completely alone.

She stayed on the floor until the sun came up.

Chapter 25: The Blues Mother

A week passed.

Malone did not come back.

Della stayed in the cabin. She read the books on the shelf. She played the old piano in the corner.

She sang to keep herself company.

On the eighth day, she heard a car.

She looked out the window. It was not Malone.

It was Mamie Rose.

Della opened the door. "How did you find me?"

"Lena told me. She heard from Malone."

"Is he okay?"

"He's suspended. Internal affairs. They said he mishandled a witness."

"Me."

"You."

Mamie came inside. She looked around the cabin.

"Can't stay here forever."

"I know."

"Lena found you a way out. A train. Tomorrow night. To Memphis."

"Memphis?"

"You got family there?"

"A cousin. We haven't spoken in years."

"Time to make up."

Della sat down. "What about my testimony?"

"It's already on record. It'll come out at trial. If there is a trial."

"What do you mean?"

"Sal's lawyers are good. They might bury it."

"Then what was the point?"

"The point is you're alive. That's the point."

Mamie sat beside her. She took her hand.

"You can't save everyone, child. Sometimes you just save yourself."

Chapter 26: The Pianist's Secret

Lena came to the cabin that night.

She brought food. Money. A train ticket.

"You look terrible," she said.

"Thanks."

"I brought you a dress. You'll need to look different."

The dress was plain. Brown. A widow's dress.

"I'm not going to a funeral."

"You might be. If you don't get on that train."

Lena sat down. She lit a cigarette.

"There's something I need to tell you."

"What?"

"Corinne's not the only one working for Sal."

Della looked at her. "What are you saying?"

"I'm saying I've been doing jobs for him too. Small things. Delivering messages. Watching people."

"Lena—"

"He pays me. I need the money. My mother's sick. She needs medicine."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I want you to know. Before you go. So you know who you're leaving behind."

Della looked at her friend. Her face was hard. Her eyes were wet.

"I'm sorry," Lena said.

"I know."

"I didn't tell him where you were. I swear."

"I know."

Della took her hand.

"Come with me."

"I can't. My mother."

"Then stay safe."

"I will."

They sat in silence. The fire crackled.

"This is goodbye," Della said.

"Yeah," Lena said. "It is."

Chapter 27: The Trap

The next morning, Malone appeared.

He looked worse than before. Dark circles. Unshaven.

"They reinstated me," he said. "Internal affairs cleared me. No evidence of misconduct."

"Because there was none."

"Doesn't matter. They needed to look like they were doing something."

"What happens now?"

"We set a trap. A sting. We grab Sal when he's moving product."

"And me?"

"You're the bait."

Della felt her stomach drop. "No."

"It's the only way. He has to make a move. He has to take the shipment himself. We have it on good authority he's bringing in a truckload from Canada tomorrow night."

"Where?"

"The old stockyards. He uses them for distribution."

Malone looked at her. "You don't have to be in the room. Just nearby. So he knows you're there. So he makes a mistake."

"And if he doesn't?"

"Then we try something else."

Della thought about it. The train to Memphis left in two days.

"One condition," she said.

"Name it."

"After this, I'm gone. No more favors. No more bait."

"Agreed."

She held out her hand.

He shook it.

"Tomorrow night," he said.

"Tomorrow night."

Chapter 28: The Betrayal

The stockyards were a maze of pens and loading docks.

Della stood in a second-floor office. The window looked down at the main floor.

Malone was outside with eight men. Handpicked. Men he trusted.

"Remember," he said over the radio. "You're here to witness. You stay out of sight."

"I remember."

The truck arrived at eleven. A flatbed. Canvas cover.

Men got out. They opened the back. Crates of whiskey.

Then Sal arrived. He stepped out of a black sedan. He was smiling.

"Good evening," he said. "Let's get this done."

Della watched. Her heart was pounding.

Then she saw something wrong.

A car pulled up behind Sal's. Two men got out. They were not Sal's men.

They were cops.

Uniformed. With badges.

Malone's voice crackled over the radio. "Who called the uniforms? Who called the uniforms?"

No one answered.

The cops walked up to Sal. They shook his hand.

The trap had been tipped.

Della turned. She ran for the stairs.

She made it to the ground floor. Malone was there. His face was white with rage.

"We're compromised. Get out. Now."

They ran.

Behind them, someone yelled.

A shot rang out.

Chapter 29: Sal's Offer

They made it to Malone's car. He drove like a madman.

"The whole thing was a setup," he said. "They knew. They knew everything."

"Who knew?"

"Someone in the department. Someone high up. Maybe the alderman himself."

"What do we do now?"

"I don't know."

They drove in silence. The city passed by. Dark. Cold.

Malone pulled over near the lake. He shut off the engine.

"I can't protect you anymore," he said. "Not like this."

"I know."

"There's a bus station. Three blocks east. You take a bus to St. Louis. Then another to Memphis. No train. They'll be watching the trains."

"Okay."

He reached into his jacket. He pulled out an envelope.

"Money. Enough to get you there and then some."

She took it. "Thank you."

"I'm sorry I couldn't do more."

"You did enough."

She opened the door. The cold wind hit her face.

"Della."

She turned.

"Be careful."

She nodded. She walked toward the bus station.

But before she got there, a car pulled up beside her.

The window rolled down.

Sal's face.

"Get in," he said.

She did not move.

"Get in, Della. I'm not going to hurt you. I want to talk."

"About what?"

"About your future."

She looked at the bus station. It was three blocks away.

She looked at Sal. His face was calm.

"I'm listening."

Chapter 30: The Temptation

She got in the car.

Sal drove. He did not look at her.

"You know, I never wanted this," he said.

"Wanted what?"

"Any of it. The killing. The hiding. I wanted to be a legitimate businessman."

"But you're not."

"No. I'm not."

He pulled over. They were on a quiet street. Trees. Houses. A church on the corner.

"I have a proposition for you."

"I'm listening."

"You drop your testimony. You come back to the club. You get your recording contract. I make you a star."

"And what do you get?"

"I get my alibi back. I get my witness to disappear. I get to live another day."

"That's not a proposition. That's blackmail."

"It's business."

Della looked at him. His face was open. Sincere.

"You're a murderer," she said.

"I'm a survivor. Same as you."

She thought about the bus station. The money in her pocket. The long road to Memphis.

She thought about The Sapphire Lounge. The lights. The music. The dream.

"If I come back, what happens to Malone?"

"Nothing. He goes back to his life. You go back to yours."

"And Corinne?"

"She stays second singer."

"And Frankie?"

"Frankie does what I tell him."

She looked out the window. The church was lit up. A cross on the steeple.

"Give me a day," she said.

"Take tonight. Tomorrow morning, I want your answer."

He drove her back to the cabin. She got out.

She stood in the cold and watched his taillights disappear.

She had a choice to make.

A real choice.

Chapter 31: The Recording Contract

Mamie Rose was waiting at the cabin.

"You look like you seen a ghost."

"I saw Sal."

"And he let you live?"

"He made me an offer."

Mamie raised an eyebrow. "What kind of offer?"

"My career. Recording contract. Everything I wanted."

"And what does he want?"

"My silence."

Mamie sat down. She looked at Della for a long time.

"You know what I think?"

"What?"

"I think you're considering it."

Della did not answer.

"Child, I have seen women give up everything for less. A recording contract. That's a dream. But whose dream? His or yours?"

"Both."

"Both. That's the problem. He made you. He owns you. That contract he's offering? It'll have chains on it. You won't be able to breathe without his permission."

Della sat down across from her. "I'm tired of running."

"I know."

"I'm tired of being scared."

"I know that too."

"But I'm more tired of being owned."

Mamie smiled. "That's my girl."

A knock at the door.

Della opened it.

A man stood there. Suit. Hat. A briefcase.

"Della Monroe?"

"Yes."

"I'm Arthur Patterson. Okeh Records. I believe Mr. Capri mentioned I'd be calling."

Chapter 32: The Proof

Della sat across from Arthur Patterson at the small kitchen table.

He was younger than she expected. Maybe thirty. Nervous hands.

"Mr. Capri speaks very highly of you," he said.

"I'm sure he does."

"He says you're the most talented singer in Chicago."

"He's biased."

"Maybe. But I heard you sing at the Coliseum. He's not wrong."

He opened his briefcase. He pulled out a contract.

"Three years. Six sides a year. Touring support. Advances against royalties."

Della looked at the contract. Her name was typed at the top.

"Can I think about it?"

"Of course. But Mr. Capri said you were eager to sign."

"I am eager. But I'm also careful."

Patterson nodded. He put the contract back.

"Take your time. My card is inside."

He stood. He shook her hand. He left.

Della looked at the card. Arthur Patterson. A&R. Okeh Records.

Mamie came back in. "Well?"

"He's real. The contract is real."

"So what's the catch?"

"The catch is Sal."

Della turned the card over. On the back, in small handwriting:

"Meet me at the old Okeh studio. Midnight. Bring proof."

She looked at Mamie.

"Proof of what?"

"He wants proof of what Sal did."

"Why?"

"Because Okeh has a reputation. They don't want to be connected to a gangster."

Della looked at the card.

This was her chance. If she could give Patterson proof that Sal was a criminal, Okeh would have a reason to cut ties with him.

And she would have a way out.

"Where's the Okeh studio?"

"On Washington Street. But it's been closed for years."

"Then that's where I'm going."

Chapter 33: The Handoff

Della went back to The Sapphire Lounge.

It was stupid. It was dangerous. But it was the only way.

She needed the ledger.

She went through the coal chute again. Through the passage. Up through the basement.

The club was closed. Dark. Empty.

She crept to Sal's office. The door was locked.

She picked the lock. Her hands were steady now.

She found the ledger. Still in the drawer.

She tucked it into her coat. She turned to leave.

The light came on.

Corinne stood in the doorway. A gun in her hand.

"I knew you'd be back," Corinne said.

"Corinne. Put the gun down."

"Or what? You'll sing at me?"

"I'm not here to hurt you."

"You're here to destroy everything. Sal's business. My job. My life."

"Sal killed people, Corinne. Innocent people."

"I don't care."

"Yes, you do. That's why you're pointing a gun at me. Because you care too much."

Corinne's hand shook.

"I could shoot you," she said. "I could say you broke in. I'd be a hero."

"You'd be a murderer. Same as Sal."

Corinne stared at her. The gun wavered.

"Go," she said. "Before I change my mind."

Della moved. Past Corinne. Through the door. Down the hall.

She did not run. She walked.

She reached the alley. The cold air hit her.

She had the ledger.

She ran.

Chapter 34: Midnight Train

The Okeh studio was a three-story building on Washington Street.

Dark. Boarded up. A sign hung crooked over the door.

Della arrived at midnight. The street was empty.

She knocked on the side door.

Patterson opened it. He looked nervous.

"You got it?"

She held up the ledger.

"Let's see."

She opened it. Showed him the pages. The names. The payments.

"This is enough to put him away for life," he said.

"I know."

"Why are you giving this to me?"

"Because I want to be free. And because Okeh needs to know who they're dealing with."

Patterson nodded. He took the ledger.

"I'll have our lawyers look at this. If it's real, we'll cut ties with Capri. And we'll honor your contract. Direct. No manager."

"And if it's not enough?"

"Then you disappear. And I never saw you."

He held out his hand.

She shook it.

"Thank you," she said.

"Thank me when you're on the other side."

She left. She walked to the train station. She bought a ticket to Memphis.

The train left at 3 AM.

She sat on the bench. The station was empty.

She watched the clock.

Two hours.

She could make it.

Chapter 35: The Shootout

The train was boarding.

Della stood in line. Her ticket in her hand. The conductor was checking passes.

She stepped forward.

"Ticket?"

She handed it over.

The conductor looked at it. Nodded. "Car seven."

She walked toward the train.

Then she saw him.

Frankie. At the far end of the platform.

He was looking right at her.

She ran.

"Hey!" Frankie shouted. "Stop!"

She ran toward the train. The doors were closing.

She jumped. She grabbed the railing. She pulled herself up.

A shot.

Wood splintered beside her head.

She crawled into the car. People screamed.

Another shot.

She ran through the car. People dove to the floor.

The train started moving.

Frankie appeared at the far door. Gun raised.

Della kept running. Through the next car. Through the next.

Another shot. Glass shattered.

She reached the last car. The door to the platform was open.

Frankie was still coming.

Then Lena appeared.

Lena stepped out from between two seats. She had a gun.

"Get down!" Lena shouted.

Della dropped.

The gun went off.

Frankie staggered. He looked down at his chest. Blood spread across his white shirt.

He fell.

Lena grabbed Della. "Come on."

They jumped from the train. Onto the gravel. They rolled.

The train kept moving. Gaining speed.

Frankie lay still on the platform.

"Is he—"

"Dead? I hope so."

Lena pulled Della to her feet.

"We have to go. Now."

They ran into the dark.

Chapter 36: Sal's Basement

The basement was cold. Damp.

Della sat on the concrete floor. Her wrists were bound. Her ankles too.

She did not know how they had found her.

Lena had led her to a safe house. A room above a bar. They had been there for two hours.

Then the door broke open.

Men with guns. Lena had fought. They had hit her. She went down.

They had taken Della.

Now she was in Sal's basement. The warehouse near the stockyards.

She had been here before. The night of the failed sting.

The door opened.

Sal walked in. He carried a chair. He sat down across from her.

"You have caused me a lot of trouble," he said.

"I know."

"Frankie is dead. My best man. Dead."

"I know."

"My shipment was seized. The federales have it now."

"I know."

"Do you know what happens next?"

"I have an idea."

Sal leaned forward. "I don't want to kill you, Della. I never wanted that."

"Then don't."

"I don't have a choice. You know too much. You've seen too much."

"So have a lot of people."

"Those people are handled. You're the last loose end."

He stood. He walked to the door.

"Someone will be here in an hour. They'll make it quick. I promise you that."

He left.

The door locked.

Della sat in the dark.

She had one hour to live.

Chapter 37: The Confession

The hour passed.

No one came.

Della sat in the dark. She listened. Nothing.

Then Sal returned.

He looked different. Shaken. He carried a bottle of whiskey.

"I can't do it," he said.

"Can't do what?"

"I can't kill you."

He sat down. He opened the bottle. He drank.

"I've been thinking. About my mother. About what she would think of me now."

"She would be ashamed."

"She would be right."

He drank again.

"You want to know the truth, Della? The whole truth?"

"Tell me."

"Tommy Russo was my friend. We grew up together in Sicily. We came to America on the same boat."

"Then why did you kill him?"

"Because he was going to testify. Against me. Against everything I built."

"He was your friend."

"He was a threat."

Sal looked at her. His eyes were red.

"I killed Arthur Bell too. The newsboy. He saw me dump Tommy's body. He was just a kid. A kid who sold newspapers."

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I'm tired. I'm tired of lying. I'm tired of being the monster."

He put down the bottle.

"I'm going to let you go."

"What?"

"I'm going to let you go. But you have to promise me something."

"What?"

"You leave Chicago. You never come back. You forget my name."

"Done."

He stood. He pulled a knife from his pocket. He cut her bonds.

She stood. Her legs were shaking.

"Go. The back door is unlocked."

She walked to the door. She turned back.

"Sal?"

"What?"

"Come with me."

He laughed. It was a hollow sound.

"Where would I go?"

"Away. Anywhere."

"I can't. This is my city. My empire. I built it."

"And it built you a grave."

She walked out.

The door closed behind her.

Chapter 38: The Lock

Della was free.

She stood outside the warehouse. The air was cold. The sky was gray.

She did not know where to go.

She walked. Her legs carried her without thinking.

She passed the stockyards. Passed the train tracks. Passed the rows of abandoned houses.

She reached a diner. It was open. 24 hours.

She went inside. She ordered coffee.

She sat in the booth and tried to think.

Malone. She needed to find Malone.

But she did not have his number. She did not have his address.

She had nothing.

The coffee was bitter. She drank it anyway.

The door opened. A man walked in.

He was tall. Thin. He wore a long coat.

He sat at the counter.

She looked at him. He looked at her.

Something in his face was wrong.

She stood. She walked to the bathroom.

The window was small. She could fit.

She pushed it open. She climbed through.

She dropped into the alley.

She ran.

Behind her, the door banged open.

"Stop!"

She did not stop.

She ran through alleys. Through streets. Through the dark.

She had to find Malone.

She had to end this.

Chapter 39: The Phones

Malone was at his desk.

He had been working for eighteen hours. He had not slept.

The phone rang.

"Malone."

"It's me."

"Della? Where are you?"

"I don't know. A diner. Somewhere on the South Side."

"Stay there. I'll come get you."

"No. They're everywhere. Sal's men, the cops, everyone."

"Then tell me where to meet you."

She was quiet for a moment.

"The old Okeh studio. Washington Street. One hour."

"I'll be there."

"Malone?"

"Yeah?"

"Bring help. Real help."

She hung up.

Malone looked at the phone.

He had an idea.

He opened his drawer. He pulled out a file.

A case file. Five years old. A federal agent had tried to build a case against Sal. He had failed.

But the agent was still around. Still working.

Malone picked up the phone again.

He dialed a number.

"Federal Bureau of Investigation. How may I direct your call?"

"Agent James Callahan, please."

"One moment."

A click. Then a voice.

"Callahan."

"This is Detective Malone, Chicago PD. I have a witness who can put Sal Capri away for life."

"I'm listening."

"But I need backup. Real backup. Not the corrupt kind."

"How many men do you need?"

"I'll take whatever you can give me."

"Give me two hours."

"We might not have two hours."

"Then make it one."

The line went dead.

Malone stood. He grabbed his coat.

He had a federal agent. A witness. And a chance.

It was all he needed.

Chapter 40: Corinne's Regret

Della did not go to the Okeh studio.

She went back to The Sapphire Lounge.

It was stupid. It was dangerous. But she needed answers.

She went through the coal chute. Through the basement. Up the stairs.

The club was closed. Empty.

She went to Corinne's dressing room.

Corinne was there. She was packing.

"You're supposed to be dead," Corinne said.

"Change of plans."

"You should leave. Before Sal finds out."

"I need your help."

"My help?"

"You helped me once. At the club. You let me go."

"I made a mistake."

"Make another one."

Corinne stopped packing. She looked at Della.

"What do you want?"

"Sal's schedule. Where he'll be tonight."

"Why?"

"Because I'm going to end this."

Corinne stared at her. Her face was unreadable.

Then she opened her purse. She pulled out a piece of paper.

"He's at the warehouse. He has a meeting with the alderman at midnight."

"How do you know?"

"Because I'm supposed to be there. To sing."

"Then go. And tell me what happens."

Corinne nodded.

"I'm sorry," she said. "For everything."

"I know."

"I was jealous. Of your voice. Of your future."

"I know."

"I wanted what you had."

"You can still have it. Just not through Sal."

Corinne wiped her eyes.

"Be careful," she said.

"You too."

Della left. She went to meet Malone.

Chapter 41: The Tunnel

Malone was waiting at the Okeh studio.

He was not alone. A man stood beside him. Tall. Serious. Federal suit.

"This is Agent Callahan," Malone said.

"The FBI?"

"Prohibition Unit," Callahan said. "Same thing."

"I have Sal's location. The warehouse. Midnight. He's meeting the alderman."

"How do you know?"

"A source."

Callahan nodded. "We've been wanting Mulroney for years. This is good."

"Here's the problem," Malone said. "The warehouse has one entrance. One exit. He'll see us coming."

"There's another way," Della said.

Both men looked at her.

"The Sapphire Lounge has a tunnel. An old speakeasy escape route. It connects to the building next to the warehouse."

"How do you know?"

"Corinne told me. She said it was used during the old days. When the Feds raided."

Callahan looked at Malone. "This could work."

"We'd need a map," Malone said.

"I can draw it," Della said. "I've been in the basement."

She picked up a pencil. She drew.

The tunnel. The warehouse. The stockyards.

"There," she said. "That's the way in."

Callahan studied the map.

"Tonight," he said. "We end this."

Chapter 42: Malone's March

They gathered at midnight.

Malone. Callahan. Two federal agents. Three Chicago cops that Malone trusted.

And Della.

"You're not coming," Malone said.

"I'm the only one who knows the tunnel."

"You can draw a map."

"I can lead you. In the dark. Blind. You'll get lost."

Malone looked at Callahan.

"She's got a point," Callahan said.

"Fine. But you stay behind me. No matter what."

"Agreed."

They moved through the alley. Dark. Silent.

They reached the back of the Sapphire Lounge. The coal chute.

Della led them through. Down the passage. Into the basement.

She found the tunnel entrance. A false wall. Behind it, a narrow passage.

"It's dark," she said. "Stay close."

They moved through the tunnel. Single file. The walls were damp. The floor was dirt.

It took ten minutes to reach the other end.

A wooden door. Rusted hinges.

Della pushed it open.

They were in the warehouse basement.

Above them, voices.

Sal's voice. Mulroney's voice.

Malone looked at his men.

"Ready?"

They nodded.

Malone drew his gun.

"Let's go."

Chapter 43: The Loyal Few

They climbed the stairs.

The warehouse was vast. Crates stacked to the ceiling. A single light hung from the rafters.

Sal stood in the center. Mulroney beside him. Two guards at the doors.

"You're surrounded," Malone said.

Sal turned. He did not look surprised.

"Detective. I was wondering when you'd show up."

"You're under arrest. You too, Mulroney."

Mulroney laughed. "On what charges?"

"Murder. Bribery. Violation of the Volstead Act. Take your pick."

"You have no evidence."

"I have a ledger. I have a witness. I have a tunnel full of federal agents."

Sal's face changed. He looked at Della.

"You brought them here."

"I did."

"How?"

"Corinne."

Sal's jaw tightened. "I should have killed her when I had the chance."

"You should have done a lot of things differently."

The guards reached for their guns.

Callahan stepped forward. "Federal agents. Drop your weapons."

The guards looked at Sal.

Sal looked at Della.

"It's over, Sal," she said.

He stared at her. A long moment.

Then he nodded.

"Put the guns down," he said.

The guards dropped them.

Malone stepped forward. He pulled out handcuffs.

"Salvatore Capri, you have the right to remain silent..."

Chapter 44: The Warehouse

The handcuffs clicked.

Sal did not resist. He stood still while Malone read him his rights.

Mulroney was not so calm.

"This is outrageous," he said. "I'm an alderman. I have rights."

"You have the right to shut up," Callahan said. "That's about it."

The federal agents moved through the warehouse. They found crates of whiskey. Cases of gin. Barrels of beer.

"This is enough to put him away for ten years," Callahan said.

"That's just the alcohol," Malone said. "The murder charges are the real weight."

Sal looked at Della. His face was unreadable.

"You did what you had to do," he said.

"I know."

"I don't blame you."

"I don't care if you do."

He almost smiled. "You're tough, Della. Tougher than I gave you credit for."

"I learned from the best."

"Who?"

"You. You taught me how to survive."

He nodded. He looked away.

The agents led him out. Mulroney followed, still protesting.

Della stood in the empty warehouse.

Malone came up beside her.

"It's over," he said.

"Is it?"

"For you, yes. You're free."

She looked at him. His face was tired. Relieved.

"What about the trial?"

"Six months. Maybe a year. You'll have to testify."

"I know."

"After that, you can go anywhere. Do anything."

"I know."

She walked to the door. The night air was cold. The stars were out.

She was free.

It felt strange.

Chapter 45: Frankie's Stand

The papers called her a hero.

Della Monroe. The Singer Who Brought Down a Kingpin.

She hated the name.

She stayed in Mamie's apartment. She did not go out.

Lena came to visit. She brought food. News.

"Frankie's alive," she said.

"What?"

"She shot him in the chest. He lived. He's in the county jail."

"Will he talk?"

"He's already talking. Singing like a canary. He's giving up everyone. Sal. Mulroney. The whole operation."

Della sat down. "Then it's really over."

"Almost. They're still looking for Corinne."

"Corinne?"

"She ran. After Sal was arrested. No one knows where she went."

"Good for her."

"Yeah." Lena sat down. "Good for her."

They sat in silence. The afternoon sun came through the window.

"What will you do now?" Lena asked.

"I got a letter from Okeh. They want me to come to New York. Record."

"That's big."

"I know."

"Are you going?"

Della looked out the window. The city spread before her. Smoke. Steel. Sky.

"Yeah," she said. "I think I am."

"Good."

"It's a long way from Memphis."

"You've come a long way from Memphis."

Della smiled.

"Yeah," she said. "I have."

Chapter 46: Sal's Escape

Three weeks passed.

Della packed her things. A suitcase. A trunk. The dress she had worn the first night at The Sapphire Lounge.

She was leaving tomorrow.

Then the news came.

Sal had escaped.

He had been transferred to the county courthouse for a hearing. The van had been ambushed. Two guards were dead. Sal was gone.

Malone showed up at Mamie's door. His face was gray.

"He's coming for you," he said.

"He doesn't know where I am."

"He'll find out."

Della felt the fear return. Cold. Sharp.

"What do I do?"

"You leave tonight. Not tomorrow. Tonight."

"Where?"

"Anywhere. Just don't tell me where. I don't want to know."

He handed her a gun.

"Do you know how to use this?"

"Point and shoot."

"Aim first. Then shoot."

She took it. It was heavy.

"Thank you," she said.

"Don't thank me. Stay alive."

He left.

Della looked at the gun. Then at her suitcase.

She had been so close.

So close to being free.

She picked up the suitcase. She walked to the door.

Behind her, a sound.

The window broke.

Chapter 47: The Rooftop

Della ran.

She ran through the apartment. Through the kitchen. Out the back door.

The fire escape was rusted. She climbed.

She heard him below her. Sal. His voice was calm.

"Come back, Della. Let's talk."

She climbed faster. The fire escape shook.

She reached the roof.

The city spread out below her. Lights. Buildings. The lake in the distance.

She ran across the tar paper. Her feet slipped.

Sal came up behind her. He was breathing hard.

"Where will you go?" he asked.

"Away from you."

"That's not far enough."

He walked toward her. His hands were empty.

"I don't want to hurt you," he said.

"You've already hurt everyone I know."

"I know. I'm sorry."

"Sorry doesn't bring Tommy back. Or Arthur. Or any of them."

"No. It doesn't."

He stopped. He was ten feet away.

"But I'm still sorry."

He took another step.

Della pulled out the gun.

"Don't," she said.

"You won't shoot me."

"Try me."

He took another step.

Della's finger tightened on the trigger.

"Last warning," she said.

He kept walking.

Chapter 48: The Fall

He lunged.

Della stepped aside.

Sal's momentum carried him forward. His hands reached for her. Grabbed her arm.

The gun went off.

The sound was enormous. It bounced off the rooftops.

Sal staggered. He looked down. Blood spread across his shirt.

He had been shot. But it was not Della's bullet.

Malone stood on the fire escape. His gun was raised.

"Drop," he said.

Della dropped.

Sal looked at Malone. Then at Della. Then at the sky.

He took a step backward.

The edge of the roof.

Another step.

"Sal," Della said. "Don't."

He looked at her. His eyes were clear.

"I told you," he said. "I'm tired."

He stepped back again.

And then he was gone.

The fall was three stories. He landed on the pavement below.

Della ran to the edge.

Sal lay on the ground. His arms were spread. His eyes were open.

He was dead.

Malone came up beside her. He put his hand on her shoulder.

"It's over," he said.

Della looked down at the body.

It was over.

She felt nothing.

Chapter 49: The Aftermath

The papers had a field day.

"Capri Plummets to Death." "Singer Survives Kingpin's Last Stand." "Alderman Mulroney Indicted."

Della did not read them.

She stayed at Mamie's. She played the piano. She sang.

Malone came to visit. He was promoted to captain.

"You look good," he said.

"I feel good."

"The trial's in two months. Your testimony is all they need."

"I'll be there."

"After that, you're done. Free and clear."

"I know."

Malone sat down. He looked older. Softer.

"I'm going to miss you," he said.

"You'll find another singer to save."

"I don't want another singer."

She smiled. "You're a good man, Michael Malone."

"Don't tell anyone. I have a reputation."

She laughed. It was the first time she had laughed in months.

He stood. He held out his hand.

"Take care of yourself, Della."

"You too."

He left.

Della looked out the window. The city was gray. Cold. Beautiful.

She was ready to leave.

Chapter 50: The Sapphire Throat

The Sapphire Lounge was closed.

The yellow tape was gone. The doors were locked. A For Lease sign hung in the window.

Della stood across the street. She looked at the building.

She remembered the first night she sang there. The lights. The crowd. The feeling that she had made it.

She had made it. Just not the way she expected.

She had a train to catch. A contract to sign. A life to start.

But first, she had one more song to sing.

She walked to the door. It was locked. She used her key.

The club was dark. Musty. The chairs were stacked. The piano was silent.

She walked to the stage. She stood in the center.

The room was empty. But she could hear it. The clinking glasses. The laughter. The applause.

She opened her mouth.

She sang.

No band. No microphone. Just her voice in the empty room.

She sang about Chicago. About the river. About the men who died.

She sang about freedom.

When she was done, the room was silent.

She walked off the stage. She grabbed her suitcase.

She locked the door behind her.

The train station was three blocks away.

She walked. The wind was cold. The sky was clear.

She thought about Sal. About Tommy. About Arthur Bell.

She thought about Lena. About Mamie. About Malone.

She thought about the road ahead.

She reached the station. She bought her ticket. She boarded the train.

The whistle blew. The train pulled out.

Chicago fell away behind her.

She leaned her head against the window. The countryside passed by.

She was free.

She closed her eyes.

And she sang.
