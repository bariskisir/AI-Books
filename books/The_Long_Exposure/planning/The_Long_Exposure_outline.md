# The Long Exposure — Outline

## Premise

At the Vela Ridge Observatory, a century-old facility sold off and slated to go dark, the last staff astronomer, Tomas Reye, has the task of closing it. Before the great archive of glass photographic plates — an entire century of recorded sky — is crated and dispersed, a young digitization technician, Sable Okonkwo, is sent to scan it. On a plate exposed one winter night in 1970, she finds a point of light that no catalogue accounts for, flagged in faded grease pencil by a hand neither of them recognizes. The hand belonged to Ada Mireu, a plate assistant and "computer" who measured the heavens by hand in an era that did not let women near the eyepiece, who flagged a guest star nobody believed, and who walked off the mountain in the spring of 1971 and out of the observatory's history. As Tomas and Sable follow the anomaly through ledgers and cold rooms, the past opens — and Tomas must reckon with the fact that he was there, a junior man of twenty-two, the night Ada was sent away, and that he said nothing.

A novel about attention as a form of love: the light of dead things still arriving, who gets remembered, and the late, necessary work of giving a name back to the dark. Adult characters; clean subject matter. Spare, concrete prose; no lyrical refrains.

## Core Cast

- **Tomas Reye**, 68 — the last staff astronomer at Vela Ridge; methodical, reticent, widowed. Was a junior observer on the mountain in 1970–71.
- **Sable Okonkwo**, 29 — digitization technician hired by the foundation; sharp, impatient, exact; granddaughter of a Lagos surveyor; learning what the work costs.
- **Ada Mireu** (1968–71 thread) — plate assistant and computer; brilliant, overlooked, dry-humored; the one who saw the guest star first.
- **Director Hessel** — observatory director in 1970; ambitious, careful with credit, fluent in the soft cruelties of hierarchy.
- **Ignatz Vorne** — night-assistant and telescope operator in 1970; Ada's only real ally on the mountain; older now, still living in the valley town.
- **Marisol Reye** — Tomas's late wife, a schoolteacher in the valley; present in memory.
- **Petra Lund** — foundation liaison overseeing the sale and closure; not a villain, but the instrument of the ending.
- **Dr. Okwe / the journal** — the present-day astrophysics establishment Tomas and Sable must convince.
- **Liane Mireu** — Ada's daughter, found late; closes the loop.

## Structure

Two interleaved timelines. **Present** (autumn, the closing of the observatory): Tomas and Sable. **Past** (1968–1971): Ada on the mountain, with the young Tomas at its edges. The braid tightens until, near the end, the two threads meet at the same night seen from two sides. Roughly four movements of ~12–13 chapters.

## 50 Chapter Plan

### Part One — The Closing (1–12)
1. **The Last Astronomer** — Tomas climbs to the dark dome on the morning the sale closes; we learn the mountain is being switched off and he is the one to do it.
2. **The Foundation's Girl** — Sable arrives with crates and a scanner; first friction between her schedule and his ceremony.
3. **The Cold Room** — Tomas shows Sable the plate vault: a century of sky in glass, alphabetized by night.
4. **First Light, Last Light** — Sable scans her first plates; Tomas explains what a photographic plate is and why it still matters.
5. **The Hand in Grease Pencil** — On plate VR-1970-0114, Sable finds a circled point of light and three letters she can't place: "A.M."
6. **Not in the Catalogue** — They check the modern catalogues; the star isn't there, isn't an artifact, isn't a flaw in the glass.
7. **The Logbook** — The night logbook for that date is in a hand that isn't the duty astronomer's; the margin note is initialed A.M.
8. **A Name Nobody Keeps** — Tomas searches the staff rolls; "computer, A. Mireu" appears in 1968, vanishes after spring 1971. No photograph, no forwarding address.
9. **Marisol's Chair** — Evening; Tomas's grief for his wife established; he avoids the question of why Ada's initials make his hands cold.
10. **Ignatz in the Valley** — Tomas drives down to find the one man left who was there in 1970; Ignatz is old, lucid, and wary.
11. **What the Plate Remembers** — Ignatz confirms: there was a woman who measured better than any of them, and a star nobody would let her have.
12. **The Three Nights** — Sable finds two more plates: the star present on Jan 14 and 16, gone by Jan 19. A genuine transient. The hunt begins in earnest.

### Part Two — Ada (13–25)
13. **Computer** — 1968: Ada arrives at Vela Ridge to measure plates by hand; the rule that women do not operate the telescope.
14. **The Measuring Engine** — Ada's craft: blink comparators, position micrometers, the discipline of the eye; she is faster and truer than the men.
15. **Ignatz, Night Assistant** — Ada and Ignatz form a quiet alliance over the long cold nights; he runs the dome, she runs the numbers.
16. **Director Hessel** — Hessel recognizes Ada's value and the inconvenience of it; he keeps her in the basement and her name off the papers.
17. **The Young Man from the Valley** — 1970: Tomas Reye, 22, hired as a junior observer; bright, deferential, hungry to belong. He notices Ada.
18. **A Borrowed Eye** — Against the rule, Ignatz lets Ada look through the eyepiece once; what she sees changes how she measures.
19. **January 14** — Ada, checking the night's plates, finds a point of light where the catalogue says nothing should be.
20. **The Second Plate** — January 16: it's still there, and brighter; she begins the calculations that could make it a discovery.
21. **The Rule of Credit** — Ada brings it to Hessel; the politics of who may "find" a star; he tells her to be certain before she embarrasses the observatory.
22. **January 19** — The star is gone. A transient — exactly the kind of thing that, confirmed, makes a career. Ada has the only timed record.
23. **The Plate That Walked** — Ada documents everything in her notebooks; Tomas helps her at night, half in love with the work, half with her seriousness.
24. **Hessel's Telegram** — Hessel cables a colleague at a larger institution; the discovery begins, quietly, to change owners.
25. **The Spring Meeting** — A review where Ada's transient is presented — without her name; the young Tomas in the room, saying nothing.

### Part Three — The Anomaly (26–38)
26. **The Scan Confirms** — Present: Sable's high-resolution scans prove the point is astrophysical, not emulsion; its position is exact across three plates.
27. **What It Could Be** — Tomas and Sable work the physics: a recurrent nova, a flare, a foreground event — and how to tell after forty years.
28. **The Missing Notebooks** — Ada's measurement notebooks should be in the archive. They aren't. Someone removed them.
29. **Petra Lund's Deadline** — The foundation liaison arrives; the crating schedule is fixed; Tomas asks for time he may not get.
30. **The Basement Index** — Sable finds the old card index where the computers logged their work; a whole stratum of uncredited labor surfaces.
31. **Ignatz's Box** — Ignatz reveals he kept one of Ada's notebooks for fifty years, afraid to throw it away, afraid to read it.
32. **The Numbers Hold** — Ada's hand-measured positions match Sable's scans to within the error of the glass; the woman was right, and exact.
33. **Hessel's Paper** — In the library, Tomas finds the 1972 paper that reported the transient under Hessel's name and a junior author's — and a single buried acknowledgment of "staff assistance."
34. **The Junior Author** — The junior author on Hessel's paper is a young T. Reye. Tomas confronts his own name on the theft.
35. **Why He Stayed** — Tomas tells Sable the truth: he was rewarded for silence, kept the post forty years, never spoke. His whole career rests on the night Ada left.
36. **The Last Night, 1971** — Past: the night Ada is sent off the mountain; Hessel's quiet ultimatum; Ignatz unable to stop it; the young Tomas watching from the stairs.
37. **What She Took** — Ada leaves with nothing but her own notebooks; the observatory closes the file; the men go back to the eyepiece.
38. **The Light Still Coming** — Present: Tomas resolves to set the record straight before the dome goes dark, whatever it costs his name.

### Part Four — Giving the Dark a Name (39–50)
39. **Building the Case** — Sable and Tomas assemble plates, scans, logbook, and Ada's notebook into a defensible claim of priority.
40. **The Journal** — They draft a paper crediting the 1970 transient to Ada Mireu; the modern establishment's skepticism of a fifty-year-old plate claim.
41. **Petra's Choice** — The foundation could bury this to clean up the sale; Petra, given the evidence, chooses to slow the crating instead.
42. **Finding Ada** — Sable's search through records: Ada Mireu left astronomy, taught mathematics, married, lived a long ordinary life — and died two years ago.
43. **Liane** — They find Ada's daughter, Liane; a careful first phone call; the daughter who never knew her mother once found a star.
44. **The Notebooks Speak** — Liane has the rest of Ada's notebooks in a trunk; the full record, including what Ada thought of the men who took it.
45. **Ignatz's Testimony** — Ignatz signs a statement of what he witnessed in 1970–71; the last living eyewitness corroborates the claim.
46. **The Referee** — The paper goes out; a referee's hard, fair questions; the work of proving the dead were right.
47. **The Dome Goes Dark** — The decommissioning day arrives; Tomas runs the great telescope one final time, on Ada's coordinates.
48. **Accepted** — The transient is formally re-attributed; "Mireu's Star" enters the record; Tomas's own name moves, at last, to the right side of the page.
49. **Liane on the Mountain** — Liane comes to Vela Ridge; she and Tomas and Sable stand in the cold room among the plates; the loop closes.
50. **The Long Exposure** — The observatory empty, the archive saved and digitized, Tomas hands Sable the keys to the data and leaves the mountain — the light he failed once finally delivered.
