Chapter 1: The Letter

The letter arrived on a Tuesday.

Isobel MacKenzie stood at the window of her father's townhouse and watched the postman climb the wet Edinburgh street. The city was gray, the sky gray, the sea beyond the Firth a smear of pewter. The house was empty now. Her father had been dead six weeks. The servants had been let go. The rooms echoed.

She took the envelope from the brass tray. The paper was thick, the handwriting unfamiliar—a man's hand, firm and plain, with none of the flourishes that gentlemen of the city affected. She broke the seal and unfolded the sheet.

Dear Miss MacKenzie,

I write at the suggestion of the Northern Lighthouse Board, who have informed me of your circumstances. I am in need of a wife. My post as keeper of Sgeir Mhor is solitary, and the Board believes a married man is a steady man. I am told you are of good character and without prospects.

I cannot offer society or comfort. I can offer shelter, food, and the protection of a man who keeps his word. If you are willing, I will arrange your passage.

I await your reply.

Hamish Campbell

She read it twice. The words were plain, almost brutal in their honesty. A proposal without courtship, without sentiment. A transaction of survival.

She sat at her father's desk. The leather blotter still held the impression of his pen. She found a sheet of paper and dipped the nib in ink.

Yes, she wrote. I will come.

She signed her name, folded the letter, and sealed it with wax. The next morning, she posted it. Two weeks later, her passage was arranged.

She did not tell anyone where she was going. There was no one left to tell.

---

Chapter 2: Departure

The train carried Isobel west through the lowlands. She pressed her face to the glass and watched the country open—fields of stubble, stone walls, hills that grew wilder as the tracks unwound.

Her trunk was strapped in the luggage rack, containing everything she owned. Three dresses, one wool coat, her mother's Bible bound in cracked leather, and a small trowel for gardening. She had wrapped it in oilcloth to protect the blade.

She had said goodbye to no one. There was no one to say goodbye to. Her father's creditors would take the house by the end of the month. She had sold what she could to buy her passage. A few coins remained in her purse. And in her carpetbag, wrapped in paper, she carried seeds—carrot, thyme, a small packet of lavender.

At Oban the train stopped. She gathered her skirts and stepped onto the platform. The air smelled of salt and fish and coal smoke, a sharp change from the soot of Edinburgh. A woman in a wool shawl took her elbow.

"You'll be the keeper's new wife," the woman said. It was not a question.

Isobel nodded.

"The Lord help you," the woman said, and walked away without another word.

Isobel stood alone on the platform, her trunk at her feet, the wind pulling at her bonnet. She looked out at the gray water. Somewhere beyond the islands, beyond the mist, was Sgeir Mhor. Somewhere beyond that was her life.

She picked up her trunk and began to walk toward the harbor.

---

Chapter 3: Oban

Isobel spent the night in a boarding house near the harbor. The landlady, Mrs. Ferguson, was a widow with kind eyes and a sharp tongue. She showed Isobel to a room with a narrow bed and a window that looked out on the water.

"A lighthouse keeper," Mrs. Ferguson said, setting down a bowl of broth. "You know what you're about?"

"I know what he wrote," Isobel said.

"And what did he write?"

Isobel ate her broth. The landlady waited, her arms folded across her apron.

"He wrote that he needed a wife," Isobel said. "He wrote that he could offer shelter and food. He did not lie."

Mrs. Ferguson sat down across from her. The kitchen was warm. The fire crackled. Outside, the sea slapped against the harbor wall.

"I knew a keeper's wife once," Mrs. Ferguson said. "She lasted two years. Then she went mad. They found her walking the shore at dawn, talking to the waves. She did not know her own name by the end."

Isobel put down her spoon. "Is that meant to frighten me?"

"It's meant to prepare you. There is a difference."

Isobel looked at the window. The glass was dark with night. She could hear the water moving, a sound that would become the background of her life. "I have nowhere else to go," she said.

Mrs. Ferguson nodded slowly. The sharpness left her face. "Then you'll need a thicker coat. I've got one my husband left. It's not pretty, but it's warm. I'll fetch it for you in the morning."

They sat in silence for a moment. Then Mrs. Ferguson reached across the table and touched Isobel's hand.

"Be stubborn," she said. "Stubborn women survive."

---

Chapter 4: The Boat

The supply boat was called the Norna. She was thirty feet of tarred wood and salt-stained sail, captained by a man named Angus who spoke mostly through his teeth and spat over the side at regular intervals.

Isobel stood on the deck and held the rail. Oban shrank behind her, the houses becoming specks, then nothing. The islands came and went—green shoulders of land that faded into mist as they passed. She tried to fix them in her memory, but they all looked the same: dark rock, pale grass, the white line of surf at their feet.

Angus pointed east with his chin. "Sgeir Mhor," he said.

She saw nothing but water and sky. The horizon was a blurred line where one gray became another.

"Another hour," he said. "Weather holds, we'll have you there by noon."

The wind picked up. The Norna rose and fell, her timbers groaning. Isobel's stomach turned. She gripped the rail until her knuckles were white. She vomited over the side, the broth from Mrs. Ferguson's kitchen disappearing into the gray water.

Angus said nothing. He handed her a tin cup of water. She drank. He spat over the side and adjusted the tiller.

The hour passed slowly. There was nothing to see but the sea and the sky and the distant shape of islands that never came closer.

Then Angus pointed again. "There."

Isobel followed his gaze. In the distance, a white tower rose from the water like a bone.

---

Chapter 5: Sgeir Mhor

The lighthouse rose from the mist like a finger of bone.

Isobel saw it first as a smudge against the gray, then as a shape, then as a tower of whitewashed stone rooted to a black rock. The rock was not large—perhaps two hundred yards across at its widest, a shoulder of granite that the sea had gnawed into cliffs on every side. There was no soil, no grass, no tree. Just stone and the white tower and the endless water.

There was no harbor. Angus brought the Norna alongside a stone jetty slick with spray. He threw a line to a rusted iron ring and pulled the boat close.

Isobel stood on the deck and looked up. The tower was taller than she had imagined. The lantern room glinted at the top, catching a weak shaft of sun through the cloud.

"Your husband will be down directly," Angus said. "He knows we're here."

She waited. The waves smacked the jetty. The wind pulled at her bonnet. She held it with one hand and her skirts with the other.

Then the door at the base of the tower opened. A man stepped out.

He was tall and broad-shouldered, with a beard shot through with gray. He wore a wool sweater and canvas trousers stained with salt and oil. His face was weathered, his skin dark from wind and spray. His eyes were the color of the sea—gray-green, distant.

He looked at her. She looked at him.

He took her trunk from Angus without a word. He hefted it onto his shoulder and turned toward the tower.

"Come," he said.

She followed him inside.

---

Chapter 6: Hamish

Hamish Campbell carried her trunk up the spiral staircase on his shoulder. The stairs wound around and around, narrow and worn, past small windows that let in strips of gray light.

Isobel counted the steps. Forty-two to the first landing. Another turn. Another flight. Her legs ached by the second floor.

The living quarters were on the third floor. A kitchen with a black iron stove, a wooden table, two chairs. A parlor with a worn sofa and a shelf of books. A bedroom with a narrow bed and a crucifix on the wall. Everything was clean but spare—no curtains, no pictures, no touch of softness.

He set down the trunk in the parlor and stood by the window, his hands at his sides. He looked out at the sea as if he could not help himself.

"You'll want to rest," he said. His voice was low, roughened by disuse. "The boat journey is hard."

"Thank you," she said.

He nodded and left. She heard his boots on the stairs, going down. Then the sound of a door closing.

She sat on the sofa. The springs creaked. The room was quiet except for the sound of water—the sea against the rock below, a sound that would never stop.

She looked around at her new home. The walls were thick stone, whitewashed. The windows looked out on the sea in three directions. There was no land in sight. No smoke from a distant chimney. No sail on the horizon.

She was alone on a rock in the Atlantic with a man who had said six words to her.

---

Chapter 7: The Tower

Isobel explored the lighthouse that afternoon while Hamish worked the lamp.

The tower was seven floors tall. She counted each one as she climbed. The kitchen and parlor on the third floor. Above them, Hamish's bedroom—a narrow bed, a wooden chest, a window that faced west. Above that, the storage room: cans of oil, rolls of wick, tools hanging on pegs. Above that, the machinery room, where the great wheel and weights drove the rotation of the lamp.

Above everything, the lantern room.

She stopped at the threshold. The room was a ring of glass and iron, open to the sky on every side. The wind pressed against the panes. In the center stood the lamp—a massive arrangement of glass prisms and brass fittings, a machine built to throw light across miles of dark water.

Hamish was polishing a lens with a leather cloth. He did not turn around.

"The light turns on a bed of mercury," he said. "Four tons of metal floating on a thin pool. A child can push it."

She stepped closer. The lenses caught her reflection and bent it. She saw herself multiplied, fragmented, thrown back from a dozen angles. Her face in a dozen pieces.

"Every night," he said, "I light it. Every morning, I put it out. That is what I do."

She watched his hands move over the glass. They were careful hands, steady and sure. The hands of a man who had done the same work for sixteen years.

---

Chapter 8: First Night

Hamish showed Isobel to the parlor. A bed had been made up on the sofa—crisp white sheets, a wool blanket, a hard pillow. The sofa was narrow. She would have to lie still to avoid falling off.

"You'll sleep here," he said.

"Not in your room?"

He looked at her. His face was unreadable, a mask of weathered skin and deep lines. "No."

He turned. He climbed the stairs to the lantern room. She heard each footfall. Then the strike of a match. The click of the oil valve. The soft whoosh as the flame caught.

Then the beam began to turn. She saw it pass the window—a sweep of light, then darkness, then light again. Every ten seconds.

She sat on the sofa. The lighthouse hummed around her. The machinery of the lamp, a low mechanical thrum. The wind against the glass. The sea gnawing at the rock below, endless and hungry. She had never heard such a sound. It was not a sound she could ignore.

The beam passed the window again. Light. Dark. Light.

She lay down fully dressed. The blanket was rough against her cheek. She closed her eyes. The beam continued its circuit, indifferent.

She did not sleep. Not truly. She lay in the dark and listened to the sounds of a world she did not yet understand.

---

Chapter 9: The Routine

Hamish rose before dawn. Isobel heard his boots on the stairs—first going up, then coming down. The click of the lamp being extinguished. The silence that followed.

She found him in the kitchen, standing at the stove, boiling water for tea. He did not ask how she had slept. He poured two cups and set one on the table for her. Steam rose from the surface.

"The days have a shape here," he said. "I trim the lamp in the morning. I clean the lenses. I check the oil stores. At midday I eat. In the afternoon I make repairs. Before sunset I light the lamp again."

"I can cook," she said.

"There's food in the pantry. Salt beef, oats, potatoes, dried peas. The supply boat comes every three weeks."

"I can make bread."

He looked at her. "The oven is difficult. It burns hot and cold without warning."

"I will learn it."

He nodded once, a small motion. He drank his tea standing at the window, looking out at the sea. The morning light was thin and pale. He put the cup in the basin and went downstairs.

Isobel sat at the table. She wrapped her hands around the cup. The tea was strong and dark, bitter. She drank it slowly, watching the sun climb over the water.

The routine had been established. She understood now that she would have to find her place within it.

---

Chapter 10: The Garden

On the eastern side of the rock, where the cliff dropped to the sea, Isobel found a patch of soil.

It was small—perhaps six feet square—sheltered by a ridge of granite that broke the worst of the wind. The salt spray did not reach it. The sun, when it shone, fell directly on the dark earth.

She knelt and touched the soil with her fingers. It was dark and damp, thin and full of small stones. But it was earth. Real earth. She had brought seeds.

She found a broken piece of iron in the coal store and used it to dig. The work was hard. The soil resisted. Stones clinked against the iron. She cleared them one by one, building a small pile at the edge of the patch.

Hamish found her at sunset. He stood on the path above, his hands in his pockets.

"What are you doing?"

"Making a garden."

"There's no garden here. The salt kills everything."

"I'll build a wall."

He said nothing. He looked at the small square of turned earth. He looked at her hands, raw and blistered. He went back inside without another word.

She worked until the light failed. Then she climbed the stairs to the tower, washed her hands in the basin until the water ran pink, and sat down to eat alone.

He did not come down to dinner that night.

---

Chapter 11: The Bell

The fog bell hung on the western cliff, a heavy iron dome with a clapper that struck when the mechanism was wound.

When the mist came—thick, white, swallowing the tower—Hamish wound the mechanism, and the bell began to ring.

Clang. Clang. Clang.

The sound was iron on iron. Relentless. It did not pause. It did not vary. Every three seconds, the same note, resonating through the rock.

Isobel lay on the sofa with a pillow over her head. The sound vibrated through the stone of the tower. She could feel it in her teeth, in her ribs, in the bones of her skull. There was no escape from it.

She went upstairs to the lantern room. Hamish was sitting by the lamp, reading a book. The beam swept past. He turned a page.

"How long does it go?"

"Until the fog lifts."

"And when will that be?"

He turned another page. "When it's ready."

She stood in the doorway. Her head ached. Her nerves were frayed. She wanted to scream, to throw something, to make him understand what the sound was doing to her.

He did not look up.

She went back down to the parlor. She sat in the dark and counted the rings. At thirty-seven she stopped. At eighty-two she began again. She counted until her mind went numb.

The fog lifted at dawn.

The silence woke her.

---

Chapter 12: The Diary

Isobel found the diary in the bottom drawer of a dresser in the parlor.

She was looking for extra blankets. Winter was coming. The air in the tower had a damp chill that settled in her bones. She pulled open the drawer, expecting wool, and found papers instead.

Receipts. Old letters. A broken watch with a rusted chain. A button.

At the bottom, a leather-bound book, small and worn, its spine cracked from use.

She opened it. The handwriting was a woman's—round, careful, educated. The ink had faded to brown.

August 12th, 1856. I have been here three weeks. Hamish is kind but silent. I think he misses the mainland. I miss my mother's garden.

Isobel's hand stopped. She turned to the first page.

Morag Campbell, Sgeir Mhor, 1856.

She sat on the floor. The boards were cold. She read.

August 14th. He lit the lamp tonight and I watched from the stairs. There is something beautiful about it. Something holy.

September 3rd. I have not heard a human voice besides his in three weeks. I try to make conversation but he answers in single words. I do not think he means to be cold. I think he has forgotten how to be warm.

October 12th. I am learning his silences. There are different kinds. The silence of work. The silence of tiredness. The silence of remembering something sad.

Isobel closed the book. Her hands were shaking.

---

Chapter 13: The Question

Isobel found Hamish on the jetty, mending a lobster pot. The twine was coarse. His fingers worked quickly, threading, pulling, knotting.

"Who was Morag?"

His hands stopped moving. The twine hung loose between his fingers. The waves lapped at the stone below them.

"Where did you hear that name?"

"I found her diary."

He was silent for a long time. A gull circled overhead. The sea rose and fell.

"She was my wife," he said.

"What happened to her?"

"She died."

"I know that. How?"

He put down the pot. He stood and looked out at the water. His shoulders were set. His jaw was tight.

"She got sick. The damp. There was no doctor in time. The boat could not get through."

"I'm sorry."

He picked up the pot again. His hands resumed their work. "The diary is private. You should not have read it."

"I did not know what it was. I was looking for blankets."

He did not reply. He pulled the twine tight and cut it with a knife.

She stood there for a minute, waiting. He did not look at her. She turned and walked back to the tower.

He did not speak to her for three days after that.

---

Chapter 14: The Storm

The storm came from the west.

Isobel saw it building on the horizon—a wall of black cloud, a line of white where the sea began to break. The wind rose. The windows rattled.

Hamish came down from the tower. "Secure everything," he said.

She did not know what to secure. She watched him carry the coal bucket inside. He lashed the boat to the jetty with extra rope. He checked each window, each latch, each door.

The storm hit at dusk.

The wind screamed. Rain lashed the tower horizontally. The sea rose and threw itself against the rock, and the tower shook with each impact. Isobel sat on the floor of the parlor with her hands over her ears. The building groaned. The windows flexed. She was certain the stone would break.

She climbed the stairs to the lantern room. Hamish was standing by the lamp, calm, watching the waves through the glass.

"Is it going to hold?"

He looked at her. His face was quiet. "It's held for sixty years. It will hold tonight."

She stood beside him. The sea boiled below them—white and black, endless, furious. The beam of the lamp swept across the chaos, steady as a heartbeat.

She watched the waves. She watched his face. She stayed.

---

Chapter 15: The Light

The storm lasted two days.

Isobel did not leave the lantern room. She sat in the chair by the wall, wrapped in a blanket, and watched Hamish tend the lamp. He checked the flame. He wiped the glass. He adjusted the mechanism that turned the great lenses.

His hands never shook.

She saw it clearly for the first time: this was not just his work. It was his prayer. His meditation. The only language he spoke with fluency.

On the second night, the wind shifted. The rain stopped. The clouds began to break.

Hamish stood at the glass and watched. A patch of stars appeared. Then another. The sea began to calm.

"She would have liked you," he said.

Isobel did not move.

"Morag," he said. "She would have liked you."

He said nothing else. He trimmed the lamp and sat down in his chair. She stayed in the lantern room until dawn, wrapped in the blanket, watching the stars wheel overhead.

When morning came, the storm was gone. The sea was flat and gray. The tower was still standing.

She had survived her first storm.

---

Chapter 16: The Ship

A schooner passed close to Sgeir Mhor on a clear morning in October.

Isobel was on the cliff, gathering driftwood for the stove. The wood was scarce on the rock—everything that washed up was claimed—but she had found a few good pieces.

She looked up and saw the ship.

It was beautiful. White sails against blue water. A dark hull cutting through the sea. It was close enough that she could see figures moving on the deck.

She dropped the wood. She ran to the edge of the cliff and began to wave. She waved both arms over her head. She shouted.

"Hello! Hello!"

Her voice was swallowed by the wind. The schooner sailed on. No one on the deck looked her way. It moved past the rock, past her small figure on the cliff, and continued east toward the mainland.

She stood on the cliff until the sails disappeared. Then she picked up her driftwood and walked back to the tower.

Hamish was at the door. He had been watching her from the threshold.

"You get used to it," he said.

She walked past him into the tower.

She did not know if she would ever get used to it. The loneliness of being seen but not seen. The way the world passed by without her.

---

Chapter 17: The Garden Dies

The salt spray killed the seedlings in three days.

Isobel found them on the fourth morning. Brown stalks. Withered leaves. The carrot seeds had not even sprouted. The lavender was a memory, a dry stem lying flat against the soil.

She knelt in the dirt and touched the dead plants. She had watered them each morning. She had talked to them. She had believed they would grow.

She began to cry.

She sat back on her heels and cried for the garden. She cried for Edinburgh, for the ship that had not seen her, for the man upstairs who could not speak to her. She cried for her father, for the townhouse, for everything she had lost.

She did not hear him approach. But suddenly there was a cup of tea beside her, set on the stone wall. Steam rose from it.

She looked up. Hamish was already walking back to the tower. He did not look back.

She picked up the tea. It was hot and sweet. He had put sugar in it.

She sat in the ruined garden and drank it. The tea was warm. The wind was cold. The sea was gray.

She drank every drop.

---

Chapter 18: The Story

Isobel finished Morag's diary in the dark of an October evening.

She sat by the window in the parlor. The light was fading. She read by the last glow of the sky.

The entries were brief, scattered. Morag had not written every day. She had written when the loneliness pressed too hard to contain.

December 1st. I miss his voice. He is kind, but he has forgotten how to speak. I think the sea has taken his words. I do not blame him. I blame the water.

January 14th. The damp is in my chest now. I cough in the mornings. I do not tell him. He worries too much. He would try to get me to the mainland. The boat cannot go out in winter.

February 22nd. I am weaker today. Hamish sat with me after he lit the lamp. He held my hand. He did not speak. But his hand was warm.

March 3rd, 1857. I am afraid.

The journal ended there.

Isobel closed the book. The lighthouse hummed around her. The beam swept past the window.

She thought of Morag. Nineteen years old. Dying in this room, in this bed, while the man she loved lit the lamp above her.

She understood him now. The silence was not coldness. It was the only way he knew to survive what he had survived.

---

Chapter 19: The Photograph

Isobel found the photograph tucked between the last pages of the diary.

It was a small daguerreotype in a brass frame. The woman in it was young—barely more than a girl—with dark hair parted in the middle and a lace collar at her throat. Her eyes were large and dark. She was not smiling. People did not smile in photographs then.

But there was something gentle in her eyes. Something soft.

Isobel studied the face. This was Morag. She had lived in this room. She had loved the same man. She had died here.

Isobel thought about putting the photograph back in the drawer. But she did not. She set it on the windowsill in the parlor, where the morning light would touch it.

Hamish saw it the next day. He stood in the doorway, looking at the photograph. His face was still. Unreadable.

He did not say anything. He did not move it.

But that night, when Isobel came down from the lantern room, the photograph was no longer on the windowsill.

It was on the mantelpiece.

He had moved it to a place of honor.

---

Chapter 20: The Birds

Every morning at dawn, the seabirds gathered on the western cliff.

Isobel watched them from the kitchen window. Guillemots and razorbills, their black-and-white bodies packed so tightly on the ledges that the rock seemed to move. Puffins with their bright beaks, comical and serious at once. Gulls that screamed and fought and wheeled over the water.

She began to sit on the cliff edge after breakfast. She did not go close enough to disturb them. She sat on a flat stone and watched.

The birds had a rhythm. They left in the morning to fish, flying low over the water. They returned at midday. They cleaned their feathers. They called to one another. They did not seem lonely.

One morning, Hamish came down and found her on the cliff.

"You watch them every day," he said.

"I like them."

"They'll leave in winter."

"Where do they go?"

"Out to sea. They live on the water. They do not come back until spring."

"Then they come back."

He nodded. "They always come back."

She looked at the birds. She thought about the word back. She thought about whether she would ever use it for herself.

---

Chapter 21: First Snow

Winter came to Sgeir Mhor in a single night.

Isobel woke to a changed world. The air was sharp and cold in her lungs. The windows were frosted on the inside. She scraped a circle in the ice with her finger and looked out.

The rock was white. The sea was gray and flat beneath a low sky. The snow had fallen during the night, covering everything—the path, the jetty, the garden wall she had built. The tower stood white against a white sky.

The supply boat had not come. It would not come now until spring. That was the rule. The winter sea was too dangerous.

Isobel stood at the window and felt the weight of it. They were alone. Cut off. There would be no letters, no news, no visitors. No way off the rock.

Hamish came down from the lantern room. "Snow," he said.

"I see it."

"We'll need to ration the coal."

She nodded. He looked at her for a moment longer than usual. His eyes searched her face.

"You'll be all right," he said.

It was not a question. It was not quite a reassurance. But she heard the effort in it. He was trying.

"I know," she said.

He pulled on his coat and went outside to secure the coal store. She watched him from the window, a dark figure moving through the white.

---

Chapter 22: The Lamp

"Come," Hamish said one afternoon in early December. "I'll teach you."

They climbed to the lantern room. The air was cold against the glass. The sea stretched away in every direction, gray and flat under a winter sky.

He showed her the work of the light.

He showed her how to trim the wick with the curved scissors—a precise angle, a clean cut. He showed her how to clean the lenses with the leather cloth, working from the center outward to avoid streaks. He showed her the mercury bath that made the lamp turn, the weights that drove the mechanism, the valves that controlled the oil flow.

"Every night at sunset," he said. "Every morning at dawn. It cannot be missed."

"What happens if it goes out?"

He looked at her. His eyes were serious. "A ship could be lost. Men could die."

She took the cloth from his hand. She cleaned the lens as he had shown her. Her hand was steady. He watched in silence.

When she finished, the glass was clear. The brass was polished.

"Good," he said.

That was all. But she felt the word settle in her chest like a small warm thing.

---

Chapter 23: The Crack

A crack appeared in the lantern room glass on a night so cold the sea steamed.

Isobel heard it—a sound like a gunshot, sharp and sudden. She ran up the stairs. Hamish was already there, standing before the glass with his hands at his sides.

The crack ran from the frame to the center of the pane. It was thin but long. Light caught it and split.

"The cold," he said. "The glass contracts. It happens sometimes."

"Can you fix it?"

He went down to the storeroom and came back with putty and a roll of canvas. He worked for an hour, his hands steady, sealing the crack, pressing the canvas into the putty, smoothing the edges.

When he finished, he stepped back.

"It will hold," he said. "But it's weaker now. If we get another storm before spring..."

He did not finish. He did not need to.

Isobel looked at the cracked glass. The sea beyond was dark. The wound in the glass was small, but it was there. A weakness in their fortress.

That night, she lay awake listening. The glass did not break. But she heard it flex in the wind, and she did not sleep.

---

Chapter 24: The Chair

Isobel began to sit with Hamish during the night watches.

She did not ask permission. One night in early December, she carried her chair up to the lantern room and set it next to his. She sat down. She did not speak.

He looked at her. He said nothing.

They sat together in the dark. The lamp turned. The beam swept the sea. The silence was complete.

The first night, she stayed an hour. The second night, two hours. The third night, she fell asleep in the chair, her head drooping to the side.

She woke at dawn with his coat over her shoulders. He was standing at the glass, extinguishing the lamp.

She sat up. The coat slipped.

"You were cold," he said.

She did not mention it. Neither did he. But when she went back to the parlor that morning, the chair remained in the lantern room. He had not moved it back.

And the coat was on its hook every morning, waiting for the next night.

It was their first negotiation without words.

---

Chapter 25: The Story He Tells

Hamish spoke on the longest night of the year.

The winter solstice. The sea was black. The sky was black. The wind had died completely, leaving the lighthouse in a pocket of absolute stillness.

Isobel was in her chair. The lamp turned. The silence stretched like a held breath.

"I was in the tower," he said.

His voice was low. He did not look at her. He looked at the dark glass.

"When she died. I was lighting the lamp."

She did not speak. She did not move.

"I knew she was sick. I knew. But the light had to be lit. It was my duty. The Board. The ships. The men at sea. I told myself she would wait."

He stopped. The lamp turned.

"She did not wait. I came down and she was gone. Just gone. The bed was still warm."

His hands were in his lap. They were very still.

"You think you can hold duty and love in two hands," he said. "But you cannot. Not always."

Isobel looked at his hands. She wanted to touch them. She wanted to take them.

She did not. She stayed in her chair. She let the silence hold his words.

---

Chapter 26: The Tears

Isobel cried for Morag.

She did not plan it. She was washing the dishes after dinner. The water was warm. The window was dark. And the tears came.

She stood at the basin, her hands in the soapy water, and wept. The tears ran down her face and dripped into the dishwater. She did not try to stop them.

Hamish was at the table. He did not move.

She cried for a girl she had never met, who had died alone in this room while her husband lit a lamp. She cried for Hamish, who had carried that guilt for thirteen years. She cried for herself.

When she stopped, the kitchen was quiet. The water had gone cold.

Hamish had not moved from his chair. His hands were on the table. His face was drawn.

"I'm sorry," she said.

"Don't be."

She dried her hands and sat down across from him. They looked at each other across the narrow table.

He did not comfort her. She did not expect him to. But he stayed in the room. He did not leave.

That was something. That was everything.

---

Chapter 27: The Frost

The frost came in January and did not leave.

Ice formed on the inside of the windows. Isobel woke each morning to a layer of white on the glass. She scraped it with a knife and watched her breath hang in the air. The cold was constant, creeping, never defeated.

The coal supply was low. They burned only enough to keep the kitchen warm. The rest of the tower was cold—the parlor, the bedroom, the staircase. She wore her coat inside. She wore gloves to read.

Hamish spent most of his time in the lantern room, where the lamp gave off a steady heat. Isobel joined him there. They sat close together, wrapped in blankets, and watched the light turn over the dark water.

One morning, she found him in the storeroom, breaking up an old crate for kindling. His breath plumed in the cold.

"How long until spring?" she asked.

"Two months. Maybe three."

"Will we have enough coal?"

He took a long time to answer. He broke another board.

"We'll manage."

She thought about the word manage. On the mainland, it meant something ordinary. Here, it meant survival.

---

Chapter 28: The Kitchen

Isobel learned to bake bread in the small iron stove.

The first loaf was hard as stone. She had to break it with the back of a knife. The second burned on the bottom—the oven's heat was uneven. The third did not rise. The yeast was old. The fourth was edible. The fifth was good.

She brought a slice to Hamish with his tea. He took it. He examined it. He bit into it.

He chewed.

"Better," he said.

He ate the whole slice. Then he held out his hand for another.

She gave it to him. She did not smile. But she felt something warm in her chest—a small achievement, a tiny sovereignty.

She began to bake every third day. The oven became less difficult. The bread became reliable. She learned to feel the heat with her hand held above the iron, learned to know when the fire was right.

And Hamish began to eat more at dinner.

It was a small victory. But on Sgeir Mhor, small victories were the only kind.

---

Chapter 29: The Fever

Isobel fell ill in February.

It started as a cough. A tickle in her throat. She ignored it. Then the fever came, sudden and fierce. Her body began to shake, and she could not stop.

She woke on the sofa with blankets piled on her. Hamish was leaning over her. His face was tight with fear—the first emotion she had clearly seen in him since she arrived.

"You have a fever," he said.

"I know."

He put a hand on her forehead. His palm was rough and cool.

"You need rest."

She tried to laugh. "Where would I go?"

He did not smile. He went to the kitchen and came back with a basin of water and a cloth. He pressed the wet cloth to her forehead. The cold was a shock.

"Don't move," he said.

She lay still. The lighthouse hummed. The lamp turned above them. He changed the cloth. He brought her broth and made her drink it. He did not speak.

But every time she opened her eyes, he was there.

Watching. Waiting. Keeping his own vigil.

---

Chapter 30: The Recovery

The fever broke on the fifth day.

Isobel woke weak and drenched in sweat, but clear-headed for the first time in a week. The room was quiet. Sunlight was falling through the window.

Hamish was asleep in the chair beside the sofa. His head was tipped back. His mouth was open. He looked older in sleep.

She watched him for a minute. She had never seen him sleep before. He had not left her. He had sat by her side for five days.

She shifted. His eyes opened.

"You're awake," he said.

"I'm better."

He looked at her. He did not say anything. But something passed between them. A recognition.

He had been afraid. He had not said it. He would never say it. But she had seen it in the way his shoulders dropped when she spoke, in the breath he let out, in the way he closed his eyes for just a moment.

She reached out and touched his hand.

"Thank you," she said.

He pulled his hand away. He stood up.

"I need to check the lamp."

He went up the stairs. But at the landing, he stopped. He looked back at her for just a moment.

She nodded.

He continued up.

---

Chapter 31: The Wreck

The ship came apart on the rocks in a March gale.

Isobel heard it before she saw it. A grinding, splintering sound beneath the wind. Wood breaking. The sea tearing at something.

She ran to the window. The sea was white and black, thrashing. And in the middle of it, a shape. A hull. A broken mast.

"Hamish!"

He was already at the door, pulling on his oilskin coat. His hands moved quickly, efficiently. He had the boat hook in his hand.

"There are men out there," he said.

"The boat—you cannot—"

He looked at her. His eyes were steady. "I can."

He was down the stairs before she could argue. She followed him to the jetty. The waves were enormous, smashing against the stone, throwing spray over the path. He untied the small rowboat.

"Get back inside," he said.

He pushed off into the breakers.

She stood on the jetty and watched him go.

---

Chapter 32: The Rescue

Isobel watched from the cliff as Hamish rowed into the wreck.

The small boat disappeared between waves. Then reappeared. He was strong—stronger than she had known. His arms pulled the oars with a steady rhythm, timing the swells, reading the water.

He reached the wreck. She saw him reach over the side and pull a figure into the boat. Another. Then a third. He took three men from the sea.

The return was harder. The boat was heavy now. The waves pushed against him. She could see him straining, his back curved against the wind, his arms pulling with everything he had.

He reached the jetty. She ran down.

The survivors were half-drowned. Two were conscious, coughing seawater. One was not moving. Hamish lifted the unconscious man onto the stone and began to pump his chest with both hands.

"Water," Hamish said. "Blankets. Hurry."

She ran.

When she came back with blankets, the man was coughing. Alive. Hamish was kneeling beside him, breathing hard.

He looked up at her. His face was wet with spray.

"Get them inside," he said.

---

Chapter 33: The Stranger

The sailor's name was Callum MacKinnon. He was nineteen years old, from a village near Oban, and he would not stop talking.

He sat by the stove in the kitchen, wrapped in Hamish's spare coat, drinking tea in great gulps. The other two sailors were in the parlor, recovering. One had a broken arm. The other had a gash on his head that Isobel had bandaged.

"Thought I was done for," Callum said. "Saw the rock and thought—well, that's it then. But your man came out. In that little boat like the devil himself."

Hamish stood by the window. He said nothing.

"You're brave," Callum said to him. "Brave as any man I've met. I'd be dead if not for you."

Hamish turned away.

Callum looked at Isobel. "You're his wife?"

"Yes."

"Must be hard. Living out here with no company."

She glanced at Hamish. He was still at the window, looking out at the sea.

"It has its compensations," she said.

---

Chapter 34: The Envy

Callum stayed for two weeks.

The weather would not lift. One gale followed another. The sea remained dangerous. The three men could not leave.

Isobel found herself looking forward to Callum's voice. He filled the silence. He told stories of Glasgow, of Dublin, of a girl in Liverpool with red hair. He laughed. He teased. He made the tower feel alive.

She began to notice that she smiled more when he was in the room.

One evening, she caught herself brushing her hair before dinner. She stopped. She looked at her reflection in the small mirror.

She was not unhappy. But she was hungry. Hungry for conversation. For laughter. For a world beyond the rock.

She set down the brush.

She was married. And Hamish had rowed into a storm to save Callum's life. He had risked everything for strangers.

She did not brush her hair before dinner again.

---

Chapter 35: The Goodbye

The weather cleared on a Tuesday.

Callum and the other sailors stood on the jetty. A fishing boat had answered Hamish's signal and was approaching from the east. They would be taken to Oban.

Callum shook Hamish's hand. "I owe you my life."

Hamish nodded.

Callum turned to Isobel. He took her hand in both of his.

"Thank you," he said. "For everything. The food. The care."

"Take care of yourself," she said.

"I will." He hesitated. "You could come with us. There's room in the boat."

She felt the weight of the offer. The mainland. Laughter. People.

She looked at Hamish. He was standing apart, his hands at his sides, looking at the sea. His shoulders were set. He was not watching her.

"No," she said. "My place is here."

Callum nodded. He let go of her hand and climbed into the boat.

She watched until the boat was a speck on the horizon. Then she went back into the tower.

Hamish was in the kitchen. He had made tea.

He slid a cup across the table to her.

She sat down. She drank it.

Neither of them said a word.

---

Chapter 36: The Walk

"Take me to the top of the rock."

Hamish looked at her. "Why?"

"Because I want to see what you see."

He led her up a path she had not noticed before. It was narrow, winding up the western slope. The rock rose. The wind grew stronger. The sound of the sea faded into the sound of the air.

At the top, the island fell away on all sides. The sea stretched to every horizon. The lighthouse stood below them, a white needle against the sky.

"There," Hamish said. He pointed east. "The mainland. On a clear day you can see the hills."

She looked. She saw nothing but water and the faint line where sky met sea. But she believed him.

"What do you see when you look out here?" she asked.

He was silent for a long time. The wind moved his hair.

"Everything I have," he said. "And everything I lost."

She stood beside him. The wind pulled at her dress. The sea moved below them, gray and endless and alive.

"It's beautiful," she said.

He looked at her.

"Yes," he said.

---

Chapter 37: The Touch

It happened at the dinner table. Simple. Accidental.

Isobel reached for the salt. Hamish reached for the bread. Their hands brushed.

He did not pull away. She did not pull away.

For a moment—two heartbeats—his fingers rested against hers. The contact was light, barely there. But she felt it through her whole body.

Then he withdrew. He took the bread. He ate.

She sat with her hand still extended, her fingers tingling.

After dinner, she washed the dishes. He sat in his chair by the window. Neither spoke.

But that night, when they sat in the lantern room, their chairs were closer than before.

Neither had moved them. They had simply drifted together, like the tide.

---

Chapter 38: The Bed

Isobel carried her blankets up the stairs on a soft spring evening.

Hamish was in his room, sitting on the edge of the bed. He looked up when she appeared in the doorway. The lamp was burning low. His face was half in shadow.

"Move over," she said.

He did not move.

"I am your wife," she said. "And I am tired of sleeping alone."

He looked at her. She could not read his face. Then he shifted to one side of the bed.

She laid her blankets on the other side. She did not undress. She lay down on top of the covers.

They lay in the dark, not touching. The lamp turned above them. The beam swept past the window every ten seconds.

"Are you comfortable?" he asked.

"Yes."

A pause.

"Isobel."

"Yes?"

"Thank you. For staying."

She turned her head on the pillow. His face was a shadow in the darkness.

"Where else would I go?" she said.

He did not answer. But after a long moment, his hand found hers under the blanket. His fingers curled around her palm.

She held on.

---

Chapter 39: The Word

He said her name.

It happened in the morning. She was at the stove, stirring porridge. He came down from the lighthouse.

"Isobel."

She turned. It was the first time he had said her name aloud. In all these months, he had never used it.

"Yes?"

He stood in the doorway. His hands were at his sides. His face was uncertain.

"The light needs cleaning. Will you help me?"

"Yes."

She followed him up the stairs. She watched his back as he climbed. His shoulders were broad. His hair was gray at the temples. He had said her name.

She held that word in her mouth for the rest of the day.

Isobel. She tasted it. It was sweet.

---

Chapter 40: The Spring

The supply boat returned in April.

Isobel stood on the jetty and watched it approach—the same boat, the same captain. Angus tied off the line and handed down the crates.

"Letters for you," he said.

She took them. Three letters, all from Edinburgh. Her cousin. The family solicitor. An old friend from school.

She carried them up to the tower and sat at the kitchen table. She opened them slowly.

Her cousin wrote that the townhouse had been sold. The solicitor wrote that her father's debts were finally settled. Her friend wrote of parties and engagements and the latest gossip from the city.

She read them all. She set them aside.

She went to the window and looked out at the sea. The water was blue. The sky was clear. The birds were back.

She thought of Edinburgh. She did not miss it.

She folded the letters and put them in the drawer with Morag's diary. Then she went outside to plant her garden.

---

Chapter 41: The Decision

Isobel sat down at the kitchen table with paper and pen.

Dear Margaret, she wrote.

I am well. The winter here is hard, but I am stronger than I knew. My husband is a good man. He does not speak much, but I have learned to listen to silence.

I am not coming back to Edinburgh.

She stopped. She read the words. She had not known she would write them until they appeared on the page. But they were true.

This is my home now. The sea is my neighbor. The lighthouse is my charge. I have planted a garden. I have learned to tend the lamp.

I am content.

She signed her name. She folded the letter and sealed it with wax.

She gave it to Angus when the supply boat left. She watched it disappear over the water, carrying her decision away from her.

The next morning, she woke and felt the island under her. The rock was solid beneath the tower. She did not feel trapped. She felt held.

---

Chapter 42: The Garden Again

Isobel planted again.

But this time, she built a wall first.

She spent three days carrying stones from the eastern shore. They were heavy, rough, cold. She carried them one by one and stacked them in a low curve around the patch of soil. A breakwater against the salt wind.

Her back ached. Her hands bled. But the wall grew.

Hamish watched from the path. He did not offer to help. But on the second day, she found a pile of flat stones beside the wall. He had carried them up from the jetty while she was sleeping.

She used them for the top course. They were perfect.

She planted the seeds. Carrots, thyme, lavender. She watered them with fresh water from the cistern. She checked them every morning.

The first green shoot appeared on a Thursday.

She knelt and touched it. It was small and fragile and alive.

She looked up at the tower. Hamish was in the lantern room, polishing the glass. She waved.

He raised a hand. Just for a moment.

Then he went back to work.

---

Chapter 43: The Promise

Isobel found Hamish on the cliff at sunset.

He was sitting on the edge, his legs hanging over the drop. The sea below was deep and dark. She sat down beside him, her skirts gathered around her.

"What are you thinking about?" she asked.

"The light."

"Tell me."

He looked at the horizon. The sun was setting, staining the sea orange and gold. The clouds were tinged with pink.

"When I was a boy," he said, "I lived in a fishing village on the west coast. My father was a fisherman. He went out one night and did not come back. The fog was thick. There was no lighthouse on that coast."

She was silent.

"I decided then. I would become the light. I would be what my father did not have."

"And are you?"

He turned to her. His eyes were dark in the fading light.

"I am trying."

She took his hand. He let her.

"When I came here," she said, "I thought I was marrying a stranger. But I was marrying a man who keeps a promise. That is not nothing."

He looked at their hands, joined on the rock between them.

"I will try to be worthy of that," he said.

She squeezed his fingers.

"You already are."

---

Chapter 44: The Song

Isobel sang while she worked.

It started without thought. She was baking bread, and the kitchen was quiet except for the fire, and the tune came out of her like breath.

She sang an old song her mother had taught her. A Gaelic ballad about a woman who waited by the sea for her lover to return. The melody was slow and sad and beautiful.

She did not notice Hamish in the doorway until she had finished.

She turned. He was standing there, listening.

"Don't stop," he said.

She felt her face grow warm. "I did not know you were there."

"I liked it. The song."

"It is an old one. My mother used to sing it."

"Sing it again."

She did. She sang it through, and then another, and then another. She sang the songs of her childhood, songs she had forgotten she knew.

He sat at the table and listened. He did not join. He never joined.

But he listened.

That night, in the lantern room, she hummed while the lamp turned. He sat beside her, and the sea below them made its own music.

---

Chapter 45: The Boat Trip

"Come," Hamish said one morning in July. "I'll take you out."

He led her down to the jetty and helped her into the small boat. The wood was warm from the sun. She sat in the bow, holding the sides.

He pushed off and began to row.

The rock receded. The tower shrank against the sky.

She watched it from the water. The white stone against the blue. The lantern room glittering. The cliffs black at the base where the sea had worn them smooth for centuries.

She had seen the lighthouse a hundred times. But never like this. Never from the outside looking in.

"Look," Hamish said.

He pointed back.

She turned. The lighthouse stood at the center of the sea. Small. Proud. Alone. It was a promise kept in stone and glass.

"It is beautiful," she said.

"Aye," he said. "It is."

He rowed in a slow circle around the rock. She saw every angle. The garden wall. The fog bell. The jetty. The windows of their home.

When they returned, she stepped onto the stone and looked up at the tower.

She had seen it from the outside. Now she understood what it meant to be inside.

---

Chapter 46: The Summer

Summer came gently to Sgeir Mhor.

The days stretched long. The sun rose early and set late. The sea calmed to a soft blue, and the wind was warm.

The birds nested and fed their young. The garden grew. The carrots pushed green leaves through the soil. The thyme spread across the stones. The lavender opened in purple flowers.

Isobel and Hamish settled into a rhythm that was almost domestic. He tended the light. She tended the house and the garden. They ate together. They sat together in the lantern room. They slept in the same bed.

They talked. Not much—a few words each day. But the words were easier now. He told her about his father. She told him about Edinburgh. He described the fish that swam in the waters around the rock. She described her mother's garden in the spring.

The conversations spanned days. A sentence in the morning. A reply at dinner. A story over tea.

But it was enough.

She began to think of the lighthouse not as a prison, but as a world. A small world, complete. A world with its own seasons, its own language, its own love.

---

Chapter 47: The Visitor

The minister came in August.

He arrived on the supply boat. A thin man in a black coat, carrying a leather Bible. He stayed for one night.

He asked Isobel questions at dinner. Was she well? Did she have enough provisions? Did she attend to her prayers?

She answered politely.

Then he asked: "Are you happy, Mrs. Campbell?"

She paused. She looked across the table at Hamish. He was watching her, his face still.

"Yes," she said. "I am."

The minister nodded. He seemed satisfied.

But later, when Hamish was in the lantern room and the minister was preparing for bed, he spoke to her again.

"Most women would not choose this life," he said.

"I did not choose it," she said. "But I have chosen to stay."

"And that is enough?"

She looked out the window. The sea was dark. The beam of the lamp swept across it, steady and sure.

"Yes," she said. "It is."

---

Chapter 48: The Anniversary

One year.

Isobel woke on a soft September morning and realized it had been a year since she had arrived on Sgeir Mhor. The sun was warm. The sea was calm.

She walked the cliff path. The same path Hamish had walked her on their first day. The same path she had walked alone a hundred times since.

The sea was calm today. The horizon was clear. She could see the faint blue line of the mainland, distant and hazy.

It no longer looked like another world. It looked like a distant country, one she had visited long ago.

She heard footsteps behind her. Hamish came up and stood beside her.

"One year," she said.

"I know."

"I am glad I came."

He did not say anything. He did not need to.

They stood together on the cliff. The lighthouse stood behind them, white and steady against the sky.

She took his hand.

"Let us go in," she said. "I will make tea."

---

Chapter 49: The Light in the Dark

Isobel lit the lamp herself for the first time on a winter night.

Hamish had fallen asleep in his chair. The first time she had seen him sleep before the light was lit. She looked at his face in the dim light of the room. He was tired. The years of solitude, the winters, the grief of Morag—they were all there, written in the lines around his eyes.

She did not wake him.

She went up to the lantern room alone.

She had watched him do it a hundred times. She knew the steps. She trimmed the wick. She filled the oil. She struck the match.

The flame caught. She adjusted the flow. The light grew.

She set the mechanism in motion. The great lenses began to turn. The beam swept across the dark water.

She stood back and looked at what she had done.

The light burned steady and bright.

She was the keeper now. Not in name. But in the way that mattered.

She went back down to Hamish. She touched his shoulder.

"The light is lit," she said.

He opened his eyes. He looked at her. He understood.

He took her hand.

---

Chapter 50: The Sea, Still

Morning on Sgeir Mhor.

Isobel stood on the cliff with Hamish beside her. The sea was calm—so calm it barely moved. The water was the color of slate and silver, stretching to a sky that held no clouds.

The lighthouse rose behind them. The lamp was dark. The day's work was done. The sun climbed over the horizon, casting long shadows across the rock.

"It is quiet today," she said.

"It happens sometimes. The sea rests."

She looked at the water. She thought of all the days. The storms. The fog. The winter dark. She thought of Morag's diary in the drawer. She thought of the cracked glass, the dead garden, the fever. The rescue. The minister. The song.

She thought of his hand under the blanket in the dark.

"Do you miss her?" she asked.

Hamish was quiet for a long time.

"Yes," he said. "Every day."

She nodded.

"But differently now," he said. "That is what time does. It changes things."

She took his hand. His fingers closed around hers.

"Thank you for telling me."

He looked down at their hands. Then he looked at the sea.

"Isobel."

"Yes?"

"I love you."

The words hung in the air. Three words. Simple. She had waited a year to hear them.

She squeezed his hand.

"I know," she said. "I have known for a while."

He almost smiled. Just a twitch at the corner of his mouth. But it was a smile.

They stood on the cliff and watched the sea. The world was small. The world was theirs.

The lighthouse stood behind them, its light ready for the night.

But for now, the morning was quiet, and the sea was still.
