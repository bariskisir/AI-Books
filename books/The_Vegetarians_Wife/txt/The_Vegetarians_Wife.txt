Chapter 1: The Empty Plate

Hana scraped the rice into the bin. She washed the dish. She dried it and put it away. Jae sat at the table with his hands folded. He watched her move around the kitchen.

"I'm just not hungry," he said.

She nodded. She had heard him the first time. She filled a glass with water and set it in front of him. He did not touch it.

The kitchen was quiet. The clock ticked. Outside, a neighbor's dog barked once and fell silent. Hana wiped the counter with a cloth. She folded the cloth over the tap.

"Maybe tomorrow," she said.

Jae did not answer. His eyes were fixed on something beyond the window. The street was empty. The streetlights had not come on yet.

Hana turned off the kitchen light. Jae stayed at the table. She stood in the doorway and watched him sit in the dark. His silhouette did not move.

"Honey," she said. "Come to bed."

He stood slowly, like a man rising from deep water. He walked past her without looking. She heard his footsteps on the stairs. The bedroom door closed.

Hana stood alone in the kitchen. The plate was clean and dry in the rack. She had cooked for nothing. She had set the table for nothing. She opened the refrigerator and looked at the leftovers. She closed the door.

In the morning, the glass of water was still full.

Chapter 2: A Small Explanation

"I don't need to eat," Jae said at breakfast.

Hana was cracking eggs into a bowl. She stopped. The yolk broke.

"Everyone needs to eat," she said.

Jae shook his head. He was calm. He was not angry or sad. He spoke the way he spoke about the weather.

"My body doesn't want it."

Hana put down the whisk. She sat across from him. She looked at his face. His cheeks were still full. His eyes were clear.

"You haven't eaten in three days," she said.

"I know."

"Are you trying to lose weight?"

"No."

"Is it a political thing? A religious thing?"

"No."

She waited. He said nothing else. He picked up his coffee cup and drank. She watched his throat move as he swallowed.

At work, Hana could not focus. She taught her afternoon class by rote. The students' faces blurred. She kept seeing Jae's calm eyes.

She bought groceries on the way home. She bought his favorite things. Salmon. Asparagus. Dark chocolate. She cooked with care. She set the table with cloth napkins.

Jae sat down. He looked at the food. He picked up his fork. He moved a piece of salmon to the side of his plate.

"Thank you," he said. "It looks wonderful."

He did not take a bite.

Hana ate her dinner in silence. She watched him watch the food cool. When she cleared the plates, his portion was untouched.

"I'm just not hungry," he said again.

The words were smooth. They were rehearsed. She wondered who he had practiced for.

Chapter 3: The First Week

Hana bought broth. She bought protein powder. She bought meal replacement drinks in cardboard cartons.

Jae accepted the broth. He held the cup in both hands. He brought it to his lips. He swallowed once, twice, three times. Then he set the cup down.

"Thank you," he said.

He drank water. He drank black tea. He did not eat.

On the fifth day, Hana made soup. She blended vegetables into a smooth puree. She added cream. She seasoned it carefully. She brought it to him in a bowl.

Jae ate three spoonfuls. He put the spoon down.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Don't apologize."

"I know this is hard for you."

She wanted to scream. She wanted to shake him. She sat very still and breathed through her nose.

"It's hard for both of us," she said.

He nodded. He looked at her with something like pity. She did not want his pity.

That night she lay awake beside him. His breathing was steady. She put her hand on his chest. His heart beat slow and even. He was alive. He was right here.

She pressed her palm against his ribs. She could feel them now. The flesh was softening, thinning. She pulled her hand away.

In the morning, she checked the trash. The soup bowl was in the sink. The broth cup was in the bin. He had eaten almost nothing.

She called Dr. Elias.

Chapter 4: The Second Week

Dr. Elias came on a Tuesday. He was a young man with kind eyes and a calm voice. He sat across from Jae in the living room.

"Tell me what's happening," he said.

Jae described it simply. He did not feel hunger. Food tasted like ash. His body did not want it.

Dr. Elias nodded. He asked questions. Did Jae feel nauseous? No. Any pain? No. Dizziness? No. Weakness? A little. But not much.

"Let me examine you."

In the bedroom, Hana waited. She heard the murmur of voices. She heard the soft click of the stethoscope. She folded her hands. She unfolded them.

Dr. Elias came out alone.

"I can't find anything wrong," he said.

"Nothing?"

"Vitals are normal. Reflexes are normal. He's lost some weight, but he's not dehydrated. There's no fever, no infection."

"Then what is it?"

Dr. Elias hesitated. "It could be psychological. Depression. Anxiety. Sometimes these things manifest physically."

"Jae isn't depressed."

"You would know better than me. But I'd like to run some tests. Blood work. A scan."

Hana agreed. She sat beside Jae while Dr. Elias drew blood. The needle went in. Jae did not flinch. He watched the blood fill the vial with quiet curiosity.

"I'll call you with the results," Dr. Elias said.

After he left, Jae went upstairs. Hana heard the bedroom door close. She stood in the living room and looked at the place where Jae had sat. The cushion was still warm.

She pressed her hand into the warmth. She held it there until it cooled.

Chapter 5: Vital Signs

The results came back normal.

Hana read the email three times. Hemoglobin normal. White count normal. Thyroid normal. Liver function normal. Everything normal.

"He's healthy," Dr. Elias said on the phone.

"Then why won't he eat?"

"I don't know."

The silence stretched. Hana watched a bird land on the windowsill. It tilted its head. It flew away.

"Keep monitoring him," Dr. Elias said. "If anything changes, call me."

"Anything changes," Hana repeated. "He's starving to death."

"He's not starving. His body is holding steady. I don't understand it either."

She hung up. She stood in the kitchen. The refrigerator hummed. She opened it and looked at the neat rows of containers. Vegetables. Rice. Sauce. Food she had prepared for a man who would not eat.

She took out a container of stew. She ate it standing over the sink. She barely tasted it.

Jae came downstairs. He wore a sweater that hung loose on his frame.

"What did the doctor say?"

"Nothing. He said nothing."

Jae nodded. He walked to the window. He pressed his palm to the glass.

"The light is different today," he said.

She looked at the window. The sun was bright. The room was full of light. It looked the same as always.

"It looks the same to me," she said.

"Not to me."

She watched him stand at the window. His hand left a print on the glass. She did not wipe it away.

Chapter 6: The Dream of Light

Jae woke her in the night.

"I dreamed of a white field," he said.

His voice was soft. She turned on the lamp. He was sitting up. His eyes were open but not focused on her.

"No animals," he said. "No sound. Just the field and the sky."

"What does it mean?"

"I don't know. But it was beautiful."

The word hung in the air. Beautiful. He had called the empty field beautiful.

She lay back down. He lay beside her. She listened to his breathing. It was slow, rhythmic, like a tide going out.

"Were you afraid?" she asked.

"No."

"Not at all?"

"There was nothing to fear."

She closed her eyes. She tried to imagine the field. White grass. White sky. No horizon. No boundary. Just endless blankness.

She opened her eyes. The ceiling was dark. The lamp cast a small circle of light. Jae had turned his head toward the window.

"What are you looking at?"

"The stars," he said.

"There are no stars. It's cloudy."

"There are always stars."

She did not argue. She turned off the lamp. In the dark, she felt his hand find hers. His fingers were cold. She held them.

She fell asleep holding his hand. In the morning, he was still awake. He had not slept at all.

Chapter 7: A Body Changing

Jae was disappearing.

Not all at once. In increments. A hollow appearing beneath his cheekbones. The sharp ridge of his collarbone pushing against his skin. His wrists thin as branches.

Hana watched him undress. He did not hide from her. He stood in the bedroom in his underwear and folded his clothes with precision.

"You're so thin," she said.

He looked down at himself. "I suppose so."

"Aren't you worried?"

"Worried about what?"

She gestured at his body. "This. You."

He considered the question. His fingers touched his own ribs.

"It doesn't feel like dying," he said. "It feels like becoming."

She did not ask what he meant. She was afraid of the answer.

At dinner, she set a plate for herself. She did not set one for him. He sat across from her and watched her eat.

"The smell of food doesn't bother you?" she asked.

"No."

"It used to bring you into the kitchen. You used to say you could smell dinner from the front door."

He tilted his head. "I remember that."

"But you don't feel it anymore."

"No."

She ate her rice. Each grain was separate, distinct. She chewed slowly. He watched. His eyes were patient.

After dinner, she washed the dishes. He stood behind her. She felt his presence like a draft from an open window.

"Tell me about your day," he said.

She told him. Her voice filled the kitchen. She talked about her students, her colleague who was retiring, the leaky faucet in the bathroom. He listened. He nodded. He asked questions.

He did not eat. He did not drink. He stood in the doorway of the kitchen and listened to his wife talk, and she thought: this is what it will be like when he is gone.

Chapter 8: The Smell of Cooking

Hana fried onions in butter.

The kitchen filled with the smell of caramelizing sugar. It was her favorite smell. She had always believed that a kitchen that smelled of onions was a kitchen that was alive.

She turned to look at Jae. He sat in his chair at the table. His hands were folded.

"Do you smell that?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Is it nothing? Is it ash?"

He considered. "It smells like onions."

"Does it make you want to eat?"

"No."

She turned back to the stove. She added garlic. She added thyme. The steam rose. Her eyes watered. It was the onions, not crying.

She told herself it was the onions.

She plated the food. She sat down. She ate alone. Jae watched. His stillness was complete. He did not fidget. He did not check his phone. He simply watched her eat.

"You used to love my cooking," she said.

"I still love it."

"You don't eat it."

"I love that you cook. I love watching you."

She put down her fork. "That's not the same."

"I know."

She pushed her plate away. The food was half-eaten. She was not hungry anymore.

Jae reached across the table. His hand covered hers. His palm was dry. His fingers were long and pale.

"It will be okay," he said.

"How do you know?"

"I don't. But I believe it."

She pulled her hand away. She took her plate to the sink. She scraped the remains into the bin. She washed the dish. She dried it. She put it away.

The kitchen still smelled of onions. It would smell like onions for hours.

Chapter 9: The Doctor Returns

Dr. Elias came again. He brought a portable ultrasound machine. He set it up in the living room.

"I want to look at his organs," he said.

Jae lay on the sofa. He lifted his shirt. His ribcage was a cage of light. Dr. Elias pressed the wand to his skin. The image appeared on the screen.

"Everything looks normal," Dr. Elias said.

"Of course it does," Hana said.

Dr. Elias did not respond. He moved the wand. He captured images. He clicked and saved.

"How much weight has he lost?"

"Twelve kilos," Hana said.

"In three weeks?"

"Yes."

Dr. Elias sat back. He looked at Jae. Jae lay still. His eyes were closed.

"Mr. Kim, are you in any pain?"

"No."

"Discomfort?"

"No."

"Do you have any thoughts of harming yourself?"

"No."

"Any thoughts of death?"

Jae opened his eyes. "I think about death all the time. But I'm not trying to die."

Dr. Elias wrote something in his notes. Hana watched his pen move across the paper.

"I'm recommending a specialist," Dr. Elias said. "A psychiatrist who works with eating disorders."

"We can't afford a specialist."

"Your insurance covers it."

Hana looked at Jae. He was still lying on the sofa. His eyes were closed again. His chest barely moved.

"Fine," she said. "We'll see the specialist."

After Dr. Elias left, Hana sat beside Jae. She touched his forehead. His skin was cool.

"I'm sorry," she whispered.

"For what?"

"For not being able to fix this."

He opened his eyes. "There's nothing to fix."

He said it with such certainty. She wanted to believe him. She wanted to believe that this was not a tragedy unfolding in her living room.

She could not believe him.

Chapter 10: The Third Week

Jae stopped drinking water.

Hana noticed the glass on the bedside table. It had been full for two days. She picked it up. She brought it to him.

"Drink," she said.

He shook his head.

"You haven't had anything in two days."

"I'm not thirsty."

"Jae, you need water. Your body needs water."

"My body doesn't want it."

She stood over him. The glass shook in her hand. Water splashed onto her wrist.

"Drink the water."

He looked at her. His eyes were pale. She had not noticed how pale they had become. The brown had faded to something like amber.

"Why?" he asked.

"Because I'm asking you. Because I'm your wife. Because I don't want to watch you die."

He took the glass. He drank. She watched his throat move. He drank half the glass and handed it back.

"Thank you," she said.

He nodded. He lay back against the pillow. His eyes closed.

She sat beside him. She counted his breaths. They were slow. She counted twelve in a minute. A normal person breathed sixteen or more.

"Jae?"

"Yes?"

"Are you afraid?"

"A little."

She took his hand. "I'm afraid too."

He squeezed her fingers. His grip was weak. It used to be strong. He used to be able to crush her hand if he wanted. She had complained about it. She had told him to be gentler.

She would give anything to feel that grip again.

"I'm going to figure this out," she said.

He did not answer.

"Jae?"

He was asleep. Or something like sleep. His chest rose and fell. His hand was loose in hers.

She sat with him until dark. She did not turn on the light.

Chapter 11: Thirst

Jae's lips cracked. The skin peeled. He ran his tongue over them but there was no moisture.

Hana brought a cup of water with a straw. She held it to his mouth.

"Just a sip."

He drank. A small sip. The water ran down his chin. She wiped it with her sleeve.

His tongue was white. She saw it when he opened his mouth.

"Open wider," she said.

He obeyed. His mouth was dry as paper. The insides of his cheeks were pale.

"I need to get you to drink more."

"I can't."

"Try."

He tried. He took three sips. He swallowed with difficulty.

"That's good," she said. "That's very good."

He closed his eyes. She wet a cloth with cool water and pressed it to his lips. He did not resist.

She called Dr. Elias.

"He needs IV fluids," she said.

"Bring him to the clinic."

"He won't go."

"Then I'll come to you."

Dr. Elias came that evening with a bag of saline. He set up the drip in the bedroom. He inserted the needle into Jae's arm. Jae did not flinch.

The fluid dripped. Jae's body accepted it. Dr. Elias watched the bag empty.

"This isn't sustainable," he said.

"I know."

"At some point, we'll need to hospitalize him."

"Not yet."

"Hana."

"Not yet."

Dr. Elias packed his equipment. He wrote a note. He left it on the table.

"Call me when you're ready," he said.

After he left, Hana sat beside Jae. The IV had been removed. There was a small bandage on his arm.

"I'm sorry," she said.

He did not answer. He was looking at the ceiling. His lips moved. He was whispering something.

"What?" she asked.

He did not stop. She leaned closer.

"The door," he whispered. "The door is green."

Chapter 12: The Dream of Silence

Jae dreamed again. This time he told her everything.

"The field is endless," he said. "There's no wind. No birds. No sound at all. But there's a house."

"A house?"

"A small house. With a green door. The door is closed."

"Did you go inside?"

"Not yet. But I will."

She was sitting beside the bed. He was lying down. His eyes were closed. He spoke as if reading from a script.

"What's inside the house?" she asked.

"I don't know. But I need to see."

She touched his hand. "I don't want you to go."

He opened his eyes. "I don't think I have a choice."

"Of course you have a choice. You choose to eat. You choose to drink. You choose to stay."

He shook his head. "It's not that simple."

"Then explain it to me."

He was quiet for a long time. She thought he had fallen asleep.

"When I stopped eating," he said finally, "it wasn't a decision. It was more like... remembering something I had forgotten."

"Remembering what?"

"Another way of being."

She let go of his hand. She stood up. She walked to the window. The street was empty. A single car passed. Its headlights swept across the room.

"I don't understand," she said.

"I know."

"And I'm not sure I want to."

He did not answer. She pressed her forehead to the glass. It was cold.

In the mirror of the window, she saw his reflection. He was sitting up. He was looking at her. His eyes were the color of honey in the dark.

Chapter 13: IV Fluids

The drip became routine.

Dr. Elias came every other day. He set up the saline. He checked Jae's vitals. He asked questions. Jae answered. The answers made less sense each time.

"Do you know where you are?" Dr. Elias asked.

"Home."

"Do you know the date?"

"The date doesn't matter here."

"Here?"

"My home."

Dr. Elias looked at Hana. She looked away.

After the IV, Jae's color improved. His lips softened. His tongue lost the white coating. But he did not eat. He did not drink on his own.

"He can't maintain this," Dr. Elias said in the kitchen.

"I know."

"He'll need a feeding tube soon."

"Not yet."

"You're running out of time."

She knew. She knew better than anyone. She watched him shrink. She watched his clothes hang loose. She watched his belt pull tight to the last notch.

"I'll talk to him," she said.

She went upstairs. Jae was awake. He turned his head when she entered.

"I heard," he said.

"Then you know."

"I won't accept a feeding tube."

"You'll die without it."

He looked at her. His face was calm. The bones were sharp beneath his skin.

"Maybe that's what's supposed to happen."

She sat on the bed. The mattress dipped. Her weight shifted him toward her.

"Please," she said. "Please don't leave me."

He touched her face. His fingers traced her cheekbone. Her jaw. Her lips.

"I'm already gone," he said. "I'm just waiting for my body to catch up."

Chapter 14: A Wife's Work

Hana stopped going to work.

She called the school. She said she needed personal leave. Her voice was steady. She sounded professional. She did not say her husband was starving himself for no reason.

She spent her days beside him. She read to him. She told him about her day. She described the weather.

"Clouds today," she said. "Big ones. They look like they might rain."

"Rain is good," he said.

"The garden needs it."

"Everything needs water."

She stopped. He was not looking at her. He was looking at the ceiling. His lips were moving again.

"What do you see?" she asked.

"The ceiling."

"What do you really see?"

He turned his head. "The house," he said. "The green door."

"Is it open?"

"Almost."

She took his hand. "Don't go inside."

"I have to."

"Wait. Just wait."

He closed his eyes. He did not answer.

She stayed beside him. She counted his breaths. She timed them. They were getting slower.

She got up. She went to the kitchen. She opened the refrigerator. The shelves were full. She had been buying food out of habit. Vegetables. Meat. Cheese. A carton of eggs.

She took out an egg. She cracked it into a bowl. She scrambled it. She ate it standing at the counter.

The egg was good. She tasted it. Salt. Butter. The richness of yolk.

She washed the bowl. She dried it. She put it away.

Then she went back upstairs and sat beside her husband and watched him drift further away.

Chapter 15: The Things He Knows

"You had a dog," Jae said.

Hana looked up from her book. "Yes. When I was a girl."

"His name was Toby."

"That's right."

"He died when you were twelve. Your father buried him under the apple tree."

She closed the book. "How did you know that?"

Jae shrugged. "You told me."

"I didn't. I never told you that story."

"Are you sure?"

"Yes. I'm sure."

He frowned. His brow furrowed. It was the first expression she had seen on his face in days.

"I remember it," he said. "I can see it. The tree. The hole your father dug. You wore a blue dress."

She felt the air leave her lungs. She had worn a blue dress. Her mother had made her change before the burial. She had cried. The dress had gotten muddy anyway.

"How do you know this?"

"I don't know."

"Jae."

"I see it. I see things. Pictures. Like memories that aren't mine."

She put the book aside. She moved closer to him. His face was thin. His eyes were bright.

"What else do you see?"

"A kitchen. An old woman. She's making soup."

"Do you know her?"

"No. But she's kind."

"Are you afraid of these pictures?"

He shook his head. "They're not frightening. They're just... information. Like someone is telling me stories."

"Who?"

"I don't know."

She watched his face. He was telling the truth. She could always tell when he lied. His left eye twitched. Today it was still.

"Tell me more," she said.

He closed his eyes. "There's a city. I don't know its name. There are towers. White towers. The streets are made of sand."

"That sounds beautiful."

"It is. But it's empty. Everyone is gone."

"Where did they go?"

He opened his eyes. "To the field."

Chapter 16: The Forgotten Year

"What year did we get married?"

Jae blinked. He looked at the ceiling.

"2014," he said.

"No. 2016."

"Right. 2016."

"The church was St. Mary's. You wore a gray suit."

"I remember."

"What color were the flowers?"

He hesitated. "White."

"The bridesmaids wore blue."

"Yes."

"What shade?"

He closed his eyes. "Navy."

"No. It was periwinkle."

He opened his eyes. He looked at her. For a moment, she saw something like confusion.

"I don't remember that."

"You were there. You saw them."

"I remember the suit. I remember the cake."

"Do you remember the first dance?"

"We danced to that song. The one we liked."

"What was the song?"

He could not answer.

She waited. She watched him search. His face was open. He was trying.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"It's okay."

"It's not."

"It's fine."

But it was not fine. He had forgotten their wedding. He had forgotten the song. He had forgotten the color of the flowers.

She left the room. She went to the closet. She found the box with their wedding album. She opened it. The photographs were crisp. She looked at the faces. Her own face, young and hopeful. His face, young and present.

He had been there. He had stood beside her. He had made vows.

She brought the album to the bedroom. She opened it on the bed.

"Look," she said.

He looked. He turned the pages. He examined each photograph.

"I don't remember this day," he said.

She closed the album. She took it downstairs. She put it back in the closet.

She sat in the dark living room. She did not turn on the light.

Chapter 17: Maps in His Palm

Jae asked for paper.

Hana brought a notebook and a pen. He sat up in bed. He drew.

The lines were precise. They were not the drawings of a man losing his mind. They were architectural. Measured.

"What is it?" she asked.

"A city."

She looked at the page. Streets intersected at perfect angles. A river curved through the center. There were squares. Parks. A wall with gates.

"I don't recognize it," she said.

"It's not here."

"Where is it?"

"In the field. Beyond the house."

She looked at his hand. The pen moved with confidence. He did not hesitate. He did not correct.

"How do you know what it looks like?"

"I see it."

"Since when?"

"Since the dreams started."

He finished the drawing. He dated it at the bottom. June 5.

"That's today's date," she said.

"I know."

"But you don't know today's date when I ask you."

"I know it when I need to."

She took the drawing. She studied it. The detail was extraordinary. Tiny windows in each building. A bridge with visible cables. Trees along the river.

"You've never been to a city like this," she said.

"I have been. In my sleep."

She put the drawing on the dresser. She looked at it all evening. She could not stop looking at it.

She called Dr. Elias.

"I think he's hallucinating," she said.

"He's drawing maps of a city that doesn't exist."

"I'll come tomorrow."

She hung up. She went to the bedroom. Jae was asleep. The pen was still in his hand.

She gently took it from him. His fingers did not resist.

Chapter 18: The House in the Field

"Describe the house," Hana said.

Jae was awake. His eyes were on the ceiling. She had learned to recognize when he was seeing the field. His pupils dilated. His breathing slowed.

"It's small," he said. "Wooden. White paint that's peeling. The roof is tin."

"The green door?"

"Green. Dark green. The paint is chipping. There's a window next to the door. The glass is cracked."

"What else?"

"A step. One step up to the door. There's a mat. It says Welcome."

She laughed. A bitter sound.

"Your hallucination has a welcome mat."

"It's not a hallucination."

"Then what is it?"

"A place. A real place."

"How can it be real if you've never been there?"

He turned his head. His eyes met hers. They were pale. Almost colorless.

"I am there now," he said. "Part of me. A part I didn't know I had."

She did not answer. She could not.

"Don't be afraid," he said.

"I'm not afraid."

"You are. I can feel it."

"How?"

"I don't know. But I can."

She left the room. She went downstairs. She opened the front door and stood on the porch. The air was cool. The street was quiet.

She looked at the houses on her street. They were ordinary houses. Brick and siding. White doors. Black doors. No green doors.

She went back inside. She locked the door. She checked it twice.

Chapter 19: Dr. Elias Worries

Dr. Elias came in the morning. He brought a colleague. A woman with sharp glasses and a clipboard.

"This is Dr. Park," he said. "A neurologist."

Hana shook the woman's hand. Her grip was firm.

"I'd like to run some tests," Dr. Park said. "MRI. EEG. Just to rule things out."

"What things?"

"Tumors. Lesions. Seizure activity."

"He doesn't have a tumor."

"Then the tests will show nothing, and we'll be reassured."

Hana agreed. She signed the forms.

Jae did not protest. He let them take him to the hospital. He lay still in the machine. He did not flinch at the noise.

Hana waited in the hallway. She watched the door. She counted the tiles on the floor.

Dr. Park came out after an hour.

"Results are clean," she said. "No tumors. No lesions. Normal brain activity."

"Then what's wrong with him?"

Dr. Park hesitated. "I don't know."

"Is there a name for this?"

"There are conditions. Eating disorders. Psychotic disorders. But your husband doesn't fit any of them."

"So what do I do?"

"Keep him hydrated. Keep him alive. And wait."

"Wait for what?"

"For something to change."

Hana drove Jae home. He sat in the passenger seat. He looked out the window.

"They didn't find anything," he said.

"No."

"Because there's nothing to find."

She gripped the steering wheel. "You're disappearing, Jae. There's something to find."

He touched her arm. His fingers were cold.

"I'm not disappearing," he said. "I'm arriving."

Chapter 20: Hana Says No

Dr. Elias called.

"There's a facility," he said. "In Seoul. They specialize in unexplained medical conditions."

"No."

"Hana, he needs round-the-clock care."

"Not yet."

"He's down to thirty-eight kilos."

"I know."

"He can't walk more than a few steps."

"I know."

"You can't do this alone."

"Watch me."

She hung up. She turned off her phone. She sat in the kitchen. The house was quiet.

Jae was upstairs. She had carried him to the bathroom that morning. His body was light. She could lift him now. The man who used to carry her over the threshold.

She went upstairs. Jae was in bed. His eyes were closed.

"I'm not sending you away," she said.

He opened his eyes. "I know."

"They want to put you in a hospital. Tube feeding. Monitoring."

"I don't want that."

"I know."

She sat beside him. She took his hand. His fingers were like twigs.

"I had a dream," she said. "Last night. I was in the field. I saw the house."

He looked at her. His eyes sharpened.

"What did you see?"

"Green door. White house. Peeling paint."

"You saw it."

"Yes."

He smiled. It was a small smile. It was the first smile she had seen in weeks.

"Then you understand," he said.

"I don't understand anything."

"You saw the house. You're connected now."

"Connected to what?"

"I don't know. But we're connected. You and me. And the house."

She did not want to be connected. She wanted her husband back. She wanted him to eat. She wanted him to drink. She wanted him to be normal.

But she had seen the house. She could not unsee it.

Chapter 21: The Eyes Change

Hana noticed it in the morning light.

She was drawing the curtains. Sunlight fell across the bed. Jae's eyes were open. They were not brown anymore.

"What color are your eyes?" she asked.

"Brown."

"They're not."

She brought a mirror. She held it in front of his face.

He looked. He did not react.

"Amber," he said. "Or honey."

"They were brown last week."

"Were they?"

"Yes."

He looked again. He did not seem concerned.

"I don't know," he said. "I didn't notice."

She put the mirror aside. She sat on the bed. She examined his face. His skin was pale, almost translucent. She could see veins at his temples.

"Do you have pain anywhere?"

"No."

"Headaches?"

"No."

"Double vision?"

"No."

She touched his cheek. The bone was sharp. His skin was cool.

"Your body is changing," she said.

"Yes."

"And you're not worried?"

"It doesn't hurt. It doesn't feel wrong."

"But it is wrong. You know that, right? A human body needs food. A human body needs water."

"This body doesn't."

She pulled her hand away. She stood up. She walked to the door.

"Where are you going?" he asked.

"To call your doctor."

"There's no point."

She left the room. She closed the door behind her.

In the hallway, she leaned against the wall. She pressed her palms to her eyes. She stood there for a long time.

When she went back in, Jae was drawing again. Another map. Another city.

"Who are you?" she whispered.

He did not answer.

Chapter 22: No Sleep

Jae stopped sleeping.

She noticed on the third night. She woke at three in the morning. He was sitting up. His eyes were open.

"Are you okay?"

"Yes."

"Why are you awake?"

"I'm not tired."

She checked the clock. It was three in the morning. He had been awake for over forty hours.

"You need to sleep."

"I try. I close my eyes. But I don't fall asleep."

"What happens?"

"My mind stays awake. It visits the field."

"The field again."

"Yes. The field is clearer at night."

She sat up. She turned on the lamp. His face was illuminated. He looked tired. Dark circles under his eyes. His skin was gray.

"You can't stay awake forever."

"There is no forever."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only answer I have."

She lay back down. She closed her eyes. She felt him beside her. He was still. Unmoving. She did not hear him breathe.

"Jae?"

"Yes?"

"Breathe. I need to hear you breathe."

She heard a slow inhale. A slow exhale. She counted. Six breaths in a minute.

"You're breathing too slow."

"It's enough."

"It's not enough. You're going to stop."

"Maybe."

She opened her eyes. She turned to look at him. His face was calm. His eyes were fixed on the window.

"Don't stop," she said. "Please."

He looked at her. He smiled. It was a sad smile.

"I'll try."

She held his hand. She stayed awake until morning. She counted each breath. She did not let go.

Chapter 23: Weight

Jae weighed thirty-four kilos.

The scale was in the bathroom. Hana had bought it to track his decline. She wished she had not.

He stood on it. The numbers blinked. She looked away.

"What is it?" he asked.

"Thirty-four."

"That's not so bad."

"You're a hundred and eighty centimeters tall. You should be seventy kilos."

He stepped off the scale. He swayed. She caught his arm.

"You're too weak."

"I'm fine."

"Lean on me."

He leaned. His weight was nothing. She could carry him. She hated that she could carry him.

Back in bed, he closed his eyes. She covered him with a blanket. The blanket was flat over his body. She could see his shape. He was all angles.

"I used to be big," he said.

"You were never big. You were normal."

"I used to lift weights."

"I remember."

"You liked my arms."

She did not answer. She remembered his arms. She remembered the muscle beneath the skin. She remembered how safe she felt when he held her.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Don't apologize."

"I'm sorry for what I'm doing to you."

She took his hand. She pressed it to her cheek.

"Just stay," she said. "That's all I ask."

He did not promise. He did not say he would.

He closed his eyes. She watched his chest rise. She watched it fall.

She waited for the next breath. It came. She exhaled.

Chapter 24: The Bed

Hana moved to the guest room.

She told herself it was practical. She needed sleep. He kept her awake. The slow breathing. The stillness. The silence.

She told herself it was temporary. One night. Two.

She did not go back.

The guest room was cold. The bed was unfamiliar. She lay awake anyway. She stared at the ceiling. She listened to the house.

She got up. She walked to the master bedroom. She stood in the doorway.

Jae was lying on his back. His arms were at his sides. He did not move. He did not seem to breathe.

She stepped closer. She touched his chest. His heart was beating. Slow, but present.

"Are you awake?" she whispered.

"Yes."

"I moved to the guest room."

"I know."

"You didn't say anything."

"You need to sleep."

She sat on the edge of the bed. The mattress barely moved. He was so light.

"I don't know what to do," she said.

"Nothing."

"Nothing?"

"You don't need to do anything. This isn't your fault."

"Whose fault is it?"

"No one's."

She lay down beside him. She pressed her body against his. He was cold. She put her arm across his chest.

"I'm not leaving," she said.

"I know."

"I mean it. I'm going to be here until the end."

He did not ask what end. He knew.

She fell asleep beside him. She dreamed of the white field. She walked toward the green door.

She woke before she reached it.

Chapter 25: The Things He Forgets

"Do you know my name?"

Jae blinked. "Hana."

"Good. Who is the president?"

He hesitated. "I don't know."

"Which year is it?"

"2025."

"2026."

"Close enough."

"It's not close. It's a year off."

He shrugged. The gesture was barely visible. He did not have the strength for a full shrug.

"Where do you work?"

"I don't work anymore."

"Where did you work?"

He frowned. "I was an architect."

"Yes. What firm?"

He could not answer. His face twisted. He was trying. She could see the effort.

"Shin and Partners," she said.

"Right. Shin and Partners."

"Your boss's name?"

"I don't remember."

"Lee. His name was Lee."

"Lee. Yes."

She took a breath. "When did we meet?"

"At a party."

"Whose party?"

"I don't remember."

"Jae's co-worker. The one who quit. He had a party at his apartment."

"Yes. That party."

"What was the first thing you said to me?"

He closed his eyes. He was searching. She watched him search.

"You wore a blue dress," he said quietly.

"I did."

"You were standing by the window. I said you looked like you were thinking about something important."

"You did. You said that."

"Did I?"

"Yes. And then you asked me what I was thinking about."

"What were you thinking about?"

"I don't remember. It doesn't matter."

She touched his face. His skin was cold.

"I remember you," he said.

"Remember the important things."

"You are the important thing."

She kissed his forehead. His skin tasted like salt.

Chapter 26: The Things He Knows

"Your mother had a sister," Jae said.

Hana stopped reading. She had been reading aloud. A novel. Something with a plot.

"Yes. Aunt Mira."

"She died before you were born."

"That's right."

"She had a kitchen with yellow curtains."

Hana closed the book. "I don't know that. I never saw her kitchen."

"I see it. The curtains are yellow. There's a ceramic rooster on the windowsill."

She felt cold. Her mother had a ceramic rooster. It had belonged to Aunt Mira. She had mentioned it once, years ago. Maybe.

"Can you see other things?"

"Sometimes. When I'm in the field. The house shows me things."

"The house shows you things."

"Yes. Pictures. Memories. Not mine."

"Whose?"

"Everyone's."

She set the book aside. She moved closer to him.

"Show me," she said.

"I can't. You have to see them yourself."

"You said I was connected now. Because I dreamed of the house."

"Yes."

"Then show me how to see."

He took her hand. His fingers were cold. He placed her palm on his chest. His heart beat beneath her hand.

"Close your eyes," he said.

She closed them.

"Think about the field. The white grass. The silence."

She tried. She saw only darkness.

"I can't."

"Try harder."

She pressed her palm against his heart. She counted the beats. They were slow.

The darkness shifted. She saw a hint of white. Far away. Small.

"I see something."

"Keep looking."

The white expanded. It became a field. She was standing at the edge. The grass was pale. The sky was pale. There was no horizon.

"Keep going."

She walked. The grass did not bend beneath her feet. There was no sound. The silence was absolute.

She saw the house. Small. White. Green door.

She opened her eyes.

"I saw it," she whispered.

"I know."

Chapter 27: The Green Door

"Tell me about the door," Hana said.

Jae lay in bed. His eyes were closed. But he was not asleep. She knew the difference now.

"What do you want to know?"

"Is it real?"

"It's real in the field."

"Have you opened it?"

"Not yet."

"Why not?"

"I'm not ready."

"Ready for what?"

He opened his eyes. They were pale. Almost white.

"To see what's inside."

She thought about this. She thought about the door in her dream. The green paint. The cracked window. The welcome mat.

"What do you think is inside?"

"I don't know. Something I need."

"Something you need more than me?"

He looked at her. His expression was unreadable.

"It's not about more or less."

"Then what is it about?"

"Completion."

She stood up. She walked to the window. The street was empty. The sky was gray.

"I don't want you to complete anything. I want you to stay here. With me."

"I am here."

"For now."

He did not argue. He did not promise.

"I'll wait," she said. "I'll wait until you're ready. And then I'll open the door with you."

"You can't."

"Watch me."

He smiled. It was the saddest smile she had ever seen.

"That's the thing about the door," he said. "Only one person can walk through."

She turned away. She pressed her forehead to the glass.

"I hate this house," she said.

She did not know which house she meant.

Chapter 28: Dr. Elias's Final Visit

Dr. Elias came for the last time.

He sat in the living room. He did not bring his bag. He did not bring the IV.

"I can't help him anymore," he said.

Hana nodded.

"His body is shutting down. His kidneys. His liver. There's nothing I can do."

"I know."

"I've never seen anything like this."

"Neither have I."

Dr. Elias looked at his hands. He was a young doctor. This was the first patient he had lost.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Thank you for trying."

"There's a specialist in Tokyo. I could make a call."

"He won't go."

"Do you want me to come by? For moral support?"

She shook her head. "I need to do this alone."

He stood. He handed her a card. His personal number.

"Call me if you need anything. Anything at all."

She took the card. She put it in her pocket.

He left. She watched his car drive away. She stood at the door for a long time.

She went upstairs. Jae was awake. He looked at her.

"He's gone," she said.

"Yes."

"He can't help you."

"I know."

"No one can help you."

"You can."

She sat beside him. She took his hand.

"Tell me how."

"Just be here. That's all."

She held his hand. She watched his chest rise and fall. The intervals were long. She counted. Ten seconds between breaths.

"I'm afraid," she said.

"Don't be."

"There's nothing left."

"There's still the field."

She closed her eyes. She saw the white grass. The green door. The door was open. Just a crack.

She opened her eyes.

"The door is open," she said.

"Yes."

"What does that mean?"

"Almost time."

Chapter 29: A Quiet Kitchen

Hana sat in the kitchen.

The room was clean. She had cleaned it that morning. Dishes washed. Counter wiped. Floor swept.

The refrigerator hummed. Inside, food was going bad. Milk. Vegetables. Meat. She had stopped cooking.

She opened the refrigerator. She took out a carton of milk. She sniffed it. Sour. She poured it down the sink.

She took out the vegetables. Limp. She threw them in the bin.

She took out the meat. Gray. She wrapped it in newspaper and put it in the freezer.

She stood at the counter. The kitchen was empty now. No food on the counters. No pots on the stove.

She had been a wife who cooked. It was how she showed love. It was how she filled the silence.

Now the silence was full. She did not need to fill it.

She sat at the table. Jae's chair was empty. She touched the place where he used to sit.

She remembered him at this table. Reading the paper. Drinking coffee. Laughing at something on his phone.

That man was gone.

A different man was upstairs. A man who did not eat. Who did not sleep. Who saw things she could not see.

She loved him. She loved them both.

She went upstairs. She sat beside him.

"I cleaned out the refrigerator," she said.

"Good."

"I threw away the food."

"Good."

"I don't know if I'll ever cook again."

He did not answer. He was looking at the ceiling.

"Jae?"

"The door is open," he said.

Chapter 30: The Reflection

Hana stood in front of the bathroom mirror.

She looked older. The lines around her eyes were deeper. Her skin was pale. She had not been sleeping.

She touched her face. She looked at her hands. They were the same hands. But everything else had changed.

She thought about who she had been before. A wife. A teacher. A woman who planned dinners and went to bed at eleven.

Now she was a caretaker. A witness. A woman who counted breaths.

She did not recognize herself.

She went to the bedroom. Jae was lying still. She lay beside him.

"Do you remember what I used to look like?" she asked.

"Yes."

"Do I still look like that?"

He turned his head. He looked at her. His eyes were pale, unfocused.

"You look tired."

"That's not what I asked."

"You look like someone who has been fighting."

"I have been."

"I know."

He reached for her hand. She let him take it.

"I'm sorry," he said.

"Stop apologizing."

"I'm sorry for what I've taken from you."

She closed her eyes. Tears leaked from the corners. She did not wipe them.

"Give it back," she whispered.

"I can't."

"I know."

She lay beside him. She pressed her face to his shoulder. She felt the bone beneath the skin.

"I'm still here," she said.

"I know."

"When you go, I'll still be here."

"Yes."

"I don't know how to live without you."

He did not have an answer. She did not expect one.

She fell asleep beside him. She dreamed of the field. She walked to the house. The green door was open.

She looked inside. She saw nothing but light.

Chapter 31: Jae Stops Speaking

Three days of silence.

Jae did not speak. He lay in bed. His eyes were open. He blinked. He breathed.

But he did not speak.

Hana talked for both of them. She told him about the weather. She told him about a bird that had built a nest in the eaves. She told him about the mail.

"I got a bill today," she said. "Electric. You used to handle the bills."

Silence.

"I think I can manage. I'll set up automatic payments."

Silence.

She touched his face. His skin was cool.

"Do you understand me?"

A blink. Slow. Deliberate. Yes.

"You can't speak anymore?"

Another blink. Yes.

"Is it the field?"

A blink. Yes.

"Are you still in there?"

A blink. Yes.

She took his hand. She pressed it to her cheek.

"I'm here," she said. "I'm not going anywhere."

He blinked. Slow. She took it as acknowledgment.

She stayed beside him. She read to him. She held his hand. She waited.

On the third night, she dreamed of the field again. The door was open wider. She could see the room inside. A chair. A table. A figure sitting in the chair.

She woke before she saw the face.

Chapter 32: The Narrow Bed

Hana moved back to the master bedroom.

The guest room was a mistake. She needed to be close. She needed to feel his presence, even if he was not there.

She lay beside him. The bed was too big for his body. He was a shadow. A suggestion of a man.

She pressed herself against his side. She put her arm across his chest. His heart beat beneath her palm.

"I'm here," she whispered.

He did not answer. He could not.

She closed her eyes. She did not sleep. She listened to his breathing. The pauses between breaths were long.

She counted. Twelve seconds. Fifteen. Eighteen.

She pressed harder. His heart was still beating.

"I will always be here," she said.

She did not know if she was telling him or herself.

In the morning, the pauses were longer. Twenty seconds. She timed them.

She called Dr. Elias. He did not answer. She left a message.

"I don't know how much time he has left."

She hung up. She sat beside Jae. She took his hand.

"Whenever you're ready," she said.

She did not know if she meant it.

Chapter 33: The Dream Shared

Hana dreamed.

She was in the field. The white grass stretched in all directions. The sky was white. There was no boundary.

The house stood in the distance. The green door was wide open.

She walked toward it. Her feet did not touch the grass. She floated.

Inside the house, there was a room. A wooden table. A single chair. A window with cracked glass.

Jae sat in the chair.

He looked different. His cheeks were full. His eyes were brown. He was wearing the shirt she had bought him for his birthday two years ago.

"Hana," he said.

"You're here."

"I'm always here."

"Come back with me."

He shook his head. "I can't."

"The door is open. You can go either way."

"I chose this way."

"Choose me."

He stood. He walked to her. He touched her face. His hand was warm. It was the hand of the man she married.

"I love you," he said. "But I can't stay."

"Why?"

"I don't belong there anymore."

"Then I'll stay here with you."

He shook his head. "You don't belong here."

"Neither do you."

"I do. I'm learning."

"Learning what?"

"To be what I'm becoming."

She woke. The bedroom was dark. Jae was beside her. His chest was still.

"No," she said.

She pressed her hand to his chest. A heartbeat. Slow, but present.

She lay back. She did not close her eyes.

Chapter 34: What Remains

Hana found the photographs.

They were in a box under the bed. Wedding photos. Vacation photos. Photos of Jae before.

She spread them on the bed. She looked at each one.

Jae on a beach. Jae in a suit. Jae laughing at a restaurant. Jae holding a cat. Jae asleep on a sofa.

The man in the photographs was not the man beside her.

That man had muscles. That man had a face full of life. That man had opinions and appetites and a loud laugh.

The man beside her was a skeleton with skin. His eyes were pale. His laugh was gone.

She looked at the photograph of their wedding. Jae in his gray suit. She in her white dress. They were holding hands. They were smiling.

She put the photographs back in the box. She closed the lid.

She looked at Jae. His eyes were open. He was looking at the ceiling.

"I found our wedding pictures."

A blink.

"You looked so happy."

A blink.

"Do you remember being happy?"

A pause. A slow blink.

"I'm not sure."

She put her face in her hands. She cried. The sound was ugly. She did not care.

Jae's hand moved. It found her arm. His fingers rested on her wrist.

She looked up. His eyes were on her.

"I remember you," he whispered.

His voice was a rasp. It had been days.

"I remember loving you."

She took his hand. She pressed it to her mouth.

"I remember," she said. "I remember everything."

Chapter 35: The Door Opens

"I see it clearly now," Jae said.

His voice was barely audible. Hana leaned close.

"The door is open. All the way. I can see the room."

"What's in the room?"

"A table. A chair. A window."

"Is it empty?"

"No. There's someone there."

"Who?"

"I don't know. They're waiting for me."

She held his hand. His grip was weak.

"Don't go yet."

"I have to."

"Five more minutes."

He smiled. A thin smile.

"Five more minutes."

She looked at the clock. She watched the seconds pass.

"They're gone," he said.

"No. There are still three minutes."

"Time works differently here."

"Everywhere is here. You're here."

"I'm in both places."

She pressed her forehead to his. His skin was cold.

"Tell me about the person," she said.

"Old. A woman. She's wearing a white dress."

"What does she want?"

"To show me something."

"What?"

"I don't know. I have to go see."

"Don't."

"I have to."

The seconds passed. The clock ticked.

"Promise me you'll come back."

He did not answer.

"Jae, promise me."

"I promise I'll try."

It was not enough. But it was all he could give.

Chapter 36: A Visitor

Dr. Elias came without being called.

He walked into the house. He did not knock. Hana found him standing in the living room.

"I had a feeling," he said.

"He's dying."

"I know."

She took him upstairs. He looked at Jae. He did not take out his stethoscope.

"Mr. Kim, can you hear me?"

A blink.

"Do you know where you are?"

A blink.

"Do you want to be here?"

A long pause. No blink.

"Jae, do you want to be here?"

A blink. Slow. But definite.

"You want to be here?"

Another blink.

Hana felt a sob rise in her throat. She swallowed it.

"He wants to stay," she said.

"For now."

Dr. Elias looked at the IV stand. The bag was empty.

"I'll set up a new bag."

"Don't bother."

"It will keep him comfortable."

"He's comfortable."

Dr. Elias nodded. He sat in the corner. He did not leave.

Hana sat beside Jae. She held his hand.

"The doctor is here," she said.

Jae blinked.

"He's worried about you."

A blink.

"So am I."

A blink. She could not read it.

"What do you need?" she asked.

A long pause. Then his lips moved. She leaned closer.

"Nothing," he whispered. "I need nothing."

Chapter 37: The Vigil

Hana did not leave the room.

She sat in the chair beside the bed. She held Jae's hand. She watched his chest rise and fall.

The pauses between breaths were thirty seconds now.

She counted each pause. She held her own breath until he breathed again.

Dr. Elias brought her tea. She did not drink it.

"Rest," he said.

"I can't."

"You need to take care of yourself."

"I am. I'm here. This is what I need."

He left the tea on the table. It grew cold.

The afternoon passed. The evening came. The room grew dark. Hana did not turn on the light.

Jae's breathing was a thread. Thin. Getting thinner.

She lay beside him. She put her arm across his chest. She pressed her ear to his heart.

"It's okay," she whispered. "You can go."

She did not know if she meant it.

His hand moved. His fingers touched her hair.

She looked up. His eyes were open. They were clear. They were the pale amber of the field.

"I love you," he whispered.

"I love you too."

"You'll be okay."

"I won't."

"You will. You're strong."

"Don't leave me."

He did not answer. His hand fell from her hair. His chest stopped moving.

"No."

She pressed her ear to his chest. Silence.

"Jae."

She waited. The silence continued.

"Jae."

His chest rose. A breath. Then another.

He was still there. But barely.

Chapter 38: The Last Word

The night was long.

Hana did not sleep. She held Jae's hand. She talked to him.

She talked about their first date. About the time he got lost driving to the beach. About the cat they had adopted that ran away.

"Do you remember the cat?"

A blink.

"He was gray. You named him Pebble. He used to sleep on your chest."

A blink.

"I found a photo of him yesterday. In the box under the bed."

A blink. Then his lips moved.

"Pebble," he whispered.

"Yes. You remember."

A breath. Slow.

"Everything."

His voice was a thread. She held it.

"Everything," she repeated.

He looked at her. His eyes were clear.

"The door is open," he said.

"I know."

"I can see inside now."

"What do you see?"

"Light."

"Just light?"

"And you."

"Me?"

"You're there. Waiting for me."

"I'm here. I'm right here."

"Both. You're in both places."

She did not understand. She did not need to.

"Don't be afraid," she said.

"I'm not."

"Good."

He closed his eyes. His breathing slowed.

"Jae?"

"I'm still here."

"Say my name."

"Hana."

"Say it again."

"Hana."

She held his hand. She listened to his breath.

"I'm here," she said.

But he was already gone.

Chapter 39: The Empty Room

The bed was empty.

Hana had not noticed when he left. She had fallen asleep. Her head on his chest. His hand in her hair.

She woke to silence. The kind of silence that comes after a sound stops.

The bed was empty. The sheets were flat.

She sat up. She looked around the room. His clothes were in the closet. His books were on the nightstand.

But Jae was not there.

She walked to the bathroom. Empty.

She walked downstairs. Empty.

She opened the front door. The street was quiet.

"Jae?"

No answer.

She walked back upstairs. She looked at the bed. The sheets were cold.

She touched the pillow. It still held the shape of his head.

She sat on the edge of the bed. She did not cry.

She waited. She did not know for what.

The clock ticked. The afternoon passed. The light changed.

Jae did not come back.

Chapter 40: The Search

Hana called the police.

"My husband is missing," she said.

"Missing?"

"He was here. In bed. Now he's gone."

"Did he leave on his own?"

"I don't know. I fell asleep. When I woke up, he was gone."

"How long have you been married?"

"Eight years."

"Is he ill?"

"He hasn't eaten in weeks. He's too weak to walk."

The officer paused. "We'll send someone."

The police came. They looked in the closets. They looked in the yard. They walked the neighborhood.

"He's not here," the officer said.

"I told you."

"Any idea where he might have gone?"

"The field."

"What field?"

"The white field. The one in his dreams."

The officer wrote something in his notebook. She knew what he was thinking. He was thinking she was crazy.

"You might want to check the hospitals," he said.

She did not answer.

After they left, she got in her car. She drove. She drove through the city. She drove through the suburbs. She drove into the countryside.

She did not know what she was looking for.

She drove until the road ended. She sat in the car. The sun was setting.

She thought about the house. The green door.

She started the engine. She drove home.

The house was empty. It would be empty forever.

Chapter 41: The Police

The officer came back the next day.

"Mrs. Kim, we've checked the hospitals. We've checked the bus station. No one matching your husband's description has been seen."

"He's not going to a hospital."

"Where would he go?"

"The field."

"Mrs. Kim, I need you to be clear with me."

"I am being clear."

"Is your husband suicidal?"

"No."

"Is he a danger to himself?"

"He's not eating. He hasn't eaten in weeks."

"Then why didn't you hospitalize him?"

"I tried. He refused."

The officer sighed. "We'll keep looking. But without any leads..."

"I know."

"Call us if you hear from him."

She nodded. She showed him out.

She stood in the living room. The house was still. She had never noticed how much noise Jae made. His footsteps. His breathing. The rustle of his clothes.

Now there was nothing.

She sat on the sofa. She stared at the wall.

She thought about the green door. She thought about the white field.

She thought about Jae's hand in hers. The last time she had felt it.

She got up. She put on her coat.

She was going to find the field.

Chapter 42: The House in the Field

Hana drove into the countryside.

She did not know where she was going. She followed instinct. She took roads she had never taken.

She drove for two hours. The city fell away. The suburbs fell away. She passed farms and forests and fields.

She saw a white house with a green door.

She stopped the car.

She sat behind the wheel. She looked at the house. It was small. Wooden. White paint peeling. Tin roof.

Just like Jae had described.

She got out. She walked to the door. The step creaked under her weight. The welcome mat was faded.

She knocked. No answer.

She tried the handle. It was unlocked.

She pushed the door open.

The room inside was empty. A wooden table. A single chair. A window with cracked glass.

Just like her dream.

She stepped inside. The floor was dusty. The air was still.

She touched the table. It was real. Solid. Wood.

She sat in the chair. She looked out the cracked window. The field beyond was green, not white. It was an ordinary field. Grass. Weeds. A fence line.

But she knew. This was the house.

She stayed in the chair. She waited.

The sun moved across the sky. She did not move.

"Jae," she whispered.

The silence answered.

Chapter 43: An Old Woman

A farmer's wife found Hana.

She was an old woman. Her face was lined. Her hands were rough.

"I saw your car," she said. "Are you lost?"

Hana shook her head. "I found what I was looking for."

The woman looked at the house. She nodded.

"Others have come here. Looking for someone."

"Others?"

"Women. Two of them. Years apart."

Hana stood. "What happened to the men?"

The woman shook her head. "They disappeared. Same story. Stopped eating. Stopped sleeping. Went looking for something."

"Did they find it?"

"I don't know. They were never seen again."

Hana felt cold. "Tell me about them."

The woman sat on the step. She pulled a cigarette from her pocket. She lit it.

"The first one was twenty years ago. A young man. He lived in the city with his wife. He stopped eating. She brought him to the countryside, thinking the fresh air would help. He wandered off. They found his clothes in this house."

"And the second?"

"Five years later. Another husband. Another wife. Same story."

"What did the wives do?"

"They grieved. They moved on. What else is there?"

Hana sat beside her. The woman offered the cigarette. Hana took it. She smoked. She had not smoked in years.

"What if he's still here?" Hana asked.

The woman looked at her. Her eyes were old. They had seen too much.

"He's not."

"How do you know?"

"Because I know what this place is. It's a door."

"A door to where?"

"To somewhere else. Somewhere people go when they don't belong here anymore."

Chapter 44: The Second Story

Hana drove to the nearest town.

She found the library. She asked for old newspapers.

The librarian showed her to the archives. Microfilm. Yellowed pages.

She searched for articles about missing men. She found two.

The first was dated 2004. A man named Park. He had stopped eating. His wife had searched for weeks. He was never found.

The second was dated 2011. A man named Choi. Same story. Same ending.

She copied the articles. She read them in her car.

They were the same as Jae. The same symptoms. The same disappearance. The same house with the green door.

She drove back to the house. She parked. She sat in the car.

The sky was gray. The house was dark.

She thought about the old woman's words. A door. To somewhere else.

She walked to the door. She opened it. She stood in the empty room.

"Jae," she said. "If you can hear me, I'm here. I found the house."

Silence.

"I don't know where you went. But I found the door."

Silence.

"I love you."

Silence.

She stood in the empty room. She waited.

The wind blew through the cracked window. It sounded like a voice.

She closed her eyes. She saw the white field. She saw the green door. It was open.

Inside, there was light.

She opened her eyes. The room was empty. She was alone.

But she felt him. Close. Like breath on her skin.

Chapter 45: Grief and Bread

Hana went home.

She showered. She changed her clothes. She went to the grocery store.

She bought flour. Yeast. Salt. Butter.

She went home. She cleared the kitchen counters.

She made bread.

She measured the flour. She mixed the yeast. She kneaded the dough. Her hands worked. Her mind went quiet.

The bread rose. She punched it down. She shaped it. She let it rise again.

She baked it. The kitchen filled with the smell of bread.

She took it out. She let it cool. She cut a slice.

She ate it. The bread was good. Warm. Real.

She ate another slice.

She sat at the table. The table where Jae used to sit. The table where he had stopped eating.

She ate.

"This is for you," she said.

She ate another slice.

"I'm not giving up."

She finished the slice. She wiped her hands.

"But I'm not starving with you."

She went to bed. She slept. She did not dream.

Chapter 46: The Letter

A letter arrived three days later.

It was in the mailbox. An envelope. No return address. Her name in Jae's handwriting.

She opened it with shaking hands.

The letter was short.

"Hana,

I am in the white field. The door opened. I walked through. I am not coming back.

Do not look for me. Do not wait for me. Live your life.

I love you. I will always love you.

Jae"

She read it three times. She folded it. She put it in her pocket.

She walked into the living room. She sat on the sofa.

The letter was real. She could feel it in her pocket.

She took it out. She read it again.

"Do not wait for me."

She put the letter on the table.

"I will," she said.

She did not know how long she would wait. But she knew she could not stop.

She went to the kitchen. She made tea. She sat at the table with the letter.

The words blurred. She blinked. The tears fell.

She did not wipe them away.

Chapter 47: The Green Door Found

Hana went back to the house.

She brought a flashlight. She brought water. She brought Jae's photograph.

She opened the green door. She stepped inside.

The room was empty. Dust on the floor. The chair was still there. The table.

She sat in the chair. She placed the photograph on the table.

"I'm here," she said.

She waited.

The light through the cracked window shifted. The room grew darker.

She turned on the flashlight. The beam cut through the dust.

She looked at the walls. They were bare. Plaster. Cracks.

She stood. She walked to the back of the room. There was a door she had not noticed. A door in the back wall. Painted white.

She opened it.

A hallway. Narrow. Dark.

She walked down it. The flashlight beam shook.

The hallway ended at another door. This one was green.

She touched the handle. It was cold.

She turned it. The door opened.

Beyond it was the white field.

She stood at the threshold. The grass was pale. The sky was pale. There was no sound.

She stepped forward. Her foot touched the grass. It did not bend.

She stood in the field. She was there. She had found it.

"Jae," she called.

The silence absorbed her voice.

She waited. Nothing.

She turned. The green door was behind her. It was the only thing in the field.

Chapter 48: Inside

Hana walked through the field.

The grass did not move. Her footsteps made no sound. The light was constant. No shadow.

She walked for what felt like hours. The door behind her grew small.

She saw a figure in the distance.

She walked toward it.

The figure grew larger. A man. Sitting in a chair.

She recognized the chair. It was the chair from the room.

She recognized the man.

"Jae."

He looked up. His face was thin. His eyes were brown.

"Hana."

She knelt in front of him. She touched his face. His skin was warm.

"You're here," she said.

"I'm here."

"You're alive."

"I'm here."

She wrapped her arms around him. His body was solid. He had weight. He was not the skeleton she had left behind.

"How is this possible?"

"The field," he said. "It gives back what it takes."

"Then come home."

"I can't."

"Why not?"

He touched her face. His fingers were warm.

"Because I am the field now. And the field is me."

She pulled away. She looked at him.

"I don't understand."

"I know."

"You're speaking. You're solid. You're alive."

"Here. Not there."

"Then stay here with me. I'll stay too."

"You can't. You have a body. A life. People who need you."

"I need you."

"You need to live."

She started to cry. He held her.

"I love you," he said. "But I can't go back."

She held him. She felt his heart beat.

"One more minute," she said.

"One more minute."

They sat in the field. The light did not change. The silence held them.

Chapter 49: The Field

Hana stayed in the field.

She did not know how long. Time did not pass. The light did not change.

Jae sat beside her. They did not speak. They did not need to.

She watched the horizon. There was no horizon. Just white blending into white.

"Do you see the house?" she asked.

"No."

"The green door?"

"It's gone."

"Then how do I go back?"

"You turn around."

She looked behind her. The green door was there. Small. Distant.

"It was not there before."

"It always was. You just couldn't see it."

She stood. He stood beside her.

"Will I see you again?" she asked.

"I don't know."

"Can I come back?"

"If you find the door."

She looked at him. He was the man she married. Full. Alive.

"It shouldn't be this hard," she said.

"It is hard. That's what makes it real."

She kissed him. His lips were warm.

"Goodbye, Jae."

"Goodbye, Hana."

She turned. She walked toward the door. She did not look back.

She reached the door. She opened it. She stepped through.

She was in the empty room. The photograph was on the table.

She closed the door behind her.

Chapter 50: The Return

Hana drove home.

The sun was setting. The sky was orange and red. It was beautiful.

She parked in the driveway. She sat in the car. She did not want to go inside.

But she went inside.

The house was quiet. She turned on the lights. She opened the windows. She let the air in.

She went to the kitchen. She opened the refrigerator. There was food. She had bought it. It was still fresh.

She took out vegetables. She took out rice. She took out eggs.

She cooked.

The kitchen filled with the smell of food. It was a good smell. It was a living smell.

She sat at the table. She had made too much. Enough for two.

She ate.

The food was good. She tasted it. She chewed. She swallowed.

She ate until she was full.

She washed the dishes. She dried them. She put them away.

She went to the bedroom. The bed was made. Jae's side was empty.

She lay down. She closed her eyes.

She thought about the field. The white grass. The silence.

She thought about Jae. Sitting in his chair. Waiting.

She opened her eyes. The ceiling was white.

"I'll find you again," she whispered.

She turned off the light.

In the dark, she felt something. A touch. A warmth.

She smiled.

She fell asleep.

The next morning, she made breakfast. She set the table. One plate. One cup.

She ate.

She went to work. She taught her class. The students listened. She was good at her job.

She came home. She cooked dinner. She ate alone.

She did not set a second plate.

But sometimes, late at night, she would wake and feel the cold touch of a hand she could not see.

And she would know.

He was still there.

In the field. In the house. In the space between her heart and the silence.

Waiting.
