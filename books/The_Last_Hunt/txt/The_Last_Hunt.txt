Chapter 1: The Visitor

The horse appeared at first light. Santiago saw it from his door. A bay gelding, lathered, carrying a man in a dust-caked coat. The rider reined in fifty yards out and waited.

Santiago stood in the doorway of his shack. He had been awake since dark. Old men did not sleep through the night. He watched the rider and knew.

The man swung down stiffly. His boots hit the hardpan and he stood there a moment, letting his weight settle. He was taller than Santiago remembered. Grayer. The years had pulled the flesh tight across his bones.

"Tracker," the man said.

Santiago said nothing.

The man walked forward. His spurs chimed. He stopped at the threshold and removed his hat. His hair was thin and white at the temples.

"You know who I am."

Santiago knew. He had known the moment he saw the horse. A man carries his past in his bones. This one carried a good deal of it.

"McAllister," Santiago said.

The Ranger nodded. He looked past Santiago into the dark of the shack. One room. Dirt floor. A fire pit. A bedroll in the corner.

"You still live like this."

"It is my home."

McAllister nodded again. He had something to say. Santiago could see it sitting in his throat, waiting.

"A girl is missing," McAllister said. "Three days now. I need you to find her."

The wind moved across the plain. Santiago listened to it. He had been listening to that wind for sixty-eight years.

"Why would I help you?"

McAllister did not answer. He did not have to. They both knew the answer was complicated.

Chapter 2: Old Bones

Santiago bent to retrieve his tobacco pouch from beside the fire pit. His knees complained. He ignored them.

He had been a young man once. Fast. Quiet. He could track a deer across bare rock. He could run all day without water. That man was long gone.

"What happened to the girl?" he asked.

"Taken," McAllister said. "From her bed. Her father found her window open at dawn."

"Who took her?"

"We don't know. Three men, maybe four. They rode west toward the Staked Plains."

Santiago packed his pipe. His fingers moved slowly. Arthritis in both hands now. He struck a match and drew until the tobacco caught.

"How old is the girl?"

"Fifteen."

Santiago blew smoke. He thought of his daughter. She would be forty now if she had lived. The fever took her in the second winter at the Reservation.

"The Rangers cannot find her?"

McAllister's jaw tightened. "The Rangers lost the trail yesterday. Too much wind. Too much hard ground."

"So you come to me."

"I come to you."

Santiago smoked. The sun was fully up now. It threw long shadows across the dirt floor. A fly buzzed at the window.

"I am old," Santiago said. "My eyes are not what they were."

"They are better than any man I have."

Santiago looked at McAllister. The Ranger's face was honest. It always had been. That was the terrible thing about him. McAllister had never lied about what he did. He had simply done it and lived with it.

"I will think on it," Santiago said.

"We don't have time for thinking."

"Then you should have come sooner."

Chapter 3: The Offer

McAllister sat on Santiago's only stool. The wood creaked under him. Santiago remained standing by the window.

"I can pay," McAllister said. "Thirty dollars. More if we find her alive."

Santiago did not answer. Money meant little to him. He had lived on nothing for so long that he had forgotten how to want things.

"Her father has nothing," McAllister went on. "He is a tenant farmer. His wife died two years ago. The girl is all he has left."

Santiago turned from the window. "Tell me about the girl."

"Her name is Maria Gutierrez. She helps her father with the stock. She can ride. She can shoot. She speaks English and Spanish."

"And the men who took her?"

"We found tracks. Three horses. One of them carried double. They headed west and then turned north."

"Into the Comanche country."

McAllister nodded. He understood what he was asking. The old hunting grounds. The land Santiago had been born on and driven from.

"There is a camp out there," McAllister said. "Men who trade in stolen things. Horses, cattle. Sometimes people."

"You knew this camp existed."

"I suspected."

"And you did nothing."

McAllister met his eyes. "I am one man. I cannot clean the whole territory."

Santiago laughed. It was a dry sound, like stones grinding. "You cleaned it once. You and your Rangers. You cleaned it very well."

The words hung between them. McAllister did not look away.

"That was then," he said. "This is now. A girl is out there. I cannot find her alone."

Santiago picked up his knife. He tested the edge with his thumb. It was sharp.

"I will go," he said. "Not for you. For the girl."

Chapter 4: The Debt

Santiago did not trust McAllister. He trusted what he could see. The ground. The sky. The shape of a track in the dust.

But there was a debt between them. Not the kind McAllister meant. The Ranger thought Santiago owed him for letting him live on the edge of the settlement. For not running him off the land.

That was not the debt.

The debt was older. It went back to the winter of 1874. Santiago's village had been burned. The women and children had been herded onto wagons. The men who resisted had been shot.

Santiago had been one of the men who resisted.

He had taken a bullet in the shoulder and fallen into a ravine. McAllister had found him there, bleeding into the mud. The Ranger could have finished him. It would have been easy.

Instead, McAllister had walked past. He had pretended not to see.

Santiago never understood why. He had spent forty years wondering. Sometimes he thought it was mercy. Sometimes he thought it was cowardice. Most days he thought it was nothing. Just a man tired of killing.

But the debt existed. Santiago had lived because McAllister had let him live. And now the Ranger was calling it in.

Not in words. McAllister would never speak of it. But it sat between them as they gathered supplies. As they saddled the horses. As they turned west into the rising sun.

Santiago touched the old scar on his shoulder. It ached in cold weather. It ached now.

Chapter 5: The Leaving

Santiago packed his saddlebag with care. A change of socks. A flint. His tobacco. A small bag of dried meat. His knife.

He did not own a gun. He had not carried one since the Reservation. The Comanche agent had taken their weapons. He had never replaced his.

"You will need a rifle," McAllister said.

"No."

"Three men. Maybe four. Armed."

"I have my knife."

McAllister studied him. Trying to understand. Santiago did not explain.

The truth was simpler than the Ranger would believe. Santiago was tired of guns. He had seen too many of them. Heard too many shots. He had buried too many people who died at the end of a barrel.

A knife was different. A knife was close work. Honest work. You looked a man in the eye when you used it.

McAllister handed him a canteen. Santiago took it. He tied it to his saddle.

"You will slow us down," McAllister said.

"I am slow because I am old. You are slow because you do not know where you are going."

McAllister almost smiled. Almost.

They mounted up. Santiago's horse was a dun mare. Not young. Not fast. But steady. She would last.

He did not look back at the shack. There was nothing there he wanted to remember.

They rode west. The sun was at their backs. The shadows of the horses stretched out long before them.

Chapter 6: The Photograph

They stopped at noon to rest the horses. McAllister pulled a biscuit from his saddlebag and offered half to Santiago. Santiago took it.

While they ate, McAllister reached into his coat and removed a small photograph. It was wrapped in oilcloth. He handed it over.

Santiago studied the image. A girl. Dark hair pulled back. Serious eyes. She was not smiling. The photographer had told her to be still and she had obeyed.

Her hands were in her lap. Strong hands. A farmer's hands.

"This is her," McAllister said.

Santiago traced the edge of the photograph with his thumb. The girl looked directly at the camera. She did not blink.

"What do you see?" McAllister asked.

Santiago did not answer at first. He looked at the girl's eyes. There was something in them. Not fear. Something harder.

"She is not afraid," Santiago said.

"She should be."

"She is afraid. But she does not show it. That is different."

McAllister took the photograph back. He wrapped it carefully in the oilcloth and returned it to his coat.

"Her father gave me that. It is all he has of her."

"We will bring her back."

"You sound sure."

Santiago stood. His knees cracked. He walked to his horse and lifted the canteen to his lips.

"I am not sure of anything. But the girl is alive. I can feel it."

"How?"

"The same way I know the wind will shift. The same way I know rain is coming. A tracker feels things. You have to."

McAllister said nothing. He finished his biscuit and stood.

They rode on.

Chapter 7: The Farm

The Gutierrez homestead appeared in the late afternoon. A small house of unpainted wood. A barn leaning eastward. A corral with three thin cows.

The father stood in the yard. He had been watching the horizon since his daughter vanished. His eyes were red from lack of sleep.

Santiago saw him and felt the shape of the man's grief. It was familiar. He had worn it himself.

McAllister introduced them. The father's name was Tomas Gutierrez. He shook Santiago's hand with both of his.

"Thank you for coming," he said. His voice was raw.

Santiago nodded.

"Show me her room."

The room was small. A bed with a thin mattress. A wooden chair. A crucifix on the wall. A window facing east.

Santiago knelt. His knee touched the floor. He examined the window frame.

"Was it locked?"

"Yes. He broke the latch."

Santiago found the broken wood. Fresh splinters. The window had been pried open from outside.

He looked at the floor. Boot prints. Men's boots. Size ten, maybe. Two different patterns.

He found the girl's footprints near the bed. Small. She had been standing when they took her. She had not gone willingly.

"Where was the father?"

"In the barn. He heard nothing."

Santiago nodded. He moved to the window and looked out. The ground was hard and dry. But there were marks.

"They dropped her on the ground outside," he said. "She struggled. You can see where her heels dug in."

McAllister looked. He saw only dirt.

"Dawn," Santiago said. "They took her just before dawn."

"How do you know?"

"Because the dew on the grass was still there. They walked through it and bent the blades. By noon the grass would have sprung back."

McAllister nodded. He was learning.

Chapter 8: The Room

Santiago stayed in the girl's room for a long time. He sat on the edge of the bed and looked at the walls.

There were drawings. Small sketches on scraps of paper, pinned to the wood. A horse. A bird. A woman's face.

The girl drew. That meant she saw things. She paid attention.

He picked up a piece of paper from the floor. A drawing of a flower. A bluebonnet. Done carefully, petal by petal.

He folded the paper and put it in his pocket.

The father appeared in the doorway. He watched Santiago with hollow eyes.

"She is a good girl," he said. "She does not cause trouble."

"I know."

"She takes care of me. Since her mother died. She cooks. She cleans. She reads to me at night."

"What does she read?"

"Anything. The Bible. Newspapers. A book about birds that her mother left her."

Santiago stood. He walked to the window and ran his finger along the broken latch.

"Does she have a boyfriend?"

"No. She is too young."

"Did she ever talk about leaving?"

"No. She loves this place. She would never leave."

Santiago nodded. He believed him.

"One more thing," Santiago said. "Do you know anyone in the west? Family? Friends?"

"No. We came from the east. San Antonio. We have no one out there."

Santiago looked at the drawing of the bluebonnet. The girl had signed it in the corner. Small letters. M.G.

"I will find her," Santiago said.

He said it because he meant it. He said it because the father needed to hear it. He said it because he had not been able to save his own daughter, and the weight of that failure was still heavy on his back.

Chapter 9: The Tracks

The trail led west. Santiago found the sign easily once he knew what to look for.

Three horses. One carried double. The tracks were clear in the sandy soil. The men had not tried to hide their passage. They were not worried about being followed.

"They are confident," Santiago said.

"They should be. It took the Rangers two days to find their trail."

"They are not confident in their skill. They are confident because no one has ever caught them."

McAllister considered this. "You think they have done this before."

"I think they have done many things before. They know how to move. They know how to take a girl without waking the house."

He pointed at the ground. "See how the tracks are spaced? They rode fast but not hard. They are conserving the horses."

"How long will that last?"

"Three, maybe four days. Then they will need to stop and rest. Or they will trade for fresh mounts."

They followed the trail across open country. The sun was hot. The wind was dry. Santiago's ankle ached from the long ride, but he did not complain.

McAllister rode beside him. The Ranger was quiet. Santiago preferred it that way.

At dusk, they reached a dry creek bed. The tracks crossed it and continued west.

"We make camp here," Santiago said.

"We could push another hour."

"The horses need water. So do we."

McAllister started to argue, then stopped. He dismounted and began unsaddling his horse.

Santiago sat on a rock and watched the fading light. The tracks would still be there in the morning. They always were.

Chapter 10: The First Camp

They built a small fire. McAllister cooked coffee in a tin pot. Santiago sat apart, his back against a rock.

The night was clear. Stars appeared one by one. Santiago counted them without meaning to. Old habit.

McAllister handed him a cup of coffee. Santiago took it.

"You do not talk much," McAllister said.

"There is nothing to say."

"There is always something to say."

Santiago sipped the coffee. It was bitter. He did not mind.

"I remember the last time we shared a fire," McAllister said.

Santiago looked at him.

"It was 1874. You were wounded. I found you in a ravine."

"I remember."

"I carried you to a camp. Left you with food and water."

"I remember."

McAllister poked the fire. Sparks rose into the dark.

"Why did you do it?" Santiago asked.

McAllister was quiet for a long time. When he spoke, his voice was low.

"I do not know. I have asked myself that question many times. I still do not have an answer."

"You could have killed me."

"Yes."

"You were supposed to."

"Yes."

Santiago finished his coffee. He set the cup aside.

"Maybe you were tired," he said.

"Maybe."

"That is what I tell myself. That you were tired of killing. That you had seen enough blood."

McAllister looked at him. In the firelight, his face was carved in shadow.

"I was tired," he said. "God help me, I was so tired."

They sat in silence. The fire crackled. A coyote called in the distance.

Santiago lay down on the hard ground. He closed his eyes. Sleep came slowly, as it always did.

Chapter 11: Dust and Silence

The second day was hotter. The sky was white with heat. The land stretched flat to every horizon.

Santiago rode with his eyes on the ground. He read the trail like other men read a page. The depth of the prints told him the weight of the riders. The spacing told him their speed. The edges told him how long ago they passed.

"They are a day and a half ahead," he said.

"Can we catch them?"

"If they stop. If they rest. If they make mistakes."

McAllister wiped his forehead. His shirt was dark with sweat.

"What if they do not stop?"

"Then we will follow until they do."

The trail crossed a stretch of hard caliche. Santiago dismounted and walked, scanning the surface. He found a scratch on a rock. A horseshoe nail had caught.

He stood and looked west. The land shimmered with heat.

"They are heading for the Caprock," he said.

"How do you know?"

"Because there is nothing else out there. No water. No towns. No reason to go that way unless you do not want to be found."

McAllister nodded. The Caprock was bad country. Broken mesas and deep canyons. Good for hiding. Good for dying.

"Your ankle," McAllister said.

"It is fine."

"It is not fine. I saw you limp when you dismounted."

Santiago did not answer. He mounted his horse and rode on.

McAllister followed. There was nothing else to do.

Chapter 12: The Confession

McAllister pulled his horse alongside Santiago's. The sun was directly overhead. No shadows anywhere.

"I have been tracking men for thirty years," McAllister said. "I thought I was good at it."

"You are good enough."

"But I cannot read sign. Not the way you do. I see dirt. You see a story."

Santiago said nothing.

"Who taught you?"

"My father. His father before him. It is not something you learn in a school. You learn it by walking. By falling. By going hungry when you read the sign wrong."

McAllister was quiet for a moment. "I never had a teacher like that."

"You had teachers. You chose not to learn from them."

"What do you mean?"

Santiago looked at him. "The Comanche knew this land for hundreds of years. You could have learned from us. Instead, you tried to erase us."

McAllister's face went still. "That was war."

"It was war for you. For us, it was the end of the world."

They rode in silence. The horses' hooves made soft sounds in the dust.

"I am sorry," McAllister said.

The words hung in the air. Santiago did not acknowledge them. He did not know if he believed them. He did not know if it mattered.

"I am sorry for what was done," McAllister said. "That is the truth."

"Keep your truth. Find the girl. That is all I ask."

McAllister nodded. They rode on.

Chapter 13: The Water Hole

They found the water hole in the late afternoon. A shallow depression in the rock, fed by a spring. Cottonwood trees grew around it, their leaves trembling in the heat.

The tracks led straight to it. The men had stopped here. Dismounted. Let the horses drink.

Santiago knelt by the edge. He examined the mud.

"They stayed an hour," he said. "Maybe longer. They ate here."

He pointed to a spot where the ground was disturbed. "One man sat here. He dropped something."

Santiago's fingers found it. A button. Brass. From a coat.

He held it up. "The girl's?"

McAllister took it. "I do not know."

"Keep it. It may matter later."

McAllister put it in his pocket.

Santiago walked the perimeter of the water hole. He found the girl's footprints near the water. Small. She had been allowed to drink.

"She is alive," he said. "They are keeping her alive."

McAllister exhaled. He had been holding his breath.

"That is good."

"It means they want something for her. Ransom. Or they plan to sell her."

McAllister's face hardened. "They will not sell her."

"We will make sure of it."

They filled their canteens and let the horses drink. The water was cool and clear. Santiago splashed some on his face.

The cottonwood leaves whispered above them. Santiago thought of his daughter. She had loved the sound of leaves.

He pushed the memory away. There was work to do.

Chapter 14: The Story of the Horse

They camped that night under a stand of mesquite. The moon was not yet up. The stars were thick and bright.

McAllister was restless. He could not sit still. He walked the perimeter of the camp twice before settling.

"You are worried," Santiago said.

"I do not like being in open country at night."

"There is nothing out here that wants to kill you."

"Except the men we are following."

"Then you should sleep. Tomorrow will be long."

McAllister sat down. He looked at Santiago. "You told me about your father teaching you to track. You never told me about your horse."

Santiago was quiet. The question surprised him.

"My horse was a paint," he said. "A good horse. Fast. Smart. I raised her from a foal."

"What happened to her?"

"A Ranger patrol took her in '74. They were clearing the Reservation. They took everything. My horse. My blankets. The last of my tobacco."

McAllister said nothing.

"One of the Rangers recognized her value. He kept her for himself. I watched him ride her away."

"Do you know what happened to the Ranger?"

"I know."

McAllister waited.

"I killed him," Santiago said. "Three years later. I found him at a trading post. He did not recognize me. I cut his throat."

The confession hung in the air. McAllister did not flinch.

"Did you get the horse back?"

"No. She was already dead. He had ridden her into the ground."

McAllister nodded slowly. He reached for his canteen and drank.

"Was I that Ranger?" he asked.

"No. You were a different kind of man."

"How do you know?"

"Because you are still alive."

Chapter 15: The Dead Man

They found the body at midday. A man sprawled face-down in the gravel. Buzzards circled overhead.

McAllister dismounted first. He approached with his hand on his revolver. The man did not move.

"He is dead," Santiago said from his horse.

McAllister rolled the body over. The man's eyes were open. His throat had been cut.

"He has been dead a day," Santiago said. "Maybe less."

McAllister searched the body. A knife. A few coins. A folded map.

"He is one of them," McAllister said. "The men who took the girl."

"How do you know?"

"The boots. They match the prints at the farm."

Santiago dismounted. He examined the ground around the body.

"They killed him here. He did not expect it. There is no sign of a struggle."

"Falling out among thieves."

"Or he wanted to turn back. They did not trust him."

McAllister stood. He looked at the body. "We should bury him."

"No."

"Why not?"

"Because the animals need to eat. And the men we are following need to know they are being followed."

McAllister stared at him.

"Leave him," Santiago said. "It is a message. They will see the buzzards and know someone is coming."

McAllister hesitated. Then he nodded. He remounted his horse.

They rode past the body without looking back. The buzzards dropped down as soon as they were gone.

Chapter 16: The Split

The trail divided at a dry wash. Two sets of tracks went north. One set continued west.

Santiago dismounted and examined both paths.

"They split up," McAllister said.

"Yes."

"Which way do we go?"

Santiago did not answer immediately. He walked the north trail first. Then the west trail. He came back and crouched on his heels.

"The girl went west."

"How do you know?"

"The west tracks are deeper. One horse is carrying double. The north tracks are lighter. Two riders, no extra weight."

McAllister looked from one trail to the other. "Why would they split?"

"They are being careful. If we follow the wrong trail, we lose time. If we split up, they can pick us off."

McAllister considered. "So we follow the girl."

"Yes."

"And the other two?"

Santiago looked north. The tracks disappeared into the heat haze.

"They will come back. Men like this do not abandon their business. They will circle around or meet ahead."

"We could be riding into a trap."

"We are already riding into a trap. The only question is whether we see it before it closes."

McAllister nodded. He was learning to trust Santiago's judgment.

They took the west trail. The sun was brutal. The land was empty. Santiago's ankle throbbed with every step of his horse.

He did not slow down.

Chapter 17: The Storm

The dust storm hit without warning. One moment the air was still. The next, the horizon turned brown and the wind came screaming.

McAllister yelled something. The wind tore his words away.

Santiago dismounted. He grabbed his horse's reins and led her toward a cluster of rocks. The dust bit his face. He could not see more than a few feet.

They reached the rocks. Santiago found a hollow where the wind was less fierce. He crouched down and pulled his bandana over his mouth.

McAllister joined him. They huddled against the stone while the world turned to dust.

"Will it last long?" McAllister shouted.

Santiago shook his head. He did not know. Storms like this could last an hour or a day. The wind did not follow rules.

They waited. The horses stood with their heads down, eyes closed. The dust piled up around their hooves.

After two hours, the wind dropped. The air cleared slowly. Everything was covered in a fine brown grit.

Santiago stood. He brushed the dust from his clothes.

"We lost time," he said.

"How much?"

"Half a day. Maybe more."

McAllister's jaw tightened. "The trail?"

"Gone. The wind covered everything."

"Can you pick it up again?"

Santiago looked at the blank landscape. The tracks were gone. Erased. He would have to find them again.

"Yes," he said. "I can."

He did not know if he was telling the truth.

Chapter 18: The Scar

They made camp early. The storm had tired the horses and the men. Santiago's ankle was bad. He did not say anything.

He went to wash at a small stream. He removed his shirt and knelt. The water was cold.

McAllister came up behind him. He stopped when he saw Santiago's back.

The scars were old. Thick and white. They crossed his shoulders like a map of rivers. Some were from the lash. Some from the blade.

"Who did that?" McAllister asked.

Santiago did not turn around. "The agent. At the Reservation. He believed in discipline."

"Jesus."

"He was not a bad man. He was a weak man. Weak men are worse."

McAllister stood there. He did not know what to say.

"It happened in the first year," Santiago said. "I tried to leave. They caught me. The agent had me whipped. Thirty-nine lashes."

"Did you try to leave again?"

"No. I learned."

McAllister sat down on the bank. He watched the water flow past.

"I did not know," he said.

"There is a great deal you do not know."

Santiago finished washing. He put his shirt back on. The wet cloth clung to his scars.

"I am sorry," McAllister said. Again.

Santiago looked at him. The Ranger's face was full of something that might have been shame.

"Your sorry does not change the scars," Santiago said. "But it is something."

McAllister nodded. He looked away.

They ate in silence. The fire burned low. The stars came out.

Santiago lay down and closed his eyes. The scars on his back ached. They always did when the weather changed.

Chapter 19: The Hunters

Santiago saw the riders first. A line of specks on the southern horizon.

He raised his hand. McAllister reined in.

"How many?"

"Six. Maybe seven."

"Can you tell who?"

Santiago squinted. The heat shimmer made it hard.

"Comancheros," he said. "They are driving horses."

McAllister's hand went to his rifle.

"No," Santiago said. "They have not seen us. We will let them pass."

They moved their horses behind a rise. They waited. The riders grew larger. Santiago counted them. Seven men. They led a string of horses.

The Comancheros passed a quarter mile away. They did not look toward the rise.

One of them sang as he rode. A Spanish song. Santiago caught the words. It was about a woman who left.

When they were gone, Santiago and McAllister came out from behind the rise.

"They are heading east," McAllister said. "Toward the settlements."

"They will sell the horses. Buy whiskey. Ride back out."

"You know them?"

"I know their kind. They trade in anything. Horses. Whiskey. Women."

"We should have stopped them."

Santiago shook his head. "We are two men. They are seven. And the girl is west, not south."

McAllister looked after the Comancheros. His hand was still on his rifle.

"Another time," he said.

"Another time."

They rode on. The sun was dropping. The shadows lengthened.

Santiago thought about the song. About the woman who left. He wondered if the Comanchero had lost someone, or if he was just singing.

Chapter 20: The Night Watch

Santiago took the first watch. He sat with his back to a rock and faced the darkness.

The night was quiet. An owl called. A cricket answered. The wind moved through the grass like a living thing.

He thought about his wife. Her name had been Winona. She had died in the third year at the Reservation. The fever took her. The same fever that took his daughter.

He had buried them both on a hillside facing east. So their spirits could see the sunrise.

He touched the pouch at his neck. He carried a lock of Winona's hair in it. He had carried it for forty years.

McAllister slept a few yards away. The Ranger's breathing was slow and even. He slept the sleep of a man who had learned to sleep anywhere.

Santiago watched the stars wheel overhead. He thought about the girl. Maria. He pictured her face from the photograph. Strong hands. Serious eyes.

She was out there somewhere. Tied to a horse or locked in a room. She was probably awake. She was probably watching the same stars.

He hoped she was not afraid. He hoped she was angry.

Anger kept you alive. He knew that better than most.

He stayed awake until the moon set. Then he woke McAllister for the second watch.

"The middle of the night is the hardest," he said.

McAllister sat up. "I know."

"Keep your eyes on the dark. The dark moves."

"I will."

Santiago lay down. He closed his eyes. He dreamed of the girl's face, but in the dream she was older. She looked like Winona.

Chapter 21: The Canyon

The trail led into a canyon. Red rock walls rose on either side. The floor was sandy and soft.

Santiago stopped at the entrance. He studied the walls.

"This is a good place for an ambush," he said.

"You think they are waiting?"

"I think they are smart enough to consider it."

McAllister drew his rifle. He held it across his saddle.

They moved forward slowly. The canyon wound deeper into the rock. The walls grew higher. The light grew dimmer.

Santiago watched for sign. The tracks were clear. The girl had passed this way. He could see where her horse had stumbled on a loose rock.

"Here," he said.

He dismounted. There was a scrap of cloth tied to a bush. A piece of fabric, torn from a sleeve.

McAllister looked at it. "From her dress?"

"No. From a shirt. A man's shirt."

"One of the kidnappers?"

Santiago examined it. The cloth was fresh. It had been torn recently.

"Might be a marker," he said.

"Who would leave a marker?"

"A man who wants to be found."

McAllister frowned. "Why would one of them want to be found?"

"Maybe he does not anymore. Maybe he wants out."

They moved on. The canyon narrowed. The walls closed in.

Santiago felt the weight of the rock around them. He had been in places like this before. Places where the walls remembered the old spirits.

He hoped the spirits were watching.

Chapter 22: The Girl's Voice

Maria Gutierrez counted the days by the position of the sun. She had been tied to a horse for three days. Then a wagon for two. Now she was in a room.

The room was made of stone. A single door. A window too small to crawl through. A pile of straw in the corner.

The men who took her spoke Spanish. She understood them. She did not let them know.

They argued about what to do with her. One wanted to take her to the border. Another wanted to send a message to her father. A third was quiet. He watched her.

She watched him back.

She had learned to be still. To make herself small. To look at the floor.

But she did not look at the floor. She looked at everything else. The door. The window. The way the light fell.

She found the loose stone on the second day. It was in the wall by the window. She worked it free with her fingers.

It was not big enough to be a weapon. But it could make a sound. If she needed to make a sound.

She kept the stone in her pocket.

The quiet man brought her food. Bread and water. He never spoke. He put the food down and left.

Maria watched his hands. He was right-handed. He carried a knife on his left hip. A bad draw.

She filed the information away. You never knew what would matter.

At night, she listened to the men's voices. She learned their names. Rico. Pablito. The quiet one was called El Lobo.

She learned they had done this before.

She learned they did not plan to let her go.

She held the stone in her pocket and waited.

Chapter 23: The Argument

"We need to go faster," McAllister said.

The canyon had opened into a valley. They were making good time. It was not good enough for McAllister.

"The horses cannot go faster," Santiago said.

"Then we leave the horses."

"And walk? Into unknown country?"

"If we ride into that camp in broad daylight, they will see us coming."

"So we wait for dark."

"We are losing time. Every hour is another hour she is with them."

Santiago stopped his horse. He turned to face McAllister.

"You want to go in angry. That is how men die."

"I am not angry. I am focused."

"You are angry. I can see it in the way you sit your horse."

McAllister's jaw tightened. "And what if I am? She is fifteen years old. She has been gone for five days."

"Anger will not help her. Patience will."

"Easy for you to say. You do not have a daughter."

Santiago went still. The words hit like a blow.

McAllister saw it. He realized what he had said. His face changed.

"Santiago—"

"I had a daughter. She died. Do not tell me what I know."

He turned his horse and rode on.

McAllister followed. He did not apologize. He did not try to explain.

They rode in silence for the rest of the afternoon.

Chapter 24: The Cave

They found the cave in the canyon wall. The entrance was hidden by a fallen slab of rock. Santiago almost missed it.

He stopped when he saw the markings on the wall. Old markings. Comanche.

He dismounted. He walked to the wall and touched the symbols with his fingers.

"What is it?" McAllister asked.

"My people."

The markings were faded. Weather had worn them smooth. But Santiago could still read them. A hunting party had passed this way. They had killed a buffalo. They had given thanks.

"This was a sacred place," Santiago said. "Men came here to fast. To pray. To seek visions."

He ducked through the entrance. The cave was small. The floor was covered in dry dust. There were more markings on the walls. Handprints. Animal shapes.

Santiago knelt. He placed his palm against a handprint on the wall. His hand was larger. But the shape was the same.

He did not know whose hand it was. His grandfather's. His great-grandfather's. Someone who had lived and died before the white men came.

He stayed there for a long time. McAllister waited outside.

When Santiago came out, his eyes were wet.

"Whoever made that handprint," he said. "They are watching us now."

McAllister did not answer.

They made camp near the cave. Santiago did not sleep well. He kept thinking about the handprint. The way it fit against his own hand.

Chapter 25: The Dream

Santiago dreamed of the burning.

In the dream, he was young again. He was running. Smoke filled his lungs. Women were screaming. Children were crying.

The soldiers were everywhere. They moved through the village with torches. The grass roofs caught fire one by one.

Santiago ran toward his family's lodge. His mother was inside. His wife. His daughter.

He tried to reach them. His legs would not move fast enough.

He woke with McAllister's hand on his shoulder.

"You were calling out," McAllister said.

Santiago sat up. His heart was pounding. His shirt was wet with sweat.

"The dream," he said.

"I figured."

McAllister handed him a canteen. Santiago drank.

"You want to talk about it?"

"No."

McAllister sat down. The fire had burned low. The embers glowed.

"I have dreams too," McAllister said. "Different ones. Same kind."

Santiago looked at him.

"The war," McAllister said. "I see their faces. The men I killed."

"How many?"

"I stopped counting."

Santiago handed the canteen back. "I never stopped counting. I remember every one."

They sat in the dark. The fire popped. A coal settled.

"Does it get easier?" McAllister asked.

"No."

McAllister nodded. He had known the answer. He had just wanted to hear it.

They sat together until the sky began to lighten. Neither of them slept again.

Chapter 26: The Rope

They found the rope at the edge of a dry wash. A length of hemp, frayed at both ends. Dark stains on the fibers.

Santiago picked it up. He held it to the light.

"Blood," he said.

McAllister's face went pale. "Is it hers?"

"I cannot tell. But the rope was used to tie someone."

He examined the ground around it. Drag marks. A scuffle. The sand was disturbed.

"She tried to run," Santiago said. "They caught her. Tied her hands."

McAllister took the rope. He wrapped it around his hand. His knuckles were white.

"We are close," Santiago said.

"How close?"

"Half a day. Maybe less."

"Then we ride."

They rode. The trail was fresh now. Easy to read. The men were not being careful anymore.

Santiago watched the ground and felt a cold certainty settle into his chest. The kidnappers knew they were being followed. They were not running. They were preparing.

He touched his knife. It was still sharp.

He hoped he would not need it. He knew he would.

Chapter 27: The Trade Post

The trading post was a low building of adobe and wood. A sign hung over the door. Cruz & Son. General Merchandise.

Santiago dismounted. His ankle sent a spike of pain up his leg. He did not show it.

An old man sat on the porch. His face was brown and creased. He watched them approach without interest.

"Looking for something?" he asked.

"Information," McAllister said.

"Information costs."

"I can pay."

The old man studied them. His eyes moved from McAllister's badge to Santiago's face. He lingered on Santiago.

"Comanche," he said.

"Once," Santiago said.

The old man nodded. "I knew a Comanche once. He was a good man. Drank too much."

"He is dead now?"

"Everyone is dead now."

McAllister stepped forward. "Three men passed this way. With a girl."

The old man did not answer immediately. He picked at his teeth with a splinter of wood.

"They passed four days ago. Bought supplies. Flour. Coffee. Cartridges."

"Where were they headed?"

"West. Toward the mesa."

"Is there a camp out there?"

"There is always a camp out there."

McAllister reached into his pocket. He placed a coin on the porch. The old man looked at it but did not pick it up.

"The girl," the old man said. "She looked at me when they rode through. She did not call out. She just looked."

Santiago listened.

"Her eyes were dry," the old man said. "She was not crying. She was watching."

Santiago nodded. He had seen the same thing in the photograph.

"Thank you," he said.

The old man picked up the coin. He bit it.

"Watch your back," he said. "The big one is fast."

Chapter 28: The Bottle

They made camp near a stand of juniper. McAllister pulled a bottle from his saddlebag. Whiskey. He uncorked it and drank.

Santiago watched him.

"You carry whiskey?"

"For nights like this."

McAllister drank again. He offered the bottle to Santiago. Santiago took it. The whiskey burned going down.

"My first kill was a boy," McAllister said.

Santiago said nothing.

"He was fifteen. Maybe sixteen. He had a lance. I had a rifle. It was not a fair fight."

"War is not fair."

"Does not matter. I still see his face."

McAllister took the bottle back. He stared into the fire.

"I enlisted because I had nothing else. My father was dead. My mother had remarried. There was no land for me."

"So you became a Ranger."

"So I became a killer. The badge was just an excuse."

Santiago looked at him. The firelight played on the Ranger's face. He looked old. He looked tired.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because I want you to understand. I am not a good man. I never was. But I am trying to do one good thing before I die."

"By finding the girl."

"By bringing her home. By proving that the badge means something."

Santiago took the bottle. He drank again.

"The badge means what you make it mean," he said. "It has never saved anyone. Only people save people."

McAllister nodded. He took the bottle and corked it.

They sat in silence. The fire crackled. The stars turned overhead.

Chapter 29: The Shortcut

Santiago studied the map by firelight. The paper was worn and creased. The ink had faded in places.

"There is a way through the badlands," he said. "It cuts a day off the journey."

McAllister leaned over to look. "I have heard of that route. It is dangerous."

"Everything is dangerous."

"Men have died trying to cross those canyons."

"Men die in their beds too."

McAllister sat back. "Can you find the way?"

"I can find it."

"And the horses?"

"Mostly the horses."

McAllister considered. He looked at the map. He looked at the darkness beyond the fire.

"How much time would it save?"

"One day. Maybe two."

"Then we take it."

Santiago folded the map. He put it in his pocket.

"The badlands have no water. We need to fill every canteen before we enter."

"We will fill them at dawn."

Santiago nodded. He lay down and closed his eyes.

He did not sleep. He lay awake, listening to the wind. He thought about the canyon of the old markings. He thought about the handprint on the wall.

He was going into the badlands. The spirits would guide him. Or they would not.

Either way, he would find the girl.

Chapter 30: The Fall

The badlands were worse than Santiago remembered. The ground was broken and unstable. The heat was a physical weight.

They had been riding for four hours. The canteens were half empty.

Santiago's horse stepped in a hole. The ground gave way. The mare went down.

Santiago had a moment to react. He kicked free of the stirrups and threw himself sideways. He hit the ground hard.

The horse scrambled up. She stood trembling, her leg bloody.

Santiago tried to stand. Pain exploded in his ankle. He fell back.

McAllister was there. He knelt beside Santiago.

"How bad?"

Santiago did not answer. He pulled up his pant leg. The ankle was swelling. The skin was tight and red.

"It is not broken," he said. "But it is bad."

"Can you ride?"

Santiago looked at the horse. Her leg was gashed. The blood ran into the dust.

"Your horse?"

"I will walk."

"You cannot walk on that ankle."

"I can."

McAllister helped him stand. Santiago put weight on the ankle. Fire shot up his leg.

He did not make a sound.

He limped to his horse. The mare was favoring her right leg. They were both hurt.

Santiago mounted. The pain was a white-hot thing.

"We keep going," he said.

McAllister looked at him. He started to speak. He stopped.

They rode on. Slow. The sun was merciless.

Chapter 31: The Splint

They stopped under an overhang of rock. The only shade for miles.

Santiago sat down and pulled off his boot. The ankle was swollen to twice its size.

"We need to splint it," McAllister said.

"I know."

McAllister found a straight branch. He broke it to length. He tore a strip from his shirt.

"Give me your foot."

Santiago hesitated. Then he extended his leg.

McAllister worked quickly. He bound the branch to Santiago's ankle. Tight enough to hold. Not tight enough to cut off blood.

"You have done this before," Santiago said.

"I have done a lot of things."

The splint helped. The pain dulled to a steady throb.

"Your mare cannot carry you much farther," McAllister said.

"I know that too."

"We can double up on my horse. Let her rest."

Santiago shook his head. "Riding double is slow. And we are almost there."

"Almost where?"

Santiago pointed west. "The mesa. The camp is at the base of it."

McAllister looked. The mesa was a dark shape on the horizon. Miles away. But visible.

"We can make it by nightfall," Santiago said.

"With your ankle?"

"With my ankle."

McAllister helped him stand. Santiago mounted his mare. The horse flinched but held.

They rode. The mesa grew larger.

Chapter 32: The Slow Mile

The last mile was the longest. The sun was dropping. The shadows were long.

Santiago's ankle was a drumbeat of pain. He had stopped feeling individual pulses. It was just a wall of hurt.

He did not stop.

McAllister rode beside him. The Ranger had stopped talking. He was watching the mesa. Watching for movement.

"Will they have guards?" McAllister asked.

"Probably one. Maybe two."

"How do we get past them?"

"We do not get past them. We go through them."

McAllister looked at him. "You said patience."

"I said patience when we were far away. We are not far away anymore."

The mesa loomed above them. A massive block of sandstone, carved by wind and time. The camp would be at its base. Hidden in the shadow.

Santiago stopped his horse. He listened.

"Something is wrong."

"What?"

"It is too quiet."

McAllister listened. He heard nothing. No voices. No horses. No fire.

"Maybe they moved on."

"No. The tracks lead here. They are here."

They sat on their horses. The mesa was silent.

Santiago felt the old hunter's instinct stir. Something was waiting for them.

"Be ready," he said.

He touched his knife.

They moved forward.

Chapter 33: The Second Camp

Maria heard them before she saw them. The crunch of boots on gravel. The low murmur of voices.

El Lobo was at the door. He opened it and looked at her.

"Stay quiet," he said.

She stayed quiet.

He closed the door. She heard him walk away.

The stone was in her pocket. She had been holding it for two days now. It was smooth from her touch.

She went to the window. The small one. Too small to crawl through. But wide enough to see.

She saw the mesa. The red rock. The sky turning orange with sunset.

She saw two figures on horseback. Small against the vastness.

Her heart beat faster.

She did not know if they were rescuers or more captors. She did not know if they were coming for her or for something else.

But they were coming.

She moved away from the window. She pressed herself against the wall. She held the stone.

If they were coming to save her, she would be ready.

If they were coming for other reasons, she would be ready for that too.

She was not a child. She was not a victim.

She was Maria Gutierrez, and she was still alive.

Chapter 34: The Signal

Santiago found the cloth at the edge of the camp. A scrap of blue fabric, tied to a creosote bush.

He pulled it loose. He held it up.

"It is from her dress," he said.

"You are sure?"

"The dye is the same as in the photograph. And it is fresh."

He examined the bush. The fabric had been tied deliberately. Not torn by accident.

"She left this for us. She is marking the way."

McAllister took the cloth. He folded it carefully and put it in his pocket.

"She is smart," he said.

"Yes."

They crept closer to the camp. It was a collection of low buildings. A stone hut. A lean-to. A corral with three horses.

Smoke rose from a fire pit. A man sat beside it. He was cleaning a rifle.

"That is one," Santiago whispered.

"There are two more inside."

"I see them."

The man at the fire was Rico. The big one. The one the trader had warned about.

Santiago studied the layout. The girl was in the stone hut. The door faced the fire. Any approach would be seen.

"We wait for dark," Santiago said.

"Agreed."

They pulled back. They found a place behind a rock outcropping. They settled in to wait.

Santiago checked his knife. McAllister checked his rifle.

The sun dropped. The shadows deepened.

Night was coming.

Chapter 35: The Three

As full dark settled, the men in the camp grew visible by firelight. Santiago counted three. He had been right.

The big one, Rico, sat by the fire. He was cleaning his rifle with slow, deliberate movements.

The second man, Pablito, was shorter. He walked between the fire and the corral, restless.

The third, El Lobo, was harder to see. He sat in the shadows of the lean-to. Still. Watching.

"El Lobo is the dangerous one," Santiago whispered.

"Why?"

"Because he does not move. He does not speak. He watches."

McAllister studied the man. "He is the one who stays in the shadows."

"Yes."

"The one who does the thinking."

"No. The one who does the killing."

McAllister's hand tightened on his rifle.

"We take them in order," Santiago said. "The big one first. Then the short one. El Lobo last."

"How do we take the big one?"

"I will take him. You cover me with the rifle."

"Santiago—"

"I have done this before."

McAllister looked at him. The firelight reflected in his eyes.

"I know you have. That is what worries me."

Santiago did not answer. He pulled his knife from its sheath. The blade caught the firelight.

"Wait for my signal," he said.

He slipped into the darkness.

Chapter 36: The Plan

Santiago moved like an old man because he was an old man. But he moved like an old man who had spent his life learning how not to be heard.

His ankle screamed. He ignored it.

He circled the camp clockwise. Staying low. Staying in the shadows.

He stopped at the edge of the firelight. Rico was still cleaning his rifle. He had taken it apart. The pieces were spread on a cloth.

He could not fire it. Not quickly.

Good.

Santiago waited. He watched. He timed the man's movements.

Rico picked up a rag. He ran it through the barrel. He set the rag down. He reached for the oil.

Santiago moved.

He crossed the firelight in three steps. His knife was low. His body was low.

Rico looked up. His eyes went wide.

Santiago's knife caught him under the chin. A hard thrust. Up and in.

Rico made a sound. A small one. His hands came up. They found nothing.

Santiago lowered him to the ground. He pulled the knife free.

One down.

Pablito was at the corral. He had not heard.

McAllister was watching from the rocks. His rifle was trained on the lean-to. El Lobo had not moved.

Santiago wiped his knife on Rico's shirt. He moved toward the corral.

Chapter 37: The Old Ways

Santiago remembered the lessons. How to walk without sound. How to breathe through your mouth so your breath did not whistle. How to become part of the night.

His father had taught him. A lifetime ago.

"Do not be a man," his father had said. "Be a shadow. Be the wind. The wind does not announce itself."

Santiago became the wind.

He reached the corral. Pablito was leaning on the fence, smoking a cigarette. The smoke curled upward. He was watching the horses.

Santiago came up behind him.

Pablito sensed something. He started to turn.

Santiago's arm went around his neck. The knife touched his throat.

"Quiet," Santiago said.

Pablito went rigid.

"You are going to walk toward the fire. Slowly. Your hands where I can see them."

Pablito nodded.

They walked. Santiago kept the knife against his back.

They reached the fire. Pablito saw Rico's body. His breath caught.

"Down," Santiago said.

Pablito knelt. Santiago bound his hands with a strip of rawhide.

"Where is El Lobo?"

Pablito pointed toward the lean-to. "Inside. He is always inside."

Santiago looked. The lean-to was dark. He could not see the man anymore.

"He knows," Santiago said.

He turned to find McAllister. The Ranger was already moving.

Chapter 38: The Girl

Maria heard the sounds. A scuffle. A body hitting the ground. Then silence.

She moved to the door. She pressed her ear against the wood.

Nothing.

She tested the door. Locked from the outside.

She looked around the room. The walls were stone. The floor was dirt. The window was too small.

But the stone in her pocket was hard. And the door was old.

She raised the stone. She brought it down on the lock. The sound was loud in the small room.

She hit it again. The wood splintered.

She hit it a third time. The lock broke.

She pushed the door open.

The camp was lit by the fire. She saw a body by the fire. A man kneeling with his hands bound.

She saw another figure. An old man with a knife. He moved like a ghost.

She saw a third man. The Ranger. He was moving toward the lean-to.

And she saw El Lobo.

He was behind the lean-to. He had circled around. He was raising his rifle.

Maria did not think. She ran.

"Behind you!" she screamed.

The old man turned. El Lobo's rifle came up.

Maria picked up a rock from the ground. She threw it. It hit El Lobo in the shoulder.

He staggered. His shot went wild.

The Ranger's rifle cracked. El Lobo went down.

The camp was suddenly very quiet.

Maria stood in the firelight, breathing hard.

The old man looked at her. His knife was still in his hand.

"You are Maria," he said.

"Yes."

"Good."

And he sat down, hard, on the ground.

Chapter 39: The Shot

McAllister's shot had taken El Lobo in the chest. The man lay on his back, eyes open. He was not breathing.

McAllister walked over and stood above him. He looked at the body for a long time.

Then he holstered his rifle.

He walked to Maria. She was standing by the fire. Her hands were shaking. She could not make them stop.

"You are safe now," McAllister said.

She looked at him. Her eyes were dry.

"I know."

McAllister almost smiled.

Santiago was sitting on the ground. His ankle was screaming. His hands were bloody.

Maria walked over to him. She sat down beside him.

"Thank you," she said.

Santiago looked at her. In the firelight, she looked older than fifteen.

"You threw the rock," he said.

"Yes."

"That was smart."

"I had to do something."

Santiago nodded. He looked at his hands. The blood was drying.

"Your hands are shaking," he said.

"I know."

"That is normal."

She looked at her hands. She looked at him.

"Are your hands shaking?" she asked.

Santiago looked at his hands. They were steady.

"No," he said. "I have done this too many times."

She looked at him. She did not look away.

"I am glad you came," she said.

Santiago nodded. He did not know what to say.

They sat together by the fire. The night was quiet. The stars were out.

Chapter 40: The Knife

Santiago cleaned his knife in the firelight. He used a cloth and water from a canteen. He worked slowly, methodically.

McAllister watched him.

"You did not have to kill him," McAllister said.

"Rico? Yes, I did."

"He was unarmed."

"His rifle was in pieces. His pistol was on his belt."

"That is what I mean. He was not a threat."

"He was a threat to the girl. He was a threat to every girl he had ever taken. He would do it again."

McAllister said nothing.

"I have spent my life watching men like that," Santiago said. "They do not change. They do not stop. The only thing that stops them is death."

"We could have taken him back. Put him on trial."

"And he would have escaped. Or bought his way out. Or served five years and gone back to taking girls."

McAllister looked at the body. Rico lay on his back. His eyes were closed.

"Maybe you are right," McAllister said.

"I am right."

Santiago finished cleaning his knife. He tested the edge. It was sharp.

"The other one," he said. "Pablito. He will talk. He will tell you who sent them. Who they were selling her to."

"Will he talk to me?"

"No. He will talk to me."

McAllister looked at him. "What will you do?"

"I will speak with him. In Spanish. He will tell me what I need to know."

"And then?"

Santiago sheathed his knife. "And then he is yours. To do with what you will."

McAllister nodded.

Santiago stood. His ankle screamed. He did not show it.

He walked to where Pablito sat, hands bound.

They had work to do.

Chapter 41: The Third Man

Santiago crouched in front of Pablito. The man was young. Early twenties. His eyes were wet.

"Tell me who sent you," Santiago said in Spanish.

Pablito looked at him. "I do not know."

"Yes, you do."

"He was just a man. He paid us to take the girl."

"Who paid you?"

"I do not know his name. He met us in a cantina. He gave us money and a description."

"Describe him."

Pablito swallowed. "Tall. Thin. A scar on his chin. He wore a black hat."

Santiago looked at McAllister. "Does that mean anything to you?"

McAllister frowned. "There is a man who owns land near the border. He is known for... acquisitions."

"This man sent them."

"Probably."

Santiago turned back to Pablito. "What were you supposed to do with the girl?"

"Take her to the border. A man would meet us there. He would pay the rest."

"Ransom?"

"No. Purchase."

Santiago's jaw tightened. He stood up.

McAllister stepped forward. He pulled Pablito to his feet.

"You are going to lead us to the border," McAllister said.

Pablito nodded. He was crying.

Santiago walked away. He did not want to look at the man anymore.

Maria was standing by the fire. She had heard everything.

"Purchase," she said.

"Yes."

She said nothing. Her face was still.

"I am sorry," Santiago said.

"Do not be sorry. You came for me. That is what matters."

Santiago looked at her. She was fifteen years old. She had been kidnapped. She had seen men die. And she was comforting him.

"You are a strong girl," he said.

"I am a strong woman."

Santiago nodded. "Yes. You are."

Chapter 42: The Trade

They left at dawn. Pablito rode ahead, his hands tied to the saddle. McAllister followed with a rifle across his lap.

Maria rode beside Santiago. The old man's mare had been rested. Her leg was better.

"You are limping," Maria said.

"My ankle."

"How bad?"

"Bad enough."

"You should rest it."

"After."

She looked at him. He felt her eyes on him.

"You are old," she said.

"Yes."

"Old enough to be my grandfather."

"Yes."

"So why are you doing this? Risking your life for a girl you have never met?"

Santiago considered the question. He had asked himself the same thing.

"Because someone had to," he said.

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only answer I have."

She was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "My father could not pay you."

"I did not ask him to."

"Then why?"

Santiago looked at the horizon. The sun was rising. The sky was orange and pink.

"Because I have a daughter," he said. "She died. I could not save her."

Maria said nothing.

"Maybe I am trying to save her now. In you."

She reached over and touched his arm.

"I am sorry," she said.

"I am sorry too."

They rode in silence. The sun rose higher. The day grew warm.

Chapter 43: The End of It

They reached the border crossing in the late afternoon. A small town. A few buildings. A dusty street.

Pablito pointed to a cantina. "He said he would meet us there."

McAllister dismounted. He handed his reins to Santiago.

"Watch the girl," he said.

"I am watching her."

McAllister walked into the cantina. He was inside for ten minutes.

When he came out, he was alone.

"The man is dead," he said.

"Dead?"

"Shot. Three days ago. His body is in the back room."

Santiago absorbed this. "Who killed him?"

"No one knows. The cantina owner found him."

McAllister looked at Pablito. The young man's face had gone pale.

"It is over," McAllister said. "There is no one to pay. No one to take her."

Pablito started to speak. McAllister cut him off.

"I do not want to hear it."

They left Pablito with the local sheriff. The man would be taken to jail. There would be a trial.

Maria watched from her horse. She did not say anything.

Santiago watched her. He saw her shoulders relax. Just a little.

"What now?" she asked.

"Now we take you home."

She nodded. She looked back at the cantina. Then she turned her horse east.

Without a word, she started riding.

Santiago and McAllister followed.

Chapter 44: The Silence

The ride back was quiet. The urgency was gone. The danger was past. The silence that remained was heavy.

Maria rode between them. She did not speak much. She answered questions when asked. She ate when food was offered. She slept when they stopped.

But she did not talk.

McAllister tried. He asked about her farm. Her father. Her plans.

She answered in short sentences. Nothing more.

"She is in shock," Santiago said one night.

"She will not talk to me."

"She does not trust you."

"Why not?"

"Because you are a man. Because you carry a gun. Because you wear a badge."

McAllister looked at him. "I saved her."

"You and I killed three men in front of her. She saw it. She heard it. She will not forget it."

"I was trying to help her."

"I know. She knows. But knowing is not the same as feeling."

McAllister sat down. He stared at the fire.

"What do I do?"

"Nothing. You let her heal. You do not push."

McAllister nodded. He looked at Maria. She was sitting apart from them, her back against a rock. She was watching the stars.

"She will be all right," Santiago said.

"How do you know?"

"Because she threw a rock at a man with a rifle. Girls like that survive."

McAllister almost smiled.

"I hope you are right," he said.

"I am right."

They sat in silence. The fire crackled. The stars moved overhead.

Chapter 45: The Ride Back

Maria rode beside Santiago on the third day. Her horse matched his pace.

"Tell me about your daughter," she said.

Santiago was quiet for a moment. "Her name was Nalin. She was eight years old when she died."

"What happened?"

"The fever. It swept through the Reservation. My wife caught it first. Then my daughter."

"How old was your wife?"

"Twenty-six."

Maria was quiet. The horses walked on.

"Do you have pictures of them?"

"No. I have nothing. Only my memory."

"That is hard."

"It is not hard. It is life. Life takes things. You learn to live with what remains."

Maria looked at him. "You sound like a priest."

"I am not a priest. I am just an old man who has lost too many people."

She reached into her pocket. She pulled out the bluebonnet drawing. The one she had made.

"I drew this before they took me," she said. "You can have it."

Santiago looked at the drawing. The flower was perfect. Every petal in place.

"Thank you," he said.

He took it. He folded it carefully. He put it in his shirt pocket, next to his heart.

They rode on.

"Will you stay?" she asked.

"At your farm?"

"Yes. My father would give you work."

"I have work. I have my shack. My quiet."

"It is lonely."

"Yes."

She looked at him. "You could stay."

Santiago considered. He looked at the landscape. The open plains. The big sky.

"We will see," he said.

Chapter 46: The Story

On the fourth night, Maria finally talked.

They were sitting around the fire. She had eaten. She had drunk water. Her hands were steady.

"I want to tell you what happened," she said.

"You do not have to," McAllister said.

"I want to."

She told them. She told them about the night they took her. How she woke with a hand over her mouth. How she fought. How they tied her and put her on a horse.

She told them about the days on the trail. The heat. The thirst. The way the men argued.

She told them about the stone hut. The window. The loose stone she found in the wall.

"I was going to kill them," she said. "If you had not come, I would have killed them."

Santiago believed her.

"How?" McAllister asked.

"I would have waited. El Lobo brought me food every night. He unlocked the door. When he came in, I would have hit him with the stone. Then I would have taken his knife."

"And then?"

"I would have crept out. Killed the others in their sleep. Taken a horse."

McAllister stared at her. "You planned all of that?"

"I had a lot of time to plan."

Santiago nodded. "She would have done it."

Maria looked at him. "You believe me."

"I know you would have. I saw you throw the rock."

She almost smiled. Almost.

"I am not a victim," she said.

"No," Santiago said. "You are a survivor."

She looked at the fire. Her face was lit by the flames.

"I think I will go to sleep now," she said.

She lay down. She closed her eyes.

In a few minutes, she was breathing evenly.

McAllister looked at Santiago. "She is something."

"Yes," Santiago said. "She is."

Chapter 47: The Crossing Back

They reached the canyon with the Comanche markings. Santiago stopped his horse.

"Wait here," he said.

He dismounted. His ankle complained. He walked to the cave entrance.

The handprint was still there. His father's hand. Or his grandfather's. He touched it.

He did not know when he would see it again. He might not see it again.

He pressed his palm against the stone. The same shape. The same fingers.

"I am coming back," he said. "To stay."

The words surprised him. He had not known he was going to say them.

But they felt right. The old land. The old ways.

He would come back.

He turned and walked back to the horses.

Maria watched him. "What did you say to the wall?"

"I said goodbye."

"You are not coming back here?"

"I am. That is why I said goodbye."

She did not understand. That was fine. She would learn.

They rode on. The canyon narrowed. Then it opened into the plains.

The farm was two days away.

Santiago touched the drawing in his pocket. The bluebonnet.

He thought about the handprint on the wall.

He thought about home.

Chapter 48: The Homecoming

The Gutierrez farm appeared on the horizon. A small shape in the vast land.

Maria saw it first. Her back straightened. Her hands tightened on the reins.

"Home," she said.

"Yes."

They rode down the long slope. The details grew clearer. The unpainted house. The leaning barn. The corral.

A figure emerged from the house. Tomas Gutierrez. He saw them.

He began to run.

Maria slid off her horse before it had stopped. She ran to meet him.

They collided. The father wrapped his arms around his daughter. He was crying. She was not.

Not yet.

Santiago watched from his horse. He felt something in his chest. A loosening. A warmth.

He had done this. He had brought her back.

Tomas looked up. He saw Santiago and McAllister. He nodded. His face was wet.

"Thank you," he said. "Thank you."

Santiago nodded.

Maria pulled away from her father. She walked back to Santiago.

"You will stay for dinner," she said. It was not a question.

"I will stay."

"Good."

She took his horse's reins. She led him toward the house.

Santiago looked back at McAllister.

"You coming?"

McAllister hesitated. "I do not know if I belong here."

"The girl asked for you."

"She asked for you."

"She asked for both of us."

McAllister dismounted. He followed them to the house.

Chapter 49: The Last Word

Dinner was simple. Beans. Tortillas. A bit of meat. It tasted better than anything Santiago had eaten in years.

Tomas talked. He could not stop talking. He told them about the cows. The weather. The roof that needed fixing.

He was filling the silence with words. Santiago understood.

Maria ate quietly. She watched her father. She watched Santiago. She watched McAllister.

After dinner, McAllister and Santiago stepped outside.

The sun was setting. The sky was red and gold.

"I will write my report," McAllister said. "I will make sure the sheriff knows what happened."

"Good."

"I will tell him about the man in the cantina. The one who paid them."

"Good."

McAllister looked at him. "What will you do now?"

Santiago considered. "I will rest here tonight. Then I will go home."

"To your shack."

"To my shack."

"You do not have to. You could stay here. The Gutierrez could use a hand."

Santiago shook his head. "I am not a farmer."

"Then what are you?"

Santiago looked at the sky. The first stars were appearing.

"I am a tracker. I find things."

"You found her."

"Yes."

"That is enough."

"It is enough."

McAllister held out his hand. Santiago looked at it. The hand that had once hunted his people.

He took it. They shook.

"Goodbye, Santiago."

"Goodbye, Ranger."

McAllister mounted his horse. He rode east toward the settlement.

Santiago watched him until he was a speck on the horizon.

Then he went inside.

Chapter 50: The Horizon

Santiago left at dawn. His ankle was better. He could almost walk without a limp.

Maria stood in the doorway. She held a bundle of food.

"For the journey," she said.

He took it. "Thank you."

"Will I see you again?"

"Maybe."

She looked at him. Her eyes were dry. She was not crying.

"I will remember you," she said.

"I will remember you too."

"Good."

She stepped forward and hugged him. Brief. Fierce.

Then she stepped back.

Santiago mounted his horse. He looked at the farm. The house. The barn. The girl in the doorway.

He turned his horse west.

He did not look back.

The plains opened before him. The sky was blue and endless. The wind moved through the grass.

He thought about the handprint on the cave wall. He thought about his daughter. He thought about the girl he had saved.

He touched the drawing in his pocket. The bluebonnet.

The sun was at his back. The horizon was ahead.

Home was somewhere in that direction. He would find it.

He had always found things.

The horse walked on. The wind blew. The world was quiet and vast and full of ghosts.

Santiago rode into the morning light.

THE END
