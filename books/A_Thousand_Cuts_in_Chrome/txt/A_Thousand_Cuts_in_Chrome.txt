A THOUSAND CUTS IN CHROME

A Novel

---

ARC I: THE GLASS JAWBREAKER

---

Chapter 1: The Chrome That Aches

The rain didn't fall in the Sprawl. It seeped. Industrial runoff mixed with condensation from a thousand cooling towers and dripped down like the city was sweating out its poison. I sat in my office on the forty-third floor of the Lomax Building, watching the droplets race each other down the window glass, and tried to remember the last time I'd seen the sun. The chrome arm hung heavy at my side, its servos humming their constant complaint.

Three weeks. Maybe four. The corporate weather domes over the enclaves on the ridge kept their skies pristine — eternal spring for the people who owned the world. Down here in the Chasm, we got whatever their scrubbers didn't want.

I flexed my left hand. Chromed alloy, five fingers, ball joints at the knuckles, servos that hummed at a frequency just below hearing. They'd given it to me after the explosion in the Vega docks. Free of charge, they said. Part of the settlement. What they didn't tell me was that the nerve interfaces would degrade after two years, and the replacement part cost more than I'd make in a lifetime of chasing cheating spouses and missing rich kids.

The hand ached. Not real pain — the nerves were long dead. A ghost signal from the interface, a phantom cramp that told me the chrome was eating into the bone socket. I'd need surgery in six months. Or amputation.

The door chimed. Not a buzz — a chime. That meant someone from the upper levels had made it past the building's security. Down here, people knocked. Up there, they announced themselves.

"Come in," I said.

The door slid open and she stepped through. Not walked — stepped, like she was used to floors that didn't stick. She wore a grey coat that probably cost more than my yearly rent, cut to hide the corporate insignia, but I'd seen that cut before. Nakamori Group. Their designers had a thing for asymmetric lapels.

Her face was pale. Not sick pale — scared pale. The kind of pale that comes from three sleepless nights and a decision you never thought you'd have to make.

"Dex Voss?" she said.

"That's me."

"I need you to find someone."

I leaned back in my chair. The springs groaned. "Missing person?"

"Yes."

"Family?"

She hesitated. "No. Not exactly."

I waited. The city hummed below us, a low thrum of maglev trains and waste processors and a million people trying to survive. The kind of sound that never stopped, never changed, just existed like the air itself.

"His name is Kaito Nakamura," she said. "He's — he was — a witness in a case against my employer. He disappeared three days ago. The police won't touch it. The corp security division is compromised. I need someone outside."

"Outside the system," I said.

"Outside everything."

I looked at my chrome hand. The servos whined.

"Tell me everything," I said.

She sat down.

---

Chapter 2: The Contract in Grey

Her name was Aiko Tanaka. Mid-level legal liaison for Nakamori Group's internal ethics division. The kind of job that sounded important until you realized the ethics division existed to make sure the corp could say they had one in court.

"Kaito Nakamura was scheduled to testify in a civil suit," she said, hands folded in her lap, perfectly still. "He claimed to have witnessed Nakamori's bio-engineering division violating the Geneva Accord protocols regarding sentient tissue cultivation."

"Sentient tissue," I repeated.

"Living organisms grown in vitro for medical and industrial applications. The Accord of '68 prohibits cultivating tissue that can achieve consciousness."

"And he saw them doing it."

"He had documentation. Video. Lab access logs. He was a janitor in the bio-division for twelve years. Nobody pays attention to the janitor."

I pulled out a notepad — real paper, real pen. Old habits. "What's his connection to you?"

"I interviewed him. Prepared his testimony." She looked down. "I believe him."

"And now he's gone."

"Vanished. His apartment is untouched. His personal effects are there. His neural implant hasn't pinged a GPS signal in seventy-two hours."

That was the detail that made my neck prickle. Neural implants didn't stop pinging unless they were removed, destroyed, or someone with the right equipment had suppressed them. Corp-grade suppression.

"Who else have you talked to?"

"Three private investigators in the last two days. All declined."

"Why?"

Aiko Tanaka met my eyes. "Because the first one was found dead in his office yesterday. They ruled it a heart attack, but he was thirty-four years old with no medical history."

I set down my pen. "And you came to me."

"You're a former Nakamori employee. You know the system. And you don't have a family."

I almost laughed. "That's your selling point? I'm expendable?"

"You're independent," she said. "And you're still alive."

I looked at my chrome hand again. Former Nakamori employee. That was one way to put it. Another way was: they used me for eight years as a security specialist, sent me into a dock that they knew was rigged, and gave me a hand as a consolation prize.

"One hundred thousand," I said.

She blinked. "Excuse me?"

"My rate. Missing person case, potential corp involvement, someone already dead. One hundred thousand up front. Non-refundable."

"I don't have that kind of — "

"Then find someone else."

She sat there for a long moment. The rain drummed against the window. Somewhere in the building, a pipe banged.

"I can get you forty," she said. "And full access to Nakamori's internal records."

"Full access?"

"Read-only. But unfiltered. My credentials."

I studied her face. Scared, yes. But also desperate. The kind of desperate that meant she knew something she wasn't telling me.

"How do I know this isn't a trap?"

"You don't." She stood up and placed a data chip on my desk. "Forty thousand. It's already been transferred. The access codes are on the chip."

She walked to the door, then stopped.

"Mr. Voss. If you find him — if you find Kaito — be careful what you ask him. He may not have the answers you expect."

Then she was gone.

I picked up the data chip. It was warm from her pocket.

---

Chapter 3: The Janitor's Apartment

Kaito Nakamura's apartment was in Sector 7-G, a middle-transit zone that sat between the corporate enclaves and the Chasm — not rich enough to be safe, not poor enough to be lawless. The kind of place where people kept their heads down and their doors locked.

I used the key card Aiko had given me. The lock clicked open. The smell hit me first — old rice, stale air, something metallic underneath. Not death. But close.

The apartment was one room with a bathroom alcove. A bed, a desk, a kitchen counter with a single burner. The walls were bare except for a calendar from three years ago. Everything was neat. Too neat.

I knelt by the bed. The sheets were military-tight — corners folded, no wrinkles. But under the pillow, I found a crease. Someone had lifted the pillow, put it back, but not perfectly aligned.

I checked the desk. Drawers open, contents undisturbed. A janitor's uniform hung in the closet, pressed and clean. No hidden compartments. No data chips taped under drawers.

Then I checked the floor. The apartment was on the seventh story. Concrete slab with cheap laminate. But near the window — a scuff mark. Faint, almost invisible. The kind made by a shoe pivoting under weight.

Someone had climbed in through the window. Seventh floor. That meant equipment — grapples, harnesses, maybe a drone. Professional.

I pulled out my scanner and swept the room. No listening devices. No cameras. But the scanner picked up something else — a faint electromagnetic residue on the bed frame. Low frequency, pulsed. The kind left by a neural interface dampener.

They hadn't just taken him. They'd paralyzed him first. Shut down his implant and left him conscious but unable to move.

I thought about what Aiko said. *Be careful what you ask him. He may not have the answers you expect.*

I looked at the calendar on the wall. Three years old. Why keep an expired calendar? I walked over and flipped through the pages. Nothing marked. No appointments, no reminders. But the paper felt different on one page — thicker, like something was behind it.

I peeled back the page for November. Taped to the inside was a photograph. A woman, maybe thirty, with dark hair and tired eyes. Standing in front of a building I didn't recognize. On the back, in faded ink: *M. Sato, BioDiv, intake date 11/3.*

BioDiv. Nakamori's bio-engineering division.

Kaito Nakamura knew someone who worked there. A woman who was processed on intake day.

I slipped the photo into my coat and took one last look around the apartment. The room was clean. Too clean. Like it had been staged. Like someone had arranged everything to make it look like a janitor lived here.

And I was starting to wonder if Kaito Nakamura was really a janitor at all.

---

Chapter 4: The Bio-Division

Nakamori Bio-Engineering occupied floors seventeen through thirty of the main tower, a mirrored monolith that pierced the smog layer and kept its upper floors in permanent sunlight. I'd worked here once, before the hand. Before everything.

I used Aiko's access codes at the security checkpoint. The system blinked green, and the guard waved me through without making eye contact. This deep in the corporate zone, the guards didn't look at faces. They looked at credentials.

The elevator hummed upward. I flexed my chrome hand. The ache was worse today.

Floor twenty-three was the vat room. I'd read the file Aiko gave me, but seeing it in person was different. Row after row of transparent cylinders, each one filled with a nutrient solution that glowed faintly blue. Inside each cylinder, something grew.

Tissue cultures, the official records said. Skin grafts for burn victims. Organ scaffolding for transplant patients. All legal, all above board.

But some of the cylinders were different. Larger. The blue glow was deeper, almost purple. And inside them, I could see shapes.

I pressed my face to the glass. The shape inside was humanoid. Arms, legs, a torso. The face was undeveloped — smooth, featureless, like a mannequin before the features are painted on.

"Beautiful, aren't they?"

I turned. A woman stood behind me. Lab coat, glasses, a name tag that read *Dr. Hadashi*. She was maybe fifty, with grey-streaked hair and eyes that had seen too much to be surprised by anything.

"Come to see the vat-born?" she asked.

"Just touring. New security contract."

She smiled. "I know who you are, Dex Voss. You used to work in dock security. Before the explosion."

I said nothing.

"Word travels in a building like this." She stepped closer to the vats. "These are the advanced models. Fully formed neural architecture. They can feel pain, you know. They just can't express it."

"That's legal?"

"It's borderline." She tapped the glass. "The Accord says we can't cultivate consciousness. But consciousness is a spectrum. Where do you draw the line? When the nerve tissue fires? When the brain stem develops? When the eyes open?"

She was enjoying this. Testing me.

"When they can say no," I said.

Dr. Hadashi's smile widened. "An interesting ethical position for a man with a bionic arm wired directly into his central nervous system."

I looked at my hand. The chrome gleamed under the blue light.

"Where do they go?" I asked. "The finished ones."

"Medical facilities. Research institutions. Some are used in corporate training simulations."

"And some disappear."

The smile vanished. "I don't know what you mean."

"I think you do." I pulled out the photograph of M. Sato. "Does this woman look familiar?"

Dr. Hadashi's face went blank. Completely, professionally blank.

"I've never seen her."

But her eyes had flicked to the left when she said it. Toward a door marked *Incinerator Access*.

I put the photograph away.

"Thank you for the tour, Doctor."

I walked toward the elevator. The blue light from the vats followed me, casting long shadows on the floor.

Behind me, I heard Dr. Hadashi pick up a phone.

---

Chapter 5: The Empty File

I spent the next three hours in a rented data booth in the Chasm, running Aiko's access codes through every database I could crack. The Nakamori internal system was vast — HR records, lab logs, security footage, financial transfers. I searched for Kaito Nakamura.

He existed. Barely.

Employment record: janitorial staff, twelve years. Tax filings, direct deposit slips, a company housing allowance. Everything a person needed to exist on paper.

But there was nothing before Nakamori. No birth certificate. No education records. No medical history. No family references. He appeared in the system at age twenty-eight, fully formed, with a biometric profile that matched no existing database.

I ran the same search for M. Sato.

She didn't exist at all.

The photograph in my pocket showed a woman who had no records, no history, no digital footprint. The only evidence she'd ever been alive was a faded ink note on the back of a photo hidden in a missing man's calendar.

I leaned back in the booth and stared at the cracked ceiling. The air smelled like ozone and old synth-leather.

A janitor who never existed. A woman who was never born. A corp that grew people in vats.

I thought about what Aiko had said. *He may not have the answers you expect.*

Maybe Kaito Nakamura wasn't a witness to a crime. Maybe he was the crime itself.

I pulled out my phone and called Aiko. It rang six times before she answered.

"I need to see the testimony," I said. "The full statement Kaito was going to give."

There was a long pause.

"Mr. Voss," she said. "There is no testimony."

"What?"

"Kaito Nakamura never gave a statement. He refused. He said he needed more time. He said — " Her voice cracked. "He said he needed to remember."

"Remember what?"

"Who he was."

The line went dead.

I sat in the dark booth, the phone still pressed to my ear, and listened to the hum of the city.

Kaito Nakamura didn't know if he was real. And neither did I.

---

Chapter 6: The Clinic

I found the clinic at the edge of Sector 12, a converted warehouse with a neon sign that read *MEMORY IS PRICELESS*. The kind of place that offered neural reconstruction, memory recovery, and "identity therapy" — a euphemism for people who weren't sure if their past was real.

Kaito had been here. I found his name in the appointment logs, buried under three layers of encryption that would have stopped most amateur hackers. But I had Nakamori-level decryption tools, and the logs opened like a flower.

Six appointments over two months. The last one was three days before he disappeared.

The receptionist was a woman in her sixties with steel-grey hair and augments at her temples — data jacks, the old kind, the kind that meant she'd been in the business since before neural lace was consumer-grade.

"I'm looking for information about a patient," I said.

"I can't share patient records."

"Kaito Nakamura."

Her face didn't change, but her hands stopped moving on the keyboard. "I said I can't share — "

"He's missing. The police won't look. His employer is a megacorp that grows conscious tissue in violation of international law. I'm the only person trying to find him before he ends up in an incinerator."

She looked at me for a long moment. Then she stood up and walked to the back of the clinic.

"Follow me."

The back room was small, windowless, lined with filing cabinets — real paper files, the kind that couldn't be hacked. She pulled one and handed it to me.

"His file. Take it."

I opened it. Inside were session notes, handwriting, a dozen pages.

"He came in asking about his memories," she said. "He said he had dreams of a life that didn't match his records. A childhood he couldn't have had. A mother whose face he could see but couldn't place."

"How did he pay?"

She hesitated. "That's the strange part. He paid in Nakamori scrip. Employee credit. But the account number traced back to someone else."

"Who?"

"Sato. M. Sato."

I looked at the file. The handwriting was neat, medical. *Patient presents with identity dysphoria. Possible false memory syndrome. Implanted memories suspected.*

"Implanted memories," I said.

"It's an experimental technique. Used by military and corporate intelligence. You can take a blank neural architecture — a vat-grown mind — and implant a complete life history. Childhood, education, relationships, trauma. Everything."

"And they believe it?"

"They don't just believe it. They live it. The memories are as real to them as yours are to you." She touched her temple. "The difference is, theirs never happened."

I closed the file.

"Thank you."

"Find him," she said. "Before they finish what they started."

---

Chapter 7: The Real Kaito

I read the file in my office that night, by the light of a single lamp and the neon glow from the street below.

Kaito Nakamura's therapy sessions painted a picture of a man unraveling. He'd come to the clinic because of nightmares — vivid, recurring dreams of a woman in a white room, a needle in his arm, a voice asking him questions. He'd wake up screaming, he told the therapist. He'd wake up with memories of things he'd never done.

Faces he'd never seen. Words he'd never spoken. A childhood spent in a city he'd never visited.

The therapist's notes were clinical, careful. *Patient exhibits classic signs of memory intrusion. Identity boundaries are porous. Recommend full neural audit.*

Memory intrusion. Someone else's memories leaking into his. But that wasn't possible unless the memories were designed to be there. Unless he was designed to receive them.

I thought about the vats in Nakamori's bio-division. The humanoid shapes floating in blue light. The featureless faces waiting for features to be painted on.

Kaito Nakamura was a blank slate. A vessel. They grew him, programmed him with a false identity, and inserted him into the janitorial staff. He spent twelve years cleaning floors, waiting for orders he didn't know existed.

But something went wrong. The memories started leaking. The programming degraded. He started asking questions.

And then he found M. Sato.

The woman in the photograph. The woman who didn't exist.

She was another vat-born. Another blank slate. But she'd been in the bio-division longer. Maybe she'd figured it out first. Maybe she'd tried to tell him.

I looked at the back of the photograph again. *Intake date 11/3.*

Intake. That was the word they used for harvested vat-grown tissue. When the body reached full development, they removed it from the vat and processed it for use. Medical. Commercial. *Witness.*

Kaito Nakamura was scheduled to testify against Nakamori. But he wasn't a witness to a crime. He was evidence of one.

And somewhere in the city, in a safe house or a lab or an incinerator, he was waiting for someone to figure that out.

My phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number.

*Stop looking. Or join the dead.*

I looked at the screen for a long moment. Then I turned off the lamp and sat in the dark, thinking.

---

Chapter 8: The Shadow in the Alley

They came for me at midnight.

I was walking back to my office from a noodle stand, my chrome hand tucked into my coat pocket, when I heard the footsteps. Two sets, behind me, matching my pace.

I didn't turn. I kept walking, counting the steps to the next corner. Fourteen. Twelve. Ten.

They sped up. So did I.

The alley to my left was a shortcut to the Lomax Building. Dark, narrow, lined with dumpsters and old pipes. Exactly the kind of place you didn't go at night.

I went in anyway.

The footsteps followed. I heard a knife slide out of a sheath — that distinctive metallic whisper. I kept walking.

"You made a mistake, Voss," a voice said. "Should have taken the money and stayed quiet."

I stopped. Turned.

Two men. Both in dark suits, both with the same asymmetric lapels I'd seen on Aiko Tanaka's coat. Nakamori security. Their faces were blank — professionally blank, like the kinds of faces that had seen bodies before.

"Who sent you?" I asked.

"Does it matter?"

"I like to know who to bill."

The first one laughed. It was not a friendly laugh.

"You're not billing anyone, Voss."

He came at me fast, knife low, aiming for the gut. I sidestepped, grabbed his wrist with my chrome hand, and squeezed. The servos whined. The bones in his wrist cracked like dry twigs.

He screamed. The knife clattered to the ground.

The second one was smarter. He pulled a stun gun from his jacket and aimed. I threw the first man at him — they collided, stumbled, and I was already moving.

My chrome hand caught the second man across the jaw. His head snapped back. He hit the ground and stayed there.

I stood over them, breathing hard. The ache in my hand was gone, replaced by the dull throb of servos working at maximum capacity.

"Tell whoever sent you," I said, "that I'm not done yet."

I walked out of the alley, leaving them in the dark.

---

Chapter 9: The Woman Who Never Was

M. Sato lived in a basement apartment in Sector 14, a district so deep in the Chasm that the sun never reached it. The address wasn't in any system — I found it through the therapy clinic's billing records, cross-referenced with a utility account that had been paid in cash for three years.

The door was unlocked.

I pushed it open, chrome hand raised, ready for anything.

The apartment was empty. A mattress on the floor, a hot plate, a stack of books. The walls were covered in photographs — hundreds of them, all of the same woman. M. Sato. In different clothes, different locations, different ages. A life documented in images.

But something was wrong.

The faces didn't match. In some photos, she had a mole on her cheek. In others, she didn't. In some, her eyes were brown; in others, grey. The details shifted from picture to picture, like someone had been trying to remember a face and kept getting it wrong.

I picked up one of the photos. On the back, in faded ink: *Intake date 4/17. Model 7-C.*

I grabbed another. *Intake 6/22. Model 9-A.*

Another. *Intake 1/8. Model 12-B.*

Different models. Different intake dates. Different women.

M. Sato wasn't one person. It was a designation. A batch number.

I looked at the wall again, and this time I saw it clearly — the faces were similar but not identical, like siblings, like copies, like products off an assembly line.

They'd been growing witnesses for years. Hundreds of them. Thousands. Each one implanted with a false identity, a fake history, a life designed to be credible in court.

And Kaito Nakamura was one of them.

But something had gone wrong with him. He'd started to remember. Not his real memories — he had no real memories. But the memories of others. The other models. The other witnesses.

He was a broken vessel, leaking the lives of everyone who came before him.

I sat down on the mattress, surrounded by the faces of a thousand people who never existed, and tried to figure out what to do next.

My phone buzzed. A text from Aiko.

*They know. Get out. Now.*

The door slammed open behind me.

---

Chapter 10: The Extraction

I didn't turn around. I dove sideways, rolled, came up with my chrome hand extended. The man in the doorway had a gun — not a stun gun, a real one, with a suppressor.

"Voss," he said. "You're making this difficult."

"I get that a lot."

He stepped into the room, gun leveled at my chest. Behind him, I saw two more figures in the hallway. Nakamori security. Professional. Armed.

"Dr. Hadashi sends her regards," he said. "She was hoping you'd take the hint."

"I'm not good at hints."

The gun didn't waver. "The vat-born are corporate property. What you've found doesn't concern you. You'll forget everything, sign a non-disclosure, and go back to your missing persons cases."

"And if I don't?"

"I have orders to extract you permanently."

I looked at the walls. The faces of a hundred vat-born witnesses stared back, their mismatched features frozen in time.

"They're people," I said. "Not property."

"Legally, they're neither. They're products. And products don't have rights."

I felt something snap inside me. Not in my hand — in my chest. The part that had been quiet since the explosion, since the hand, since I'd stopped caring about anything but the next case and the next paycheck.

I stood up slowly, hands visible.

"You're going to shoot me in a basement in Sector 14," I said. "The body will never be found. Is that the plan?"

"Something like that."

He was calm. Professional. He'd done this before.

The photograph on the wall behind him caught my eye. *Model 7-C.* The face of a woman who never existed, looking out at me with eyes that were slightly too far apart.

I thought about Kaito Nakamura. About the dreams he'd had. The lives he'd remembered. The person he thought he was and the person he was made to be.

I thought about my chrome hand. The nerve interfaces that were slowly killing me. The phantom pains of a limb that was never really mine.

We were the same, him and me. Built by a corporation, programmed to serve, starting to break down.

"One question," I said.

The gunman tilted his head. "What?"

"Did you know the vat-born can feel pain?"

I punched the wall with my chrome hand. Not the gunman — the wall. The servos screamed. The alloy fingers punched through the drywall like paper, hit something solid, and kept going.

The lights went out.

The building's main conduit ran through this wall. I'd seen it on the way in — a junction box, poorly concealed, standard for basement units. My hand found the wiring and shorted everything.

The gunman fired. The muzzle flash lit the room for a split second. I was already moving, low, toward the door.

I hit him in the throat with my chrome elbow. He went down choking.

The other two were blind, firing into the dark. I didn't give them time to adjust. The first one caught a punch to the temple. The second one I grabbed by the collar and slammed into the wall until he stopped moving.

Silence. The smell of ozone and cordite.

I stood in the dark, surrounded by the bodies of Nakamori security, and listened to my chrome hand whine as the servos cooled.

The photograph wall was still faintly visible in the emergency light. Dozens of faces. Dozens of lives that never were.

I took one of the photos. *Model 7-C.* The one that looked most like the woman in Kaito's calendar.

Then I walked out into the rain.

Chapter 11: The Safe House

The rain had stopped by the time I reached the safe house, but the city was still sweating. Steam rose from the pavement, mixing with the exhaust of idling trucks and the chemical fog that never quite lifted.

The safe house was a studio above a fish market in Sector 18, owned by an old contact from my Nakamori days — a woman named Chen who'd been fired the same week I was and had built a career renting anonymous spaces to people who needed to disappear. No lease, no records, cash only.

I laid out what I had on the floor. The photograph of M. Sato. The clinic file. The data chip from Aiko. One of the photos from the wall of faces.

I was missing something. A connection. A thread that tied it all together.

I picked up the photo of Model 7-C. Woman, maybe thirty, dark hair, tired eyes. Same as the one in Kaito's calendar. But now that I looked closer, I saw differences. The jaw was slightly wider. The nose slightly different. Like a sculptors iteration — same design, different mold.

I checked the back of the photo from Kaito's apartment. *M. Sato, BioDiv, intake date 11/3.*

And the one I'd taken from the wall. *Intake date 4/17. Model 7-C.*

Different dates. Different models. But both labeled M. Sato.

I'd assumed M. Sato was a person. She wasn't. She was a product line.

The M stood for Model. The Sato was the batch designation.

Kaito hadn't hidden a photo of a woman he knew. He'd hidden a photo of a woman he was made from.

I closed my eyes and let that sink in. Kaito Nakamura was built using genetic material from Sato-series vat-grown models. He was a second-generation construct. A copy of a copy.

And somewhere in his implanted memories, he'd found traces of the original.

The phone buzzed. Aiko.

"I'm in trouble," she said. Voice low, urgent. "They know I gave you the access codes. I'm under house arrest in my apartment. They're coming for me in the morning."

"How long do you have?"

"Six hours. Maybe less."

"I'll come get you."

"Don't. It's a trap. They want you to come. That's why they haven't taken me yet."

She was right. Of course she was right.

"If I don't come, they'll take you to a black site."

"If you do come, they'll take us both."

I looked at the photographs spread across the floor. A thousand faces. A thousand lives that were never born.

"Tell me about the trial," I said. "The one Kaito was going to testify in."

A pause. "It's not one trial. It's three. Nakamori is being sued by three separate plaintiffs. All of them claim their family members were replaced by vat-grown duplicates."

"Replaced?"

"The originals were abducted. Their identities were harvested — memories, biometrics, behavioral patterns. The vat-grown copies were implanted with these identities and inserted back into their lives. The families didn't notice for months. In some cases, years."

I felt cold.

"The copies — did they know?"

"That's the worst part," Aiko said. "No. They didn't. They genuinely believed they were the original person. Every memory, every relationship, every trauma — it was all real to them. The only difference was that none of it ever happened."

I thought about Kaito. The nightmares. The leaking memories. The face of a woman he'd never met.

"They're defective," I said. "The copies. They start to break down."

"The original models had a shelf life. Five years before neurological degradation set in. But Nakamori couldn't sell a product that expired. So they developed new models with longer stability. The newer ones — the ones they're growing now — they can last indefinitely."

"Then why did Kaito start remembering?"

"Because he wasn't a copy," she said. "He was a prototype."

The word hit me like a punch.

"A prototype for what?"

"For a new line of witnesses. People who could be inserted into any situation, with any history, and testify with perfect conviction because they believed their own lies." Her voice cracked. "But the prototype was unstable. He started developing... empathy. For the other models. The ones waiting in the vats."

"He wanted to free them."

"He wanted to testify. Not as a witness for Nakamori — against them. He wanted to tell the world what they'd done."

Silence. The fish market below was closing, metal shutters rolling down.

"I'm coming to get you," I said.

"Voss — "

"Six hours. Be ready."

I hung up and started gathering my things.

Chapter 12: House Arrest

Aiko Tanaka lived in a corporate apartment tower in Sector 3, a luxury building that rose above the smog line and offered views of the city that most people would never see. The kind of building with retinal scanners and armed lobby security and a waiting list that took years.

Getting in was the easy part. I still had her access credentials, and they still worked on the building's main entrance. The guards didn't look twice at a man in a repair uniform carrying a tool kit — I'd picked up the disguise from a supply closet in the basement.

The elevator to the forty-second floor required a separate code. I didn't have it. But the fire stairs were accessible, and forty-two floors was nothing compared to the climb I'd done in the Vega docks with a shattered arm.

Forty-two floors later, I was breathing hard, my chrome hand throbbing. I cracked the stairwell door and checked the hallway.

Clear.

Aiko's apartment was at the end of the hall. I saw the surveillance immediately — two cameras, one at each corner, covering the approach. No blind spots.

I looked at the ceiling. The fire suppression system had a service panel. I climbed up, popped it open, and shorted the sprinkler control.

The sprinklers activated. The cameras went dark, lenses sprayed with water. I dropped down and moved fast.

The door was locked — biometric, keypad, deadbolt. I pulled out the access chip Aiko had given me and pressed it to the reader. The light stayed red.

They'd revoked her credentials.

I took a step back and kicked the door at the lock point. Once. Twice. On the third kick, the frame splintered and the door swung open.

Aiko stood in the living room, a small bag in her hand, her eyes wide.

"You're here."

"Let's go."

We ran for the stairwell. Behind us, alarms started blaring.

---

Chapter 13: The Tunnel

The building's security response was faster than I'd expected. By the time we hit the thirtieth floor, I could hear boots in the stairwell above and below. They'd boxed us in.

I pushed Aiko through the door on the thirtieth floor. "This way."

"Where? There's nowhere — "

I didn't answer. I was looking for the service shaft. Every Nakamori building had them — maintenance access for the environmental systems, big enough for a person to crawl through. I'd helped design the security protocols for these buildings. I knew where the gaps were.

The shaft was at the end of the hall, behind a panel labeled *DANGER: HIGH VOLTAGE*. I pried it open with my chrome hand, ignoring the warning.

"Inside. Quick."

We crawled through the shaft, past humming ducts and dripping pipes, until we reached an access ladder that led down to the sub-basement. The parking garage. From there, a maintenance tunnel connected to the old transit system — abandoned for a decade, but still passable.

"Where does it lead?" Aiko asked, her voice echoing in the dark.

"Sector 20. The Dead Zone."

"The Dead Zone is ungoverned territory. No law, no security, no — "

"Exactly. They won't follow us there."

We moved through the darkness, our footsteps echoing in the empty tunnel. The air was stale, heavy with the dust of a decade.

"Kaito came through here," I said. "Didn't he?"

Aiko was silent.

"He didn't just disappear from his apartment. He ran. He went through the tunnels, crossed into the Dead Zone, and Nakamori couldn't find him."

"Their jurisdiction ends at the boundary," she said quietly. "The corporate accords prevent them from operating in unincorporated territory."

"But they sent people after him anyway."

"Yes."

"And they killed the first investigator who took the case."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Aiko stopped walking. I could barely see her face in the dim emergency light.

"Because Kaito didn't just remember the other models," she said. "He remembered the originals. The people they were copied from. He knew where they were being held."

"Held? I thought the originals were abducted and replaced. The copies went back to their lives."

"That's what I thought too. But Kaito found something else. A facility. A holding center. The originals are still alive."

I stared at her.

"They're not making copies to replace people," she said. "They're making copies to harvest them. The originals are kept alive in stasis, and their identities are farmed — extracted, packaged, implanted into vat-grown bodies that can be sold."

"Sold to who?"

"Anyone who needs a new identity. Witness protection. Intelligence operatives. Corporate executives who want a second life."

The tunnel stretched ahead of us, dark and endless.

"How many?"

"I don't know. Hundreds. Maybe thousands."

I started walking again. There was nothing else to do.

---

Chapter 14: The Dead Zone

Sector 20 wasn't a place people went voluntarily. It was a pocket of the city that had been cut off from corporate jurisdiction after the Treaty of '72, a no-man's-land where the laws of the enclaves didn't apply and the only order came from whoever held the most guns.

The entrance was a checkpoint made of shipping containers and chain-link fence. Three men stood guard, their weapons visible, their faces hard.

I approached with my hands visible. "I'm looking for someone."

"Everyone's looking for someone," the guard said. "Costs to look."

I held up a credit chip. "I'm not here for trouble."

"Trouble finds everyone in the Zone."

He took the chip, scanned it, nodded. The fence opened.

Aiko and I stepped through.

The Dead Zone was like walking into a different century. No neon, no maglev, no corporate logos. Just concrete buildings, narrow streets, and the smell of wood smoke and cooking oil. People watched us from doorways, their eyes tracking our movements.

"Where do we start?" Aiko asked.

"Kaito didn't come here by accident. He had a contact. Someone who knew about the holding facility."

I pulled out the photo of M. Sato — Model 7-C. The face that had been in Kaito's calendar.

"There's a woman in the Zone who runs a data brokerage. If anyone knows where Kaito is, she does."

"Who is she?"

"Her name's Ruby. She used to work for Nakamori. Bio-division records. She got out before the purge."

"You seem to know a lot of former Nakamori employees."

"We're a brotherhood of the screwed over."

We walked deeper into the Dead Zone, past makeshift markets and open fires, past children playing with scraps of chrome and old men drinking from bottles wrapped in paper bags. The Zone had its own rhythm, its own laws.

Ruby's data brokerage was in a basement beneath a noodle shop, protected by a door that weighed half a ton and a retinal scanner that had been jury-rigged from military surplus.

I knocked. A slot opened.

"Voss," I said. "I need a location."

The slot closed. The door opened.

Ruby was a small woman in her fifties, with grey hair cropped short and augments running up both sides of her neck — data jacks, the high-end kind, wired directly into her visual cortex. She was blind to the physical world but could see data streams like most people saw light.

"You look like hell," she said.

"Missed you too."

"Who's the corpo?"

"Aiko. She's with me."

Ruby's blind eyes tracked toward Aiko. "She's tagged. Nakamori loyalty implant. They can track her neural signature anywhere in the world."

My blood went cold.

"You knew?"

"I just read it. You didn't?"

I looked at Aiko. Her face was pale.

"I didn't know," she said. "I swear. They must have done it when I was hired. Standard medical screening."

"It's in the base of her skull," Ruby said. "Very small. Very deep. Removable, but not by me."

"Can you disable it?"

"Temporarily. I can broadcast a jamming signal that'll mask it for about six hours. After that, they'll find her."

"Do it."

Ruby turned and walked into the back of her shop, her fingers trailing along a wall covered in cables. "I'll need access to her spinal interface. It's going to hurt."

Aiko swallowed. "Do it."

I waited in the front room, surrounded by stacks of old data drives and monitors showing feeds from a hundred different security cameras across the Zone. The hum of Ruby's equipment filled the space.

After twenty minutes, Ruby came back. "Done. She's asleep. She'll wake up in an hour."

"And Kaito?"

"Your missing vat-born checked in three days ago. Paid cash for a data packet and a location."

"What location?"

Ruby's blind eyes found me. "A facility in Sector 26. Underneath the old Nakamori research center. The one that was supposedly decommissioned in '69."

"The holding center. For the originals."

"If you say so." She handed me a data chip. "The coordinates. And a warning: the facility is still active. Nakamori has it listed as a cold storage archive, but the power draw tells a different story."

"How do I get in?"

"You don't. But there's an old maintenance tunnel from the transit system. Runs right underneath the main chamber. Kaito used it."

"He went in?"

"Three days ago. He hasn't come out."

I took the chip. "I need a way to contact you securely."

"You already have it. And Voss — "

"What?"

"The readings from that facility. They're not just storage. There are life signs. Dozens of them. But not human."

"What are they?"

Ruby's face was hard to read in the dim light. "Something between. Something in the middle."

She turned away and disappeared into the cables and monitors.

---

Chapter 15: The Facility

The old Nakamori research center sat on the edge of Sector 26, a concrete box that had been abandoned for thirty years — or so the records said. The fences were rusted, the windows were broken, and the parking lot was cracked with weeds.

But the power draw Ruby mentioned was visible if you knew where to look. The security cameras were new. The locks were electronic. And the ground above the underground levels was warm — heated by the equipment below.

I found the maintenance tunnel entrance behind a collapsed shed. The grate was rusted but I pried it open with my chrome hand, servos whining under the strain.

The tunnel descended into darkness. I pulled out a flashlight and climbed down.

The smell hit me first. Organic. Wet. The smell of a body that's been kept alive too long.

The tunnel opened into a chamber that made me stop breathing.

Rows of tanks lined the walls, each one filled with a blue-green solution. And inside each tank, a body floated. Naked. Hairless. Eyes closed.

They were human. Or close enough. But their features were smooth, unfinished — like the vat-grown models in the bio-division, but older. More advanced.

I walked down the row, counting. Twenty tanks. Thirty. Fifty.

At the end of the room, a computer terminal glowed. I approached it, my footsteps echoing.

The screen showed a directory. Names. Dates. Biometric profiles.

The originals. The people who'd been abducted and replaced by vat-grown copies.

I scrolled through the list. Forty-seven names. Forty-seven people who'd been stripped of their identities, their lives, their families. Kept alive in stasis while their memories were harvested and sold.

And at the bottom of the list, a name I recognized.

*Nakamura, Kaito.*

But the date next to it was thirty years ago.

I stared at the screen, my mind racing.

Kaito Nakamura — the real Kaito Nakamura — had been taken thirty years ago. The copy — the one who'd been working as a janitor, the one who'd come to the clinic with nightmares, the one I was trying to find — was a third-generation construct. A copy of a copy. A replica built from the memories of a man who'd been in a tank for three decades.

The real Kaito was down here. Somewhere.

I looked at the tanks again. Which one was him? Which one was the original?

A sound behind me. Footsteps. Slow, deliberate.

I turned.

A figure stood in the dim light. Thin. Pale. Wearing a lab coat that had once been white.

Dr. Hadashi.

"Mr. Voss," she said. "I was hoping you'd find your way here."

---

Chapter 16: The Architect

Dr. Hadashi looked different in the green glow of the holding tanks. Older. Tireder. The professional calm she'd shown in the bio-division was gone, replaced by something rawer.

"How long have you been running this?" I asked.

"Thirty years. Since the first successful harvest."

She walked past me, her hand trailing along the glass of a tank. Inside, a woman floated, her face slack, her eyes closed.

"The public thinks the Geneva Accord of '68 ended vat-grown consciousness. It didn't. It just drove the research underground. Nakamori realized that the real value wasn't in growing tissue — it was in growing identities."

"People."

"Blank vessels that could be filled with any history, any personality, any memory. Imagine the applications. Intelligence operatives who genuinely believe their cover stories. Witnesses who can't be broken on cross-examination because they're not lying. Executives who can be reborn in a new body when the old one fails."

She stopped at a tank near the end of the row. Inside, a man floated. His face was lined, older than the others.

Kaito Nakamura. The original.

"He was our first successful harvest," she said. "The first time we extracted a complete identity and successfully implanted it into a vat-grown body."

"How many copies did you make?"

"Of Kaito? Seven. The first four were unstable. The fifth lasted six years before neurological degradation set in. The sixth lasted nine. The seventh — the one you're looking for — was different. He developed self-awareness. He started asking questions."

"He's defective."

"He's an evolution." She turned to face me. "The vat-born aren't supposed to develop independent consciousness. They're supposed to be vessels — blank slates that hold the implanted identity without modification. But the seventh iteration began to... integrate. The implanted memories merged with something new. Something emergent."

"You're saying he developed a soul."

"I'm saying he developed something we didn't program. And that makes him the most valuable asset Nakamori has ever produced."

"Why?"

"Because if we can understand how it happened, we can replicate it. Control it. Manufacture people with genuine consciousness — but consciousness that we design."

I stared at her. "You're talking about creating life."

"I'm talking about perfecting it."

I looked at the tank. At the real Kaito Nakamura, floating in his artificial womb, his life stolen and copied and sold.

"And him? What happens to the originals?"

"They remain in stasis. Their neural patterns continue to generate new memory data — experiences, emotions, dreams. We harvest that data and use it to refresh the vat-born copies. The originals are our most valuable resource."

"They're prisoners."

"They're investments."

My chrome hand clenched. The servos whined.

"The seventh iteration — the one I'm looking for. Where is he?"

Dr. Hadashi smiled. "He's here. He came back."

"Why?"

"Because he found out what he was. And he wanted to meet the original."

A door opened at the far end of the chamber. A figure stepped through.

Kaito Nakamura. The copy. The seventh iteration.

He looked older than his file photo. Gaunt. His eyes were hollow, like he hadn't slept in days.

"Mr. Voss," he said. "I've been expecting you."

---

Chapter 17: The Conversation

Kaito sat on the edge of a gurney, his hands clasped between his knees. The green light from the tanks made his skin look translucent, like he was already halfway to becoming a ghost.

"You've been looking for me," he said.

"Yes."

"Did you find what you were looking for?"

I didn't know how to answer that.

"You're not a witness," I said. "You're the evidence."

"I know."

"You were built to testify against Nakamori. But you're not testifying about a crime you saw. You're testifying about a crime you are."

"I know."

"How long have you known?"

He looked at his hands. "I've suspected for about a year. The dreams started. Faces I'd never seen. Places I'd never been. At first I thought I was losing my mind. Then I realized I was finding it."

"The memories of the original. Leaking through."

"Not just the original." He looked up. "All of them. The other copies. The other vessels. Every time I closed my eyes, I saw their lives. A dozen different childhoods. A dozen different mothers. A dozen different deaths."

"Deaths?"

"The first four iterations were unstable. They only lasted a few years before their neural architecture collapsed. But the memories don't die when the body does. They transfer. Pass down. I remember their last moments — the confusion, the fear, the silence."

I sat down across from him. The floor was cold, damp.

"Why did you come back here?"

"To see him. The original."

"And?"

"I wanted to ask him if he felt the same things. The same emptiness. The same sense of being incomplete."

"What did he say?"

Kaito laughed. It was not a happy sound. "He can't talk. He's been in stasis for thirty years. His brain activity is minimal. He's a memory farm — kept alive to generate fresh experiences that can be harvested."

"But you came anyway."

"I had to see. I had to know if there was something real at the center of all this. A person. A soul."

"And?"

Kaito looked at the tank where the original floated. "There's nothing left. He's a shell. They've taken everything. His memories, his emotions, his identity. There's nothing inside him but a machine that keeps his body alive."

We sat in silence for a long moment.

"What are you going to do?" I asked.

"I don't know. Dr. Hadashi wants to study me. Nakamori wants to terminate me. The families of the originals want me to testify."

"What do you want?"

He looked at me, and for a moment I saw something flicker in his eyes. Something that might have been anger, or hope, or both.

"I want to be real."

---

Chapter 18: The Offer

Dr. Hadashi gave us the room. Probably because she was watching through the cameras anyway.

"You need to leave," I said. "I can get you out. The Dead Zone is safe. I know people who can hide you."

"And then what? Spend the rest of my life running from Nakamori?"

"It's better than letting them dissect you."

Kaito shook his head. "You don't understand. I'm not running anymore. I came here to find the truth. I'm not leaving without it."

"The truth is in those tanks. Forty-seven originals. All of them alive, all of them being harvested. You can take that to the press, to the courts — "

"To who? Every major media outlet is owned by a corporation. Every judge in the city is on a corporate retainer. The system is built to protect people like Nakamori, not punish them."

"Then what's the plan?"

He looked at me. "You were a security specialist. You know this building's layout. You know how to get in and out without being detected."

"I was. I'm a PI now."

"Same skills. Different pay scale."

"What are you asking me to do?"

"Forty-seven people in those tanks. Forty-seven lives that were stolen. If I can free them — if I can wake them up and get them out — the story becomes undeniable. Not a vat-born witness testifying about corporate crimes. Forty-seven victims, alive, able to speak for themselves."

I looked at the tanks. The green light. The floating bodies.

"That's a rescue mission. Not an extraction."

"Yes."

"I'm one man with a chrome arm and a bad attitude."

"I know."

"Dr. Hadashi has a security team. Nakamori will have reinforcements on the way."

"I know."

Kaito stood up. "I don't expect you to say yes. I just wanted someone to know. Someone outside Nakamori. Someone who could tell the story if I don't make it out."

He started walking toward the tanks.

"Where are you going?"

"To find the release controls. The stasis pods have emergency override systems. I've been studying the schematics for months."

"You're going to do this alone?"

"I've been alone my whole life. I'm used to it."

I watched him walk away. Watched his thin frame move through the green light, past the tanks of stolen people.

My chrome hand ached.

"The hell with it."

I stood up and followed him.

---

Chapter 19: The Awakening

The release controls were in a sub-basement level below the main chamber, behind a door that required three forms of authentication. I cracked the panel with my chrome hand, shorted the circuits, and the door slid open with a groan.

The room was small, filled with monitoring equipment and a central console. The screens showed vital signs for all forty-seven originals.

Kaito sat down at the console. His fingers moved across the keyboard.

"The emergency release will wake them gradually. We need to stagger the revival to avoid cardiac stress."

"How long?"

"Twenty minutes for the first batch. Then we can start moving them to the surface."

"Twenty minutes before Nakamori shows up with an army."

"Hopefully they'll be too busy dealing with the media."

I stared at him. "You called the media?"

"Not yet. But I will." He pulled out a data chip. "Full documentation. Everything I've found. I'll broadcast it on every open channel when we start the release. By the time Nakamori shuts it down, someone will have seen it."

"Or they'll kill us before anyone can verify."

"That's a risk I'm willing to take."

The first tank began to drain. The blue-green solution lowered, and the body inside started to stir.

I watched as the woman — the one whose tank Dr. Hadashi had touched — coughed, choked, drew her first breath of air in thirty years.

Her eyes opened.

She didn't know where she was. She didn't know who she was. But she was alive.

Kaito was already at the next tank.

---

Chapter 20: The Siege

They came faster than I expected.

I was helping the third revival — a man in his fifties whose muscles had atrophied so badly he couldn't stand — when the alarms started. Red lights. Klaxons. The sound of boots on metal stairs.

"They're here," I said.

Kaito didn't look up from the console. "Four more tanks. I need four more."

"You don't have four more. They're at the sub-basement door."

I moved to the door, pulling a pistol from my coat. I'd taken it off one of the guards in the basement apartment, and I was glad I had.

The door at the top of the stairs slammed open. Two men in Nakamori security gear came through, weapons raised.

I fired twice. The first shot hit the wall. The second caught the lead guard in the shoulder. He went down.

The second guard returned fire. I dove behind the console as bullets sparked off the metal.

"Three more minutes!" Kaito shouted.

"You have one!"

I popped up, fired again. The second guard retreated up the stairs.

"They're calling for backup," I said.

"Then we need to move faster."

The fourth tank drained. The fifth. Bodies coughing, gasping, waking into a world they didn't recognize.

The door at the top of the stairs burst open again. More guards. At least six.

I emptied my clip. One went down. The others kept coming.

"We're out of time!"

Kaito hit a final key. "That's seven. Best I can do."

"Seven is better than none."

I grabbed the nearest revived — the woman from the first tank — and pulled her toward the maintenance tunnel. Kaito followed, supporting a man who could barely walk.

Behind us, gunfire.

Ahead of us, darkness.

We ran.

---

END OF ARC II

ARC III: THE REAL VICTIM

---

Chapter 21: The Survivors

Seven of them. That's all we got out.

Seven bodies that had been floating in nutrient solution for decades, their muscles atrophied, their minds shattered by the reawakening. They sat in Ruby's basement, wrapped in blankets, drinking water in slow, careful sips.

The woman from the first tank was named Yuki. Or she had been, once. Thirty years ago. Before Nakamori took her.

"I don't remember my family," she said. Her voice was rough, unused. "I remember... a garden. Cherry blossoms. A child's hand in mine."

"Daughter or son?" I asked.

"I don't know."

She started crying. Silent tears that ran down her face without her seeming to notice.

Aiko moved to sit beside her. "It's okay. You don't have to remember everything right now."

Ruby pulled me aside. "The jamming signal on her loyalty implant will fail in two hours. After that, Nakamori will know exactly where she is."

"Her?"

"Aiko. I can only mask one neural signature at a time, and I'm using all my equipment to keep the revived stable."

"Then we need to move Aiko."

"I can't move," Aiko said from across the room. She'd heard. "If I leave, the implant will ping the moment I'm out of jamming range. I need it removed."

"Ruby?"

"Not a surgeon. And the implant is deep — wrapped around the brainstem. One wrong move and she's dead."

I paced the room. Seven survivors. One dying woman. A city full of corporate enemies.

"There's a surgeon in the Dead Zone," I said. "Old-school. Does black-market chrome work. If anyone can remove it, he can."

"Who?" Ruby asked.

"Goes by Sol. Used to work for Nakamori medical. He was fired after he refused to install loyalty implants in new employees."

"He's still alive?"

"Last I heard."

I looked at Aiko. "I'll take you to him. Ruby, keep the survivors here. Don't let anyone in."

"And Kaito?"

Kaito was in the corner, staring at his hands. He hadn't spoken since we left the facility.

"He stays with them."

"Voss — "

"He's the only one who knows what those people have been through. They need him."

I grabbed my coat. Aiko stood up, unsteady.

"Let's go find a surgeon."

---

Chapter 22: The Surgeon

Sol's clinic was hidden in the basement of a ruined hospital in Sector 22, accessible only through a tunnel that had been clawed out of the rubble by hand. The sign on the door read *SOL'S FIXES — NO QUESTIONS, NO RECORDS, CASH ONLY*.

The man himself answered the door. He was ancient — eighty if he was a day — with hands that trembled until he picked up a scalpel. Then they became rock steady.

"Voss," he said. "I heard you were dead."

"Disappointing, I know."

"Who's the corpo?"

"She needs an implant removed. Nakamori loyalty chip. Base of the brainstem."

Sol's eyes narrowed. "Those aren't meant to be removed."

"Nothing's impossible."

"The mortality rate is sixty percent."

"I know."

"You're asking me to kill this woman."

"I'm asking you to save her."

Sol looked at Aiko. She looked back, her face pale but steady.

"I consent," she said. "Whatever happens."

Sol studied her for a long moment. Then he stepped aside and let us in.

The clinic was small but clean. Surgical lights, an ancient operating table, cabinets full of tools that gleamed under the harsh white light.

"Lie down," he said to Aiko. "This is going to hurt."

---

Chapter 23: The Knife

I waited in the outer room while Sol worked. The walls were lined with old medical textbooks and photographs of patients — before and after shots of chrome installations, scar revision, organ replacement. A gallery of people made whole by a man the corporate system had discarded.

Through the door, I heard Aiko cry out once. Then silence.

The clock on the wall ticked. Minutes stretched into hours.

I thought about Kaito. About the survivors in Ruby's basement. About the forty still floating in that facility, waiting to be revived.

I thought about my chrome hand. The ache that never went away. The interface that was slowly killing me.

Sol came out after two hours. His hands were covered in blood.

"She's alive."

I exhaled.

"The implant is out. I've got it here if you want it."

He held up a small metal device, no bigger than a grain of rice. Wires trailed from it like the legs of a dead spider.

"Can you trace it back here?"

"Not if I destroy it now." He dropped it into a disposal unit. The device sizzled, popped, and was gone.

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. She'll be weak for a week. And the implant site will need monitoring — if there's infection, she'll need antibiotics I don't have."

"I'll get them."

Aiko was awake when I went in. Pale, bandaged, but awake.

"Did it work?"

"It worked. You're free."

She tried to smile. "I don't feel free. I feel like someone drilled a hole in my skull."

"That's because someone drilled a hole in your skull."

She laughed, winced, touched her head.

"The survivors — are they okay?"

"They're alive. That's all I can promise right now."

"The media. Did anyone pick up the broadcast?"

I shook my head. "Nakamori shut it down before it reached anyone who mattered. The story's buried."

"Then we buried them for nothing."

"Not for nothing. The survivors are out. They're real. They can testify."

"To who? Who's going to believe seven people who don't remember their own names?"

She was right. I didn't want to admit it, but she was right.

"Then we find someone who will."

---

Chapter 24: The Journalist

Her name was Mira Chen. Investigative journalist for the Chasm Chronicle, one of the last independent news outlets in the city. She'd won awards for her coverage of corporate corruption, survived two assassination attempts, and kept a file on Nakamori that was thicker than my arm.

I found her in a bar in Sector 15, nursing a drink and watching the door.

"You're Voss," she said when I sat down.

"That bother you?"

"Depends why you're here."

"I have a story. The biggest you'll ever see. Nakamori has been growing human beings in vats, stealing their identities, and selling them to the highest bidder. I've got seven survivors in a safe house who can prove it."

Mira didn't blink. "I've heard rumors."

"The rumors are true."

"Proof?"

I slid a data chip across the table. "Full documentation. Facility schematics. Identity records. Biometric data on forty-seven originals still in stasis."

She picked up the chip, turned it over in her fingers.

"If this is real — "

"It's real."

" — then Nakamori will come after everyone involved. You. Me. The survivors."

"I know."

"Why are you giving this to me?"

"Because you're the only journalist in the city who isn't owned."

She looked at me for a long time. Then she pocketed the chip.

"I'll need to interview the survivors."

"I'll arrange it."

"And I'll need protection. If Nakamori finds out I'm working on this — "

"You'll have it."

She finished her drink. "Tomorrow. Noon. I'll bring a camera."

She stood and walked out, leaving me alone at the table with my chrome hand and the ache that never stopped.

---

Chapter 25: The Interview

Mira Chen showed up at Ruby's basement at exactly noon, carrying a camera that looked older than I was and a notepad covered in handwriting.

The survivors were ready. Yuki had dressed in clean clothes that Ruby had found somewhere. The others had done what they could to look human again.

Mira set up her camera in the corner. She didn't use lights — natural light only, she said. Made the subjects more comfortable.

"Start with your name," she said to Yuki.

"I don't know my name."

"Then tell me what you remember."

Yuki talked for an hour. About the garden. About the child's hand. About waking up in a tank with no memory of how she got there.

The others talked too. Fragments. Shards of lives that had been stolen.

Mira's face was unreadable as she recorded. But her hand shook when she wrote in her notepad.

After the interviews, she pulled me aside.

"This is bigger than I thought."

"How big?"

"The originals — the ones still in the facility — they're not just random people. I recognized one of the names. Takeshi Morimoto. He was a journalist. Disappeared thirty years ago. His investigation into Nakamori was buried after he vanished."

"He was taken because he was investigating them."

"Yes. And I think there are others. Lawyers. Activists. Whistleblowers. Nakamori didn't just steal people's identities — they targeted specific people who posed a threat."

"The perfect crime. They abduct their enemies, replace them with copies, and the copies live quiet lives while the originals are harvested for their memories."

"And no one ever knows."

Mira looked at the survivors, huddled together in Ruby's basement.

"How long do I have before the story breaks?"

"If Nakamori finds out you have this footage? Hours."

"Then I need to move fast."

She packed her camera and left.

---

Chapter 26: The Hunted

The first bullet came through Ruby's window at 2 AM.

I was asleep on the floor, wrapped in a coat, dreaming of chrome hands and green water. The glass shattered, and I was already moving, dragging the nearest survivor to the ground.

"Stay down!"

Ruby was at her console, fingers flying across the keyboard. "Nakamori security. They triangulated through the jamming signal. They're two blocks out."

"How many?"

"Twelve. Maybe more."

I looked at the survivors. Seven people who couldn't fight, couldn't run, couldn't even remember their own names.

"Ruby, is there a way out?"

"Basement drain. Leads to the old sewer system. It'll be tight, but they can make it."

"Lead them. I'll hold the front."

"Voss — "

"Go."

Ruby grabbed Yuki's hand. "Follow me. Stay low. Don't make a sound."

They disappeared through a hatch in the floor. I turned to face the door.

The second bullet came through the wall. Then the third. Then the door exploded inward.

I fired blind, three shots, heard someone grunt. Then I was moving, chrome hand extended, looking for targets.

Four men came through the door. I hit the first one in the chest, the second in the throat, dodged a knife from the third and drove my chrome fist through his ribs.

The fourth one had a shotgun.

He fired.

I twisted, took the blast in my left shoulder. The chrome arm tore free, sparks flying, wires exposed. I hit the ground hard.

The gunman advanced, raising the shotgun for a final shot.

A shot rang out. The gunman dropped.

Behind him stood Aiko, Sol's surgical pistol still smoking in her hand. Her bandages were red with fresh blood.

"You're welcome," she said.

Then she collapsed.

---

Chapter 27: The Arm

I woke up in a different basement. Same smell of damp concrete, same dim light, but different walls. Different faces.

Ruby was there. Aiko was there. The survivors were huddled in the corner.

"Where's the arm?" I asked.

"Gone," Ruby said. "The shotgun shredded it. I had to cut the remaining connections to stop the bleeding."

I looked at my left shoulder. Where the chrome arm had been, there was bandaged stump.

"I'm going to miss that ache."

"You're going to miss a lot more if we don't get you to another surgeon. The wound is contaminated. You need antibiotics and a proper seal."

"Sol?"

"Too far. And Nakamori will have his clinic under surveillance."

"So I'm going to bleed out in a basement?"

Aiko moved to my side. Her face was pale from her own surgery, but she was on her feet.

"No. You're not."

"You're in no shape to — "

"Shut up, Voss."

She turned to Ruby. "Where's the nearest black-market clinic?"

"Two sectors over. But it's run by a former Nakamori surgeon. He might report us."

"Then we don't give him the choice." She looked at me. "I'm getting you fixed. After everything you've done — "

"I haven't done anything."

"You got us out. You got them out." She nodded at the survivors. "Now it's my turn to get you out."

She pulled me to my feet. The world spun.

"Ruby, keep the survivors safe. If Mira's story breaks, Nakamori will be too busy to hunt them. Until then, stay dark."

"Where are you going?"

"The only place Nakamori won't look."

She slung my good arm over her shoulder and led me out into the dark.

---

Chapter 28: The Edge

The clinic was in the Neutral Zone, the strip of land between Nakamori territory and the rival Shindo Corporation's enclave. Neither corp claimed it. Both patrolled it. And people who went there at night usually didn't come back.

Aiko half-carried me through the empty streets, past burned-out buildings and shuttered shops. The blood from my shoulder had soaked through the bandage and was dripping down my side.

"You're going to die if we don't hurry," she said.

"Then hurry."

The clinic was a converted shipping container with an LED sign that read *MEDICAL* in five languages. The door was unlocked.

Inside, a man in his sixties sat behind a desk, reading a paper book. He looked up when we entered, took in my condition, and sighed.

"Another one."

"Can you help him?" Aiko asked.

"I can. It's going to cost."

"Whatever he asks," I said.

The surgeon — if that's what he was — stood up and gestured to a table. "Sit. Let me see the damage."

He unwrapped my shoulder and whistled. "The chrome interface is shot. The nerve connections are exposed. Whoever did the original installation did shoddy work — I can see corrosion at the bone anchor."

"It's two years old."

"Old enough to be unsafe." He prodded the wound. "I can clean it, seal it, and fit you with a temporary prosthetic. But it won't be as good as the original."

"What kind of prosthetic?"

"Military surplus. Old Shindo model. No nerve interface — it reads muscle movement through external sensors. Clunky, but functional."

"Do it."

He worked for two hours. Aiko sat in the corner, watching the door, Sol's pistol in her hand.

When he was done, I had a new arm. Black, matte, with visible actuators and a hand that looked more like a claw than fingers.

"Not pretty," the surgeon said. "But it'll work until you can afford better."

"How much do I owe you?"

"Nothing. I knew the man who did your original installation. He was a butcher. Consider this a correction."

He walked us to the door. "There's a Shindo patrol coming this way. You should go out the back."

We went.

---

Chapter 29: The Broadcast

Mira's story broke at dawn.

We watched it on a stolen screen in an abandoned building — Aiko, me, Ruby, the seven survivors. The footage was raw. Yuki's face filled the frame as she talked about the garden she remembered, the child whose name she couldn't recall.

Mira's voiceover laid out the facts: Nakamori Group had been running an illegal vat-grown human program for thirty years. They'd abducted at least forty-seven people and replaced them with copies. They were harvesting memories from living human beings and selling them as products.

The broadcast went to every independent outlet in the city. Within an hour, it had been picked up by international news. Within three hours, the corporate enclaves were in lockdown.

"She did it," Aiko whispered. "She actually did it."

"This is just the beginning," I said. "Nakamori will deny everything. They'll say the footage is fabricated. They'll bury it in legal challenges for years."

"But people will know."

"Some people. Not enough."

Yuki looked at me. Her eyes were clearer than they'd been since we pulled her from the tank.

"Then we need to give them more."

"What do you mean?"

"There are still forty people in that facility. If we go back — if we free them all — no one can deny it."

"That's suicide. Nakamori will have the facility locked down."

"Then we find another way."

She stood up. The others stood with her.

"We're not hiding anymore," she said. "We've been hidden our whole lives. We're done."

I looked at my new arm. The claw. The matte black metal.

"Then let's finish this."

---

Chapter 30: The Return

The facility was different this time. The rusted fences had been replaced. The parking lot was full of Nakamori security vehicles. Armed guards patrolled the perimeter.

"They know we're coming," Aiko said.

"Of course they do. They've got forty-seven reasons to expect us."

We were in a building across the road, watching through cracked blinds. Ruby had rigged a jammer that would disable their external communications for exactly twelve minutes.

"Twelve minutes," I said. "That's all we have."

"It's enough," Kaito said.

He'd joined us. I hadn't asked him to. He'd just appeared, carrying a bag of tools and a look of grim determination.

"There's a secondary access point," he said. "Old maintenance shaft that feeds directly into the stasis chamber. They don't know about it — it wasn't on the official schematics."

"How do you know?"

"Because I found it the last time I was here. I didn't use it because I wanted to see the originals first."

"And now?"

"Now I've seen enough."

We moved at dusk. The jammer went active. The perimeter lights flickered and died.

Kaito led us through a drainage ditch, under a collapsed fence, into a tunnel that hadn't been used in decades. The air was thick with dust and the bones of small animals.

The shaft opened into the stasis chamber. The tanks were still there. The green light was still pulsing.

But the room was empty of guards.

"Why aren't they here?" Aiko whispered.

"Because they're all at the main entrance," I said. "Waiting for us to come the obvious way."

Kaito moved to the console. "I'm starting the revival sequence. All remaining tanks. It'll take ten minutes."

"Make it five."

The tanks began to drain. The green solution receded. Bodies stirred, coughed, opened their eyes.

Forty people, waking from a thirty-year dream.

The alarms started at minute eight.

"Go," I said. "Get them out. I'll hold the door."

Aiko grabbed my arm. "Voss — "

"Go."

She went.

I turned to face the door, my claw hand extended, and waited for Nakamori to come.

END OF ARC III

ARC IV: THE TRIAL OF MEMORY

---

Chapter 31: The Aftermath

They came through the door eight seconds after the alarms. I counted.

Eight seconds was enough time to pick up a fallen guard's rifle, kick over a table for cover, and sight the first target.

I put three rounds through the door before they got it open. One hit the lead guard in the thigh. The other two hit the wall behind him as he dropped.

Then they were through — four, five, six men — and I was firing, rolling, using the consoles and tanks for cover. The stasis chamber became a war zone.

Behind me, Kaito worked feverishly at the main console. Forty revival sequences running in parallel. The tanks drained one after another, bodies emerging from the green solution like the newly born.

"We need more time!" he shouted.

"You have exactly as much time as I can give you!"

I fired again. Another guard went down. But they had numbers and I had a borrowed rifle and one good arm.

A grenade clattered across the floor.

I grabbed it, threw it back.

The explosion took out the door and the man behind it.

Silence. The ringing in my ears was deafening.

"Go," I said. "Now."

The survivors were moving — forty people in various states of confusion and terror, being guided by Yuki and the others from the first batch. They moved through the maintenance shaft like a river of ghosts.

Aiko appeared at my side. "You're coming."

"Right behind you."

She didn't believe me. I could see it in her eyes.

"Voss — "

"I said I'm coming."

The shaft swallowed us. Darkness. The sound of forty people breathing, coughing, crying.

Behind us, the alarms continued to scream.

---

Chapter 32: The Cost

The Dead Zone was overflowing.

Forty-seven survivors. Seven from the first rescue, forty from the second. They filled every basement, every warehouse, every abandoned building in the sector. Ruby's data brokerage became a triage center.

Sol had been smuggled in. He was working around the clock, treating malnutrition, muscle atrophy, psychological trauma. The survivors had been in stasis for an average of twenty-four years. Their bodies had forgotten how to function.

"This one needs dialysis," he said, pointing to a man in his fifties. "This one needs surgery for a untreated fracture that healed wrong. This one — " He stopped at a woman who sat motionless, staring at nothing. "This one may never come back."

"The ones who do — will they remember anything?"

"Not for a while. Maybe never. The memory extraction process damages the hippocampus over time. Some of them will recover fragments. Others will be blank slates."

"Blank slates with thirty-year gaps in their lives."

"Worse. Blank slates with no memories of who they were. Their families have moved on. Their jobs are gone. Their children are adults who don't recognize them."

I looked around the room. Forty-seven people who had been stolen from the world and were now being returned to it, broken and empty.

"What do we do with them?"

"Find their families. Reunite who we can. Give the rest new identities and a chance to start over."

"It's not enough."

"No. It never is."

---

Chapter 33: The Witness

Mira Chen had been hiding in the Zone since her broadcast went live. Nakamori had put a bounty on her head — five million for information leading to her capture.

She didn't seem afraid. If anything, the danger had sharpened her.

"The international press is picking up the story," she said. "Three major networks are running segments tonight. The UN Human Rights Council has announced an investigation."

"Will it matter?"

"Eventually. But Nakamori isn't waiting. They've already started a media campaign — calling the survivors 'illegal bioweapons' and me 'a foreign agent.' They're trying to muddy the story."

"It's working?"

"With some people. Not with the ones who matter."

She handed me a tablet. The screen showed a man in his early forties, well-dressed, standing in front of a courthouse.

"Who's this?"

"Takeshi Morimoto's son. The journalist who was abducted thirty years ago. He's been searching for his father his entire adult life."

"He knows his father is alive?"

"He knows now. He watched the broadcast. He recognized his father's face — even after thirty years."

"What does he want?"

"To testify. To speak for his father and the others whose families have been waiting."

I looked at the survivors in the room. The blank faces. The empty eyes.

"Can he?"

"He's already contacted a human rights lawyer. They're filing a class-action suit against Nakamori. They want survivors to testify. They want evidence. They want justice."

"Justice." The word felt foreign in my mouth.

"Voss. This is what we fought for."

I looked at my claw hand. The matte black metal. The arm that had replaced the arm that had replaced the one I was born with.

"I didn't fight for anything. I took a case."

"Took a case. Broke into a corporate facility. Freed forty-seven prisoners. Got shot, lost an arm, killed who knows how many corporate security officers. That's not 'taking a case.'"

"It was supposed to be a missing person."

Mira laughed. "It never is."

---

Chapter 34: The Deposition

The lawyer's name was Sato. Not related to the M. Sato designation — a coincidence, she said, though I didn't believe in coincidences anymore.

She was young, sharp, with the kind of eyes that had seen too much injustice to be surprised by any of it.

"Mr. Voss. I need you to testify."

"About what?"

"About everything you've seen. The facility. The tanks. The survivors. The corporate cover-up."

"I'm not a reliable witness. I'm a PI with a criminal record and a vendetta."

"Exactly. You're the perfect witness. Your testimony is corroborated by physical evidence and the testimony of forty-seven victims. And your background makes you immune to character attacks — you've already been discredited by Nakamori once."

She was talking about the explosion. The dock. The settlement.

"They'll say I'm bitter. Seeking revenge."

"Of course they will. But it won't matter, because the truth is on your side."

I looked at my claw hand. "I don't even know what the truth is anymore."

Sato leaned forward. "The truth is that Nakamori has been committing crimes against humanity for thirty years. The truth is that forty-seven people have been tortured, their identities stolen, their lives erased. The truth is that you found them. You freed them. You're the only person who can tell the whole story."

"Dr. Hadashi. She's the one who ran the program."

"She's already been arrested. Internal Nakamori purge — they're trying to distance themselves from the scandal."

"She'll never testify."

"She doesn't have to. We have enough evidence to bury them."

"Then you don't need me."

Sato sat back. "I need you because the trial isn't about evidence. It's about narrative. The jury needs to hear from someone who saw it with their own eyes. Someone who fought. Someone who lost something."

She looked at my arm.

"You're that someone, Mr. Voss."

---

Chapter 35: The Preparation

The trial was set for three months out. The courthouse was in neutral territory — a building that had been designated as international jurisdiction after the Treaty of '72. No corporate security could enter without permission.

The survivors were given housing in a protected compound. Medical care. Counseling. The basics of a life that had been denied them for decades.

I spent my days in a small room in the Zone, reviewing testimony, going over evidence, trying to make sense of everything I'd seen.

Aiko visited often. She'd recovered from her surgery and had become something like a liaison between the survivors and the legal team.

"They're getting better," she said one evening. "Some of them. Yuki remembered her daughter's name."

"Yeah?"

"Akiko. She woke up one morning and said it. Just the name. But it's a start."

"That's good."

"Yuki asked about you."

"Me? Why?"

"She said you looked like someone who needed to remember something important."

I didn't know what to say to that.

"She's not wrong," Aiko said. "You've been different since the arm. More distant."

"I lost an arm. I'm allowed to be distant."

"You lost a chrome arm that was slowly killing you. You're healthier now than you've been in years."

I flexed my claw hand. The external sensors tracked the movement. It felt like I was wearing a glove that didn't fit.

"It's not about the arm."

"Then what is it?"

I looked at the window. The city glowed beyond the glass, its neon lights a thousand cuts in the dark.

"When I started this case, I knew what I was. A broken PI with a chrome arm and no future. Now I don't know what I am."

"You're the same person."

"Am I? I've killed people. I've broken into corporate facilities. I've helped free forty-seven prisoners. That's not who I was."

"It's who you became."

I turned to face her. "And what happens after the trial? After Nakamori is convicted? What happens to me?"

"Whatever you want."

"I don't know what I want."

Aiko was quiet for a moment. Then she said: "That's okay. You don't have to know right now."

She left me alone with the neon and the dark.

---

Chapter 36: The Offer

Three days before the trial, I got a visitor.

He came to my room in the Zone, unannounced, in a suit that cost more than I'd made in the last five years. He was old — seventy, maybe older — with the weathered face of someone who'd spent a lifetime in corporate politics.

"You're Dex Voss," he said.

"That's right."

"My name is Kenji Nakamori."

I stood up slowly.

"I'm not here to threaten you," he said. "I'm here to offer you a deal."

"I'm listening."

"The program was illegal. I admit that. I didn't know about it — not in detail. But I was the CEO when it started, so the responsibility is mine."

"So you're here to confess?"

"I'm here to offer a settlement. Full reparations to the survivors. A public acknowledgment of wrongdoing. Resignation of all executives involved."

"And in exchange?"

"You drop the criminal case. You let Nakamori handle the punishment internally."

"Internal punishment. Like firing the people responsible? Give them golden parachutes and let them retire in luxury?"

"If that's what you think — "

"That's what I know. I've seen how corporate justice works. Someone takes the fall. Everyone else walks."

Nakamori sat down across from me. He looked tired. Old. Human.

"I'm dying," he said. "Cancer. Six months, maybe less. I wanted to fix this before I go."

"By covering it up?"

"By making it right. The survivors get everything. Money. Medical care. New identities. A chance at a life."

"And the ones who ran the program? Dr. Hadashi? The security chief who sent men to kill me?"

"They'll be prosecuted. I'll make sure of it."

"Internal prosecution. Corporate justice."

He didn't answer.

I looked at my claw hand. "The trial starts in three days. I'll testify. The world will know what Nakamori did. And then the courts will decide."

"You're making a mistake."

"Probably. I make a lot of them."

"I've made a lot of mistakes. This isn't one of them."

Kenji Nakamori stood up. He walked to the door, then stopped.

"I didn't know," he said. "About the program. About the vat-born. I didn't know."

"Does that matter?"

"I don't know." He left.

I sat alone in the dark, thinking about the difference between ignorance and innocence.

---

Chapter 37: The Testimony

The courthouse was a fortress. International guards, metal detectors, biometric screening. Every journalist in the city was outside, cameras pointed at the doors like weapons.

I wore a suit that Aiko had bought for me. It was the first suit I'd worn since my Nakamori days. It didn't fit well.

The courtroom was full. The survivors sat in the front rows, dressed in clothes that looked new but felt foreign to them. Yuki was there, holding the hand of a woman who might be her daughter — the DNA test was still pending.

Sato sat at the plaintiff's table, surrounded by stacks of evidence.

And at the defense table sat Dr. Hadashi, in handcuffs, her face blank.

I took the stand.

"Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?"

"I do."

Sato stood up. "Mr. Voss. Tell us how you came to be involved in this case."

I told them. From the beginning. Aiko's visit to my office. The empty apartment. The clinic. The facility. The tanks. The bodies floating in green light.

I told them about Kaito. About the vat-born. About the woman who never existed.

I told them about the people who tried to kill me, the arm I lost, the survivors I helped free.

When I finished, the courtroom was silent.

Sato approached the witness stand. "Mr. Voss. You've been described by Nakamori's legal team as a biased witness — a disgruntled former employee seeking revenge. How do you respond?"

I looked at Dr. Hadashi.

"Everything I've said can be verified. The facility still exists. The evidence is there. The survivors are sitting in this room. If Nakamori wants to discredit me, they can explain why they've been growing human beings in vats for thirty years."

"Objection," the defense lawyer said. "Argumentative."

"Sustained."

But the damage was done. I saw it in the jury's eyes.

---

Chapter 38: The Verdict

The trial lasted six weeks.

Dr. Hadashi testified. Under immunity, she explained the program in clinical detail — the extraction process, the memory implantation, the vat-grown bodies. She spoke like she was describing a manufacturing process, which, to her, it was.

The survivors testified. Some could barely speak. Others found their voices in the courtroom, telling stories of gardens and children and lives that had been taken from them.

The jury deliberated for three days.

I spent those days in my room, staring at the wall, waiting.

Aiko brought food I didn't eat. Ruby sent messages I didn't read.

On the third day, the verdict came.

Guilty on all counts.

The courtroom erupted. The survivors cried. Yuki's daughter — confirmed by DNA — held her mother and wept.

I sat in the back row, watching.

Kenji Nakamori had died two weeks into the trial. Cancer. He never saw the verdict.

Dr. Hadashi was sentenced to life in prison. The other executives received sentences ranging from ten to thirty years.

The Nakamori Group was dissolved. Its assets were seized and used to fund a reparations trust for the survivors.

Justice, they called it.

I called it a beginning.

---

Chapter 39: The Aftermath

The city didn't change.

The corporate enclaves still rose above the smog. The Chasm still drowned in neon and rain. The rich still lived in their towers, and the poor still died in the streets.

But something had shifted. Nakamori's fall had created a power vacuum. The other corps were scrambling to fill it, jockeying for position, absorbing Nakamori's assets like vultures on a corpse.

And somewhere in the chaos, the survivors were trying to rebuild their lives.

I visited them in the compound. Yuki was learning to walk again — her muscles had been weak from decades of disuse, but physical therapy was bringing her back. She remembered more every day.

"I remember the cherry blossoms now," she said. "And the sound of my daughter laughing. I remember her name."

"Akiko."

"Yes." She smiled. "Thank you."

"For what?"

"For finding me. For bringing me back."

"I didn't do much."

"You did everything."

I didn't know how to answer that.

Kaito had stayed in the compound too. He'd become something like a counselor to the other survivors — he understood what they were going through in a way no one else could.

"You came back for me," he said when I found him. "When I was just a case file. A missing person. You came back."

"It was my job."

"No. It was more than that." He looked at me with those hollow eyes that had seen too much. "You saw a person where there was supposed to be nothing. You saw someone worth saving."

"You saw the same in them."

"I saw what I was. What I am." He paused. "The other copies — the ones who replaced the originals. They're out there, still living their lives, still believing they're real. What happens to them now?"

I didn't have an answer.

"They're people too," he said. "They didn't choose to be born. They didn't choose to have someone else's memories. They're as much victims as the originals."

"What do you want to do about it?"

Kaito looked at his hands. "I want to find them. All of them. I want to tell them the truth."

"And if they don't want to hear it?"

"Then I leave them alone. But they deserve to know."

---

Chapter 40: The Road

I left the compound at midnight.

The city was quiet. The rain had stopped, leaving the streets slick with reflected neon. I walked without destination, my claw hand at my side, the ache in my shoulder a familiar companion.

Aiko found me on a bridge overlooking the Chasm.

"I thought you might be here."

"It's where I end up."

She stood beside me, watching the lights below.

"What now?" she asked.

"I don't know. Go back to PI work, I guess. There's always someone who needs finding."

"Is that what you want?"

"It's what I do."

The wind picked up, carrying the smell of rain and industry.

"Yuki asked me to thank you," Aiko said. "Properly. She wanted you to know that she's going to write a book. About everything. The tank, the trial, the years she lost."

"She should."

"Kaito is going with her. He wants to find the other copies."

"And you?"

Aiko was quiet for a long moment.

"I'm going back to work. Corporate law. But not for a corp. There's a firm in the Zone that's taking cases against the enclaves. Human rights violations. Environmental crimes. They need someone who knows how the system works from the inside."

"Good."

She turned to face me. "What about you, Dex? What are you going to do?"

I looked at my claw hand. The matte black metal that had replaced the chrome that had replaced my flesh.

"I'm going to keep going."

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only one I have."

She nodded. "I guess that's enough."

She walked away, her footsteps fading into the city noise.

I stayed on the bridge, watching the lights, waiting for the sun to rise.

---

END OF ARC IV

ARC V: A THOUSAND CUTS

---

Chapter 41: The Return

Six months after the trial, the door chimed.

Same sound. Same office. Same rain streaking the window.

I looked up from my desk, where I'd been staring at a case file I couldn't focus on. The new arm — a proper chrome replacement, paid for by the survivors' trust fund — hummed quietly as I flexed the fingers.

"Come in."

The woman who entered was not Aiko. She was younger, early twenties, with nervous eyes and clothes that had been expensive five years ago.

"Mr. Voss? My name is Emi Sato."

"M. Sato?"

She blinked. "No. Emi. My name is Emi."

I gestured to the chair. "Have a seat."

She sat, hands clasped in her lap. "I've heard what you did. For the survivors of the Nakamori program."

"That was six months ago."

"My mother was one of them."

I sat up straighter. "Which one?"

"Model 7-C. That's what they called her. But her name was Hanako. Hanako Sato."

The photo. M. Sato. The woman in Kaito's calendar.

"I found a picture of her," I said. "Hidden in a missing person's apartment."

"That was my mother. She was taken when I was three years old. They replaced her with a copy. I grew up with the copy. I never knew."

"How did you find out?"

"When the trial happened. I saw the coverage. I saw the photo of the woman in the tank." Her voice cracked. "I recognized her. She was older, thinner, but it was her. It was my mother."

"Have you met her?"

Emi shook her head. "I tried. The compound won't let me in without authorization. They say she's not ready. They say the trauma is too deep."

"I can help with that."

"You can?"

"I know some people. But that's not why you're here."

Emi looked down. "No."

"What do you need?"

"My father. The copy — the one who raised me — he disappeared. After the trial. He found out what he was. He couldn't handle it."

"He ran?"

"He left a note. Said he was going to find the original. To apologize."

"That was six months ago."

"He's been sending messages. Coded. He's found something. A new program. A new facility. He said it was bigger than Nakamori."

I felt the familiar prickle at the back of my neck.

"Where is he?"

"That's the problem. His last message came from a dead relay. He said he was going to expose them. Then nothing."

"And you want me to find him."

"I want you to finish what you started."

I looked at my chrome hand. The new one. The one that didn't ache.

"Give me the messages."

She handed me a data chip.

"Mr. Voss — be careful. He said they were watching. He said they were everywhere."

"Who?"

"He didn't say. But he said the name. The same one that was on my mother's file."

"M. Sato. It's not a person."

"No." She looked at me with eyes that had seen too much. "It's something else. Something dark."

I studied her face. She had her mother's jawline — I could see it now, comparing her to the photo still pinned to my board. Model 7-C. A designation, not a name.

"Your mother. Hanako. She's been out of the compound for two months. She asks about you."

Emi's face crumpled. "I know. I've been too scared to visit."

"She doesn't remember you. But she knows she had a daughter. She feels the absence."

"How do you live with that?"

I looked at my chrome hand. "You learn to carry it."

She thanked me and left.

I sat alone, thinking about phantom limbs and missing daughters and the weight of lives unlived.

---

Chapter 42: The Message

The coded messages led to the dead relay, but I was able to trace its origin. A terminal in Sector 31 — a corporate black zone, unincorporated, unmonitored.

I went alone.

The terminal was in a data haven, a building that served as neutral ground for information brokers, off-grid journalists, and people who needed to communicate without corporate oversight.

The operator was a man named Grey, augments so extensive his face was more metal than flesh.

"Voss. Haven't seen you since the trial."

"You have a message for me."

"Maybe. Who's asking?"

"Emi Sato. Her father."

Grey's expression didn't change. "The copy. Yeah, he was here. Looked bad. Thin. Scared."

"What did he want?"

"He wanted to send a message. But not through normal channels. He said it had to reach someone specific."

"Me?"

"No. Someone else. Someone who could finish what he started."

"Who?"

Grey leaned forward. "He said the name. Mariko. Does that mean anything to you?"

It didn't. But it felt important.

"What else did he say?"

"He said the program wasn't Nakamori's. It was older. Bigger. Nakamori was just a franchise. A local operation."

"Franchise?"

"He said the real program is global. Every major corp has one. They've been running them for decades. The vat-grown witnesses, the identity harvesting — it's all part of something bigger."

I thought about the tanks. The forty-seven originals. The copies living their stolen lives.

"Where did he go?"

"After the message? He went to find Mariko."

"Mariko who?"

Grey shook his head. "That's all I know. But Voss — watch your back. The people behind this aren't like Nakamori. They don't leave survivors."

I thanked Grey and walked out into the neon night.

---

Chapter 43: The Network

I spent the next week digging into the name Mariko.

The search led nowhere in official databases, but the underground networks had whispers. A woman named Mariko who'd worked for a dozen corps over forty years. A data broker who'd handled sensitive information for the highest bidders. A ghost who'd been erased from every official record but lived in the memories of those who'd crossed her path.

And she was still alive.

I found her in a medical clinic in the borderlands between two corporate territories — a neutral zone where the wounded of both sides went to heal.

She was old. Seventy, maybe older. Her body was a patchwork of chrome and scar tissue, the remnants of a life spent in the gray spaces between legality.

"You're Voss," she said when I found her. Her voice was a rasp, damaged by something — smoke, chemicals, time.

"You know me."

"I know what you did. The Nakamori facility. The trial."

"I'm looking for someone. A copy. He came to find you."

"The vat-born. Yes. He found me."

"Where is he?"

"Dead."

The word hit me like a punch.

"When?"

"Three weeks ago. He found me, told me what he knew, and then they found him. Corporate security. Clean team. They don't leave witnesses."

"You didn't warn him?"

"I didn't have to. He knew the risks. He came anyway."

"Why?"

Mariko looked at me with old, tired eyes. "Because he wanted to be real. He wanted to do something that mattered. And he did."

"The program. He said it was bigger than Nakamori."

"It is. Nakamori was a local franchise. The real program is global. Every major corporation has a bio-division that runs parallel to the official one. They grow people, harvest identities, and sell them to the highest bidder."

"Who runs it?"

"No one. Everyone. It's a network. A cartel. They share resources, technology, and buyers. And they protect each other."

"Why haven't they been exposed?"

"Because the people who know about it are either dead, bought, or part of it."

I sat down heavily. "Then what's the point? What's the point of any of this?"

Mariko smiled. It was not a happy smile. "That's what the vat-born asked me. He said: 'If the system is rigged, if the truth doesn't matter, then why fight?'"

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him that the truth always matters. Even when it doesn't change anything. Because the alternative is worse."

"Where do I find the network?"

"You don't. They find you."

"I was found six months ago. Someone's been feeding me information. Someone wanted me to expose Nakamori. Who?"

Mariko was quiet for a long time.

"There's a faction within the network that wants to bring it down. People who've seen what it does and want to stop it. They've been working in the shadows for years, feeding information to investigators, journalists, anyone who can hurt the program."

"They've been using me."

"They've been helping you. There's a difference."

"Who are they?"

"I can't tell you that. But I can tell you this — the same people who helped you take down Nakamori are watching the other corps. They're waiting for the right moment to strike."

"What are they waiting for?"

She looked at me. "Someone to lead them."

The word sat between us. Heavy. Impossible.

"Lead them where? To what?"

"To a war they can't win alone. But with you — with someone who's already proven they can take down a corp — they might have a chance."

"Mariko. I'm one man. Two arms, one of them chrome, none of them fully functional. I'm not a general."

"The best generals never planned to be. They saw a fight that needed fighting and stepped into it."

"What about you? You're still alive. You know more than I do."

"I'm old. I'm tired. And I'm dying. You're not."

I looked at my chrome hand. The city's lights reflected in the polished metal, broken into a thousand tiny stars.

"If I say no?"

"Then you walk away. No shame in that. You've done more than most. More than I ever did."

"And if I say yes?"

Mariko smiled. "Then you change the world."

---

Chapter 44: The Choice

I went back to my office and sat in the dark.

The case file for Emi Sato's father sat on my desk. Closed. Unsolved. Another missing person who would never be found.

But the real case — the one I'd stumbled into six months ago — was still open.

I thought about the network. The global program. The people being grown in vats, their identities harvested and sold. The families who would never know what happened to their loved ones.

I thought about Kaito. Yuki. The survivors who were trying to rebuild their lives.

I thought about my chrome hand. The new one. The one that didn't ache.

And I thought about what Mariko had said. *Someone to lead them.*

I was not a leader. I was a PI with a bad attitude and a history of making things worse. The idea of taking on a global conspiracy was insane.

But so was taking on Nakamori. And I'd done that.

The door chimed.

"Come in."

Aiko Tanaka walked in. She looked different — healthier, stronger. The corporate paleness had been replaced by something more alive.

"I heard you were back."

"Word travels."

"I also heard you're looking into something new."

"I'm thinking about it."

She sat down across from me. "You look like you've seen a ghost."

"Something like that."

"Tell me."

I told her. About Emi. About her father. About the network. About Mariko. About the global program that made Nakamori look like a local operation.

When I finished, Aiko was silent for a long time.

"What are you going to do?"

"I don't know."

"Yes, you do."

I looked at my chrome hand. The reflection of the city's lights glinted off the polished metal.

"If I go after this — if I go after the network — I'm not coming back. This isn't a case. It's a war."

"Then we fight."

"We?"

"You're not alone, Dex. You never were."

I looked at her. In the dim light of the office, she looked like something solid. Something real.

"Mariko said the network is everywhere. Every major corp. Every government. We can't fight that."

"Then we don't fight it all at once. We fight it one piece at a time. One corp. One facility. One survivor at a time."

"That could take a lifetime."

"I don't have anything else planned."

I almost smiled.

"Neither do I."

---

Chapter 45: The Gathering

Mariko died three days later. Natural causes, the clinic said. But I knew better.

The network had found her. Cleaned up the loose end.

But before she died, she'd sent me a package. A data chip. A list of names.

People inside the network who wanted to bring it down.

I contacted them one by one. Using dead relays. Encrypted channels. The kind of communication that left no trace.

Twelve of them agreed to meet.

They gathered in the basement of Ruby's data brokerage — the same basement where the first survivors had been hidden. Old friends and new allies.

Kaito came. There wasn't much of him left. He'd tracked the remaining copies, and some had accepted the truth, and some had tried to kill him. But he'd survived.

Aiko came. She'd left her human rights firm and was working full-time on dismantling the network.

Yuki came. She'd written her book. It had been published in twelve countries. She was ready to fight.

And twelve others. Former Nakamori employees. Whistleblowers. Survivors. People who had been touched by the program and wanted to see it end.

I stood at the front of the room, my chrome hand at my side.

"Mariko is dead. The network killed her. But before she died, she gave me something. A list. Names of people inside the network who want to burn it down from within."

I held up the data chip.

"I don't know who these people are. I don't know if they can be trusted. But I know they have access. Information. Resources. And I know they want the same thing we do."

I looked around the room. At the faces of the people who had survived.

"Nakamori was one facility. One program. There are dozens more. Hundreds, maybe. And they're still running. They're still growing people. They're still stealing lives."

"But we stopped one. We can stop another. And another. And another. One at a time. Until the whole system falls."

"If you want to walk away, walk away. No one will blame you. You've already given more than anyone should have to."

No one moved.

I looked at each of them. Yuki, who'd been in a tank for thirty years and now wrote books that moved nations. Kaito, who'd been born in a vat and become more human than the people who made him. Aiko, who'd started as a corporate lawyer and ended as a revolutionary. Ruby, who'd given up her anonymity to fight an enemy that could kill her with a single call.

"I won't lie to you," I said. "This will probably kill us. If not tomorrow, then next year. Or the year after. The network doesn't forgive. It doesn't forget. And it has more resources than we can imagine."

I paused.

"But we have something they don't. We have the truth. We have the faces of the people they've stolen. We have the stories that they've tried to erase. And we have each other."

I nodded. "Then let's get to work."

---

Chapter 46: The Plan

The twelve names on Mariko's list became our eyes inside the network.

They fed us information. Locations of facilities. Names of executives. Shipping manifests. Buyers. The whole ugly machinery of a global industry built on stolen lives.

We spent six months planning.

It was the longest six months of my life. Every day brought new risks, new threats, new faces of people who'd been stolen from their lives. Ruby built a command center in the basement, a wall of screens showing data streams from a dozen sources. She worked eighteen hours a day, barely sleeping, running on stimulants and stubbornness. "I didn't survive Nakamori to die tired," she said when I told her to rest.

Kaito mapped the network's structure — the connections between corps, the money trails, the patterns of abduction and replacement. He'd become something else in the months since the trial. The hollow man I'd met in the facility was gone. In his place was someone with a mission. "They made me to be a witness," he said. "I'm going to be the witness that destroys them."

Aiko coordinated with journalists, lawyers, human rights organizations. Building the legal framework for a coordinated strike. She was on the phone constantly, her voice hoarse from talking, her eyes ringed with dark circles. But she never stopped. "Every call is a thread," she said. "Pull enough threads and the whole thing unravels."

I planned the raids.

The first target was a Shindo facility in the eastern corporate zone. Smaller than Nakamori. Less security. A proving ground for our strategy.

We hit it at night. Twelve people. Three vehicles. A jammer that cut communications for exactly four minutes.

We freed twenty-two survivors. Planted explosives that destroyed the facility's infrastructure. And left behind evidence that would tie Shindo to the network.

The operation was clean. No casualties. No one captured.

We did it again three weeks later. A Mitsubishi-linked facility in the southern zone. Freed eighteen survivors. Planted evidence. Destroyed the infrastructure.

The network started to notice.

---

Chapter 47: The Response

The network hit back.

Our safe houses were raided. Our contacts started dying. The twelve names inside the network were whittled down to six.

We went underground. Abandoned the command center. Moved to a new location every forty-eight hours.

The tension was constant. The fear was a background hum that never stopped.

But we kept fighting.

A facility in the western zone. Freed fourteen.

A facility in the northern corporate territory. Freed thirty-one.

Each raid was harder than the last. The network was learning. Adapting. They'd started booby-trapping the tanks — if we tried to free the survivors, the facility would self-destruct.

We lost two people in the western raid. Good people. People who'd survived Nakamori and didn't deserve to die in another corporate hell.

But we kept going.

Because what else could we do? The network was still out there. The tanks were still full. The stolen lives were still being harvested and sold.

We fought because the alternative was worse.

---

Chapter 48: The Betrayal

The information came from inside the network — one of our remaining contacts, a woman who'd worked for Shindo for twenty years.

"There's a summit," she said. "All the major players. The heads of the network's bio-divisions. They're meeting in three weeks to discuss the threat you've become."

"Where?"

"A neutral location. A facility in international waters. An old oil platform converted into a conference center."

"That's a target we can't hit."

"You don't have to hit it. You just have to be there. The network is going to announce a new initiative. A new product line. You need to know what it is."

"We need more than information. We need to stop it."

"There's someone at the summit who can help. A defector. One of the original architects of the program."

"Why hasn't he come forward?"

"Because he's afraid. He knows what the network does to traitors."

"Who is he?"

"He calls himself Doctor Zero. He helped design the first vat-grown consciousness. He knows everything."

"Can you get us in?"

"I can get you credentials. After that, you're on your own."

I took the credentials.

The night before I left, I sat with Aiko on the roof of our current safe house. The city sprawled below us, a lattice of light and shadow. Somewhere out there, the network was meeting. Planning. Deciding the fate of thousands of people who didn't know they'd been born in tanks.

"Come back," Aiko said.

"Planning to."

"I mean it. Don't be a hero. Get the information, get out."

"Heroes don't get information. Heroes get dead. I'm just a PI with a bad arm."

She almost smiled. "You have two bad arms now."

"Progress."

We watched the city in silence. The hum of maglev trains. The distant drone of corporate air traffic. The sound of a billion lives being lived, most of them oblivious to the war being fought in their name.

"Mariko's people say the network has eyes everywhere," Aiko said. "They'll know you're coming."

"Then I'll have to be faster than their eyes."

"Don't be clever. Be alive."

I stood up. "I'll be both. That's my specialty."

She didn't laugh. She just looked at me with those eyes that had seen too much.

I went inside to pack.

---

Chapter 49: The Summit

The platform was three hours from the coast, a rusting structure that had been converted into a corporate retreat. The helipad was busy with incoming aircraft. The lights blazed against the dark water.

I went alone. The credentials got me past the initial security, but the internal checks were tighter. Biometric scanners at every door. I had to move fast.

Doctor Zero was in a conference room on the lower deck, surrounded by screens showing the network's global operations. He was old — older than Mariko had been — with eyes that had seen the birth of an industry and the corpses it had left behind.

"You're Voss," he said.

"Doctor Zero."

"That's not my real name, but it'll do."

"You helped build this. The vat program. The identity harvesting."

"I helped build the foundation. I didn't know what they'd build on top of it."

"That's what everyone says."

He smiled bitterly. "I know. I'm not asking for forgiveness."

"Then what are you asking for?"

"A chance to help you tear it down."

"Why now?"

"Because they're about to do something worse. The new initiative — they're going to start growing witnesses for court cases. Not just people who can't remember their real lives. People with manufactured memories. Perfect testimonies. The ability to convict anyone the network wants convicted."

"That's already happening."

"Not at scale. Not globally. The new program will produce thousands of witnesses a year. They'll be inserted into trials around the world. They'll end democracies. They'll end justice."

"Where are they going to build this?"

"The facility is already built. Underneath the platform. They're going to activate it at the summit."

"Then we need to destroy it."

Doctor Zero looked at me. "That's why I called you here."

He handed me a key card. "This will access the main control room. From there, you can trigger a self-destruct sequence. But you have fifteen minutes before the security system resets."

"Fifteen minutes."

"Make them count."

I took the card and walked out.

---

Chapter 50: The Thousandth Cut

The control room was at the heart of the platform, three levels underground. The corridors were empty — most of the summit attendees were in the main hall, listening to speeches about the future of their industry.

I used Doctor Zero's key card. The door slid open.

The room was a semicircle of consoles, each one controlling a different aspect of the facility. Life support. Power. Security. And the self-destruct.

I sat down at the main console and started the sequence.

Fifteen minutes.

The alarms started at minute twelve.

They'd found me. Of course they had. Doctor Zero was either dead or compromised. It didn't matter.

I kept working. The self-destruct sequence was complex — multiple failsafes, multiple authorization codes. I bypassed them one by one.

At minute eight, the first guards arrived.

I used the console's control panel as a weapon. Not elegant. Effective.

At minute six, two more.

I was running out of room. Running out of time.

At minute four, the final authorization code went through.

The self-destruct sequence was active.

I turned to face the door. A dozen security guards stood there, weapons raised.

"You can't stop it," I said. "It's already done."

The lead guard raised his gun.

And then the world exploded.

---

I woke up in the water.

The platform was a burning wreck behind me, flames reaching toward the sky. Debris floated on the surface, some of it still smoldering.

I was alive. I don't know how. Maybe the explosion had thrown me clear. Maybe someone had pulled me out. Maybe the network wanted me to survive, to spread the story, to prove that they couldn't be stopped.

I didn't care.

I floated on my back, staring at the stars, and let the current carry me.

---

Three months later, I was in my office when the door chimed.

Same sound. Same rain. Same city.

But different. All of it different.

Aiko stood in the doorway. She looked tired, but there was something new in her eyes. Something that hadn't been there before.

"They're still out there," she said. "The network. They're rebuilding."

"I know."

"Doctor Zero survived. He's in hiding. He wants to meet."

"I know."

"There's a facility in the western territory. New construction. They're already operational."

"I know."

She looked at me. "Then you know what we have to do."

I stood up from my desk. My chrome hand gleamed under the office light.

"Yeah. I know."

I grabbed my coat and followed her out the door.

The rain was still falling. The neon still blazed. The city was still a thousand cuts in chrome, each one a wound that never fully healed.

But we kept fighting.

Because that's what you do.

You find the missing. You free the imprisoned. You tear down the walls, one by one, until the light gets in.

And when the world tries to cut you down, you cut back.

A thousand times.

A thousand cuts.

Until the whole thing falls.

---

"I think I know what's coming next," I said as we reached the street.

Aiko looked at me. Steam rose from the pavement, catching the neon and turning it into something almost beautiful. "Another facility?"

"Another fight. Another war. It never ends."

"Does that bother you?"

I thought about it. About Kaito, who found purpose in helping other copies. About Yuki, who wrote a book and became a voice for the voiceless. About the survivors who rebuilt their lives from nothing.

"Used to," I said. "Now I think that's the point. The fight is the thing. Not winning. Just... being on the right side of it."

"That's surprisingly philosophical for a man with two chrome arms and a drinking problem."

"Don't tell anyone. I have a reputation."

We walked into the rain, toward the next fight, the next cut, the next chance to make the world a little less dark.

Somewhere, in a facility none of us knew about yet, someone was waiting to be found.

We were already on our way.
