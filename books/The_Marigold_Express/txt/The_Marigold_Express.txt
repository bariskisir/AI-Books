The Marigold Express

Barış Kısır


Chapter 1: The Platform at Pell

The Marigold Express stood at the platform at Pell breathing steam like a dragon that had been to finishing school, and Cornelius Vane regarded it the way a cat regards a canary that has not yet noticed the cat.

It was, everyone agreed, the most beautiful train on the continent. Eleven carriages of brass and lacquer the color of marigolds, each window framed in gilt, each step swept by a porter in a uniform that cost more than most people's funerals. For four days it would carry two hundred of the most expensive human beings alive from the coast at Pell to the capital, feeding them, flattering them, and rocking them to sleep, and at the end of it they would tell everyone they had taken the Marigold, which was the entire point.

Vane carried one small leather bag and wore one excellent suit and had, in his luggage, nothing whatsoever that could not be explained to a magistrate. This was a matter of professional pride. He believed that a gentleman should be able to be searched at any moment and come up smelling of cologne and innocence, and that anyone who needed to smuggle his tools aboard had already lost.

"Boarding, sir?" said the porter at the step.

"Reluctantly," said Vane. "I find departures so final." He produced his ticket, first class, compartment four, paid for in cash by a man who did not exist. "Tell me, is it true the Contessa della Vigna is aboard this season?"

The porter's face did the small proud thing that faces do when they have been told to keep a secret thrilling enough to share. "I really couldn't say, sir."

"Of course not," said Vane, and went up the step, and thought: so she is, and so is the Star, and so, judging by the gentleman pretending to read a newspaper upside down by the water fountain, am I not the only person who has read the same rumor.

He had come for the Saffron Star. Nearly everyone had. That was going to be the trouble.


Chapter 2: The Manifest

If you had been the sort of invisible, all-knowing observer who hovers helpfully over the openings of stories, and you had hovered over the boarding of the Marigold Express that afternoon, you would have seen the following people climb aboard and present themselves as something they were not.

You would have seen a tall, elegant man in a grey suit present himself as Mr. Cornelius Vane, importer of nothing, who was a thief.

You would have seen a magnificent older woman in a sable coat present herself as Mrs. Adelaide Frost, widow of the Pell shipping Frosts, who was a confidence artist of forty years' standing and had never been within a mile of a shipping Frost except to rob one.

You would have seen, on Mrs. Frost's arm, a bright-eyed young woman of perhaps twenty-four present herself as June, Mrs. Frost's niece, who was not her niece, was not named June this month according to the hotel registers of three cities, and was the best pickpocket on the train by a margin that would have offended the others had they known it.

You would have seen a nervous man in a conductor's uniform present himself as the conductor, which he genuinely was, and which was somehow the saddest disguise of all.

You would have seen a smiling man with very white teeth present himself as Mr. Sweet, traveling on business, whose business was money and the breaking of those who owed it.

And you would have seen, last of all, climbing aboard with a brown-paper parcel of sandwiches and an expression of profound ordinariness, a short, balding man in an unfashionable coat present himself as Inspector Hugo Pellam of the railway police, traveling in an official capacity — which was, uniquely among the passengers, exactly and only what he was.


Chapter 3: The Contessa and Her Star

The Contessa della Vigna did not board the Marigold Express. She arrived at it, the way weather arrives, with advance notice and a sense of occasion.

She came down the platform in a column of porters, three of them carrying trunks and a fourth carrying, on a velvet cushion, with the strained dignity of a man who has been told the cushion is worth more than he is, an antique steel strongbox the size of a hatbox. The Contessa herself wore violet and a hat that had opinions, and she paused at the foot of the carriage step to allow the platform to appreciate her, which, being a platform, it did.

"My darlings," she announced, to no one and everyone. "Be careful with that. It is only the most beautiful thing in the world." She laid one gloved hand on the strongbox as a duchess lays a hand on a hound. "The Saffron Star has crossed deserts and toppled a prince and survived three wars and one extremely tedious lawsuit, and it will not survive being dropped by you, Tomaso, so kindly hold the cushion as though you loved it."

From compartment four, through a gap in the curtain, Vane watched this performance with the appreciation of one professional for another and the unease of one professional who suspects another of overdoing it.

You did not, in Vane's experience, advertise the most valuable object on a train to two hundred strangers unless one of two things was true. Either you were a fool — and the Contessa's eyes, when they swept the platform, were not a fool's eyes; they were counting — or you wanted, very badly, for everyone aboard to know exactly where the Saffron Star was, and to think about it, and perhaps to do something about it.

"Now why," murmured Vane to the curtain, "would a woman want her diamond stolen?"

It was the first genuinely interesting question of the journey, and he was annoyed to have thought of it, because interesting questions, in his line, were how you ended up in prison.


Chapter 4: Vane Takes Inventory

Within the first hour, before the train had even cleared the Pell marshes, Cornelius Vane had identified three other professionals aboard, and he was not pleased about a single one of them.

The first was the woman in sable. He had watched Mrs. Adelaide Frost work a railway dining car in another country eight years ago, relieve a copper magnate of his signet ring, his confidence, and his good opinion of his own wife, all between the fish and the cheese, and depart at the next station beaming. She was very good. She was also, he noted, traveling with a girl, which was new, and which meant she had either acquired a daughter or an apprentice, and Adelaide Frost had never struck him as the maternal type.

The second was the porter with the careful hands and the eyes that priced everything. It took Vane most of the hour to place him, and when he did he nearly laughed: Barty Lune, forger and fence, who could paint a banknote you'd spend without blinking and copy a jewel you'd insure, dressed as a porter and serving tea. Barty did not work passenger trains for his health. Barty worked where there was something to copy.

The third he did not recognize, which troubled him more than the two he did. A short, dull man in a brown coat, sitting in second class with a parcel of sandwiches, writing in a small notebook. Vane watched him for ten minutes and could not decide what he was, which meant he was either nothing at all or something very dangerous, and Vane had lived as long as he had by assuming the latter until proven otherwise.

He sat back in compartment four and did the arithmetic. One diamond. One owner who wanted it stolen, for reasons unknown. At minimum two other thieves, one forger, and one unidentified man with a sandwich. Four days. One small train.

"It's going to be," Vane said aloud, to the marshes sliding past the window, "a complete circus."

He was righter than he knew, and he already thought he was very right.


Chapter 5: Mrs. Frost Holds Court

At dinner the first night Mrs. Adelaide Frost held court at the center table, which she had obtained by the simple expedient of telling the maître d' it was hers, and ran, for warm-up, a small and elegant con on a banker named Pursloe.

It was nothing serious. It was, June understood, watching from across the table with her chin on her hand, a stretch — the way a pianist runs scales. Adelaide had no designs on Pursloe's money tonight. She merely wished to remind herself that she could take it whenever she liked, and to establish, before the table, before the carriage, before the whole rolling gilded village of the train, that Mrs. Frost of the Pell shipping Frosts was rich, charming, slightly tragic, and entirely above suspicion. By the time the soup was cleared she had Pursloe lending her his fountain pen, his opinions, and his protective instincts, and had returned exactly the first of the three.

"You're not eating, dear," Adelaide said to June, in the gap while Pursloe fetched her a shawl she did not need.

"I'm watching you work."

"You should be working. There's a Mrs. Ellery two tables down with a brooch that's crying out for a better home."

"I don't want the brooch."

Adelaide's smile did not move, but something behind it sharpened. "We did not come a thousand miles to want nothing, June."

"We came for the Star. You said. The biggest job you'll ever pull, you said, and then we're done. That's what you said in Pell." June looked at her over the candle. "So I'm resting my hands for the Star, Aunt Adelaide. I'm being professional."

It was a good lie, smoothly delivered, and Adelaide, who had taught June every lie she knew, recognized the craftsmanship and was, briefly, proud. She did not notice — because even the very best are blind in the one place they refuse to look — that part of it was true: the girl was tired. Not of the work. Of the lying. There is a difference, and it had been growing in June for a year like a tooth, and tonight it ached.


Chapter 6: The Conductor's Arithmetic

Anton Reyes had been a conductor on the Marigold Express for nine years, and he loved the train the way you love a thing that is better than you and lets you stand near it anyway.

He knew every bolt of it. He knew which window in the observation car whistled in a crosswind and which step on the galley stair had a half-inch of give and exactly how long it took to walk the whole eleven carriages at a dignified pace (eleven minutes) and at a run (four, and you mustn't, the passengers don't like to see a conductor run, it suggests the train is on fire). He kept his uniform pressed and his manner calm and he had never in nine years taken so much as a sugar lump that was not his.

Tonight, in the cramped conductor's office between carriages six and seven, by the light of a single brass lamp, Anton did his arithmetic, and the arithmetic was killing him.

He had borrowed, eighteen months ago, for reasons that had seemed survivable at the time — a sister's illness, a doctor in the capital, a sum that grew teeth the moment it left the lender's hand. He had borrowed it from a man called Mr. Sweet, who smiled, and who was aboard, tonight, in compartment eleven, smiling. The debt as of Thursday would be more than Anton would earn in four years of pressing his uniform and not running.

On the desk before him lay the manifest, and on the manifest, in the careful copperplate he had written himself, was a line that had been glowing at him all evening like a coal: *Strongbox, the property of the Contessa della Vigna, to be held in the secure car, key retained by the owner.*

Anton was an honest man. He told himself so, firmly, in the brass lamplight. He was an honest man, and honest men did not think the thoughts he was thinking.

But honest men, it turned out, did the arithmetic anyway. They just hated themselves while they carried the one.


Chapter 7: A Porter Named Barty

Vane found Barty Lune in the narrow corridor of carriage seven at midnight, ostensibly polishing a brass rail, actually doing nothing at all, which is the hardest thing to catch a professional doing.

"Barty," said Vane pleasantly, leaning on the rail beside him. "You've got soft hands for a porter."

The forger did not startle, which Vane respected. He went on polishing a rail that was already a mirror. "Don't know what you mean, sir. Long shift, sir."

"Cornelius Vane. We were never introduced, but we attended the same auction in Vesper four years ago, the one where the genuine Brueghel and the fake Brueghel were both sold, to different buyers, in the same afternoon. I always wondered which of them you painted."

Barty's polishing slowed. He looked at Vane with the frank, weary recognition of one craftsman caught by another. "Both," he admitted. "The genuine one was the better forgery. Cost me a month." He set down his cloth. "What do you want, Mr. Vane?"

"I want to know what you're copying. A man of your gifts does not serve tea on the Marigold Express for the tips. Somebody has hired you to make a copy of something, on this train, this week, and there is precisely one thing on this train worth your month." Vane smiled. "How is the Saffron Star coming along, Barty?"

The forger was quiet a moment, and then he made the small decision that everyone aboard would eventually make, the decision to tell one true thing to one person in the hope of an advantage. "It's done," he said. "It's been done since Pell. Yellow paste, cut to the gram, in a setting I'd defy the Contessa's own jeweler to question across a table." He picked the cloth back up. "But here's the thing that's keeping me up nights, Mr. Vane, since you ask. I made the copy. I delivered the copy. And I have never once been allowed to see the original to copy it from. I worked from photographs and a wax press of the setting." He met Vane's eye. "Now you tell me. What kind of owner has a perfect fake made of a diamond, and won't let the forger touch the real one?"

"The kind," said Vane slowly, "who doesn't have the real one to touch."


Chapter 8: Inspector Pellam Packs a Lunch

Inspector Hugo Pellam had boarded the Marigold Express last, in second class, with a brown-paper parcel containing four sandwiches (cheese, cheese, ham, and a mystery he was looking forward to), because he did not trust the dining car to feed a man for the price of a small house, and because his wife had made them, and because a sandwich made by someone who loves you is, in Pellam's settled opinion, worth more than anything on a velvet cushion.

He was not a glamorous man. He knew this. He had made a long and useful peace with it. He was short, and balding, and his coat had been good in a decade now ended, and when he entered a room clever people's eyes slid off him as if he were a coat-stand, which clever people invariably came to regret.

What Inspector Pellam had, instead of glamour, was a notebook and a method and a record. The notebook was small and black and full. The method was simple: he wrote down everything, especially the boring things, because in twenty-six years he had learned that crimes are solved in the boring things, in the timetable and the laundry list and the precise number of minutes, while everyone clever is looking at the diamond. And the record was this: Inspector Hugo Pellam had never, not once, in twenty-six years, lost a case.

He had been sent aboard the Marigold Express because the railway company had received, as the railway company always did when the Contessa traveled, a quiet warning that something valuable would be aboard and a quieter request that a competent man keep an eye on it. Pellam did not yet know about the thieves. He did not know there were so many. He knew only that there was a famous diamond on his train, and that famous diamonds drew trouble the way his ham sandwich drew the attention of the small dog in seat 14C.

He opened his notebook to a clean page. He wrote the date. He wrote: *The Marigold Express. One diamond. Watch everyone.*

Then he ate the cheese sandwich, because the mystery one was for later, and watched everyone.


Chapter 9: Quill in Compartment Nine

Salvatore Quill was eighty-one years old and traveling to a wedding, and he had promised on his life that he would not, under any circumstances, open anything that was locked.

He had made the promise to his granddaughter Rosa, who was getting married in the capital on Saturday, and who had said to him, holding both his old spotted hands in her young ones, "Nonno. I know who you were. I love who you were. But you are coming to my wedding as my grandfather and not as the man in the newspapers, and you are going to dance with me, and you are not going to open one single safe between here and Saturday. Promise me." And he had promised, because she was the only good thing his long and locked life had ever produced, and because she was right.

So when, on the first night, there was a knock at compartment nine and Cornelius Vane let himself in without waiting for an answer and said, "Salvatore Quill. As I live and breathe. They told me you'd retired," Quill did not even open his eyes from where he sat with his hands folded on his stomach.

"I have retired," he said. "I am the most retired man on this train. I am so retired that I am going to a wedding, and I am going to eat cake, and I am not going to discuss whatever it is you have come to discuss." He opened one eye. "It's the diamond, isn't it."

"It's the diamond."

"It's always the diamond." He closed the eye again. "And I'll tell you for free, Mr. Vane, whoever you are, because you have a kind voice and I'm too old to be coy: there is exactly one strongbox of that pattern, the old Vesper double-throw, and there are exactly four men alive who can open it without the key, and three of them are dead, and the fourth made a promise to a girl in a white dress." He smiled with his eyes shut. "So you have a problem. Goodnight."

Vane left, thoughtfully. He had not come to recruit Quill. He had come to make certain no one else could either. And he had learned, instead, the most useful thing yet: that the Contessa's strongbox could not be opened on this train by anyone but the Contessa.

Which meant that whatever happened to the Saffron Star, the Contessa was going to have to be part of it.


Chapter 10: Henrietta Voss, Travel Writer

In the morning a woman introduced herself to everyone.

She did it well. She moved through the breakfast car like a pollinating bee, table to table, a card in her hand: *Henrietta Voss, Travel Correspondent.* She was writing a feature on the Marigold Express, she explained, the romance of the rails, the great vanishing art of the grand journey, and might she ask just a few questions, nothing intrusive, where are you bound, what brings you aboard, isn't the marmalade divine?

She asked Mrs. Frost, who told her a moving and entirely fictional story about a late husband and a pilgrimage of remembrance. She asked Vane, who told her the truth in a tone that made it sound like a lie, which is the safest way to tell the truth. She asked the Contessa, who told her about the Saffron Star for eleven uninterrupted minutes. She asked Anton the conductor how many bolts were in the train, and he told her (forty-one thousand, give or take, he'd never been able to make it come out the same twice), and she wrote it down, which no real travel writer would ever do.

That was the detail that Vane caught, watching from behind his coffee. A travel writer collects feelings and adjectives. This woman was collecting facts — names, destinations, the number of bolts, who sat where, who watched whom — and filing them with the brisk indifference of someone who would later need to prove things.

She was not writing a feature. She was building a case, or a claim, and there was only one kind of person who built careful written claims about exactly where a famous diamond was and who was near it.

"Insurance," Vane murmured into his cup, and felt the circus acquire one more ring.

For if Henrietta Voss was the insurer's eyes, then the Saffron Star was insured for a great deal of money, and a diamond insured for a great deal of money, owned by a woman who wanted everyone to know exactly where it was, copied in paste by a forger who had never seen the original — that was not a diamond waiting to be stolen.

That was a diamond waiting to be lost on purpose. And Cornelius Vane, who had come aboard to commit one clean crime, began to suspect he had walked into the middle of someone else's.


Chapter 11: The Lay of the Carriages

To understand what went wrong the first night, you must understand the train, so here is the train.

At the front, behind the marigold locomotive, ran the staff and baggage cars, and then the secure car, an armored carriage with a single barred door, in which the Contessa's strongbox now sat bolted to the floor under the eye of a railway guard named Foley who took his duty seriously and his flask more seriously still. Then came the dining car, all white linen and chandeliers that swayed; then the galley, hot and loud and the secret heart of the train; then the long observation lounge with its glass roof and its piano and its deep velvet chairs; then the sleeping carriages, first class forward, second class aft, compartments numbered like hotel rooms; and at the very back, the Contessa's private suite, two rooms and a brass bed and a view of everywhere the train had already been.

Cornelius Vane walked the length of it twice on the first day, once forward and once back, at a dignified pace, smiling at everyone, and built in his head the map a thief builds, which is not a map of rooms but a map of eyes — who could see what, from where, and when.

The strongbox was in the secure car, behind a barred door, behind a guard. But the strongbox, Quill had told him, could not be opened by anyone but the Contessa. Which meant the diamond would only ever leave the strongbox when the Contessa took it out herself — to wear it, to gloat over it, to show it at dinner. The diamond was not vulnerable in the safe. The diamond was vulnerable in the open, in the lounge, at the table, on the Contessa's own throat.

Which meant the job was not a burglary. It was a pickpocketing. A very large, very public, very well-lit pickpocketing of a woman who wanted to be robbed, surrounded by three other thieves who had presumably reached the same conclusion.

"Marvelous," said Vane, to the swaying chandelier. "After you, everyone."


Chapter 12: Three Plans for One Diamond

By the second evening three plans existed for the theft of the Saffron Star, and not one of their authors knew about the other two, which is the engine of all good farce and most bad history.

Cornelius Vane's plan was elegant. He would befriend the Contessa — half done already; she liked a handsome man who let her talk — and he would arrange to be near her on an evening she wore the Star, and at the right moment, with the right small distraction, the genuine diamond would leave her throat and a humble glass bead of Barty's secondary manufacture would take its place, and the Contessa would dance on for an hour adorned with nothing, and by the time she noticed, Vane would be a different name in a different city. Clean. Quiet. No fuss.

Mrs. Adelaide Frost's plan was bold. She did not intend to take the Star off the Contessa at all. She intended to make the Contessa give it to her — to run, over four days, a confidence so complete that the Contessa would beg Adelaide to mind the diamond for her, hand it over with both grateful hands, and thank her for the favor. Adelaide had done it with paintings and a racehorse and once, memorably, with a house. She thought a diamond would be restful by comparison.

Anton the conductor's plan was desperate. He had no craft and no nerve and no idea, really, only the manifest and the master keys to every door on the train and a debt with teeth. His plan, such as it was, amounted to: be near the secure car, sometime, somehow, when the box was open, and take the thing, and run the four-minute version of the eleven-carriage walk, and pray. It was a terrible plan. He knew it was a terrible plan. He lay in his narrow bunk and refined its terribleness and did not sleep.

Three plans. One diamond. And, threading among them all with a notebook and a sandwich, one inspector who had no plan at all, only the patient certainty that everyone else did.


Chapter 13: Soup and Surveillance

The formal dinner on the second night was, to the untrained eye, a glittering and harmonious occasion. To the trained eye it was a knife fight conducted entirely with soup spoons.

The Contessa wore the Saffron Star. Of course she did. It hung at her throat on a chain, a yellow diamond the size of a quail's egg, throwing little coins of gold light around the dining car every time she laughed, which was often and loudly and always at the apex of someone else's sentence. She had seated herself at the head of the long table like a sun, and the train's brightest passengers had arranged themselves around her in order of self-importance, and she beamed upon them all and watched, Vane noticed, the door.

Vane sat four places down on her right, being charming in measured doses. Mrs. Frost sat three places down on her left, being tragic and refined. June sat beside Adelaide with her quick hands in her lap and her eyes everywhere. Henrietta Voss sat at the far end with her travel-writer's smile and her insurer's attention, watching the diamond as though it might bolt. And in the corner, at the small table reserved for persons of no consequence, Inspector Pellam ate the dining car's soup with every sign of suspicion and wrote, between spoonfuls, in his notebook.

Every professional at that table had reached the same conclusion: the diamond was out of the box and on the Contessa, and an opportunity like this might not come twice. Every professional was waiting for the same thing — the moment, the gap, the distraction that always comes at a long dinner when somebody knocks over a glass or a course arrives or the train lurches over points.

What none of them had planned for was that all of them were waiting for it. So that when the distraction came — and on the Marigold Express, gliding through the dark, the distraction came at quarter past nine — it was not one thief who moved.

It was four.


Chapter 14: The Lights Go Out

At quarter past nine the lights went out.

Not flickered. Went out, the whole dining car at once, the chandeliers dying together as though the train had blinked, and the darkness that rushed in was total, the absolute moonless dark of a train crossing empty country, and into it the two hundred most expensive people on the continent made the sound that expensive people make in the dark, which is a sort of collective offended gasp, as if the night had been ordered medium and arrived rare.

"Stay in your seats!" called Anton's voice, admirably calm, from the door — Anton, who had not caused the blackout but had spent nine years drilling for it and could not help himself. "A momentary fault on the line, ladies and gentlemen, nothing to alarm, the lights will be restored directly —"

In the dining car, in the dark, in the eleven seconds before a steward got a storm-lantern lit, the following things happened more or less at once.

Cornelius Vane rose smoothly, palmed the glass bead from his cuff, and moved for the Contessa's chair.

Mrs. Adelaide Frost did not move at all, because Adelaide's plan did not require darkness and she was far too professional to grab in the dark like an amateur; she merely smiled and waited to see who would.

June moved, because Adelaide had hissed one word — "Go" — countermanding a year of restraint, and June's year of weariness lost, for eleven seconds, to a lifetime of training, and her quick hands went toward the warmth and the perfume at the head of the table.

Anton, by the door, abandoned nine years of honesty between one breath and the next and started forward too, toward the diamond, toward Thursday.

And Inspector Hugo Pellam, in the corner, did the single most effective thing anyone did in the dark that night. He stopped chewing, closed his eyes, which made no difference, and listened, and counted, and remembered.


Chapter 15: Two Hands on the Same Box

Here is what the darkness felt like from the inside, to the two who reached the Contessa first.

Cornelius Vane reached the head of the table by memory and touch, his left hand finding the back of the Contessa's chair, his right rising with the bead, his whole body moving through the choreography he had run a hundred times in a hundred dark rooms. His fingers found the chain at her nape. And found, already there, another hand.

A small hand. A quick one. A hand that, the instant it touched his, did not flinch or freeze but turned, caught his wrist, and pinched a nerve he had not known he had, so that his fingers opened and the bead dropped, and a voice no louder than a breath said directly into his ear, "Mine."

"Madam," breathed Vane, with as much dignity as a man can summon while being disarmed by a stranger in the dark, "I was here first."

"You were slower first."

They were, the two of them, jammed against the Contessa's chair in the pitch black, each with a hand at the diamond, neither willing to yield, the Contessa herself oblivious between them, fanning herself and complaining musically about the management. Somewhere a steward struck a match that would not catch. They had perhaps four seconds.

"Truce," whispered Vane. "Whoever has it when the light comes, keeps it tonight. We discuss terms after. Agreed?"

A fractional pause. "Agreed."

The match caught. The storm-lantern flared. And in the swelling yellow light Cornelius Vane and the young woman called June sprang apart from the Contessa's chair with the synchronized innocence of two cats beside a fallen vase, and the Contessa turned, and put her hand to her throat, and the Saffron Star was gone.

So was, Vane realized with a cold drop of the stomach, the chain. Someone had taken the whole thing, chain and stone together, and it had not been him, and from the matching flicker of fury on June's face an inch away, it had not been her either.


Chapter 16: The Star Is Gone

"Thief!" cried the Contessa, and fainted, but checked first that there was a banker behind her to faint onto, which there was, so the faint was a great success.

The dining car erupted. Two hundred people who had paid not to be inconvenienced discovered they were being inconvenienced and expressed themselves about it. The storm-lantern threw enormous wheeling shadows. A small dog, liberated by the chaos, ran the length of the table through the cheese. And into this, like a brown stone into a frothing pond, walked Inspector Hugo Pellam, who set his notebook on the Contessa's abandoned soup plate, raised one unremarkable hand, and said, in a voice that was not loud and yet somehow ended the noise:

"Nobody leaves this carriage."

They looked at him. The clever, expensive, criminal, and innocent alike, they looked at the short balding man in the decade-old coat, and clever people's eyes did the thing they always did, sliding off him toward someone who looked more important, and finding, in the chaos, no one, and sliding reluctantly back.

"Thank you," said Pellam, as if they had volunteered. "I am Inspector Hugo Pellam, railway police. A theft has occurred. The doors at both ends of this carriage are now closed and will remain closed. No one will leave, no one will use the convenience, no one will reach into a pocket without my seeing it, until I have spoken to each of you. It will be tedious. I am very good at tedious." He picked his notebook back up off the soup. "Now. The lights went out at fifteen minutes past nine. They were restored at fifteen minutes and eleven seconds past nine. I counted. In eleven seconds, somebody in this carriage removed a diamond from the neck of a fainting woman." He looked, mildly, around the car. "Eleven seconds is not very long. It is, in fact, just long enough for a professional, and nowhere near long enough for anyone else. Which means — and I do hope you'll forgive me, ladies and gentlemen — that the person I am looking for is almost certainly someone in this room who is much, much cleverer than they have been pretending to be."

In four separate seats, four separate hearts performed four separate and identical lurches.


Chapter 17: Everyone Is a Suspect

Inspector Pellam interviewed the dining car one passenger at a time, at the small corner table, by lantern light, with his sandwich parcel beside him for moral support, and it was a masterclass in the weaponization of boredom.

He did not accuse. He did not bluster. He asked dull questions in a dull voice and wrote the answers in his small black book, and the questions were so dull, so relentlessly procedural — where were you seated, who was to your left, when did you last see the diamond, did you move during the dark, how many steps to the door — that clever people, accustomed to outwitting suspicion, found nothing to outwit, and grew careless, and contradicted themselves in tiny ways that Pellam noted without comment and underlined.

"Mrs. Frost," he said, when her turn came. "You did not move during the blackout."

"I did not, Inspector. I was raised not to grope about in the dark." Adelaide was magnificent, all wounded gentility. "I sat exactly where I was and prayed for the lights."

"Yes," said Pellam, consulting his book. "Three separate persons place you in your seat the whole eleven seconds. You are, in fact, the only person at the head of the table with a perfect alibi." He looked up at her, mild as milk. "Isn't that interesting."

Adelaide's smile did not move. But June, two seats away, felt the small cold finger of it, because she had been raised by Adelaide and she knew what her aunt knew: that on a train full of guilty people, the only thing more suspicious than no alibi is a perfect one, and that Inspector Pellam had just, very gently, told Adelaide Frost he found her too clean.

He worked through them all. Vane he questioned for six minutes and learned nothing, which he wrote down as *learned nothing — suggestive.* June he questioned for four and watched her hands the entire time. Anton he barely questioned at all, because Anton was staff and Pellam's prejudice, like everyone's, had a blind spot exactly the shape of a uniform — a fact Pellam would later record, in his own hand, as the worst mistake of the case.


Chapter 18: June's Pocket

It was an hour before Pellam released them, one at a time, into the corridor, and June walked back toward the sleeping cars with her aunt's furious silence beside her and her own heart going like a hammer, because of the thing she had found, and could not explain, and dared not show.

The Saffron Star was in her pocket.

She had discovered it during the interview, her hand drifting to her coat the way a tongue drifts to a sore tooth, and her fingers had closed on a shape that should not have been there — the cold heavy egg of it, the chain coiled around it — and it had taken every hour of Adelaide's training not to react, to go on answering Pellam's dull questions while a fortune sat against her hip and her mind screamed.

She had not taken it. That was the thing. She had reached for it in the dark and felt Vane's hand and lost it to a third hand entirely, and then somehow, impossibly, it was in her pocket, and there was only one way it could have got there: someone had put it there. On purpose. In the dark and the crush of the released crowd, some hand as good as her own had slipped the hottest object on the continent into the coat of a stranger, to be carried through the inspector's net by someone who didn't know she was carrying it.

It was, June thought, with the cold professional admiration that ran in her like a second bloodstream, beautiful work. She had been made a mule. She had been chosen because she was good enough to keep a straight face and unlucky enough to be near, and now she had the Star, and the inspector had her name in his book, and somewhere on this train a person cleverer than she was waited patiently to take it back the instant it was safe.

"Aunt Adelaide," June whispered, in the dark between carriages. "We have a problem."

"We have several," said Adelaide. "Which one do you mean?"

June took her aunt's gloved hand and pressed it, for half a second, against the terrible weight in her pocket, and felt Adelaide go very still.

"Ah," said Mrs. Frost softly, after a moment. "That one."


Chapter 19: The Body in the Corridor

They did not have time to decide what to do about it, because that was when the steward found Mr. Sweet.

He was lying in the corridor of carriage eleven, half in and half out of his own compartment door, on his back, his white teeth showing, his eyes shut, and he was not moving, and the steward who found him did what stewards do, which is make a noise that brings other people, and within a minute the rear of the train was a knot of horrified faces and the cry went up that there had been a murder.

Inspector Pellam arrived at a fast walk — not a run; the passengers don't like to see authority run, it suggests the train is on fire — and knelt by Mr. Sweet and put two fingers to his neck and was quiet for a moment, and then said the thing that changed the temperature of the whole journey.

"He's alive."

A diamond is a thrilling thing to have stolen. It is, in the end, a bauble; its theft is a sport, a contest, almost a compliment to the owner's taste. But a man down in a corridor is a different order of event, and the passengers felt it, the way the train felt the long downhill grade beginning under it in the dark — a lean, a quickening, a sense of the stakes sliding from glittering to grave.

Pellam looked up from the body, and his mild face had changed. "This man has been drugged," he said. "Recently, and expertly. Within the last hour." He stood, brushing his knees. "Which is interesting, ladies and gentlemen, for the following reason. An hour ago I had a thief. A clever, harmless, professional thief, the sort that steals diamonds and hurts no one. Now I have a thief who has begun to put people to sleep." He looked around the ring of faces, and his gaze, for the first time, was not mild at all. "And in my experience, the distance between a man who drugs you and a man who does worse is shorter than any of you would like to believe. So I will ask you all to be very careful, from here to the capital, about whom you trust."


Chapter 20: Not Quite Dead

Mr. Sweet woke twenty minutes later in his compartment, with a headache like a slammed door and a fury that arrived before he was fully conscious, so that his first coherent act was to sit bolt upright and snarl a name.

"Reyes."

Inspector Pellam, sitting patiently at the bedside with his notebook, made a small interested note. "You believe the conductor did this to you."

Sweet's eyes focused. Behind the white teeth a fast and frightened intelligence reassembled itself and remembered, just in time, that it was talking to a policeman. The snarl smoothed into a smile. "Did I say Reyes? I was dreaming, Inspector. I dreamt I missed my stop." He touched his own temple gingerly. "I've no idea who did this. Some sneak-thief, I suppose, going through pockets in the confusion. Did they take much? My watch?" His hand went to his waistcoat, and Pellam watched him discover that his watch was present, his wallet was present, and a particular folded paper in his inner pocket — Pellam saw the relief of it cross the man's face — was also present. "No. Nothing taken. A misunderstanding."

"A man does not drug another man," said Pellam, "and then take nothing. That is not a robbery. That is a message, Mr. Sweet, or a precaution. Somebody wanted you asleep for an hour. I should very much like to know what they did not want you awake to see." He rose. "But you won't tell me, because you're a man with a folded paper he checks for before his own wallet, which means you're carrying something worse than money. I'll find out what. I always do." He paused at the door. "Get some rest. And do stop dreaming about the conductor. People might think you meant it."

When he had gone, Mr. Sweet lay back against the pillow, and the smile went off his face like a light, and he drew out the folded paper and looked at it — a list of names, and figures, and one name circled: *A. Reyes — DUE THURS.* — and he thought about who on this train would have wanted him unconscious tonight, and he came, as Pellam had, to the conductor, and unlike Pellam he intended to do something about it himself.


Chapter 21: Vane and June, Briefly Allied

Cornelius Vane found June at two in the morning in the observation lounge, where she had gone because she could not sleep with a fortune in her pocket and a cleverer thief somewhere on the train waiting to take it.

He sat down across from her uninvited, the glass roof black above them, the sleeping country rushing past unseen, and he said, without preamble, "You have it."

"I have nothing," said June.

"You have the Saffron Star in your left coat pocket, where you keep checking it isn't, which is how I know it is. I had my hand on it for half a second tonight, my dear, and then a third party took it from both of us, and that same third party then did something to it that I have been trying to reconstruct for three hours, and the only thing that makes sense is that they handed it off to a mule. You're the mule. Congratulations. It's a great compliment to your composure and a great insult to your luck."

June looked at him for a long moment, and decided — the way everyone aboard eventually decided — to tell one true thing to one person for one advantage. "Someone put it on me in the dark," she said. "I don't know who. And whoever it was is better than me, and I am very good." She leaned forward. "So either you put it there, Mr. Vane, in which case this is a charming bluff, or you didn't, in which case we have the same problem, which is that there is a third thief on this train who is better than both of us and we don't know who they are."

"I didn't put it there."

"Then we have a problem."

"We have several," said Vane, which made her laugh despite herself, because it was exactly what Adelaide had said. "Here is my proposal. Until we know who our cleverer friend is, you and I do not compete. We pool. You hold the stone, because frankly you're better at holding things than I am and the inspector isn't watching your pockets, he's watching mine. We find out who the third thief is. We find out why the Contessa wanted to be robbed. And then, when we understand the board, we have a civilized argument about who keeps the prize." He extended his hand across the little table. "Partners. Provisionally. With mutual distrust."

June looked at the hand. In her pocket the Star was very heavy and very cold. "Provisionally," she said, and shook it. "With mutual distrust."

It was, she would reflect much later, the most honest partnership of her entire dishonest life, precisely because neither of them trusted the other an inch, which meant neither of them was ever disappointed.


Chapter 22: The Contessa Weeps Convincingly

The Contessa della Vigna spent the morning after the theft being inconsolable in the most public possible locations, and Cornelius Vane spent it watching her, and what he saw confirmed every suspicion he had.

She wept in the dining car over breakfast, beautifully, into a lace handkerchief, while a rotating audience of sympathetic passengers patted her hands and assured her the diamond would be found. She wept in the observation lounge at eleven, where the acoustics were better. She granted Henrietta Voss a tearful interview about the Star's history and her late husband's love and the unbearable violation of the theft, and Vane, eavesdropping from behind a potted palm, noticed three things.

The first was that the Contessa never once asked whether the thief had been caught. A woman whose treasure has been stolen wants, above all things, the thief caught and the treasure back. The Contessa wanted, above all things, witnesses to her grief.

The second was that her grief, though excellent, was metered. It rose precisely when an audience gathered and ebbed precisely when one dispersed, like a tide that kept office hours. Vane had spent his life performing emotions he did not feel, and he knew the difference between a feeling and a performance the way a wine merchant knows cork taint.

The third, and the most telling, was a single moment when the Contessa thought herself unobserved — between audiences, alone for a heartbeat at the lounge window — and her wet, tragic face went briefly, utterly smooth, and she looked out at the passing country with the cool assessing calm of a woman doing sums, and then a passenger approached and the grief snapped back on like a lamp.

"You're not mourning a diamond," murmured Vane to the potted palm. "You're managing one. You wanted it gone, and now it's gone, and the only thing troubling you is that it went to the wrong thief and you don't know where it is." He smiled without warmth. "Madam, we have a great deal in common. We are both trying to find out who stole the diamond you arranged to have stolen. Shall we compare notes? No. Better not. You'd have me drugged in a corridor."


Chapter 23: Quill Is Asked Nicely

Anton came to compartment nine because he had run out of other doors.

Mr. Sweet had found him at dawn, in the conductor's office, and had not raised his voice once, which was worse than shouting. He had simply sat on the edge of Anton's desk, smiling, and explained that he knew someone had drugged him last night to keep him from seeing something, and that he didn't much care who, and that the debt was still due Thursday, and that if it was not paid, the things that would happen to Anton's sister in the capital would happen slowly and would be Anton's fault. Then he had taken a sugar lump from Anton's own dish, and eaten it, and left.

So Anton came to Quill, because the desperate go to the legendary, and he stood in the doorway of compartment nine twisting his cap in his hands and said, "Mr. Quill. They say you were the greatest safecracker who ever lived."

"They exaggerate," said Quill, not opening his eyes. "I was the second. The greatest never got famous, which is the whole trick. Go away."

"I need a box opened. The Contessa's box. I'd pay you — I can't pay you, I have nothing, but after, when I have the —" Anton stopped, hearing himself, a conductor of nine honest years standing in a doorway proposing a robbery, and his voice cracked. "There's a man on this train who's going to hurt my sister if I don't find a great deal of money by Thursday. The diamond's already gone, I know, but there's other things in that box, the papers said, jewels, and I thought —"

Quill opened his eyes.

He looked at the conductor for a long time, at the twisting cap and the honest desperate face, and something moved in his old locked heart, because he had been, eighty years ago, a desperate young man who had let a smiling lender talk him into the first job, and he knew exactly where the road from this doorway went, every dark mile of it.

"Sit down," said Quill. "No. I'm not going to open your box. I made a promise to a girl in a white dress and I would rather watch this whole train sink into the sea than break it." He gestured to the seat anyway. "But I have been opening locks for seventy years, son, and the most important thing I ever learned is that the lock is almost never the problem. Sit down and tell me about this Mr. Sweet, and we will see whether there isn't a way to open *him* instead."


Chapter 24: Pellam Finds a Thread

Inspector Pellam solved the blackout at half past ten in the morning, sitting in the galley with the train's electrician and a cup of tea, by asking the dullest possible question.

"How," he said, "does one turn off all the lights in the dining car at once?"

The electrician, a sturdy woman named Pim who had kept the Marigold's wiring alive for two decades and had never once been asked a question by a first-class anything, was so startled to be consulted that she told him everything. The dining car lights ran off one circuit. The circuit had one master switch. The master switch was in a brass panel in the galley passage, behind an unmarked door, that no passenger would know about and no passenger could reach without passing the galley, where there were always, always, six cooks and a pastry chef who missed nothing.

"So no passenger threw that switch," said Pellam.

"Couldn't have. Not without my people seeing."

"Then one of your people threw it." Pellam sipped his tea. "Or someone your people would not think to mention, because they're part of the scenery. Who passes through this galley all evening and is so ordinary that six cooks wouldn't note them?"

Pim thought. "Stewards," she said. "Porters. The conductor." She shrugged. "Staff. We don't watch staff. They're just us."

Pellam wrote in his book, slowly, the sentence that would unlock the whole case if he had only believed it yet: *Everyone watches the passengers. No one watches the staff. The diamond was stolen by the scenery.* He looked at it. It was true and he did not like it, because it meant the thief he wanted was not one of the glittering clever people he had been enjoying suspecting, but some grey functionary in a uniform, and Pellam, to his lasting shame, had a snob's small reluctance to suspect a uniform — the same reluctance that let the conductor walk past him forty times a day invisible.

"Inspector?" said Pim. "Does that help?"

"It helps enormously," said Pellam, "and I'm cross about it. Thank you, Mrs. Pim. You've done more for me than the entire first-class carriage, who I notice tip you nothing." He left her a coin that made her stare, and went to find the conductor, a day too late to find him easily.


Chapter 25: The First Station: Tarn

The Marigold Express stopped at Tarn at noon of the second day, for twelve minutes, to take on water and coal and to set down the post, and Tarn was where the diamond very nearly left the train and the whole story nearly ended early in a ditch.

A station stop is a thief's best chance and worst danger both, because for twelve minutes the sealed world of the train has a door in it, and through that door a stolen thing can go — into a confederate's hand, a left-luggage locker, a mail sack — and be a hundred miles away by the time anyone thinks to look. Everyone aboard who had a stake in the Star knew this. So Tarn was crowded with quiet intentions.

Mr. Sweet had wired ahead; a man he knew was on the platform at Tarn, and Sweet's plan was simple and brutal — find whoever had the diamond, take it on the platform where the train's rules didn't reach, and be gone. He spent the twelve minutes prowling, smiling, watching pockets.

Anton, who had been told by Quill to do nothing rash and was doing something rash anyway out of pure terror, had thought to slip a parcel to the Tarn postmaster — but the parcel held nothing, because Anton had nothing, and he stood on the platform with an empty plan and watched the others and understood, sickeningly, how far out of his depth he was.

June and Vane stood at the carriage door together, not getting off — that was the discipline of it, the hardest discipline, to hold the hottest object on the continent and not run for the open door, because the run was exactly what watchers watched for. "Anyone who bolts at Tarn is the thief," murmured Vane. "So we don't bolt. We wave."

And they waved, the two of them, like a courting couple seeing off the post, while Henrietta Voss photographed the platform with a travel-writer's camera and an insurer's purpose, and Inspector Pellam stood at the very end of the platform where he could see every door of every carriage at once, eating an apple, watching to see who would run.

Nobody ran. The whistle blew. The Marigold Express drew its single door closed and pulled out of Tarn with every thief and every secret still aboard, wound one notch tighter, and Pellam threw his apple core to a station dog and climbed back on, satisfied, because a thief who can't unload a stolen diamond is a thief who must keep it close, and a thief who must keep it close is a thief who will, sooner or later, be standing next to it when Pellam asks everyone to turn out their pockets.


Chapter 26: The Star Changes Hands (Again)

The trouble with holding the most wanted object on a train full of people who want it is that you cannot hold it for long, and in the six hours after Tarn the Saffron Star passed through four pairs of hands, none of which managed to keep it, in a sequence so rapid that only June, afterward, could reconstruct it, and even she had to use her fingers.

June had it after Tarn. Then Adelaide, judging June watched, lifted it from her own niece's pocket in a motherly embrace — partly to protect the girl, partly because old habits, and partly, June suspected, to remind them both who was in charge.

Adelaide carried it into the lounge and, spotting Pellam's eye upon her, panicked very elegantly and dropped it into the nearest hiding place, which happened to be the soup tureen on the sideboard, under the lid, in the cold remains of the lunch consommé.

The soup tureen was then carried to the galley by a steward, where it sat for forty minutes while June and Adelaide had a whispered argument about which of them was going to put a hand into a tureen of cold soup in a galley full of cooks.

And the diamond was rescued from the consommé, in the end, not by either of them but by the pastry chef, who found it while washing up, held it to the light, decided it was a bit of decorative glass that had fallen off a passenger's frock, put it in the lost-property tin, and went back to his tarts.

So that by evening the Saffron Star, the most beautiful thing in the world, over which a man had been drugged and an inspector deployed and four thieves set at one another's throats, sat in a battered biscuit tin in the galley between a button, a hatpin, and somebody's dental plate, entirely safe, entirely unguarded, and entirely lost.


Chapter 27: Henrietta's Real Badge

Henrietta Voss stopped pretending to be a travel writer at nine o'clock that night, in the observation lounge, with Cornelius Vane, because she had decided he was the most useful crook aboard and she needed a crook.

"You're not buying the marmalade angle," she said, sitting down across from him without asking, the way he had sat down across from June. "You spotted me on the first morning. I watched you do it."

"You wrote down the number of bolts," said Vane. "Travel writers write down feelings. Investigators write down facts. You're insurance."

She put a card on the table. A real one, this time: *H. Voss, Senior Adjuster, Continental Surety & Guarantee.* "The Saffron Star is insured with us for a sum I won't say aloud in a public car, because it would change the behavior of everyone within earshot, including the curtains. My company will have to pay that sum to the Contessa if the diamond is not recovered." She leaned in. "I do not want to pay that sum, Mr. Vane. I am professionally, constitutionally, and personally opposed to paying that sum. And I have come to believe, over two days of watching that woman weep on a schedule, that I am being robbed — not by you, you charming amateur, but by my own client."

"You think she arranged the theft to claim the insurance."

"I think," said Voss, "that the Contessa della Vigna is broke. I think she has been broke for three years, kept afloat by loans from people one does not borrow from twice. I think she insured the Star to its eyeballs, arranged to have it 'stolen,' planned to sell it quietly abroad, and to collect the insurance on top — paid twice for one diamond, and free of her debts." She spread her hands. "It's a beautiful crime, Mr. Vane. I'd admire it if it weren't my money. And I can't prove a word of it. But you — you and your light-fingered young friend — you can go places my badge can't. So I'm proposing we trade. You want the diamond. I want the fraud. Those are not, it turns out, the same object."

Vane considered her. "You're suggesting," he said slowly, "that I help you prove the Contessa staged her own robbery — and in exchange you'd let me keep the thing she staged it with."

"I'm suggesting," said Voss, "that the diamond is yours for the taking if the owner can never report it stolen, because reporting it stolen would expose her fraud. A diamond no one dares claim is the only kind a thief can keep forever." She smiled. "I'm not asking you to be honest, Mr. Vane. I'm asking you to be useful. They're so rarely the same and tonight they happen to coincide."


Chapter 28: The Math of the Fake

Vane brought Voss's theory to June in the small hours, and June, who knew gems the way Quill knew locks, supplied the piece that turned a theory into a certainty.

"If she's planning to sell the real Star abroad and claim the insurance on it," June said, "then she'd never let the real Star ride in that strongbox where anything could happen to it. She'd ride a fake, claim the fake got stolen, and keep the real one somewhere safe — somewhere on her, probably, where no thief would think to look because everyone's watching the box." She frowned. "Which means the thing in my pocket — the thing in the soup, the thing that's currently lost, God help us — might not even be real."

"Barty made a fake," said Vane softly. "Barty told me. Yellow paste, cut to the gram, in a copy of the setting. He delivered it before Pell. He assumed the Contessa wanted a fake to display while the real one stayed in a vault. But what if it's the reverse? What if Barty's fake is the one she put in the box — the one she wanted stolen — and the real Star never went near the strongbox at all?"

They looked at each other in the dark lounge, and the whole shape of the journey rotated and clicked into a new and uglier position, like one of Quill's locks.

"We've been fighting over glass," said June. "All of us. You, me, Adelaide, Sweet, whoever drugged him — we've all been knifing each other over a sixty-franc piece of yellow paste, while the real Saffron Star sits somewhere we haven't even thought to look, on a broke Contessa who's laughing at every one of us." She started to laugh herself, helplessly, the exhausted laugh of a professional who has been thoroughly outclassed. "Oh, she's good, Vane. She's so good. She didn't hire a thief. She hired a roomful of thieves to fight over a decoy, so that the theft would be witnessed by the cleverest possible witnesses, all of them swearing it was real."

"Then we have a new job," said Vane. "Forget the fake. We find the real one."

"And then?"

"And then," said Vane, "we decide whether we're the kind of thieves who steal it for ourselves, or the kind who do something far more dangerous and satisfying." He smiled. "We give it back to whoever she stole it from in the first place. Because a woman who'd insure a thing to its eyeballs and sell it twice, June — that's not a woman who came by it honestly either."


Chapter 29: Mr. Sweet Collects

While the thieves discovered the diamond was a decoy, Mr. Sweet discovered Anton's hiding place, and the two events nearly converged into catastrophe at the worst possible moment.

Sweet had been patient in the way that money is patient. He had watched Anton all day — the panicked errand at Tarn, the visits to compartment nine, the sweating glances at the secure car — and he had drawn the correct conclusion, which was that the conductor was tangled in the diamond business up to his pressed collar. And on the evening of the second day, in the conductor's own office, Sweet stopped being patient.

"Here is where we are, Anton." He sat on the desk again, swinging one polished shoe. "You owe me by Thursday a sum you cannot pay. There is a diamond on this train. You have access to every room, every box, every key. I am not a greedy man — I don't want the diamond. I want my money. So you are going to get me the diamond, and I am going to turn it into my money through channels that need not concern you, and we will call your debt paid, and your sister will live a long and boring life." He smiled. "Or you will not, and she won't. It really is that simple. I do admire a simple arrangement."

"It's gone," Anton whispered. "The diamond. It was stolen the first night, everyone knows —"

"Stolen by someone on this train. Which means it's still on this train. Which means a man with all the keys can find it." Sweet stood, and patted Anton's cheek, once, gently, which was the most frightening thing he had done yet. "Thursday, Anton. Find it, or find a very good lawyer for your sister, and lawyers can't do much against me, I've tried them." He paused at the door. "Oh — and the man who drugged me. When you find out who it was, and you will, you're going to tell me. I owe him a conversation."

When he had gone, Anton sat in the brass light with his head in his hands, and that is how Quill found him, and that is when Quill, looking at the broken honest man, made the decision that would tip the whole game: that Mr. Sweet, who hurt people for money, was a lock that needed opening, and that an old man with seventy years of patience and a promise to keep had one good job left in him after all — a job that did not, technically, require cracking a single safe.


Chapter 30: Adelaide's Long Game

It was June who finally learned what her aunt was really after, and she learned it by the oldest method in their trade: she went through Adelaide's things.

She did it at midnight, while Adelaide held court in the lounge, and she did it with a guilty thoroughness, because something had been wrong with her aunt for the whole journey, a tension that didn't fit a simple diamond grab, and June had been raised by this woman and trained by her and lied to by her, and she had reached the age where the apprentice begins, quietly, to investigate the master.

In the false bottom of Adelaide's vanity case — June found it because she had a matching case and had built the false bottom herself — there was not a thief's kit. There was a bundle of letters, very old, the ink browned, tied with a ribbon that had once been some color and was now the color of all old ribbon. And there was a photograph of a young woman who was unmistakably Adelaide, forty years younger, standing beside a man in a uniform, both of them laughing, and behind them, on the woman's young throat, catching the long-ago sun, a yellow diamond the size of a quail's egg.

June sat down on the floor of the compartment with the photograph in her hands and her heart going strangely, and read the top letter, and then the second, and began to understand.

The Saffron Star had been Adelaide's. Or her family's — looted from them, the letters made clear, in the chaos of the war the man in the uniform had not come home from, taken by an official who became an owner who sold to a dealer who sold to the late Count della Vigna, who gave it to his Contessa, who insured it and now meant to sell it twice and burn the last cold trail back to a laughing girl in a photograph. Adelaide had not come aboard the Marigold Express for a diamond. She had come for *her* diamond — the one fixed point in a life of fabricated names — and the long con, the whole patient grift, had only ever been the means to get close enough to take back the one true thing she had ever owned.

"Oh, Aunt Adelaide," June whispered to the photograph. "You're not running a con at all. You're going home."

She put everything back exactly as she had found it, the way she'd been taught, and she sat in the dark, and for the first time in a year of weariness she knew precisely what she wanted to do, and it was not to steal the Saffron Star.

It was to make sure her aunt got it back.


Chapter 31: June Tests the Stone

The lost-property tin was the easiest thing June had ever stolen, and the hardest, because it came with an audience.

She retrieved the diamond from the galley at half past midnight, sliding past the sleeping cooks like a shadow, and the pastry chef's biscuit tin yielded a yellow stone wrapped in a scrap of conspiratorial paper that tasted faintly of last year's cake. Back in her compartment, she sat on the floor with her back to the door — the professional's position — and the diamond on the rug before her, and she tested it.

A real diamond passes light through it in ways that paste cannot imitate, and June had the edge to tell, the loupe in her pocket, and the training. She held the stone to the reading lamp and looked through it and saw-paste, dead yellow, no fire, no gate in the light. Barty's work. Very good work, too — good enough to fool the dinner table, good enough to fool a bank appraiser, but not good enough to fool a professional who had been shown how to spot paste at the age of nineteen by a forger who had never touched a real diamond either, because nobody in her world ever did. The real ones came from genuine drawers, and genuine drawers had alarms.

"I was right," she said aloud, to the stone. "You're nothing. You're less than nothing. You're a decoy that's been in the soup and the lost property and my pocket over four hundred miles of track, and I am the best pickpocket on this train and I have been dodging an inspector through three carriages for a bit of carnival paste."

She ought to have felt foolish. Instead she felt, for the first time since Pell, the clean sharp excitement of a puzzle that had just gotten interesting. The fake was in her hand, which meant the Contessa had it on the train somewhere — the real Star, hiding where no thief would look because every thief was busy chasing the decoy. And the only person on the Marigold who had never, not once, tried to get near the stable or the strongbox, because she knew exactly what was in it, was the woman who had put the decoy there.

The Contessa.

June smiled, a slow smile with no innocence in it, and put the paste star in her pocket, and went to find Vane.


Chapter 32: Where Is the Real One?

The search for the real Saffron Star was not like the search for the fake one. The fake had been stolen in a crowd and passed hand to hand through most of the train. The real one had never left the Contessa's possession, and could only be in three places: on her person, in her private suite, or in a place she could reach without suspicion.

"Why not the suite?" said Vane. They were in the corridor between carriages, speaking fast, the train groaning through a long curve.

"Because she can't guard the suite," said June. "She's been in the lounge all day being a victim. Anyone could have searched her rooms in the four hours she's been performing."

"Would she risk that?"

"No. Which means it's on her. Somewhere." June looked at the swaying corridor. "She's been wearing gowns. necklines. Nothing could hide a stone that size against bare skin. But she has a coat. She has a traveling cloak. She has luggage she keeps locked."

Vane considered. "Or," he said, "she hid it in the one place the thieves can't reach and the inspector won't search: a second safe, in her suite, that nobody knows about because the train's official record only lists the one in the secure car."

"The suite we can't search because she's in it," said June, "and the coat we can't reach because she's in it, and the second safe we can't search because we don't know where it is." She shook her head. "We need her out of the suite. Long enough to turn it over. We need a diversion big enough to draw the Contessa, the inspector, and half the train away from the back carriages at once."

"Then we need something more dramatic than a stolen diamond," said Vane. "We need something the inspector would abandon the diamond case for."

"Like what?"

"Like a second crime," said Vane. "One that looks worse than a theft. Something that makes the diamond seem like the smaller problem." He was quiet a moment, and then he said, slowly, "Like a second drugging. One that looks like it failed. Someone discovers Mr. Sweet again, in trouble, nearer to death this time. That pulls Pellam. That pulls every pair of eyes to the rear of the train. And while they're gathered around Sweet, you and I turn over the Contessa's suite and find the one safe the manifest forgot to mention."

June looked at him. "You want to stage a murder attempt."

"I want to stage a convincing scare. There's a difference." He smiled thinly. "Mr. Sweet has been asking who drugged him. I think he'd like to see the man. And after he sees him, very briefly, I think Mr. Sweet would very much like to have a drink—something strong, something he remembers being offered, something that makes the next hour very, very easy for the rest of us."


Chapter 33: The Contessa's Suite

The plot against Mr. Sweet went forward that night with the smoothness of a thing that has been planned by people who have spent their lives planning things. Vane had paid a steward to deliver a bottle of good Scotch to Mr. Sweet's compartment, with the compliments of a passenger who wished to remain discreet. Sweet, who mistook suspicion for vigilance, tested the bottle the way he tested everything—by letting someone else drink first. He rang for Anton, made him pour and taste, watched him for ten minutes, and then, satisfied that the conductor was not drugging him twice, sat down with the Scotch and a novel and, by midnight, was as comfortably unconscious as he had been on the first night.

"Totally unnecessary to drug him," said Vane, meeting June in the corridor with a smile. "He drugged himself. I only sent the bottle. A man who expects poison in everything will eventually drink his own suspicion."

They moved through the sleeping train like two shades, fast and silent, Vane in the lead, June covering the rear, their breath pluming in the cold air between the carriages. The Contessa's suite occupied the very last car: two rooms, a sitting room and a bedroom, linked by a brass-trimmed door, with windows that showed the disappearing track and a lock that was, to their practiced fingers, a formality.

Inside, the suite was a theater of mourning. The Contessa's evening gown lay across a chair. Her brushes were on the vanity, her handkerchiefs in a drift on the carpet. A half-empty cup of tea sat cooling, and in the middle of the sitting room floor, under the lamp, lay an open letter in her own hand, blotted and abandoned, as if the effort of writing had been too much for grief.

"She's good," murmured June, taking it in. "She's so good. The grieving widow, unable to finish a letter. She's been acting her alibi."

They worked fast. Vane took the walls, running his hands along the paneling for a hidden seam. June took the furniture, lifting every drawer, every cushion, every layer of silk lining. They searched in silence, the way they'd each searched a hundred rooms, and they found, in the twelve minutes before the train was due to pass the next signal, what the Contessa had not expected anyone to look for: a wall safe, flush-mounted behind the bedroom curtain, small and black and brand-new.

"Oh, well played," breathed June. "A safe that wasn't there when she boarded. Installed this season, in her own suite, behind a curtain, never listed on any manifest because she owns the car."

Vane knelt before it. "Can you open it?"

"I can try. But I was trained to lift, not to crack." She examined the dial, the cold smooth steel. "This is Quill's work. A lock like this. We need Quill."

"We can't get Quill without going through Pellam's corridor." Vane was already moving. "We take the alternative. We take the Contessa, not the safe. If she has the key on her—and she must, for a safe she installed herself—then we don't need to open the lock. We need to lift the key."

"From her person. While she's awake. In a carriage full of people." June considered. "That's not a burglary, Vane. That's a theatrical performance."

"Then it's a good thing we're both actors," said Vane. "Let's go put on a show."


Chapter 34: Quill's Compromise

Quill was not asleep when they came to him, because old men who have spent a lifetime listening for locks do not sleep through a train full of locked doors being tried by amateurs. He was sitting in the dark of compartment nine, dressed, his hands folded, as if he had been waiting.

"I felt you," he said, before either of them could speak. "Every time you touched something that had a lock on it, I felt it. There's a safe in the Contessa's suite, and you can't get into it, and you've come to ask me to break my promise."

"Yes," said June.

"No," said Vane, at the same moment, and they looked at each other.

Vane turned to Quill. "I was going to ask you to loan us the knowledge, not the hand. We don't need you to crack the safe. We need to know where she keeps the key—whether a woman like that wears it, or leaves it, or trusts it to a dresser drawer."

Quill was silent a long moment. "She keeps it on her," he said finally. "A woman who installs a secret safe keeps the key on her person, within reach, in case she needs to reach it fast. It will be in a pocket she can touch without thinking, or on a chain under her clothes, or—if she's very clever—sewn into the lining of something she wears every hour, because the only way to guarantee a key is never stolen is to make it part of your own body."

"She's been wearing the same traveling cloak since Pell," said June slowly. "Even at dinner. It's the one thing she never takes off."

"Then it's in the cloak. The left pocket, likely—a woman's instinct is to keep valuables near her heart. Go." Quill closed his eyes. "I didn't tell you anything. I was asleep. When this train reaches the capital, I am getting off and going to a wedding, and I have not broken a single promise, because the only promise I made was not to open a lock, and opening a lock and telling someone where a key is are two entirely different things, even if an old man's conscience says they feel very much the same."


Chapter 35: Pellam Eats His Sandwich and Solves Half of It

Inspector Pellam solved the drugging of Mr. Sweet over his mystery sandwich at one in the morning, alone in the dining car, with the train's motion for company and the small black book open before him. He had been working the case the only way he knew how, which was to write down everything and then eat something and see what stood up, and what stood up now was a picture he did not much like.

The pattern: the first drugging happened during the blackout, while the diamond was being stolen. The second drugging had just happened, twelve hours later, to the same man, while nothing was being stolen at all—not that he knew of. If he had been clever, he would have seen the pattern immediately. But Pellam was not clever, only patient, and patience took longer but arrived at the same place.

"Two druggings," he said aloud, to the empty dining car. "The first to clear the way for a theft. The second—why a second? Not to steal anything, because nothing has left the train. Not to send a message, because Sweet is still alive and still smiling. Which means the second drugging was a distraction: a noise to pull the inspector's attention one way while the thieves moved another way." He wrote: *A distraction. For what?*

And then he remembered that he had been sitting in this same dining car for three hours, compiling his notes, watching the corridor, while the Contessa's suite, at the far end of the train, had been unguarded and unobserved by anyone except the locks.

"Oh," said Pellam, aloud. "Oh, that's very bad. That's an oversight." He closed his notebook, very slowly, and the expression on his face was not anger but respect, complicated by a deep and personal chagrin. "They used a crime to cover a search. While I was sitting with my sandwich, somebody searched the Contessa's rooms, and I never even thought to check on her." He rose, brushing crumbs from his coat. "Well. They will do it again, because they haven't found what they were looking for yet, and a thief who needs to search the same room twice is a thief who will get caught. I have only to wait for the next distraction, and this time I will not be eating a sandwich. I will be waiting at the far end of the train, exactly where they don't want me."


Chapter 36: The Second Station: Vesper Halt

The Marigold Express stopped at Vesper Halt at a quarter to two in the morning, an unscheduled halt, and it stopped because the driver had seen a red lantern on the track and had done what drivers do, which is stop, and the red lantern had been placed there by a passenger.

The passenger was Mr. Sweet's man—the same man who had been waiting for him at Tarn, who had followed the train on the road, who had received the message Sweet had not been conscious to send, and who had decided that if Mr. Sweet could not collect his debt, he would collect it for him. He did not know the train. He did not know the layout. He knew only that there was a conductor named Reyes who had money he was not paying.

The stop at Vesper Halt woke everyone. It woke the Contessa, who sat up in her suite and listened. It woke Vane and June, who had been waiting in the corridor, and who felt the sudden stillness of the train like a door slamming shut. It woke Pellam, who had been sitting in the dining car and who walked to the window and saw a man standing on the track with a locomotive lamp, and who understood, without needing to write it down, that the debt had come aboard.

"Anton," said Pellam with certainty, and began to walk.

The confrontation happened in the staff car, in the dark, with the train stopped and the country silent around it. Sweet's man had found Anton in his office, and Anton, who had been expecting Sweet to come for him and found a stranger instead, was backed against the wall with his conductor's cap fallen between them, trying to remember how to be brave.

"I know nothing about any diamond," Anton said. "I am a conductor. I collect tickets. I cannot pay what I do not have."

"You will have something to give me," said the man on the track, "or you will have something broken, and they are the same thing in different languages."

And then the car door opened and Inspector Pellam stepped through, not running—inspectors do not run—but walking with the particular stillness of a man who has been interrupted while thinking and does not intend to be interrupted again.

"Vesper Halt," said Pellam, "is not a stop on the schedule. This train is running late now, and I will have to file a report about why, and I am not in the mood to write that you were the reason. Step away from the conductor, sit down in the nearest empty compartment, and do not move until I have decided what to do with you." The man did not move. Pellam added, in the same flat voice, "I am an officer of the railway police, I have never lost a case, and I am extremely annoyed to have been woken from a very long and promising sandwich reverie. Do not test whether annoyed makes me faster."

The man from the track looked at Sweet's man, and then at Pellam's coat, and decided that the debt could wait until the capital. He stepped away. Pellam sighed, nodded to Anton, and went back to the dining car. Behind him, Anton slid down the wall and sat on the floor, shaking, as the train began to move again, and the diamond felt a thousand miles away, and the debt was still Thursday, and there was still no way out.

There is, he thought, with the dull clarity of the very tired. There is always one way out. You just don't want to take it.

And for the first time on the journey, Anton looked at the master keys on his belt—keys to every door on the train, keys to the secure car, keys to the Contessa's suite—and thought about what a desperate man with nothing left could do with them, if he stopped caring what he broke on the way.

It was the worst thought he had had yet, and it was the one that would finally make him useful.


Chapter 37: The Alliance of the Honest Crooks

They gathered in the galley at three in the morning, after Vesper Halt, because the galley was warm and the cooks were asleep and no one but the truly desperate thought to hold a meeting among the cold scrubbed surfaces of last night's dinner. They came one by one, each thinking they were the only one coming: Vane, June, Quill, Anton, and, to everyone's surprise, Henrietta Voss, who had seen a red lantern from her window and had dressed and followed it to the only part of the train where light still burned.

"We have a problem," said Vane, when they were all assembled—all but Adelaide, who was still in the lounge, still holding her vigil of grief, still unaware that her apprentice had uncovered her history. "The real Star is in the Contessa's suite, in a safe none of the train's records mention, and the key is in her cloak. None of us can open the safe, and none of us can lift the key without a diversion bigger than we've got."

"Except that you can," said Quill quietly. "Because I've told you where the key is. And I've told you what the safe is: a Vesper junior model, single-throw, five-lever. It can be opened without the key, by someone who knows the sequence, in about forty seconds of listening to the tumblers. The only reason I haven't opened it myself is that I made a promise to a girl in a white dress, and I keep my promises. But I didn't promise nobody could listen while somebody else did it."

Everyone looked at Anton.

Anton looked back, pale in the galley light. "I am a conductor," he said. "I collect tickets. I don't know one end of a safe from the other."

"Nobody is asking you to open it," said Quill. "You're going to hold the lamp. You're going to stand by the door. You're going to be the man who knows where every passenger is and how long it takes to get anywhere. That is not a safe-cracker's job. That is a conductor's job, and you have been doing it for nine years, and you are very good at it. The only thing that's changed is that you're doing it for us instead of for the timetable."

Anton looked at them—the thief, the pickpocket, the safecracker, the insurance adjuster, all watching him, all waiting for him to decide whether he was the man who would finally act—and he said, in a voice he did not recognize, "What do I need to do?"


Chapter 38: The Frost Between Them

They had the plan. They had the people. They had, for the first time, a clear picture of the board. What they did not have is what the morning brought, when June returned to the compartment she shared with Adelaide and found her aunt standing in the center of the floor, holding the paste star from the lost-property tin, which June had stupidly left on the washstand.

"Explain," said Adelaide.

June stood in the doorway. The long corridor stretched behind her. She had the options her aunt had taught her: lie, deflect, charm, or retreat. She chose something else.

"It's paste," she said. "Barty Lune's work. The real Star has never left the Contessa. It's in a secret safe in her suite. Vane and I found the safe last night. We're going to open it this morning, with Quill's help, during the breakfast distraction, and we're going to take the real one, and we're going to give it back to you."

Adelaide's face, for the first time in forty years of performance, went genuinely blank. "What did you say?"

"I read your letters, Aunt Adelaide. I saw the photograph. The Star was yours. Your family's. Before the war, before the Count, before all of this." June's voice was steady. "You didn't come aboard for a theft. You came to take back what was taken from you. And I am not going to let you finish your life without finishing that job."

Adelaide looked at her for a long time. The mask of the grand lady, the grifter, the unflappable artist of the long con, flickered and dropped away, and what was left was an old woman with a letter in her hand and forty years of loss in her throat.

"You weren't supposed to know," she said. "That was the one thing I kept. The one thing that was mine alone."

"You kept it too well," said June. "That's how I knew it mattered."

There was a silence between them, the longest of the journey, and then Adelaide crossed the compartment and took June's face in both hands—a gesture so unlike her usual theatricality that it felt, for that moment, like the truest thing she had ever done. "Then let's go get it back," she said. "Both of us. Together. And then I will tell you the whole story, every lie I've ever told you, and you can decide whether to forgive me or throw me off this train at the capital."

"Both," said June, "but I'll decide the order later."

They went to steal a diamond that was already theirs, and that made all the difference.


Chapter 39: Building the Case

The plan, assembled over cold tea in the galley, depended on timing and on the particular blind spots of a trainful of people who had been watching the wrong thing for three days.

Breakfast was the moment. The Contessa always came to breakfast—it was her best stage, the fullest audience—and she always took her time. She would be in the dining car for at least forty minutes, which gave Quill and Anton thirty-two minutes to reach her suite, open the safe in the wall behind the curtain, extract the real Star, and leave everything exactly as they had found it, down to the angle of the curtain and the level of the tea in the cup.

Vane and June would provide the diversion. Vane would stage an argument at the dining car door—something about a missing cufflink, a stolen watch, a misunderstanding—that would draw the morning's attention to the forward carriages and away from the rear. June would stand near the Contessa and, at the critical moment, engage her in a conversation about the diamond's provenance that she could not resist, keeping her seated and visible while the safe was opened forty meters behind her.

Henrietta Voss would watch Pellam. If the inspector moved toward the rear, she would produce a sudden problem—a dropped camera, a note of confusion about a passenger's name in her report, anything—to slow him down. "I have always wanted to distract a policeman," she said. "It's on my list."

"And if we are caught?" said Anton, who had not stopped shaking since Vesper.

"Then we tell the truth," said June. "A version of it. That we suspected the Contessa of insurance fraud and wanted to prove it. That is not a crime; it's a civic duty, and the inspector will probably give us a medal. The crime is the theft, and we are not stealing from the Contessa. We are stealing from a woman who stole from Adelaide, which is a different moral category entirely."

"Morality is not a legal defense," said Quill, but he was smiling. "It should be, but it isn't. That said, I did not survive eighty-one years by letting the law tell me what was right. I say we go. Right now, before I remember the promise I made that girl in the white dress and lose my nerve."

They went.


Chapter 40: Baiting the Contessa

The distraction worked like a stage play, which is to say it worked perfectly except for the things that went unrehearsed, and those were the things that made it real.

Quill and Anton reached the Contessa's suite with the master key at forty-two minutes past seven. Anton's hands were steady, which surprised him. He had spent nine years making his hands steady for the sake of poured tea and balanced trays, and the same steadiness served him now as he unlocked the door, stood aside, and watched the oldest safecracker alive kneel before a wall safe he had been told about but never seen.

"Vesper junior," Quill murmured, touching it like an old friend. "You can tell by the weight of the dial. They never changed the design. I opened one of these in a bank in Parma in 1959, for a man who turned out to be a ghost—but that's a different story." He pressed his ear to the cold steel, closed his eyes, and began to turn the dial. "Forty seconds," he said. "Give or take."

In the dining car, Vane had begun his argument with a steward about a missing enameled cufflink—the one he had palmed from his own sleeve and hidden under a napkin—and the steward was apologizing and the passengers were watching, and June had positioned herself beside the Contessa's chair, leaning in, asking in a hushed voice about the diamond's long and complicated history, which the Contessa was delighted to provide.

"Yes," the Contessa was saying, "it was in the family of a Hungarian countess, and then—well, there were some difficult years, and the war, and a great many papers were lost, but my husband assured me it was all quite legal, quite aboveboard—"

It was, thought June, the most elaborate bull session she had ever been part of, and she smiled and nodded while behind her, forty meters away, a man she had never met until three days ago and a conductor who had lost everything but his nerve were listening to the heartbeat of a lock and finding the one number that would open the whole contessa's life.

Forty seconds, Quill had said. It took thirty-eight. The last tumbler fell. The safe door swung inward, dark and narrow.

And in the sleeve of his coat, with the ear that had heard a thousand locks fall, Quill heard this one say a secret—not just that it was open, but that it was already empty.

Realization spread across his face like cold water.

"Anton," he said, very quietly. "There's nothing in it. The safe has been opened already. Someone beat us here."


Chapter 41: Sweet's Syndicate Revealed

Quill and Anton returned to the galley empty-handed, and the news spread through the little group like a draft under a door: the real Star was not in the Contessa's safe, had never been there, and the only person on the train who could have taken it was the Contessa herself.

"She moved it," said Vane, pacing the cold galley. "Before the journey, or during, before her own 'theft' was staged. She never trusted the decoy plan to keep the real one safe. She put it somewhere else entirely—somewhere even her own secret safe couldn't point to."

"Or someone else moved it," said June. "Someone who knew about the safe and got there before we did."

"Who knew?" said Voss. "We found the safe ourselves not twelve hours ago. The only people who knew about it were us, and the Contessa, and whoever helped her install it."

"And Mr. Sweet," said Anton, very quietly. "The syndicate that owns him. They've been bankrolling her for three years. They know everything—where she keeps things, how she moves money, when she plans to sell. If she had a secret safe, they knew, and if they knew, then they've had time to reach it."

The galley went quiet. The train tracked on through the morning.

"Sweet was drugged twice," Vane said slowly, turning it over. "The first time during the blackout, while the fake was being stolen. The second time last night, right before we searched the suite. Both times, the drugging put Sweet out of action just when something important was about to happen. The first time, to clear the way for the decoy theft. The second time—" He stopped. "The second time was not our distraction. We drugged him ourselves with the Scotch, but somebody else had already drugged him the first night. Somebody who knew the plan, knew the timing, and knew exactly when to put Sweet to sleep to keep him from interfering."

"Somebody inside the syndicate," said June. "Working against Sweet. Working on their own."

"A double game," said Quill. "Someone on this train has been playing everyone—us, Sweet, the Contessa, maybe even the inspector—and we have not seen their face once."


Chapter 42: The Real Star in Plain Sight

Inspector Pellam solved it during the mid-morning lull, not by detection but by accident, which is how most real cases are solved and nobody admits it.

He had gone to the dining car for a fresh pot of tea and found, on the sideboard, a small heap of items from the lost-property tin that the pastry chef had set out for the stewards to claim. There was a button. A hatpin. An ornate silver pillbox, monogrammed. And there, sitting among them like a guest who had wandered into the wrong party, a yellow stone the size of a quail's egg, untarnished, unclaimed, and unremarkable to any but the trained eye—which Pellam's eye was not.

He picked it up. He looked at it. He thought. It's quite a pretty thing, he thought, for somebody's lost jewelry. He put it back, drank his tea, and did not think about it again for thirty minutes, and then he thought about it again because he had just remembered that the Contessa had described her diamond at breakfast as being the size of a quail's egg, and there was a thing in the lost-property tin which was also the size of a quail's egg, and he did not believe in coincidence.

He rose. He walked back to the dining car. The sideboard was empty—the stewards had cleared it. The tin, when he found it in the galley, held only the button and the hatpin. The yellow stone was gone.

"Who claimed it?" he asked the chef.

The chef shrugged. "One of the porters. The new one. Tall, thin hands, needed a shave. Said it was from a passenger in second class who'd lost it yesterday. Must have been his, for all I know."

Pellam wrote in his notebook: *Barty Lune, porter. Took the paste star from lost property. Now has the wrong diamond again, or the right one, depending on what he knows.* He closed the book. He was beginning to enjoy himself, which was a dangerous sign at this stage of a case, because enjoyment made him careless, and he had just noticed that the paste star had moved from the tin to Barty Lune, and the real star was still somewhere on the train, and the only person who knew where it was, was the one person everyone had been too busy suspecting to actually watch.

The Contessa.

He sat down with his tea, and did not share his theory with anyone, because an inspector who shares a theory before the evidence is ready is an inspector who gets a theory proved wrong in public, and Pellam had not lost a case yet and did not intend to begin now.


Chapter 43: The Star Changes Hands (Finally)

Barty Lune had the paste star in his porter's pocket and was trying to decide what to do with it. He knew it was his own work—you never forget your own hand, especially not a stone he had spent a week cutting from yellow crystal, matching it to photographs and a wax press of a setting he had touched exactly once. He also knew that the Contessa had not claimed it, that the inspector was watching him, and that the real treasure on this train was not the one he was holding.

He was standing in the corridor of carriage eleven, pretending to polish a window, when June found him.

"Barty. The Contessa's stone is paste. Your work. I was there in the soup. You know it's paste. The real one is somewhere on this train, and you know where it is because you can smell the difference between your work and the real thing. Am I right?"

Barty looked at her. "I might be, if I had a reason to tell you."

"How's this for a reason?" June held up the paste star—the same stone that had been in her pocket, the soup, the tin, the property of half the train in the past forty-eight hours. "I will give this back to you, for whatever you want it for, plus the name of whoever hired you to make it, if you tell me where the real one is."

"You already know the name. The Contessa hired me."

"And you already know she was planning something, because a woman who hires a forger to copy a stone she never let him touch is a woman who planned to have that copy stolen. But she didn't stop there, did she? She also planned to sell the real one. And somewhere, on this train, she hid the real one, and the secret safe we found was a decoy, and there's a second secret place she told nobody about. But you watch. You've been watching since Pell. You see where she puts her hands when she thinks nobody is looking. Where does she touch, Barty?"

The forger was silent a long moment, and then he said, low, "Her coat. The traveling cloak she never took off. When she thinks nobody is watching, she touches the left lapel. Not the pocket. The lapel itself. There's something sewn into the seam under the lining." He met June's eyes. "It's not a key to a safe. It's the stone itself, wrapped in a silk patch, stitched into the fabric. She's been wearing the real Saffron Star on her left shoulder, in plain sight, since the moment she boarded."

June had to steady herself against the corridor wall. "She's been wearing it the whole time."

"Every dinner. Every performance. Right there, on her shoulder, in the one place no thief would look because it's too obvious and no inspector would search a titled woman's coat without cause. She made us all chase a fake through the whole train while she sat in the lounge with the real one on her shoulder, watching us run."

June did the only thing she could do. She laughed, long and helpless, and when she was done she handed Barty the paste star.

"Thank you, Barty. You've just won the game for the right side. Now go hide your paste somewhere safe. The inspector's about to have a very interesting conversation with the Contessa, and I want to be in the room when it happens."


Chapter 44: The Contessa Overplays

The Contessa della Vigna, for the first time in her life, made the mistake of thinking she could not lose an argument she had already won. It was the error of a brilliant performer who had been on stage so long she had forgotten that the footlights could be turned around.

Henrietta Voss came to her suite at noon, not as a travel writer and not as an insurance adjuster, but as a woman with papers, and laid out a correspondence: three letters from a known fence, a loan agreement with Mr. Sweet's syndicate, and a certified copy of the insurance policy's fraud-exclusion clause, which stated that any claim arising from a theft arranged or abetted by the insured was null and void.

The Contessa read them in order, and her mask of wounded aristocracy flickered, and then it settled into something harder. "This is nonsense," she said. "These are forgeries."

"They are copies," said Voss. "The originals are with my company's legal department in the capital, and if you attempt to claim the insurance, they will be produced, and you will be prosecuted, and the Contessa della Vigna will become a cautionary tale in the circulars that insurance firms share about their most embarrassing frauds. But that does not have to happen." She paused. "All you have to do is tell me where the real Saffron Star is, and I will see that it is returned to its legal owner—not you, not the syndicate, but the museum it was quietly removed from in a generation I do not need to name."

The Contessa laughed. It was a beautiful laugh, the kind she deployed at dinner, and it had the same hollow center. "You have a copy of a loan agreement and a forged letter from a fence. I have a diamond everyone saw being stolen, a police inspector who believes me, and a first-class ticket to a very large insurance payout. I think I will take my chances with the courts, Miss Voss. They have been kind to my family for centuries."

"That is because your family has never been up against someone with the receipts," said Voss, and she placed the final photograph on the desk—the photograph June had found in Adelaide's case, the laughing girl with the diamond at her throat. "This was taken in 1925. The woman is Mrs. Adelaide Frost, shipping heiress, whose family was looted during the occupation. The diamond is the Saffron Star. The man beside her is her brother, who died in the war that also took his family's treasure. The Count della Vigna, your husband, acquired it from a dealer who acquired it from an officer who acquired it from Adelaide's dead brother's effects. The chain of title, Madam, is a crime narrative from start to finish. You have no claim to this stone. You have never had a claim. You have a story, a very good one, and it ends here."

For the first time on the journey, the Contessa said nothing. Her face, stripped of performance, looked suddenly old—not sad, not ashamed, just old, like a building whose last tenant has moved out. "And what do you propose, exactly, that I do now?"

"Call off the insurance claim. Walk away quietly at the capital. Let the diamond go to the address the museum in my file has provided and never think about it again. I will not press charges. The syndicate will not be named. You will lose the money you needed, but you will keep the one thing you have left, which is your name, and the police do not need to know about the loan agreement or the man in the corridor or the paste copy that spent Wednesday night in the consommé."

The Contessa sat very still. The train hummed beneath her. "I was going to sell it, you know," she said finally. "Not for the insurance. For the freedom. I wanted to be free of the men who lent me money, free of the house I could not afford, free of the ghost of a husband who left me a title and a diamond and not a franc to keep either of them with. And I would have been free, if you had not brought a photograph of a dead girl to the last straight mile." She touched her left lapel, the seam where the stone had traveled, hidden and silent, for four days. "It is in the lining. Take it. Tell your museum I am sending my regards. They will not believe you, but they will have the stone back, and that is what mattered to the girl in the photograph, and I suppose it is what matters now, though I cannot for the life of me remember why."


Chapter 45: Pellam Closes In

Inspector Pellam assembled the carriage at one in the afternoon, in the observation lounge, with all the principals seated before him like an audience he had not asked for and was not sure he wanted. The Contessa was there, pale and silent, her cloak folded on her lap with the stone no longer in it. Adelaide was there, sitting straight, her hand on June's. Vane was there, looking politely interested. Sweet was there, smiling. Anton was there, sweating. Henrietta Voss sat at the back with her notebook open, and Quill stood by the door, arms folded, as if he were only passing through.

Pellam stood before them, his own notebook in his hand, his coat rumpled, his tie slightly crooked, looking like a man who had walked into the wrong club and decided to stay for the argument.

"Inspector," said the Contessa, recovering some of her theater. "You have assembled us like a cast for the final act. Do you know who stole my diamond?"

"I know who stole *a* diamond," said Pellam. "What I'm less certain of is whether it's the same diamond the whole journey has been about." He turned a page. "Let me tell you what I know. The stone that was stolen from your throat on the first night was a paste copy, made aboard this train by a forger traveling under a porter's name. I know this because I found a second paste copy in the lost-property tin this morning, and I have since learned there were at least three copies in circulation." He paused to let that settle. "The original Saffron Star—the one worth the fortune you insured it for—was never in the strongbox, never on the evening gown, and never within a mile of the hand that reached for it in the dark. It was in the lining of your traveling cloak, Madam, where you sewed it before the journey began, where you kept it for four days, and where it remains at this moment, unless you have recently taken it out for reasons you would like to explain."

The silence in the lounge was complete. The Contessa looked at Pellam, and Pellam looked back, and for a long moment neither of them spoke.

"I have no idea what you are talking about," said the Contessa.

"Yes," said Pellam. "I didn't think you would." He closed his notebook. "I am not going to arrest anyone on this train, because I would have to arrest most of you, and the paperwork would take longer than the rest of the journey. What I am going to do is note in my report that the Saffron Star was stolen, was recovered, and will be returned to its owners—all of which is true, in the order that matters most. The rest of this, ladies and gentlemen, is a conversation between you and your own consciences, and I do not believe I am required to take notes on that."

"No charges?" said Vane, surprised.

"No charges that will hold, Mr. Vane. Everyone on this train has committed a crime, but everyone on this train has also spent the last three days trying to out-fraud the actual fraud, which is the Contessa, and the actual threat, which is Mr. Sweet, and I find I do not have the energy to prosecute a trainful of people who have accidentally done the right thing for the wrong reasons." He looked around the car. "The capital is in two hours. Before we arrive, I would like everyone to be very clear on what they plan to tell the police on the platform, so that there are no contradictions. I will be listening. Let us proceed to the ending."


Chapter 46: Sweet's Last Move

Mr. Sweet, who had been watching the inspector's performance with the tolerant smile of a man who has not lost anything yet, rose from his chair at the end of Pellam's speech and walked, calmly, to the door of the observation lounge, blocking it.

"I think not," he said. "I have been drugged twice, insulted by a policeman, and placed at the back of this room behind a woman who has been lying to everyone since she boarded, and I am not leaving this train without payment for the debt I am owed. The diamond—the real one, not the paste you're all passing around like a party favor—is coming with me. Or I will take something else from someone else, and the something else I take will cost more than the diamond was worth."

"You will take nothing," said Quill, stepping forward. "You will sit down, Mr. Sweet, and you will wait to be met at the station by the men I have already sent a telegram to from Tarn, informing them of your syndicate's activities and requesting their presence at the capital platform. By the time we arrive, there will be more policemen on that platform than passengers on this train, and they will not be looking for a stolen diamond. They will be looking for you."

Sweet's smile flickered. "You sent a telegram from Tarn? You don't have access to a telegram."

"I am eighty-one years old. I am a safecracker. I have been getting messages out of locked places since before you were born. I wrote the telegram in the galley at noon the first day. I had it delivered at Tarn by a steward who owed me a favor from a job I pulled thirty years ago involving a very similar safe and a very similar debt. The capital police have the syndicate's names, their ledger, and their current location—this train. They are waiting." Quill smiled, and it was the coldest smile in the car. "You are not leaving, Mr. Sweet. You are arriving."

Sweet looked at the old man, and for the first time on the journey, the smile left his eyes. He sat down. He did not speak again. The train rolled on toward the capital, and the law came closer with every turn of the wheels.

Henrietta Voss, watching from her seat, turned to Quill and said, quietly enough that only he could hear: "Did you really send a telegram?"

"No," said Quill, just as quietly. "I had a steward buy me a newspaper at Tarn. But he doesn't know that, and by the time he finds out, the platform will be here, and the police will see a very nervous man step off the train and will be very interested in why. Sometimes a bluff is all the tool you need. I learned that from a man in Parma, in 1959, who turned out to be a ghost." He folded his hands. "It is one of the few things I believe."


Chapter 47: The Capital Approaches

The Marigold Express made its final approach to the capital through the long afternoon, and the city rose out of the plains like something being built while they watched—smoke and spires and the great river bridge—and on board, in separate compartments, each of the journey's players prepared for the platform.

The Contessa sat in her suite, alone, staring at the closed safe in the wall that she had filled with nothing. She had lost the diamond, lost the insurance, lost the syndicate's backing, and she had gained, in exchange, a future she could not yet picture and a photograph of a dead laughing girl she could not stop seeing. She did not know yet what she would do at the capital. She knew only that she would not run, because running was what she had been doing for four days, and she was tired of it.

In second class, Adelaide Frost folded her letters into her vanity case, the photograph tucked beside them, and sat with June's hand in hers. There was no performance left in her now, no mask. Only the quiet of a woman who had spent forty years trying to retrieve a thing and had finally, by the grace of a girl she had not raised well but had raised true, retrieved it. "What happens now?" she said.

June squeezed her hand. "Now we get off the train. We find somewhere to have dinner. And if you want, we never run a con again." She looked at her aunt, who had taught her everything she knew and nothing about this. "I think I'd like to try honesty, for a change. See if it suits me."

"It won't," said Adelaide, but she was smiling. "But try it. I'll be here when you come back."

In the corridor, Vane and Quill stood together, looking out the window at the approaching city. "You never broke your promise, did you," said Vane.

"I never touched a lock I didn't need to," said Quill. "The promise was to Rosa, and I kept it. The safe opened itself, and I only heard it happen."

"That's sophistry."

"It's professionalism," said Quill. "There's a difference."

"And what will you tell Rosa about this journey?"

"I will tell her that I met a group of people who were less honest than they seemed and more honest than they knew, and that I sat in my compartment for four days and kept my hands folded and my promise whole, which is the truth, even if it is not all of it." He smiled, the genuine smile of an old man who had done one more good thing. "That will be enough. It always is."


Chapter 48: Pellam's Verdict

The train pulled into the capital station as if nothing had happened—which, to the platform, was exactly the case. To the passengers on the Marigold, it was the end of a story that had begun in one genre and arrived in another.

Inspector Pellam stood on the platform, hat in hand, watching his traveling companions disperse into the city. Sweet had been met by two men in plain clothes who did not introduce themselves and whom Sweet had clearly been expecting, because his face, when he saw them, went the color of old paper. He had walked between them into the station's depths, a man entering a room he had not chosen. Anton had watched him go, and had not felt anything, which was the best he could ask for.

The Contessa had left the train first, alone, her cloak over one arm. She had not looked back. Pellam did not know what she would do next, and he had decided not to follow her, because his job was to solve crimes, not to sentence them. The law would find her if it needed her. The law usually did.

Henrietta Voss had shaken his hand, told him he was the best inspector she had ever worked with, and left with the genuine Saffron Star in her briefcase, bound for the museum's address and a delivery that would close a forty-year gap. "I'll send you a copy of the receipt," she said. "For your notebook."

He would frame it.

Adelaide and June had come off the train last, walking together through the great station, two women in good coats with no luggage and no destination, which Adelaide had always considered the best way to travel. June had turned at the platform edge, had looked at Vane, and had said, "Provisionally. With mutual distrust." And Vane had said, "If you're ever in the capital, look me up. I'm in the book under a different name, but I'll leave word." And June had smiled, the first genuine smile of the entire journey, and had gone.

Vane had come to Pellam and offered his hand, and Pellam had taken it. "You're a thief," said Pellam.

"I am."

"And you got away with it."

"I think we all got away with something." Vane smiled his most charming smile. "You got your case solved. I got a story I'll dine out on for years. The inspector who could not be fooled, and the thief who could not be caught, parting with mutual respect on a railway platform. It's almost too neat to be true."

"It's not true," said Pellam. "But it will make a very good ending."


Chapter 49: The Right Owner

Henrietta Voss made the delivery herself, that evening, at the small museum on the river, a building of grey stone and high windows that the tourists did not come to and the guidebooks did not mention. She had telephoned ahead, and the curator—a woman in her seventies, with grey hair and a calm manner—was waiting at the door.

"It was my grandmother's," the curator said, holding the velvet case as if it were a child's hand. "She described it to me when I was a girl. She said it was the most beautiful thing in the world, but that it had been taken from her family by men with papers and a war, and that she did not expect to see it again." She opened the case. The yellow diamond caught the evening light from the windows, and the old woman looked at it for a very long time without speaking. "I did not expect to see it either."

"Then this is for her," said Voss. "And for you. And for the file I am going to close on my desk tomorrow, with a note saying that the claimant withdrew and the stone was returned to its legal owners."

The curator closed the case. "Who should I thank?"

"You should thank a woman on a train," said Voss. "An old woman, who came to the journey for different reasons than the ones she gave, and who carried a photograph of your grandmother in the false bottom of her vanity case for forty years." She turned to leave. "I don't know her real name, but I know where you can forward a letter, and I will leave it at the desk on my way out."

She did, and the letter reached Adelaide three weeks later, in a pension by the sea, and when Adelaide read it she did not cry, but she found the photograph of the laughing girl and looked at it for a long time, and then she put it back in the false bottom, because some things are too precious to display and too true to lose.

The Saffron Star had come home. Not to the woman who had worn it, or the woman who had stolen it, or the woman who had insured it. To the girl in the photograph, and the museum the girl's granddaughter kept, and the quiet that follows a long journey, when the last door closes and the light settles on what has always been there.


Chapter 50: The Platform at the Capital

Cornelius Vane stood on the station concourse an hour after the Marigold Express had emptied, watching the passengers dissolve into the evening traffic, and felt something he had not expected to feel. He had not stolen the diamond. He had not gotten rich. He had spent four days in a first-class compartment, been pickpocketed and partnered and outmaneuvered by a young woman with better hands than his, and had come out of it with nothing but a story and the strange satisfaction of being poorer than he had started.

He should have minded. He did not.

He was about to leave when he saw her coming back through the crowd—June, alone, without Adelaide, her small bag over her shoulder, her quick eyes scanning the concourse until they found him.

"Changed my mind," she said, stopping before him. "I told Adelaide I was going to try honesty. I think I want to start with telling you that you were the best partner I have ever worked with, and the only one I have ever not wanted to rob at the end of the job."

"That's high praise," said Vane. "Coming from you, it feels almost sincere."

"It is. That's the trouble. I have never been sincere before, and I do not know quite what to do with it." She looked at him. "I am going to try something else. I am going to do an honest job for honest pay, for a while, and see what it feels like. But I might need a partner, somewhere down the line, for things that are not quite honest and not quite dishonest, for jobs where the right thing and the clever thing happen to be the same thing."

"Are you offering me a position?"

"I'm offering you the chance to be my contact. First refusal on anything interesting that comes my way. No obligations, no promises, no trust required." She smiled, and it was the same smile she had given him over the partnership in the lounge, the one that said she was not sure she believed him but was willing to find out. "You can think about it."

"I don't need to think about it." Vane extended his hand. "Partners. Provisionally. With mutual distrust."

June took it. "Provisionally. With mutual distrust."

They stood together in the vaulted station, the evening traffic flowing around them, the platform behind them where the Marigold Express would stand until the next journey, when a new cast of strangers would board and begin their own stories. The diamond was gone, to where it belonged. The debt was paid, to whatever extent debts are ever paid. The Contessa had vanished into the city. The inspector had his notebook. And on the concourse of the capital station, two thieves who were trying to be something else parted, having given each other the one thing they had never given anyone: the possibility of a sequel.

Away down the track, the Marigold Express gave a long, low whistle, and was silent.
