Chapter 1: The Heat

River sat on the concrete steps of the Laundromat. The asphalt shimmered like water. He had been sitting there for forty-seven minutes, counting the cracks in the parking lot. He was at forty-three when a man in a blue pickup pulled in and hauled a garbage bag of clothes to the door. The man looked at River and nodded. River nodded back. The air was thick and wet. The temperature had hit ninety-seven by noon. It was three-thirty now and the heat had not broken. Cicadas buzzed in the palmetto scrub behind the Laundromat, a sound like a wire pulled tight. River watched a dog cross the road, ribs showing through its fur. The dog disappeared between two trailers. River shifted his weight. The concrete burned through his shorts. He had been up since six. He had eaten a bowl of cereal and watched the morning news alone. The television was always on in the Corsos' trailer. It filled the silence his mother left. She had been gone since yesterday afternoon. This was not unusual. But the longer he sat, the more the heat pressed down, and the more he felt like something was waiting. He did not know what. He only knew that the world felt heavier than it had yesterday.

Chapter 2: Marlboro Lights

Dina came home at six-thirty. River heard the Dart before he saw it — the rattling muffler, the squealing belt. He stood up. The car pulled into the dirt patch beside the trailer and stopped. His mother got out. She was wearing her waitress uniform, the blue one with the coffee stains. Her left eye was swollen, the skin around it purple and tight. She walked past him without speaking and went inside. River followed. She was standing at the kitchen counter, lighting a cigarette. Her hands shook. The kitchen smelled like smoke and burned coffee. River opened the refrigerator and looked inside. There was a jar of pickles, a carton of eggs, and a stick of butter. He closed the door. "You want me to make something?" he said. Dina exhaled. The smoke curled toward the ceiling. "There's macaroni in the cupboard," she said. He boiled the water. He added the noodles. He watched the cheese powder turn the water orange. Dina sat at the table and smoked and did not speak. When the macaroni was ready, River put a bowl in front of her. She looked at it. She looked at him. "Car door," she said. River nodded. He sat down across from her and ate his own bowl in silence. The television murmured in the living room. The weatherman was pointing at a cloud over the Atlantic. River watched the map over his mother's shoulder. The cloud was getting bigger.

Chapter 3: The Weather Channel

River woke before the sun. He padded to the living room in his bare feet and turned on the television. The Weather Channel was his favorite thing. He liked the maps and the graphics and the way the meteorologists talked about pressure systems like they were old friends. He turned the volume low so it would not wake his mother. On the screen, a meteorologist stood in front of a satellite image. A cluster of clouds moved west from the coast of Africa. The man called it a tropical wave. River watched the dotted line extend across the Atlantic. The computer models showed several possible paths. One of them bent toward Florida. River sat cross-legged on the floor. The light from the television made his face blue. He watched until the commercial break, then got up and poured himself a bowl of cereal. The milk was almost gone, only a thin film left in the bottom of the carton. He wrote "MILK" on a piece of notebook paper and stuck it to the refrigerator with a magnet shaped like a lime. He added "SOUP" and "BREAD" and "WATER" to the list. His mother would not see the list. She never looked at the refrigerator.

Chapter 4: Mrs. Delgado's Porch

The mail came at eleven. River walked to the cluster of mailboxes at the entrance of the park. He sorted through the envelopes and found one for Mrs. Delgado — a water bill in a window envelope. He carried it to her trailer, which was painted pale yellow with a white trim. Her porch had a rocking chair and a pot of marigolds, the flowers orange and gold, the only bright color on the lot. He knocked on the screen door. Mrs. Delgado came to the door wiping her hands on a dishtowel. She was small and straight-backed, her gray hair pulled into a tight bun. Her eyes were dark and quick, the kind that missed nothing. "Mail," River said. She took the envelope. She did not open it. "Come in," she said. "I have iced tea." He stepped inside. Her trailer was cool and smelled like cumin and lemon. The living room had a sofa covered in crackling plastic and a framed photograph of a man in a military uniform. Mrs. Delgado poured two glasses of tea from a sweating glass pitcher. She added sugar from a ceramic jar shaped like a rooster. She handed one to River. "You are alone again," she said. It was not a question. River sipped the tea. It was cold and sweet. "My mother is sleeping," he said. Mrs. Delgado sat down in her rocking chair. She looked at him over the rim of her glass. "The heat is bad today," she said. "Stay here until it passes." River sat on the sofa, careful not to crackle the plastic. He did not know how to talk to old people. But the tea was good, and the shade was cool, and he did not want to go home.

Chapter 5: The Mobile Home

The trailer was a double-wide, beige with brown trim, set on concrete blocks behind the Laundromat. The steps were plywood, splintered at the edges. The screen door had a hole in it the size of a fist, the mesh pushed inward. Inside, the carpet was the color of sand, stained in worn paths from the kitchen to the bathroom to the bedroom. The walls were paneled in fake wood. The air conditioner sat in the living room window, a heavy white box that dripped water into a bucket on the floor. River emptied the bucket twice a day. The kitchen had a gas stove with three working burners. The refrigerator hummed and rattled. The bathroom had a tub with a rust ring and a medicine cabinet with a mirror that was slightly cracked, a fault line running top to bottom. River's room was the smaller of the two bedrooms. His bed was a twin mattress on the floor. His clothes were in a plastic bin. He had a bookshelf made of cinder blocks and boards, filled with paperbacks from the library. On the wall above his bed, he had taped a map of the United States. He had marked the places he wanted to go with colored pushpins. There were seven of them: Montana, Oregon, Maine, Colorado, Washington, Alaska, and Newfoundland. He had never been outside Florida. He traced the routes with his finger sometimes, imagining the roads he would take.

Chapter 6: Dina's Shift

Dina came out of the bedroom at four in the afternoon. Her eye had turned from purple to green at the edges, the swelling going down. She had combed her hair and put on lipstick. She looked tired but presentable. "I got a shift tonight," she said. She was pulling on her white sneakers. "Double shift. I'll be back in the morning." River was sitting on the floor, reading a library book about sharks. He looked up. "The storm is getting stronger," he said. "It's a tropical storm now. They named it Celia." Dina stopped tying her shoe. She looked at him. "It's not going to hit us," she said. "They always say that." "They don't always say that." "They said Andrew was going to miss us, and it didn't." Dina stood up. She picked up her purse from the counter. The purse was worn brown leather, the strap held together with duct tape. "I have to go," she said. "There's money on the counter for pizza." She walked to the door. She paused with her hand on the screen. She did not turn around. "Lock the door," she said. The screen door banged shut. The Dart coughed and started. River listened to the sound fade down the road. He went back to his book. The sharks in the photographs were the size of his hand on the page. The text said they could grow to twenty feet. He tried to imagine twenty feet.

Chapter 7: The Upgrade

River woke up at two in the morning. The television was still on. He had fallen asleep on the floor with his book open on his chest, a shark frozen mid-leap on the page. The Weather Channel had a special report. The tropical wave was now Hurricane Celia. It had been upgraded to a Category 2. The cone of uncertainty had narrowed. It pointed directly at the Florida peninsula. The projected landfall was somewhere between Fort Myers and Cape Canaveral. River's trailer park was between Fort Myers and Cape Canaveral. He sat up. His neck was stiff from the floor. He watched the satellite loop of the storm spinning over the warm water. It looked like a white wound, rotating with terrible patience. The meteorologist said the water temperatures were in the upper eighties. The storm was expected to strengthen. River went to the kitchen and opened the pantry. There were three cans of soup, a box of saltines, and a jar of peanut butter. He counted the cans. He counted them again. He went to the living room and added "SOUP" to the list on the refrigerator with a heavy hand. The pencil broke. He sharpened it with a butter knife and added "FLASHLIGHT" and "BATTERIES" and "PLYWOOD." He knew he would not get any of them.

Chapter 8: Randy's Announcement

Randy Phelps nailed a sheet of paper to the bulletin board outside the office. The paper had a map of the park and a list of instructions in block print: BOARD WINDOWS. FILL GAS TANKS. FILL WATER CONTAINERS. KNOW YOUR EVACUATION ZONE. Randy had used a permanent marker and his handwriting slanted hard to the right. A small crowd gathered. Wanda from Lot 16 asked if the storm was really coming. Randy said it was. Tommy from Lot 9 asked where to get plywood. Randy said the hardware store on 27 had a pile in the back. River stood at the edge of the crowd. He did not say anything. He watched the way the adults moved — fast, jerky, like insects before rain. After the crowd dispersed, Mrs. Delgado appeared beside him. She had a scarf tied over her gray hair. She looked at the bulletin board. "Do you have plywood?" she said. River shook his head. "Do you have a plan?" River thought about the three cans of soup. He thought about the dead batteries in the flashlight. He thought about the empty milk jug in the fridge. "No," he said. Mrs. Delgado nodded. She did not scold him. She did not offer comfort. She put her hand on his shoulder and held it there for a moment. Her palm was warm and dry. Then she walked back to her yellow trailer.

Chapter 9: The Lie

River walked to the grocery store on the highway. The parking lot was full. People were loading cases of water and bags of ice into their trunks. A man was filling a gas can at the pump. The air had changed. It felt charged, like before a thunderstorm but bigger. The sky had a yellow tinge. Inside the store, the water aisle was almost empty. River grabbed two gallon jugs from the bottom shelf. He found the last loaf of bread. He bought a can of SpaghettiOs and a box of granola bars. He paid with crumpled bills from the jar on the kitchen counter. The cashier, a teenage girl with braces, did not look at him. She scanned his items and took his money and moved to the next customer. The line stretched to the back of the store. On the way back, he passed Randy's truck. Randy was unloading plywood from the bed, sweating through his shirt. "Hey, kid," Randy said. "Your mom get supplies?" River stopped. "She's at the grocery store," he said. "She'll be back soon." Randy nodded. He wiped his forehead with his sleeve. "Good. You tell her to board up early. This one's going to be bad." River nodded. He kept walking. The gallon jugs cut into his fingers. He did not look back.

Chapter 10: The Bathtub

The wind started around noon. River filled the bathtub with water from the tap. He lined it with pillows and a blanket from his bed. He put the flashlight on the edge of the sink — the one with new batteries he had bought at the gas station. He carried the three cans of soup, the can opener, the jar of peanut butter, and the box of granola bars into the bathroom. He put them in the sink. He closed the toilet lid and sat down. The wind moaned through the gap in the screen door. The trailer creaked. River looked at his reflection in the medicine cabinet mirror. The crack ran from the top to the bottom, splitting his face in two. He wondered if the storm would break the mirror completely. He wondered if that was bad luck. He picked up the flashlight. He turned it on and off. On and off. The light was bright and yellow. He turned it off and sat in the dark. The wind was louder now. He could hear things hitting the side of the trailer — branches, loose siding, something that sounded like metal. He got into the bathtub and pulled the blanket over his head and waited.

Chapter 11: First Rain

The rain came first as scattered drops, then as a wall. River watched from the kitchen window, standing on his tiptoes. The drops hit the glass at an angle and streaked sideways. Within minutes he could not see the Laundromat. He could not see the road. The world had gone gray and liquid. The trailer began to shudder. The air conditioner rattled in its frame, the bolts groaning. River stepped back from the window. The wind screamed. A piece of something — a branch, a trash can, a piece of someone's roof — hit the side of the trailer with a sound like a gunshot. River flinched. He went to the bathroom and stood in the doorway. The bathtub waited, filled and pillowed. But he was not ready to get in yet. He wanted to see. He wanted to know what it looked like when the world ended. He stood in the doorway and watched the gray press against the windows. The sound was a constant roar, like standing under a waterfall. He had read about hurricanes. He had watched the satellite loops. Nothing had prepared him for the noise.

Chapter 12: The Power Goes

The lights flickered once, twice, then died. The refrigerator stopped humming. The television went dark. The sudden absence of sound was worse than the noise. River stood in the dark living room. The only light came from the windows, which had gone black with rain and cloud. He felt for the flashlight on the bathroom sink. He found it. He clicked the switch. Nothing. He clicked it again. Nothing. He shook it. Something rattled inside. He opened the battery compartment. The batteries were not dead. They were gone. He must have put the old flashlight back on the sink by mistake, the one with no batteries. He stood in the dark. The wind howled around the trailer. He felt his way to the kitchen, his hands out, touching the wall. He opened drawers until he found a book of matches. He lit one. The flame shook in his hand. He used the light to find a candle stuck to a saucer on the counter — the one his mother kept for when the power went out during thunderstorms. He lit the candle. The flame steadied. It was not much, but it was something to hold onto.

Chapter 13: The Roof Cries

The first drip landed on River's shoulder. He looked up. A dark stain spread across the ceiling panel above the kitchen table. Water pooled in the center, then dripped. He moved the candle. A second drip started near the refrigerator. He put a pot under it. A third started in the hallway. He used a mixing bowl. A fourth, in the living room by the television. He had nothing left. He used his mother's purse, emptying it on the counter and placing it open on the floor. The water drummed against plastic and metal — drip-drip-drum, drip-drip-drum — a rhythm that had no end. River sat in the hallway with his back against the wall, the candle flickering on the floor beside him. The roof cried. The walls shook. The wind was a living thing outside, trying to find a way in. He watched the water fill the pots. He listened to them fill, each drip a small percussion. He had read that the human brain could not tolerate silence. That was not the problem here. The problem was that the silence was full of sounds he could not identify.

Chapter 14: The Worst of It

The trailer lifted off its blocks. River felt it happen — a lurch, a tilt, a settling that was wrong. The floor listed to one side. He scrambled into the bathroom. He got into the bathtub. He pulled the mattress over himself. The world went dark. The sound was not wind anymore. It was pressure, a weight against his ears, a vibration in his chest that would not stop. The trailer groaned and shifted on its foundation. River curled into a ball in the cold water. He thought of his mother. He thought of the sharks in his book. He thought of Mrs. Delgado's iced tea and the crack in the medicine cabinet mirror. He did not want to die in a bathtub. He pressed his palms against his ears. He prayed. Not to a God he knew, but to something — the storm, the sky, the water in the tub, the cold porcelain against his back. Please. Please. Please. The word lost its meaning. The wind did not care. But River kept saying it anyway. He said it until his throat was dry. He said it until the word was just a sound.

Chapter 15: The Eye

The silence woke him. River did not know when he had fallen asleep. He lifted the mattress and looked around. Light came through the bathroom window — gray and thin, but light. The wind had stopped. The rain had stopped. Everything was still, perfectly still. River climbed out of the tub. His legs were numb and cold. He walked to the front door. It was gone. The door frame was empty, a ragged hole where the screen and the solid door had been. He stepped outside. The world was gray and quiet. The sky was a pale, bruised white. The air was cool and damp. The Laundromat was still there, but the roof was gone. A car was on its side in the drainage ditch. Trees were down everywhere, their roots exposed like white bones. The trailer park looked like someone had picked it up and shaken it hard. River walked to the middle of the road. The silence pressed against his ears like cotton. He could see the horizon. He had never been able to see the horizon from here before. There had always been a wall of trees.

Chapter 16: The Back Half

It came back. The wind returned from the opposite direction, harder and meaner. River ran. The front door was gone but the trailer was still there, tilted on its blocks. He jumped through the empty frame. He grabbed the bathroom door and pulled. It would not close. The trailer had shifted. The door was stuck, the frame out of square. The wind hit the trailer like a fist. The walls bulged inward, the paneling groaning. River dropped to his knees. He crawled to the bathroom. He pushed the mattress into the tub. He got in. He covered himself with the wet blanket. The front door frame was empty, a mouth open to the storm. The wind reached inside. It tore the cabinet doors off the kitchen hinges, wood splintering. It threw the refrigerator onto its side with a crash that shook the floor. It ripped the television off its stand and carried it away through the empty door frame. River heard all of it from inside the bathtub. He pressed his face into a pillow and screamed until his throat was raw. The sound did not carry. The wind swallowed everything.

Chapter 17: Stuck

Afterward — minutes or hours, River could not tell — he tried to get out. The bathroom door would not move. He pushed. It shifted an inch and stuck. He put his shoulder against it. Nothing. He looked at the hinges. The pins were on his side, brass pins with flat heads. He found a screwdriver in the medicine cabinet — a small one, meant for eyeglasses. It was not the right tool, but it was the only one he had. He worked the bottom pin loose with his fingers. It took a long time. The pin was tight. His fingers cramped. He kept going. The pin came out with a scrape of metal. He did the top pin the same way, slower, more careful. The door came free in his hands. He leaned it against the wall and stepped into the hallway. The trailer was a wreck. The front half was open to the sky, the roof peeled back. The back half was crushed. A tree had fallen across the bedroom. The trunk was thick as a man's chest. It had punched through the roof like paper. River stared at the tree. If he had been in his room, he would be dead.

Chapter 18: The Basement

The trailer had no basement, but the concrete pad beneath it offered a gap. The crawlspace was a foot of clearance above the ground, filled with spiders and dust and the smell of wet dirt. River found the access hatch in the utility closet. He pried it open with the screwdriver. He looked down into the dark. He did not want to go in. The wind screamed through the empty front of the trailer. A piece of debris hit the overturned fridge. River climbed down. The dirt was damp and cold. He crawled on his elbows, dragging the can of SpaghettiOs he had grabbed from the sink. He found a dry spot near the center of the pad, where the ground rose slightly. He sat with his back against the concrete. The wind was muffled here. The crawlspace vibrated like the inside of a drum. A possum watched him from the shadows, its eyes reflecting whatever dim light found its way in through gaps in the skirting. River opened the SpaghettiOs with the pull tab. He ate them cold, scooping the sauce with his fingers. The possum watched. River did not offer to share.

Chapter 19: The Long Hour

River told himself a story. He did not know if it was true. He told it anyway. His father was a fisherman from the Keys. He had hands like leather. He could tie knots that would never come undone. He had left before River was born, not because he wanted to, but because the sea called him back. He thought about River every day. He had a photograph of River in his wallet, tucked behind his fishing license. One day he would come back. One day they would go out on the boat together and his father would teach him to read the water. River knew the story was a lie. His mother had told him the truth once, drunk and crying at the kitchen table: his father was a man she met at a bar in Daytona. He sold timeshares. He was the kind of man who forgot your name after he got what he wanted. River kept both versions in his head, side by side, like two photographs of the same empty room. He tried both on for size. Neither fit. The possum had moved closer. River ate another handful of SpaghettiOs and watched the possum watch him.

Chapter 20: Morning

River woke to light. Real light. The crawlspace was bright. He crawled to the hatch and pushed it open. The sky was blue. The wind was gone. The air smelled like wet wood and gasoline. River climbed out. The trailer was unrecognizable. The tree had crushed the back half completely. The front half was gone — walls, roof, everything, swept away by the wind. The road was buried under branches and debris. He walked to where the Laundromat used to be. The building was a skeleton — cinder block walls, no roof, no machines. The washing machines and dryers were gone, carried off somewhere. The office had no roof either. Randy's truck was on its side, wheels in the air, one rear tire still slowly spinning. River walked to the drainage ditch. Dina's Dart was there, upside down, wheels in the air. He stood on the edge of the ditch and looked at the car. The windows were broken. The interior was full of brown water. A bird had landed on the undercarriage. His mother was not in it. He did not know if this was the best moment of his life or the worst.

Chapter 21: What Remains

River spent the morning walking through the wreckage. He found his books scattered in a mud puddle fifty yards from where the trailer used to be. The pages were swollen and illegible, the covers torn. He left them. He found his mother's photograph — the one from before he was born, the one where she was laughing on a beach — warped and wet, the paper soft as cloth. He dried it on his shirt as gently as he could. He found a shoe that did not belong to him, a woman's sandal with a broken strap. He found a photograph of a woman he did not know, her face creased and water-stained. He found a child's doll, half-buried in mud, its dress torn. He found the Laundromat's sign, twisted and torn, lying in the middle of the road like a dead bird. He did not find any people. The park was empty. Everyone had either evacuated or been taken somewhere. Everyone except him. He walked back to the crawlspace. He sat on the concrete pad. He ate the last of the granola bars. He drank rainwater from a bucket that had landed upside-down and stayed mostly clean. He waited.

Chapter 22: The Dart

River walked back to the drainage ditch. The Dart was still there, resting on its roof in the brown water. He slid down the embankment, his shoes sinking into the mud. The water was up to his knees, muddy and warm. He circled the car slowly, looking at it from every angle. The driver's side door was open, hanging by one hinge. The windshield was shattered into a milky web of cracks. The interior was full of mud and leaves and small branches, the seats swollen with water. He reached in and opened the glove compartment. Water spilled out, brown and gritty, running down his arm. He found the registration, the insurance card, a half-empty pack of gum, a book of matches from a bar in Tampa called The Anchor. No note. No explanation. Just the ordinary debris of his mother's life. River put the damp registration in his pocket. He climbed out of the ditch. He walked back to the remains of his home. The sun was hot. The sky was clear and blue. It was hard to believe the storm had ever happened.

Chapter 23: Mrs. Delgado

River found her near the office, standing in the rubble of her trailer. The pale yellow walls were collapsed inward. The white trim was scattered in pieces across the lot. The porch was gone entirely. The rocking chair was lodged in a tree twenty feet away, one arm missing. Mrs. Delgado was holding her sewing machine. It was the only thing she had saved. She had a gash on her forearm, deep and bleeding. The blood ran down her wrist and dripped off her fingers. Her dress was torn at the shoulder. Her hair had come loose from its bun. She was crying. River had never seen an adult cry before. It was different from a child crying. It was quieter. There was no hope in it, only acceptance. "I have a first-aid kit," River said. She looked at him. Her eyes were wet and red. "Where?" she said. "In the office. I saw it when Randy was putting up the notice." She nodded. She did not let go of the sewing machine. River led her to the office. The door was gone. The floor was covered in wet ceiling tiles that crunched under their feet. He found the first-aid kit behind the counter, still in its plastic box.

Chapter 24: First Aid

River opened the first-aid kit. He had watched Rescue 911. He knew the basics. He took out the gauze and the tape and the bottle of saline. He poured saline over the cut. Mrs. Delgado winced but did not make a sound. The wound was deep but clean — a piece of metal or glass had sliced through the skin, missing the artery. River pressed gauze to it. He wrapped it with the roll of bandage. He taped the end down. His hands were steady. He had never done this before on a real person. "You did this before," Mrs. Delgado said. "No," River said. "I watched it on television." Mrs. Delgado laughed. It was a broken sound, half-cough, half-sob. "Television," she said. "I do not have one. Maybe I should get one." River finished the bandage. He checked the tension. "It's tight," he said. "Not too tight." "It's good," she said. She looked at her bandaged arm. She looked at River. "You are a good boy." River did not know what to say. No one had ever called him a good boy before. He packed up the first-aid kit and put it back behind the counter.

Chapter 25: The Relief

The National Guard arrived in the afternoon. Olive trucks rolled down the highway, kicking up dust. Soldiers in fatigues set up a checkpoint at the entrance of the park. They distributed water and MREs in brown plastic pouches. River and Mrs. Delgado sat on an overturned spool of cable, eating crackers and cheese spread from plastic packets. Mrs. Delgado shared her crackers with a stray dog that appeared from nowhere. It was a mutt, brown and thin, with a cut on its ear. "You cannot adopt every animal," River said. "Watch me," Mrs. Delgado said. She broke off another piece of cracker. The dog took it gently from her fingers. A soldier came over. He was young, maybe nineteen, with a sunburned nose and a shaved head. "You folks okay?" he said. "You need a medic?" Mrs. Delgado shook her head. "We are fine," she said. "You have a list of survivors?" The soldier pulled a clipboard from under his arm. "Who am I looking for?" "River Corso," River said. "And Delia Delgado." The soldier wrote it down with a pencil he licked first. "We'll get you on the bus to the shelter," he said.

Chapter 26: Randy's Inventory

Randy Phelps appeared at dusk, limping, his shirt torn, his face bruised and dirty. He had a spiral notebook in his hand. He stood at the entrance of the park and called out names. People answered. Some had evacuated. Some had ridden the storm out in bathtubs and closets. Some were missing. Randy wrote in his notebook with a pencil stub. He crossed out names. He circled others. Seven people unaccounted for. Dina Corso was one of them. River heard his mother's name and felt his stomach drop. He walked over to Randy. "She's on the list," he said. "My mom. She was in Tampa. She didn't come back." Randy looked at him. The big man's face was haggard. He did not say he was sorry. He did not offer false hope. He put his hand on River's shoulder. "We'll find her, kid," he said. It was the same thing everyone said. But Randy said it like he meant it. Like he had already decided that finding Dina Corso was his problem now. River nodded. He went back to sit with Mrs. Delgado.

Chapter 27: The Phone Bank

The Red Cross set up a trailer with a satellite phone. A line formed. People held scraps of paper with phone numbers written in pencil or pen, some smeared with water damage. Mrs. Delgado stood in line for an hour, patient and still. When she reached the phone, she dialed a number from memory. She spoke in Spanish, fast and low. River stood beside her. He watched her face. She was talking to her son. Her eyes filled with tears but her voice did not break. She handed the phone to River. "He wants to say hello," she said. River put the phone to his ear. "Hello?" "This is Luis," said a deep voice. "Thank you for taking care of my mother." "I didn't," River said. "She took care of me." Luis was quiet for a moment. "I'm coming to get her," he said. "I'm coming to get both of you." The line crackled. "She is a stubborn woman," Luis said. "But she is good." "I know," River said. He handed the phone back to Mrs. Delgado. She spoke for another minute, then hung up. She wiped her eyes with the back of her good hand.

Chapter 28: The Shelter

The bus took them to a high school gymnasium. The floor was covered in cots, row after row of them, green canvas and metal frames. The air smelled like sweat and bleach and instant coffee. Volunteers handed out blankets and pillows that came wrapped in plastic. Mrs. Delgado chose two cots in the corner, against the wall. She put her sewing machine under her cot. She sat on the edge and looked around at the crowded room. River sat on his cot. He had never slept in a room with this many people before. A baby was crying somewhere. An old man was snoring with a wet rattle. Someone was talking loudly on a cell phone. "I cannot sleep in here," Mrs. Delgado said. River looked at her. "Me neither." She lay down anyway. She closed her eyes. She began to hum. It was a slow song, in a minor key. River did not recognize the melody. But it wrapped around him like a blanket. He lay down. He closed his eyes. The cot was hard. The blanket smelled like detergent. He listened to Mrs. Delgado hum until the song carried him into sleep.

Chapter 29: The Search

In the morning, River found the volunteer coordinator. She was a woman in her forties with a clipboard and a radio on her belt. She had short gray hair and reading glasses on a chain around her neck. River told her his mother was missing. He told her she was driving home from Tampa when the storm hit. He told her her name was Dina Corso. The woman wrote it down. She asked for a description. River described his mother: dark hair, about five-foot-five, a scar above her left eyebrow from a car accident when she was nineteen. She drove a '78 Dodge Dart, green. She was a waitress. She smoked Marlboro Lights. The woman wrote everything down. She had a serious face, the kind that did not give anything away. When she finished writing, she looked at River. "We'll do everything we can," she said. Her voice was kind. But her eyes said something else. They said: prepare yourself. River understood. He thanked her and walked back to his cot. He sat down. He did not cry. He was done crying for now.

Chapter 30: The News

A list was posted on the bulletin board outside the gymnasium office. It was typed on white paper. It said "FATALITIES" at the top in bold letters. River stood in front of it. He read each name. There were twelve names on the list. He read them all. He read them again, slowly, from top to bottom, making sure he did not skip any. Dina Corso was not among them. River felt a surge of relief so strong his knees went weak. Then he felt something else. A darker thought. She was not on the list, but she was not here either. She was somewhere. The somewhere could be a hospital. It could be a ditch. It could be a motel room in a town she had always wanted to run to. He did not know. The not-knowing was a new kind of storm. He folded the list — he had torn it from the board while no one was watching — and put it in his pocket, next to the car registration. He pressed his hand against the pocket to feel the paper there.

Chapter 31: The Motel

FEMA moved the displaced to a motel on the interstate. The lobby was crowded with people holding plastic bags of belongings, their faces blank with exhaustion. The rooms were small and smelled like carpet cleaner and cigarette smoke that had soaked into the curtains years ago. River shared a room with Mrs. Delgado and three strangers — a woman and her two children, a boy about River's age and a younger girl. The woman's name was Cheryl. Her trailer had been completely destroyed. She had lost everything. She did not complain. She combed her daughter's tangled hair with her fingers and told her son to stop kicking the headboard. River sat on his bed. The television worked. He watched the Weather Channel. The cleanup had begun. The recovery was underway. The storm had moved out to sea and was weakening over cooler water. He looked out the window. The parking lot was full of cars with out-of-state plates. He did not know how long they would be here. The sign outside said "Sunshine Motor Inn." It was missing the 'n' in Sunshine. He thought that was probably how things were now.

Chapter 32: Luis Arrives

Luis Delgado drove down from Miami in a red pickup truck. He was a big man, broad-shouldered, with a gray mustache and gentle hands. He wore a guayabera shirt and work boots. He hugged his mother for a long time, his face buried in her gray hair. When he let go, his eyes were wet. He turned to River. He held out his hand. River shook it. Luis's hand was hard and warm. "You're the boy," Luis said. "I'm the boy," River said. Luis smiled. "You look like you need a good meal." He took them to a diner on the highway. The diner was full of people in the same situation — tired, dirty, wearing borrowed clothes. Luis ordered for all three of them without looking at the menu. He ordered rice and beans and fried plantains and a whole fried fish with its head still on. He told River to eat until he could not eat anymore. River did. The food was hot and good and salty. It was the first real meal he had eaten in days. He ate until his stomach hurt.

Chapter 33: The Call

The motel office called the room. The phone rang at seven in the morning. Mrs. Delgado answered. She listened. Her face changed, the lines around her mouth deepening. She held the phone out to River. "For you," she said. River took the phone. It was warm from her hand. "Hello?" "River?" It was his mother's voice. It sounded thin and far away, like a radio station fading in and out. "Mom?" "I'm in a hospital," she said. "In Lakeland. I don't know how I got here." River sat down on the edge of the bed. His heart was beating fast against his ribs. "You don't remember the storm?" "I don't remember anything," she said. "The storm. The car. Tampa. It's all gone. Like it never happened." River closed his eyes. "Are you okay?" "I'm okay," she said. "They say I hit my head pretty hard. They say I'll be fine. But I don't remember, River. I don't remember." Her voice broke into pieces. "I don't remember your face." River held the phone. He did not know what to say. "It's okay," he said finally. "I remember for both of us."

Chapter 34: The Visit

Luis drove River to Lakeland in his pickup. The highway was lined with downed trees and blue tarps on roofs. The hospital was a low white building with a covered entrance. River walked through the automatic doors. He asked for his mother's room at the front desk. The nurse pointed down the hall. Room 214. He walked. The floor was linoleum, buffed to a shine. The air smelled like antiseptic and cafeteria food. He knocked on the door. "Come in," she said. He opened the door. Dina was sitting up in bed. Her hair was clean, brushed away from her face. She had a bandage on her forehead, white gauze taped to her skin. She was eating Jell-O from a plastic cup. She looked at him. She smiled. It was the smile of someone trying to remember how. "Hi, River," she said. "Hi, Mom," he said. He stood in the doorway. He did not know if he should go in. "Come here," she said. "Let me look at you." He walked to the bed. She took his face in her hands. She looked at him like she was reading a book she had read before but lost. "You have my eyes," she said. "I know." "Your father's mouth." "I know." She let go. She picked up the Jell-O. She ate another spoonful. "I don't remember the storm," she said. "I know," River said. He sat down in the chair beside the bed.

Chapter 35: The Doctor

A neurologist came in. Dr. Patel. She was young, with kind eyes and a calm voice. She pulled a chair close to the bed and sat down. She explained that Dina had a concussion. The brain had swollen. There was some damage to the hippocampus, the part that forms new memories. The amnesia was retrograde — she had lost the weeks before the crash. She might regain them. She might not. The brain was unpredictable. River listened carefully. He understood most of it. He had read a book about the brain last year from the public library. He asked Dr. Patel if the amnesia could be selective, if the brain could choose what to forget. She looked at him, surprised by the question. She said yes. The brain protected itself. It could bury things that were too painful. River looked at his mother. She was looking out the window at a tree with no leaves, its branches bare against the white sky. He wondered what the brain had buried. He wondered if it was the storm, or something else. The something else was heavier than any hurricane. He thanked Dr. Patel. She left. The room was quiet.

Chapter 36: Mrs. Delgado's Offer

Back at the motel, Mrs. Delgado sat River down on the bed. She took his hands in hers. Her palms were rough and warm. "Your mother is in the hospital," she said. "You cannot stay here alone. I have a FEMA trailer in my son's backyard in Miami. You will stay with me until your mother is better." River started to say no. He started to say he was fine. He started to say he could take care of himself, that he had been taking care of himself his whole life. Mrs. Delgado squeezed his hands, cutting him off. "I know you can take care of yourself," she said. "But you do not have to. That is what I am trying to teach you." River looked at her, into her dark eyes. She was not asking. She was telling him what was going to happen. "Okay," he said. Mrs. Delgado nodded. She let go of his hands. "Good," she said. "Now help me pack." She stood up and started folding clothes into a plastic bag. River watched her for a moment, the way she moved without hesitation. Then he got up and helped her.

Chapter 37: Miami

Miami was nothing like central Florida. The air was saltier and thicker. The buildings were taller, painted pink and blue and yellow. The highway was packed with cars that moved fast and close, drivers leaning on their horns. River pressed his face to the window of Luis's pickup. He saw the ocean for the first time when they crossed a long bridge. It was blue and endless and it took his breath away. The water stretched to the horizon, flat and glittering under the sun. Luis's house was a small bungalow in a neighborhood called Hialeah. The yard had a mango tree and a chain-link fence and a concrete saint in a blue alcove. The FEMA trailer was set up in the backyard, plugged into the house's electricity. It was small and hot. It had two beds, a small table, and a mini-fridge. Mrs. Delgado made it feel like home within an hour. She put a lace doily on the table. She hung a crucifix on the wall. She filled a vase with flowers from the garden. River sat on his bed and watched her.

Chapter 38: The Yard

Mrs. Delgado planted a garden in Luis's backyard. She pulled weeds with her bare hands, yanking them from the root. She turned the soil with a hand trowel, breaking up clods until the dirt was soft and dark. She planted seeds in straight rows, pressing each one into the earth with her thumb. Tomatoes. Peppers. Cilantro. River helped. He did not know anything about plants. He had never grown anything in his life. Mrs. Delgado showed him. She showed him how deep to put the seeds — twice the length of the seed itself. She showed him how much to water — enough to wet the soil but not drown it. She told him the soil here was young. It remembered nothing. It would grow whatever you gave it. River liked that idea. He liked the idea that dirt had no memory, that it did not hold grudges. He dug a hole with his finger. He dropped in a seed, a small brown disk. He covered it with dirt. He poured water over it. He did not know if anything would grow. But he did it anyway. Mrs. Delgado nodded. "Good," she said. "Now you are a gardener."

Chapter 39: The Phone Call

Dina called every night at seven. The first few calls were short. She asked about the weather. She asked if he was eating. She asked if Luis's house had a pool. River answered her questions in short sentences. He asked his own. How are you feeling. What did the doctors say. When can you leave. Dina's answers were vague, drifting. She was getting better. The doctors were hopeful. Maybe next week. Then one night: "What color are my eyes?" River asked. There was a pause on the line, a long crackling silence. "Brown," Dina said. "They're brown." River closed his eyes. His mother's eyes were green. They had always been green. He had looked at them every day of his life. She had told him the wrong color. "Yeah," he said. "Brown." He hung up softly. He sat on the bed in the FEMA trailer. He looked at his hands. He did not know if she had forgotten or if she had never known. Both possibilities hurt in different ways. He lay down on the bed and stared at the ceiling until the light through the window turned orange and then gray.

Chapter 40: The Visit Again

Luis put River on a Greyhound bus to Lakeland. The bus was mostly empty. River sat by the window and watched the landscape change from city to swamp to city, from strip malls to orange groves to billboards for roadside attractions. He got off at the Lakeland station, a concrete building with a faded sign. He walked to the hospital. Dina was sitting in a chair by the window, wearing regular clothes for the first time — jeans and a white blouse. She looked better. Her face had color. Her hair was brushed and pulled back. She smiled when she saw him. "Hi, River." "Hi, Mom." He sat on the edge of the bed. She stayed in the chair. They looked at each other. Sun came through the window and lit up the dust floating in the air between them. "I'm sorry," she said. "For what?" "For everything." River did not answer. Dina looked out the window at the parking lot. "I didn't mean to leave you," she said. "I know," River said. But he was not sure he believed it.

Chapter 41: The Return

Dina was discharged on a Thursday. Luis drove River to pick her up. They drove to the trailer park. The park was still a wreck. The roads had been cleared. A few new trailers had been set up. Most lots were empty, concrete pads with weeds growing through the cracks. The Corsos' plot was empty. The concrete pad was cracked and stained. The tree that had crushed the bedroom was gone, cut into sections and hauled away. The Dart was gone. Dina stood in the middle of the empty lot. She did not speak. She turned in a slow circle. River watched her. He wondered what she saw. The memory of the trailer. The years she had spent inside it. The meals she had cooked. The fights she had picked. The life she had built and abandoned and lost. After a long time, she sat down on the concrete pad. River sat beside her. "There's nothing here," she said. "I know." They sat in silence. The sun went down. The sky turned orange and then purple and then dark. Dina did not cry. River did not cry. They sat until the stars came out.

Chapter 42: The Paperwork

The FEMA office was a converted storefront in a strip mall. Fluorescent lights buzzed overhead. Folding chairs lined the walls. A row of desks with computers that looked older than River sat under a poster of a smiling family. Dina sat across from a caseworker, a thin woman with glasses on a beaded chain. She filled out forms. She signed where the caseworker pointed with a pen. She asked River to read the fine print. She said she forgot her reading glasses. Dina had never worn reading glasses. River looked at her. She looked back at him. Her face was unreadable. He read the fine print. The policy was standard. No hidden clauses. He told her it was fine. She signed with her usual signature, a quick loop and a line. She smiled at the caseworker. She collected her copies and put them in a manila envelope. Outside, she lit a cigarette. Her hands were steady. "I don't have reading glasses," she said. "I know." "I just wanted you to check." She took a long drag. "You're better at it than me."

Chapter 43: The New Place

The apartment was in Kissimmee. Two bedrooms. A living room with a carpet that was mostly clean. A kitchen with a refrigerator that did not hum or rattle. A window that faced a parking lot and, beyond it, a field of scrub grass. It was not much. It was more than the trailer had ever been. Dina hung the warped photograph on the wall — the one of her before she got tired. She put it in a frame she bought at a thrift store for fifty cents. She hung it above the sofa. River looked at the photograph. His mother was young, maybe nineteen. She was standing on a beach in a white dress. She was laughing. Her hair was blowing across her face. She looked happy. River had never seen her that happy. He wondered if the photograph was a lie or a promise. Dina stood beside him. "I used to be that girl," she said. "You're still her," River said. Dina did not answer. She touched the edge of the frame. She adjusted it a quarter-inch to the left. She stepped back. She nodded. It was not a perfect home. But it was a home.

Chapter 44: Mrs. Delgado's Kitchen

River visited Mrs. Delgado before she returned to the rebuilt trailer park. She was in Luis's kitchen, packing her sewing machine into a cardboard box. The kitchen smelled like garlic and onions. She had made lunch. Black beans and rice with fried eggs on top, the yolks bright orange against the white. River ate at the small kitchen table. Mrs. Delgado watched him eat with the satisfaction of someone who had cooked. "I made you something," she said. She took a small cloth bag from the counter. She handed it to him. He opened it. Inside was a set of sewing needles in a paper envelope, a spool of white thread, and a silver thimble. "I don't know how to sew," River said. "You will learn," Mrs. Delgado said. "Everyone needs to know how to sew. A hole in your shirt is a hole in your life. You fix it before it gets bigger." River held the bag in his hands. "Thank you," he said. Mrs. Delgado nodded. She went back to packing her dishes. River put the bag in his pocket. He finished his lunch. He helped her wash the dishes at the sink.

Chapter 45: The Apartment

River fell into a routine. School. He started seventh grade two weeks late. The teacher introduced him to the class. The kids looked at him with the look kids give the new kid whose house got destroyed by a hurricane. He sat in the back. He did his work. He did not make friends. That was fine. After school, he walked to the grocery store. He bought milk. He bought eggs. He bought whatever his mother put on the list taped to the refrigerator. He walked home. He did his homework. He watched the Weather Channel, but less often now. Dina came home at six. She worked the day shift at a diner called Mary's. She was home every night now. She made dinner. They ate together at the table. The television was off during meals. Dina asked about his day. River asked about hers. The conversation was small. It was ordinary. It was what River had always wanted. He did not know how to trust it. At night he lay awake and listened to the refrigerator hum. It did not sound right. It was too quiet.

Chapter 46: The Scar

River visited the trailer park. He took the bus from Kissimmee and walked from the highway. The park was being rebuilt. New trailers were going up, beige and white and pale green. The Laundromat had a roof again and lights inside. The office had a new door, painted red. Mrs. Delgado's trailer was new — same pale yellow, same white trim. The porch was rebuilt. The rocking chair was back, freshly painted white. Mrs. Delgado was on the porch, shelling peas into a ceramic bowl. River sat on the steps. She looked at him with those dark eyes that missed nothing. "Show me your arm," she said. He rolled up his sleeve. The scar from the crawlspace was pale and thin, like a piece of thread under his skin. He had forgotten about it until now. Mrs. Delgado traced it with her finger. Her touch was light and warm. "It will fade," she said. "It already has," River said. "Not completely. But mostly." Mrs. Delgado nodded. She went back to shelling peas. "Mostly is enough," she said.

Chapter 47: The Gift

Before River left, Mrs. Delgado went inside her new trailer. She came back with a small wooden box. It was dark wood, worn smooth at the edges from years of handling. She handed it to him. "Open it," she said. Inside was a black-and-white photograph with a scalloped edge. A young couple stood in front of a church with a domed roof. The woman was wearing a white dress that touched the ground. The man was in a suit that was too big for him, the sleeves bunched at his wrists. They were both smiling wide smiles. "That's me," Mrs. Delgado said. "And my husband. Our wedding day. Havana, 1968." River looked at the photograph. Mrs. Delgado was beautiful. She looked like she had just won something precious. "Why are you giving this to me?" River asked. "Because you know what it means," she said. "To lose everything. To start over. You are the only person I know who understands." River looked at the photograph. He looked at Mrs. Delgado standing on her rebuilt porch. "I'll keep it safe," he said. "I know you will," she said.

Chapter 48: Dina's Truth

Dina came home late. Not diner-late. Not shift-late. Late-late. Eleven o'clock. The television was off. River was still awake, sitting on the sofa reading a book about the ocean. She came in and sat down beside him. She smelled like cigarettes and something else, something River could not name. "I need to tell you something," she said. River put down his book. "The night of the storm," she said. "I didn't just go to work." River waited. "I was going to leave," she said. "I packed a bag. I had money saved. I was going to drive to Tampa and keep driving. I was going to leave you." The words hung in the air. She said it like she had been holding it for a long time, like it hurt to let go. River looked at her. "Why are you telling me this?" he asked. "Because you deserve the truth," she said. "And because I changed my mind. On the road. I turned around. And then the storm hit." River did not speak. He looked at his hands. He looked at his mother. She was crying silently. "I don't know if I can forgive you," he said. "I know," Dina said. "That's okay."

Chapter 49: The Cold Water

River walked to a lake near the apartment. It was a small lake, surrounded by pine trees that leaned slightly from years of prevailing wind. The water was brown at the edges where the tannins leached from the roots, and dark in the center. He sat on a fallen log. He took off his shoes. He walked to the water's edge. The mud was cool between his toes. He put his hand in the water. It was cold. He thought about his mother. He thought about Mrs. Delgado. He thought about the storm. The trailer. The crawlspace. The photograph of his mother on the beach. The apartment. The new school. The scar on his arm. The sewing machine. The wedding picture of Mrs. Delgado and her husband. He thought about all of it at once, a collision of images that made his head feel heavy. The cold water covered his hand up to the wrist. He held it there until his fingers went numb. He pulled his hand out. He dried it on his pants. He put his shoes back on. He walked home. The apartment was warm. His mother was in the kitchen, making dinner. The television was off. She was humming something quiet.

Chapter 50: The Garden

A year later. River and his mother drove to the trailer park in a used sedan Dina had bought with the insurance money. The park was whole again. New trailers. New people. The Laundromat had a sign again. Mrs. Delgado's garden was full. Tomatoes hung red and heavy on the vines. Peppers curved toward the sun, green and purple and red. Cilantro grew in a fragrant green bush. Mrs. Delgado was on her knees, pulling weeds with steady, practiced hands. Dina knelt beside her and started helping without being asked. River sat on the porch. The rocking chair creaked with his weight. The sky was clear. The air was warm and thick with the smell of growing things. He watched the two women work. His mother and Mrs. Delgado. Strangers a year ago. Now something else. Something River did not have a word for yet. Mrs. Delgado looked up. She caught his eye and smiled. Dina looked up. She smiled too. River smiled back. He leaned back in the rocking chair. He closed his eyes. He listened to the sound of their hands in the dirt and the birds in the trees and the distant hum of the highway. The water was warm. He was not alone.
